<p>The <b>Lamborghini Diablo</b> is a high-performance <a href="/wiki/Mid-engine_design" title="Mid-engine design">mid-engined</a> <a href="/wiki/Sports_car" title="Sports car">sports car</a> that was built by <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> automaker <a href="/wiki/Lamborghini" title="Lamborghini">Lamborghini</a> between 1990 and 2001. It was the first Lamborghini capable of attaining a top speed in excess of 200 miles per hour (320 km/h). After the end of its production run in 2001, the Diablo was replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Lamborghini_Murci%C3%A9lago" title="Lamborghini Murciélago">Lamborghini Murciélago</a>. <i>Diablo</i> means "devil" in Spanish.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_development">History of development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamborghini_Diablo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At a time when the company was financed by the Swiss-based Mimran brothers, Lamborghini began development of what was codenamed <i>Project 132</i> in June 1985 as a replacement for the <a href="/wiki/Lamborghini_Countach" title="Lamborghini Countach">Countach</a> model. The brief stated that its top speed had to be at least 315 km/h (196 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lambocarsdia_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-lambocarsdia-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The design of the car was contracted to <a href="/wiki/Marcello_Gandini" title="Marcello Gandini">Marcello Gandini</a>, who had designed its two predecessors. When Chrysler bought the company in 1987, providing money to complete its development, its management was uncomfortable with Gandini’s designs and commissioned its design team in Detroit to execute a third extensive redesign, smoothing out the trademark's sharp edges and corners of Gandini's original design, and leaving him famously unimpressed. In fact, Gandini was so disappointed with the "softened" shape that he would later realize his original design in the <a href="/wiki/Cizeta-Moroder_V16T" title="Cizeta-Moroder V16T">Cizeta-Moroder V16T</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lambocarsdia_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-lambocarsdia-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The car became known as the Diablo, carrying on Lamborghini's tradition of naming its cars after breeds of fighting bulls. The Diablo was named after a ferocious bull raised by the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Veragua" title="Duke of Veragua">Duke of Veragua</a> in the 19th century, famous for fighting an epic battle with 'El Chicorro' in Madrid on July 11, 1869.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lambocarsdia_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-lambocarsdia-4">[4]</a></sup> In the words of <i><a href="/wiki/Top_Gear_(2002_TV_series)" title="Top Gear (2002 TV series)">Top Gear</a></i> presenter <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Clarkson" title="Jeremy Clarkson">Jeremy Clarkson</a>, the Diablo was designed "solely to be the biggest head-turner in the world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The development is believed to have cost a total of 6 billion <a href="/wiki/Italian_lira" title="Italian lira">Italian lira</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lambocarsdia_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-lambocarsdia-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexican_version">Mexican version</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamborghini_Diablo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mexican version">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Lamborghini Diablo was sold as the Lamborghini Costanga from 1993 through 2000 in Mexico due to controversy against the "Diablo" name, and the fact that Mexico's majority were Bible-reading Catholics. For the Mexican market, the engine was detuned from 492 PS to 440 PS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> U.S. magazine <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor_Week" title="Motor Week">Motor Week</a></i> tested the Costanga and found it capable of accelerating from 0-60 mph in 5.4 seconds and completing the 1/4-mile in 13.2 seconds at 111 mph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

Original Lamborghini Diablo</div>

<p>The Diablo was presented to the public for sale on January 21, 1990. Its power came from a 5.7 L (348 cu in), 48-valve version of the existing <a href="/wiki/Lamborghini_V12" title="Lamborghini V12">Lamborghini V12</a> featuring <a href="/wiki/Overhead_camshaft#Dual_overhead_camshaft" title="Overhead camshaft">dual overhead cams</a> and computer-controlled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_fuel_injection" title="Electronic fuel injection">multi-point fuel injection</a>, producing a maximum output of 492 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_horsepower" title="Metric horsepower">PS</a> (362 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt" title="Kilowatt">kW</a>; 485 <a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">hp</a>) and 580 N·m (428 lb·ft) of torque. The vehicle could reach 100 km/h (62 mph) in about 4.5 seconds, with a top speed of 202 mph (325 km/h). The Diablo was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rear-wheel_drive" title="Rear-wheel drive">rear-wheel drive</a> and the engine was <a href="/wiki/Mid-engine_design" title="Mid-engine design">mid-mounted</a> to aid its weight balance.</p>
<p>The Diablo came better equipped than the Countach; standard features included fully adjustable seats and steering wheel, electric windows, an <a href="/wiki/Alpine_Electronics" title="Alpine Electronics">Alpine</a> stereo system, and <a href="/wiki/Power_steering" title="Power steering">power steering</a> from 1993 onwards. <a href="/wiki/Anti-lock_braking_system" title="Anti-lock braking system">Anti-lock brakes</a> were not initially available, although they would eventually be used. A few options were available, including a custom-molded driver's seat, remote CD changer and <a href="/wiki/Subwoofer" title="Subwoofer">subwoofer</a>, rear <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spoiler_(automotive)" title="Spoiler (automotive)">spoiler</a>, factory fitted luggage set (priced at $2,600) and an exclusive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breguet_(watch)" title="Breguet (watch)">Breguet</a> clock for the dash (priced at $10,500).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lambocarsdia_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-lambocarsdia-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/1995_Formula_One_season" title="1995 Formula One season">1995</a>, this Lamborghini had a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Safety_Car#Formula_One" title="Safety Car">Safety Car</a> role in <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a>, most notably at the Canadian Grand Prix where, however, it did not need to be officially deployed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diablo_VT">Diablo VT</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamborghini_Diablo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diablo VT">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lamborghini_Diablo_VT_(20005779784).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="5184" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Lamborghini_Diablo_VT_%2820005779784%29.jpg/220px-Lamborghini_Diablo_VT_%2820005779784%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Lamborghini_Diablo_VT_%2820005779784%29.jpg/330px-Lamborghini_Diablo_VT_%2820005779784%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Lamborghini_Diablo_VT_%2820005779784%29.jpg/440px-Lamborghini_Diablo_VT_%2820005779784%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lamborghini_Diablo_VT_(20005779784).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Lamborghini Diablo VT</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Diablo VT was introduced in 1993. Although the VT differed from the standard Diablo in a number of ways, by far the most notable change was the addition of <a href="/wiki/Four-wheel_drive" title="Four-wheel drive">all wheel drive</a>, which made use of a <a href="/wiki/Viscous_coupling_unit" title="Viscous coupling unit">viscous</a> center <a href="/wiki/Differential_(mechanical_device)" title="Differential (mechanical device)">differential</a> (a modified version of <a href="/wiki/Lamborghini_LM002" title="Lamborghini LM002">LM002</a>'s 4WD system). This provided the new nomenclature for the car (VT stands for <i>viscous traction</i>). The new drivetrain could direct up to 25% of the torque to the front wheels to aid traction during <a href="/wiki/Wheelspin" title="Wheelspin">rear wheel slip</a>, thus significantly improving the handling characteristics of the car.</p>
<p>Other improvements debuting on the VT included front air intakes below the driving lamps to improve brake cooling, larger intakes in the rear arches, a more ergonomic interior with a revised dashboard, electronically adjustable <a href="/wiki/Shock_absorber" title="Shock absorber">dampers</a>, four-piston brake calipers, power steering, and minor engine refinements. Many of these improvements, save the four-wheel drive system, soon transferred to the base Diablo, making the cars visually nearly identical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diablo_SE30_and_SE30_Jota">Diablo SE30 and SE30 Jota</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamborghini_Diablo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diablo SE30 and SE30 Jota">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DiabloSE_30.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1130" data-file-width="1600" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/DiabloSE_30.jpg/220px-DiabloSE_30.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/DiabloSE_30.jpg/330px-DiabloSE_30.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/DiabloSE_30.jpg/440px-DiabloSE_30.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DiabloSE_30.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Diablo SE30</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Diablo SE30 was introduced in 1994 as a limited-production special model to commemorate the company's 30th anniversary. The car was designed largely as a street-legal race vehicle that was lighter and more powerful than the standard Diablo. The engine received a healthy boost to 530 PS (390 kW; 523 hp) by means of a tuned fuel system, freer-flowing exhaust, and magnesium intake manifolds. The car remained rear-wheel drive to save weight, and omitted the electrically adjustable shock absorbers of the VT model, but it was equipped with adjustable-stiffness <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sway_bar" title="Sway bar">anti-roll bars</a> which could be controlled from the interior, on the fly.</p>
<p>The car's weight was lowered by replacing the power glass side windows with fixed plexiglas (with a small sliding vent window as on many race cars) and removing luxury features such as the air conditioning, stereo, and power steering. Carbon fiber seats with 4-point race harnesses and a fire suppression system added to the race nature of the vehicle.</p>
<p>On the outside, the SE30 differed from other Diablo models with a revised front fascia featuring straked brake cooling ducts and a deeper spoiler, while the rear cooling ducts were changed to a vertical body-colored design. The raging bull emblem was moved from the front of the luggage lid to the nose panel of the car between the front indicators. The engine lid had slats covering the narrow rear window, while a larger spoiler was installed as standard equipment. The single rear fog lamp and rear backup lamp, which had been on either side of the rear grille, were moved into the bumper; this change would be applied to all Diablo models across the lineup. Completing the exterior variations were special magnesium alloy wheels, SE30 badging, and a new metallic purple paint color (this could be changed upon request).</p>
<p>Only 150 SE30 models were built, and of these, about 15 were converted to "Jota" specification (although 28 Jota kits were produced). The "Jota" was a factory modification kit designed to convert the race-oriented SE30 into an actual circuit racer, albeit at the cost of street-legal operation. A revised engine lid with two ducts protruding above the roofline forced air into the intake system; a similar lid design would later be used on the Diablo SV model. With even more tuning of the Diablo's venerable V12 engine, the Jota kit produced nearly 603 PS (444 kW; 595 hp) and 639 N·m (471 lb·ft) of torque. An open exhaust system produced deafening engine roar, one of the main contributing factors to the Jota's track-only status, although some owners converted back to standard exhaust in order to enjoy their "super Diablo" on the road. The rear-view mirror from the interior was also removed because it was completely useless in conjunction with the revised engine lid, further adding to the race feeling of the car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diablo_SV">Diablo SV</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamborghini_Diablo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diablo SV">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lamborghini_Diablo_SV_coupe.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="990" data-file-width="1684" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Lamborghini_Diablo_SV_coupe.jpg/220px-Lamborghini_Diablo_SV_coupe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Lamborghini_Diablo_SV_coupe.jpg/330px-Lamborghini_Diablo_SV_coupe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Lamborghini_Diablo_SV_coupe.jpg/440px-Lamborghini_Diablo_SV_coupe.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lamborghini_Diablo_SV_coupe.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Lamborghini Diablo SV</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Diablo SV was introduced in 1995 at the Geneva Auto Show, reviving the <i>super veloce</i> title first used on the <a href="/wiki/Lamborghini_Miura#P400SV" title="Lamborghini Miura">Miura SV</a>. The SV was based on the standard Diablo and thus lacked the four-wheel drive of the VT. A notable feature of the SV was an increase in horsepower to 517 PS (380 kW; 510 hp), which, paired with the two-wheel drive layout, could increase the likelihood of loss of traction during hard driving. Interestingly, despite its higher power output, the SV was priced as the entry-level model in the Diablo range, falling below the standard Diablo by a small margin. An adjustable rear spoiler was installed as standard equipment and could be color-matched to the car body or formed from carbon fiber. Other exterior changes included black tail lamp surrounds, repositioned rear fog and reverse lamps as on the SE30, dual front foglamps (rather than the quad style found on all previous models), an extra set of front brake cooling ducts, a ducted engine lid similar to that installed on the Diablo SE30 Jota, and optional "SV" decals for the sides of the car. The SV also featured larger diameter front brakes (340 mm (13.4 in)) and a corresponding increase in front wheel size to 18 inches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1998, a limited 20-car run of Diablo SV's was produced exclusively for the United States market and called the <i>Monterey Edition</i>. The most notable feature of this edition was the use of the SE30/VT Roadster style of air intakes in front of the rear wheels, unlike the traditional (and persisting) SV style. Several of the cars were painted in unusual, vibrant colors. One Monterey Edition, featuring an upgraded engine and brakes, was driven by <a href="/wiki/Mario_Andretti" title="Mario Andretti">Mario Andretti</a> during the Lamborghini-sponsored "Running of the Bulls" event in California. The Monterey Edition was foreseen to be a collectible, but due to the popularity of the fixed-lamp models to follow (see below), its value did not rise significantly over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Diablo VT Roadster was introduced in December 1995 and featured an electrically operated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber">carbon fiber</a> <a href="/wiki/Targa_top" title="Targa top">targa top</a> which was stored above the engine lid when not in use. Besides the roof, the roadster's body was altered from the fixed-top VT model in a number of ways. The front bumper was revised, replacing the quad rectangular driving lamps with two rectangular and two round units. The brake cooling ducts were moved inboard of the driving lamps and changed to a straked design, while the rear ducts featured the vertical painted design seen on the SE30.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DIABLO_VT_ROADSTER_1999.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="1600" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/DIABLO_VT_ROADSTER_1999.jpg/220px-DIABLO_VT_ROADSTER_1999.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/DIABLO_VT_ROADSTER_1999.jpg/330px-DIABLO_VT_ROADSTER_1999.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/DIABLO_VT_ROADSTER_1999.jpg/440px-DIABLO_VT_ROADSTER_1999.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

1999 Lamborghini Diablo VT Roadster</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The engine lid was changed substantially in order to vent properly when the roof panel was covering it. The roadster also featured revised 17 inch wheels. The air intakes on top/sides were made larger than the coupe Diablos. In <b>1998</b> the wheels have been updated to 18 inches, and the engine power raised to 530 HP by adding the variable valve timing system. Top speed specification was raised to 335 km/h (208 mph).</p>
<p>In <b>1999</b> the dashboard received a major optical update by Audi, and the pop-up headlights were replaced by fixed headlights, same as for the coupés. This resulted in a better aerodynamic shape and modern optics. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facelift_.281998-2001.29">Facelift (1998-2001)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamborghini_Diablo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Facelift (1998-2001)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>\"New generation"/</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diablo_SV_.281999.29">Diablo SV (1999)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamborghini_Diablo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Diablo SV (1999)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lamborghini launched a facelifted Diablo in 1999, simplifying the model range by eliminating the "base" Diablo (since the SV model had become the new entry-level trim anyway) and applying universal revisions across the lineup. The most immediately noticeable exterior change was the replacement of the previous Diablo's pop-up headlamp units with fixed composite lenses, borrowed under license from their original application in the <a href="/wiki/Nissan_300ZX" title="Nissan 300ZX">Nissan 300ZX Z32</a>. All Diablos were also fitted with new 18 inch wheels.</p>
<p>The Diablo range also received an updated interior. Instead of the traditional flat dashboard with a separate upright instrument binnacle, as in many Italian sports cars of the era (and the previous Diablo), the new dash was an integrated wave-shaped design. A thin strip of black glass ran the length of the dash and contained various instrument indicator and warning lamps. This aesthetic design was inspired by <a href="/wiki/Bang_%26_Olufsen" title="Bang &amp; Olufsen">Bang &amp; Olufsen</a> Hi-Fi products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under the engine lid, the tried-and-true V12 was bumped to 536 PS (394 kW; 529 hp) and 605 N·m (446 lb·ft) of torque for both the SV and VT models and now featured <a href="/wiki/Variable_valve_timing" title="Variable valve timing">variable valve timing</a>. For the first time in a Lamborghini, the Diablo was equipped with a Kelsey-Hayes <a href="/wiki/Anti-lock_braking_system" title="Anti-lock braking system">ABS</a> unit, complementing larger diameter brake rotors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diablo_SV_SE35">Diablo SV SE35</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamborghini_Diablo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Diablo SV SE35">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Diablo SV SE35 introduced in 1999 as a limited-production special model, build by Swiss Lamborghini importer Roland Affolter, to commemorate the company's 35th anniversary. Produced in very limited numbers of just 9 cars. It is recognisable by the bull (from the logo) on the sides of the car.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diablo_VT_and_VT_Roadster_.281999.29">Diablo VT and VT Roadster (1999)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamborghini_Diablo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Diablo VT and VT Roadster (1999)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lamborghini_Diablo_VT_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Lamborghini_Diablo_VT_2.jpg/250px-Lamborghini_Diablo_VT_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Lamborghini_Diablo_VT_2.jpg/375px-Lamborghini_Diablo_VT_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Lamborghini_Diablo_VT_2.jpg/500px-Lamborghini_Diablo_VT_2.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lamborghini_Diablo_VT_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1999 Diablo VT Roadster, US-spec (note front fascia and rear brake ducts)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The second generation VT coupé and roadster received the same cosmetic and mechanical upgrades as the SV model, including the open headlamps, restyled interior, 536 PS (394 kW; 529 hp) engine, and ABS; little else was changed from the previous generation. All US-spec VT models, coupé and roadster alike, shared the same unique front and rear fascias as seen on the original VT Roadster, along with the vertical painted rear brake ducts that had debuted on the SE30 model; these cosmetic variations were available as options on rest-of-world VT coupés.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>A special run of twelve Diablo VT's was produced exclusively for the United States market in 1999 and called the <i>Alpine Edition</i>. As the Diablo had been utilizing <a href="/wiki/Alpine_Electronics" title="Alpine Electronics">Alpine</a> stereo equipment since its inception, this very limited production was intended to showcase and celebrate the Lamborghini/Alpine connection. The Alpine Edition was a fairly standard Diablo VT with no engine modifications and some extra bits of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber">carbon fiber</a> trim in various locations, but the big news was the multimedia system. The stereo receiver was the top-end CVA-1005 model, with integrated navigation system; also included in the package was a DVD player, 6-disc CD changer, and Alpine's top quality tweeters, midrange drivers, and subwoofers, powered by "Lamborghini" badged Alpine amplifiers. Alpine logos adorned the seat headrests, floormats, and the special car cover included with this rare model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another special twelve-car run of Diablos for the US market consisted of VT Roadsters and was called the <i>Momo Edition</i>. Like the <i>Alpine Edition</i>, the Momo Edition catered to the US car buyer's interest in aftermarket upgrade products. Lamborghini, rather than spending money to develop certain automotive components, had been using aftermarket suppliers such as Alpine and <a href="/wiki/Momo_(company)" title="Momo (company)">MOMO</a> to outfit the Diablo. The Momo Edition was again a fairly standard VT Roadster, but featured special upholstery, MOMO 4-point seatbelt harnesses, and MOMO chromed wheels. Like the Alpine Edition, the Momo Edition also had MOMO logos embroidered in the seat headrests and floor mats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The VT Roadster enjoyed one final limited run of 30 cars for the 2000 model year, after the introduction of the Diablo VT 6.0 (see below). This "Millennium Roadster" model was available in just two colors, Titanium Metallic and yellow, with the 10 cars exported to the United States all finished in Titanium Metallic. Besides an optional carbon fiber spoiler, special two-tone leather interior, and the shorter-ratio SV rear differential (providing enhanced acceleration), this model featured no significant changes from the previous design, and merely served as a final tribute to the outgoing roadster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diablo_GT">Diablo GT</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamborghini_Diablo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Diablo GT">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:330px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lamborghini_Diablo_GT_g%27wich_front.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1560" data-file-width="3255" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Lamborghini_Diablo_GT_g%27wich_front.JPG/328px-Lamborghini_Diablo_GT_g%27wich_front.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Lamborghini_Diablo_GT_g%27wich_front.JPG/492px-Lamborghini_Diablo_GT_g%27wich_front.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Lamborghini_Diablo_GT_g%27wich_front.JPG/656px-Lamborghini_Diablo_GT_g%27wich_front.JPG 2x" width="328"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lamborghini_Diablo_GT_g%27wich_front.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Lamborghini Diablo GT</div>
</div>

<p>The Diablo SE30 and its optional Jota upgrade kit had been quite sporty and race-oriented, but Lamborghini took this concept a step further in 1999 with its introduction of the very limited production Diablo GT, of which only 80 examples were produced for sale. The Diablo GT was a completely race-oriented model differing in nearly every aspect from the more mainstream Diablos. The cars were fitted with radically altered aggressive bodywork, a stripped-down interior, and an enlarged engine. With the exclusivity came a large price tag of nearly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Dollar" title="United States Dollar">$</a>300,000 and availability limited to Europe. Some GT models were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grey_import_vehicles#United_States" title="Grey import vehicles">imported into the US</a> and a few may have been converted to road-legal US specification.</p>
<p>The Diablo GT was noticeably different on the exterior. While previous Diablo models had differed one from another with subtle fascia refinements or changes in the brake cooling ducts, the Diablo GT opted for an all new black carbon fiber front air dam with large brake ducts and a central vent for the oil cooler (the car still featured driving lamps, the single pair of round units featured on the Diablo VT Roadster). In the front luggage compartment lid, a large air extractor was added, while the small corner vents on the front fenders were changed to NACA style ducts. The fenders themselves were widened to accommodate a wider front track. In the rear, the bumper and its lamps were removed entirely, replaced by a carbon fiber diffuser that forced the fog and backup lamps into the outer pair of tail lamps, and shielded a pair of large center-mounted exhaust pipes. The engine lid featured a large central ram air duct protruding above the roof; a rear spoiler was standard equipment. This radical new body was composed mostly of carbon fiber, with the steel roof and aluminum doors being the only components to retain their standard material. Special 3-piece OZ wheels finished the GT's exterior package.</p>
<p>On the inside, the Diablo GT featured more prominent carbon fiber panels, race-spec bucket seats with 4-point seatbelt harnesses, a smaller steering wheel, and an optional <a href="/wiki/Alpine_Electronics" title="Alpine Electronics">Alpine</a> LCD screen for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">GPS</a> navigation and a bumper mounted <a href="/wiki/Backup_camera" title="Backup camera">backup camera</a>. Despite the racing pretenses of the vehicle, air conditioning was still installed as standard equipment; airbags could be optionally omitted.</p>
<p>While previous Diablos had tuned and tweaked the 5.7 L (348 cu in) engine with various ignition and fuel system upgrades, the Diablo GT opted for a larger-displacement alternative. While the basic V12 block remained the same, the engine was stroked from 80 mm (3.1 in) to 84 mm (3.3 in) for a new displacement of 6.0 L (366 cu in); this engine, which would later be used in the revised Diablo VT 6.0, produced (in GT trim) 583 PS (429 kW; 575 hp) and 630 N·m (465 lb·ft) of torque. The transmission was the same 5-speed used in other Diablos, but different gear ratios could be specified by the race-oriented buyer. Rear-wheel drive was used to save weight, as usual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diablo_VT_6.0_and_VT_6.0_SE">Diablo VT 6.0 and VT 6.0 SE</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamborghini_Diablo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Diablo VT 6.0 and VT 6.0 SE">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lamborghini_Diablo.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Lamborghini_Diablo.JPG/250px-Lamborghini_Diablo.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Lamborghini_Diablo.JPG/375px-Lamborghini_Diablo.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Lamborghini_Diablo.JPG/500px-Lamborghini_Diablo.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lamborghini_Diablo.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Lamborghini Diablo VT 6.0</div>

<p>After <a href="/wiki/Audi" title="Audi">Audi</a> AG took over Lamborghini from its former Southeast Asian owners, MyCom and V'Power, in 1998, they set out to modernize and refine the Diablo, while its replacement, the <a href="/wiki/Lamborghini_Murci%C3%A9lago" title="Lamborghini Murciélago">Murciélago</a>, was developed. Audi tasked <a href="/wiki/Luc_Donckerwolke" title="Luc Donckerwolke">Luc Donckerwolke</a> with designing a more refined, civilized, modern Diablo. The VT 6.0, released for sale for the 2000 model year, was the result of that design and featured significant styling changes both inside and out.</p>
<p>Externally, the Diablo VT 6.0 differed from its predecessors with a revised front fascia that featured two large air intakes (similar to those later used on the Murciélago). The air dam, nose panel, and fenders were all reworked and smoothed, the indicators were enlarged and shifted in position, and the small air inlets in the tops of the fenders were deleted. The rear of the car remained familiar, but the taillight surrounds were now body-colored (rather than transparent red or black) and the lamps themselves used the configuration seen on the limited Diablo GT. Unlike previous Diablos, which had almost all used 3-piece alloy wheels, the VT 6.0 rested on monobloc cast aluminum 18 inch OZ rims, which were styled with a 5-hole "phone dial" design similar to that seen on the later models of the <a href="/wiki/Lamborghini_Countach" title="Lamborghini Countach">Countach</a>. On the inside, the interior was further refined in typical German fashion; the new-styled dash introduced on the 1999 Diablo range was retained, but a prominent carbon fiber center console was fitted, the air conditioning was improved, and the seat and pedal alignment was revised.</p>
<p>The VT 6.0, as per its name, also featured the new 6.0 L (370 cu in) V12 introduced in the Diablo GT (a stroker version of the traditional Diablo 5.7 liter V12). The motor had updated <a href="/wiki/Electronic_control_unit" title="Electronic control unit">ECU</a> software in addition to new intake and <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_system" title="Exhaust system">exhaust</a> systems and a refined <a href="/wiki/Variable_valve_timing" title="Variable valve timing">variable valve timing</a> system with slightly less aggressive <a href="/wiki/Camshaft" title="Camshaft">camshafts</a> than had been used in the earlier versions. This powerplant produced 583 PS (429 kW; 575 hp) and 620 N·m (457 lb·ft) of torque, more than any prior standard Diablo.</p>
<p>Because of the preparations being made for the upcoming <a href="/wiki/Lamborghini_Murci%C3%A9lago" title="Lamborghini Murciélago">Murciélago</a>, the Diablo VT coupé was the only available variant, with no more roadster or SV models planned; however, customers could specially order a rear-wheel drive version of the VT 6.0 if they so desired. Due to the influx of financial resources and engineering expertise from Audi, the Diablo 6.0 VT had superior build quality to the prior model years, making the 6.0 VT the most practical of all the Diablos. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before the Diablo was retired, Lamborghini produced a limited 2001 model year 42-car production run of a special edition Diablo VT 6.0 SE, this powerplant produced 557 PS (410 kW; 549 hp). This model was only available in two colors; the gold metallic "Oro Elios" represented sunrise, while the color-shifting bronze/maroon "Marrone Eklipsis" represented sunset. Little else changed, save for a new magnesium intake manifold, special upholstery treatment, "Lamborghini" badged brake calipers, comprehensive road map software in the navigation system, and enhanced carbon fiber trim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Factory_racing_specials">Factory racing specials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamborghini_Diablo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Factory racing specials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diablo_SV-R">Diablo SV-R</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamborghini_Diablo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Diablo SV-R">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unveiled at the 1996 Geneva Salon, the Diablo SV-R is a lightweight competition version of the SV and the first Lamborghini to be officially built for motorsport purposes, as <a href="/wiki/Ferruccio_Lamborghini" title="Ferruccio Lamborghini">Ferruccio Lamborghini</a> had never desired to build "street legal race cars" like rival <a href="/wiki/Ferrari" title="Ferrari">Ferrari</a>. Rather than comply with the requirements for any established racing series, Lamborghini created its own <i>Lamborghini Supertrophy</i> which ran for four years (replaced later with the <i>GTR Supertrophy</i> for the <a href="#Diablo_GTR">Diablo GTR</a>), with its inaugural round held as the support race to the <a href="/wiki/1996_24_Hours_of_Le_Mans" title="1996 24 Hours of Le Mans">1996 24 Hours of Le Mans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diabloreg_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-diabloreg-37">[37]</a></sup> The 28 Diablo SV-R's entered, which were built in 4 months on the Diablo assembly line along with production SV's, all finished this first event without significant problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SVR_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-SVR-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Diablo SV-R featured a stripped-down interior with a rollcage, racing seat, and removable steering wheel; the power glass side windows were replaced with fixed Plexiglass with traditional race-style sliding sections. On the exterior, the electric pop-up headlamps were replaced either with fixed units (similar to those which appeared later on the road cars in 1999) or with open ducting for the front brakes. A larger, deeper front spoiler was fitted, while the rear bumper was replaced with a diffuser assembly and the traditional Diablo "wing" was replaced with a true adjustable carbon fiber spoiler. Side skirts were added for aerodynamics, but this left so little ground clearance that pneumatic air jacks also had to be installed to raise the car for service in the pit lane; similar jacks can be seen in use on the more recent <a href="/wiki/Ferrari_F430_Challenge" title="Ferrari F430 Challenge">Ferrari F430 Challenge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Lightweight, hollow center-lock OZ wheels were used, although these were later switched to stronger Speedline units. Linear-rate springs were used with Koni shock absorbers and were adjusted to about twice the stiffness of stock Diablo SV suspension.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SVR_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-SVR-38">[38]</a></sup> With all modifications, the SV-R weighed 1,385 kg (3,053 lb), 191 kg (421 lb) less than the factory SV.</p>
<p>Under the engine lid, the traditional 5.7 liter V12 remained, but was boosted to 540 PS (397 kW; 533 hp) and 598 N·m (441 lb·ft) by means of a revised fuel system and <a href="/wiki/Variable_valve_timing" title="Variable valve timing">variable valve timing</a>, which would later appear on production Diablos. The engine was bolted up to a 6-speed manual transmission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SVR_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-SVR-38">[38]</a></sup> Each car sold came with a season's factory support and an entry to the one-make series. All repairs and maintenance were carried out by Lamborghini themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The series' first title winner was BPR regular, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bscher" title="Thomas Bscher">Thomas Bscher</a>, who became involved with the business side of the brand in later years. In total, 31 examples of the SV-R were produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diabloreg_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-diabloreg-37">[37]</a></sup> Only a few of these have been modified for road use, including one in the United States which received a Diablo VT 6.0 front clip and was painted with the Stars and Stripes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SVR_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-SVR-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diablo_GTR">Diablo GTR</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamborghini_Diablo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Diablo GTR">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After campaigning the Diablo SV-R for four years in the Diablo Supertrophy, Lamborghini launched a completely new car for the 2000 season. Just as the SV-R was a race-ready SV, the Diablo GTR, introduced at the 1999 <a href="/wiki/Bologna_Motor_Show" title="Bologna Motor Show">Bologna Motor Show</a>, converted the already impressive <a href="#Diablo_GT">Diablo GT</a> into a track machine with power improvements, a stripped interior, and weight reduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GTR_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-GTR-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>The GTR interior was stripped down to save weight; the air conditioning, stereo, and sound and heatproofing were removed, and a single racing seat with 6-point seatbelt harness, <a href="/wiki/Momo_(company)" title="Momo (company)">MOMO</a> fire suppression system and steering wheel, complete integrated roll cage, fixed Plexiglass windows with sliding sections, and fresh air intake were fitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GTR_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-GTR-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>The GT had already featured a radically styled body, but the GTR took this a little further with features such as a very large rear spoiler bolted directly to the chassis like a true race car, 18 inch hollow magnesium Speedline centerlock wheels, pneumatic air jacks for raising the car in the pit lane (like the SV-R, it was too low for a rolling jack), and an emergency fuel shutoff switch on the left front fender.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GTR_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-GTR-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>The GTR utilized the same basic 6.0 liter V12 engine that had made its debut on the street-legal GT, but with revised fuel and ignition systems, individual throttle bodies, a dynamic air intake duct system, variable valve timing, <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> connecting rods, and a lightened crankshaft. These improvements allowed the engine to produce 598 PS (440 kW; 590 hp) and 640 N·m (472 lb·ft) of torque. The engine was bolted to the usual 5-speed transmission in a rear-wheel drive layout. Extra heat exhangers were added for the differential and transmission oil to prevent overheating under extreme racing conditions. A fast-filling racing fuel cell replaced the standard gasoline tank. The suspension was stiffened and lowered, and racing brake calipers were installed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GTR_41-3"><a href="#cite_note-GTR-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thirty cars were planned, 40 were built, and 40 chassis were prepared to replace cars wrecked in racing accidents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GTR_41-4"><a href="#cite_note-GTR-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the hands of multiple <a href="/wiki/Australian_Drivers%27_Championship" title="Australian Drivers' Championship">Australian Drivers' Champion</a> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Stokell" title="Paul Stokell">Paul Stokell</a>, a Diablo GTR run by Team Lamborghini Australia won the <a href="/wiki/2003_Australian_Nations_Cup_Championship" title="2003 Australian Nations Cup Championship">2003</a> and <a href="/wiki/2004_Australian_Nations_Cup_Championship" title="2004 Australian Nations Cup Championship">2004</a> <a href="/wiki/Australian_Nations_Cup_Championship" title="Australian Nations Cup Championship">Australian Nations Cup Championships</a>. The GTR was also raced by Stokell, <a href="/wiki/Luke_Youlden" title="Luke Youlden">Luke Youlden</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Hackett_(racing_driver)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peter Hackett (racing driver) (page does not exist)">Peter Hackett</a> and Danish driver <a href="/wiki/Allan_Simonsen_(racing_driver)" title="Allan Simonsen (racing driver)">Allan Simonsen</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2003_Bathurst_24_Hour" title="2003 Bathurst 24 Hour">2003 Bathurst 24 Hour</a> race where after qualifying 6th would go on to finish 8th outright after suffering a number of punctures throughout the race.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS1_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-NS1-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outside_tuning">Outside tuning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamborghini_Diablo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Outside tuning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diablo_VTTT">Diablo VTTT</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamborghini_Diablo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Diablo VTTT">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Lamborghini Diablo VTTT (<i>viscous traction twin turbo</i>) was an extremely limited production (6 made in 1995, 2 made in 1998, although some say 7 overall) modification of the standard Diablo VT, offered as a special dealer upgrade by Platinum Motors, the Lamborghini dealership of southern <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. The cars were equipped with twin blueprinted, water-cooled, <a href="/wiki/Garrett_AiResearch" title="Garrett AiResearch">Garrett</a> T4 <a href="/wiki/Twin-turbo" title="Twin-turbo">turbochargers</a> with electronically controlled <a href="/wiki/Wastegate" title="Wastegate">wastegates</a>, custom-built <a href="/wiki/Intercooler" title="Intercooler">intercoolers</a>, competition-type <a href="/wiki/Poppet_valve" title="Poppet valve">valves</a> with race-type guides, <a href="/wiki/Cylinder_head" title="Cylinder head">cylinder heads</a> with <a href="/wiki/Cylinder_head_porting" title="Cylinder head porting">polished ports</a>, and a reprogrammed <a href="/wiki/Fuel_injection#Electronic" title="Fuel injection">electronic fuel injection</a> system. Modifications to the drivetrain included a custom <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">Kevlar</a> <a href="/wiki/Clutch#Multiple_plate_clutch" title="Clutch">twin-plate clutch</a> to cope with the extra torque and a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gear_ratio#Wide-ratio_vs._close-ratio_transmission" title="Gear ratio">short ratio</a> <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(mechanics)" title="Transmission (mechanics)">gearbox</a> to improve acceleration. The brakes were upgraded with cross-drilled, ventilated discs and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber">carbon fiber</a> <a href="/wiki/Brake_pad" title="Brake pad">brake pads</a>. The VTTT featured a dash-mounted switch with three different engine settings including a very limited <a href="/wiki/Valet_parking" title="Valet parking">valet</a> mode and two levels of turbocharger boost (6 psi (0.41 bar) and 9 psi (0.62 bar)).</p>
<p>The extensive modifications to the VT commanded a high premium, nearly doubling the car's sticker price to <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>500,000.</p>
<p>With approximately 760 PS (559 kW; 750 hp) on tap at full turbo boost, the VTTT was able to achieve a top speed of about 222 mph (357 km/h) with the car set at 650 hp, or 255 mph (410 km/h) with all 750 hp being used, although no official number has been produced due to the car's rarity and the fact that it was not a production model.</p>
