<p><b>Usimare Ramesses III</b> (also written <b>Ramses</b> and <b>Rameses</b>) was the second Pharaoh of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twentieth_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Twentieth dynasty of Egypt">Twentieth Dynasty</a> and is considered to be the last <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Kingdom" title="New Kingdom">New Kingdom</a> king to wield any substantial authority over Egypt. His long reign saw the decline of Egyptian political and economic power, linked to a series of invasions and internal economic problems.</p>
<p>Ramesses III was the son of <a href="/wiki/Setnakhte" title="Setnakhte">Setnakhte</a> and Queen <a href="/wiki/Tiy-Merenese" title="Tiy-Merenese">Tiy-Merenese</a>. He was probably murdered by an assassin in <a href="/wiki/Harem_conspiracy" title="Harem conspiracy">a conspiracy</a> led by one of his secondary wives, <a href="/wiki/Tiye_(20th_dynasty)" title="Tiye (20th dynasty)">Tiye</a>, and her son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentaweret" title="Pentaweret">Pentaweret</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ramesses_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ramesses' two main names transliterate as wsr-mꜢʿt-rʿ–mry-ỉmn rʿ-ms-s–ḥḳꜢ-ỉwnw. They are normally realised as <b>Usermaatre-meryamun Ramesse-hekaiunu</b>, meaning "The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ma%27at" title="Ma'at">Ma'at</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ra" title="Ra">Ra</a> is strong, Beloved of <a href="/wiki/Amun" title="Amun">Amun</a>, Born of <a href="/wiki/Ra" title="Ra">Ra</a>, Ruler of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopolis_(Ancient_Egypt)" title="Heliopolis (Ancient Egypt)">Heliopolis</a>".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ascension">Ascension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ramesses_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ascension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ramesses III is believed to have reigned from March 1186 to April 1155 BC. This is based on his known accession date of I Shemu day 26 and his death on Year 32 III Shemu day 15, for a reign of 31 years, 1 month and 19 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Alternate dates for his reign are 1187 to 1156 BC.</p>
<p>In a description of his <a href="/wiki/Coronation_of_the_pharaoh" title="Coronation of the pharaoh">coronation</a> from Medinet Habu, four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dove" title="Dove">doves</a> were said to be "dispatched to the four corners of the horizon to confirm that the living <a href="/wiki/Horus" title="Horus">Horus</a>, Ramses III, is (still) in possession of his throne, that the order of <a href="/wiki/Maat" title="Maat">Maat</a> prevails in the cosmos and society".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LambertGeorge2000_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-LambertGeorge2000-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tenure_of_constant_war">Tenure of constant war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ramesses_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tenure of constant war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Delta" title="Battle of the Delta">Battle of the Delta</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_collapse" title="Bronze Age collapse">Bronze Age collapse</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Weihrauchopfer_RamsesIII_aus_KV11.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="608" data-file-width="650" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Weihrauchopfer_RamsesIII_aus_KV11.jpg/200px-Weihrauchopfer_RamsesIII_aus_KV11.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Weihrauchopfer_RamsesIII_aus_KV11.jpg/300px-Weihrauchopfer_RamsesIII_aus_KV11.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Weihrauchopfer_RamsesIII_aus_KV11.jpg/400px-Weihrauchopfer_RamsesIII_aus_KV11.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Weihrauchopfer_RamsesIII_aus_KV11.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ramses III offering incense, wall painting in KV11.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During his long tenure in the midst of the surrounding political chaos of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Dark_Ages" title="Greek Dark Ages">Greek Dark Ages</a>, Egypt was beset by foreign invaders (including the so-called <a href="/wiki/Sea_Peoples" title="Sea Peoples">Sea Peoples</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Libya" title="Ancient Libya">Libyans</a>) and experienced the beginnings of increasing economic difficulties and internal strife which would eventually lead to the collapse of the Twentieth Dynasty. In Year 8 of his reign, the Sea Peoples, including <a href="/wiki/Philistines" title="Philistines">Peleset</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denyen" title="Denyen">Denyen</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shardana" title="Shardana">Shardana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meshwesh" title="Meshwesh">Meshwesh</a> of the sea, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tjekker" title="Tjekker">Tjekker</a>, invaded Egypt by land and sea. Ramesses III defeated them in two great land and sea battles. Although the Egyptians had a reputation as poor seamen, they fought tenaciously. Rameses lined the shores with ranks of archers who kept up a continuous volley of arrows into the enemy ships when they attempted to land on the banks of the Nile. Then, the Egyptian navy attacked using grappling hooks to haul in the enemy ships. In the brutal hand-to-hand fighting which ensued, the Sea People were utterly defeated. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papyrus_Harris" title="Papyrus Harris">Harris Papyrus</a> states:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>As for those who reached my frontier, their seed is not, their heart and their soul are finished forever and ever. As for those who came forward together on the seas, the full flame was in front of them at the Nile mouths, while a stockade of lances surrounded them on the shore, prostrated on the beach, slain, and made into heaps from head to tail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Ramesses III claims that he incorporated the Sea Peoples as subject peoples and settled them in Southern Canaan, although there is no clear evidence to this effect; the pharaoh, unable to prevent their gradual arrival in Canaan, may have claimed that it was his idea to let them reside in this territory. Their presence in Canaan may have contributed to the formation of new states in this region such as Philistia after the collapse of the Egyptian Empire in Asia. Ramesses III was also compelled to fight invading Libyan tribesmen in two major campaigns in Egypt's Western Delta in his Year 5 and Year 11 respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_turmoil">Economic turmoil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ramesses_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economic turmoil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The heavy cost of these battles slowly exhausted Egypt's treasury and contributed to the gradual decline of the Egyptian Empire in Asia. The severity of these difficulties is stressed by the fact that the first known labor strike in recorded history occurred during Year 29 of Ramesses III's reign, when the food rations for the favoured and elite royal tomb-builders and artisans in the village of <b>Set Maat her imenty Waset</b> (now known as <a href="/wiki/Deir_el-Medina" title="Deir el-Medina">Deir el-Medina</a>), could not be provisioned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Something in the air (possibly the <a href="/wiki/Hekla_3_eruption" title="Hekla 3 eruption">Hekla 3 eruption</a>) prevented much sunlight from reaching the ground and also arrested global tree growth for almost two full decades until 1140 BC. The result in Egypt was a substantial increase in grain prices under the later reigns of Ramesses VI–VII, whereas the prices for fowl and slaves remained constant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Thus the cooldown affected Ramesses III's final years and impaired his ability to provide a constant supply of grain rations to the workmen of the Deir el-Medina community.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RamesesIII.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="282" data-file-width="289" height="215" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/RamesesIII.JPG/220px-RamesesIII.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8e/RamesesIII.JPG 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RamesesIII.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Osirid statues of Ramses III at his temple in Karnak (in the first courtyard of the Great Temple of Amun).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>These difficult realities are completely ignored in Ramesses' official monuments, many of which seek to emulate those of his famous predecessor, <a href="/wiki/Ramesses_II" title="Ramesses II">Ramesses II</a>, and which present an image of continuity and stability. He built important additions to the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_temple" title="Egyptian temple">temples</a> at <a href="/wiki/Luxor_Temple" title="Luxor Temple">Luxor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karnak" title="Karnak">Karnak</a>, and his funerary temple and administrative complex at <a href="/wiki/Medinet_Habu_(temple)" title="Medinet Habu (temple)">Medinet-Habu</a> is amongst the largest and best-preserved in Egypt; however, the uncertainty of Ramesses' times is apparent from the massive fortifications which were built to enclose the latter. No temple in the heart of Egypt prior to Ramesses' reign had ever needed to be protected in such a manner.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conspiracy_and_death">Conspiracy and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ramesses_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conspiracy and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Harem_conspiracy" title="Harem conspiracy">Harem conspiracy</a></div>
<p>Thanks to the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a> <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Papyrus_of_Turin" title="Judicial Papyrus of Turin">trial transcripts</a> (dated to Ramesses III), it is now known that there was a plot against his life as a result of a royal <a href="/wiki/Harem_conspiracy" title="Harem conspiracy">harem conspiracy</a> during a celebration at <a href="/wiki/Medinet_Habu_(location)" title="Medinet Habu (location)">Medinet Habu</a>. The conspiracy was instigated by <a href="/wiki/Tiye_(20th_dynasty)" title="Tiye (20th dynasty)">Tiye</a>, one of his three known wives (the others being <a href="/wiki/Tyti" title="Tyti">Tyti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iset_Ta-Hemdjert" title="Iset Ta-Hemdjert">Iset Ta-Hemdjert</a>), over whose son would inherit the throne. Tyti's son, Ramesses Amonhirkhopshef (the future <a href="/wiki/Ramesses_IV" title="Ramesses IV">Ramesses IV</a>), was the eldest and the successor chosen by Ramesses III in preference to Tiye's son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentaweret" title="Pentaweret">Pentaweret</a>.</p>
<p>The trial documents<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> show that many individuals were implicated in the plot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Chief among them were Queen <a href="/wiki/Tiye_(20th_dynasty)" title="Tiye (20th dynasty)">Tiye</a> and her son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentaweret" title="Pentaweret">Pentaweret</a>, Ramesses' chief of the chamber, <a href="/wiki/Pebekkamen" title="Pebekkamen">Pebekkamen</a>, seven royal butlers (a respectable state office), two Treasury overseers, two Army standard bearers, two royal scribes and a herald. There is little doubt that all of the main conspirators were executed: some of the condemned were given the option of committing suicide (possibly by poison) rather than being put to death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> According to the surviving trial transcripts, a total of three separate trials were started, while 38 people were sentenced to death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The tombs of Tiye and her son Pentaweret were robbed and their names erased to prevent them from enjoying an afterlife. The <a href="/wiki/Egyptians" title="Egyptians">Egyptians</a> did such a thorough job of this that the only references to them are the trial documents and what remains of their tombs.</p>
<p>Some of the accused <a href="/wiki/Harem" title="Harem">harem</a> women tried to seduce the members of the judiciary who tried them but were caught in the act. Judges who were involved were severely punished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_Ancient_History_2000.2C_p._247_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_Ancient_History_2000.2C_p._247-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Louvre_egyptologie_22.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="448" data-file-width="512" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Louvre_egyptologie_22.jpg/250px-Louvre_egyptologie_22.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Louvre_egyptologie_22.jpg/375px-Louvre_egyptologie_22.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Louvre_egyptologie_22.jpg/500px-Louvre_egyptologie_22.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Louvre_egyptologie_22.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Red granite sarcophagus of Ramesses III (Louvre)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It is not certain whether the assassination plot succeeded since <a href="/wiki/Ramesses_IV" title="Ramesses IV">Ramesses IV</a>, the king's designated successor, assumed the throne upon his death rather than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentaweret" title="Pentaweret">Pentaweret</a>, who was intended to be the main beneficiary of the palace conspiracy. Moreover, Ramesses III died in his 32nd year before the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Papyrus_of_Turin" title="Judicial Papyrus of Turin">summaries of the sentences</a> were composed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> but the same year that the trial documents<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> record the trial and execution of the conspirators.</p>
<p>Although it was long believed that Ramesses III's body showed no obvious wounds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_Ancient_History_2000.2C_p._247_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_Ancient_History_2000.2C_p._247-12">[12]</a></sup> a recent examination of the mummy by a German forensic team, televised in the documentary <i>Ramesses: Mummy King Mystery</i> on the Science Channel in 2011, showed excessive bandages around the neck. A subsequent CT scan that was done in Egypt by Ashraf Selim and Sahar Saleem, professors of Radiology in Cairo University, revealed that beneath the bandages was a deep knife wound across the throat, deep enough to reach the vertebrae. According to the documentary narrator, "It was a wound no one could have survived."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-14">[14]</a></sup> The December 2012 issue of the British Medical Journal quotes the conclusion of the study of the team of researchers, led by <a href="/wiki/Zahi_Hawass" title="Zahi Hawass">Dr Zahi Hawass</a>, the former head of the Egyptian Supreme Council of Antiquity, and his Egyptian team, as well as Dr Albert Zink from the Institute for Mummies and the Iceman of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Academy_of_Bozen" title="European Academy of Bozen">European Academy of Bolzano/Bozen</a> in Italy, which stated that conspirators murdered pharaoh Ramesses III by cutting his throat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Zink observes in an interview that:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"The cut [to Ramesses III's throat] is...very deep and quite large, it really goes down almost down to the bone (spine) - it must have been a lethal injury."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>A subsequent study of the CT scan of the mummy of Ramesses III's body by Sahar Saleem revealed that the left big toe was likely chopped by a heavy sharp object like an ax. There were no signs of bone healing so this injury must have happened shortly before death. The embalmers placed a prosthesis-like object made of linen in place of the amputated toe. The embalmers placed 6 amulets around both feet and ankles for magical healing of the wound for the life after. This additional injury of the foot supports the assassination of the Pharaoh, likely by the hands of multiple assailants using different weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livescience_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Livescience-18">[18]</a></sup> Before this discovery it had been speculated that Ramesses III had been killed by means that would not have left a mark on the body. Among the conspirators were practitioners of magic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> who might well have used poison. Some had put forth a hypothesis that a snakebite from a <a href="/wiki/Viperinae" title="Viperinae">viper</a> was the cause of the king's death. His mummy includes an <a href="/wiki/Amulet" title="Amulet">amulet</a> to protect Ramesses III in the afterlife from snakes. The servant in charge of his food and drink were also among the listed conspirators, but there were also other conspirators who were called the snake and the lord of snakes.</p>
<p>The same study determined that the mummy of an unknown man buried with him was a good candidate for Ramesses's son Pentaweret although it could not determine his cause of death. Both mummies shared Y chromosomal haplogroup <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E1b1a" title="E1b1a">E1b1a</a> and 50% of their genetic material, which Zink stated "is typical of a father-son relationship."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In one respect the conspirators certainly failed. The crown passed to the king's designated successor: Ramesses IV. Ramesses III may have been doubtful as to the latter's chances of succeeding him, given that, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harris_Papyrus" title="Harris Papyrus">Great Harris Papyrus</a>, he implored Amun to ensure his son's rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ramesses_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Great Harris Papyrus or <a href="/wiki/Papyrus_Harris_I" title="Papyrus Harris I">Papyrus Harris I</a>, which was commissioned by his son and chosen successor <a href="/wiki/Ramesses_IV" title="Ramesses IV">Ramesses IV</a>, chronicles this king's vast donations of land, gold statues and monumental construction to Egypt's various temples at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piramesse" title="Piramesse">Piramesse</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopolis_(Ancient_Egypt)" title="Heliopolis (Ancient Egypt)">Heliopolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Egypt" title="Memphis, Egypt">Memphis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Athribis" title="Athribis">Athribis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hermopolis" title="Hermopolis">Hermopolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Girga" title="Girga">This</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abydos,_Egypt" title="Abydos, Egypt">Abydos</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coptos" title="Coptos">Coptos</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eileithyiaspolis" title="Eileithyiaspolis">El Kab</a> and other cities in Nubia and Syria. It also records that the king dispatched a trading expedition to the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Punt" title="Land of Punt">Land of Punt</a> and quarried the copper mines of Timna in southern Canaan. Papyrus Harris I records some of Ramesses III's activities:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"I sent my emissaries to the land of Atika, [i.e., Timna] to the great copper mines which are there. Their ships carried them along and others went overland on their donkeys. It had not been heard of since the [time of any earlier] king. Their mines were found and [they] yielded copper which was loaded by tens of thousands into their ships, they being sent in their care to Egypt, and arriving safely." (P. Harris I, 78, 1-4)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>Ramesses began the reconstruction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temple_of_Khons_at_Karnak" title="Temple of Khons at Karnak">Temple of Khonsu</a> at <a href="/wiki/Karnak" title="Karnak">Karnak</a> from the foundations of an earlier temple of <a href="/wiki/Amenhotep_III" title="Amenhotep III">Amenhotep III</a> and completed the Temple of <a href="/wiki/Medinet_Habu_(temple)" title="Medinet Habu (temple)">Medinet Habu</a> around his Year 12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> He decorated the walls of his Medinet Habu temple with scenes of his Naval and Land battles against the <a href="/wiki/Sea_Peoples" title="Sea Peoples">Sea Peoples</a>. This monument stands today as one of the best-preserved temples of the New Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mummy" title="Mummy">mummy</a> of Ramesses III was discovered by antiquarians in 1886 and is regarded as the prototypical Egyptian Mummy in numerous Hollywood movies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> His tomb (<a href="/wiki/KV11" title="KV11">KV11</a>) is one of the largest in the <a href="/wiki/Valley_of_the_Kings" title="Valley of the Kings">Valley of the Kings</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chronological_dispute">Chronological dispute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ramesses_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Chronological dispute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is uncertainty regarding the exact dates of the reign of Ramesses III. This uncertainty affects the dating of the <a href="/wiki/Late_Bronze_Age_collapse" title="Late Bronze Age collapse">Late Bronze/Iron Age transition</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a>. This transition is defined by the appearance of <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greece" title="Mycenaean Greece">Mycenaean</a> LH IIIC:1b (<a href="/wiki/Philistines" title="Philistines">Philistine</a>) pottery in the coastal plain of <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a>, generally assumed to correspond to the settlement of <a href="/wiki/Sea_Peoples" title="Sea Peoples">Sea People</a> there at the 8th year of Ramesses III.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Radiocarbon dates and other external evidence permit this transition to be as late as 1100 BCE, compared to the conventional dating of c.1179 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some scientists have tried to establish a chronological point for this pharaoh's reign at 1159 BC, based on a 1999 dating of the "<a href="/wiki/Hekla_3_eruption" title="Hekla 3 eruption">Hekla 3 eruption</a>" of the Hekla volcano in Iceland. Since contemporary records show that the king experienced difficulties provisioning his workmen at <a href="/wiki/Deir_el-Medina" title="Deir el-Medina">Deir el-Medina</a> with supplies in his 29th Year, this dating of Hekla 3 might connect his 28th or 29th regnal year to c. 1159 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> A minor discrepancy of 1 year is possible since Egypt's granaries could have had reserves to cope with at least a single bad year of crop harvests following the onset of the disaster. This implies that the king's reign would have ended just 3 to 4 years later around 1156 or 1155 BC. A rival date of "2900 BP" (950 BC) has since been proposed by scientists based on a re-examination of the volcanic layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Given that no Egyptologist dates Ramesses III's reign to as late as 1000 BC, this would mean that the Hekla 3 eruption presumably occurred well after Ramesses III's reign. A 2002 study, using high-precision radiocarbon dating of a peat deposit containing ash layers, put this eruption in the range 1087-1006 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogaard2002_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bogaard2002-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
