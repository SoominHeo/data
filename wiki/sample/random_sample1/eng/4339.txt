<p><i><b>Rhamphorhynchus</b></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="/ŋ/ 'ng' in 'sing'">ŋ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>, <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/-/ affix">-</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/-/ affix">-</span></span>/</a></span></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> "beak snout") is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of long-tailed <a href="/wiki/Pterosaur" title="Pterosaur">pterosaurs</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> period. Less specialized than contemporary, short-tailed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pterodactyloid" title="Pterodactyloid">pterodactyloid</a> pterosaurs such as <i><a href="/wiki/Pterodactylus" title="Pterodactylus">Pterodactylus</a></i>, it had a long tail, stiffened with <a href="/wiki/Ligament" title="Ligament">ligaments</a>, which ended in a characteristic diamond-shaped vane. The jaws of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> housed needle-like teeth, which were angled forward, with a curved, sharp, beak-like tip lacking teeth, indicating a diet mainly of <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>; indeed, fish and <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopod</a> remains are frequently found in <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> stomachal contents, as well as coprolites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although fragmentary <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> remains possibly belonging to <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> have been found in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, the best preserved specimens come from the <a href="/wiki/Solnhofen" title="Solnhofen">Solnhofen</a> limestone of <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. Many of these fossils preserve not only the bones but impressions of soft tissues, such as wing membranes. Scattered teeth believed to belong to <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> have been found in Portugal as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cranfield-Rhamphorhynchus_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-cranfield-Rhamphorhynchus-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhamphorhynchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The largest known specimen of <i>Rhamphorhynchus muensteri</i> (catalog number BMNH 37002) measures 1.26 meters (4.1 ft) long with a wingspan of 1.81 m (5.9 ft).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Skull">Skull</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhamphorhynchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Skull">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Contrary to a 1927 report by pterosaur researcher <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Broili" title="Ferdinand Broili">Ferdinand Broili</a>, <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> lacked any bony or soft tissue crest, as seen in several species of contemporary small pterodactyloid pterosaurs. Broili claimed to have found a two millimeter tall crest made of thin bone that ran much of the skull's length in one <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> specimen, evidenced by an impression in the surrounding rock and a few small fragments of the crest itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-broili1927_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-broili1927-4">[4]</a></sup> However, subsequent examination of this specimen by Wellnhofer in 1975 and Bennett in 2002 using both visible and ultraviolet light found no trace of a crest; both concluded that Broili was mistaken. The supposed crest, they concluded, was simply an artifact of preservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wellnhofer1975_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-wellnhofer1975-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bennett2002_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-bennett2002-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The teeth of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> intermesh when the jaw is closed and are suggestive of a piscivorous diet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cranfield-Rhamphorhynchus_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-cranfield-Rhamphorhynchus-3">[3]</a></sup> There are twenty teeth in the upper jaws and fourteen in the lower jaws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cranfield-Rhamphorhynchus_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-cranfield-Rhamphorhynchus-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_classification">History and classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhamphorhynchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History and classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rhamphorhynchus_reconstruction_Riou_1863.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="956" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Rhamphorhynchus_reconstruction_Riou_1863.jpg/220px-Rhamphorhynchus_reconstruction_Riou_1863.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Rhamphorhynchus_reconstruction_Riou_1863.jpg/330px-Rhamphorhynchus_reconstruction_Riou_1863.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Rhamphorhynchus_reconstruction_Riou_1863.jpg/440px-Rhamphorhynchus_reconstruction_Riou_1863.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rhamphorhynchus_reconstruction_Riou_1863.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
One of the first ever restorations of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> from 1863, shown with tracks now known to belong to <i><a href="/wiki/Mesolimulus" title="Mesolimulus">Mesolimulus</a></i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The classification and <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomy</a> of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i>, like many pterosaur species known since the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a>, is complex, with a long history of reclassification under a variety of names, often for the same specimens.</p>
<p>The first named specimen of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> was brought to the attention of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samuel_Thomas_von_Soemmerring" title="Samuel Thomas von Soemmerring">Samuel Thomas von Soemmerring</a> by the collector <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_Graf_zu_M%C3%BCnster" title="Georg Graf zu Münster">Georg Graf zu Münster</a> in 1825. Von Soemmerring concluded that it belonged to an ancient bird. When further preparation uncovered teeth, Graf zu Münster sent a cast to Professor <a href="/wiki/Georg_August_Goldfuss" title="Georg August Goldfuss">Georg August Goldfuss</a>, who recognised it as a pterosaur. Like most pterosaurs described in the mid 19th century, <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> was originally considered to be a species of <i><a href="/wiki/Pterodactylus" title="Pterodactylus">Pterodactylus</a></i>. However, at the time, many scientists incorrectly considered <i>Ornithocephalus</i> to be the valid name for <i>Pterodactylus</i>. This specimen of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> was therefore originally named <i>Ornithocephalus Münsteri</i>. This was first mentioned in 1830 by Graf zu Münster himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-munster1830_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-munster1830-7">[7]</a></sup> However, the description making the name valid was given by Goldfuss in an 1831 follow-up to Münster's short paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldfuss1831_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldfuss1831-8">[8]</a></sup> Note that the <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Zoological_Nomenclature" title="International Commission on Zoological Nomenclature">ICZN</a> later ruled that non-standard Latin characters, such as <i>ü</i>, would not be allowed in scientific names, and the spelling <i>münsteri</i> was emended to <i>muensteri</i> by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lydekker" title="Richard Lydekker">Richard Lydekker</a> in 1888.</p>
<p>In 1839, Münster described another specimen that he considered to belong to <i>Ornithocephalus</i> (i.e. <i>Pterodactylus</i>), with a distinctive long tail. He named it <i>Ornithocephalus longicaudus</i>, meaning "long tail", to differentiate it from the specimens with short tails (the true specimens of <i>Pterodactylus</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-munster1839_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-munster1839-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ornithocephalus_M%C3%BCnsteri.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="398" data-file-width="436" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ornithocephalus_M%C3%BCnsteri.jpg/220px-Ornithocephalus_M%C3%BCnsteri.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ornithocephalus_M%C3%BCnsteri.jpg/330px-Ornithocephalus_M%C3%BCnsteri.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f1/Ornithocephalus_M%C3%BCnsteri.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ornithocephalus_M%C3%BCnsteri.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Holotype <i>R. munsteri</i> skull as illustrated in 1851</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1845, <a href="/wiki/Christian_Erich_Hermann_von_Meyer" title="Christian Erich Hermann von Meyer">Hermann von Meyer</a> officially emended the original species <i>Ornithocephalus münsteri</i> to <i>Pterodactylus münsteri</i>, since the name <i>Pterodactylus</i> had been by that point recognized as having priority over <i>Ornithocephalus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vonmeyer1845_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-vonmeyer1845-10">[10]</a></sup> In a subsequent 1846 paper describing a new species of long-tailed 'pterodactyl', von Meyer decided that the long-tailed forms of <i>Pterodactylus</i> were different enough from the short-tailed forms to warrant placement in a <a href="/wiki/Subgenus" title="Subgenus">subgenus</a>, and he named his new species <i>Pterodactylus (Rhamphorhynchus) gemmingi</i> after a specimen owned by collector Captain <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carl_Eming_von_Gemming&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carl Eming von Gemming (page does not exist)">Carl Eming von Gemming</a> that was later by von Gemming sold for three hundred guilders to the <a href="/wiki/Teylers_Museum" title="Teylers Museum">Teylers Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Haarlem" title="Haarlem">Haarlem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vonmeyer1846_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-vonmeyer1846-11">[11]</a></sup> It was not until 1847 that von Meyer elevated <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> to a full-fledged genus, and officially included in it both long-tailed species of <i>Pterodactylus</i> known at the time, <i>R. longicaudus</i> (the original species preserving a long tail) and <i>R. gemmingi</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vonmeyer1847_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-vonmeyer1847-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> is <i>R. longicaudus</i>; its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_specimen" title="Type specimen">type specimen</a> or <a href="/wiki/Holotype" title="Holotype">holotype</a> also was sold to the Teylers Museum, where it still resides as TM 6924.</p>
<p>The original species, <i>Pterodactylus muensteri</i>, remained misclassified until a re-evaluation was published by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Owen" title="Richard Owen">Richard Owen</a> in an 1861 book, in which he renamed it as <i>Rhamphorhynchus münsteri</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-owen1861_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-owen1861-13">[13]</a></sup> The type specimen of <i>R. muensteri</i>, described by Münster and Goldfuss, was lost during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. If available, a new specimen or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neotype" title="Neotype">neotype</a> is designated the type if the original is lost or deemed too poorly preserved. <a href="/wiki/Peter_Wellnhofer" title="Peter Wellnhofer">Peter Wellnhofer</a> declined to designate a neotype in his 1975 review of the genus, because a number of high quality casts of the original specimen were still available in museum collections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bennett1995_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-bennett1995-14">[14]</a></sup> These can serve as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastotype" title="Plastotype">plastotypes</a>.</p>
<p>By the 1990s (and following Wellnhofer's consolidation of many previously named species), about five species of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> were recognized from the Solnhofen limestone of Germany, with a few others having been named from Africa, Spain, and the UK based on fragmentary remains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wellnhofer1975_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-wellnhofer1975-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bennett1995_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-bennett1995-14">[14]</a></sup> Most of the Solnhofen species were differentiated based on their relative size, and size-related features, such as the relative length of the skull.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bennett1995_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-bennett1995-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rhamphorhynchus_skull.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="889" data-file-width="800" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Rhamphorhynchus_skull.png/170px-Rhamphorhynchus_skull.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Rhamphorhynchus_skull.png/255px-Rhamphorhynchus_skull.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Rhamphorhynchus_skull.png/340px-Rhamphorhynchus_skull.png 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rhamphorhynchus_skull.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Restored skull</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1995, pterosaur researcher Chris Bennett published an extensive review of the currently recognized German species. Bennett concluded that all the supposedly distinct German species were actually different year-classes of a single species, <i>R. muensteri</i>, representing distinct age groups, with the smaller species being juveniles and the larger adults. Bennett's paper did not cover the British and African species, though he suggested that these should be considered indeterminate members of the family <a href="/wiki/Rhamphorhynchidae" title="Rhamphorhynchidae">Rhamphorhynchidae</a> and not necessarily species of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> itself. Despite the reduction of the genus to a single species, the type species remains <i>R. longicaudus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bennett1995_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-bennett1995-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>cladogram</i> (family tree) of rhamphorhynchids below is the result of a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" title="Phylogenetic">phylogenetic</a> analysis published by Andres &amp; Myers in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LoneStarPterosaurs_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-LoneStarPterosaurs-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleobiology">Paleobiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhamphorhynchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Paleobiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_history">Life history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhamphorhynchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Life history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

</div>
<p>Traditionally, the large size variation between specimens of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> has been taken to represent species variation. However, in a 1995 paper, Bennett argued that these "species" actually represent year-classes of a single species, <i>Rhamphorhynchus muensteri</i>, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flaplings" title="Flaplings">flaplings</a> to adults. Following from this interpretation, Bennett found several notable changes that occurred in <i>R. muensteri</i> as the animal aged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bennett1995_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-bennett1995-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Juvenile <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> had relatively short skulls with large eyes, and the toothless beak-like tips of the jaws were shorter in juveniles than adults, with rounded, blunt lower jaw tips eventually becoming slender and pointed as the animals grew. Adult <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> also developed a strong upward "hook" at the end of the lower jaw. The number of teeth remained constant from juvenile to adult, though the teeth became relatively shorter and stockier as the animals grew, possibly to accommodate larger and more powerful prey. The pelvic and pectoral girdles fused as the animals aged, with full pectoral fusion attained by one year of age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bennett1995_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-bennett1995-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The shape of the tail vane also changed across various age classes of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i>. In juveniles, the vane was shallow relative to the tail and roughly oval, or "<a href="/wiki/Scalpel" title="Scalpel">lancet</a>-shaped". As growth progressed, the tail vane became <a href="/wiki/Rhomboid" title="Rhomboid">diamond</a>-shaped, and finally <a href="/wiki/Triangle" title="Triangle">triangular</a> in the largest individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bennett1995_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-bennett1995-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>The smallest known <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> specimen has a wingspan of only 290 millimeters; however, it is likely that even such a small individual was capable of flight. Bennett examined two possibilities for hatchlings: that they were <a href="/wiki/Altricial" title="Altricial">altricial</a>, requiring some period of parental care before leaving the nest, or that they were <a href="/wiki/Precocial" title="Precocial">precocial</a>, hatching with sufficient size and ability for flight. If precocious, Bennett suggested that clutches would be small, with only one or two eggs laid per clutch, to compensate for the relatively large size of the hatchings. Bennett did not speculate on which possibility was more likely, though the discovery of a pterosaur embryo (<i><a href="/wiki/Avgodectes" title="Avgodectes">Avgodectes</a></i>) with strongly ossified bones suggests that pterosaurs in general were precocial, able to fly soon after hatching with minimal parental care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wz05_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-wz05-16">[16]</a></sup> This theory was contested by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Histological" title="Histological">histological</a> study of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> that showed the initial rapid growth was followed by a prolonged period of slow growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metabolism">Metabolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhamphorhynchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Metabolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rhamphorhynchidae_-_Rhamphorhynchus_muensteri.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1313" data-file-width="2286" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Rhamphorhynchidae_-_Rhamphorhynchus_muensteri.JPG/240px-Rhamphorhynchidae_-_Rhamphorhynchus_muensteri.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Rhamphorhynchidae_-_Rhamphorhynchus_muensteri.JPG/360px-Rhamphorhynchidae_-_Rhamphorhynchus_muensteri.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Rhamphorhynchidae_-_Rhamphorhynchus_muensteri.JPG/480px-Rhamphorhynchidae_-_Rhamphorhynchus_muensteri.JPG 2x" width="240"/></a>

</div>
</div>
<p>Having determined that <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> specimens fit into discrete year-classes, Bennett was able to estimate the growth rate during one year by comparing the size of one-year-old specimens with two-year-old specimens. He found that the average growth rate during the first year of life for <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> was 130% to 173%, slightly faster than the growth rate in <a href="/wiki/Alligator" title="Alligator">alligators</a>. Growth likely slowed considerably after sexual maturity, so it would have taken more than three years to attain maximum adult size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bennett1995_14-7"><a href="#cite_note-bennett1995-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>This growth rate is much slower than the rate seen in large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pterodactyloid" title="Pterodactyloid">pterodactyloid</a> pterosaurs, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Pteranodon" title="Pteranodon">Pteranodon</a></i>, which attained near-adult size within the first year of life. Additionally, pterodactyloids had <i>determinate growth</i>, meaning that the animals reached a fixed maximum adult size and stopped growing. Previous assumptions of rapid growth rate in rhamphorhynchoids were based on the assumption that they needed to be <a href="/wiki/Warm-blooded" title="Warm-blooded">warm-blooded</a> to sustain active flight. Warm-blooded animals, like modern <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a>, normally show rapid growth to adult size and determinate growth. Because there is no evidence for either in <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i>, Bennett considered his findings consistent with an <a href="/wiki/Ectotherm" title="Ectotherm">ectothermic</a> metabolism, though he recommended more studies needed to be done. Cold-blooded <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i>, Bennett suggested, may have basked in the sun or worked their muscles to accumulate enough energy for bouts of flight, and cooled to ambient temperature when not active to save energy, like modern <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bennett1995_14-8"><a href="#cite_note-bennett1995-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Swimming">Swimming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhamphorhynchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Swimming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> is often depicted as an aerial piscivore, recent evidence suggests that, much like most modern aquatic birds, it probably foraged while swimming. Like several <a href="/wiki/Pteranodontia" title="Pteranodontia">pteranodontians</a> it has hatchet-shaped deltopectoral crests, a short torso and short legs, all features associated with water based launching in pterosaurs. Its feet are broad and large, being useful for propulsion, and the predicted floating position is adequate by pterosaur standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The animal's ability to swim may account for the genus' generally excellent fossil record, being in a position where preservation would be much easier.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_dimorphism">Sexual dimorphism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhamphorhynchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sexual dimorphism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Both <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Koh_Ting-Pong&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Koh Ting-Pong (page does not exist)">Koh Ting-Pong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Wellnhofer" title="Peter Wellnhofer">Peter Wellnhofer</a> recognized two distinct groups among adult <i>Rhamphorhynchus muensteri</i>, differentiated by the proportions of the neck, wing, and hind limbs, but particularly in the ratio of skull to <a href="/wiki/Humerus" title="Humerus">humerus</a> length. Both researchers noted that these two groups of specimens were found in roughly a 1:1 ratio, and interpreted them as different sexes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wellnhofer1975_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-wellnhofer1975-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-koh1937_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-koh1937-19">[19]</a></sup> Bennett tested for sexual dimorphism in <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> by using a statistical analysis, and found that the specimens did indeed group together into small-headed and large-headed sets. However, without any known variation in the actual form of the bones or soft tissue (morphological differences), he found the case for sexual dimorphism inconclusive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bennett1995_14-9"><a href="#cite_note-bennett1995-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Head_orientation">Head orientation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhamphorhynchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Head orientation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rhamphorynchus_gemmingi_jconway.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="420" data-file-width="675" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Rhamphorynchus_gemmingi_jconway.jpg/220px-Rhamphorynchus_gemmingi_jconway.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Rhamphorynchus_gemmingi_jconway.jpg/330px-Rhamphorynchus_gemmingi_jconway.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Rhamphorynchus_gemmingi_jconway.jpg/440px-Rhamphorynchus_gemmingi_jconway.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rhamphorynchus_gemmingi_jconway.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Depiction by John Conway</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 2003, a team of researchers led by <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Witmer" title="Lawrence Witmer">Lawrence Witmer</a> studied the brain anatomy of several types of pterosaurs, including <i>Rhamphorhynchus muensteri</i>, using endocasts of the brain they retrieved by performing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CAT_scan" title="CAT scan">CAT scans</a> of fossil skulls. Using comparisons to modern animals, they were able to estimate various physical attributes of pterosaurs, including relative head orientation during flight and coordination of the wing membrane muscles. Witmer and his team found that <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> held its head parallel to the ground due to the orientation of the <i>osseous labyrinth</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Inner_ear" title="Inner ear">inner ear</a>, which helps animals detect <a href="/wiki/Sense_of_balance" title="Sense of balance">balance</a>. In contrast, pterodactyloid pterosaurs, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Anhanguera_(pterosaur)" title="Anhanguera (pterosaur)">Anhanguera</a></i>, appear to have normally held their heads at a downward angle, both in flight and while on the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-witmeretal2004_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-witmeretal2004-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Daily_activity_patterns">Daily activity patterns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhamphorhynchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Daily activity patterns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Comparisons between the <a href="/wiki/Sclerotic_ring" title="Sclerotic ring">scleral rings</a> of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> and modern birds and reptiles suggest that it may have been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a>, and may have had activity patterns similar to those of modern nocturnal seabirds. This may also indicate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niche_partitioning" title="Niche partitioning">niche partitioning</a> with contemporary pterosaurs inferred to be <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Scaphognathus" title="Scaphognathus">Scaphognathus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pterodactylus" title="Pterodactylus">Pterodactylus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhamphorhynchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

Fossil specimen WDC CSG 255, including a <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> with a <i><a href="/wiki/Leptolepides" title="Leptolepides">Leptolepides</a></i> fish trapped in the <a href="/wiki/Pharynx" title="Pharynx">pharynx</a> and caught in the jaws of an <i><a href="/wiki/Aspidorhynchus" title="Aspidorhynchus">Aspidorhynchus</a></i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Several limestone slabs have been discovered in which fossils of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> are found in close association with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ganoid" title="Ganoid">ganoid</a> fish <i><a href="/wiki/Aspidorhynchus" title="Aspidorhynchus">Aspidorhynchus</a></i>. In one of these specimens, the jaws of an <i>Aspidorhynchus</i> pass through the wings of the <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> specimen. The <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> also has the remains of a small fish, possibly <i><a href="/wiki/Leptolepides" title="Leptolepides">Leptolepides</a></i>, in its throat. This slab, cataloged as WDC CSG 255, may represent two levels of predation; one by <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> and one by <i>Aspidorhynchus</i>. In a 2012 description of WDC CSG 255, researchers proposed that the <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> individual had just caught a <i>Leptolepides</i> while it was swimming. As the <i>Leptolepides</i> was travelling down its <a href="/wiki/Pharynx" title="Pharynx">pharynx</a>, a large <i>Aspidorhynchus</i> would have attacked from below the water, accidentally puncturing the left wing membrane of the <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> with its sharp <a href="/wiki/Rostrum_(anatomy)" title="Rostrum (anatomy)">rostrum</a> in the process. The teeth in its snout were ensnared in the fibrous tissue of the wing membrane, and as the fish thrashed to release itself the left wing of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> was pulled backward into the distorted position seen in the fossil. The encounter resulted in the death of both individuals, most likely because the two animals sank into an <a href="/wiki/Anoxic_waters" title="Anoxic waters">anoxic</a> layer in the water body, depriving the fish of oxygen. The two may have been preserved together as the weight of the head of <i>Aspidorhynchus</i> held down the much lighter body of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FT12_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FT12-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>A large number of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> species have been named, but are currently considered year-classes of <i>R. muensteri</i> by most researchers. Currently recognized specimens of <i>Rhamphorhynchus</i> have previously been published on under the following junior synonyms:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olshevsky2001_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-olshevsky2001-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Note that</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhamphorhynchus_(orchid)" title="Rhamphorhynchus (orchid)">Rhamphorhynchus</a> <i>is also a genus of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orchid" title="Orchid">orchid</a>, named in 1977 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Botanist" title="Botanist">botanist</a> L.A. Garay. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_nomenclature" title="Biological nomenclature">biological nomenclature</a>, the same name may be used for an <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a> that has already been used for a <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> or vice versa.</i></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Odontorhynchus.22">"Odontorhynchus"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhamphorhynchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title='Edit section: "Odontorhynchus"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Odontorhynchus" <i>aculeatus</i> was <a href="/wiki/Holotype" title="Holotype">based on</a> a skull with lower jaws that is now lost. This set of jaws supposedly differed in having two teeth united at the tip of the lower jaw, and none at the tip of the upper jaw. The skull was 6.5–7.0 cm (2.56–2.76 in), making it a small form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ES36_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ES36-24">[24]</a></sup> Stolley, who described the specimen in 1936, argued that <i>R. longicaudus</i> also should be reclassified in the genus "Odontorhynchus". Both Koh and Wellnhofer rejected this idea, arguing instead that "Odontorhynchus" was a junior synonym of <i>R. longicaudus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wellnhofer1975_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-wellnhofer1975-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-koh1937_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-koh1937-19">[19]</a></sup> Bennett agreed with their assessments, and included both "Odontorhynchus" and <i>R. longicaudus</i> as synonyms of <i>R. muensteri</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bennett1995_14-10"><a href="#cite_note-bennett1995-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
