<p><b>L'Anse aux Meadows</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> from the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i>L'Anse-aux-Méduses</i> or "Jellyfish Cove") is an <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_site" title="Archaeological site">archaeological site</a> on the northernmost tip of the island of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Canada" title="Provinces of Canada">province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a>. Discovered in 1960, it is the most famous site of a <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">Viking</a> settlement in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.</p>
<p>Dating to around the year 1000, L'Anse aux Meadows is widely accepted as evidence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian_trans-oceanic_contact" title="Pre-Columbian trans-oceanic contact">pre-Columbian trans-oceanic contact</a>. It is notable for its possible connection with the attempted colony of <a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">Vinland</a> established by <a href="/wiki/Leif_Erikson" title="Leif Erikson">Leif Erikson</a> around the same period or, more broadly, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Norse colonization of the Americas">Norse exploration of the Americas</a>. It was named a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage_site" title="World Heritage site">World Heritage site</a> by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> in 1978.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%27Anse_aux_Meadows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The site now known as L'Anse aux Meadows was first recorded as <i>Anse à la Médée</i> ("the Médée's Cove") on a French <a href="/wiki/Nautical_chart" title="Nautical chart">nautical chart</a> made in 1862. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toponym" title="Toponym">toponym</a> probably referred to a ship named after the Greek mythological figure of <a href="/wiki/Medea" title="Medea">Medea</a>, which would have been a typical name for seagoing vessels at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brochure_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-brochure-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cove" title="Cove">cove</a> facing the modern village of L'Anse aux Meadows is still named <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9d%C3%A9e_Bay" title="Médée Bay">Médée Bay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brochure_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-brochure-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>How the village itself came to be named "L'Anse aux Meadows" is not clear. <a href="/wiki/Parks_Canada" title="Parks Canada">Parks Canada</a>, which manages the site, states that the current name was anglicized from "Anse à la Médée" after English speakers settled in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brochure_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-brochure-2">[2]</a></sup> Another possibility is that "L'Anse aux Meadows" is a corruption of the French designation "L'Anse aux Méduses", which means "<a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">Jellyfish</a> Cove".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The shift from "Méduses" to "Meadows" may have occurred because the landscape in the area tends to be open, with <a href="/wiki/Meadow" title="Meadow">meadows</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_significance">Discovery and significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%27Anse_aux_Meadows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery and significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1960, the archaeological remains of a <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> village were discovered in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newfoundland" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a> by two <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norwegians</a>: the explorer <a href="/wiki/Helge_Ingstad" title="Helge Ingstad">Helge Ingstad</a> and the archaeologist <a href="/wiki/Anne_Stine_Ingstad" title="Anne Stine Ingstad">Anne Stine Ingstad</a>, who were husband and wife. Based on the idea that the Old Norse name "<a href="/wiki/Vinland#Etymology" title="Vinland">Vinland</a>", mentioned in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icelandic_Sagas" title="Icelandic Sagas">Icelandic Sagas</a></i>, meant "wine-land", historians had long speculated that the region contained wild grapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> Because of this, the common hypothesis before the Ingstads' theories was that the <a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">Vinland region</a> existed somewhere south of the northern <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> coast, because that is roughly as far north as <a href="/wiki/Vitis" title="Vitis">grapes</a> grow naturally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Ingstads doubted this theory, saying "that the name Vinland probably means land of meadows...and includes a peninsula."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This speculation was based on the belief that the Norse would not have been comfortable settling in areas along the American Atlantic coast. This dichotomy between the two views could have possibly been driven due to <a href="/wiki/Vinland#Name" title="Vinland">the two historic ways in which the first vowel sound of "Vinland" could be pronounced.</a></p>
<p>In 1960, George Decker, a citizen of the small fishing hamlet of L'Anse aux Meadows, led Helge Ingstad to a group of mounds near the village that the locals called the "old Indian camp".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brochure_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-brochure-2">[2]</a></sup> These bumps covered with grass looked like the remains of houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discovery_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discovery-7">[7]</a></sup> Helge Ingstad and <a href="/wiki/Anne_Stine_Ingstad" title="Anne Stine Ingstad">Anne Stine Ingstad</a> carried out seven archaeological excavations there from 1961 to 1968. They investigated eight complete house sites and the remains of a ninth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> They determined that the site was of Norse origin because of definitive similarities between the characteristics of structures and artifacts found at the site compared to sites in Greenland and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> from around 1000 CE.</p>
<p>Though a possible <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> settlement has been found in southern Newfoundland at <a href="/wiki/Point_Rosee" title="Point Rosee">Point Rosee</a>, L'Anse aux Meadows is currently the only confirmed Norse site in North America. It represents the farthest-known extent of European exploration and settlement of the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> before the voyages of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> almost 500 years later. Historians have speculated that there were other settlement sites, or at least Norse-Native American trade contacts, in the Canadian Arctic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2012, possible Norse outposts were identified in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nanook,_Baffin_Island&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nanook, Baffin Island (page does not exist)">Nanook</a> at <a href="/wiki/Tanfield_Valley" title="Tanfield Valley">Tanfield Valley</a> on <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Island" title="Baffin Island">Baffin Island</a>, as well as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nunguvik&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nunguvik (page does not exist)">Nunguvik</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Willows_Island&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Willows Island (page does not exist)">Willows Island</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Avayalik_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Avayalik Islands (page does not exist)">Avayalik Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Norse_site">Norse site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%27Anse_aux_Meadows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Norse site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The archaeological excavation at L'Anse aux Meadows was conducted in the 1960s by an international team led by <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeologist</a> Anne Stine Ingstad under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Parks_Canada" title="Parks Canada">Parks Canada</a> in the 1970s. Following each period of excavation, the site was reburied to protect and conserve the cultural resources.</p>
<p>The Norse settlement at L'Anse aux Meadows has been dated to approximately 1,000 years ago (carbon dating estimate 990–1050 CE),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CordellLightfoot2008_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-CordellLightfoot2008-12">[12]</a></sup> an assessment that tallies with the relative dating of artifact and structure types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Today the area mostly consists of open, grassy lands, but 1000 years ago, there were forests that were convenient for boat-building, house-building and iron extraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The remains of eight buildings (labeled from A–J) were found. They are believed to have been constructed of <a href="/wiki/Sod" title="Sod">sod</a> placed over a wooden frame. Based on associated artifacts, the buildings were identified as dwellings or workshops. The largest dwelling (F) measured 28.8 m × 15.6 m (94 ft × 51 ft) and consisted of several rooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canada-15">[15]</a></sup> Three small buildings (B, C, G) may have been workshops or living quarters for lower-status crew or slaves. Workshops were identified as an <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> <a href="/wiki/Forge" title="Forge">smithy</a> (building J) containing a <a href="/wiki/Hearth" title="Hearth">forge</a> and iron <a href="/wiki/Slag" title="Slag">slag</a>, a carpentry workshop (building D), which generated wood debris and a specialized boat repair area containing worn <a href="/wiki/Rivet" title="Rivet">rivets</a>. Other things found at the site consisted of common everyday Norse items, including a stone oil lamp, a <a href="/wiki/Sharpening_stone" title="Sharpening stone">whetstone</a>, a bronze fastening pin, a bone knitting needle and part of a spindle. Stone weights, which were found in building G, may have been part of a loom. The presence of the spindle and needle suggests that women as well as men inhabited the settlement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discovery_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Discovery-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is no way of knowing how many people lived at the site at any given time; archaeological evidence of the dwellings suggest it had the capacity of supporting 30 to 160 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kolodny2012_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kolodny2012-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Food remains included <a href="/wiki/Juglans_cinerea" title="Juglans cinerea">butternuts</a>, which are significant because they do not grow naturally north of <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>. Their presence probably indicates the Norse inhabitants traveled farther south to obtain them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vinland_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vinland-17">[17]</a></sup> There is evidence to suggest that the Norse hunted an array of animals that inhabited the area. These included caribou, wolf, fox, bear, lynx, marten, all types of birds and fish, seal, whale and walrus. This area is no longer rich in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_(food)" title="Game (food)">game</a> due in large part to the harsh winters. This forces the game to either hibernate or venture south as the wind, deep snow, and sheets of ice cover the area. These losses made the harsh winters very difficult for the Norse people at L'Anse aux Meadows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This lack of game supports archaeologists' beliefs that the site was inhabited by the Norse for a relatively short time.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-European_settlements">Pre-European settlements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%27Anse_aux_Meadows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pre-European settlements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newfoundland#Pre-colonisation" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland § Pre-colonisation</a></div>
<p>Before European settlement, there is evidence of <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_peoples_in_Canada" title="Aboriginal peoples in Canada">aboriginal</a> occupations in the area of L'Anse aux Meadows, the oldest dated at roughly 6,000 years ago. None was contemporaneous with the Norse occupation. The most prominent of these earlier occupations were by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorset_people" title="Dorset people">Dorset people</a>, who predated the Norse by about 200 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Connection_with_Vinland_sagas">Connection with Vinland sagas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%27Anse_aux_Meadows&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Connection with Vinland sagas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Territories_and_Voyages_of_the_Vikings_blank.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1091" data-file-width="1578" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Territories_and_Voyages_of_the_Vikings_blank.png/220px-Territories_and_Voyages_of_the_Vikings_blank.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Territories_and_Voyages_of_the_Vikings_blank.png/330px-Territories_and_Voyages_of_the_Vikings_blank.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Territories_and_Voyages_of_the_Vikings_blank.png/440px-Territories_and_Voyages_of_the_Vikings_blank.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Territories_and_Voyages_of_the_Vikings_blank.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Territories, voyages, and conquests of the Norse at L'Anse aux Meadows</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Adam_of_Bremen" title="Adam of Bremen">Adam of Bremen</a>, a German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleric" title="Cleric">cleric</a>, was the first European to mention Vinland. In a text he composed around 1073, he wrote that</p>
<blockquote>
<p>He [i.e., the Danish king, <a href="/wiki/Sweyn_II_of_Denmark" title="Sweyn II of Denmark">Sven Estridsson</a>] also told me of another island discovered by many in that ocean. It is called Vinland because vines grow there on their own accord, producing the most excellent wine. Moreover, that unsown crops abound there, we have ascertained not from fabulous conjecture but from the reliable reports of the Danes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>This excerpt is from a history Adam composed of the <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Bremen" title="Archbishopric of Bremen">archbishops of Hamburg-Bremen</a> who held ecclesiastical authority over Scandinavia (the original home of the Norse people) at the time.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_saga" title="Norse saga">Norse sagas</a> are written versions of older oral traditions. Two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icelandic_sagas" title="Icelandic sagas">Icelandic sagas</a>, commonly called the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saga_of_the_Greenlanders" title="Saga of the Greenlanders">Saga of the Greenlanders</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Saga_of_Erik_the_Red" title="Saga of Erik the Red">Saga of Erik the Red</a></i>, describe the experiences of Norse Greenlanders who discovered and attempted to settle land to the west of Greenland, which they called Vinland. The sagas suggest that the Vinland settlement failed because of conflicts within the Norse community, as well as between the Norse and the native people they encountered, whom they called <a href="/wiki/Skr%C3%A6ling" title="Skræling">Skrælingar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern archaeological studies have suggested that the L'Anse aux Meadows site was not Vinland itself, but rather was within a larger area called Vinland, which extended south from L'Anse aux Meadows to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Lawrence_River" title="St. Lawrence River">St. Lawrence River</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hurst2013_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hurst2013-22">[22]</a></sup> The village at L'Anse aux Meadows served as an exploration base and winter camp for expeditions heading southward into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_of_St._Lawrence" title="Gulf of St. Lawrence">Gulf of St. Lawrence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vinland_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vinland-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-damals_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-damals-23">[23]</a></sup> The settlements of Vinland mentioned in these two sagas, <a href="/wiki/Leifsbudir" title="Leifsbudir">Leifsbudir</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leif_Ericson" title="Leif Ericson">Leif Ericson</a>) and Hóp (Norse Greenlanders), have both been claimed as the L'Anse aux Meadows site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-damals_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-damals-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
