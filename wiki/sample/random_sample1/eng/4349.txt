<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major-General" title="Major-General">Major-General</a> <b>Lachlan Macquarie</b> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Companion_of_the_Order_of_the_Bath" title="Companion of the Order of the Bath">CB</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/ɒr/ 'or' in 'moral'">ɒr</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a>: <span lang="gd" xml:lang="gd"><i>Lachann MacGuaire</i></span>; 31 January 1762 – 1 July 1824)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was a <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> officer and colonial administrator from <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. He served as the fifth and last autocratic <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_New_South_Wales" title="Governor of New South Wales">Governor of New South Wales</a> from 1810 to 1821,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and had a leading role in the social, economic and architectural development of the colony. He is considered by historians to have had a crucial influence on the transition of <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> from a <a href="/wiki/Penal_colony" title="Penal colony">penal colony</a> to a free settlement and therefore to have played a major role in the shaping of Australian society in the early nineteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> An inscription on his tomb in Scotland describes him as "The Father of Australia".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_career">Early life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lachlan_Macquarie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lachlan Macquarie was born on the island of <a href="/wiki/Ulva" title="Ulva">Ulva</a> off the coast of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isle_of_Mull" title="Isle of Mull">Isle of Mull</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Inner_Hebrides" title="Inner Hebrides">Inner Hebrides</a>, a chain of islands off the West Coast of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. He was a gentleman of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Highland" title="Scottish Highland">Scottish Highland</a> family <a href="/wiki/Clan_MacQuarrie" title="Clan MacQuarrie">clan MacQuarrie</a> which possessed Ulva, <a href="/wiki/Staffa" title="Staffa">Staffa</a>, and a region of the Isle of Mull for over one thousand years, and his forebears were buried on <a href="/wiki/Iona" title="Iona">Iona</a>. Governor Macquarie's father, a "man of Intelligence, polite, and much of the world", supposedly attained the age of 103 years, dying on 4 January 1818.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOVERNOR_MACQUARIE_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-GOVERNOR_MACQUARIE-6">[6]</a></sup> His mother was the daughter of a Maclaine chieftain who owned a castle on the Isle of Mull.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Macquarie left the island at the age of 14.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keay_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keay-8">[8]</a></sup> If he did attend the <a href="/wiki/Royal_High_School,_Edinburgh" title="Royal High School, Edinburgh">Royal High School of Edinburgh</a>, "as tradition has it",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> it was only for a very brief period because, at the same age, he volunteered for the army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellis.2C_M.H..2C_1952.2C_p._4_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ellis.2C_M.H..2C_1952.2C_p._4-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Macquarie joined the <a href="/wiki/84th_Regiment_of_Foot_(Royal_Highland_Emigrants)" title="84th Regiment of Foot (Royal Highland Emigrants)">84th Regiment of Foot</a> on 9 April 1777, travelling with it to North America in 1777 to take part in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American War of Independence</a>. As a new recruit on the way to America he participated in the <a href="/wiki/84th_Regiment_of_Foot_(Royal_Highland_Emigrants)" title="84th Regiment of Foot (Royal Highland Emigrants)">Battle of the Newcastle Jane</a>. This battle was the first naval victory for a British merchant ship over an American <a href="/wiki/Privateer" title="Privateer">privateer</a>. He was initially stationed at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_of_Halifax" title="City of Halifax">Halifax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, and was commissioned as an <a href="/wiki/Ensign_(rank)" title="Ensign (rank)">ensign</a> five months after his arrival. On 18 January 1781, he was promoted to lieutenant and transferred to the <a href="/wiki/71st_(Highland)_Regiment_of_Foot" title="71st (Highland) Regiment of Foot">71st (Highland) Regiment of Foot</a>, and served with them in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellis.2C_M.H..2C_1952.2C_p._4_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ellis.2C_M.H..2C_1952.2C_p._4-10">[10]</a></sup> In June 1784 he returned to Scotland as a half-pay lieutenant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellis.2C_M.H..2C_1952.2C_p._4_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ellis.2C_M.H..2C_1952.2C_p._4-10">[10]</a></sup> Three years later, on Christmas Day 1787 he received his commission as lieutenant in the 77th Regiment, where he saw service with the army in India and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>. Macquarie became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freemason" title="Freemason">Freemason</a> in January 1793 at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombay" title="Bombay">Bombay</a>, in Lodge No. 1 (No. 139 on the register of the English "Moderns" <a href="/wiki/Grand_Lodge" title="Grand Lodge">Grand Lodge</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He was promoted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_(land)" title="Captain (land)">Captain</a> on 9 November 1789, Major on 12 March 1801. During 1801 he had accompanied Sir David Baird and the Indian Army to Egypt, with the rank of Deputy Adjutant General, and was present at the capture of Alexandria and the final expulsion of the French Army from Egypt. Two years later, 1803, he was in London, as Assistant Adjutant General to Lord Harrington, who commanded the London district. In 1803 and 1804 saw him on active service in India. He returned to London in 1807, commanding the <a href="/wiki/73rd_(Perthshire)_Regiment_of_Foot" title="73rd (Perthshire) Regiment of Foot">73rd Regiment of Foot</a>, and on 8 May was appointed Governor of New South Wales and its dependencies, leaving for the colony on 22 May 1809, in HMS <i>Dromedary</i>, where he landed officially on 31 December 1809, at Sydney Cove.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOVERNOR_MACQUARIE_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-GOVERNOR_MACQUARIE-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1793 he married Jane Jarvis, daughter of the Chief Justice of <a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a>. Three years later she died of <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governor_of_New_South_Wales">Governor of New South Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lachlan_Macquarie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Governor of New South Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lachlanmacquarie.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="772" data-file-width="635" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Lachlanmacquarie.jpg/200px-Lachlanmacquarie.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Lachlanmacquarie.jpg/300px-Lachlanmacquarie.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Lachlanmacquarie.jpg/400px-Lachlanmacquarie.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lachlanmacquarie.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>
</div>
<p>In November 1807, Macquarie's cousin <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Macquarie" title="Elizabeth Macquarie">Elizabeth Henrietta Campbell</a> became his second wife. In April 1809 Macquarie was appointed Governor of <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>. In making this appointment, the British government reversed its practice of appointing naval officers as governor and chose an army commander in the hope that he could secure the co-operation of the unruly <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales_Corps" title="New South Wales Corps">New South Wales Corps</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and aided by the fact he arrived in New South Wales at the head of his own military unit, the <a href="/wiki/73rd_(Perthshire)_Regiment_of_Foot" title="73rd (Perthshire) Regiment of Foot">73rd Regiment</a>. At the head of regular troops he was unchallenged by the New South Wales Corps whose members had become settled in farming, commerce and trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Macquarie was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_Kingdom)" title="Colonel (United Kingdom)">Colonel</a> in 1810, <a href="/wiki/Brigadier" title="Brigadier">Brigadier</a> in 1811 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major-General" title="Major-General">Major-General</a> in 1813, while serving as governor.</p>
<p>The Macquaries departed from England in May 1809 aboard <a href="/wiki/HMS_Howe_(1805)" title="HMS Howe (1805)">HMS <i>Dromedary</i></a>, accompanied by <a href="/wiki/HMS_Hindostan_(1804)" title="HMS Hindostan (1804)">HMS <i>Hindostan</i></a>. They reached Sydney on 28 December 1809. He started as a governor on 1 January 1810 and he appointed <a href="/wiki/John_Thomas_Campbell" title="John Thomas Campbell">John Campbell</a> as his secretary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first task Macquarie had to tackle was to restore orderly, lawful government and discipline in the colony following the <a href="/wiki/Rum_Rebellion" title="Rum Rebellion">Rum Rebellion</a> of 1808 against Governor <a href="/wiki/William_Bligh" title="William Bligh">William Bligh</a>. Macquarie was ordered by the British government to arrest both <a href="/wiki/John_Macarthur_(wool_pioneer)" title="John Macarthur (wool pioneer)">John Macarthur</a> and Major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Johnston_(New_South_Wales)" title="George Johnston (New South Wales)">George Johnston</a>, two of the leaders of the Rum Rebellion. However, by the time Macquarie arrived in Sydney in December 1809, both Macarthur and Johnston had already sailed for England to defend themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes.2C_R..2C_1986.2C_p._294_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes.2C_R..2C_1986.2C_p._294-16">[16]</a></sup> Macquarie immediately set about cancelling the various initiatives taken by the rebel government—for example, all "pardons, leases and land grants" made by the rebels were revoked, (although many were later re-issued).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes.2C_R..2C_1986.2C_p._294_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes.2C_R..2C_1986.2C_p._294-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although with the arrival of Lachlan Macquarie, the New South Wales Corps and its monopoly were ended, the military influence survived, with military officers having sway over the justice system. A great gulf existed between the officers and their factional division which included the free settlers ("exclusives") and convicts who had completed their term of imprisonment and were now settlers ("emancipists").</p>
<p>New South Wales suffered severe drought in 1812 and 1813, there was widespread loss of crops and livestock and by 1814 many farmers were close to insolvency because of the drought and ensuing depression.</p>
<p>In 1814 a Second Charter of Justice was issued for New South Wales. It defined how the civil court system was to be structured. Three new Courts of Civil Judicature were to be established in New South Wales: the Governor's Court, the Lieutenant-Governor's Court and the Supreme Court. Jeffrey Hart Bent, the brother of the Judge Advocate, arrived in the colony as the first judge of the new Supreme Court.</p>
<p>Courts need lawyers and Macquarie's efforts to allow emancipist attorneys to appear before the Supreme Court was blocked by Jeffrey Bent, who, with his brother, had his allegiances with the military and exclusive settlers. Later in 1814, two solicitors, Fredrick Garling and Moore, arrived in New South Wales. English law was to be followed as far as it was possible. Where new ordinances or laws were needed, they were to be consistent with English laws as far as the particular circumstances of the colony would allow. Many of the settlers were discontented with this, because they questioned whether some of the governors' ordinances were, in fact, valid. Claims were made in New South Wales and in England that governors were exceeding their authority by making ordinances that were in conflict with English laws.</p>
<p>Macquarie's relationship with the new Court was never harmonious. The brothers Bent, in their key legal positions, quickly became opponents of the Governor, and personal antipathy affected decisions on both sides. Like most of the governors before him, Macquarie's noble ideals were undermined by harsh realities and constant opposition. In 1816 he enforced his new proclamation against trespassing on the Government Domain by having three trespassers (all free settlers) flogged. This incident was one of several of which Bent and others complained to the British Government as examples of Macquarie's authoritarian excesses. As a result, Macquarie was censured by Lord Bathurst, the Secretary of State for Colonies, and in 1819 <a href="/wiki/John_Bigge" title="John Bigge">Commissioner John Thomas Bigge</a> was sent to enquire into affairs in New South Wales.</p>
<p>Macquarie took control of the colony, breaking the power of the Army officers such as <a href="/wiki/John_Macarthur_(wool_pioneer)" title="John Macarthur (wool pioneer)">John Macarthur</a>, who had been the colony's <i>de facto</i> ruler since Bligh's overthrow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> He was "the last British proconsul sent to run New South Wales as a military autocracy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1812, the first detailed inquiry into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convictism_in_Australia" title="Convictism in Australia">convict system in Australia</a> by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Select_Committee_(Westminster_System)" title="Select Committee (Westminster System)">Select Committee</a> on <a href="/wiki/Penal_transportation" title="Penal transportation">Transportation</a>, supported in general Macquarie's liberal policies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> However, the committee thought that fewer <a href="/wiki/Ticket_of_leave#Australian_convicts" title="Ticket of leave">tickets of leave</a> should be issued and opposed the governor having the power to grant <a href="/wiki/Pardon" title="Pardon">pardons</a>. The committee concluded that the colony should be made as prosperous as possible so as to provide work for the convicts and to encourage them to become settlers after being given their freedom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On a visit of inspection to the settlement of <a href="/wiki/Hobart" title="Hobart">Hobart Town</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Derwent_River,_Tasmania" title="Derwent River, Tasmania">Derwent River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Van_Diemen%27s_Land" title="Van Diemen's Land">Van Diemen's Land</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>) in November 1811, Macquarie was appalled at the ramshackle arrangement of the town and ordered the government surveyor <a href="/wiki/James_Meehan" title="James Meehan">James Meehan</a> to survey a regular street layout. This survey determined the form of the current centre of the city of Hobart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>Macquarie is credited with producing the first official currency specifically for circulation in Australia. Foreign coins were common in the early years of the New South Wales colony but much of this coin left the colony as a result of trade with visiting merchant ships. To secure a reliable supply of coins, in 1812 Macquarie purchased 40,000 <a href="/wiki/Spanish_dollar" title="Spanish dollar">Spanish dollar</a> coins and had a convicted forger named William Henshall cut the centres out of the coins and counter stamp them to distinguish them as belonging to the colony of New South Wales and prevent them being useful elsewhere. The central plug (known as a "dump") was valued at 15 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_One_Penny_coin_(pre-decimal)" title="British One Penny coin (pre-decimal)">pence</a> and the rim (known as a <a href="/wiki/Holey_dollar" title="Holey dollar">holey dollar</a>) became a five-<a href="/wiki/Shilling_(British_coin)" title="Shilling (British coin)">shilling</a> piece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The new currency was proclaimed in the <i><a href="/wiki/Sydney_Gazette" title="Sydney Gazette">Sydney Gazette</a> of 10 July 1813, with offences of forgery, utterance or exportation of the new currency being punishable by seven years in the Newcastle coal mines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></i></p>
<p>The end of the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> in 1815 brought a renewed flood of both convicts and settlers to New South Wales, as the sea lanes became free and as the rate of unemployment and crime in Britain rose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Macquarie presided over a rapid increase in population and economic activity. By the time of his departure for London on 15 February 1822,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> the white population had reached an 'estimated' 36,969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_reformer_and_explorer">As reformer and explorer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lachlan_Macquarie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: As reformer and explorer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Central to Macquarie's policy was his treatment of the <a href="/wiki/Emancipist" title="Emancipist">emancipists</a>: convicts whose sentences had expired or who had been given conditional or absolute pardons. By 1810 emancipists had outnumbered the free settlers, and Macquarie set the tone himself by appointing emancipists to government positions: <a href="/wiki/Francis_Greenway" title="Francis Greenway">Francis Greenway</a> as colonial architect<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ward.2C_R..2C_1975.2C_p._37_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ward.2C_R..2C_1975.2C_p._37-27">[27]</a></sup> and Dr <a href="/wiki/William_Redfern" title="William Redfern">William Redfern</a> as colonial <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgeon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> He scandalised settler opinion by appointing an emancipist, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Thompson_(jurist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Andrew Thompson (jurist) (page does not exist)">Andrew Thompson</a>, as a magistrate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and by inviting emancipists to tea at Government House. In exchange, Macquarie demanded that the ex-convicts live reformed (Christian) lives. He required that former convicts regularly attend church services, and in particular, strongly encouraged formal Christian (Anglican) marriages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Macquarie was the greatest sponsor of exploration the colony had yet seen. In 1813 he authorised <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Blaxland" title="Gregory Blaxland">Blaxland</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Wentworth" title="William Wentworth">Wentworth</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Lawson_(explorer)" title="William Lawson (explorer)">Lawson's</a> <a href="/wiki/1813_crossing_of_the_Blue_Mountains" title="1813 crossing of the Blue Mountains">successful crossing of the Blue Mountains</a> where they found the great plains of the interior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BlaxlandADB_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-BlaxlandADB-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> There he ordered the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Bathurst,_New_South_Wales" title="Bathurst, New South Wales">Bathurst</a>, Australia's first inland city. He appointed <a href="/wiki/John_Oxley" title="John Oxley">John Oxley</a> as surveyor-general and sent him on expeditions up the coast of New South Wales and inland to find new rivers and new lands for settlement. Oxley discovered the rich <a href="/wiki/Northern_Rivers" title="Northern Rivers">Northern Rivers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_England_(Australia)" title="New England (Australia)">New England</a> regions of New South Wales, and in what is now <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a> he explored the present site of <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">Brisbane</a>.</p>
<p>The street layout of modern central Sydney is based upon a street plan established by Macquarie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ward.2C_R..2C_1975.2C_p._37_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ward.2C_R..2C_1975.2C_p._37-27">[27]</a></sup> The colony's most prestigious buildings were built on <a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Street,_Sydney" title="Macquarie Street, Sydney">Macquarie Street</a>. Some of these still stand today. What has survived of the Georgian 'Rum Hospital' serves as the Parliament House of the state of New South Wales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ward.2C_R..2C_1975.2C_p._37_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ward.2C_R..2C_1975.2C_p._37-27">[27]</a></sup> It is probable that the hospital was designed by Macquarie himself, in collaboration with his wife. The building's wide <a href="/wiki/Veranda" title="Veranda">verandas</a> were evidently inspired by Macquarie's familiarity with English colonial architecture in India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes.2C_R..2C_1986.2C_p._297_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes.2C_R..2C_1986.2C_p._297-33">[33]</a></sup> The elaborate stables which Macquarie commissioned for Government House are part of the modern structure housing the Sydney Conservatorium of Music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharpe2000_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sharpe2000-34">[34]</a></sup> Both of these buildings were constructed by Macquarie in defiance of the British government's ban on expensive public building projects in the colony<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes.2C_R..2C_1986.2C_p._297_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes.2C_R..2C_1986.2C_p._297-33">[33]</a></sup> and reflect the tension between Macquarie's vision of Sydney as a Georgian city and the British government's view of the colony as a dumping ground for convicts to be financed as cheaply as possible.</p>
<p>Another fundamental reform initiated by Macquarie of enduring significance was made on 15 August 1820, shortly before his return to England, when he ordered all traffic on New South Wales roads to keep to the left.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The origin of the name "Australia" is closely associated with Macquarie. "Australia", as a name for the country which we now know by that name, was suggested by <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Flinders" title="Matthew Flinders">Matthew Flinders</a>, but first used in an official despatch by Macquarie in 1817.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Macquarie's policies, especially his championing of the emancipists and the lavish expenditure of government money on public works, aroused opposition both in the colony and in London, where the government still saw New South Wales as fundamentally a penal colony. His statement, in a letter to the Colonial Secretary, that <i>"free settlers in general... are by far the most discontented persons in the country"</i> and that <i>"emancipated convicts, or persons become free by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indentured_servitude" title="Indentured servitude">servitude</a>, made in many instances the best description of settlers"</i>, was much held against him.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Despite opposition from the British government, Macquarie encouraged the creation of the colony's first bank, the <a href="/wiki/Westpac" title="Westpac">Bank of New South Wales</a>, in 1817.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aboriginal-settler_relations">Aboriginal-settler relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lachlan_Macquarie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aboriginal-settler relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Macquarie is regarded as having been ambivalent towards the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Aborigine" title="Australian Aborigine">Australian Aborigines</a>. He ordered punitive expeditions against Aboriginal people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> However, when dealing with friendly tribes, he developed a strategy of nominating a 'chief' to be responsible for each of the clans, identified by the wearing of a brass breast-plate engraved with his name and title. Although this was a typically European way of negotiation, it often did reflect the actual status of elders within tribes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Macquarie also pioneered the concept of returning land to Aboriginal control, commencing with areas surrounding Broken Bay and Georges Head.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-env2_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-env2-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1814 Macquarie founded the Native Institution in Parramatta for the education of Aboriginal children. Aboriginal children were enrolled in the school, sometimes forcibly, and were brought up in accordance with European education and culture. Although the children in the School were well treated, modern social analysis indicates their forced enrolment and subsequent Europeanised social education as a conscious attempt to reduce the influence and future of indigenous culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-env2_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-env2-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern historians have also identified the forced taking of children for the School as a cause of the outbreak of open conflict between the indigenous population and Governor Macquarie's administration, commencing in 1814 and continuing for at least two years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-env2_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-env2-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>Leaders of the free settler community complained to London about Macquarie's policies, and in 1819 the government appointed an English judge, <a href="/wiki/John_Bigge" title="John Bigge">John Bigge</a>, to visit New South Wales and report on its administration. Bigge generally agreed with the settlers' criticisms, and his reports on the colony led to Macquarie's resignation in 1821; he had, however, served longer than any other governor. Bigge also recommended that no governor should again be allowed to rule as an autocrat, and in 1824 the <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales_Legislative_Council" title="New South Wales Legislative Council">New South Wales Legislative Council</a>, Australia's first legislative body, was appointed to advise the governor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Macquarie returned to Scotland, and died in London in 1824 while busy defending himself against Bigge's charges. But his reputation continued to grow after his death, especially among the emancipists and their descendants, who were the majority of the Australian population until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_rushes" title="Gold rushes">gold rushes</a>. Today he is regarded by many as the most enlightened and progressive of the early governors who sought to establish Australia as a country, rather than as a prison camp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The nationalist school of Australian historians have treated him as a proto-nationalist hero. Macquarie formally adopted the name Australia for the continent, the name earlier proposed by the first circumnavigator of Australia, <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Flinders" title="Matthew Flinders">Matthew Flinders</a>. As well as the many geographical features named after him in his lifetime, he is commemorated by <a href="/wiki/Macquarie_University" title="Macquarie University">Macquarie University</a> in Sydney.</p>
<p>Macquarie was buried on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isle_of_Mull" title="Isle of Mull">Isle of Mull</a> in a remote mausoleum with his wife, daughter and son. His grave in Mull is maintained by the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_of_Australia" title="National Trust of Australia">National Trust of Australia</a> and is inscribed "The Father of Australia".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>

<p>Many places in Australia have been named in Macquarie's honour (some of these were named by Macquarie himself). They include:</p>
<p>At the time of his governorship or shortly thereafter:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Island" title="Macquarie Island">Macquarie Island</a> between <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> the subsequent tectonic plate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macquarie_Ridge" title="Macquarie Ridge">Macquarie Ridge</a> and junction which align northwards from the island are also named after Macquarie.</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Macquarie_River" title="Macquarie River">Macquarie River</a> a significant inland river in <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> which passes <a href="/wiki/Bathurst,_New_South_Wales" title="Bathurst, New South Wales">Bathurst</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wellington,_New_South_Wales" title="Wellington, New South Wales">Wellington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dubbo" title="Dubbo">Dubbo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warren,_New_South_Wales" title="Warren, New South Wales">Warren</a> before entering the <a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Marshes" title="Macquarie Marshes">Macquarie Marshes</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Barwon_River_(New_South_Wales)" title="Barwon River (New South Wales)">Barwon River</a></li>
<li>Mount Macquarie, highest point in the <a href="/wiki/Blayney,_New_South_Wales" title="Blayney, New South Wales">Blayney Shire</a> at 1100 metres above sea level. For a time it was named <b>Mount Lachlan</b></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lachlan_River" title="Lachlan River">Lachlan River</a>, another significant river in New South Wales</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Port_Macquarie" title="Port Macquarie">Port Macquarie</a>, a city at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Hastings_River" title="Hastings River">Hastings River</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Mid_North_Coast" title="Mid North Coast">Mid North Coast</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Pass" title="Macquarie Pass">Macquarie Pass</a>, a route traversing the escarpment between the <a href="/wiki/Illawarra" title="Illawarra">Illawarra</a> district and the Southern Highlands district of New South Wales</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Rivulet" title="Macquarie Rivulet">Macquarie Rivulet</a>, a river 23 kilometres long which rises near <a href="/wiki/Robertson,_New_South_Wales" title="Robertson, New South Wales">Robertson, New South Wales</a> and drains into <a href="/wiki/Lake_Illawarra" title="Lake Illawarra">Lake Illawarra</a></li>
<li>Around Sydney:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Culvert" title="Macquarie Culvert">Macquarie Culvert</a> in the Royal Botanic Gardens</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Street,_Sydney" title="Macquarie Street, Sydney">Macquarie Street</a>, one of the principal streets of downtown Sydney, home of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_South_Wales_Parliament" title="New South Wales Parliament">New South Wales Parliament</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macquarie_Place" title="Macquarie Place">Macquarie Place</a> a small park in the Sydney CBD</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Lighthouse" title="Macquarie Lighthouse">Macquarie Lighthouse</a>, Australia's first and longest operating navigational light</li>
<li>The former Fort Macquarie on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bennelong_Point,_New_South_Wales" title="Bennelong Point, New South Wales">Bennelong Point</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Fields,_New_South_Wales" title="Macquarie Fields, New South Wales">Macquarie Fields</a>, now a suburb of Sydney but named by surveyor Evans after the governor<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>In Tasmania:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Street,_Hobart" title="Macquarie Street, Hobart">Macquarie Street</a>, one of the principal streets of <a href="/wiki/Hobart" title="Hobart">Hobart</a></li>
<li>Macquarie Street, one of the principal streets of the historic town of <a href="/wiki/Evandale,_Tasmania" title="Evandale, Tasmania">Evandale</a>, a town he founded in 1811</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Harbour" title="Macquarie Harbour">Macquarie Harbour</a>, on the west coast</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Macquarie_River_(Tasmania)" title="Macquarie River (Tasmania)">Macquarie River</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>In New South Wales,
<ul>
<li>Macquarie Hill, formerly known as Mount Macquarie, in <a href="/wiki/Wingecarribee_Shire" title="Wingecarribee Shire">Wingecarribee Shire</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Highlands,_New_South_Wales" title="Southern Highlands, New South Wales">Southern Highlands, New South Wales</a></li>
<li>Macquarie Pier, built in 1818 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hunter_River" title="Hunter River">Hunter River</a> for the port of Newcastle, a breakwater linking Coal Island, now known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobby%27s_Head" title="Nobby's Head">Nobby's Head</a>, to the mainland at South Head (now Fort Scratchley)</li>
<li>The Macquarie Arms Hotel at <a href="/wiki/Windsor,_New_South_Wales" title="Windsor, New South Wales">Windsor, New South Wales</a> built in 1815. It ceased operating in 1840, but reopened in 1874 and has been used continuously as a hotel ever since. Windsor also contains a Macquarie Street.</li>
<li>Lachlan Macquarie Ward, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parramatta,_New_South_Wales" title="Parramatta, New South Wales">Parramatta, New South Wales</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Many years after his governorship:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Park,_New_South_Wales" title="Macquarie Park, New South Wales">Macquarie Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Links,_New_South_Wales" title="Macquarie Links, New South Wales">Macquarie Links</a>, suburbs of Sydney.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Centre" title="Macquarie Centre">Macquarie Shopping Centre</a>, North Ryde</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Macquarie,_Australian_Capital_Territory" title="Macquarie, Australian Capital Territory">Macquarie</a>, a suburb of <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">Canberra</a>, Australia</li>
<li>Lachlan Street, Macquarie, Canberra, Australia</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Division_of_Macquarie" title="Division of Macquarie">Division of Macquarie</a>, one of the first 75 <a href="/wiki/Divisions_of_the_Australian_House_of_Representatives" title="Divisions of the Australian House of Representatives">Divisions of the Australian House of Representatives</a> created for the Australian Parliament in 1901.</li>
</ul>
