<p>In <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, the <b>Laffer curve</b> is a representation of the relationship between rates of <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">taxation</a> and the resulting levels of <a href="/wiki/Government_revenue" title="Government revenue">government revenue</a>. Proponents of the Laffer curve claim that it illustrates the concept of taxable income elasticity—i.e., <a href="/wiki/Taxable_income" title="Taxable income">taxable income</a> will change in response to changes in the rate of taxation.</p>
<p>The Laffer curve postulates that no tax revenue will be raised at the extreme tax rates of 0% and 100% and that there must be at least one rate which maximizes government taxation revenue. The Laffer curve is typically represented as a graph which starts at 0% tax with zero revenue, rises to a maximum rate of revenue at an intermediate rate of taxation, and then falls again to zero revenue at a 100% tax rate. The shape of the curve is uncertain and disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>One implication of the Laffer curve is that increasing tax rates beyond a certain point will be counter-productive for raising further tax revenue. A hypothetical Laffer curve for any given economy can only be estimated and such estimates are controversial. <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Palgrave_Dictionary_of_Economics" title="The New Palgrave Dictionary of Economics">The New Palgrave Dictionary of Economics</a></i> reports that estimates of revenue-maximizing tax rates have varied widely, with a <a href="/wiki/Mid-range" title="Mid-range">mid-range</a> of around 70%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dictionaryofeconomics_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-dictionaryofeconomics-2">[2]</a></sup> Generally, economists have found little support for the claim that tax cuts from current rates increase tax revenues or that most taxes are on the side of the Laffer curve where additional cuts could increase government revenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-howfar_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-howfar-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pecorino1995_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pecorino1995-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although economist <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Laffer" title="Arthur Laffer">Arthur Laffer</a> does not claim to have invented the Laffer curve concept,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heritage_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heritage-8">[8]</a></sup> it was popularized in the United States with policymakers following an afternoon meeting with <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Ford</a> Administration officials <a href="/wiki/Dick_Cheney" title="Dick Cheney">Dick Cheney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Donald_Rumsfeld" title="Donald Rumsfeld">Donald Rumsfeld</a> in 1974 in which he reportedly sketched the curve on a napkin to illustrate his argument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The term "Laffer curve" was coined by <a href="/wiki/Jude_Wanniski" title="Jude Wanniski">Jude Wanniski</a>, who was also present at the meeting. The basic concept was not new; Laffer himself notes antecedents in the writings of the 14th-century social philosopher <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun" title="Ibn Khaldun">Ibn Khaldun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heritage_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heritage-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_issues">Theoretical issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laffer_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Theoretical issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Justifications">Justifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laffer_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Justifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Laffer-Curve.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="751" data-file-width="933" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Laffer-Curve.svg/220px-Laffer-Curve.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Laffer-Curve.svg/330px-Laffer-Curve.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Laffer-Curve.svg/440px-Laffer-Curve.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Laffer-Curve.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Laffer curve: t* represents the rate of taxation at which maximal revenue is generated. This is the curve as drawn by Laffer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heritage_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Heritage-8">[8]</a></sup> however, the curve might not have only a single peak or be symmetrical at 50%.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Laffer explains the model in terms of two interacting effects of taxation: an "<a href="/wiki/Arithmetic" title="Arithmetic">arithmetic</a> effect" and an "economic effect."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heritage_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Heritage-8">[8]</a></sup> The "arithmetic effect" assumes that tax revenue raised is the tax rate multiplied by the revenue available for taxation (or tax base). Thus revenue R is equal to t×B where t is the tax rate and B is the taxable base (<i>R=t×B</i>). At a 0% tax rate, the model assumes that no tax revenue is raised. The "economic effect" assumes that the tax rate will affect the tax base itself. At the extreme of a 100% tax rate, the government theoretically collects zero revenue because taxpayers change their behavior in response to the tax rate: either they lose their incentive to work, or they find a way to avoid paying taxes. Thus, the "economic effect" of a 100% tax rate is to decrease the tax base to zero. If this is the case, then somewhere between 0% and 100% lies a tax rate that will maximize revenue.</p>
<p>Graphical representations of the curve sometimes appear to put the rate at around 50%, if the tax base reacts to the tax rate linearly, but the optimal rate could theoretically be <i>any</i> percentage greater than 0% and less than 100%. Similarly, the curve is often presented as a parabolic shape, but there is no reason that this is necessarily the case. The effect of changes in tax can be cased in terms of elasticities, where the optimal elasticity of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tax_base" title="Tax base">tax base</a> with respect to the tax is equal to 1. This is done by differentiating <i>R</i> with respect to <i>t</i> and grouping terms to reveal that the rate of change of <i>R</i> with respect to <i>t</i> is equal to the sum of elasticity of the tax base plus one all multiplied by the tax base. Thus as elasticity surpasses one absolute value, revenues begin to fall. The problem is similar to that of the monopolist who must never increase prices beyond the point at which the elasticity of demand exceeds one in absolute value.</p>
<p>Wanniski noted that all economic activity would be unlikely to cease at 100% taxation, but it would switch from the exchange of money to barter. He also noted that there can be special circumstances in which economic activity can continue for a period at a near 100% taxation rate (for example, in <a href="/wiki/War_economy" title="War economy">war economy</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wanniski.2C_J._10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wanniski.2C_J.-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Various efforts have been made to quantify the relationship between tax revenue and tax rates (for example, in the United States by the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Budget_Office" title="Congressional Budget Office">Congressional Budget Office</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBO_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBO-11">[11]</a></sup> While the interaction between tax rates and tax revenue is generally accepted, the precise nature of this interaction is debated. In practice, the shape of a hypothetical Laffer curve for a given economy can only be estimated. The relationship between tax rate and tax revenue is likely to vary from one economy to another and depends on the elasticity of supply for labor, as well as various other factors. Even in the same economy, the characteristics of the curve could vary over time. Complexities such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progressive_taxes" title="Progressive taxes">progressive taxes</a> and possible differences in the incentive to work for different income groups complicate the task of estimation. The structure of the curve may also be changed by policy decisions. For example, if tax loopholes and <a href="/wiki/Tax_shelter" title="Tax shelter">tax shelters</a> are made more readily available by legislation, the point at which revenue begins to decrease with increased taxation is likely to become lower.</p>
<p>Laffer presented the curve as a pedagogical device to show that in some circumstances, a reduction in tax rates will actually increase government revenue and not need to be offset by decreased government spending or increased borrowing. For a reduction in tax rates to increase revenue, the current tax rate would need to be higher than the revenue maximizing rate. In 2007, Laffer said that the curve should not be the sole basis for raising or lowering taxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems">Problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laffer_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Growth_in_US_tax_revenue_by_top_tax_rate.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Growth_in_US_tax_revenue_by_top_tax_rate.svg/220px-Growth_in_US_tax_revenue_by_top_tax_rate.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Growth_in_US_tax_revenue_by_top_tax_rate.svg/330px-Growth_in_US_tax_revenue_by_top_tax_rate.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Growth_in_US_tax_revenue_by_top_tax_rate.svg/440px-Growth_in_US_tax_revenue_by_top_tax_rate.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Growth_in_US_tax_revenue_by_top_tax_rate.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Growth in US tax revenue against the top rate of income tax</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 2012, economists surveyed by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a> rejected the viewpoint that the Laffer Curve's postulation of increased tax revenue through a rate cut applies to federal <a href="/wiki/Income_tax_in_the_United_States" title="Income tax in the United States">US income taxes</a> of the time in the medium term. When asked whether a “cut in federal income tax rates in the US right now would raise taxable income enough so that the annual total tax revenue would be higher within five years than without the tax cut,” none of the economists surveyed agreed and 71% disagreed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Laffer assumes that the government would collect no income tax at a 100% tax rate because there would be no incentive to earn income. Research has developed theoretical mathematical models in which a Laffer curve can slope continuously upwards all the way to 100%,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> but it is not clear whether or when the assumptions on which such mathematical models are based hold in real economies. Additionally, the Laffer curve depends on the assumption that tax revenue is used to provide a public good that is separable in utility and separate from labor supply, which may not be true in practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Laffer curve as presented is also simplistic in that it assumes a single tax rate and a single labor supply. Actual systems of public finance are more complex. There is serious doubt about the relevance of considering a single marginal tax rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dictionaryofeconomics_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-dictionaryofeconomics-2">[2]</a></sup> In addition, revenue may well be a <a href="/wiki/Multivalued_function" title="Multivalued function">multivalued function</a> of tax rate; for instance, an increase in tax rate to a certain percentage may not result in the same revenue as a decrease in tax rate to the same percentage (a kind of <a href="/wiki/Hysteresis" title="Hysteresis">hysteresis</a>). Furthermore, the Laffer curve does not take explicitly into account the nature of the tax avoidance taking place. It is possible that if all producers are endowed with two survival factors in the market (ability to produce efficiently and ability to avoid tax), then the revenues raised under tax avoidance can be greater than without avoidance, and thus the Laffer curve maximum is found to be farther right than thought. The reason for this result is that if producers with low productive abilities (high production costs) tend to have strong avoidance abilities as well, a uniform tax on producers actually becomes a tax that discriminates on the ability to pay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Empirical_data">Empirical data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laffer_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Empirical data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tax_rate_at_which_revenue_is_maximized">Tax rate at which revenue is maximized</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laffer_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tax rate at which revenue is maximized">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p><a href="/wiki/The_New_Palgrave_Dictionary_of_Economics" title="The New Palgrave Dictionary of Economics">The New Palgrave Dictionary of Economics</a> reports that a comparison of academic studies yields a range of revenue maximizing rates that centers around 70%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dictionaryofeconomics_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-dictionaryofeconomics-2">[2]</a></sup> In the early 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Edgar_L._Feige" title="Edgar L. Feige">Edgar L. Feige</a> and Robert T. McGee developed a macroeconomic model from which they derived a Laffer Curve. According to the model, the shape and position of the Laffer Curve depend upon the strength of supply side effects, the progressivity of the tax system and the size of the unobserved economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Economist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Pecorino&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paul Pecorino (page does not exist)">Paul Pecorino</a> presented a model in 1995 that predicted the peak of the Laffer curve occurred at tax rates around 65%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pecorino1995_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pecorino1995-7">[7]</a></sup> A draft paper by Y. Hsing looking at the United States economy between 1959 and 1991 placed the revenue-maximizing average federal tax rate between 32.67% and 35.21%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hsing1996_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hsing1996-20">[20]</a></sup> A 1981 article published in the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Political_Economy" title="Journal of Political Economy">Journal of Political Economy</a></i> presented a model integrating empirical data that indicated that the point of maximum tax revenue in Sweden in the 1970s would have been 70%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A 2011 paper by Trabandt and Uhlig published in the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Monetary_Economics" title="Journal of Monetary Economics">Journal of Monetary Economics</a></i> presented a model that predicted that the US and most European economies were on the left of the Laffer curve (in other words, that raising taxes would raise further revenue).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-howfar_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-howfar-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Congressional_Budget_Office_analysis">Congressional Budget Office analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laffer_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Congressional Budget Office analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2005, the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Budget_Office" title="Congressional Budget Office">Congressional Budget Office</a> (CBO) released a paper called "Analyzing the Economic and Budgetary Effects of a 10 Percent Cut in Income Tax Rates'. This paper considered the impact of a stylized reduction of 10% in the then existing marginal rate of <a href="/wiki/Income_tax_in_the_United_States" title="Income tax in the United States">federal income tax</a> in the US (for example, if those facing a 25% marginal federal income tax rate had it lowered to 22.5%). Unlike earlier research, the CBO paper estimates the budgetary impact of possible <a href="/wiki/Macroeconomics" title="Macroeconomics">macroeconomic</a> effects of tax policies, that is, it attempts to account for how reductions in individual income tax rates might affect the overall future growth of the economy, and therefore influence future government tax revenues; and ultimately, impact deficits or surpluses. In the paper's most generous estimated growth scenario, only 28% of the projected lost revenue from the lower tax rate would be recouped over a 10-year period after a 10% across-the-board reduction in all individual income tax rates. In other words, deficits would increase by nearly the same amount as the tax cut in the first five years, with limited feedback revenue thereafter. Through increased budget deficits, the tax cuts primarily benefiting the wealthy will be paid for—plus interest—by <a href="/wiki/Taxation_in_the_United_States" title="Taxation in the United States">taxes borne relatively evenly by all taxpayers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The paper points out that these projected shortfalls in revenue would have to be made up by federal borrowing: the paper estimates that the federal government would pay an extra US$200 <a href="/wiki/Billion" title="Billion">billion</a> in interest over the decade covered by the paper's analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBO_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-CBO-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Optimal_taxation">Optimal taxation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laffer_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Optimal taxation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Optimal_tax" title="Optimal tax">Optimal tax</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Employment_growth_by_top_tax_rate.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Employment_growth_by_top_tax_rate.svg/220px-Employment_growth_by_top_tax_rate.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Employment_growth_by_top_tax_rate.svg/330px-Employment_growth_by_top_tax_rate.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Employment_growth_by_top_tax_rate.svg/440px-Employment_growth_by_top_tax_rate.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Employment_growth_by_top_tax_rate.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Comparison of US employment growth by top income tax rate, 1940–2016.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>One of the uses of the Laffer curve is in determining the rate of taxation which will raise the maximum revenue (in other words, "optimizing" revenue collection). However, the revenue maximizing rate should not be confused with the optimal tax rate, which economists use to describe a tax which raises a given amount of revenue with the least distortions to the economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giertz_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Giertz-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laffer_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Laffer has presented the examples of Russia and the Baltic states, which instituted a <a href="/wiki/Flat_tax" title="Flat tax">flat tax</a> with rates lower than 35% around the same time that their economies started growing. He has similarly referred to the economic outcome of the <a href="/wiki/Economic_Recovery_Tax_Act_of_1981" title="Economic Recovery Tax Act of 1981">Kemp-Roth tax cuts</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kennedy_tax_cuts" title="Kennedy tax cuts">Kennedy tax cuts</a>, the 1920s tax cuts, and the changes in US <a href="/wiki/Capital_gains_tax" title="Capital gains tax">capital gains tax</a> structure in 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heritage_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Heritage-8">[8]</a></sup> Some have also cited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hauser%27s_Law" title="Hauser's Law">Hauser's Law</a>, which postulates that US federal revenues, as a percentage of GDP, have remained stable at approximately 19.5% over the period 1950 to 2007 despite changes in marginal tax rates over the same period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Others however, have called Hauser's Law "misleading" and contend that tax changes have had large effects on tax revenues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>An example of the Laffer curve affecting tax revenue was when a Victorian era <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Window_Tax" title="Window Tax">tax on windows</a>, over a span of years, resulted in grand houses being built with fewer windows, and hence a reduction in revenue from the window tax.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>More recently, based on Laffer curve arguments, Kansas Governor <a href="/wiki/Sam_Brownback" title="Sam Brownback">Sam Brownback</a> greatly reduced state tax rates in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto1_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-29">[29]</a></sup> The state, which had previously had a budget surplus, experienced a budget deficit of about $200 million in 2012. Drastic cuts to state funding for education and infrastructure have been implemented because of the budget deficits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto2_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laffer_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laffer_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:40%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>The Laffer Curve, by the way, was not invented by me. For example, Ibn Khaldun, a 14th-century philosopher, wrote in his work <i>The <a href="/wiki/Muqaddimah" title="Muqaddimah">Muqaddimah</a></i>: "It should be known that at the beginning of the dynasty, taxation yields a large revenue from small assessments. At the end of the dynasty, taxation yields a small revenue from large assessments."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;">Arthur Laffer, <i>The Laffer Curve: Past, Present, and Future</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heritage_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-Heritage-8">[8]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>The term "Laffer curve" was reportedly coined by <a href="/wiki/Jude_Wanniski" title="Jude Wanniski">Jude Wanniski</a> (a writer for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i>) after a 1974 dinner meeting at the Two Continents Restaurant in the <a href="/wiki/Hotel_Washington_(Washington,_D.C.)" title="Hotel Washington (Washington, D.C.)">Washington Hotel</a> with <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Laffer" title="Arthur Laffer">Arthur Laffer</a>, Wanniski, <a href="/wiki/Dick_Cheney" title="Dick Cheney">Dick Cheney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donald_Rumsfeld" title="Donald Rumsfeld">Donald Rumsfeld</a>, and his deputy press secretary Grace-Marie Arnett.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heritage_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-Heritage-8">[8]</a></sup> In this meeting, Laffer, arguing against President <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a>'s tax increase, reportedly sketched the curve on a napkin to illustrate the concept.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Cheney did not accept the idea immediately, but it caught the imaginations of those present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Laffer professes no recollection of this napkin, but writes: "I used the so-called Laffer Curve all the time in my classes and with anyone else who would listen to me".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heritage_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-Heritage-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Laffer himself does not claim to have invented the concept, attributing it to 14th-century Tunisian scholar Ibn Khaldun in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Muqaddimah" title="Muqaddimah">Muqaddimah</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heritage_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-Heritage-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brederode_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brederode-33">[33]</a></sup> and, more recently, to <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heritage_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-Heritage-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precedents">Precedents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laffer_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Precedents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:40%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>If the tax-payers complain to you of the heavy incidence to taxation, of any accidental calamity, of the vagaries of the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoons</a>, of the recession of the means of <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>, of floods, or destruction of their crops on account of excessive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> and if their complaints are true, then reduce their taxes. This reduction should be such that it provides them opportunities to improve their conditions and eases them of their troubles.</p>
<p>Decrease in state income due to such reasons should not depress you because the best investment for a ruler is to help his subjects at the time of their difficulties. They are the real wealth of a country and any investment on them even in the form of reduction of taxes, will be returned to the State in the shape of the prosperity of its cities and improvement of the country at large. At the same time you will be in a position to command and secure their love, respect and praises along with the revenues</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;">Ali ibn Abi Talib <i><a href="/wiki/Nahj_al-Balagha" title="Nahj al-Balagha">Nahj al-Balagha</a>, Letter 53</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Imam_Ali_Net._34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Imam_Ali_Net.-34">[34]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>There are historical precedents other than those cited directly by Laffer. For example, in 1924, Secretary of Treasury <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Mellon" title="Andrew Mellon">Andrew Mellon</a> wrote: "It seems difficult for some to understand that high rates of taxation do not necessarily mean large revenue to the government, and that more revenue may often be obtained by lower rates". Exercising his understanding that "73% of nothing is nothing", he pushed for the reduction of the top income tax bracket from 73% to an eventual 24% (as well as tax breaks for lower brackets). Mellon was one of the wealthiest people in the United States, the third-highest income-tax payer in the mid-1920s, behind <a href="/wiki/John_D._Rockefeller" title="John D. Rockefeller">John D. Rockefeller</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ford" title="Henry Ford">Henry Ford</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cannadine_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-cannadine-35">[35]</a></sup> While he served as Secretary of the U.S. Treasury Department his wealth peaked at around US$300–US$400 million. Personal income-tax receipts rose from US$719 million in 1921 to over US$1 <a href="/wiki/Billion" title="Billion">billion</a> in 1929, an average increase of 4.2% per year over an 8-year period, which supporters attribute to the rate cut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>An argument along similar lines was also advocated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ali_Ibn_Abi_Talib" title="Ali Ibn Abi Talib">Ali ibn Abi Talib</a>, the fourth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caliph" title="Caliph">Caliph</a>, in his letter to the Governor of Egypt, <a href="/wiki/Malik_al-Ashtar" title="Malik al-Ashtar">Malik al-Ashtar</a>. The arguments he made about a decrease in tax increasing both state revenues and the welfare of the citizens are similar to those cited by President <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> in the 1980s. <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a> also expressed similar arguments in his essay <i>Of Taxes</i> in 1756, as did fellow Scottish economist <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a>, twenty years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wanniski.2C_J._10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wanniski.2C_J.-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Democratic party made a similar argument in the 1880s when high revenue from import tariffs raised during the Civil War (1861–1865) led to federal budget surpluses. The Republican party, which was then based in the protectionist industrial Northeast, argued that cutting rates would lower revenues. But the Democratic party, then rooted in the agricultural South, argued tariff reductions would increase revenues by increasing the number of taxable imports.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_US_political_discourse">In US political discourse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laffer_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In US political discourse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_supply-side_economics">Use in supply-side economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laffer_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Use in supply-side economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Supply-side_economics" title="Supply-side economics">Supply-side economics</a></div>
<p>Supply-side economics is a school of macroeconomic thought that argues that overall economic well-being is maximized by lowering the barriers to producing goods and services (the "Supply Side" of the economy). By lowering such barriers, consumers are thought to benefit from a greater supply of goods and services at lower prices. Typical supply-side policy would advocate generally lower income tax and capital gains tax rates (to increase the supply of labor and capital), smaller government and a lower regulatory burden on enterprises (to lower costs). Although tax policy is often mentioned in relation to supply-side economics, supply-side economists are concerned with all impediments to the supply of goods and services and not just taxation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bartlett_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bartlett-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In their economics textbook <i>Principles of Economics</i> (7th edition), economists <a href="/wiki/Karl_E._Case" title="Karl E. Case">Karl E. Case</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wellesley_College" title="Wellesley College">Wellesley College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ray_Fair" title="Ray Fair">Ray Fair</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> stated “The Laffer curve shows the relationship between tax rates and tax revenues. Supply-side economists use it to argue that it is possible to generate higher revenues by cutting tax rates, but evidence does not appear to support this. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-feige_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-feige-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The lower tax rates by the Reagan administration decreased tax revenues significantly and contributed to the massive increase in federal debt during the 1980s.”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, Kansas Governor <a href="/wiki/Sam_Brownback" title="Sam Brownback">Sam Brownback</a> used supply-side and Laffer curve arguments to pass large cuts in state tax rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto1_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-29">[29]</a></sup> As a result, the state budget went from surplus to deficit, and the state had to implement cuts to education and infrastructure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto2_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaganomics">Reaganomics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laffer_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Reaganomics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Reaganomics" title="Reaganomics">Reaganomics</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:US_high-income_effective_tax_rates.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="549" data-file-width="744" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/US_high-income_effective_tax_rates.png/220px-US_high-income_effective_tax_rates.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/US_high-income_effective_tax_rates.png/330px-US_high-income_effective_tax_rates.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/US_high-income_effective_tax_rates.png/440px-US_high-income_effective_tax_rates.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:US_high-income_effective_tax_rates.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Average tax rate percentages for the highest-income U.S. taxpayers, 1945–2009</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Laffer curve and <a href="/wiki/Supply-side_economics" title="Supply-side economics">supply-side economics</a> inspired <a href="/wiki/Reaganomics" title="Reaganomics">Reaganomics</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kemp-Roth_Tax_Cut" title="Kemp-Roth Tax Cut">Kemp-Roth Tax Cut</a> of 1981. Supply-side advocates of tax cuts claimed that lower tax rates would generate more tax revenue because the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_government" title="United States government">United States government</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marginal_tax_rate" title="Marginal tax rate">marginal income tax rates</a> prior to the legislation were on the <i>right-hand</i> side of the curve. As a successful actor, Reagan himself had been subject to marginal tax rates as high as 90% during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. During the Reagan presidency, the top marginal rate of tax in the United States fell from 70% to 31%. According to the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Management_and_Budget" title="Office of Management and Budget">Office of Management and Budget</a> (OMB) historical data, federal government revenue as a percentage of GDP fell from 19.0% of GDP in 1980 to 18.4% by 1989. However, absolute revenues nearly doubled during the same time period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/David_Stockman" title="David Stockman">David Stockman</a>, Ronald Reagan's budget director during his first administration and one of the early proponents of supply-side economics, was concerned that the administration did not pay enough attention to cutting government spending. He maintained that the Laffer curve was not to be taken literally—at least not in the economic environment of the 1980s United States. In <i>The Triumph of Politics</i>, he writes: "[T]he whole California gang had taken [the Laffer curve] literally (and primitively). The way they talked, they seemed to expect that once the supply-side tax cut was in effect, additional revenue would start to fall, manna-like, from the heavens. Since January, I had been explaining that there is no literal Laffer curve."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Stockman also said that "Laffer wasn't wrong, he just didn't go far enough" (in paying attention to government spending).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantic_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some have criticized elements of Reaganomics on the basis of equity. For example, economist <a href="/wiki/John_Kenneth_Galbraith" title="John Kenneth Galbraith">John Kenneth Galbraith</a> believed that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reagan_administration" title="Reagan administration">Reagan administration</a> actively used the Laffer curve "to lower taxes on the affluent".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galbraith_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Galbraith-45">[45]</a></sup> Some critics point out that tax revenues almost always rise every year, and during Reagan's two terms increases in tax revenue were more shallow than increases during presidencies where top marginal tax rates were higher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Critics also point out that since the Reagan tax cuts, <a href="/wiki/Income_inequality_in_the_United_States" title="Income inequality in the United States">income has not significantly increased</a> for the rest of the population. This assertion is supported by studies that show the income of the top 1% nearly doubling during the Reagan years, while income for other income levels increased only marginally; income actually decreased for the bottom quintile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cumulative_Growth_In_Average_After-Tax_Income.2C_By_Income_Group.3B_graph.2C_page_19_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cumulative_Growth_In_Average_After-Tax_Income.2C_By_Income_Group.3B_graph.2C_page_19-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bush_tax_cuts">Bush tax cuts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laffer_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Bush tax cuts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bush_tax_cuts" title="Bush tax cuts">Bush tax cuts</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Economic_Growth_and_Tax_Relief_Reconciliation_Act_of_2001" title="Economic Growth and Tax Relief Reconciliation Act of 2001">Economic Growth and Tax Relief Reconciliation Act of 2001</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jobs_and_Growth_Tax_Relief_Reconciliation_Act_of_2003" title="Jobs and Growth Tax Relief Reconciliation Act of 2003">Jobs and Growth Tax Relief Reconciliation Act of 2003</a></div>
<p>The Congressional Budget Office has estimated that extending the <a href="/wiki/Bush_tax_cuts" title="Bush tax cuts">Bush tax cuts</a> of 2001–2003 beyond their 2010 expiration would increase deficits by $1.8 trillion over the following decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Economist Paul Krugman contended that supply-side adherents did not fully believe that the United States income tax rate was on the "backwards-sloping" side of the curve and yet they still advocated lowering taxes to encourage investment of personal savings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
