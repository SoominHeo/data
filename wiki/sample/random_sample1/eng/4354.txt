<p><b>Randall Garrett</b> (December 16, 1927 – December 31, 1987) was an American <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a> author. He was a prolific contributor to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astounding" title="Astounding">Astounding</a></i> and other science fiction magazines of the 1950s and 1960s. He instructed <a href="/wiki/Robert_Silverberg" title="Robert Silverberg">Robert Silverberg</a> in the techniques of selling large quantities of action-adventure science fiction, and collaborated with him on two novels about Earth bringing civilization to an alien planet.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography_and_writing_career">Biography and writing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Randall_Garrett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography and writing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Randall_Garrett_-_Unwise_Child_-_book_cover.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="412" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Randall_Garrett_-_Unwise_Child_-_book_cover.jpg/220px-Randall_Garrett_-_Unwise_Child_-_book_cover.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Randall_Garrett_-_Unwise_Child_-_book_cover.jpg/330px-Randall_Garrett_-_Unwise_Child_-_book_cover.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/38/Randall_Garrett_-_Unwise_Child_-_book_cover.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Randall_Garrett_-_Unwise_Child_-_book_cover.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cover of <i>Unwise Child</i> by Garrett</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Garrett is best known for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Darcy_(fiction)" title="Lord Darcy (fiction)">Lord Darcy</a> books, the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Too_Many_Magicians" title="Too Many Magicians">Too Many Magicians</a></i> and two short story collections, set in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alternate_history_(fiction)" title="Alternate history (fiction)">alternate world</a> where a joint Anglo-French empire still led by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plantagenet" title="Plantagenet">Plantagenet</a> dynasty has survived into the twentieth century and where magic works and has been scientifically codified. The Darcy books are rich in jokes, puns, and references (particularly to works of <a href="/wiki/Detective_fiction" title="Detective fiction">detective</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spy_fiction" title="Spy fiction">spy fiction</a>: Lord Darcy is himself partially modelled on <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a>), elements that often appear in the shorter works about the detective. <a href="/wiki/Michael_Kurland" title="Michael Kurland">Michael Kurland</a> wrote two additional Lord Darcy novels.</p>
<p>Garrett wrote under a variety of <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonyms</a> including: David Gordon, John Gordon, Darrel T. Langart (an <a href="/wiki/Anagram" title="Anagram">anagram</a> of his name), Alexander Blade, Richard Greer, Ivar Jorgensen, Clyde Mitchell, Leonard G. Spencer, S. M. Tenneshaw, Gerald Vance. He was also a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Creative_Anachronism" title="Society for Creative Anachronism">Society for Creative Anachronism</a>, as "Randall of Hightower" (a pun on "garret"). The short novel <i>Brain Twister</i>, written by Garrett in conjunction with author <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Janifer" title="Laurence Janifer">Laurence Janifer</a> (using the joint pseudonym <a href="/wiki/Mark_Phillips_(author)" title="Mark Phillips (author)">Mark Phillips</a>) was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award_for_Best_Novel" title="Hugo Award for Best Novel">Hugo Award for Best Novel</a> in 1960.</p>
<p>An inveterate punster (defining a pun as "the odor given off by a decaying mind"), he was a favorite guest at science fiction conventions and friend to many fans, especially in Southern California. According to various anecdotes in a tribute volume, Garrett was a sexual predator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Best_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Best-1">[1]</a></sup> He introduced himself to <a href="/wiki/Marion_Zimmer_Bradley" title="Marion Zimmer Bradley">Marion Zimmer Bradley</a> with an obscene Latin phrase which left her speechless and to a pregnant <a href="/wiki/Anne_McCaffrey" title="Anne McCaffrey">Anne McCaffrey</a> with "sly innuendoes" which horrified her. <a href="/wiki/Philip_Jos%C3%A9_Farmer" title="Philip José Farmer">Philip José Farmer</a> recounted an anecdote where Garrett was punched by his then-wife for having a pair of lace underpants in his pocket, and later ran naked through a hotel after being caught having sex with another woman in the wrong room. <a href="/wiki/Frank_Herbert" title="Frank Herbert">Frank Herbert</a> said "You could follow his movements around this <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Creative_Anachronism" title="Society for Creative Anachronism">creative Anachronists'</a> picnic by the squeals of the women whose bottoms he had just pinched." <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a> referred to Garrett's offending <a href="/wiki/Judith_Merril" title="Judith Merril">Judith Merril</a> enough that she emptied an ashtray over his head.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Best_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Best-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Garrett suffered an attack of <a href="/wiki/Encephalitis" title="Encephalitis">encephalitis</a> in the summer of 1979; he spent the last 8 years of his life in a <a href="/wiki/Coma" title="Coma">coma</a>.</p>
<p>In 1999, Randall Garrett won the <a href="/wiki/Sidewise_Award_for_Alternate_History" title="Sidewise Award for Alternate History">Sidewise Award for Alternate History</a> Special Achievement Award for the Lord Darcy series.</p>
<p>He was also ordained in the <a href="/wiki/Old_Catholic_Church" title="Old Catholic Church">Old Catholic Church</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Glen_Cook" title="Glen Cook">Glen Cook</a>'s private detective character <a href="/wiki/Garrett_P.I." title="Garrett P.I.">Garrett P.I.</a> is named in honor of Garrett.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Parodies_Tossed">Parodies Tossed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Randall_Garrett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Parodies Tossed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>"Parodies Tossed" was a feature of Columbia Publications' Science Fiction Stories and Future Science Fiction.</p>
<ul>
<li>"All About 'The Thing'" (1956) a.k.a. "Parodies Tossed" (1956)</li>
<li>"John W. Campbell's 'Who Goes There?'" (1956)</li>
<li>"Isaac Asimov's 'The Caves of Steel'" (1956)</li>

<li>"Parodies Tossed: Isaac Asimov's 'The Caves of Steel'" (1956)</li>
<li>"Parodies Tossed: James Blish and Michael Sherman's 'The Duplicated Man'" (1956) a.k.a. "James Blish and Michael Sherman's 'The Duplicated Man': A Review in Verse" (1956)</li>
<li>"Parodies Tossed: L. Sprague de Camp's 'Lest Darkness Fall'" (1956) a.k.a. "L. Sprague de Camp's 'Lest Darkness Fall'" (1956)</li>
<li>The last "Parodies Tossed: Immortality, C.O.D." (by Bret Hooper) review of Robert Sheckley's "Immortality Delivered" (June, 1960, final issue of Science Fiction Stories)</li>
<li>"Poul Anderson's 'Three Hearts and Three Lions': A Calypso in Search of a Rhyme" (1979) with Vicki Ann Heydron (wife)</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poems">Poems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Randall_Garrett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Poems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

</ul>
<p>The collection <i>Takeoff Too</i> included a poem, which the editor titled "The Egyptian Diamond", which was erroneously credited to Garrett. It was actually written by Jack Bennett and originally published under the title "Ben Ali the Egyptian".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-egyptian_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-egyptian-5">[5]</a></sup> Parts of "Ben Ali the Egyptian" <i>were</i> quoted in Garrett's short story "The Foreign Hand Tie."</p>
