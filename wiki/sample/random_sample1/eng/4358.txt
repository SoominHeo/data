<p><b>Operation <i>Ranch Hand</i></b> was a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">U.S. military</a> operation during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, lasting from 1962 until 1971. Largely inspired by the British use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2,4,5-T" title="2,4,5-T">2,4,5-T</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2,4-D" title="2,4-D">2,4-D</a> (<a href="/wiki/Agent_Orange" title="Agent Orange">Agent Orange</a>) during the <a href="/wiki/Malayan_Emergency" title="Malayan Emergency">Malayan Emergency</a> in the 1950s, it was part of the overall <a href="/wiki/Herbicidal_warfare" title="Herbicidal warfare">herbicidal warfare</a> program during the war called "Operation Trail Dust". <i>Ranch Hand</i> involved spraying an estimated 20 million U.S. gallons (76,000 m<sup>3</sup>) of <a href="/wiki/Defoliant" title="Defoliant">defoliants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicides</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-1">[1]</a></sup> over rural areas of <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnam" title="South Vietnam">South Vietnam</a> in an attempt to deprive the Viet Cong of <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a> cover. Areas of <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a> were also sprayed to a lesser extent. Nearly 20,000 <a href="/wiki/Sortie" title="Sortie">sorties</a> were flown between 1961 and 1971.</p>
<p>The "Ranch Handers" motto was "Only you can prevent a forest"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-1">[1]</a></sup> – a take on the popular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Forest_Service" title="U.S. Forest Service">U.S. Forest Service</a> poster <a href="/wiki/Slogan" title="Slogan">slogan</a> of <a href="/wiki/Smokey_Bear" title="Smokey Bear">Smokey Bear</a>. During the ten years of <a href="/wiki/Aerial_application" title="Aerial application">spraying</a>, over 5 million acres (20,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of forest and 500,000 acres (2,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of crops were heavily damaged or destroyed. Around 20% of the <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a> of South Vietnam were sprayed at least once.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The herbicides were sprayed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> flying <a href="/wiki/Fairchild_C-123_Provider" title="Fairchild C-123 Provider">C-123s</a> using the call sign "Hades". The planes were fitted with specially developed spray tanks with a capacity of 1,000 U.S. gallons (4 m<sup>3</sup>) of herbicides. A plane sprayed a swath of land that was 80 meters wide and 16 km (≈10 miles) long in about 4½ minutes, at a rate of about 3 U.S. gallons per acre (3 m<sup>3</sup>/km<sup>2</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Sorties usually consisted of three to five <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplanes</a> flying side by side. 95% of the herbicides and defoliants used in the war were sprayed by the U.S. Air Force as part of Operation <i>Ranch Hand</i>. The remaining 5% were sprayed by the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Chemical_Corps" title="Chemical Corps">Chemical Corps</a>, other military branches, and the <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnam" title="South Vietnam">Republic of Vietnam</a> using hand sprayers, spray trucks, <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">boats</a>, primarily around U.S. <a href="/wiki/Military_base" title="Military base">military installations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stellman_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stellman-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Defoliants">Defoliants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Ranch_Hand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Defoliants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GVN_Herbicide.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="726" data-file-width="439" height="364" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/GVN_Herbicide.png/220px-GVN_Herbicide.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/GVN_Herbicide.png/330px-GVN_Herbicide.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6b/GVN_Herbicide.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The herbicides used were sprayed at up to 50 times the concentration than for normal agricultural use.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The most common herbicide used was Herbicide Orange, more commonly referred to as <a href="/wiki/Agent_Orange" title="Agent Orange">Agent Orange</a>: a fifty-fifty mixture of two herbicides <a href="/wiki/2,4-Dichlorophenoxyacetic_acid" title="2,4-Dichlorophenoxyacetic acid">2,4-D</a> (2,4-dichlorophenoxyacetic acid) and <a href="/wiki/2,4,5-Trichlorophenoxyacetic_acid" title="2,4,5-Trichlorophenoxyacetic acid">2,4,5-T</a> (2,4,5-trichlorophenoxyacetic acid) manufactured for the U.S. Department of Defense primarily by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monsanto_Corporation" title="Monsanto Corporation">Monsanto Corporation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dow_Chemical" title="Dow Chemical">Dow Chemical</a>. The other most common color-coded <i>Ranch Hand</i> herbicides were <a href="/wiki/Agent_Blue" title="Agent Blue">Agent Blue</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cacodylic_acid" title="Cacodylic acid">cacodylic acid</a>) that was primarily used against food crops, and <a href="/wiki/Agent_White" title="Agent White">Agent White</a> (<a href="/wiki/Picloram" title="Picloram">picloram</a>) which was often used when Agent Orange was not available.</p>
<p>The Agents used—known as the <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_Herbicides" title="Rainbow Herbicides">Rainbow Herbicides</a>—their active ingredients, and years used were as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Agent_Green" title="Agent Green">Agent Green</a>: 100% <i>n</i>-butyl ester 2,4,5-T, used prior to 1966<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young2009_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Young2009-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Agent_Pink" title="Agent Pink">Agent Pink</a>: 100% 2,4,5-T (60% <i>n</i>-butyl ester 2,4,5-T, and 40% iso-butyl ester of 2,4,5-T) used prior to 1966<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young2009_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Young2009-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Agent_Purple" title="Agent Purple">Agent Purple</a>: 50% 2,4,5-T (30% <i>n</i>-butyl ester of 2,4,5-T, and 20% iso-butyl ester of 2,4,5-T) and 50% <i>n</i>-butyl ester of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2,4-D" title="2,4-D">2,4-D</a> used 1961–65</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Agent_Blue" title="Agent Blue">Agent Blue</a> (Phytar 560G): 65.6% organic <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">Arsenicical</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cacodylic_acid" title="Cacodylic acid">cacodylic acid</a> (Ansar 138) and its sodium salt <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium_cacodylate" title="Sodium cacodylate">sodium cacodylate</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young2009_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Young2009-6">[6]</a></sup> used from 1962–71 in powder and water solution<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HerbicidesMedicine1994_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-HerbicidesMedicine1994-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Agent_White" title="Agent White">Agent White</a> (Tordon 101): 21.2% (acid weight basis) <a href="/wiki/Triisopropanolamine" title="Triisopropanolamine">triisopropanolamine</a> <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> of 2,4-D and 5.7% <a href="/wiki/Picloram" title="Picloram">picloram</a> used 1966–71<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young2009_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Young2009-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HerbicidesMedicine1994_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-HerbicidesMedicine1994-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Agent_Orange" title="Agent Orange">Agent Orange</a> or Herbicide Orange, (HO): 50% <i>n</i>-butyl ester 2,4-D and 50% <i>n</i>-butyl ester 2,4,5-T used 1965–70</li>
<li>Agent Orange II:50% n-butyl ester 2,4-D and 50% <a href="/wiki/2,2,4-Trimethylpentane" title="2,2,4-Trimethylpentane">isooctyl</a> ester 2,4,5-T used after 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bull2004_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bull2004-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vallero2011_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vallero2011-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Agent Orange III: 66.6% n-butyl 2,4-D and 33.3% n-butyl ester 2,4,5-T.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Detrick_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Detrick-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>Enhanced Agent Orange, Orange Plus, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_Orange" title="Super Orange">Super Orange</a> (SO), or DOW Herbicide M-3393: standardized Agent Orange mixture of 2,4-D and 2,4,5-T combined with an oil-based mixture of <a href="/wiki/Picloram" title="Picloram">picloram</a>, a proprietary DOW Chemical product called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tordon_101" title="Tordon 101">Tordon 101</a>, an ingredient of Agent White.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SuperOrange_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-SuperOrange-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOW_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-DOW-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The herbicides were procured by the U.S. military from <a href="/wiki/Dow_Chemical_Company" title="Dow Chemical Company">Dow Chemical Company</a> (all but Blue), <a href="/wiki/Monsanto" title="Monsanto">Monsanto</a> (Orange, Purple and Pink), <a href="/wiki/Hercules_Inc." title="Hercules Inc.">Hercules Inc.</a> (Orange and Purple), Thompson-Hayward Chemical Company (Orange and Pink), <a href="/wiki/Diamond_Alkali" title="Diamond Alkali">Diamond Alkali</a>/Shamrock Company (Orange, Blue, Purple and Pink), <a href="/wiki/United_States_Rubber_Company" title="United States Rubber Company">United States Rubber Company</a> (Orange), Thompson Chemicals Corporation (Orange and Pink), Agrisect Company (Orange and Purple), Hoffman-Taft Inc. (Orange), and the <a href="/wiki/Ansul" title="Ansul">Ansul</a> Chemical Company (Blue).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young2009_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Young2009-6">[6]</a></sup> In April 1967, the USA's entire production of 2,4,5-T was confiscated by the military; foreign sources were also tapped into, including the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Chemical_Industries" title="Imperial Chemical Industries">Imperial Chemical Industries</a> (ICI).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schnibben_1992_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schnibben_1992-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>65% of the herbicides used contained <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2,4,5-trichlorophenoxyacetic_acid" title="2,4,5-trichlorophenoxyacetic acid">2,4,5-trichlorophenoxyacetic acid</a> that was contaminated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2,3,7,8-tetrachlorodibenzodioxin" title="2,3,7,8-tetrachlorodibenzodioxin">2,3,7,8-tetrachlorodibenzodioxin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stellman_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stellman-4">[4]</a></sup> a "<i>known human carcinogen</i>...by several different routes of exposure, including <a href="/wiki/Oral_administration" title="Oral administration">oral</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dermal" title="Dermal">dermal</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intraperitoneal" title="Intraperitoneal">intraperitoneal</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> About 12 million U.S. gallons (45,000 m<sup>3</sup>) of <a href="/wiki/Dioxins_and_dioxin-like_compounds" title="Dioxins and dioxin-like compounds">dioxin</a>-contaminated herbicides were sprayed over Southeast Asia during American combat operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pellow_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-pellow-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005, a <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_in_the_Vietnam_War#Agent_Orange" title="New Zealand in the Vietnam War">New Zealand</a> government minister was quoted and widely reported as saying that Agent Orange chemicals had been supplied from New Zealand to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">United States military</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">conflict</a>. Shortly after, the same minister claimed to have been mis-quoted, although this point was less widely reported. From 1962 to 1987, <i>2,4,5T</i> herbicide had been manufactured at an Ivon Watkins-Dow plant in <a href="/wiki/New_Plymouth" title="New Plymouth">New Plymouth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Ranch_Hand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For most of the war, Operation <i>Ranch Hand</i> was based at <a href="/wiki/Bien_Hoa_Air_Base" title="Bien Hoa Air Base">Bien Hoa Air Base</a> (1966–1970), for operations in the <a href="/wiki/Mekong_Delta" title="Mekong Delta">Mekong Delta</a> region where <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Patrol_boat" title="Patrol boat">patrol boats</a> were vulnerable to attack from areas of <a href="/wiki/Undergrowth" title="Undergrowth">undergrowth</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Shore" title="Shore">water's edge</a>. Storage, mixing, loading, and washing areas and a parking ramp were located just off the base's inside taxiway between the Hot Cargo Ramp and the <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control#Airport_control" title="Air traffic control">control tower</a>. For operations along the central coast and the <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh_trail" title="Ho Chi Minh trail">Ho Chi Minh trail</a> regions, <i>Ranch Hand</i> operated out of <a href="/wiki/Da_Nang_Air_Base" title="Da Nang Air Base">Da Nang Air Base</a> (1964–71). Other bases of operation included <a href="/wiki/Ph%C3%B9_C%C3%A1t_Air_Base" title="Phù Cát Air Base">Phù Cát Air Base</a> (1968–1970), <a href="/wiki/Tan_Son_Nhut_Air_Base" title="Tan Son Nhut Air Base">Tan Son Nhut Air Base</a> (1962–66), <a href="/wiki/Nha_Trang_Air_Base" title="Nha Trang Air Base">Nha Trang Air Base</a> (1968–69), <a href="/wiki/Phan_Rang_Air_Base" title="Phan Rang Air Base">Phan Rang Air Base</a> (1970–72), and <a href="/wiki/Tuy_Hoa_Air_Base" title="Tuy Hoa Air Base">Tuy Hoa Air Base</a> (1971–72).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Other bases were also used as temporary staging areas for <i>Ranch Hand</i>. The Da Nang, Bien Hoa and Phu Cat Air bases are still heavily contaminated with <a href="/wiki/Dioxins_and_dioxin-like_compounds" title="Dioxins and dioxin-like compounds">dioxin</a> from the herbicides, and have been placed on a priority list for containment and clean-up by the Vietnamese government.</p>
<p>The first aerial spraying of herbicides was a test run conducted on 10 August 1961 in a village north of <a href="/wiki/%C4%90%E1%BA%AFk_T%C3%B4" title="Đắk Tô">Đắk Tô</a> against <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">foliage</a>. Testing continued over the next year and even though there was doubt in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">State Department</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">The Pentagon</a> and the White House as to the efficacy of the herbicides, Operation <i>Ranch Hand</i> began in early 1962. Individual spray runs had to be approved by <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> until November 1962, when Kennedy gave the authority to approve most spray runs to the <a href="/wiki/Military_Assistance_Command,_Vietnam" title="Military Assistance Command, Vietnam">Military Assistance Command, Vietnam</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassador_to_South_Vietnam" title="United States Ambassador to South Vietnam">U.S. Ambassador to South Vietnam</a>. <i>Ranch Hand</i> was given final approval to spray targets in eastern Laos in December 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The issue of whether or not to allow <a href="/wiki/Crop_destruction" title="Crop destruction">crop destruction</a> was under great debate due to its potential of violating the <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Protocol" title="Geneva Protocol">Geneva Protocol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> However, American officials pointed out that the British had previously used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2,4,5-T" title="2,4,5-T">2,4,5-T</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2,4-D" title="2,4-D">2,4-D</a> (virtually identical to America's use in Vietnam) on a large scale throughout the <a href="/wiki/Malayan_Emergency" title="Malayan Emergency">Malayan Emergency</a> in the 1950s in order to destroy bushes, crops, and trees in effort to deny communist insurgents the cover they needed to ambush passing convoys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Indeed, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Secretary_of_State" title="U.S. Secretary of State">Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/Dean_Rusk" title="Dean Rusk">Dean Rusk</a> told <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._President" title="U.S. President">President</a> <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> on November 24, 1961, that "[t]he use of defoliant does not violate any rule of <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a> concerning the conduct of chemical warfare and is an accepted tactic of war. Precedent has been established by the British during the emergency in Malaya in their use of aircraft for destroying crops by chemical spraying."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The president of South Vietnam, <a href="/wiki/Ngo_Dinh_Diem" title="Ngo Dinh Diem">Ngo Dinh Diem</a> began to push the <a href="/wiki/Military_Assistance_Advisory_Group" title="Military Assistance Advisory Group">U.S. Military Advisory Group</a> in Vietnam and the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> to begin crop destruction in September 1961, but it was not until October 1962 when the White House gave approval for limited testing of Agent Blue against crops in an area believed to be controlled by the <a href="/wiki/Viet_Cong" title="Viet Cong">Viet Cong</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Soon after, crop destruction became an integral part of the <i>Ranch Hand</i> program.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Targeting_(warfare)" title="Targeting (warfare)">Targets</a> for the spray runs were carefully selected to satisfy the strategic and <a href="/wiki/Psychological_warfare" title="Psychological warfare">psychological operations</a> goals of the U.S. and South Vietnamese military. Spray runs were surveyed to pinpoint the target area and then placed on a priority list. Due to the low <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> (ideally 150 feet (46 m)) required for spraying, the C-123s were escorted by <a href="/wiki/Fighter_aircraft" title="Fighter aircraft">fighter</a> <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplanes</a> that would <a href="/wiki/Strafing" title="Strafing">strafe</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aerial_bomb" title="Aerial bomb">bomb</a> the target area in order to draw out any <a href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_warfare" title="Anti-aircraft warfare">ground fire</a> if the area was believed to be 'hot'. Spray runs were planned to enable as straight a run as possible to limit the amount of time the planes flew at low altitude. Data on the spray runs, their targets, the herbicide used and amount used, weather conditions and other details were recorded and later put into a database called the Herbicide Reporting System (HERBS) tapes.</p>
<p>The effectiveness of the spraying was influenced by many factors including weather and terrain. Spray runs occurred during the early morning hours before temperatures rose above 85 degrees and the winds picked up. <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">Mangroves</a> in the Delta region required only one spraying and did not survive once defoliated, whereas dense forests in the uplands required two or more spray runs. Within two to three weeks of spraying, the leaves would drop from the trees, which would remain bare until the next rainy season. In order to defoliate the lower stories of forest cover, one or more follow-up spray runs were needed. About 10 percent of the trees sprayed died from a single spray run. Multiple spraying resulted in increased mortality for the trees, as did following up the herbicide missions with <a href="/wiki/Napalm" title="Napalm">napalm</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aerial_bomb" title="Aerial bomb">bombing</a> <a href="/wiki/Airstrike" title="Airstrike">strikes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_community_reaction">Scientific community reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Ranch_Hand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scientific community reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The use of herbicides in the Vietnam War was controversial from the beginning, particularly for crop destruction. The scientific community began to protest the use of herbicides in Vietnam as early as 1964, when the <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_American_Scientists" title="Federation of American Scientists">Federation of American Scientists</a> objected to the use of defoliants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-27">[27]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/American_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="American Association for the Advancement of Science">American Association for the Advancement of Science</a> (AAAS) issued a resolution in 1966 calling for a field investigation of the herbicide program in Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-27">[27]</a></sup> In 1967, seventeen <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel laureates</a> and 5,000 other <a href="/wiki/Scientist" title="Scientist">scientists</a> signed a <a href="/wiki/Petition" title="Petition">petition</a> asking for the immediate end to the use of herbicides in Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/News_media" title="News media">Press</a> coverage of the controversial use of herbicides in Vietnam increased in the late 1960s.</p>
<p>In 1970, AAAS sent a team of scientists—the Herbicide Assessment Commission (HAC) consisting of Matthew Meselson, Arthur Westing, John Constable, and Robert Cook—to conduct field tests of the <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> impacts of the herbicide program in Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-27">[27]</a></sup> A 1969 report authored by K. Diane Courtney and others found that 2,4,5-T could cause <a href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">birth defects</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stillbirth" title="Stillbirth">stillbirths</a> in mice. This and follow-up studies led the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">U.S. government</a> to restrict the use of 2,4,5-T in the U.S. in April 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_27-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-27">[27]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> followed suit by 'temporarily' suspending the use of Agent Orange in Vietnam, though they continued to rely on Agent White for defoliation until supplies ran out and the last defoliation spray run took place on 9 May 1970.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Sporadic crop destruction sorties using Agent Blue and Agent White continued throughout 1970 until the final <i>Ranch Hand</i> run was flown on 7 January 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_27-5"><a href="#cite_note-:0-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
