<p><b>Lamesa</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i>lə-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">MEE</span></span></b>-sə</i></a></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> is a city in and the <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">county seat</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dawson_County,_Texas" title="Dawson County, Texas">Dawson County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR6_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR6-4">[4]</a></sup> The population was 9,422 at the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 census</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_2010_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Census_2010-5">[5]</a></sup> down from 9,952 at the 2000 census. Located south of <a href="/wiki/Lubbock,_Texas" title="Lubbock, Texas">Lubbock</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Llano_Estacado" title="Llano Estacado">Llano Estacado</a>, Lamesa was founded in 1903. Most of the economy is based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cattle_ranching" title="Cattle ranching">cattle ranching</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton farming</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Preston_Smith_(governor)" title="Preston Smith (governor)">Preston E. Smith</a> <a href="/wiki/Prison" title="Prison">prison</a> unit, named for the former <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Texas" title="Governor of Texas">governor of Texas</a>, is located just outside Lamesa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamesa,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lamesa is located in the center of Dawson County at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Lamesa%2C_Texas&amp;params=32_44_4_N_101_57_29_W_type:city"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">32°44′4″N</span> <span class="longitude">101°57′29″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">32.73444°N 101.95806°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">32.73444; -101.95806</span></span></span></a></span> (32.734439, -101.958190).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR1-7">[7]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Highway_87" title="U.S. Highway 87">U.S. Highway 87</a> (Lynn Avenue) passes through the eastern side of the city, leading north 61 miles (98 km) to <a href="/wiki/Lubbock,_Texas" title="Lubbock, Texas">Lubbock</a> and southeast 44 miles (71 km) to <a href="/wiki/Big_Spring,_Texas" title="Big Spring, Texas">Big Spring</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Highway_180" title="U.S. Highway 180">U.S. Highway 180</a> passes through the center of town as 4th Street and leads west 41 miles (66 km) to <a href="/wiki/Seminole,_Texas" title="Seminole, Texas">Seminole</a> and east 62 miles (100 km) to <a href="/wiki/Snyder,_Texas" title="Snyder, Texas">Snyder</a>. <a href="/wiki/Texas_State_Highway_137" title="Texas State Highway 137">Texas State Highway 137</a> passes through the city as Bryan Avenue and leads northwest 38 miles (61 km) to <a href="/wiki/Brownfield,_Texas" title="Brownfield, Texas">Brownfield</a> and south 45 miles (72 km) to <a href="/wiki/Stanton,_Texas" title="Stanton, Texas">Stanton</a>. <a href="/wiki/Texas_State_Highway_349" title="Texas State Highway 349">Texas State Highway 349</a> branches off Highway 137 south of Lamesa and leads southwest 55 miles (89 km) to <a href="/wiki/Midland,_Texas" title="Midland, Texas">Midland</a>.</p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city has a total area of 5.0 square miles (13.0 km<sup>2</sup>), of which 0.03 square miles (0.08 km<sup>2</sup>), or 0.62%, is water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_2010_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Census_2010-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dal_Paso_Museum">Dal Paso Museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamesa,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dal Paso Museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Dal Paso Museum, a collection of local artifacts housed in an impressive former hotel, is located in downtown Lamesa. The name is derived from the fact that Lamesa is located on the table land of the Staked Plains. On display are home furnishings, pioneer tools, and ranch and farm equipment. There are also exhibits by local artists. The museum, at 306 South First Street, has limited afternoon hours to the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR2_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-1">[1]</a></sup> of 2000, there were 9,952 people, 3,696 households, and 2,679 families resideing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 2,080.8 people per square mile (803.9/km²). There were 4,270 housing units at an average density of 892.8 per square mile (344.9/km²). The racial makeup of the city was 41.9% White non-Hispanic, 4.2% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 0.7% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 0.19% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 19.51% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 2.13% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 52.96% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 3,696 households of which 34.4% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 56.5% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 12.2% had a female householder with no husband present, and 27.5% were not families. 25.5% of all households were made up of individuals and 14.6% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.66 and the average family size was 3.20.</p>
<p>In the city the population was spread out with 29.7% under the age of 18, 8.0% from 18 to 24, 24.4% from 25 to 44, 20.4% from 45 to 64, and 17.4% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 36 years. For every 100 females, there were 90.1 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 83.3 males.</p>
<p>The median income for a household in the city was $27,362, and the median income for a family was $31,556. Males had a median income of $26,393 versus $16,826 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $16,211. About 18.1% of families and 21.9% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 33.4% of those under age 18 and 12.9% of those age 65 or over.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamesa,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lamesa is served by the <a href="/wiki/Lamesa_Independent_School_District" title="Lamesa Independent School District">Lamesa Independent School District</a>, which includes <a href="/wiki/Lamesa_High_School" title="Lamesa High School">Lamesa High School</a> and Lamesa Middle School, whose school mascots are the Golden Tornadoes and the Whirlwinds, respectively.</p>
<p>A branch of <a href="/wiki/Howard_College" title="Howard College">Howard College</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Community_college" title="Community college">community college</a> in <a href="/wiki/Big_Spring,_Texas" title="Big Spring, Texas">Big Spring</a>, is located in Lamesa.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Miscellany">Miscellany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamesa,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Miscellany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the last weekend of April, Lamesa hosts the annual Chicken-fried Steak Cook-off. Lamesa has been called "the birthplace of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicken-fried_steak" title="Chicken-fried steak">chicken-fried steak</a>", but the reporter who made the designation later confessed that the claim is fictional. Nevertheless, in 2011, <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Texas" title="Governor of Texas">Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Rick_Perry" title="Rick Perry">Rick Perry</a> declared Lamesa the home of the chicken-fried steak. In the 2013 competition, Mayor Dave Nix teamed with city councilman Greg Hughes as contestants. The community event attracted 65 sponsors and 104 booths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Entrada_al_Pacifico" title="La Entrada al Pacifico">La Entrada al Pacifico</a> is an international trade corridor that begins in <a href="/wiki/Topolobampo" title="Topolobampo">Topolobampo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, runs through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midland-Odessa_metropolitan_area" title="Midland-Odessa metropolitan area">Midland-Odessa</a> and ends in Lamesa (according to the legal definition).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Lamesa's Sky-Vue Drive-In Theater (established in 1948) is well-known regionally. It has been closed since a kitchen fire destroyed the snack bar on November 27, 2015. It was one of only 14 remaining <a href="/wiki/Drive-in_theater" title="Drive-in theater">drive-in theaters</a> in Texas. Others are in Lubbock and <a href="/wiki/Clarendon,_Texas" title="Clarendon, Texas">Clarendon</a>. Before he became famous, musician <a href="/wiki/Buddy_Holly" title="Buddy Holly">Buddy Holly</a> performed on the roof of the Sky Vue's projector building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The theater was also used as cover art and named in the title of country music album <i>Down at the Sky-Vue Drive-In</i> by country music artist <a href="/wiki/Don_Walser" title="Don Walser">Don Walser</a>. Additionally, "Hot Rod Mercury", track #2 from the album, sings about life in Lamesa. Lamesa also has an indoor movie theater, Movieland, which has two screens. <b>The Wall</b> is an edifice on which graduating seniors of Lamesa High School spray-paint their names onto the wall until next year's class adds its own graffiti on top.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_series" title="Television series">television series</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Dallas_(1978_TV_series)" title="Dallas (1978 TV series)">Dallas</a></i> had one of its more profitable oil wells, Ewing 23, in Lamesa. In one of the more dramatic scenes of the series, in season four, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.R._Ewing" title="J.R. Ewing">J.R. Ewing</a> flies in his <a href="/wiki/Learjet" title="Learjet">Learjet</a> to the Lamesa airport. Shortly thereafter, gunfire erupts and Dawson County sheriff's deputies shoot a man who blew up the oilfield after a failed effort to blackmail Ewing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamesa,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city is served by a biweekly newspaper, <i>The Lamesa Press Reporter,</i> which charges $.75 per issue, and by local and area radio stations <a href="/wiki/KPET" title="KPET">KPET</a> (AM 690), KBKN (FM), KTXC (FM), and <a href="/wiki/KJJT" title="KJJT">KJJT</a> (FM). The cable TV system is operated by Northland Cable Television. Other signals are received from stations in Lubbock, Midland-Odessa, and other area towns. Television signals are provided by ABC, CBS, NBC, PBS, Fox, Telemundo and CW stations in Lubbock and the Univision station in the Permian Basin (Midland-Odessa).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lamesa,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_Climate_Classification" title="Köppen Climate Classification">Köppen Climate Classification</a> system, Lamesa has a <a href="/wiki/Semi-arid_climate" title="Semi-arid climate">semi-arid climate</a>, abbreviated "BSk" on climate maps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The town is known for blistering summers (frequently topping 100°F) and cold winter nights (where the temperature goes below freezing on an average of 91 nights). The average annual temperature is 61.4°F, making it the ninth coldest place in Texas after cities such as Amarillo and Lubbock. Lamesa averages 17.6 inches of rain and 4 inches of snow annually. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
