<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Love Parade disaster</h1>

<p>On 24 July 2010, a crowd <b>disaster at the 2010 <a href="/wiki/Love_Parade" title="Love Parade">Love Parade</a></b> <a href="/wiki/Electronic_dance_music" title="Electronic dance music">electronic dance music</a> festival in <a href="/wiki/Duisburg" title="Duisburg">Duisburg</a>, North Rhine-Westphalia, Germany, caused the death of 21 people from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suffocation" title="Suffocation">suffocation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-helbing_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-helbing-1">[1]</a></sup> At least 500 more were injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Love Parade was a popular and free-access music festival and <a href="/wiki/Parade" title="Parade">parade</a> that originated in 1989 in Berlin. The parade featured stages, but had <a href="/wiki/Float_(parade)" title="Float (parade)">floats</a> with music, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DJs" title="DJs">DJs</a> and dancers moving through the audience. The Love Parade in Duisburg was the first time that the festival had been held in a closed-off area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Between 200,000 and <span class="nowrap">1.4 million</span> people were reported to be attending the event and 3,200 police were on hand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yahoo-24Jul_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yahoo-24Jul-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a consequence of the disaster, the organizer of the festival announced that no further Love Parades would be held and that the festival was permanently cancelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-local_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-local-7">[7]</a></sup> Criminal charges were brought against ten employees of the city of Duisburg and of the company that organized the event, but eventually rejected by the court due to the prosecutors' failure to establish evidence for the alleged acts of negligence and their causal connection to the deaths.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incident">Incident</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Parade_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Incident">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2010_07_24_arne_mueseler_0223.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="666" data-file-width="1000" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/2010_07_24_arne_mueseler_0223.jpg/220px-2010_07_24_arne_mueseler_0223.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/2010_07_24_arne_mueseler_0223.jpg/330px-2010_07_24_arne_mueseler_0223.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/2010_07_24_arne_mueseler_0223.jpg/440px-2010_07_24_arne_mueseler_0223.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2010_07_24_arne_mueseler_0223.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The inclined ramp an hour and a half before the disaster occurred</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>With the slogan "<a href="/wiki/Ars_Amatoria" title="Ars Amatoria">The Art of Love</a>", the event was one of the program elements of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RUHR.2010" title="RUHR.2010">RUHR.2010</a>, an effort to highlight cultural events in the <a href="/wiki/Ruhr" title="Ruhr">Ruhr</a> area, one of 2010's <a href="/wiki/European_Capital_of_Culture" title="European Capital of Culture">European Capitals of Culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Unterf%C3%BChrung_in_Duisburg_am_ehemaligen_G%C3%BCterbahnhof_DSCF0640.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2616" data-file-width="3488" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Unterf%C3%BChrung_in_Duisburg_am_ehemaligen_G%C3%BCterbahnhof_DSCF0640.jpg/220px-Unterf%C3%BChrung_in_Duisburg_am_ehemaligen_G%C3%BCterbahnhof_DSCF0640.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Unterf%C3%BChrung_in_Duisburg_am_ehemaligen_G%C3%BCterbahnhof_DSCF0640.jpg/330px-Unterf%C3%BChrung_in_Duisburg_am_ehemaligen_G%C3%BCterbahnhof_DSCF0640.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Unterf%C3%BChrung_in_Duisburg_am_ehemaligen_G%C3%BCterbahnhof_DSCF0640.jpg/440px-Unterf%C3%BChrung_in_Duisburg_am_ehemaligen_G%C3%BCterbahnhof_DSCF0640.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Unterf%C3%BChrung_in_Duisburg_am_ehemaligen_G%C3%BCterbahnhof_DSCF0640.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A 2008 photograph of the tunnel that leads to the site of the disaster</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The festival was staged on the area of a former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freight_station" title="Freight station">freight station</a>. The capacity of the enclosed location was limited to 250,000 people,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> but more than one million visitors were expected, based on the experience of previous years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Entrance should have been granted at 11:00, but was granted at 12:00 <a href="/wiki/Central_European_Summer_Time" title="Central European Summer Time">CEST</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A 240-metre tunnel from the east and a series of underpasses from the west met at a ramp that was supposed to be the only entrance and exit point of the festival area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A smaller ramp existed between the underpasses from the west. Because of overcrowding, police at the entrance began announcing over <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">loudspeakers</a> that new arrivals should turn around and head back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-24Jul_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-24Jul-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-France24-24Jul_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-France24-24Jul-13">[13]</a></sup> The side of the tunnel that was the entry of the parade area was closed, but people continued to enter the tunnel from the rear, despite being told it was closed. The deaths occurred as the ramp between tunnel underpasses and the festival area became overcrowded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Washingtonpost_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Washingtonpost-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>There was some debate as to how the deaths occurred. Some reports suggested they were caused by people falling off a staircase as they tried to escape the tunnel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ndtv_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ndtv-16">[16]</a></sup> However, autopsies showed that all of the fatalities were due to crushed rib cages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ndtv_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-ndtv-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A 2012 scientific analysis of the causes of the disaster dismissed the earlier descriptions of the incident as <a href="/wiki/Stampede" title="Stampede">stampede</a> or crowd panic, and instead found evidence for a phenomenon called "crowd turbulence".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-helbing_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-helbing-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lopavent GmbH, the organiser of the Love Parade, released a film,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> depicting an explanation of the events. The film is based on CCTV recordings, explanatory animations, documents, press reports and eyewitness accounts released by the organiser.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fatalities">Fatalities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Parade_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fatalities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A total of 21 people died, 13 women and 8 men, aged between 18 and 38 years. Fourteen were reported to be Germans, 7 men and 7 women. Among the seven casualties of other nationalities were two Spanish women aged 21 and 22 years, a 38-year-old Chinese woman living in Germany,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> a 22-year-old man from the Netherlands,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> a 21-year-old woman from Italy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> a 21-year-old woman from Bosnia-Herzegovina, and a 27-year-old woman from Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Fifteen died at the site, six died in hospital.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eyewitness_accounts">Eyewitness accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Parade_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Eyewitness accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The atmosphere was explosive. Many in the crowd seemed to be intoxicated. When people started falling off the stairs and pulling others with them, it became just chaotic. They just couldn't be stopped. It was a living hell.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Eyewitness police officer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-independent_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-independent-26">[26]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I will never forget the sight. There were all these twisted-up bodies of those who had been crushed. They were lying at the tunnel exit. Their faces had all turned blue.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Eyewitness woman trapped in tunnel<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-independent_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-independent-26">[26]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Bruce Cullen of <a href="/wiki/Parker,_Colorado" title="Parker, Colorado">Parker, Colorado</a> and founder of Trance Elements, a LoveParade artist/performer on float number 7 - "The Ship of Fools", mentioned that he and other performers were concerned before the event that there would be problems, stating "we all said it seems like this is not going to work". Although Cullen did not witness the actual event, he stated: "These guys didn't have this planned out right", "They didn't have enough police at the entrances in that tunnel. I am just really upset because people died. Everybody was there to have fun".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Parade_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LP2010-Trauerkerzen.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2136" data-file-width="2848" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/LP2010-Trauerkerzen.JPG/220px-LP2010-Trauerkerzen.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/LP2010-Trauerkerzen.JPG/330px-LP2010-Trauerkerzen.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/LP2010-Trauerkerzen.JPG/440px-LP2010-Trauerkerzen.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LP2010-Trauerkerzen.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Candles near where the disaster took place</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Police chose not to close down the event, fearing that doing so could spark another panic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-24Jul_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-24Jul-12">[12]</a></sup> Nearby motorway <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_59" title="Bundesautobahn 59">A59</a>, which was closed during the whole Love Parade, functioned as an access route for emergency services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>During a press conference on 25 July, organiser <a href="/wiki/Rainer_Schaller" title="Rainer Schaller">Rainer Schaller</a> stated that there would never again be another Love Parade, out of respect for those who lost their lives. "The Love Parade has always been a joyful and peaceful party, but in future would always be overshadowed by yesterday's events" he stated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abendblatt_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-abendblatt-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>A local resident published internal documents of the city administration regarding the planning of Love Parade. The city government reacted by securing a court order on 16 August forcing the removal of the documents from the site on which it was hosted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> On 20 August 2010, <a href="/wiki/WikiLeaks" title="WikiLeaks">WikiLeaks</a> released a publication entitled <i>Loveparade 2010 Duisburg planning documents, 2007–2010</i>, which comprised 43 internal documents regarding the Love Parade 2010, from the Duisburg police, contractors at the parade and other parties involved from 2007–2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The German police and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State%27s_Attorney" title="State's Attorney">State's Attorney</a> (Staatsanwaltschaft) opened a criminal investigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abendblatt_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-abendblatt-30">[30]</a></sup> In 2016, a Duisburg court rejected the case, stating that the prosecutors had failed to "establish proof for the acts of negligence the defendants have been charged with, and for their causality".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-34">[34]</a></sup> Central to the court's concerns were numerous flaws in the report by professor Keith Still, a British crowd safety expert from <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Metropolitan_University" title="Manchester Metropolitan University">Manchester Metropolitan University</a>, which formed the basis of the charges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-34">[34]</a></sup> The report's initial version from 2011 was described by <a href="/wiki/S%C3%BCddeutsche_Zeitung" title="Süddeutsche Zeitung">Süddeutsche Zeitung</a> as "sloppily written and full of errors"; the judges subjected it to more than 70 followup questions over the next few years and eventually called it "unusable due to professor Still's serious violations of the basic duties of an <a href="/wiki/Expert_witness" title="Expert witness">expert witness</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-34">[34]</a></sup> Keith Still was also criticized by newspapers and the victims' attorney for disregarding possible mistakes by the police, causing the prosecutors to only include staff of the event organizer and the city among the ten defendants it charged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Parade_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany" title="Chancellor of Germany">German Chancellor</a> <a href="/wiki/Angela_Merkel" title="Angela Merkel">Angela Merkel</a> quickly issued a statement that she was "aghast and saddened by the sorrow and the pain".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yahoo-24Jul_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yahoo-24Jul-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/President_of_Germany" title="President of Germany">German President</a> <a href="/wiki/Christian_Wulff" title="Christian Wulff">Christian Wulff</a> also expressed his condolences for the victims of the <a href="/wiki/Tragedy_(event)" title="Tragedy (event)">tragedy</a> that had "caused death, sorrow and pain amidst a peaceful festival of cheerful young people from many countries. ... My thoughts are with the victims of the tragedy and with all their family and friends".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assigning_the_blame">Assigning the blame</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Parade_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Assigning the blame">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>None of the involved organizations or officials took the blame for the disaster by 29 July. Instead, the involved parties issued several statements accusing each other in a circular manner:</p>
<p>On 26 July, Rainer Schaller, organiser of the festival, accused the police of mistakes in crowd control, which he claims led to the disaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 28 July, the interior minister of North Rhine-Westphalia rejected this and assigned all the blame to Schaller, his company Lopavent, their security concept and the festival personnel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 29 July, several hundred people rallied at Duisburg's city hall, demanding the resignation of the mayor, Adolf Sauerland, depicting him at the gallows. The protesters claimed that Sauerland had been aware of sub-standard security provisions for the festival, but pushed his administration to approve the plans nonetheless. Sauerland, however, rejected the claims and repeatedly expressed that he would not resign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> According to local trade union ver.di, members of all parts of the urban administration of Duisburg have been threatened (sanitation workers, call center agents). Traffic warden were not sent out at all for security reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 14 August it became known that Sauerland admitted to deceiving the public about the expected number of visitors at the event. The "several million people" he had been referring to prior to the parade were only "pushed numbers" for media attention in accordance with the organiser of the event. The organiser and the administration had also claimed 1.4 million visitors on the day the disaster occurred but it turned out that the festival area proper (excluding the ramp, tunnel, underpasses, and crowded streets outside the access points), which had been approved for 250,000, was apparently not filled at the time. Due to threats on his life, Sauerland was under police protection at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the Duisburg city council failed to reach a 2/3-majority to trigger an impeachment against Lord Mayor Adolf Sauerland (his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_(Germany)" title="Christian Democratic Union (Germany)">CDU</a> refused to vote against him) on 13 September, the state government announced changes<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to the municipal code of North-Rhine-Westphalia.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Interior secretary Ralf Jäger was responsible for both this reform and the police in North-Rhine-Westphalia, at the same time he is leader of the Duisburg <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democrats</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Sauerland was removed from office through a <a href="/wiki/Recall_election" title="Recall election">recall election</a> on 12 February 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pop_culture">Pop culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Parade_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pop culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The incident is directly referred to with footage in the 2011 documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Life_in_a_Day_(2011_film)" title="Life in a Day (2011 film)">Life in a Day</a></i>. In 2010 a song titled Remember Love was produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Van_Dyk" title="Paul Van Dyk">Paul Van Dyk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Oakenfold" title="Paul Oakenfold">Paul Oakenfold</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armin_Van_Buuren" title="Armin Van Buuren">Armin Van Buuren</a> with the proceeds going to the families of the victims of the disaster.</p>
<p>In 2014 German rock/metal band <i><a href="/wiki/Axxis" title="Axxis">Axxis</a></i> released a song called <i>21 Crosses</i>, which demands the truth about who was to blame, with the names of the victims being recited at the end of the song.</p>
