<p><b>Comet Lovejoy</b>, <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_naming_conventions#Comets" title="Astronomical naming conventions">formally designated</a> <b>C/2011 W3 (Lovejoy)</b>, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long-period_comet" title="Long-period comet">long-period</a> <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kreutz_Sungrazers" title="Kreutz Sungrazers">Kreutz Sungrazer</a>. It was discovered in November 2011 by Australian <a href="/wiki/Amateur_astronomy" title="Amateur astronomy">amateur astronomer</a> <a href="/wiki/Terry_Lovejoy" title="Terry Lovejoy">Terry Lovejoy</a>. The comet's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a> took it through the Sun's <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a> on 16 December 2011, after which it emerged intact, though greatly impacted by the event.</p>
<p>As Comet Lovejoy was announced on the 16th anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">SOHO</a> satellite's launch it became known as "The Great Birthday Comet of 2011", and because it was visible from Earth during the <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> holiday it was also nicknamed "The Great Christmas Comet of 2011".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sungrazer-c1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sungrazer-c1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcmag20111227_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pcmag20111227-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C/2011_W3_(Lovejoy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Comet Lovejoy was discovered on 27 November 2011 by Terry Lovejoy of <a href="/wiki/Thornlands,_Queensland" title="Thornlands, Queensland">Thornlands, Queensland</a>, during a comet survey using a 20 cm (7.9 in) <a href="/wiki/Schmidt%E2%80%93Cassegrain_telescope" title="Schmidt–Cassegrain telescope">Schmidt–Cassegrain telescope</a> and a QHY9 <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">CCD</a> camera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cometography_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-cometography-4">[4]</a></sup> It is the third comet discovered by Terry Lovejoy. He reported that it was "a rapidly moving fuzzy object" of 13th <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">magnitude</a>, and additional observations were made by him over the next couple of nights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cometography_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-cometography-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sandt20111206_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-sandt20111206-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Independent confirmation of the comet did not come until 1 December, when it was observed by <a href="/wiki/Alan_C._Gilmore" title="Alan C. Gilmore">Alan Gilmore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pamela_M._Kilmartin" title="Pamela M. Kilmartin">Pamela Kilmartin</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Mount_John_University_Observatory" title="Mount John University Observatory">Mount John University Observatory</a> in New Zealand, using its 100 cm (39 in) McLellan Telescope. Upon confirmation, an official report was made to the <a href="/wiki/Central_Bureau_for_Astronomical_Telegrams" title="Central Bureau for Astronomical Telegrams">Central Bureau for Astronomical Telegrams</a>, and the comet's existence was announced by the <a href="/wiki/Minor_Planet_Center" title="Minor Planet Center">Minor Planet Center</a> on 2 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cometography_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-cometography-4">[4]</a></sup> It is the first Kreutz-group comet discovered by ground-based observation in 40 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esa20111214_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-esa20111214-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observations">Observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C/2011_W3_(Lovejoy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Orbital_elements" title="Orbital elements">orbital elements</a> using an assumed <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_trajectory" title="Parabolic trajectory">parabolic trajectory</a> were published by Gareth Williams of the Minor Planet Center on 2 December, with an estimated perihelion of 0.0059 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> occurring near midnight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a> of 15/16 December. Further refinements were published during subsequent days, including one on 5 December, estimating perihelion at 0.0056 AU just before midnight on 15 December. On 11 December the first <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">elliptical orbit</a> was published, estimating perihelion at 0.0055 AU just after midnight on 16 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cometography_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-cometography-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEC2011-Y23_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-MPEC2011-Y23-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In space, the comet first became visible to the <a href="/wiki/STEREO" title="STEREO">STEREO</a>-A spacecraft on 3 December, and to the <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">SOHO</a> spacecraft on 14 December. As the comet approached the Sun it was observed by eighteen instruments on six satellites: STEREO-A and -B, SOHO, SDO, <a href="/wiki/Hinode" title="Hinode">Hinode</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proba-2" title="Proba-2">PROBA2</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sungrazer-navy_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-sungrazer-navy-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soho20111203_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-soho20111203-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>A small companion comet was detected in SOHO images on 14 December by Zhijian Xu, and later spotted by the twin STEREO spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sungrazer-c1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-sungrazer-c1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report201112_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-report201112-10">[10]</a></sup> It is believed to be a fragment of Comet Lovejoy that broke away several decades ago. This discovery was not unexpected, as Kreutz-group comets are often found with smaller companions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sungrazer-c1_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-sungrazer-c1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-discovery20111215_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-discovery20111215-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>At its brightest, Comet Lovejoy had an <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> of between <span class="nowrap">–3</span> and <span class="nowrap">–4</span>, which is about as bright as the planet <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sungrazer-navy_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-sungrazer-navy-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spaceweather1215_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-spaceweather1215-12">[12]</a></sup> It is the brightest <a href="/wiki/Sungrazing_comet" title="Sungrazing comet">sungrazing comet</a> ever observed by SOHO, and the brightest comet to appear since <a href="/wiki/C/2006_P1" title="C/2006 P1">Comet McNaught</a> of 2007, which shone at visual magnitude <span class="nowrap">–5.5</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sungrazer-c1_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-sungrazer-c1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brightest_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-brightest-13">[13]</a></sup> Nevertheless, Lovejoy was largely invisible to the naked eye during its peak brightness due to its proximity to the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcmag20111227_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-pcmag20111227-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-discovery20111215_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-discovery20111215-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Perihelion">Perihelion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C/2011_W3_(Lovejoy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Perihelion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Comet Lovejoy reached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a> on 16 December 2011 at 00:17 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a>, as it passed approximately 140,000 kilometres (87,000 mi) above the Sun's surface<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jplsmall_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-jplsmall-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space20111216_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-space20111216-15">[15]</a></sup> at a speed of 536 km/s (333 mi/s), or 0.18% the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-speed_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-speed-16">[16]</a></sup> It was not expected to survive the encounter due to extreme conditions in the corona, such as temperatures reaching more than one million <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvins</a> and the exposure time of nearly an hour. However, the <a href="/wiki/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory" title="Solar Dynamics Observatory">Solar Dynamics Observatory</a> (SDO), as well as other Sun-monitoring spacecraft, observed the comet emerge from the corona intact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space20111215-1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-space20111215-1-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space20111215-2_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-space20111215-2-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceNASA_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceNASA-19">[19]</a></sup> The STEREO and SOHO spacecraft continued to observe the comet as it moved away from the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceNASA_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceNASA-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before perihelion, the <a href="/wiki/Comet_nucleus" title="Comet nucleus">nucleus</a> of Comet Lovejoy had been estimated to be between 100 and 200 metres (330 and 660 ft) in diameter. Since the comet survived perihelion, it is thought that the nucleus must have been larger, perhaps up to 500 metres (1,600 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceNASA011212_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceNASA011212-20">[20]</a></sup> During the coronal passage, it is believed that a significant fraction of the comet's mass was burned off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceNASA_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceNASA-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outbound_flight">Outbound flight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C/2011_W3_(Lovejoy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Outbound flight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first ground-based observation of Comet Lovejoy post-perihelion occurred on 16 December at 19:55 UTC, when it was seen by Rick Baldridge and Brian Day of the <a href="/wiki/Foothill_Observatory" title="Foothill Observatory">Foothill Observatory</a>. Baldridge estimated the comet at <span class="nowrap">magnitude −1</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cometography_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-cometography-4">[4]</a></sup> The comet's discoverer, Terry Lovejoy, made a pair of observations on 17 December at 01:12 and 20:24 UTC, with apparent magnitudes estimated <span class="nowrap">at −1.2</span> <span class="nowrap">and −0.8</span>, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cometography_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-cometography-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Images taken on 20 December around 08:00 UTC suggested that the comet had undergone significant changes. Taken by Czech astronomer Jakub Černý using the robotic FRAM telescope at <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Auger_Observatory" title="Pierre Auger Observatory">Pierre Auger Observatory</a>, the images indicated that "the nucleus had apparently become bar-shaped and was accompanied by a bright tail ray."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cometography_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-cometography-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cerny20111219_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-cerny20111219-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Comet Lovejoy re-emerged as a <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">naked eye</a> object in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a> around 21–22 December,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UT-21dec_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-UT-21dec-22">[22]</a></sup> when astronaut Dan Burbank photographed it from the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sandt20111222_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-sandt20111222-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISS030-E-015479_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISS030-E-015479-24">[24]</a></sup> Ground-based photographers continued to capture images of the comet, which had dimmed to around 4th magnitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sandt20111222_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-sandt20111222-23">[23]</a></sup> While Lovejoy would have continued to be visible to Southern Hemisphere observers into early 2012, large telescopes would have been required to see the comet by the time it crossed into the Northern Hemisphere in February.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcmag20111227_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-pcmag20111227-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some concern was expressed after perihelion that the stresses induced in the comet by its close approach to the Sun might result in its disintegration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sungrazer-navy_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-sungrazer-navy-8">[8]</a></sup> That observers were unable to locate a distinct nucleus amidst the more visible tail furthered this idea;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcmag20111227_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-pcmag20111227-3">[3]</a></sup> using observations from the <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Auger_Observatory" title="Pierre Auger Observatory">Pierre Auger Observatory</a>, Zdeněk Sekanina and Paul Chodas determined that, while the nucleus did survive perihelion for several days, following a significant outburst of dust on 19 December, the nucleus underwent a "cataclysmic fragmentation" event on 20 December and completely disappeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sekanina2012_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sekanina2012-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the event that some portion of the nucleus did survive, the eccentricity and inclination of Comet Lovejoy's orbit avoids significant <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">perturbation</a> from planets, which leaves the possibility that the comet may return for another perihelion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sungrazer-navy_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-sungrazer-navy-8">[8]</a></sup> Using an <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(astronomy)" title="Epoch (astronomy)">epoch</a> 2050 solution, Comet Lovejoy is estimated to have about a 622-year orbit and a return to perihelion around the year 2633.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-horizons_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-horizons-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_fragmentation_history">Possible fragmentation history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C/2011_W3_(Lovejoy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Possible fragmentation history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An elliptical orbit calculated by Sekanina and Chodas in 2012 indicates that Comet Lovejoy is a fragment of an unrecorded <a href="/wiki/Sungrazing_comet" title="Sungrazing comet">sungrazer</a> that reached perihelion around 1329. The fragmentation history suggested by these authors is that a parent sungrazer, likely the comet observed in 467 <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">CE</a>, split near the Sun due to <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">tidal forces</a> during its pass. The principal fragment – or a non-tidally fragmented portion of it – returned as the <a href="/wiki/X/1106_C1" title="X/1106 C1">Great Comet of 1106</a>, but a secondary fragment was imparted a longer orbital period and returned about 1329. This secondary also split at perihelion and its principal fragment will return around 2200, likely as a cluster of further fragments. A secondary fragment of this event left on a shorter period that theoretically should bring it back to the inner solar system during the early years of the 21st century. At some point after perihelion, this secondary fragment broke apart due to non-tidal forces and one of the resulting fragments became Comet Lovejoy. Other similar fragments may also exist and might return as sungrazers in the near future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sekanina2012_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sekanina2012-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
