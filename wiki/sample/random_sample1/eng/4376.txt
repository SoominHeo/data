<p><i><b>Love Hina</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>: <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">ラブ ひな</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <a href="/wiki/Hepburn_romanization" title="Hepburn romanization">Hepburn</a>: <i>Rabu Hina</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a Japanese <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a> series written and illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Ken_Akamatsu" title="Ken Akamatsu">Ken Akamatsu</a>. It was serialized in <i><a href="/wiki/Weekly_Sh%C5%8Dnen_Magazine" title="Weekly Shōnen Magazine">Weekly Shōnen Magazine</a></i> from October 21, 1998 to October 31, 2001, with the chapters collected into 14 <i><a href="/wiki/Tank%C5%8Dbon" title="Tankōbon">tankōbon</a></i> volumes by <a href="/wiki/Kodansha" title="Kodansha">Kodansha</a>. The series tells the story of Keitarō Urashima and his attempts to find the girl with whom he made a childhood promise to enter <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tokyo" title="University of Tokyo">Tokyo University</a>. The manga was licensed for an English-language release in North America and the United Kingdom by <a href="/wiki/Tokyopop" title="Tokyopop">Tokyopop</a>, in Australia by <a href="/wiki/Madman_Entertainment" title="Madman Entertainment">Madman Entertainment</a>, and in Singapore by <a href="/wiki/Chuang_Yi" title="Chuang Yi">Chuang Yi</a>. Two novelizations of <i>Love Hina</i>, written by two anime series screenwriters, were also released in Japan by Kodansha. Both novels were later released in North America and the United Kingdom by Tokyopop.</p>
<p>A twenty-four episode anime adaptation of the manga series, produced by <a href="/wiki/Xebec_(studio)" title="Xebec (studio)">Xebec</a>, aired in Japan from April 19, 2000, to September 27, 2000. It was followed by a bonus DVD episode, Christmas and Spring television specials, and a three episode <a href="/wiki/Original_video_animation" title="Original video animation">original video animation</a> (OVA) entitled <i>Love Hina Again</i>. The anime series, special, and OVA were licensed for release in North America by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bandai_Entertainment" title="Bandai Entertainment">Bandai Entertainment</a>. In July 2007, the license was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Funimation" title="Funimation">Funimation</a>, who released a boxset of the television series in February 2009. The series is also licensed in Australia by <a href="/wiki/Madman_Entertainment" title="Madman Entertainment">Madman Entertainment</a> and in the United Kingdom by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MVM_Films" title="MVM Films">MVM Films</a>.</p>
<p>The series has proved extremely popular around the world, both commercially and critically. In Japan, the manga sold over 16 million copies; over 1 million anime DVDs were also sold. The English release of the manga has been reprinted many times. Both anime and manga have received numerous industry awards in Japan and North America, as well as praise from critics.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Hina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Love_Hina_characters" title="List of Love Hina characters">List of Love Hina characters</a></div>
<p>The story is a <i>shōnen</i> comedy that takes place in the <a href="/wiki/Kanagawa_Prefecture" title="Kanagawa Prefecture">Kanagawa Prefecture</a>, and centers on <a href="/wiki/Keitar%C5%8D_Urashima" title="Keitarō Urashima">Keitarō Urashima</a> and his attempts to fulfill a childhood promise that he made with a girl to enter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tokyo_University" title="Tokyo University">Tokyo University</a> together. However, he has forgotten the name of the girl he made the promise to and hopes to be accepted into Tokyo University in order to find her. Having failed the entrance exam twice and with his parents no longer willing to support him, he goes to stay at his grandmother's hotel, only to find that it has been converted into a female-only apartment. The tenants are about to kick him out when his aunt appears and announces that his grandmother has given him the title to the apartments. Much to their dismay Keitarō becomes the new manager of the family-owned girls' dorm Hinata House and must now balance his new responsibilities in addition to studying for the university entrance exam.</p>
<p>At Hinata House, Keitarō meets <a href="/wiki/Naru_Narusegawa" title="Naru Narusegawa">Naru Narusegawa</a>, who is also studying to enter Tokyo University. Naru ranks first in the whole of Japan on the practice exams, and Keitarō convinces her to help him study. As the two of them grow closer through their studies, and after Keitarō accidentally reads a small section of Naru's diary, he becomes increasingly convinced that Naru may be the girl with whom he made the promise. On the second day of the Tokyo University exam, Keitarō asks Naru about the promise and is stunned when she tells him he is mistaken. Despite their studying, and Naru's mock exam results, they both fail the exams. The pair then have an argument and independently run off to <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a> to clear their heads. While on their trip they settle their differences and meet <a href="/wiki/List_of_Love_Hina_characters#Mutsumi_Otohime" title="List of Love Hina characters">Mutsumi Otohime</a>, who lives in <a href="/wiki/Okinawa_Prefecture" title="Okinawa Prefecture">Okinawa</a> and is also studying for the Tokyo University exams.</p>
<p>After returning from Kyoto, Keitarō and Naru decide to retake the exams. After a while, Mutsumi moves to Tokyo, and the three begin to study together. During this period, Naru becomes convinced that Mutsumi is Keitarō's promised girl, but Mutsumi states that she made a childhood promise with Naru, not Keitarō. During the next round of Tokyo University exams, Keitarō believes he has failed them once again and runs away before finding out his results. After learning of this, Naru chases after him without checking her exam results either, and they are followed by the rest of the residents of Hinata House who announce that Keitarō and Naru both passed the exams along with Mutsumi. Unfortunately for him, Keitarō has an accident at the Tokyo University opening ceremony and is unable to attend classes for three months. After recovering from his injuries, Keitarō decides to study overseas with <a href="/wiki/List_of_Love_Hina_characters#Noriyasu_Seta" title="List of Love Hina characters">Noriyasu Seta</a>. As Keitarō is about to leave, Naru finally confesses her feelings to him at the airport and decides to wait for him to return.</p>
<p>When Keitarō returns, he and Naru finally begin to express their feelings for each other. After they deal with new obstacles, Grandma Hina returns to Hinata House and reveals Naru is the girl of Keitarō's promise. Four years later, a wedding ceremony (with a new girl, Ema Maeda, presented) is held at Hinata House for Naru and Keitarō as they finally fulfill their childhood promise to each other.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Hina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Naru_designs.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="190" height="357" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4f/Naru_designs.jpg/170px-Naru_designs.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/4/4f/Naru_designs.jpg 1.5x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Naru_designs.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Comparison between early and late designs for the character that became Naru</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Initial sketches for the series were created between September and December 1997, after the completion of <i><a href="/wiki/A.I._Love_You" title="A.I. Love You">A.I. Love You</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Early storyboards with initial character designs were created between December 1997 and January 1998, and further character designs and location sketches followed between January and April 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The last storyboards before serialization were created between April and August 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kodansha_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kodansha-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around six months before the start of serialization, character designs were still going through several revisions before being settled upon. Several characters underwent complete redesigns and name changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-production_start_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-production_start-8">[8]</a></sup> At one stage the character Naru was named Midori, and she was supposed to fall through a hole in the floor naked, bump her head on Keitaro and lose her memory. Naru's name was changed many times before the author settled on Naru Narusegawa, and her final design is similar to Saati Namba from <i>A.I. Love You</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/List_of_Love_Hina_characters#Mitsune_.22Kitsune.22_Konno" title="List of Love Hina characters">Mitsune "Kitsune" Konno</a>'s money-grubbing nature and her older, jaded, and more mature personality were originally intended to be used for <a href="/wiki/List_of_Love_Hina_characters#Kaolla_Su" title="List of Love Hina characters">Kaolla Su</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/List_of_Love_Hina_characters#Shinobu_Maehara" title="List of Love Hina characters">Shinobu Maehara</a>'s nature was settled on from the beginning of the series, however her physical appearance and age were extensively redesigned as the series concept was shaped. In her early design, Shinobu had a similar appearance to Forty Namba from <i>A.I. Love You</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout the run of the manga, the series used digital editing processes. After a rough sketch of a page was created, the page layout and basic detail were drawn and scanned into an <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a> <a href="/wiki/Macintosh" title="Macintosh">Macintosh</a>. The major page elements were then shaded or filled with patterns, and elements that were drawn separately were added digitally to the page.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The manga also used a series of "banked images", which were basic line drawings of locations, such as a characters room. Instead of redrawing a location from scratch every time it was used, these banked images could be used as a base, and extra detail added to them depending on the requirements for the scene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both of these techniques lead to characters having white outlines when copied digitally onto the scene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-design2_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-design2-16">[16]</a></sup> Parts of Hinata Inn and other locations used were inspired by real life locations and designed from photographs collected during research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kodansha_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kodansha-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Hina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manga">Manga</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Hina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Manga">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Love_Hina_chapters" title="List of Love Hina chapters">List of Love Hina chapters</a></div>
<p><i>Love Hina</i> were originally serialized in <i><a href="/wiki/Weekly_Sh%C5%8Dnen_Magazine" title="Weekly Shōnen Magazine">Weekly Shōnen Magazine</a></i>, between November 4, 1998 and November 14, 2001 for a total of 123 chapters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The series was released as a 14-volume collected edition between March 1999 and January 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The series was later released in a partially colored format known as the "Iro Hina version". The 14 Iro Hina volumes were released between July 2001 and April 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> A new seven-volume edition was released by Kodansha between June and December 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kodansha published a bilingual English and Japanese edition under the Kodansha Bilingual Comics label. Eight volumes were produced under the bilingual format between October 2000 and July 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The edition was removed from sale after the series was licensed by Tokyopop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The series was licensed for an English-language release in North America and the United Kingdom by <a href="/wiki/Tokyopop" title="Tokyopop">Tokyopop</a>, which released the 14 volumes between May 21, 2002 and September 16, 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The English release was one of Tokyopop's first releases in the "Authentic Manga" lineup of titles using the Japanese right to left reading style. In doing so the artwork remained unchanged from the original.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The series appeared consistently in Tokyopop's top five selling manga and has been reprinted several times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In August 2009, it was revealed that Tokyopop's license had been left to expire by Kodansha and would not be renewed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kodansha_Comics_USA" title="Kodansha Comics USA">Kodansha Comics USA</a> licensed the series with a new translation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> This omnibus edition was released as 5 volumes between October 2011 and March 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The series is also licensed for an English-language release in Singapore by <a href="/wiki/Chuang_Yi" title="Chuang Yi">Chuang Yi</a> and for regional language releases in France and <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Québec</a> by Pika Édition, in Spain by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gl%C3%A9nat_(publisher)" title="Glénat (publisher)">Glénat</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> by <a href="/wiki/Editora_JBC" title="Editora JBC">Editora JBC</a>, in Mexico by <a href="/wiki/Grupo_Editorial_Vid" title="Grupo Editorial Vid">Grupo Editorial Vid</a>, in Poland by Waneko, in Greece by <a href="/wiki/Compupress" title="Compupress">Compupress</a>, in Germany in German, in Norway by <a href="/wiki/Schibsted" title="Schibsted">Schibsted</a> Forlag, in Sweden by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonnier_Carlsen" title="Bonnier Carlsen">Bonnier Carlsen</a> and in Denmark by <a href="/wiki/Egmont_Manga_%26_Anime" title="Egmont Manga &amp; Anime">Egmont Manga &amp; Anime</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The September 1, 2010 issue of <i>Weekly Shōnen Magazine</i> included a six-color-page <i>Love Hina</i> <a href="/wiki/One-shot_(comics)" title="One-shot (comics)">one-shot</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fictional_crossover" title="Fictional crossover">crossover</a> <a href="/wiki/One-shot_(comics)" title="One-shot (comics)">one-shot</a> with <i><a href="/wiki/Aho_Girl" title="Aho Girl">Aho Girl</a></i> was released on August 27, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first 11 volumes sold over 6 million copies in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anime">Anime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Hina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Anime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Love_Hina_episodes" title="List of Love Hina episodes">List of Love Hina episodes</a></div>
<p><i>Love Hina</i> was adapted into a 24-episode anime television series by <a href="/wiki/Xebec_(studio)" title="Xebec (studio)">Xebec</a>, a division of <a href="/wiki/Production_I.G" title="Production I.G">Production I.G</a>. The series aired on <a href="/wiki/TV_Tokyo" title="TV Tokyo">TV Tokyo</a> April 19 through September 27, 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airdates_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-airdates-41">[41]</a></sup> The opening theme was <i>Sakura Saku</i> and the closing theme was <i>Kimi Sae Ireba</i>. Both songs were written by <a href="/wiki/Ritsuko_Okazaki" title="Ritsuko Okazaki">Ritsuko Okazaki</a> and performed by <a href="/wiki/Megumi_Hayashibara" title="Megumi Hayashibara">Megumi Hayashibara</a>. The two themes were released as a CD single, which debuted on the <a href="/wiki/Oricon" title="Oricon">Oricon</a> charts at Number 7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> A bonus 25th episode was later created and released as a DVD bonus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AoD_Xmas_review_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-AoD_Xmas_review-44">[44]</a></sup> The series and bonus episode were directed by Yoshiaki Iwasaki, written by <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8D_Aikawa_(screenwriter)" title="Shō Aikawa (screenwriter)">Shō Aikawa</a> and featured character designs by Makoto Uno.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tv_run1_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-tv_run1-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tv_run2_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-tv_run2-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Japan, the television series was released on nine DVDs by <a href="/wiki/King_Records_(Japan)" title="King Records (Japan)">Starchild Records</a> between August 3, 2000 and April 2, 2001, and sold over 1 million copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jp_dvds_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-jp_dvds-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> <i>Love Hina</i> is credited with being one of the first anime series to be available unofficially as a digitally produced <a href="/wiki/Fansub" title="Fansub">fansub</a>, with multiple groups working on the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The popularity, and widespread availability of the series in this form meant that several potential licensors of the series such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ADV_Films" title="ADV Films">ADV Films</a> had concerns over licensing the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The series was later licensed in North America by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bandai_Entertainment" title="Bandai Entertainment">Bandai Entertainment</a>, who released six DVDs between February 19 and November 19, 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> In July 2007, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Funimation_Entertainment" title="Funimation Entertainment">Funimation Entertainment</a> announced they had acquired the license to the series after Bandai's license had expired. A new boxset of the television series across 4 discs was released by Funimation on February 24, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> It was then re-released as part of Funimation's Viridian Collection on July 27, 2010. In the United Kingdom, the series is licensed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MVM_Films" title="MVM Films">MVM Films</a>, who released the series on six DVDs between September 6, 2004 and March 7, 2005, and as a boxset on May 14, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mvm_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-mvm-55">[55]</a></sup> In Australia and New Zealand the series is licensed by <a href="/wiki/Madman_Entertainment" title="Madman Entertainment">Madman Entertainment</a>, who also released the series across six DVDs between September 18, 2002, and February 11, 2003. A box set was later released on December 3, 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the television series was completed, a Christmas special, <i>Love Hina Xmas Eve: Silent Night</i>, was produced and shown on December 25, 2000 on <a href="/wiki/TV_Tokyo" title="TV Tokyo">TV Tokyo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airdates_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-airdates-41">[41]</a></sup> A DVD was released in Japan on July 4, 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jp_dvds_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-jp_dvds-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anime_encyclopedia_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anime_encyclopedia-57">[57]</a></sup> It was then released in North America on December 3, 2002 and in the United Kingdom on November 7, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mvm_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-mvm-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> The Spring Special <i>Love Hina Spring Special: I Wish Your Dream</i> was also shown on <a href="/wiki/TV_Tokyo" title="TV Tokyo">TV Tokyo</a> on April 2, 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airdates_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-airdates-41">[41]</a></sup> The DVD was released in Japan on August 1, 2001, in North American on March 18, 2003 and in the United Kingdom on May 16, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jp_dvds_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-jp_dvds-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mvm_55-2"><a href="#cite_note-mvm-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Finally, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Original_Video_Animation" title="Original Video Animation">OVA</a> series called <i>Love Hina Again</i> was released on DVD in Japan in 3 parts between January 26, 2002 and March 27, 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> A CD single featuring the opening theme "Kirari Takaramono" and the ending theme "Be for Me, Be for You" was released on February 28, 2002. A solo version was used for the first episode, and a duet with <a href="/wiki/Y%C5%ABji_Ueda" title="Yūji Ueda">Yūji Ueda</a> was used for the third episode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The North American and United Kingdom releases of <i>Love Hina Again</i> grouped the 3 parts together on one disc and were released on September 2, 2003 and January 7, 2008 respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mvm_55-3"><a href="#cite_note-mvm-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the end of the television series, <i>Love Hina Final Selection</i> was released, containing a summary of the series and "Love Live Hina", a live concert featuring all of the main cast members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Final_Selection_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Final_Selection-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>The anime was later used as the source for a <a href="/wiki/Film_comic" title="Film comic">film comic</a>, <i>Love Hina Anime Comics</i>, which told the anime story in comic form using stills from the show as the comic panels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> The anime Comics series follows the story of the television series, unaired 25th episode, and the Xmas and Spring specials and each volume contains 3 exclusive trading cards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The film comics also contain anime production info.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Light_novels">Light novels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Hina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Light novels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two novels have been written by the anime screenwriters and illustrated by Ken Akamatsu as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Side_stories" title="Side stories">side stories</a> of the main series. <i>Love Hina: Mystery Guests at Hinata Hotel</i> was written by <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8D_Aikawa_(screenwriter)" title="Shō Aikawa (screenwriter)">Shō Aikawa</a> under the pen name "Kurō Hazuki", was published in Japan by Kodansha on May 17, 2001. It was later rereleased in a bilingual edition (English and Japanese) in December 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> The second novel, <i>Love Hina: Secrets at Hinata Hotel</i> was written by Hiroyuki Kawasaki and released in Japan on February 15, 2002, with a bilingual edition released the same month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Tokyopop licensed both novels for an English-language distribution in North America, releasing the first novel under the title <i>Love Hina: The Novel, Volume 1</i> on April 11, 2006, and the second novel under the title <i>Love Hina: The Novel, Volume 2</i> on August 8, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reference_books">Reference books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Hina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reference books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two reference books for the manga series have been released for fans of the series. <i>Love Hina 0</i> was released on July 17, 2002 and contains character profiles, interviews and production info as well as other supporting materials for the first seven volumes of the manga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> <i>Love Hina Mugendai</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">ラブひな∞</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> was released on July 17, 2002 and contains character profiles, a timeline, artwork, interviews and production info. A large section is dedicated to early production sketches and handwritten development notes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two reference books have also been released for the anime series. <i>Ani-Hina Ver.1</i> was released on August 4, 2000 and <i>Ani-Hina Ver.2</i> was released on November 9, 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> Each book contains character profiles, episode summaries, production sketches and details as well as interviews and information on the voice actors; each covers half of the anime series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tv_run1_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-tv_run1-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tv_run2_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-tv_run2-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Hina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The series has seen several video games released across several platforms. The <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Color" title="Game Boy Color">Game Boy Color</a> received <i>Love Hina Pocket</i> on August 4, 2000, and <i>Love Hina Party</i> on January 26, 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Advance" title="Game Boy Advance">Game Boy Advance</a> received <i>Love Hina Advance</i> on September 7, 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Dreamcast" title="Dreamcast">Sega Dreamcast</a> received <i>Love Hina: Totsuzen no Engeji Happening</i> on September 28, 2000 and <i>Love Hina: Smile Again</i> on March 29, 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/PlayStation" title="PlayStation">Sony PlayStation</a> received <i>Love Hina 1: Ai wa Kotoba no Naka ni</i> on September 28, 2000 and <i>Love Hina 2: Kotoba wa Konayuki no Yō ni</i> on November 30, 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">Sony PlayStation 2</a> received <i>Love Hina: Gojasu Chiratto Happening</i> on May 22, 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soundtracks">Soundtracks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Hina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Soundtracks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Love_Hina_soundtracks" title="List of Love Hina soundtracks">List of Love Hina soundtracks</a></div>
<p>Prior to the start of the anime, several <a href="/wiki/Image_song" title="Image song">image songs</a> were recorded by the anime cast members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> Several <a href="/wiki/Maxi_single" title="Maxi single">maxi singles</a> were released featuring some of these image songs as well as drama tracks, also performed by the anime cast. "I Love Hina" was released on April 26, 2000 and followed by <i>Love Hina 1</i> on June 26, 2000, <i>Love Hina 2</i> on July 26, 2000 and <i>Love Hina 3</i> on August 23, 2000. <i>Love Hina 1</i> came with a box to hold the other singles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-starchild_soundtracks_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-starchild_soundtracks-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<p>There have been several <i>Love Hina</i> soundtracks released. <i>Love Hina Original Sound File</i> was released on September 21, 2000 and contains all of the background music for the series as well as many vocal songs. <i>Love Hina — Winter Special Soundtrack</i> was released on January 24, 2001 and was followed by <i>Love Hina — Spring Special Soundtrack</i> on June 6, 2001. <i>Love Hina Again Soundtrack</i> was released on April 3, 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-starchild_soundtracks_91-1"><a href="#cite_note-starchild_soundtracks-91">[91]</a></sup> Two collections of vocal songs featuring the female cast members were released: <i>Love Hina – Hinata Girls Song Best</i> was released on March 16, 2001 and <i>Love Hina – Hinata Girls Song Best 2</i> was released on October 3, 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-starchild_soundtracks_91-2"><a href="#cite_note-starchild_soundtracks-91">[91]</a></sup> Many of the songs featured on these two albums were written by <a href="/wiki/Ritsuko_Okazaki" title="Ritsuko Okazaki">Ritsuko Okazaki</a>, who released the self cover album <i>Love Hina Okazaki Collection</i> on December 16, 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> Two live concerts called <i>Love Live Hina</i> were performed by the Japanese cast members. The Tokyo Bay performance was bundled on DVD with <i>Love Hina Final Selection</i>, and the Osaka Performance was available separately.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Final_Selection_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-Final_Selection-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Love_Hina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Love Hina</i> won the <a href="/wiki/Kodansha_Manga_Award" title="Kodansha Manga Award">Kodansha Manga Award</a> for best <i>shōnen</i> title in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KodanshaHahn_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-KodanshaHahn-94">[94]</a></sup> It was selected as the "Best Manga, USA Release" at both the 2002 and 2004 <a href="/wiki/Anime_Expo" title="Anime Expo">Anime Expo</a> conventions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anime_expo_2002_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anime_expo_2002-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnimeExpo_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-AnimeExpo-96">[96]</a></sup> In 2003, the title was among the top ten graphic novels on <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_BookScan" title="Nielsen BookScan">Nielsen BookScan</a>'s list and one of the first graphic novels to ever appear in the general trade paperback list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop_culture" title="Pop culture">pop culture</a> website ICv2 voted <i>Love Hina</i> "Anime Product of the Year" in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup></p>
<p>The series was well received by critics. Tony Chen, of <a href="/wiki/Anime_News_Network" title="Anime News Network">Anime News Network</a> (ANN), found it to be a funny series, though finding the 16+ rating appropriate due to the number of jokes involving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_innuendo" title="Sexual innuendo">sexual innuendo</a>. He praised the beautiful artwork, feeling the "sexy and cute" female designs were perfect for the series and that Keitarō's design fit his dorky personality. Chen found Naru's regularly catching Keitarō making a mistake and calling him a pervert redundant and annoying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> Eric Luce of Ex.org notes an increased character development over other love comedies, and describes the series as "nothing if not whimsical".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup></p>
<p>The release of the second and third DVD's in Japan was only the second time that an anime series had consecutive number 1 chart positions. This would not occur again until over 15 years later with <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Osomatsu" title="Mr. Osomatsu">Mr. Osomatsu</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup></p>
<p>ANN's Bamboo Dong praised the anime adaptation for being very intriguing and mixing "drama, romance, and slapstick comedy in a pleasing combination". She found the music "incredibly cute" and felt it was used in a way which contributed to many of the dramatic effects in the anime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> In <i>The Anime Encyclopedia: A Guide to Japanese Animation Since 1917</i>, <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Clements" title="Jonathan Clements">Jonathan Clements</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helen_McCarthy" title="Helen McCarthy">Helen McCarthy</a> felt the female characters were a "standard rack of female anime archetypes" and that the series as a whole was a "culmination of a decade of geek-centered anime".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anime_encyclopedia_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anime_encyclopedia-57">[57]</a></sup> Kenneth Lee, writing for Ex.org, praised the look and quality of the animation, highlighting the benefits of the digital creation of the adaption over traditional cel animation. Lee recognised elements from other series such as <i><a href="/wiki/Maison_Ikkoku" title="Maison Ikkoku">Maison Ikkoku</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Kimagure_Orange_Road" title="Kimagure Orange Road">Kimagure Orange Road</a></i>, and summarised the series as "simply wonderful".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> Chris Beveridge, of AnimeOnDVD.com, noted the first anime DVD volume was "really well put together", but also felt the manga did not translate into an anime series particularly well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> He praised the Christmas special, noting that it was "several notches above the TV series" but found that while the Spring Special had amusing moments, it was rushed with bad plotting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AoD_Xmas_review_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-AoD_Xmas_review-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Love Hina Again</i> OVA received more mixed reviews, with ANN's Zac Berthschy feeling it reversed part of the plot of the main anime series and never reached the same entertainment level as the television series. The character of Kanako, Keitarō's sister, was heavily criticized for being "one of the most annoying characters ever created even though she would have been better for Keitaro than Naru."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> Beveridge praised the fun and comedy as well as the fan service, but also noted that one's enjoyment would depend on whether they still cared for the characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
