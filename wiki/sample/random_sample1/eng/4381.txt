<p><b>Russians in Finland</b> or <b>Russian Finns</b> constitute a linguistic and ethnic minority in <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>. About 30,000 people have citizenship of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-statfin1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-statfin1-2">[2]</a></sup> and Russian is the mother language of about 70,000 people in Finland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tilastokeskus-lahes-joka_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-tilastokeskus-lahes-joka-1">[1]</a></sup> which represents about 1.3% of the population.</p>
<p>Russian citizens who moved before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> are called "Old Russians". The next immigration wave happened after the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Dissolution of the Soviet Union">dissolution of the Soviet Union</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Ingrian_Finns" title="Ingrian Finns">Ingrian Finns</a> remigrated to Finland. At present, marriage and family ties are two other common reasons for Russians to immigrate to Finland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-socmag_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-socmag-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Russians_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first migratory wave of Russians began in the early 18th century, when Finland was part of <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Empire" title="Swedish Empire">Swedish Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-socmag_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-socmag-3">[3]</a></sup> About 40,000 Russian soldiers, civilian workers, and about 600 businessmen moved to the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Finland" title="Grand Duchy of Finland">Grand Duchy of Finland</a>. When <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> became independent (1917), many soldiers returned to Russia. Many businessmen stayed, including the <a href="/wiki/Sinebrychoff" title="Sinebrychoff">Sinebrychoff</a> family. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_(1917)" title="Russian Revolution (1917)">Russian Revolution</a> many <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy_(class)" title="Aristocracy (class)">aristocrats</a> and officers fled to <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> as <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee">refugees</a>. The biggest refugee wave was in 1922 when about 33,500 persons came to Finland. Many of them had <a href="/wiki/Nansen_passport" title="Nansen passport">Nansen passports</a> for many years. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kronstadt_Rebellion" title="Kronstadt Rebellion">Kronstadt Rebellion</a> about 1,600 officers fled to Finland. Russian citizens who moved in these three waves are called "Old Russians", whose 3–5,000 descendants live in Finland today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-socmag_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-socmag-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A second major wave of immigration occurred after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Fall of the Soviet Union">fall of the Soviet Union</a>. Many Russian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guest_worker" title="Guest worker">guest workers</a> came to Finland, working low-paying jobs. In the 1990s, immigration to Finland grew, and a Russian-speaking population descended from <a href="/wiki/Ingrian_Finns" title="Ingrian Finns">Ingrian Finns</a> immigrated to Finland. In the 2000s, many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nouveaux_riches" title="Nouveaux riches">nouveaux riches</a> Russians have bought estates in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Finland" title="Eastern Finland">Eastern Finland</a>.</p>

<p>According to <i>Russian Embassy in Finland</i>, there are about 50,000 Russian-speaking people in Finland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> However, in 2008 study of <a href="/wiki/Aleksanteri_Institute" title="Aleksanteri Institute">Aleksanteri Institute</a>, calculated 45,000 Russian-speaking people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Statistics_Finland" title="Statistics Finland">Statistics Finland</a>, there were 70,899 Russian-speaking people in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tilastokeskus-lahes-joka_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-tilastokeskus-lahes-joka-1">[1]</a></sup> However half of Russian-speaking immigrants are Ingrian Finns and other <a href="/wiki/Finno-Ugric_peoples" title="Finno-Ugric peoples">Finno-Ugric people</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2012, there were 30,183 people with citizenship of the Russian Federation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tilastokeskus-lahes-joka_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-tilastokeskus-lahes-joka-1">[1]</a></sup> – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dual_citizen" title="Dual citizen">dual citizens</a> included. Furthermore, there are people who have received only Finnish citizenship, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estonian_Russians" title="Estonian Russians">Estonian Russians</a>. Two common reasons for immigration were marriage, and descendant from Ingrian Finns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Russians_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian language</a> newspaper <i>Spektr</i> was founded in 1998, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_channel" title="Radio channel">radio channel</a> <a href="/wiki/Radio_Sputnik_(Finland)" title="Radio Sputnik (Finland)">Radio Sputnik</a> (<i>Russkoje Radio Helsinki</i>) broadcasts in the Russian language. Many small <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Orthodox Churchs</a> have been founded in Finland.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manifestations_of_intolerance">Manifestations of intolerance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Russians_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Manifestations of intolerance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a 2012 poll, 12% of Russians in Finland reported that they had experienced a racially motivated hate crime (as compared to an average of 5% of Russians in all EU countries).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hatecrime_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-hatecrime-9">[9]</a></sup> 27% of Russians in Finland were victims of crimes the last 12 months, for example theft, attacks, frightening threats or harassment (as compared to 17% of Russians in EU).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007 the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission_against_Racism_and_Intolerance" title="European Commission against Racism and Intolerance">European Commission against Racism and Intolerance</a> reported in its Third report on Finland <a class="external autonumber" href="http://hudoc.ecri.coe.int/XMLEcri/ENGLISH/Cycle_03/03_CbC_eng/FIN-CbC-III-2007-23-ENG.pdf" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">In its second report, ECRI recommended that action be taken to combat negative societal attitudes and manifestations of intolerance towards the members of Russian-speaking communities. However, representatives of these communities have indicated to ECRI that lack of determined action on the part of the Finnish authorities has allowed these attitudes and manifestations to intensify since ECRI's second report. ECRI is concerned at reports indicating that Russian-speakers have been the targets of violence, in at least one case resulting in death, and that the racist motivation of these acts has not always been adequately dealt with by the police. Racial harassment of Russian-speakers and racist bullying of Russian-speaking children at school have also been frequently reported. In addition, ECRI's attention has been drawn to the presence of anti-Russian material on the Internet inciting to racial hatred, and to the use of derogatory expressions to designate Russian-speakers as well as negative portrayal of these persons in the media.</td>
