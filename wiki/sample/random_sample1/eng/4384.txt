<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Russia" title="Constitution of Russia">Constitution</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian Federation</a> is divided into 85 <a href="/wiki/Federal_subjects_of_Russia" title="Federal subjects of Russia">federal subjects</a> (constituent units), 22 of which are <b><a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">republics</a></b>. Most of the republics represent areas of non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_ethnicity" title="Russian ethnicity">Russian ethnicity</a>, although there are several republics with Russian majority. The indigenous ethnic group of a republic that gives it its name is referred to as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titular_nationality" title="Titular nationality">titular nationality</a>". Due to decades (in some cases centuries) of <a href="/wiki/Human_migration" title="Human migration">internal migration</a> inside Russia, each nationality is not necessarily a majority of a republic's population.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Constitutional_status">Constitutional status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republics_of_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Constitutional status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Republics differ from other <a href="/wiki/Federal_subjects_of_Russia" title="Federal subjects of Russia">federal subjects of Russia</a> in that they have the right to establish their own <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">official language</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and have their own constitution. Other federal subjects, such as <a href="/wiki/Krai" title="Krai">krais</a> (territories) and <a href="/wiki/Oblast" title="Oblast">oblasts</a> (provinces), are not explicitly given this right. The chief executives of many republics used to have the title of president, but in 2010 an amendment to the federal law was adopted that reserves such title exclusively for the head of the Russian state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The level of actual autonomy granted to such political units varies but is generally quite extensive. The parliamentary assemblies of such republics have often enacted laws which are at odds with the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Russia" title="Constitution of Russia">federal constitution</a>. The republics' executives tend to be very powerful. However, this autonomy was lessened considerably under <a href="/wiki/President_of_Russia" title="President of Russia">Russian President</a> <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a>, who sought to impose the supremacy of the federal constitution.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The establishment of eight large "<a href="/wiki/Federal_districts_of_Russia" title="Federal districts of Russia">federal districts</a>" above the regions and republics of Russia, with presidentially appointed governors overseeing the republics' activities, has strengthened federal control, and respect for federal supremacy in the republics. In addition, Putin strengthened the position of the <a href="/wiki/Regional_parliaments_of_Russia" title="Regional parliaments of Russia">republics' legislatures</a>, while weakening their executives' power. In some republics the executive heads are elected by popular votes/for example Bashkortostan, Tatarstan etc./ while in some republics the executive heads of republics are now appointed by the President of Russia himself /for example Chechnya/. The President's nomination must be accepted by the republic's parliament. On May 30, 2014, the President of Russia, Vladimir Putin, accepted Khamitov's resignation so that he could participate in the regional elections; On 14 September 2014, most of the votes (82.17%) were given to the acting head of republic by the citizens of the Bashkortostan Republic; On 25 September 2015, the inauguration process took place in the State Council-Kurultay of the Bashkortostan Republic.</p>
<p>There are secessionist movements in most republics, but these are generally not very strong. However, there was considerable support for secession among <a href="/wiki/Tatars" title="Tatars">Tatars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bashkirs" title="Bashkirs">Bashkirs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yakuts" title="Yakuts">Yakuts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chechens" title="Chechens">Chechens</a> after the breakup of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, resulting in <a href="/wiki/First_Chechen_War" title="First Chechen War">war</a> in the case of Chechnya. The desire for secession in many republics is, however, greatly complicated by the extent to which other ethnic groups reside in their titular republics (<a href="/wiki/Tatarstan" title="Tatarstan">Tatarstan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bashkortostan" title="Bashkortostan">Bashkortostan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sakha_Republic" title="Sakha Republic">Sakha</a>; due to the <a href="/wiki/First_Chechen_War" title="First Chechen War">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Chechen_War" title="Second Chechen War">Second Chechen Wars</a>, very few non-Chechens now reside in <a href="/wiki/Chechnya" title="Chechnya">Chechnya</a>.) Also, the majority of Tatars, unlike other titular ethnic groups, reside outside of Tatarstan.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_of_Crimea">Status of Crimea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republics_of_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Status of Crimea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Annexation_of_Crimea_by_the_Russian_Federation" title="Annexation of Crimea by the Russian Federation">Annexation of Crimea by the Russian Federation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_status_of_Crimea" title="Political status of Crimea">Political status of Crimea</a></div>
<p>On March 18, 2014, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Crimea" title="Republic of Crimea">Republic of Crimea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sevastopol" title="Sevastopol">Sevastopol</a> were joined by treaty to the Russian Federation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Treaty_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Treaty-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-4">[4]</a></sup> Much of the international community and the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ukraine" title="Government of Ukraine">Ukrainian government</a> do not recognize Crimea's <a href="/wiki/Annexation_of_Crimea_by_the_Russian_Federation" title="Annexation of Crimea by the Russian Federation">accession to Russia</a> and consider Crimea an integral part of <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Former_Autonomous_Republics_and_Autonomous_Oblasts">Former Autonomous Republics and Autonomous Oblasts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republics_of_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Former Autonomous Republics and Autonomous Oblasts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_SFSR" title="Russian SFSR">Russian SFSR</a> of the former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> included three types of ethnic constituent units, viz., in the order of decreasing "autonomy" level: <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_Soviet_Socialist_Republics_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republics of the Soviet Union">Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republics</a> (or simply autonomous republics), <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_oblasts_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Autonomous oblasts of the Soviet Union">autonomous oblasts</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_okrugs_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Autonomous okrugs of the Soviet Union">autonomous okrugs</a>.</p>
<p>After the dissolution of the USSR, each "autonomous republic" was succeeded by a republic with a similar name (or, in the case of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chechen-Ingush_ASSR" title="Chechen-Ingush ASSR">Chechen-Ingush ASSR</a>, by two republics: <a href="/wiki/Chechnya" title="Chechnya">Chechnya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ingushetia" title="Ingushetia">Ingushetia</a>). Several "autonomous oblasts" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Adygea" title="Republic of Adygea">Adygea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Altai_Republic" title="Altai Republic">Altai</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karachay%E2%80%93Cherkessia" title="Karachay–Cherkessia">Karachay–Cherkessia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Khakassia" title="Khakassia">Khakassia</a>) have become "republics" as well.</p>
<p>The expression "autonomous republic" is still sometimes used for the republics of Russia. Although they are autonomous and republics, the use of this term is not technically correct, since their official names, as per 1993 <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Russia" title="Constitution of Russia">Russian Constitution</a> and their own constitutions, are simply "republic", rather than "autonomous republic".</p>
