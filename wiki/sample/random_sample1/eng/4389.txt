<p><b>Racine</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">RAY</span></span></b>-seen</i></a></span>) or (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i>rə-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">SEEN</span></span></b></i></a></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> is a city in and the <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">county seat</a> of <a href="/wiki/Racine_County,_Wisconsin" title="Racine County, Wisconsin">Racine County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. It is located on the shore of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a> at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Root_River_(Wisconsin)" title="Root River (Wisconsin)">Root River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Racine is located 22 miles south of <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a>. As of the 2013 U.S. census, the city had a population of 78,199,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uscb_quickfacts_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-uscb_quickfacts-10">[10]</a></sup> making it the fifth-largest city in Wisconsin. Its median home price of $103,625<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> makes it one of the most affordable cities in Wisconsin to buy a home. In January of 2017, it was rated "the most affordable place to live in the world" by the Demographia International Housing Affordability survey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Racine is the headquarters of a number of industries, including <a href="/wiki/Case_Corporation" title="Case Corporation">J. I. Case</a> (heavy equipment), <a href="/wiki/S._C._Johnson_%26_Son" title="S. C. Johnson &amp; Son">S. C. Johnson &amp; Son</a> (cleaning and chemical products), <a href="/wiki/Dremel" title="Dremel">Dremel</a> Corporation, <a href="/wiki/Reliance_Controls" title="Reliance Controls">Reliance Controls</a> Corporation, <a href="/wiki/Twin_Disc" title="Twin Disc">Twin Disc</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_B._Modine" title="Arthur B. Modine">Arthur B. Modine</a> (Heat Exchangers). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitchell_%26_Lewis_Company" title="Mitchell &amp; Lewis Company">Mitchell &amp; Lewis Company</a>, a wagonmaker in the 19th century, began making motorcycles and automobiles as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitchell-Lewis_Motor_Company" title="Mitchell-Lewis Motor Company">Mitchell-Lewis Motor Company</a> at the start of the 20th century. Racine was also home to <a href="/wiki/InSinkErator" title="InSinkErator">InSinkErator</a>, the first garbage disposal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malted_milk_balls" title="Malted milk balls">Malted milk balls</a> were developed in Racine. Architects of the city included <a href="/wiki/A._Arthur_Guilbert" title="A. Arthur Guilbert">A. Arthur Guilbert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Bailey_Funston" title="Edmund Bailey Funston">Edmund Bailey Funston</a>. It has several immigrant communities.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Native Americans inhabited the area of Racine as early as 10,000 BCE, but most of the artifacts that have survived, such as the burial mounds in what is now Mound Cemetery, date back only to 500 BCE or later. Historians separate the natives living in the Root watershed at that time into Woodland people, who were more common, and Hopewell people, who were more advanced. After European contact, the Miami and later the Potawatomi expanded into the area, taking part in the French fur trade.</p>
<p>In November 1674, while traveling from <a href="/wiki/Green_Bay_(Lake_Michigan)" title="Green Bay (Lake Michigan)">Green Bay</a> to the territory of the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Confederation" title="Illinois Confederation">Illinois Confederation</a>, Father <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Marquette" title="Jacques Marquette">Jacques Marquette</a> and his assistants, Jacques Largillier and Pierre Porteret, camped at the mouth of the Root River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> These were the first Europeans known to visit what is now Racine County. Further expeditions were made in the area by <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9-Robert_Cavelier,_Sieur_de_La_Salle" title="René-Robert Cavelier, Sieur de La Salle">René-Robert Cavelier, Sieur de La Salle</a> in 1679 and by François Jolliet De Montigny and <a href="/wiki/Jean_Baptiste_Bissot,_Sieur_de_Vincennes" title="Jean Baptiste Bissot, Sieur de Vincennes">Jean Baptiste Bissot, Sieur de Vincennes</a> in 1698. Nearly a century later, in 1791, a trading post would be established along <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a> near where the <a href="/wiki/Root_River_(Wisconsin)" title="Root River (Wisconsin)">Root River</a> empties into it.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Root_River_Racine_070107.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1328" data-file-width="2103" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Root_River_Racine_070107.jpg/220px-Root_River_Racine_070107.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Root_River_Racine_070107.jpg/330px-Root_River_Racine_070107.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Root_River_Racine_070107.jpg/440px-Root_River_Racine_070107.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Root_River_Racine_070107.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Root_River_(Wisconsin)" title="Root River (Wisconsin)">Root River</a>, Racine, Wisconsin</div>

<p>Following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackhawk_War" title="Blackhawk War">Blackhawk War</a>, the area surrounding Racine, which had previously been off-limits, was settled by <a href="/wiki/Yankee" title="Yankee">Yankees</a> from upstate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>. In 1834 Captain <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Knapp" title="Gilbert Knapp">Gilbert Knapp</a> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Revenue_Cutter_Service" title="United States Revenue Cutter Service">USRM</a>, who was from <a href="/wiki/Chatham,_Massachusetts" title="Chatham, Massachusetts">Chatham, Massachusetts</a>, founded the settlement of "Port Gilbert" at the place where the <a href="/wiki/Root_River_(Wisconsin)" title="Root River (Wisconsin)">Root River</a> empties into <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Knapp had first explored the area of the Root River valley in 1818, and returned with financial backing when the war ended. Within a year of Knapp's settlement hundreds of other settlers from <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> and western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> had arrived and built log cabins in the area surrounding his own. Some of the settlers were from the town of <a href="/wiki/Derby,_Connecticut" title="Derby, Connecticut">Derby, Connecticut</a>, and others came from the <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> states of Connecticut, Massachusetts, Vermont, New Hampshire and Maine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The area was previously called "Kipi Kawi" and "Chippecotton" by the indigenous peoples, both names for the Root River. The name "Port Gilbert" was never really accepted, and in 1841 the community was incorporated as the village of Racine, after the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> word for "root". After Wisconsin was admitted to the Union in 1848, the new legislature voted in August to incorporate Racine as a city.</p>
<p>In 1852, <a href="/wiki/Racine_College" title="Racine College">Racine College</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(United_States)" title="Episcopal Church (United States)">Episcopal</a> college, was founded; it closed in 1933.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Its location and many of its buildings are preserved today by the <a href="/wiki/Community_of_St._Mary" title="Community of St. Mary">Community of St. Mary</a> as part of the DeKoven Center.</p>
<p>Also in 1852, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racine_High_School" title="Racine High School">Racine High School</a>, the first public high school in Wisconsin, opened. The high school operated until 1926, when it was torn down to make way for the new Racine County Courthouse. <a href="/wiki/Washington_Park_High_School" title="Washington Park High School">Washington Park High School</a> was built to replace it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>, Racine was well known for its strong opposition to <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slavery</a>, with many slaves escaping to freedom via the <a href="/wiki/Underground_Railroad" title="Underground Railroad">Underground Railroad</a> passing through the city. In 1854 <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Glover" title="Joshua Glover">Joshua Glover</a>, an escaped slave who had made a home in Racine, was arrested by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marshals_Service" title="United States Marshals Service">federal marshals</a> and jailed in <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a>. One hundred men from Racine, and ultimately 5,000 Wisconsinites, rallied and broke into the jail to free him. He was helped to escape to <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Glover's rescue gave rise to many legal complications and a great deal of litigation. This eventually led to the <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_Supreme_Court" title="Wisconsin Supreme Court">Wisconsin Supreme Court</a> declaring the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fugitive_Slave_Law_of_1850" title="Fugitive Slave Law of 1850">Fugitive Slave Law of 1850</a> unconstitutional, and later, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wisconsin_State_Legislature" title="Wisconsin State Legislature">Wisconsin State Legislature</a> refusing to recognize the authority of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">U.S. Supreme Court</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Immigrant_culture">Immigrant culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Immigrant culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Waves of immigrants, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danish_people" title="Danish people">Danes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_people" title="German people">Germans</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Czech_people" title="Czech people">Czechs</a>, began to settle in Racine between the Civil War and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>. African Americans started arriving in large numbers during World War I, as they did in other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midwestern" title="Midwestern">Midwestern</a> industrial towns, and <a href="/wiki/Mexicans" title="Mexicans">Mexicans</a> migrated to Racine from roughly 1925 onward.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(United_States)" title="Episcopal Church (United States)">Episcopalians</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congregationalist_Church" title="Congregationalist Church">Congregationalists</a> from <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> initially dominated Racine's religious life. Racine's Emmaus Lutheran Church, the oldest Danish Lutheran Church in North America, was founded on August 22, 1851. Originally a founding member of the Danish American Lutheran Church, it has subsequently been a member of the United Danish Evangelical Lutheran Church in America (UDELCA), the <a href="/wiki/American_Lutheran_Church" title="American Lutheran Church">American Lutheran Church</a> (ALC), and, since 1988, the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America" title="Evangelical Lutheran Church in America">Evangelical Lutheran Church in America</a> (ELCA). There was also a large Catholic movement to the city, opening up churches for their own ethnicity, such as St. Stanislaus (Polish), St. Rose (Irish), Holy Name (German), St. Patrick (Irish), Sacred Heart (Italian), St. Joseph (German), St. Mary (German), Holy Trinity (Slovak), St. Casimir (Lithuanian), and others. As years passed, populations moved and St. Stanislaus, Holy Name, Holy Trinity, St. Rose, and St. Casimir merged in 1998, forming St. Richard. With new waves of people arriving, older parishes received a boost from the <a href="/wiki/Hispanic" title="Hispanic">Hispanic</a> community, which formed <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cristo_Rey_Parish&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cristo Rey Parish (page does not exist)">Cristo Rey</a>, re-energizing St. Patrick's into the strong Catholic community of today.</p>

<p>Racine has the largest <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> settlement of Danes outside of <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The city has become known for its Danish pastries, particularly <a href="/wiki/Kringle" title="Kringle">kringle</a>. Several local bakeries have been featured on the <a href="/wiki/Food_Network" title="Food Network">Food Network</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> including <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=O_%26_H_Bakery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="O &amp; H Bakery (page does not exist)">O &amp; H Bakery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bendtsen%27s_Bakery" title="Bendtsen's Bakery">Bendtsen's Bakery</a> highlighting the pastry. In June 2010, President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> stopped at an O &amp; H Bakery before hosting a town hall meeting on the economy and jobs later that afternoon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry">Industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Racine was a center of innovation and factory town almost from the beginning. The first industry in Racine County included the manufacture of fanning mills, machines that separate <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a> grain from <a href="/wiki/Chaff" title="Chaff">chaff</a>. Racine also had its share of captains of industry, including <a href="/wiki/Case_Corporation" title="Case Corporation">J. I. Case</a> (heavy equipment), <a href="/wiki/S._C._Johnson_%26_Son" title="S. C. Johnson &amp; Son">S. C. Johnson &amp; Son</a> (cleaning and chemical products), and Arthur B. Modine (Heat Exchangers). Racine's harbor was central to the shipping industry in Wisconsin in the late 19th century. Racine was also an early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_manufacturing" title="Car manufacturing">car manufacturing</a> center. One of the world's first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobiles</a> was built there in 1871 or 1872 by Dr. J. W. Cathcart,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> as was the <a href="/wiki/E._J._Pennington" title="E. J. Pennington">Pennington</a> Victoria <a href="/wiki/Tricycle" title="Tricycle">tricycle</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitchell_Motor_Car_Company" title="Mitchell Motor Car Company">Mitchell</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and the Case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1887, <a href="/wiki/Malted_milk" title="Malted milk">malted milk</a> was invented in Racine by English immigrant <a href="/wiki/William_Horlick" title="William Horlick">William Horlick</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Horlicks" title="Horlicks">Horlicks</a> remains a global brand. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garbage_disposal" title="Garbage disposal">garbage disposal</a> was invented in 1927 by <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">architect</a> John Hammes of Racine, who founded the company <a href="/wiki/InSinkErator" title="InSinkErator">InSinkErator</a>, which still produces millions of garbage disposers every year in Racine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Racine is also the home of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S.C._Johnson_%26_Son" title="S.C. Johnson &amp; Son">S.C. Johnson &amp; Son</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Wax_Headquarters" title="Johnson Wax Headquarters">headquarters</a> were designed in 1936 by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright" title="Frank Lloyd Wright">Frank Lloyd Wright</a>. Wright also designed the Wingspread Conference Center and several homes and other buildings in Racine. The city is also home to the <a href="/wiki/Dremel" title="Dremel">Dremel</a> Corporation, Reliance Controls Corporation and <a href="/wiki/Twin_Disc" title="Twin Disc">Twin Disc</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Case_New_Holland" title="Case New Holland">Case New Holland</a>’s Racine manufacturing facility, which builds two types of tractors (the New Holland T8 and the Case IH Magnum), offers public tours throughout the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p>Racine includes the <a href="/wiki/Old_Main_Street_Historic_District_(Racine,_Wisconsin)" title="Old Main Street Historic District (Racine, Wisconsin)">Old Main Street Historic District</a>. Historic buildings in Racine include the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Badger_Building&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Badger Building (page does not exist)">Badger Building</a>, <a href="/wiki/Racine_Elks_Club,_Lodge_No._252" title="Racine Elks Club, Lodge No. 252">Racine Elks Club, Lodge No. 252</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Patrick%27s_Roman_Catholic_Church_(Racine,_Wisconsin)" title="St. Patrick's Roman Catholic Church (Racine, Wisconsin)">St. Patrick's Roman Catholic Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Young_Men%27s_Christian_Association_Building_(Racine,_Wisconsin)" title="Young Men's Christian Association Building (Racine, Wisconsin)">Young Men's Christian Association Building</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chauncey_Hall_House" title="Chauncey Hall House">Chauncey Hall House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eli_R._Cooley_House" title="Eli R. Cooley House">Eli R. Cooley House</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Murray_House_(Racine,_Wisconsin)" title="George Murray House (Racine, Wisconsin)">George Murray House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hansen_House_(Racine,_Wisconsin)" title="Hansen House (Racine, Wisconsin)">Hansen House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Racine_College" title="Racine College">Racine College</a>, <a href="/wiki/McClurg_Building_(Racine,_Wisconsin)" title="McClurg Building (Racine, Wisconsin)">McClurg Building</a>, <a href="/wiki/First_Presbyterian_Church_(Racine,_Wisconsin)" title="First Presbyterian Church (Racine, Wisconsin)">First Presbyterian Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Hall_(Racine,_Wisconsin)" title="Memorial Hall (Racine, Wisconsin)">Memorial Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Racine_Depot" title="Racine Depot">Racine Depot</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Laymen_Bible_Student_Tabernacle" title="United Laymen Bible Student Tabernacle">United Laymen Bible Student Tabernacle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chauncey_Hall_Building" title="Chauncey Hall Building">Chauncey Hall Building</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_P._Hardy_House" title="Thomas P. Hardy House">Thomas P. Hardy House</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Horlick_Field" title="Horlick Field">Horlick Field</a>. The area is home to several National Register of Historic Places listed structures: <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places_listings_in_Racine_County,_Wisconsin" title="National Register of Historic Places listings in Racine County, Wisconsin">National Register of Historic Places listings in Racine County, Wisconsin</a>. The city is also home to <a href="/wiki/Regency_Mall_(Racine)" title="Regency Mall (Racine)">Regency Mall</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright" title="Frank Lloyd Wright">Frank Lloyd Wright</a> designed and built the <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Wax_Headquarters" title="Johnson Wax Headquarters">Johnson Wax Headquarters</a> building in Racine. The building was and still is considered a marvel of design innovation, despite its many practical annoyances such as leaks. Wright urged then-president Hib Johnson to build the structure outside of Racine, a city Wright thought of as "backwater." Johnson refused to have the Johnson Wax Headquarters sited anywhere other than Racine.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Racine_County_Insane_Asylum">Racine County Insane Asylum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Racine County Insane Asylum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:High_Ridge_Centre_pond.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/High_Ridge_Centre_pond.JPG/220px-High_Ridge_Centre_pond.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/High_Ridge_Centre_pond.JPG/330px-High_Ridge_Centre_pond.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/High_Ridge_Centre_pond.JPG/440px-High_Ridge_Centre_pond.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The Racine County Insane Asylum was established in 1889.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The asylum was destroyed in a fire in 1904 that was widely covered in the national press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The asylum was rebuilt after the fire, and a home for the county poor was added to the facility in 1918.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The institution was also known as the Gatliff Asylum and was served by the Gatliff stop on the railroad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Gatliff was named after Nelson Gatliff (1813–1898), an early pioneer in Racine County who owned extensive farmland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The facility was also used as a tuberculosis sanatorium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Later it was known as High Ridge Hospitals<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and High Ridge Health Care Center of Racine County.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> It was razed circa 1980. The site is now a retail development known as High Ridge Centre, with only the old pond remaining from the former asylum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>

<p>As of 2000 the median income for a household in the city was $37,164, and the median income for a family was $45,150. Males had a median income of $35,079 versus $24,279 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $17,705. About 10.8% of families and 13.9% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 20.6% of those under age 18 and 6.6% of those aged 65 or over.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_census">2010 census</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2010 census">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FactFinder_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FactFinder-3">[3]</a></sup> of 2010, there were 78,860 people, 30,530 households, and 19,222 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 5,094.3 inhabitants per square mile (1,966.9/km<sup>2</sup>). There were 33,887 housing units at an average density of 2,189.1 per square mile (845.2/km<sup>2</sup>). The racial makeup of the city was 61.8% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 22.6% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 0.5% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 0.8% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 10.3% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(U.S._Census)" title="Race (U.S. Census)">other races</a>, and 4.0% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 20.7% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 30,530 households of which 35.8% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 36.5% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 20.1% had a female householder with no husband present, 6.3% had a male householder with no wife present, and 37.0% were non-families. 30.5% of all households were made up of individuals and 9.4% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.53 and the average family size was 3.17.</p>
<p>The median age in the city was 33 years. 27.9% of residents were under the age of 18; 9.8% were between the ages of 18 and 24; 27.6% were from 25 to 44; 23.8% were from 45 to 64; and 10.9% were 65 years of age or older. The gender makeup of the city was 48.8% male and 51.2% female.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crime_rates">Crime rates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Crime rates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Racine employs <a href="/wiki/Community_policing" title="Community policing">community-oriented policing</a>, the systematic use of partnerships and problem-solving techniques to address the immediate conditions that give rise to crime. The number of crimes committed in the city in 2013 dropped in several categories to the lowest point in decades. Racine saw a 38.3 percent drop in violent crime from 2009 to 2013, making it have 10th largest decrease in the country. Property crimes were at their lowest point since 1965, while the number of violent crimes was the lowest for any year on record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrastructure">Infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Racine's municipal water is drawn from <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a>. In 2011, the city's water was named the best tasting tap water in the United States by a panel of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Conference_of_Mayors" title="U.S. Conference of Mayors">U.S. Conference of Mayors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Racine has a mayor-council form of government. The mayor is the chief executive, elected for a term of four years. The mayor appoints commissioners and other officials who oversee the departments, subject to Common Council approval. <a href="/wiki/John_Dickert" title="John Dickert">John Dickert</a> (D) has been the mayor of Racine since 2009.</p>
<p>Racine's other citywide elected official is the Municipal Judge. The city council, the legislative branch, is made up of 15 aldermen, one elected from each district in the city. The council enacts local ordinances and approves the city budget. Government priorities and activities are established in a budget ordinance usually adopted each November. Being a diverse community with a history of organized labor, the city predominantly votes for the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party (United States)</a>. However, the City's youngest City Council President was Tom Mortenson, 28, who was a leading Progressive Republican who led ethical reform that served as a model for other municipal governments.</p>
<p>Racine is represented by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ryan" title="Paul Ryan">Paul Ryan</a> (R) in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a>, and by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ron_Johnson_(Wisconsin_politician)" title="Ron Johnson (Wisconsin politician)">Ron Johnson</a> (R) and <a href="/wiki/Tammy_Baldwin" title="Tammy Baldwin">Tammy Baldwin</a> (D) in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a>. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wirch" title="Robert Wirch">Robert Wirch</a> (D) and <a href="/wiki/Van_H._Wanggaard" title="Van H. Wanggaard">Van H. Wanggaard</a> (R) represent Racine in the <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_State_Senate" title="Wisconsin State Senate">Wisconsin State Senate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tom_Weatherston" title="Tom Weatherston">Tom Weatherston</a> (R), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_Barca" title="Peter Barca">Peter Barca</a> (D), and <a href="/wiki/Cory_Mason" title="Cory Mason">Cory Mason</a> (D) represent Racine in the <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_State_Assembly" title="Wisconsin State Assembly">Wisconsin State Assembly</a>.</p>
<p>Fire protection and ambulance service is provided by the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Racine_Fire_Department" title="City of Racine Fire Department">City of Racine Fire Department</a>. Police service is provided by the Racine Police Department and <a href="/wiki/Racine_County_Sheriff" title="Racine County Sheriff">Racine County Sheriff</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_schools">Public schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Public schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Racine_Unified_School_District" title="Racine Unified School District">Racine Unified School District</a></div>
<p>Racine's public schools are administered by the Racine Unified School District, which oversees 21 elementary schools, eight middle schools, and six high schools with a combined student enrollment of around 21,000.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Higher_education">Higher education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Higher education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Parkside" title="University of Wisconsin–Parkside">University of Wisconsin–Parkside</a> is located two miles (3.2 km) south of Racine in <a href="/wiki/Kenosha,_Wisconsin" title="Kenosha, Wisconsin">Kenosha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somers,_Wisconsin" title="Somers, Wisconsin">Somers</a>. Prior to Parkside's creation there were state college campuses in both Racine and Kenosha, but with their proximity it was decided they would be better served by one larger campus in between the two cities. A campus of <a href="/wiki/Gateway_Technical_College" title="Gateway Technical College">Gateway Technical College</a>, which serves the tri-county area of the southeastern corner of Wisconsin, is located in the downtown district on Lake Michigan.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mass transit is provided by the <a href="/wiki/Belle_Urban_System" title="Belle Urban System">Belle Urban System</a> or "BUS" for short.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Taxi service is provided by Racine Taxi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Racine is also served by <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hiawatha_(Amtrak)" title="Hiawatha (Amtrak)">Hiawatha Line</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sturtevant_(Amtrak_station)" title="Sturtevant (Amtrak station)">Sturtevant station</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racine_County,_WI" title="Racine County, WI">Racine County</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Additional train service to Chicago is provided by <a href="/wiki/Metra" title="Metra">Metra</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Pacific/North_Line" title="Union Pacific/North Line">Union Pacific/North Line</a> from the downtown <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kenosha_(Metra_station)" title="Kenosha (Metra station)">Kenosha station</a>, which is located 6 miles from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racine_County,_WI" title="Racine County, WI">Racine County</a> line and 11 miles from downtown Racine.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airport">Airport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Airport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_H._Batten_Airport" title="John H. Batten Airport">Batten International Airport</a> (KRAC) is a public use airport located in Racine. Racine is one of only three Wisconsin cities, along with Milwaukee and Green Bay, to have airports with customs intake capabilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Commercial air service is provided by <a href="/wiki/O%27Hare_International_Airport" title="O'Hare International Airport">O'Hare International Airport</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitchell_Airport" title="Mitchell Airport">General Mitchell International Airport</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Racine has several examples of <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright" title="Frank Lloyd Wright">Frank Lloyd Wright</a>'s work, including the <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Wax_Headquarters" title="Johnson Wax Headquarters">Johnson Wax Headquarters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wingspread" title="Wingspread">Wingspread</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Thomas_P._Hardy_House" title="Thomas P. Hardy House">Thomas P. Hardy House</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Keland_House" title="Keland House">Keland House</a>. S.C. Johnson offers free tours of its corporate campus, and receives about 9,000 visitors per year. The <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Wax_Headquarters#Research_Tower" title="Johnson Wax Headquarters">Research Tower</a>, which is located on the SC Johnson campus, is one of only 2 existing high rise buildings designed by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright" title="Frank Lloyd Wright">Frank Lloyd Wright</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Fortaleza Hall, designed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_Foster" title="Norman Foster">Norman Foster</a>, houses the "SC Johnson Gallery: Frank Lloyd Wright At Home" and a <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright" title="Frank Lloyd Wright">Frank Lloyd Wright</a> library.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The Johnson Wax disc-shaped <a href="/wiki/Golden_Rondelle_Theater" title="Golden Rondelle Theater">Golden Rondelle Theater</a> was originally constructed as the Johnson Wax pavilion for the <a href="/wiki/1964_New_York_World%27s_Fair" title="1964 New York World's Fair">1964 New York World's Fair</a> and then <a href="/wiki/1964_New_York_World%27s_Fair#Reuse_of_pavilions_and_major_exhibits_elsewhere" title="1964 New York World's Fair">relocated</a> to Racine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Racine_Art_Museum" title="Racine Art Museum">Racine Art Museum</a>, designed by the Chicago architecture firm <a href="/wiki/Brininstool_%2B_Lynch" title="Brininstool + Lynch">Brininstool + Lynch</a>, is a modern reuse of an existing structure to house RAM’s permanent collection of contemporary craft. The building has an exterior façade of translucent acrylic panels that are illuminated at night, making the museum glow in the dark like a Japanese lantern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>The OS House, a private residence designed by the Milwaukee architecture firm <a href="/wiki/Johnsen_Schmaling_Architects" title="Johnsen Schmaling Architects">Johnsen Schmaling Architects</a>, was recognized in 2011 as one of the top 10 residential projects in the United States by the <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Architects" title="American Institute of Architects">American Institute of Architects</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">LEED Platinum</a>-certified home was also named in 2011 as one of the top 10 green projects in the country by the AIA,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> and in 2012 as one of 11 national winners in the Small Projects category.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The OS House has been featured in the <i>New York Times</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The house, an example of 21st century modern architecture, is located on the shore of Lake Michigan in Racine's south side historic district.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts_and_culture">Arts and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Arts and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Racine is home to museums, theater companies, visual arts organizations, galleries, performance groups, music organizations, dance studios, concert series and special art events<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> (for a complete list see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Arts_and_Culture_Resources_in_Racine,_Wisconsin" title="List of Arts and Culture Resources in Racine, Wisconsin">List of Arts and Culture Resources in Racine, Wisconsin</a>.)</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Racine_Art_Museum" title="Racine Art Museum">Racine Art Museum</a> is the site of the largest collection of contemporary craft in America, with over 4,000 pieces in art jewelry, ceramics, fibers, glass, metals, polymer, and wood, and over 4,000 works on paper and sculptures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> RAM's satellite campus, Wustum Museum of Fine Arts, presents exhibitions of regional artists along with art classes and workshops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The Racine Arts Council’s exhibitions feature local and regional artists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The annual Get Behind the Arts Studio Tours offer a look inside artists’ workspaces in Racine and Kenosha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Racine Theater Guild annually offers a season of seven to eight main-stage plays and musicals, Racine Children’s Theatre, Jean’s Jazz Series and Comedy Tonight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Every winter, Over Our Head Players at 6th Street Theatre hosts Snowdance, a playwriting contest in which audience members determine the winning plays. Entries for the contest come from all over the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Racine Symphony Orchestra performs 2-3 Masterworks concerts per year, several free pops concerts, and an annual concert for fifth graders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Local bands perform free noontime and evening concerts at downtown's centrally located Monument Square throughout the summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Weekly open mic opportunities for musicians and other performers are hosted by Family Power Music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>The monthly BONK! Performance Series showcases local, regional and national poets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are three opportunities for area artists and poets to receive recognition for their work: The RAM Artist Fellowship Program awards four $2,500 fellowships and solo shows every two years to artists working in a variety of media who are at all stages of their careers;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> The Racine Arts Council ArtSeed Program provides grants ranging from $500 to $1,500 to projects that are new, innovative, experimental and collaborative;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> the Racine/Kenosha Poet Laureate Program chooses one poet from Racine and one poet from Kenosha every 2 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Racine_Legion" title="Racine Legion">Racine Legion</a>, a professional <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">football</a> team and part of the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a>, played here from 1922 to 1924. Its official name was the Horlick-Racine Legion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> The team then operated as the <b>Racine Tornadoes</b> in 1926.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prom">Prom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Prom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city is known for its large prom celebration, at which students from all the high schools in the city participate in an after prom party. This was featured on the radio show <i><a href="/wiki/This_American_Life" title="This American Life">This American Life</a></i> in Episode #186 "Prom", which originally aired on June 8, 2001;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Racine's prom tradition was also the subject of the 2006 documentary <i><a href="/wiki/The_World%27s_Best_Prom" title="The World's Best Prom">The World's Best Prom</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Racine,_Wisconsin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Racine is served by the daily newspaper <i>The Journal Times</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> which is the namesake (but not current owner) of radio station <a href="/wiki/WRJN" title="WRJN">WRJN</a> (1400). The <i><a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Journal_Sentinel" title="Milwaukee Journal Sentinel">Milwaukee Journal Sentinel</a></i> formerly published a Racine-specific page on Thursdays and a Racine County section on Sundays, but dropped those in a series of downsizings that began in 2007. <i>The Insider News</i> covers issues specific to the African-American community. <i>Racinenews.org</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> offers web-based coverage for Racine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city has one television station owned by <a href="/wiki/Weigel_Broadcasting" title="Weigel Broadcasting">Weigel Broadcasting</a>, <a href="/wiki/WMLW-TV" title="WMLW-TV">WMLW-TV</a> (Channel 49), an <a href="/wiki/Independent_station_(North_America)" title="Independent station (North America)">independent station</a> which airs syndicated product, and had its analog transmitter just north of the Milwaukee County line in <a href="/wiki/Oak_Creek,_Wisconsin" title="Oak Creek, Wisconsin">Oak Creek</a>. For all intents and purposes the station serves all of southeastern Wisconsin, with the station offices located in <a href="/wiki/West_Allis,_Wisconsin" title="West Allis, Wisconsin">West Allis</a> and the station's current transmitter is located on the Weigel tower in Milwaukee's Lincoln Park. WMLW airs a Saturday morning <a href="/wiki/Public_affairs_(broadcasting)" title="Public affairs (broadcasting)">public affairs</a> program called <i>Racine &amp; Me</i>, which is produced and hosted by Jessica Tighe of sister station <a href="/wiki/WDJT-TV" title="WDJT-TV">WDJT-TV</a>, and is devoted to topics of interest to Racine residents. The station's fourth subchannel also rebroadcasts <a href="/wiki/Telemundo" title="Telemundo">Telemundo</a> affiliate <a href="/wiki/WYTU-LD" title="WYTU-LD">WYTU-LD</a>.</p>
<p>FM radio stations serving the area are <a href="/wiki/Adult_hits" title="Adult hits">adult hits</a> <a href="/wiki/WVTY" title="WVTY">WVTY</a> (92.1 FM) and <a href="/wiki/Urban_contemporary" title="Urban contemporary">urban contemporary</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WKKV" title="WKKV">WKKV</a>-FM (100.7). WVTY specifically targets Racine and Kenosha and is locally owned, while WKKV is a station owned by <a href="/wiki/IHeartMedia" title="IHeartMedia">iHeartMedia</a> that, although licensed to Racine and having a transmitter in north-central Racine County, is targeted towards Milwaukee audiences and has its offices in <a href="/wiki/Greenfield,_Wisconsin" title="Greenfield, Wisconsin">Greenfield</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sturtevant,_Wisconsin" title="Sturtevant, Wisconsin">Sturtevant</a>-licensed <a href="/wiki/WDDW" title="WDDW">WDDW</a>-FM (104.7) broadcasts a traditional Mexican music format targeting the metro area's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican-American" title="Mexican-American">Mexican-American</a> population.</p>
