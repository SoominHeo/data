<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rugby_ball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Richard_Lindon_(1816-1887).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1408" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Richard_Lindon_%281816-1887%29.jpg/150px-Richard_Lindon_%281816-1887%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Richard_Lindon_%281816-1887%29.jpg/225px-Richard_Lindon_%281816-1887%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Richard_Lindon_%281816-1887%29.jpg/300px-Richard_Lindon_%281816-1887%29.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Richard_Lindon_(1816-1887).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Richard Lindon in 1880, with two Rugby balls.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Richard_Lindon" title="Richard Lindon">Richard Lindon</a> and Bernardo Solano started making balls for <a href="/wiki/Rugby_School" title="Rugby School">Rugby school</a> out of hand stitched, leather casings and pigs’ bladders. The rugby ball's distinctive shape is supposedly due to the pig’s bladder, although early balls were more plum-shape than oval. The balls varied in size in the beginning depending upon how large the pig’s bladder was.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Until 1870, rugby was played with a near spherical ball with an inner-tube made of a pig's bladder. In 1870 Richard Lindon introduced rubber inner-tubes and because of the pliability of rubber the shape gradually changed from a sphere to an egg. In 1892 the <a href="/wiki/Rugby_Football_Union" title="Rugby Football Union">RFU</a> endorsed ovalness as the compulsory shape. The gradual flattening of the ball continued over the years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMA_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BMA-2">[2]</a></sup> The introduction of synthetic footballs over the traditional leather balls, in both rugby codes, was originally governed by weather conditions. If the playing surface was wet, the synthetic ball was used, as it didn't absorb water and become heavy. Eventually, the leather balls were phased out completely.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rugby_union">Rugby union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rugby_ball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rugby union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rugbyball2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="454" data-file-width="588" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Rugbyball2.jpg/220px-Rugbyball2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Rugbyball2.jpg/330px-Rugbyball2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Rugbyball2.jpg/440px-Rugbyball2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rugbyball2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_(sports_equipment)" title="Gilbert (sports equipment)">Gilbert</a> rugby football as used in <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The ball used in rugby union, usually referred to as a rugby ball, is a prolate spheroid essentially <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">elliptical</a> in profile. Traditionally made of brown leather, modern footballs are manufactured in a variety of colors and patterns. A regulation football is 28–30 cm (11–12 in) long and 58–62 cm (23–24 in) in circumference at its widest point. It weighs 410–460 g (14–16 oz) and is inflated to 65.7–68.8 kPa (9.5–10.0 psi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1980, leather-encased balls, which were prone to water-logging, were replaced with balls encased in synthetic waterproof materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMA_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-BMA-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Synergie" title="Gilbert Synergie">Gilbert Synergie</a> was the match ball of the <a href="/wiki/2007_Rugby_World_Cup" title="2007 Rugby World Cup">2007 Rugby World Cup</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rugby_league">Rugby league</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rugby_ball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rugby league">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Steedenfootball.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1482" data-file-width="2286" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Steedenfootball.jpg/220px-Steedenfootball.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Steedenfootball.jpg/330px-Steedenfootball.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Steedenfootball.jpg/440px-Steedenfootball.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Steedenfootball.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Steeden" title="Steeden">Steeden</a> football as used in <a href="/wiki/Rugby_league" title="Rugby league">rugby league</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rugby_league" title="Rugby league">Rugby league</a> is played with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prolate_spheroid" title="Prolate spheroid">prolate spheroid</a> shaped football which is inflated with nitrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RLIF-ILG-2004-p8_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-RLIF-ILG-2004-p8-4">[4]</a></sup> A referee will stop play immediately if the ball does not meet the requirements of size and shape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RLIF-ILG-2004-p8_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-RLIF-ILG-2004-p8-4">[4]</a></sup> Traditionally made of brown leather, modern footballs are synthetic and manufactured in a variety of colours and patterns. Senior competitions should use light coloured balls to allow spectators to see the ball more easily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RLIF-ILG-2004-p8_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-RLIF-ILG-2004-p8-4">[4]</a></sup> The football used in rugby league is known as "international size" or "size 5" and is approximately 27 cm (11 in) long and 60 cm (24 in) in circumference at its widest point. Smaller-sized balls are used for junior versions of the game, such as "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mini_footy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mini footy (page does not exist)">Mini</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Mod_league" title="Mod league">Mod</a>". A full size ball weighs between 383 and 440 g (13.5 and 15.5 oz). Rugby league footballs are slightly more pointed than rugby union footballs and larger than American footballs.</p>
<p>The Australian <a href="/wiki/National_Rugby_League" title="National Rugby League">National Rugby League</a> use balls made by <a href="/wiki/Steeden" title="Steeden">Steeden</a> while the European <a href="/wiki/Super_League" title="Super League">Super League</a> use balls made by Rhino Rugby. Steeden is also sometimes used as a noun to describe the ball itself.</p>
