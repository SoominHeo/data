<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lux</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>lux</b> (symbol: <b>lx</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI</a> unit of <a href="/wiki/Illuminance" title="Illuminance">illuminance</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luminous_emittance" title="Luminous emittance">luminous emittance</a>, measuring <a href="/wiki/Luminous_flux" title="Luminous flux">luminous flux</a> per unit area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is equal to one <a href="/wiki/Lumen_(unit)" title="Lumen (unit)">lumen</a> per square metre. In <a href="/wiki/Photometry_(optics)" title="Photometry (optics)">photometry</a>, this is used as a measure of the intensity, as perceived by the human eye, of <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> that hits or passes through a surface. It is analogous to the <a href="/wiki/Radiometry" title="Radiometry">radiometric</a> unit watts per square metre, but with the power at each <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> weighted according to the <a href="/wiki/Luminosity_function" title="Luminosity function">luminosity function</a>, a standardized model of <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> visual brightness perception. In English, "lux" is used in both singular and plural form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Explanation">Explanation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lux&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Explanation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Illuminance">Illuminance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lux&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Illuminance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Illuminance is a measure of how much <a href="/wiki/Luminous_flux" title="Luminous flux">luminous flux</a> is spread over a given area. One can think of luminous flux (measured in <a href="/wiki/Lumen_(unit)" title="Lumen (unit)">lumens</a>) as a measure of the total "amount" of visible light present, and the illuminance as a measure of the intensity of illumination on a surface. A given amount of light will illuminate a surface more dimly if it is spread over a larger area, so illuminance (lux) is inversely proportional to area when the luminous flux (lumens) is held constant.</p>
<p>One lux is equal to one lumen per <a href="/wiki/Square_metre" title="Square metre">square metre</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>1 lx = 1 lm/m<sup>2</sup> = 1 <a href="/wiki/Candela" title="Candela">cd</a>·<a href="/wiki/Steradian" title="Steradian">sr</a>/m<sup>2</sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>A flux of 1000 lumens, concentrated into an area of one square metre, lights up that square metre with an illuminance of 1000 lux. However, the same 1000 lumens, spread out over ten square metres, produces a dimmer illuminance of only 100 lux.</p>
<p>Achieving an illuminance of 500 lux might be possible in a home kitchen with a single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent_light" title="Fluorescent light">fluorescent light</a> fixture with an output of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004120000000000000♠</span>12<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span> lumens</span>. To light a factory floor with dozens of times the area of the kitchen would require dozens of such fixtures. Thus, lighting a larger area to the same level of lux requires a greater number of lumens.</p>
<p>As with other SI units, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_prefixes" title="SI prefixes">SI prefixes</a> can be used, for example a kilolux (klx) is 1000 lux.</p>

<p>The illuminance provided by a light source, on a surface perpendicular to the direction to the source, is a measure of the strength of that source as perceived from that location. For instance, a star of <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> 0 provides 2.08 microlux at the earth's surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlyter7_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schlyter7-13">[13]</a></sup> A barely perceptible magnitude 6 star provides 8 nanolux.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The unobscured sun provides an illumination of up to 100 kilolux on the Earth's surface, the exact value depending on time of year and atmospheric conditions. This direct normal illuminance is related to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_illuminance_constant&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Solar illuminance constant (page does not exist)">solar illuminance constant</a> <i>E</i><sub>sc</sub>, equal to <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005128000000000000♠</span>128<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span> lux</span> (see <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">Sunlight</a>).</p>
<p>The illumination provided on a surface by a point source equals the number of lux just described times the cosine of the angle between a ray coming from the source and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_normal" title="Surface normal">normal</a> to the surface. The illumination provided by a light source that covers a large solid angle is proportional to the cosine of the angle between the surface normal and a sort of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barycentre" title="Barycentre">barycentre</a> of the light source, so long as all of the source is above the plane of the surface. The number of lux falling on the surface equals this cosine times a number (in lux) that characterizes the source from the point of view in question.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Luminous_emittance">Luminous emittance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lux&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Luminous emittance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The lux is also a measure of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luminous_emittance" title="Luminous emittance">luminous emittance</a>, the number of lumens given off by a surface per square metre, regardless of how that light is distributed in terms of the directions in which it is emitted. It differs from the <a href="/wiki/Luminance" title="Luminance">luminance</a> (measured in <a href="/wiki/Candela_per_square_metre" title="Candela per square metre">candela per square metre</a> or "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nit_(unit)" title="Nit (unit)">nits</a>"), which does depend on the angular distribution of the emission. A perfectly white surface with one lux falling on it will emit one lux.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_between_illuminance_and_irradiance">Relationship between illuminance and irradiance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lux&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Relationship between illuminance and irradiance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like all <a href="/wiki/Photometry_(optics)" title="Photometry (optics)">photometric units</a>, the lux has a corresponding "<a href="/wiki/Radiometry" title="Radiometry">radiometric</a>" unit. The difference between any photometric unit and its corresponding radiometric unit is that radiometric units are based on physical power, with all wavelengths being weighted equally, while photometric units take into account the fact that the human eye's image-forming visual system is more sensitive to some wavelengths than others, and accordingly every wavelength is given a different weight. The weighting factor is known as the <a href="/wiki/Luminosity_function" title="Luminosity function">luminosity function</a>.</p>
<p>The lux is one lumen per square metre (lm/m<sup>2</sup>), and the corresponding radiometric unit, which measures <a href="/wiki/Irradiance" title="Irradiance">irradiance</a>, is the watt per square metre (W/m<sup>2</sup>). There is no single conversion factor between lx and W/m<sup>2</sup>; there is a different conversion factor for every wavelength, and it is not possible to make a conversion unless one knows the spectral composition of the light.</p>
<p>The peak of the luminosity function is at 555 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nm</a> (green); the eye's image-forming visual system is more sensitive to light of this wavelength than any other. For monochromatic light <i>of this wavelength</i>, the amount of illuminance for a given amount of irradiance is maximum: 683.002 lux per W/m<sup>2</sup>; the irradiance needed to make one lux at this wavelength is about 1.464 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milliwatt" title="Milliwatt">mW</a>/m<sup>2</sup>. Other wavelengths of visible light produce fewer lux per watt-per-meter-squared. The luminosity function falls to zero for wavelengths outside the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible spectrum</a>.</p>
<p>For a light source with mixed wavelengths, the number of lumens per watt can be calculated by means of the luminosity function. In order to appear reasonably "white," a light source cannot consist solely of the green light to which the eye's image-forming visual photoreceptors are most sensitive, but must include a generous mixture of red and blue wavelengths to which they are much less sensitive.</p>
<p>This means that white (or whitish) light sources produce far fewer lumens per watt than the theoretical maximum of 683.002 lm/W. The ratio between the actual number of lumens per watt and the theoretical maximum is expressed as a percentage known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luminous_efficiency" title="Luminous efficiency">luminous efficiency</a>. For example, a typical <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">incandescent light bulb</a> has a luminous efficiency of only about 2%.</p>
<p>In reality, individual eyes vary slightly in their luminosity functions. However, photometric units are precisely defined and precisely measurable. They are based on an agreed-upon standard luminosity function which is based on measurements of the spectral characteristics of image-forming <i>visual photoreception</i> in many individual human eyes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_video_camera_specifications">Use in video camera specifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lux&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Use in video camera specifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Specifications for <a href="/wiki/Video_camera" title="Video camera">video cameras</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Camcorder" title="Camcorder">camcorders</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surveillance_camera" title="Surveillance camera">surveillance cameras</a> often include a minimum illuminance level in lux at which the camera will record a satisfactory image.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A camera with good low-light capability will have a lower lux rating. <a href="/wiki/Still_camera" title="Still camera">Still cameras</a> do not use such a specification, since longer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exposure_time" title="Exposure time">exposure times</a> can generally be used to make pictures at very low illuminance levels, as opposed to the case in video cameras where a maximum exposure time is generally set by the <a href="/wiki/Frame_rate" title="Frame rate">frame rate</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-SI_units_of_illuminance"><span id="Lux_versus_footcandle"></span>Non-SI units of illuminance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lux&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Non-SI units of illuminance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The corresponding unit in English and American traditional units is the <a href="/wiki/Foot-candle" title="Foot-candle">foot-candle</a>. One foot candle is about 10.764 lux. Since one foot-candle is the illuminance cast on a surface by a one-candela source one foot away, a lux could be thought of as a "metre-candle", although this term is discouraged because it does not conform to SI standards for unit names.</p>
<p>One <a href="/wiki/Phot" title="Phot">phot</a> (ph) equals 10 kilolux.</p>
<p>One nox (nx) equals 1 millilux.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> is a measure of the illuminance of a star on the Earth's atmosphere. A star with apparent magnitude 0 is 2.54 microlux outside the earth's atmosphere, and 82% of that (2.08 microlux) under clear skies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlyter7_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schlyter7-13">[13]</a></sup> A magnitude 6 star (just barely visible under good conditions) would be 8.3 nanolux. A standard candle (one candela) a kilometre away would provide an illuminance of 1 microlux—about the same as a magnitude 1 star.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_Unicode_symbol">Legacy Unicode symbol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lux&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy Unicode symbol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> has a symbol for "lx": (㏓). It is a legacy code to accommodate old <a href="/wiki/Code_page" title="Code page">code pages</a> in some <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> languages. Use of this code is not recommended.</p>
