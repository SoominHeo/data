<p><b>Peter David Lax</b> (born 1 May 1926) is a <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungarian</a>-born <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> working in the areas of pure and applied <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>. He has made important contributions to <a href="/wiki/Integrable_system" title="Integrable system">integrable systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock waves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soliton" title="Soliton">solitonic</a> physics, hyperbolic conservation laws, and mathematical and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_computing" title="Scientific computing">scientific computing</a>, among other fields. Lax is listed as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISI_highly_cited_researcher" title="ISI highly cited researcher">ISI highly cited researcher</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_education">Life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Lax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lax was born in <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Hungary</a> to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> family. His parents Klara Kornfield and Henry Lax were both physicians, and his uncle, Albert Kornfeld (also known as Albert Korodi), was a mathematician and a friend of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le%C3%B3_Szil%C3%A1rd" title="LeÃ³ SzilÃ¡rd">LeÃ³ SzilÃ¡rd</a>. Lax began displaying an interest in mathematics at age twelve, and soon his parents hired <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B3zsa_P%C3%A9ter" title="RÃ³zsa PÃ©ter">RÃ³zsa PÃ©ter</a> as a tutor for him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmp_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-mmp-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The family left Hungary on November 15, 1941, and traveled via <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> to the United States. As a high school student at <a href="/wiki/Stuyvesant_High_School" title="Stuyvesant High School">Stuyvesant High School</a>, Lax took no math classes, but competed on the school math team; in this time, he met with <a href="/wiki/John_von_Neumann" title="John von Neumann">John von Neumann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Courant" title="Richard Courant">Richard Courant</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Erd%C5%91s" title="Paul ErdÅs">Paul ErdÅs</a>, who introduced him to <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>. As he was still 17 when he finished high school, he could avoid military service, and was able to study for three semesters at <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a>. In a <a href="/wiki/Complex_analysis" title="Complex analysis">complex analysis</a> class that he had begun in the role of a student, but ended up taking over as instructor, he met his future wife, <a href="/wiki/Anneli_Cahn_Lax" title="Anneli Cahn Lax">Anneli Cahn</a> (married to her first husband at that time).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmp_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-mmp-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conv_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-conv-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before being able to complete his studies, Lax was drafted into the U.S. Army. After basic training, the Army sent him to <a href="/wiki/Texas_A%26M_University" title="Texas A&amp;M University">Texas A&amp;M University</a> for more studies, then <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratory" title="Oak Ridge National Laboratory">Oak Ridge National Laboratory</a>, and soon afterwards to the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a> at <a href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_National_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos National Laboratory">Los Alamos</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>. At Los Alamos, he began working as a calculator operator, but eventually moved on to higher-level mathematics. After the war ended, he remained with the Army at Los Alamos for another year, while taking courses at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_New_Mexico" title="University of New Mexico">University of New Mexico</a>, then studied at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> for a semester with <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A1bor_Szeg%C5%91" title="GÃ¡bor SzegÅ">GÃ¡bor SzegÅ</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_P%C3%B3lya" title="George PÃ³lya">George PÃ³lya</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmp_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-mmp-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lax returned to NYU for the 1946-1947 academic year, and by pooling credits from the four universities at which he had studied, he graduated that year. He stayed at NYU for his graduate studies, marrying Anneli in 1948 and earning a Ph.D. in 1949 under the supervision of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kurt_O._Friedrichs" title="Kurt O. Friedrichs">Kurt O. Friedrichs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmp_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-mmp-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conv_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-conv-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Lax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a 1958 paper Lax stated a conjecture about <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics)" title="Matrix (mathematics)">matrix</a> representations for third order <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hyperbolic_polynomial&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hyperbolic polynomial (page does not exist)">hyperbolic polynomials</a> which remained unproven for over four decades. Interest in the "Lax conjecture" grew as mathematicians working in several different areas recognized the importance of its implications in their field, until it was finally proven to be true in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lax holds a faculty position in the Department of Mathematics, <a href="/wiki/Courant_Institute_of_Mathematical_Sciences" title="Courant Institute of Mathematical Sciences">Courant Institute of Mathematical Sciences</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honors">Awards and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Lax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Awards and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Academy_of_Science_and_Letters" title="Norwegian Academy of Science and Letters">Norwegian Academy of Science and Letters</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences,_USA" title="National Academy of Sciences, USA">National Academy of Sciences, USA</a>. He won a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lester_R._Ford_Award" title="Lester R. Ford Award">Lester R. Ford Award</a> in 1966<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and again in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He was awarded the <a href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Science" title="National Medal of Science">National Medal of Science</a> in 1986, the <a href="/wiki/Wolf_Prize" title="Wolf Prize">Wolf Prize</a> in 1987, the <a href="/wiki/Abel_Prize" title="Abel Prize">Abel Prize</a> in 2005 and the <a href="/wiki/Lomonosov_Gold_Medal" title="Lomonosov Gold Medal">Lomonosov Gold Medal</a> in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/American_Mathematical_Society" title="American Mathematical Society">American Mathematical Society</a> selected him as its Gibbs Lecturer for 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2012 he became a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Mathematical_Society" title="American Mathematical Society">American Mathematical Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lax also received an Honorary Doctorate from <a href="/wiki/Heriot-Watt_University" title="Heriot-Watt University">Heriot-Watt University</a> in 1990 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_CDC_6600_Incident">The CDC 6600 Incident</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Lax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The CDC 6600 Incident">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1970, the <a href="/wiki/Transcendental_Students" title="Transcendental Students">Transcendental Students</a> took a <a href="/wiki/CDC_6600" title="CDC 6600">CDC 6600</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_computer" title="Super computer">super computer</a> hostage at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NYU" title="NYU">NYU's</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Courant_Institute" title="Courant Institute">Courant Institute</a> which Lax had been instrumental in acquiring. Some of the students present, possibly members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_Underground_(organization)" title="Weather Underground (organization)">Weathermen</a>, threatened to destroy the computer with incendiary devices, but Lax managed to disable the devices and save the machine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siam_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-siam-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
