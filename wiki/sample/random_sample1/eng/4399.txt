<p>The <b>Great Exhibition of the Works of Industry of All Nations</b> or <b>The Great Exhibition</b>, sometimes referred to as the <b>Crystal Palace Exhibition</b> in reference to the temporary structure in which it was held, was an international exhibition that took place in <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_London" title="Hyde Park, London">Hyde Park</a>, London, from 1 May to 11 October 1851. It was the first in a series of <a href="/wiki/World%27s_fair" title="World's fair">World's Fairs</a>, exhibitions of <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a> that became popular in the 19th century, and it was a much anticipated event. The Great Exhibition was organized by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cole" title="Henry Cole">Henry Cole</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albert,_Prince_Consort" title="Albert, Prince Consort">Prince Albert</a>, husband of the reigning monarch, <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>. It was attended by numerous notable figures of the time, including <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Colt" title="Samuel Colt">Samuel Colt</a>, members of the <a href="/wiki/Orl%C3%A9anist" title="Orléanist">Orléanist Royal Family</a> and the writers <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Bront%C3%AB" title="Charlotte Brontë">Charlotte Brontë</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Eliot" title="George Eliot">George Eliot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alfred,_Lord_Tennyson" title="Alfred, Lord Tennyson">Alfred Tennyson</a>. Music for the opening was under the direction of Sir <a href="/wiki/George_Thomas_Smart" title="George Thomas Smart">George Thomas Smart</a> and the continuous music from the exhibited organs for the Queen's procession was "under the superintendence of <a href="/wiki/William_Sterndale_Bennett" title="William Sterndale Bennett">William Sterndale Bennett</a>".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Great_Exhibition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Great Exhibition of the Works of Industry of All Nations was organized by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Albert_of_Saxe-Coburg-Gotha" title="Prince Albert of Saxe-Coburg-Gotha">Prince Albert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cole" title="Henry Cole">Henry Cole</a>, Francis Henry, <a href="/wiki/George_Wallis" title="George Wallis">George Wallis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sir_Charles_Dilke,_2nd_Baronet" title="Sir Charles Dilke, 2nd Baronet">Charles Dilke</a> and other members of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Arts" title="Royal Society of Arts">Royal Society for the Encouragement of Arts, Manufactures and Commerce</a> as a celebration of modern <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrial</a> technology and design. It was arguably a response to the highly successful <a href="/wiki/French_Industrial_Exposition_of_1844" title="French Industrial Exposition of 1844">French Industrial Exposition of 1844</a>: indeed, its prime motive was for Britain to make "clear to the world its role as industrial leader".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kishlansky.2C_Mark_2008_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kishlansky.2C_Mark_2008-1">[1]</a></sup> Prince Albert, Queen Victoria's consort, was an enthusiastic promoter of the self-financing exhibition; the government was persuaded to form the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission_for_the_Exhibition_of_1851" title="Royal Commission for the Exhibition of 1851">Royal Commission for the Exhibition of 1851</a> to establish the viability of hosting such an exhibition. Queen Victoria and her family visited three times. Although the Great Exhibition was a platform on which countries from around the world could display their achievements, Britain sought to prove its own superiority. The British exhibits at the Great Exhibition "held the lead in almost every field where strength, durability, utility and quality were concerned, whether in iron and steel, machinery or textiles."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ffrench.2C_Yvonne_1851_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ffrench.2C_Yvonne_1851-2">[2]</a></sup> Britain also sought to provide the world with the hope of a better future. Europe had just struggled through "two difficult decades of political and social upheaval," and now Britain hoped to show that technology, particularly its own, was the key to a better future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kishlansky.2C_Mark_2008_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kishlansky.2C_Mark_2008-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sophie Forgan says of the Exhibition that "Large, piled-up ‘trophy’ exhibits in the central avenue revealed the organisers’ priorities; they generally put art or colonial raw materials in the most prestigious place. Technology and moving machinery were popular, especially working exhibits." She also notes that visitors "could watch the entire process of cotton production from spinning to finished cloth. Scientific instruments were found in class X, and included electric telegraphs, microscopes, air pumps and barometers, as well as musical, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horological" title="Horological">horological</a> and surgical instruments."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forgan.2C_S._2000_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forgan.2C_S._2000-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A special building, nicknamed <a href="/wiki/The_Crystal_Palace" title="The Crystal Palace">The Crystal Palace</a>, or "The Great <a href="/wiki/Shalimar_Gardens,_Lahore" title="Shalimar Gardens, Lahore">Shalimar</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dukemag_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-dukemag-4">[4]</a></sup> was built to house the show. It was designed by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Paxton" title="Joseph Paxton">Joseph Paxton</a> with support from <a href="/wiki/Structural_engineer" title="Structural engineer">structural engineer</a> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Fox_(civil_and_railway_engineer)" title="Charles Fox (civil and railway engineer)">Charles Fox</a>, the committee overseeing its construction including <a href="/wiki/Isambard_Kingdom_Brunel" title="Isambard Kingdom Brunel">Isambard Kingdom Brunel</a>, and went from its organisation to the grand opening in just nine months. The building was architecturally adventurous, drawing on Paxton's experience designing <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouses</a> for the <a href="/wiki/William_Cavendish,_6th_Duke_of_Devonshire" title="William Cavendish, 6th Duke of Devonshire">sixth Duke of Devonshire</a>. It took the form of a massive glass house, 1851 feet (about 564 metres) long by 454 feet (about 138 metres) wide and was constructed from <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a>-frame components and <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> made almost exclusively in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Smethwick" title="Smethwick">Smethwick</a>. From the interior, the building's large size was emphasized with trees and statues; this served, not only to add beauty to the spectacle, but also to demonstrate man's triumph over nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kishlansky.2C_Mark_2008_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kishlansky.2C_Mark_2008-1">[1]</a></sup> The Crystal Palace was an enormous success, considered an architectural marvel, but also an engineering triumph that showed the importance of the Exhibition itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ffrench.2C_Yvonne_1851_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ffrench.2C_Yvonne_1851-2">[2]</a></sup> The building was later moved and re-erected in 1854 in enlarged form at <a href="/wiki/Sydenham_Hill" title="Sydenham Hill">Sydenham Hill</a> in south London, an area that was renamed <a href="/wiki/Crystal_Palace,_London" title="Crystal Palace, London">Crystal Palace</a>. It was destroyed by fire on 30 November 1936.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dukemag_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-dukemag-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Six million people—equivalent to a third of the entire population of Britain at the time—visited the Great Exhibition. The average daily attendance was 42,831 with a peak attendance of 109,915 on 7 October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The event made a surplus of £186,000 (£18,370,000 in 2015),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-6">[6]</a></sup>, which was used to found the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">Victoria and Albert Museum</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_Museum_(London)" title="Science Museum (London)">Science Museum</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Natural_History_Museum,_London" title="Natural History Museum, London">Natural History Museum</a>. They were all built in the area to the south of the exhibition, nicknamed <a href="/wiki/Albertopolis" title="Albertopolis">Albertopolis</a>, alongside the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Institute" title="Commonwealth Institute">Imperial Institute</a>. The remaining surplus was used to set up an educational trust to provide grants and scholarships for industrial research; it continues to do so today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Exhibition caused controversy as its opening approached. Some conservatives feared that the mass of visitors might become a revolutionary mob,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newth_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-newth-8">[8]</a></sup> whilst radicals such as <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> saw the exhibition as an emblem of a <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalist</a> fetishism of commodities. King Ernest Augustus I of Hanover, shortly before his death, wrote to Lord Strangford about it:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The folly and absurdity of the Queen in allowing this trumpery must strike every sensible and well-thinking mind, and I am astonished the ministers themselves do not insist on her at least going to <a href="/wiki/Osborne_House" title="Osborne House">Osborne</a> during the Exhibition, as no human being can possibly answer for what may occur on the occasion. The idea ... must shock every honest and well-meaning Englishman. But it seems everything is conspiring to lower us in the eyes of Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In modern times, the Great Exhibition is a symbol of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victorian_Age" title="Victorian Age">Victorian Age</a>, and its thick catalogue, illustrated with steel engravings, is a primary source for High Victorian design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A memorial to the exhibition, crowned with a statue of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Albert" title="Prince Albert">Prince Albert</a>, is located behind the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Albert_Hall" title="Royal Albert Hall">Royal Albert Hall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It is inscribed with statistics from the exhibition, including the number of visitors and exhibitors (British and foreign), and the profit made.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exhibits">Exhibits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Great_Exhibition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Exhibits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The official descriptive and illustrated catalogue of the event lists exhibitors not only from throughout Britain but also from its 'Colonies and Dependencies' and 44 'Foreign States' in Europe and the Americas. Numbering 13,000 in total, the exhibits included a <a href="/wiki/Jacquard_loom" title="Jacquard loom">Jacquard loom</a>, an envelope machine, kitchen appliances, steel-making displays and a reaping machine that was sent from the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Koh-i-Noor" title="Koh-i-Noor">Koh-i-Noor</a>, meaning the "Mountain of Light," was the world's largest known diamond in 1851. It was one of the most popular attractions of the India exhibit and was acquired in 1850 as part of the Lahore Treaty.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Daria-i-Noor" title="Daria-i-Noor">Daria-i-Noor</a>, one of the rare pale pink diamonds in the world.</li>
<li>The early 8th-century <a href="/wiki/Tara_Brooch" title="Tara Brooch">Tara Brooch</a>, discovered only in 1850, the finest Irish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penannular_brooch" title="Penannular brooch">penannular brooch</a>, was exhibited by the Dublin jeweller George Waterhouse along with a display of his fashionable <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Revival" title="Celtic Revival">Celtic Revival</a> jewellery.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alfred_Charles_Hobbs" title="Alfred Charles Hobbs">Alfred Charles Hobbs</a> used the exhibition to demonstrate the inadequacy of several respected <a href="/wiki/Lock_(security_device)" title="Lock (security device)">locks</a> of the day.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Frederick_Bakewell" title="Frederick Bakewell">Frederick Bakewell</a> demonstrated a precursor to today's <a href="/wiki/Fax" title="Fax">fax</a> machine.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mathew_Brady" title="Mathew Brady">Mathew Brady</a> was awarded a medal for his <a href="/wiki/Daguerreotype" title="Daguerreotype">daguerreotypes</a>.</li>
<li>William Chamberlin, Jr. of Sussex exhibited what may have been the world's first <a href="/wiki/Voting_machine" title="Voting machine">voting machine</a>, which counted votes automatically and employed an interlocking system to prevent over-voting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firearms" title="Firearms">Firearms</a> manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Colt" title="Samuel Colt">Samuel Colt</a> demonstrated his prototype for the 1851 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colt_Navy" title="Colt Navy">Colt Navy</a> and also his older <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walker_Colt" title="Walker Colt">Walker</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colt_Dragoon" title="Colt Dragoon">Dragoon</a> revolvers.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Tempest_prognosticator" title="Tempest prognosticator">Tempest prognosticator</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">barometer</a> using <a href="/wiki/Leech" title="Leech">leeches</a>, was demonstrated at the Great Exhibition.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/America%27s_Cup" title="America's Cup">America's Cup</a> yachting event began with a race held in conjunction with the Great Exhibition.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/George_Jennings" title="George Jennings">George Jennings</a> designed the first public toilets in the Retiring Rooms of the Crystal Palace, for which he charged one penny.</li>
<li>Gold ornaments and silver enamelled handicrafts fabricated by the <a href="/wiki/Khudabadi_Sindhi_Swarankar" title="Khudabadi Sindhi Swarankar">Khudabadi Sindhi Swarankar</a> from <a href="/wiki/Sindh" title="Sindh">Sindh</a>.</li>
<li>C.C. Hornung of Copenhagen, Denmark, showed his single-cast iron frame for a <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a>, the first made in Europe.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Admission_fees">Admission fees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Great_Exhibition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Admission fees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Admission prices to the Crystal Palace varied according to the date of visit, with ticket prices decreasing as the parliamentary season drew to an end and London traditionally emptied of wealthy individuals. Prices varied from three guineas (£311.05 in 2015)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-6">[6]</a></sup> (two guineas for a woman) for a season ticket, or £1 per day (for the first two days only), then reducing to five shillings per day (until May 22).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leapman_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-leapman-14">[14]</a></sup> The admission price was then further reduced to one shilling (£4.94 in 2015),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-6">[6]</a></sup> per day – except on Fridays, when it was set at two shillings and six pence and on Saturdays when it remained at five shillings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leapman_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-leapman-14">[14]</a></sup> The one-shilling ticket proved most successful amongst the industrial classes, with four and a half million shillings (£22,217,549 in 2015),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-6">[6]</a></sup> being taken from attendees in this manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Two thousand five hundred tickets were printed for the opening day, all of which were bought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newth_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-newth-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Souvenirs:_Lane.27s_Telescopic_Views">Souvenirs: Lane's Telescopic Views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Great_Exhibition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Souvenirs: Lane's Telescopic Views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Great Exhibition of 1851 encouraged the production of souvenirs, the Lane's Telescopic Views which provided a three-dimensional view of the Great Exhibition of 1851. These were paper souvenirs that were made of printed lithographic cards which were hand-coloured and held together by cloth to give a three-dimensional view of the Great Exhibition. These views offered a miniature view of the Crystal Palace Exhibition when one viewed the cards through the peep hole on the front cover. Visitors purchased these souvenirs so that they could relive the experience of going to the exhibition.</p>
