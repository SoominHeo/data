<p>The <b>Lord Mayor of London</b> is the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mayors_in_England" title="Mayors in England">mayor</a> and leader of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London_Corporation" title="City of London Corporation">City of London Corporation</a>. Within the City, the Lord Mayor is accorded <a href="/wiki/Order_of_precedence" title="Order of precedence">precedence</a> over all individuals except the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">sovereign</a> and retains various traditional powers, rights and privileges, including the title and style <a href="/wiki/The_Right_Honourable" title="The Right Honourable">The Right Honourable</a> Lord Mayor of London.</p>
<p>This office differs from the <i><a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of London">Mayor of London</a></i>, which is a popularly elected position and covers the much larger <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a> area.</p>
<p>In 2006 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corporation_of_London" title="Corporation of London">Corporation of London</a> changed its name to the <i>City of London Corporation</i>, when the title <b>Lord Mayor of the City of London</b> was reintroduced, partly to avoid confusion with the <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of London">Mayor of London</a>. However, the legal and commonly used title remains <b>Lord Mayor of London</b>.</p>
<p>The Lord Mayor is elected at <i>Common Hall</i> each year on <a href="/wiki/Michaelmas" title="Michaelmas">Michaelmas</a>, and takes office on the Friday before the second Saturday in November, at <i>The Silent Ceremony</i>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lord_Mayor%27s_Show" title="Lord Mayor's Show">Lord Mayor's Show</a> is held on the day after taking office; the Lord Mayor, preceded by a procession, travels to the Royal Courts of Justice at the <a href="/wiki/Strand,_London" title="Strand, London">Strand</a> to swear allegiance to the sovereign before the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_judge_(England_and_Wales)" title="High Court judge (England and Wales)">Justices</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Court_of_England_and_Wales" title="High Court of England and Wales">High Court</a>.</p>
<p>One of the world's oldest continuously elected civic offices, the Lord Mayor's main role nowadays is to represent, support and promote the businesses and residents in the City of London. Today, these businesses are mostly in the financial sector and the Lord Mayor is regarded as the champion of the entire UK-based financial sector regardless of ownership or location throughout the country. As leader of the Corporation of the City of London, the Lord Mayor serves as the key spokesman for the local authority and also has important ceremonial and social responsibilities. All Lord Mayors of London are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apolitical" title="Apolitical">apolitical</a>.</p>
<p>The Lord Mayor of London typically delivers dozens of speeches and addresses per year, and attends many receptions and other events in London and beyond. Many incumbents of the office make overseas visits while Lord Mayor of London. The Lord Mayor, also <i>ex-officio</i> Rector of London's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_University_London" title="City University London">City, University of London</a> and also <a href="/wiki/Port_of_London_Authority" title="Port of London Authority">Admiral of the Port of London</a>, is assisted in day-to-day administration by the <a href="/wiki/Mansion_House,_London" title="Mansion House, London">Mansion House</a> staff who are senior administrative personnel in the Corporation of London<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and whose titles include the <a href="/wiki/Town_Clerk_of_London" title="Town Clerk of London">Town Clerk and Chief Executive</a> to <a href="/wiki/Chamberlain_(office)" title="Chamberlain (office)">Chamberlain</a> and <a href="/wiki/City_remembrancer" title="City remembrancer">Remembrancer</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Titles_and_honours">Titles and honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord_Mayor_of_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Titles and honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:William_McArthur,_Vanity_Fair,_1881-10-08.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1054" data-file-width="650" height="276" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/William_McArthur%2C_Vanity_Fair%2C_1881-10-08.jpg/170px-William_McArthur%2C_Vanity_Fair%2C_1881-10-08.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/William_McArthur%2C_Vanity_Fair%2C_1881-10-08.jpg/255px-William_McArthur%2C_Vanity_Fair%2C_1881-10-08.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/William_McArthur%2C_Vanity_Fair%2C_1881-10-08.jpg/340px-William_McArthur%2C_Vanity_Fair%2C_1881-10-08.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<p>Of the 69 cities in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the City of London is among the 30 that have Lord Mayors (or, in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lords_Provost" title="Lords Provost">Lords Provost</a>). The Lord Mayor is entitled to the style <a href="/wiki/The_Right_Honourable" title="The Right Honourable">The Right Honourable</a>; the same privilege extends only to the Lord Mayors of <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>, and to the Lords Provost of <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>. The style, however, is used when referring to the office as opposed to the holder thereof; thus, "The Rt Hon Lord Mayor of London" would be correct, while "The Rt Hon Jeffrey Mountevans" would be incorrect. The latter prefix applies only to <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Counsellors</a>.</p>
<p>A woman who holds the office is also known as a Lord Mayor. The wife of a male Lord Mayor is styled as <a href="/wiki/Lady_Mayoress" title="Lady Mayoress">Lady Mayoress</a>, but no equivalent title exists for the husband of a female Lord Mayor. A female Lord Mayor or an unmarried male Lord Mayor may appoint a female consort, usually a fellow member of the corporation, to the role of Lady Mayoress. In speech, a Lord Mayor is referred to as "My Lord Mayor", and a Lady Mayoress as "My Lady Mayoress".</p>
<p>It was once customary for Lord Mayors to be appointed <a href="/wiki/Orders,_decorations,_and_medals_of_the_United_Kingdom#Knighthood" title="Orders, decorations, and medals of the United Kingdom">knights</a> upon taking office and <a href="/wiki/Orders,_decorations,_and_medals_of_the_United_Kingdom#Baronetcy" title="Orders, decorations, and medals of the United Kingdom">baronets</a> upon retirement, unless they already held such a title. This custom was followed with a few inconsistencies from the 16th until the 19th centuries; creations became more regular from 1889 onwards. However, from 1964 onwards, the regular creation of hereditary titles such as baronetcies was phased out, so subsequent Lord Mayors were offered knighthoods (and, until 1993, most often as <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">Knight Grand Cross of the Order of the British Empire (GBE)</a>). Since 1993, Lord Mayors have not automatically received any national honour upon appointment; instead, they have been made <a href="/wiki/Knight_Bachelor" title="Knight Bachelor">Knights Bachelor</a> upon retirement, although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brown_Ministry" title="Brown Ministry">Gordon Brown's Government</a> broke with that tradition by making <a href="/wiki/Ian_Luder" title="Ian Luder">Ian Luder</a> a <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">CBE</a>, after his term of office in 2009, and the following year <a href="/wiki/Nick_Anstee" title="Nick Anstee">Nick Anstee</a> declined offers of an honour. Furthermore, foreign Heads of State visiting the City of London on a UK State Visit, diplomatically bestow upon the Lord Mayor one of their suitable national honours. For example, in 2001, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Howard_Baronets" title="Howard Baronets">Sir David Howard</a> was created a Grand Cordon (First Class) of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Independence_(Jordan)" title="Order of Independence (Jordan)">Order of Independence</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a> by <a href="/wiki/Abdullah_II_of_Jordan" title="Abdullah II of Jordan">KingÂ AbdullahÂ II</a>. Recently Lord Mayors have been appointed at the beginning of their term of office <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venerable_Order_of_Saint_John" title="Venerable Order of Saint John">Knights or Dames of St John</a>, as a mark of respect, by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">HM The Queen</a>, Sovereign Head of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venerable_Order_of_St_John" title="Venerable Order of St John">Order of St John</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord_Mayor_of_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The office of Lord Mayor was instituted in 1189, the first holder of the office being <a href="/wiki/Henry_Fitz-Ailwin_de_Londonestone" title="Henry Fitz-Ailwin de Londonestone">Henry Fitz-Ailwin de Londonestone</a>. The Mayor of the City of London has been elected by the City, rather than appointed by the Sovereign, ever since a Royal Charter providing for a Mayor was issued by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_of_England" title="John of England">King John</a> in 1215. The title "Lord Mayor" came to be used after 1354, when it was granted to Thomas Legge (then serving his second of two terms) by <a href="/wiki/Edward_II_of_England" title="Edward II of England">King Edward III</a>.</p>

<p>The last individual to serve multiple terms was <a href="/wiki/Sir" title="Sir">Sir</a> <a href="/wiki/Sir_Robert_Fowler,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Robert Fowler, 1st Baronet">Robert Fowler</a> (elected in 1883 and in 1885).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Almost 700 people have served as Lord Mayor. <a href="/wiki/Mary_Donaldson,_Baroness_Donaldson_of_Lymington" title="Mary Donaldson, Baroness Donaldson of Lymington">Dame Mary Donaldson</a>, elected in 1983, and <a href="/wiki/Fiona_Woolf" title="Fiona Woolf">Dame Fiona Woolf</a>, elected in 2013, are the only women to have held the office.</p>
<p>Some Lord Mayors in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Edward_Dalyngrigge" title="Edward Dalyngrigge">Sir Edward Dalyngrigge</a> (1392), did not reside in London. Since 1435, the Lord Mayor has been chosen from amongst the <a href="/wiki/Alderman" title="Alderman">Aldermen</a> of the City of London.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Election">Election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord_Mayor_of_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Lord Mayor is elected at Common Hall, comprising <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liveryman" title="Liveryman">liverymen</a> belonging to all of the City's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Livery_Company" title="Livery Company">livery companies</a>. Common Hall is summoned by the sitting Lord Mayor; it meets at <a href="/wiki/Guildhall,_London" title="Guildhall, London">Guildhall</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michaelmas_Day" title="Michaelmas Day">Michaelmas Day</a> (29 September) or on the closest weekday. Voting is by show of hands; if, however, any liveryman so demands, balloting is held a fortnight later.</p>
<p>The qualification to stand for election is that one must have served as a City Sheriff and be a current Alderman. Since 1385, prior service as <a href="/wiki/Sheriffs_of_the_City_of_London" title="Sheriffs of the City of London">Sheriff</a> has been mandatory for election to the Lord Mayoralty. Two Sheriffs are selected annually by Common Hall, which meets on <a href="/wiki/Midsummer" title="Midsummer">Midsummer's Day</a> for this purpose. By an ordinance of 1435, the Lord Mayor must be chosen from amongst the <a href="/wiki/Alderman" title="Alderman">Aldermen</a> of the City of London. Those on the electoral roll of each of the City's 25 Wards select one Alderman, who formerly held office for life or until resignation. Now each Alderman must submit for re-election at least once in every six years.</p>
<p>The Lord Mayor is then sworn in November, on the day before the Lord Mayor's Show (<i>see below</i>). The ceremony is known as the "Silent Ceremony" because, aside from a short declaration by the incoming Lord Mayor, no speeches are made. At Guildhall, the outgoing Lord Mayor transfers the mayoral insignia â the <a href="/wiki/Seal_(emblem)" title="Seal (emblem)">seal</a>, the purse, the sword and the <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_mace" title="Ceremonial mace">mace</a> â to the incoming Lord Mayor.</p>

<p>The day after being sworn in to office, the Lord Mayor leads a procession from the City of London to the Royal Courts of Justice in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">City of Westminster</a>, where the Lord Mayor swears allegiance to the Crown. This pageantry has evolved into one of London's longest-running and most popular annual events, known as the "Lord Mayor's Show". The Lord Mayor travels in the City's <a href="/wiki/Lord_Mayor_of_London%27s_State_Coach" title="Lord Mayor of London's State Coach">state coach</a> that was built in 1757 at a cost of Â£1,065.0s.3d. Nowadays, this festival combines traditional British pageantry with the element of <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">carnival</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and since 1959 it has been held on the second Saturday in November. Participants include the livery companies, bands and members of the military, charities and <a href="/wiki/School" title="School">schools</a>. In the evening, a fireworks display is held.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role">Role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord_Mayor_of_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Lord Mayor is a member of the City of London's governing body, the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London_Corporation" title="City of London Corporation">City of London Corporation</a> (incorporated as <i>The Mayor and Commonalty and Citizens of the City of London</i>). The Corporation comprises the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Aldermen" title="Court of Aldermen">Court of Aldermen</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Court_of_Common_Council" title="Court of Common Council">Court of Common Council</a>; the former includes only the <a href="/wiki/Alderman" title="Alderman">Aldermen</a>, while the latter includes both Aldermen and Common Councilmen. The Lord Mayor belongs to and presides over both bodies.</p>
<p>As noted earlier, the main role of the Lord Mayor is to represent, support and promote all aspects of UK-financial service industries, including maritime. They undertake this as head of the City of London Corporation and, during the year, host visiting foreign government ministers, businessmen and dignitaries; furthermore, they conduct several foreign visits of their own so as to promote British financial sectors.</p>
<p>Banquets hosted by the Lord Mayor serve as opportunities for senior Government figures to deliver major speeches. At the Lord Mayor's Banquet (held on the Monday after the Lord Mayor's Show), the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a> delivers the <a href="/wiki/Keynote" title="Keynote">keynote</a> address. At the Banker's Dinner in June, the <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer">Chancellor of the Exchequer</a> delivers a speech known as the "Mansion House Speech", which takes its name from the Lord Mayor's residence. At the Easter Banquet, also hosted each year at the Mansion House, the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Affairs" title="Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs">Foreign Secretary</a> addresses an audience of international dignitaries.</p>
<p>The Lord Mayor sometimes takes part in major state occasions, for example in 2013, the then-Lord Mayor, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Gifford" title="Roger Gifford">Roger Gifford</a> carried the Sword of Mourning at <a href="/wiki/Death_and_funeral_of_Margaret_Thatcher" title="Death and funeral of Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher's Funeral</a>, processing ahead of the <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Queen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philip,_Duke_of_Edinburgh" title="Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh">Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh</a>, into <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_Cathedral" title="St Paul's Cathedral">St Paul's Cathedral</a>.</p>
<p>The Lord Mayor performs numerous other functions, including serving as the Chief Magistrate of the City of London, <a href="/wiki/Port_of_London_Authority#Traditions" title="Port of London Authority">Admiral of the Port of London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rector_(academia)" title="Rector (academia)">Rector</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_University_London" title="City University London">City, University of London</a>, President of <a href="/wiki/Gresham_College" title="Gresham College">Gresham College</a>, President of City of London <a href="/wiki/Reserve_Forces_and_Cadets_Association" title="Reserve Forces and Cadets Association">Reserve Forces and Cadets Association</a>, and Trustee of <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_Cathedral" title="St Paul's Cathedral">St Paul's Cathedral</a>. The Lord Mayor also heads the City's Commission of Lieutenancy, which represents the Sovereign in the City of London (other counties usually have <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant" title="Lord Lieutenant">Lord Lieutenants</a>, as opposed to Commissions), and annually attends the <a href="/wiki/Treloar_School" title="Treloar School">Treloar Trust</a> (named after <a href="/wiki/Sir_William_Treloar,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir William Treloar, 1st Baronet">Sir William Treloar</a>, Lord Mayor in 1906), in <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>. The Treloar Trust runs two educational sites for disabled children, a school and college.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rights_and_privileges">Rights and privileges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord_Mayor_of_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rights and privileges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The residence of the Lord Mayor is known as <a href="/wiki/Mansion_House,_London" title="Mansion House, London">Mansion House</a>. The creation of the residence was considered after the <a href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_London" title="Great Fire of London">Great Fire of London</a> (1666), but construction did not commence until 1739. It was first occupied by a Lord Mayor in 1752, when <a href="/wiki/Gascoigne" title="Gascoigne">Sir Crispin Gascoigne</a> took up residence.</p>
<p>In each of the eighteen courtrooms of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Criminal_Court,_London" title="Central Criminal Court, London">Old Bailey</a>, the centre of the judges' bench is reserved for the Lord Mayor, in his capacity of Chief Justice of the City of London. The presiding judge therefore sits to one side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is sometimes asserted that the Lord Mayor may exclude the monarch from the City of London. The legend is based on the misinterpretation of the ceremony observed each time the sovereign enters the City. At <a href="/wiki/Temple_Bar,_London" title="Temple Bar, London">Temple Bar</a> the Lord Mayor presents the City's pearl-encrusted sword of state to the sovereign as a symbol of the latter's overlordship. The monarch does not, as is often purported, wait for the Lord Mayor's permission to enter the City. When the sovereign enters the city, a short ceremony usually takes place where the Lord Mayor symbolically surrenders his or her authority to the monarch by presenting the sword to them. If the sovereign is attending a service at St Paul's this ceremony would take place there rather than at the boundary of the City for matters of convenience.</p>
<p>The importance of the office is reflected by the composition of the <a href="/wiki/Accession_Council" title="Accession Council">Accession Council</a>, a body which proclaims the accession of new Sovereigns. The Council includes the Lord Mayor and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aldermen" title="Aldermen">Aldermen</a> of London, as well as members of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> and <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Counsellors</a>. At the <a href="/wiki/Coronation_of_the_British_monarch" title="Coronation of the British monarch">coronation banquet</a> which followed, the Lord Mayor of the City of London had the right to assist the Royal Butler. The same privilege is held by the <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Lord Mayor of Oxford</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_Winchester" title="Mayor of Winchester">Mayor of Winchester</a> may assist the Royal Cook. Such privileges have not been exercised since 1821, when the last coronation banquet (commemorating the coronation of <a href="/wiki/George_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George IV of the United Kingdom">George IV</a>) was held.</p>

<p>On formal occasions the Lord Mayor wears traditional black velvet <a href="/wiki/Court_uniform_and_dress_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Court uniform and dress in the United Kingdom">court dress</a> (old style) consisting of a coat, waistcoat and knee breeches with steel cut buttons. This is worn with black silk stockings, patent court shoes with steel buckles, white shirt with lace cuffs and a large jabot stock. This form of court dress is worn by all Lord Mayors regardless of gender.</p>
<p>When outdoors, they wear a black beaver plush tricorne hat trimmed with black ostrich feathers and a steel 'loop' for the cockade. This has been traditionally made by Patey's commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Worshipful_Company_of_Feltmakers" title="Worshipful Company of Feltmakers">Worshipful Company of Feltmakers</a> for each incumbent Lord Mayor.</p>
<p>Since 1545 the Lord Mayor of London has worn a Royal Livery <a href="/wiki/Livery_collar" title="Livery collar">Collar of Esses</a>. However, the collar's origins are not royal, Sir John Alleyn, thrice Lord Mayor, having bequeathed it to the next Lord Mayor and his successors "to use and occupie yerely at and uppon principall and festivall dayes." It was enlarged in 1567, and in its present shape has 28 Esses (the Lancastrian âSâ), Tudor roses and the tasselled knots of the Garter (alternating) and also the Portcullis, from which hangs the Mayoral Jewel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weinreb_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weinreb-9">[9]</a></sup> The collar is worn over whatever the Lord Mayor may be wearing, secured onto their underdress or State Robes by means of black or white silk satin ribbons on the shoulders.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Robes">Robes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lord_Mayor_of_London&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Robes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alderman_of_the_City_of_London" title="Alderman of the City of London">Alderman of the City of London</a> the Lord Mayor has a scarlet gown and a violet gown, which are identical to those worn by his or her fellow aldermen except that they are <a href="/wiki/Train_(clothing)" title="Train (clothing)">trained</a>. The violet robe is worn at most formal meetings of the Corporation with the scarlet robe substituted on certain days or occasions as directed by the City Ceremonial Book.</p>
<p>For State occasions when the monarch is present, the Lord Mayor wears a crimson velvet robe of state trimmed with an ermine cape and facings, very similar to a royal earl's coronation robe. It is tied with gold cordons, and dates from the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_IV" title="George IV">George IV</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weinreb_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weinreb-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>On other ceremonial occasions is worn a black silk damask robe trimmed with gold lace of a design exactly the same as that of the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a>, known as the Entertaining Gown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weinreb_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Weinreb-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>At coronations, the Lord Mayor wears a special coronation robe: a mantle of scarlet superfine wool trimmed with bars of gold lace and ermine. It is lined with white silk satin; they also carry a baton of office. After the coronation, the incumbent may personally keep their coronation robe as a token.</p>
<p>There is also a plain black gown, worn by the Lord Mayor in times of national mourning and/or grief.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weinreb_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Weinreb-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
