<p>The <i><b>London Evening Standard</b></i> (simply the <i><b>Evening Standard</b></i> before May 2009) is a local, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_daily_newspaper" title="Free daily newspaper">free daily newspaper</a>, published Monday to Friday in <a href="/wiki/Tabloid_(newspaper_format)" title="Tabloid (newspaper format)">tabloid</a> format in London.</p>
<p>It is the dominant local/regional evening paper for London and the surrounding area, with coverage of national and international news and <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> finance. In October 2009, the paper ended a 180-year history of <a href="/wiki/Newspaper_circulation" title="Newspaper circulation">paid circulation</a> and became a free newspaper, doubling its circulation as part of a change in its business plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eandp09_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-eandp09-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_Evening_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The newspaper was founded by barrister <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Lees_Giffard" title="Stanley Lees Giffard">Stanley Lees Giffard</a> on 21 May 1827, as the <i>Standard</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The early owner of the paper was Charles Baldwin. Under the ownership of <a href="/wiki/James_Johnstone_(publisher)" title="James Johnstone (publisher)">James Johnstone</a>, <i>The Standard</i> became a morning paper from 29 June 1857. <i>The Evening Standard</i> was published from 11 June 1859. <i>The Standard</i> gained eminence for its detailed foreign news, notably its reporting of events of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> (1861–1865), the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Prussian_War" title="Austro-Prussian War">Austro-Prussian War</a> of 1866, and the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a> of 1870, all contributing to a rise in circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> By the end of the 19th century, the evening edition eclipsed its morning counterpart, which folded.</p>
<p>At the turn of the century it was owned by Sir Edward Hulton, who decided to make the paper compete with the fledging <i>Daily Mail</i>. He also introduced the gossip column <i>Londoner's Diary</i>, originally billed as "a column written by gentlemen for gentlemen". In 1923, Lord Beaverbrook, owner of the <i>Daily Express</i>, bought Hulton's newspapers, although he sold them shortly thereafter to the <i>Mail'</i>s owner Lord Rothermere, with the exception of the <i>Standard</i>. It became a staunchly Conservative paper, harshly attacking Labour in 1945 in a high-profile campaign that ultimately backfired. In the 1960s the paper was now upstaged by the <i>Evening News</i>, which sold over 1 million copies nightly. During the decade, the paper also began to publish the comic strip <i>Modesty Blaise</i> that bolstered the paper's sales throughout the 1970s.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Express_Newspapers" title="Express Newspapers">Express Newspapers</a> merged the <i>Standard</i> in 1980 with the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evening_News_(London)" title="Evening News (London)">Evening News</a></i> in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_Operating_Agreement" title="Joint Operating Agreement">Joint Operating Agreement</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Associated_Newspapers" title="Associated Newspapers">Associated Newspapers</a>' <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evening_News_(London)" title="Evening News (London)">Evening News</a></i>, becoming known as the <i><b>Newstandard</b></i> until 1985 when Associated Newspapers bought out the remaining stake, turning into <i>The Standard</i>. In 1987 the <i>Evening News</i> was briefly revived to compete with Robert Maxwell's <i><a href="/wiki/London_Daily_News" title="London Daily News">London Daily News</a></i>, reverting into the <i>Evening Standard</i>. In 1988 it incorporated the byline "Incorporating the 'Evening News'", which remained until the paper's sale in 2009.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lebedev_takeover">Lebedev takeover</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_Evening_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lebedev takeover">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 21 January 2009, the Russian businessman and former <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a> agent <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Lebedev" title="Alexander Lebedev">Alexander Lebedev</a> and his son <a href="/wiki/Evgeny_Lebedev" title="Evgeny Lebedev">Evgeny Lebedev</a>, owners of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i>, agreed to purchase control of the newspaper at £1 for 64 percent ownership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> A few years earlier, 12 percent of the paper was sold to Justin Shaw and Geordie Greig. Associated News keeps the remaining 24 percent.</p>
<p>In November 2009, it was announced that the <i>London Evening Standard</i> would drop its midday "News Extra" edition from 4 January 2010 with the first edition being the West End Final, available from 2 pm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G26N09_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-G26N09-8">[8]</a></sup> One edition of 600,000 copies would be printed starting at 12:30 pm, ending 3 am starts for journalists and the previous deadline of 9 am for the first edition; twenty people were expected to lose their jobs as a result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G26N09_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-G26N09-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Previously there were three editions each weekday, excluding <a href="/wiki/Bank_holiday" title="Bank holiday">Bank holidays</a>. The first, "News Extra", went to print at 10:00 am and was available around 11 am in central London,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> slightly later in more outlying areas (such as Kent). A second edition, "West End Final", went to print at 3 pm, and the "Late Night Final" went to print at 5 pm and was available in the central area from about 6 pm<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> There was often considerable variation between the editions, particularly with the front page lead and following few pages, including the <a href="/wiki/Londoner%27s_Diary" title="Londoner's Diary">Londoner's Diary</a>, though features and reviews stayed the same. The page changes were indicated by stars in the bottom left-hand corner of each page: two stars for the second edition, three stars for the third.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In January 2010, circulation was increased to 900,000.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="May_2009_relaunch">May 2009 relaunch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_Evening_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: May 2009 relaunch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 2009, the newspaper launched a series of poster ads, each of which prominently featured the word "Sorry" in the paper's then-masthead font. These ads offered various apologies for past editorial approaches, such as "Sorry for losing touch".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> None of the posters mentioned the <i>Evening Standard</i> by name, although they featured the paper's <a href="/wiki/Eros" title="Eros">Eros</a> logo. Ex-editor <a href="/wiki/Veronica_Wadley" title="Veronica Wadley">Veronica Wadley</a> criticised the "Pravda-style" campaign saying it humiliated the paper's staff and insulted its readers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The campaign was designed by <a href="/wiki/McCann_Erickson" title="McCann Erickson">McCann Erickson</a>. Also in May 2009 the paper relaunched as the <i>London Evening Standard</i> with a new layout and masthead, marking the occasion by giving away 650,000 free copies on the day,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and refreshed its sports coverage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="October_2009:_freesheet">October 2009: freesheet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_Evening_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: October 2009: freesheet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After a long history of paid circulation, on 12 October 2009 the <i>Standard</i> became a free newspaper,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eandp09_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-eandp09-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> with free circulation of 700,000, limited to central London. In February 2010, a paid-for circulation version became available in suburban areas of London for 20p (although many places sell it for 50p).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The newspaper won the <i>Media Brand of the Year</i> and the <i>Grand Prix Gold</i> awards at the Media Week awards in October 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-topawards_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-topawards-16">[16]</a></sup> The judges said, "[the Standard has] quite simply ... stunned the market. Not just for the act of going free, but because editorial quality has been maintained, circulation has almost trebled and advertisers have responded favourably. Here is a media brand restored to health."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-topawards_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-topawards-16">[16]</a></sup> The <i>Standard</i> also won the daily newspaper of the year award at the London Press Club Press Awards in May 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Editorial_style">Editorial style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_Evening_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Editorial style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The newspaper's editor is <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Sands" title="Sarah Sands">Sarah Sands</a>, who replaced <a href="/wiki/Geordie_Greig" title="Geordie Greig">Geordie Greig</a> following his departure to the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mail_on_Sunday" title="Mail on Sunday">Mail on Sunday</a></i> in March 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Veronica_Wadley" title="Veronica Wadley">Veronica Wadley</a> was the newspaper's editor between 2002 and 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Max_Hastings" title="Max Hastings">Max Hastings</a> was editor from 1996 until he retired in 2002.</p>
<p>The <i>Evening Standard</i>, although a regional newspaper for London, also covers national and international news, though with an emphasis on London-centred news (especially in its features pages), covering building developments, property prices, traffic schemes, politics, the congestion charge and, in the <i><a href="/wiki/Londoner%27s_Diary" title="Londoner's Diary">Londoner's Diary</a></i> page, gossip on the social scene. It also occasionally runs campaigns on local issues that national newspapers do not cover in detail.</p>
<p>It has a tradition of providing arts coverage. Its most well known visual art critic is the late <a href="/wiki/Brian_Sewell" title="Brian Sewell">Brian Sewell</a>. Sewell was noted for his acerbic view of <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_art" title="Conceptual art">conceptual art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Young_British_Artists" title="Young British Artists">Britart</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Turner_Prize" title="Turner Prize">Turner Prize</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and his views attracted controversy and criticism in the art world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> He has been described as "Britain's most famous and controversial art critic".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008_London_mayoral_election">2008 London mayoral election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_Evening_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2008 London mayoral election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2008_London_mayoral_election" title="2008 London mayoral election">2008 London mayoral election</a> the newspaper –  and particularly its correspondent <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Gilligan" title="Andrew Gilligan">Andrew Gilligan</a> –  published articles in support of the Conservative candidate, <a href="/wiki/Boris_Johnson" title="Boris Johnson">Boris Johnson</a>, including frequent front-page headlines condemning <a href="/wiki/Ken_Livingstone" title="Ken Livingstone">Ken Livingstone</a>. This included the headline "Suicide bomb backer runs Ken's campaign".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_general_election">2010 general election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_Evening_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2010 general election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 5 May 2010, the newspaper stated in an editorial that, having supported <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> under <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>, the newspaper would be supporting <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservatives</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">General Election</a>, saying that "the Conservatives are ready for power: they look like a government in waiting."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2015_general_election">2015 general election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_Evening_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2015 general election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 5 May 2015, an editorial stated that the newspaper would again be supporting <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> and the Conservatives in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2015" title="United Kingdom general election, 2015">2015 General Election</a>, saying that the Conservatives have "shown themselves to be good for London."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genelec2015_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-genelec2015-25">[25]</a></sup> The newspaper did however also claim "there may be good tactical reasons to vote Liberal Democrat."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genelec2015_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-genelec2015-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2016_London_mayoral_election">2016 London mayoral election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_Evening_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2016 London mayoral election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A study conducted by the <a class="external text" href="http://www.mediareform.org.uk/" rel="nofollow">Media Reform Coalition</a> (MRC) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goldsmiths_University_of_London" title="Goldsmiths University of London">Goldsmiths University of London</a> argued that, in <a href="/wiki/London_mayoral_election,_2016" title="London mayoral election, 2016">the 2016 elections</a> for the new <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of London">Mayor of London</a>, the <i>London Evening Standard</i> was the "mouthpiece of the Conservative Party", according to MRC chair <a class="external text" href="http://www.bbk.ac.uk/culture/staff/teaching-staff/justin-schlosberg" rel="nofollow">Justin Schlosberg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martinson27April_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Martinson27April-26">[26]</a></sup> Schlosberg accused the <i>Standard</i> of being</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"systematically biased in the way that headlines and news stories were framed, selected and prioritised. This is all the more concerning because bias was concentrated in relatively prominent headlines and in news reports where readers are less likely to expect opinion to be mobilised."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>There were almost twice as many positive headlines about the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> candidate, <a href="/wiki/Zac_Goldsmith" title="Zac Goldsmith">Zac Goldsmith</a>, than for his Labour rival, <a href="/wiki/Sadiq_Khan" title="Sadiq Khan">Sadiq Khan</a>, with stories exhibiting the strongest bias against Khan also being the most prominent. 13 out of 15 official press releases from the Goldsmith campaign in the two months to 12 April were published as news stories in the paper, "reproducing headlines from the news release virtually verbatim", according to the study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martinson27April_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Martinson27April-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Freesheet_and_supplements">Freesheet and supplements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_Evening_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Freesheet and supplements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>On 14 December 2004, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Associated_Newspapers" title="Associated Newspapers">Associated Newspapers</a> launched a Monday–Friday <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_daily_newspaper" title="Free daily newspaper">freesheet</a> edition of the <i>Evening Standard</i> called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Lite" title="Standard Lite">Standard Lite</a></i> to help boost circulation. This had 48 pages, compared with about 80 in the main paper, which also had a supplement on most days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2006, the freesheet was relaunched as <i><a href="/wiki/London_Lite" title="London Lite">London Lite</a></i>. It was designed to be especially attractive to younger female readers, and featured a wide range of lifestyle articles, but less news and business news than the main paper. It was initially only available between 11.30 a.m. and 2.30 p.m. at <i>Evening Standard</i> vendors and in the central area, but later became available in the evening from its street distributors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> With the sale of the <i>Evening Standard</i>, but not the <i>London Lite</i>, to Alexander Lebedev on 21 January 2009, the ownership links between the <i>Standard</i> and the <i>Lite</i> were broken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>On Fridays, the newspaper includes a free glossy lifestyle magazine, <i>ES</i> (originally the <i>Evening Standard Magazine</i>, circulating on Saturdays until 1974), and the circulation was increased to 350,000 in September 2014. This has moved from more general articles to concentrate on glamour, with features on the rich, powerful and famous. On Wednesdays, readers can pick up a free copy of the <i>Homes &amp; Property</i> supplement, edited by Janice Morley, which includes London property listings as well as articles from lifestyle journalists including Barbara Chandler, Katie Law and <a href="/wiki/Alison_Cork" title="Alison Cork">Alison Cork</a>.</p>
<p>An entertainment guide supplement <i>Metro Life</i> (previously called <i>Hot Tickets</i>) was launched in September 2002. This was a what's-on guide with listings of cinemas and theatres in and around London, and was given away on Thursdays. It was discontinued on 1 September 2005.</p>
<p>The paper also supplies occasional CDs and DVDs for promotions. It also give Londoners a chance to win exclusive tickets to film premieres and sports tournament tickets, such as the <a href="/wiki/The_Championships,_Wimbledon" title="The Championships, Wimbledon">Wimbledon</a> Ladies Singles Final.</p>
