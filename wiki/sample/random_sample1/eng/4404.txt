<p><b>Sir Run Run Shaw</b>, <a href="/wiki/Grand_Bauhinia_Medal" title="Grand Bauhinia Medal">GBM</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">CBE</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> November 1907 – 7 January 2014), also known as <b>Shao Yifu</b> and <b>Siu Yat-fu</b>, was a Hong Kong entertainment mogul and philanthropist. He was one of the most influential figures in the Asian entertainment industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc_news_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-abc_news-4">[4]</a></sup> He founded the <a href="/wiki/Shaw_Brothers_Studio" title="Shaw Brothers Studio">Shaw Brothers Studio</a>, one of the largest film production companies in Hong Kong, and <a href="/wiki/TVB" title="TVB">TVB</a>, the dominant television company in Hong Kong.</p>
<p>A well-known philanthropist, Shaw donated billions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_dollars" title="Hong Kong dollars">Hong Kong dollars</a> to educational institutions in Hong Kong and mainland China. More than 5,000 buildings on Chinese college campuses bear his name,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinadaily_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-chinadaily-5">[5]</a></sup> as does <a href="/wiki/Shaw_College_(Hong_Kong)" title="Shaw College (Hong Kong)">Shaw College</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_University_of_Hong_Kong" title="Chinese University of Hong Kong">Chinese University of Hong Kong</a>. He also established the <a href="/wiki/Shaw_Prize" title="Shaw Prize">Shaw Prize</a> for Astronomy, Life Science &amp; Medicine and Mathematical Sciences.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run_Run_Shaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Run Run Shaw was born in <a href="/wiki/Ningbo" title="Ningbo">Ningbo</a>, Zhejiang, <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing China</a>, and moved to Shanghai as a child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He was the youngest of the six sons of Shanghai textile merchant Shaw Yuh Hsuen (1867–1920) and his wife Wang Shun Xiang (1871–1939).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-who_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-who-9">[9]</a></sup> His name at birth was <b>Shao Renleng</b> (邵仁楞), which was later changed to Shao Yifu (邵逸夫) because he thought that the average Chinese person would not know how to pronounce the character 楞 (<i>léng</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mingbao_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-mingbao-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> There are a number of explanations given for the use of his English name Run Run Shaw,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinadaily_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-chinadaily-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-6">[6]</a></sup> but Shaw reportedly said that it was simply a transliteration of his birth name Shao Renleng in the <a href="/wiki/Ningbo_dialect" title="Ningbo dialect">Ningbo dialect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinadaily_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-chinadaily-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mingbao_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-mingbao-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is some confusion surrounding Run Run Shaw's exact date of birth. He celebrated his birthdays on the 14th day of the 10th month of the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calendar" title="Chinese calendar">Chinese calendar</a>, which fell on 23 November in 2007, his 100th birthday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Birth_date_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Birth_date-1">[1]</a></sup> Many sources use 23 November 1907 as his birthdate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wall_Street_Journal_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_Street_Journal-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-6">[6]</a></sup> However, the 14th day of the 10th month of the Chinese calendar in 1907 corresponds to 19 November 1907 on the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a>, which, according to <i><a href="/wiki/China_Daily" title="China Daily">China Daily</a></i> was his birthdate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinadaily2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-chinadaily2-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>He graduated from the Shanghai YMCA School, where he learned English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-scmp-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run_Run_Shaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_ventures">Early ventures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run_Run_Shaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early ventures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1925, Shaw's brothers, led by the eldest brother <a href="/wiki/Runje_Shaw" title="Runje Shaw">Runje Shaw</a>, established <a href="/wiki/Tianyi_Film_Company" title="Tianyi Film Company">Tianyi Film Company</a> (also called Unique Film Productions) in Shanghai,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Hollywood_Reporter_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Hollywood_Reporter-15">[15]</a></sup> and Run Run began his film career doing odd jobs for the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinadaily_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-chinadaily-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1927, Shaw, then 19 years old, went to Singapore to assist his third elder brother <a href="/wiki/Runme_Shaw" title="Runme Shaw">Runme Shaw</a> in their business venture there, initially to market films to Southeast Asia's Chinese community. They established the company that would later become the <a href="/wiki/Shaw_Organisation" title="Shaw Organisation">Shaw Organisation</a>, and were involved in distributing and producing films in Southeast Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Hollywood_Reporter_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Hollywood_Reporter-15">[15]</a></sup> Tianyi produced what is considered the first <a href="/wiki/Sound-on-film" title="Sound-on-film">sound-on-film</a> Chinese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Talkie" title="Talkie">talkie</a> in 1931,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and made the first Cantonese sound film in 1932. It was highly successful, and Tianyi established a branch in Hong Kong in 1934.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Hollywood_Reporter_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-The_Hollywood_Reporter-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Just before the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Shanghai" title="Battle of Shanghai">Japanese invasion of Shanghai</a> in 1937, Tianyi moved its operation to Hong Kong, shipping its equipment from Shanghai.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Its studio in Shanghai was destroyed when the Japanese occupied the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In Hong Kong, Tianyi was reorganised as Nanyang Studio, which later became <a href="/wiki/Shaw_Brothers_Studio" title="Shaw Brothers Studio">Shaw Brothers Studio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Shaw was credited with scripting and directing the 1937 comedy film <i>Country Bumpkin Visits His In-laws</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ST_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ST-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his early days in Singapore, Run Run Shaw supervised the company's business while Runme travelled north to <a href="/wiki/British_Malaya" title="British Malaya">Malaya</a> to establish ties with local theatre owners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 1927, having noticed that there were few cinemas in Malaya, Runme decided to open four cinemas there to show their films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asiaweek_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Asiaweek-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> By 1939, the brothers owned a chain of 139 cinemas across the region;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> the chain would later include Singapore's first air-conditioned cinema, at <a href="/wiki/Beach_Road,_Singapore" title="Beach Road, Singapore">Beach Road</a>. They also established a number of amusement parks throughout the region, including Borneo, Thailand and Java, such as the <a href="/wiki/Great_World_Amusement_Park" title="Great World Amusement Park">Great World Amusement Park</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kim_Seng" title="Kim Seng">Kim Seng Road</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ST_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-ST-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HKU_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-HKU-26">[26]</a></sup> The brothers began to make Malay films in Singapore in 1937.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-malay_films1_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-malay_films1-27">[27]</a></sup> Inspired by the success of films intended for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic_Malays" title="Ethnic Malays">Malay</a> audiences, for example <i>Leila Majnun</i> in 1934,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-malay_films1_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-malay_films1-27">[27]</a></sup> and other films from the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch East Indies</a>, the brothers established Malay Film Productions (MFP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> This company would eventually produce over 160 Malay films,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> many of them starring and directed by <a href="/wiki/P._Ramlee" title="P. Ramlee">P. Ramlee</a>, until their studio at <a href="/wiki/Jalan_Ampas" title="Jalan Ampas">Jalan Ampas</a> ceased production in 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The period between the end of the 1940s and the beginning of the 1960s is known as the Golden Age of Malay Cinema, with over 300 films made between MFP and <a href="/wiki/Cathay_Organisation" title="Cathay Organisation">Cathay Keris</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In 1941, the Japanese invaded Singapore and Malaya and confiscated their film equipment. According to Shaw, he and his brother buried more than $4 million in gold, jewellery and cash in their backyard, digging it up after the war and using it to rebuild their business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shaw_Brothers_Studios">Shaw Brothers Studios</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run_Run_Shaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Shaw Brothers Studios">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1957, Run Run Shaw moved to Hong Kong, which was emerging as the new centre of Chinese-language cinema, and reorganised Tianyi's operations there as Shaw Brothers Studio. Shaw copied Hollywood by setting up a permanent production site where his actors worked and lived on 46 acres purchased from the government in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clearwater_Bay" title="Clearwater Bay">Clearwater Bay</a>. At the opening of the Shaw Movietown in December 1961, Shaw Studios had the world's largest privately owned film-production outfit with about 1,200 workers shooting and editing films daily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg-34">[34]</a></sup> Shaw productions ran up to two hours and cost as much as $50,000, a lavish sum by Asian standards in the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the 1960s, Shaw Brothers had become the biggest producer of movies in Asia. Notable films produced by Shaw include director <a href="/wiki/Li_Han-hsiang" title="Li Han-hsiang">Li Han-hsiang</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magnificent_Concubine" title="The Magnificent Concubine">The Magnificent Concubine</a></i>, which took the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Prix_(Cannes_Film_Festival)" title="Grand Prix (Cannes Film Festival)">Grand Prix</a> at the <a href="/wiki/1962_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="1962 Cannes Film Festival">1962 Cannes Film Festival</a>; the 1963 blockbuster musical film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Love_Eterne" title="The Love Eterne">The Love Eterne</a></i>, also directed by Li Han-hsiang; <a href="/wiki/King_Hu" title="King Hu">King Hu</a>'s 1966 pioneering <a href="/wiki/Wuxia" title="Wuxia">wuxia</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Come_Drink_with_Me" title="Come Drink with Me">Come Drink with Me</a></i>; and <a href="/wiki/Chang_Cheh" title="Chang Cheh">Chang Cheh</a>'s 1967 <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_One-Armed_Swordsman" title="The One-Armed Swordsman">The One-Armed Swordsman</a></i>, which broke box office records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> His companies in Shanghai, Singapore and Hong Kong made more than 1,000 movies, with annual production peaking at 50 pictures in 1974 when Shaw was described as the "Czar of Asian Movies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The popular nostalgic costume dramas of Shaw Brothers celebrated traditional Chinese values and culture, which was in contrast to the then anti-traditional ideology of Communist mainland China (particularly during the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Revolution" title="Cultural Revolution">Cultural Revolution</a>), but fit with the policy of the Nationalist government of Taiwan and US anti-Communist strategy, while not conflicting with the less provocative approach of the colonial government of Hong Kong.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Shaw Studios also popularised the <a href="/wiki/Martial_arts_film" title="Martial arts film">kung fu genre</a> that had great influence on Eastern and Hollywood directors such as <a href="/wiki/John_Woo" title="John Woo">John Woo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quentin_Tarantino" title="Quentin Tarantino">Quentin Tarantino</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc_news_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-abc_news-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The studio declined in the 1970s, partly due to competition from <a href="/wiki/Orange_Sky_Golden_Harvest" title="Orange Sky Golden Harvest">Golden Harvest</a> formed by his ex-employee <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Chow" title="Raymond Chow">Raymond Chow</a>, which came to prominence through kung fu star <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Lee" title="Bruce Lee">Bruce Lee</a> whom Shaw Brothers had previously turned down. Shaw began to focus his efforts on television.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Birth_date_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Birth_date-1">[1]</a></sup> Shaw also looked for opportunities in the United States and co-produced a handful of US films, including the 1982 sci-fi classic <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_Runner" title="Blade Runner">Blade Runner</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc_news_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-abc_news-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2000, through his company, Shaw Brothers (Hong Kong) Limited, he sold his library of 760 classic titles to Celestial Pictures Limited. Shaw Studios also entered a new era with Shaw's majority investment (through his various holding companies) in the US$180,000,000 Hong Kong Movie City project, a 1,100,000 square feet (100,000 m<sup>2</sup>) studio and production facility in <a href="/wiki/Tseung_Kwan_O" title="Tseung Kwan O">Tseung Kwan O</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-studio_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-studio-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television_Broadcasts_Limited">Television Broadcasts Limited</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run_Run_Shaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Television Broadcasts Limited">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1967, he co-founded <a href="/wiki/TVB" title="TVB">TVB</a>, the first free-to-air television station in Hong Kong, growing it into a multibillion-dollar TV empire with channels broadcast in 30 markets including the US, Canada and Taiwan, making it the world's largest producer of Chinese-language programs. Shaw took a greater interest in TVB after succeeding the deceased Harold Lee as its chairman in 1980. Shaw leased most of Shaw Brothers' filmmaking facilities to TVB in 1983. Under his chairmanship, TVB successfully launched the careers of international stars such as <a href="/wiki/Chow_Yun-fat" title="Chow Yun-fat">Chow Yun-fat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maggie_Cheung" title="Maggie Cheung">Maggie Cheung</a>, singers such as <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Cheung" title="Leslie Cheung">Leslie Cheung</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anita_Mui" title="Anita Mui">Anita Mui</a>, and directors like <a href="/wiki/Wong_Kar-wai" title="Wong Kar-wai">Wong Kar-wai</a>. In 2006, TVB had 80 percent of Hong Kong's viewers and 78 percent of the city's TV advertising market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Birth_date_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Birth_date-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2011, Run Run Shaw retired as chairman of Television Broadcasts Ltd. at the age of 104 after more than 40 years at Hong Kong's biggest television company,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> after selling his controlling stake to a group of investors including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HTC_Corporation" title="HTC Corporation">HTC Corporation</a> chairman <a href="/wiki/Cher_Wang" title="Cher Wang">Cher Wang</a> and ITC Corporation chairman Charles Chan for HK$6.26 billion in March.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-South_China_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-South_China-41">[41]</a></sup> He was then named chairman emeritus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ-38">[38]</a></sup> Shaw was one of the largest shareholders in <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s" title="Macy's">Macy's</a> after buying 10 percent of its preferred shares for US$50 million when it was nearly bankrupt in 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_life">Community life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run_Run_Shaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Community life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sir Run Run Shaw was an advocate and financial supporter of the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Arts_Festival" title="Hong Kong Arts Festival">Hong Kong Arts Festival</a> in which he became the first chairman of the Festival. He was also the chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Arts_Centre" title="Hong Kong Arts Centre">Hong Kong Arts Centre</a>'s Board of Governors. He was a member of the Board of Trustees of <a href="/wiki/United_College_(Hong_Kong)" title="United College (Hong Kong)">United College</a>, one of the colleges of the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_University_of_Hong_Kong" title="Chinese University of Hong Kong">Chinese University of Hong Kong</a> when the university began in 1967. He became the vice-chairman of the Board of Trustees in 1972 and was appointed to the University Council of the Chinese University in 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HKU_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-HKU-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CUHK_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-CUHK-42">[42]</a></sup> Other public posts included vice-president of the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Girl_Guides_Association" title="Hong Kong Girl Guides Association">Hong Kong Girl Guides Association</a> and the Hong Kong Society for Rehabilitation. He was also the first Asian president of the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Red_Cross" title="Hong Kong Red Cross">Hong Kong Red Cross</a>, and a leading figure in the fund raising of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Community_Chest_of_Hong_Kong" title="Community Chest of Hong Kong">Community Chest of Hong Kong</a> since its inception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HKU_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-HKU-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<p>Over the years, Shaw donated HK$6.5 billion to charities, schools and hospitals in Hong Kong and mainland China through the Sir Run Run Shaw Charitable Trust and the Shaw Foundation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-funeral_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-funeral-43">[43]</a></sup> including donations of HK$4.75 billion to educational institutions in mainland China, which helped to build 6,013 construction projects ranging from primary schools to university libraries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> More than 5,000 buildings in China's college campuses bear Shaw's Chinese name, "Yifu", which has become so ubiquitous that many mistake it as a generic name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinadaily_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-chinadaily-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Shaw_College_(Hong_Kong)" title="Shaw College (Hong Kong)">Shaw College</a>, the fourth constituent college of the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_University_of_Hong_Kong" title="Chinese University of Hong Kong">Chinese University of Hong Kong</a>, is also named after him, whose donations made the establishment of the college possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> His other major donations include 10 million British pounds in 1990 to help establish the Run Run Shaw Institute of Chinese Affairs at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_University" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-6">[6]</a></sup> and US$13 million for disaster relief after the <a href="/wiki/2008_Sichuan_earthquake" title="2008 Sichuan earthquake">2008 Sichuan earthquake</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Hollywood_Reporter_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-The_Hollywood_Reporter-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shaw_Prize">Shaw Prize</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run_Run_Shaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Shaw Prize">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, Shaw established an international award, the <a href="/wiki/Shaw_Prize" title="Shaw Prize">Shaw Prize</a>, for scientists in three areas of research, namely <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, mathematics, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_science" title="Life science">life</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_science" title="Medical science">medical science</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The award is up to US$1 million, and the first prize was awarded in 2004.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run_Run_Shaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was the sixth of the seven children in the Shaw family, and was nicknamed Uncle Six (<i>Luk Sook</i>). His three elder brothers, <a href="/wiki/Runje_Shaw" title="Runje Shaw">Runje Shaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Runde_Shaw" title="Runde Shaw">Runde Shaw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Runme_Shaw" title="Runme Shaw">Runme Shaw</a> were all heads of the Shaw Studio. Runme Shaw, the third elder brother who co-founded the Shaw Studio with him, died in 1985.</p>
<p>Shaw's first wife was Lily Wong Mee-chun, who died at age 85 in 1987. In 1997, he married <a href="/wiki/Mona_Fong" title="Mona Fong">Mona Fong</a> in <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Valley" title="Las Vegas Valley">Las Vegas</a>. A former singer, Mona Fong joined TVB as a procurement manager in 1969 and became the deputy chairman of TVB in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-who_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-who-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg-34">[34]</a></sup> Shaw had four children with his first wife: sons Vee Meng and Harold, and daughters Violet and Dorothy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_34-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg-34">[34]</a></sup> All of his children studied at Oxford University.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shaw was a lover of <a href="/wiki/List_of_Rolls-Royce_motor_cars" title="List of Rolls-Royce motor cars">Rolls-Royce</a> limousines<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ-38">[38]</a></sup> and a keen practitioner of <a href="/wiki/Qigong" title="Qigong">qigong</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-South_China_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-South_China-41">[41]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Bean" title="Mr. Bean">Mr. Bean</a></i> was reportedly his favourite show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shawman_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shawman-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run_Run_Shaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He died at his residence on 7 January 2014, aged 106, and survived by his four children, nine grandchildren and several great-grandchildren.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The family did not give the cause of the death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-funeral_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-funeral-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>His body was transferred from the <a href="/wiki/United_Christian_Hospital" title="United Christian Hospital">United Christian Hospital</a> to the Hong Kong Funeral Home in <a href="/wiki/North_Point" title="North Point">North Point</a> on 10 January 2014. Many local leaders attended the funeral that day, including former <a href="/wiki/Chief_Executive_of_Hong_Kong" title="Chief Executive of Hong Kong">Chief Executives</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tung_Chee-wah" title="Tung Chee-wah">Tung Chee-wah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Donald_Tsang" title="Donald Tsang">Donald Tsang</a>. Shaw's remains were brought to the Cape Collinson Crematorium in <a href="/wiki/Chai_Wan" title="Chai Wan">Chai Wan</a> later the same day. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PRC_President" title="PRC President">PRC President</a> <a href="/wiki/Xi_Jinping" title="Xi Jinping">Xi Jinping</a>, former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Premier_of_the_PRC" title="Premier of the PRC">Premier</a> <a href="/wiki/Wen_Jiabao" title="Wen Jiabao">Wen Jiabao</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_People%27s_Congress" title="National People's Congress">National People's Congress</a> Chairman <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Dejiang" title="Zhang Dejiang">Zhang Dejiang</a> sent messages of respect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-funeral_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-funeral-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours">Honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run_Run_Shaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1964, after their discovery of a small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main_belt" title="Main belt">main belt</a> <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> between <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, Chinese astronomers at the <a href="/wiki/Purple_Mountain_Observatory" title="Purple Mountain Observatory">Purple Mountain Observatory</a> named it "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2899_Runrun_Shaw" title="2899 Runrun Shaw">2899 Runrun Shaw</a>" in Shaw's honour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1974, Run Run Shaw was appointed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">Commander of the Order of the British Empire</a> (CBE). He received a <a href="/wiki/Knight_Bachelor" title="Knight Bachelor">knighthood</a> in 1977 from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II" title="Queen Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Bauhinia_Medal" title="Grand Bauhinia Medal">Grand Bauhinia Medal</a> (GBM) from the Hong Kong government in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was awarded an honorary degree of <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Social_Science" title="Doctor of Social Science">Doctor of Social Science</a> by the Chinese University of Hong Kong in 1981 for his contribution to the university and community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CUHK_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-CUHK-42">[42]</a></sup> In 1984, he was awarded an honorary degree of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Laws" title="Doctor of Laws">Doctor of Laws</a> by the University of Hong Kong to honour an outstanding contribution to applied visual arts, as well as to the community and cultural developments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HKU_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-HKU-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, coinciding with his 100th birthday, he was honoured with the Lifetime Achievement Award at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Film_Awards" title="Hong Kong Film Awards">Hong Kong Film Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Hollywood_Reporter_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-The_Hollywood_Reporter-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, Sir Run Run received the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BAFTA" title="BAFTA">BAFTA</a> Special Award for his outstanding contribution to cinema.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bafta.org_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-bafta.org-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
