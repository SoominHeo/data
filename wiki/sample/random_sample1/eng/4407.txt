<p><b>Leung Chun-ying</b>, <a href="/wiki/Grand_Bauhinia_Medal" title="Grand Bauhinia Medal">GBM</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_Peace" title="Justice of the Peace">JP</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">梁振英</span>; born 12 August 1954), also known as <b>CY Leung</b>, is a Hong Kong politician who is the third and incumbent <a href="/wiki/Chief_Executive_of_Hong_Kong" title="Chief Executive of Hong Kong">Chief Executive of Hong Kong</a>. He assumed office on 1 July 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCY-E-01_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-LCY-E-01-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCY-E-02_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-LCY-E-02-3">[3]</a></sup> Prior to his tenure as Chief Executive, Leung held various political offices, including convenor of the Executive Council and member of the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Legislative_Council" title="Provisional Legislative Council">Provisional Legislative Council</a>. As a relative unknown, Leung nearly failed to obtain enough votes to be nominated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-philaeddie20120320hks_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-philaeddie20120320hks-4">[4]</a></sup> However, he later won the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Chief_Executive_election,_2012" title="Hong Kong Chief Executive election, 2012">Hong Kong Chief Executive election</a> with 689 electoral votes, which earned him the nickname "<b>689</b>".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leung_Chun-ying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Leung Chun-ying was born on 12 August 1954 at <a href="/wiki/Queen_Mary_Hospital_(Hong_Kong)" title="Queen Mary Hospital (Hong Kong)">Queen Mary Hospital</a> in what was then <a href="/wiki/British_Hong_Kong" title="British Hong Kong">British Hong Kong</a>, the son of Leung Zung-Jan and Kong Sau-Zi. Leung's ancestral origin is Shandong, China. His father was a guard at <a href="/wiki/Government_House" title="Government House">Government House</a>.</p>
<p>Leung earned a scholarship to study at <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College,_Hong_Kong" title="King's College, Hong Kong">King's College</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_profile_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_profile-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metro_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Metro-6">[6]</a></sup> where he was a classmate of Chinese democratic activist Lau San-Cing. After graduation, he attended Hong Kong Polytechnic. In 1974, Leung undertook further studies in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Property_valuation" title="Property valuation">valuation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estate_management" title="Estate management">estate management</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bristol_Polytechnic" title="Bristol Polytechnic">Bristol Polytechnic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metro_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Metro-6">[6]</a></sup> and graduated in 1977 first in his class.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gov1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-gov1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leung later returned to Hong Kong and joined the real estate company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jones_Lang_Wootton" title="Jones Lang Wootton">Jones Lang Wootton</a>, where he worked for 5 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunantv_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-hunantv-8">[8]</a></sup> By the age of 30, he was made vice-chairman<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloomberg.com_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg.com-9">[9]</a></sup> of the JLW branch in Hong Kong,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunantv_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-hunantv-8">[8]</a></sup> and was reported to be making a yearly salary of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HK$" title="HK$">HK$</a>10 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunantv_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-hunantv-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leung became the real estate advisor for <a href="/wiki/Zhu_Rongji" title="Zhu Rongji">Zhu Rongji</a> when Zhu was Mayor and Party chief in Shanghai from 1987 to 1991. <a href="/wiki/Zhu_Rongji" title="Zhu Rongji">Zhu Rongji</a> later became the Vice-Premier and then the <a href="/wiki/List_of_premiers_of_China#Premiers_of_State_Council_of_the_People.27s_Republic_of_China_.281949-present.29" title="List of premiers of China">fifth</a> <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Premier of the People's Republic of China">Premier of the People's Republic of China</a> from March 1998 to March 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Later, in 2013, Leung appointed <a href="/wiki/Zhu_Yunlai" title="Zhu Yunlai">Levin Zhu Yunlai</a>, the elder son of former premier Zhu Rongji, as an advisor in the Hong Kong Government's Financial Services Development Council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1995 to 1996, Leung was the president of the Hong Kong Institute of Surveyors. He is a former chairman of the Hong Kong branch of <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institution_of_Chartered_Surveyors" title="Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors">Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors</a>. After holding the post, Leung became an honorary advisor for the local governments of <a href="/wiki/Shenzhen" title="Shenzhen">Shenzhen</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tianjian" title="Tianjian">Tianjian</a> and Shanghai on land reform. He has also taken up the post of international economic advisor for <a href="/wiki/Hebei" title="Hebei">Hebei</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alum_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-alum-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DTZ_Holdings">DTZ Holdings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leung_Chun-ying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: DTZ Holdings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1993, Leung set up his own surveying company C. Y. Leung &amp; Co., in Hong Kong, which then quickly set up many offices in Shanghai and Shenzhen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1995, C. Y. Leung &amp; Co joined an international alliance comprising CB Commercial, Debenham Tewson &amp; Chinnocks and <a href="/wiki/DTZ" title="DTZ">DTZ</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> By 2000, his company merged with Singapore's Dai Yuk-coeng Company (<span lang="zh-Hant-HK" xml:lang="zh-Hant-HK">戴玉祥產業諮詢公司</span>) into DTZ Debenham Tie Leung Limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2006, after a complex share swap, Leung emerged as owner of 4.61% of the 200-year-old London listed property consultancy DTZ Holdings. The deal involved HK$330 million cash and a share deal with Leung. In 2007, DTZ Holdings, replete with a US$400 million fund, expanded into the property market in mainland China. This fund was to be passed through Leung's regional company DTZ Asia Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2011, one month before Leung announced his candidacy for the Hong Kong Chief Executive post, his company DTZ was hit by a <a href="/wiki/Liquidity_crisis" title="Liquidity crisis">liquidity crisis</a>. Following this, after informing the London Stock exchange that its shares were worthless, the board of DTZ, including Leung, agreed to sell DTZ to <a href="/wiki/UGL_Limited" title="UGL Limited">UGL Limited</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smh141008_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-smh141008-17">[17]</a></sup> On 24 November 2011, Leung resigned as director from the board of DTZ and on 28 November 2011 announced his candidacy for the Hong Kong Chief Executive election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smh141008_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-smh141008-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="UGL_controversy">UGL controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leung_Chun-ying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: UGL controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In October 2014, investigative reporters from the Australian newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_Age" title="The Age">The Age</a></i>, owned by Fairfax Media, revealed details of an agreement Leung had signed on 2 December 2011, which entitled him to payment of <span style="white-space: nowrap">GB£</span>4 million from UGL in exchange for his supporting the acquisition of DTZ group assets by UGL, for not competing with UGL/DTZ and making himself available to provide advisory services for a period of two years from that date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smh141008_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-smh141008-17">[17]</a></sup> <i>The Age</i> newspaper report stated that "the payments were made in two instalments, in 2012 and 2013, after he became Hong Kong's top official'' but were not declared on Leung's register of interests. The payments relate to a deal in which UGL bought DTZ Holdings (the insolvent property services firm he was associated with), whose prospects depended on his network of managers and clients in Hong Kong and mainland China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Australian media also revealed that on the same day Leung signed the agreement, China's state-owned Tianjin Innovation Financial Investment Company had made a bid that valued DTZ at GBP100 million more than the bid by UGL, but that this more valuable bid was rejected by the DTZ board, which included Leung, and not released to shareholders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In December 2012, nine months after winning the Hong Kong Chief Executive election, Leung received the first tranche from UGL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smh141008_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-smh141008-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Fairfax_Media" title="Fairfax Media">Fairfax Media</a> revelations of this agreement and the rejected competitive bid, many more questioned Leung's integrity in the "secret transaction" signed a few days before his election, expressing concerns of potential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conflicts_of_interest" title="Conflicts of interest">conflicts of interest</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fraudulent_preference" title="Fraudulent preference">fraudulent preference</a>. Prosecutors from Hong Kong's <a href="/wiki/Independent_Commission_Against_Corruption_(Hong_Kong)" title="Independent Commission Against Corruption (Hong Kong)">Independent Commission Against Corruption</a> started investigations into this scandal, as did authorities in Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The abrupt and unprecedented sacking of Rebecca Li Bo-lan – a 30-year veteran graft-buster and the first female head of the ICAC investigative unit, appointed in June 2015 – fuelled suspicions of Leung's attempts to impede investigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp1987202_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-scmp1987202-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_appointments">Other appointments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leung_Chun-ying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other appointments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 2002 to 2007, Leung was a board member in the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Singapore" title="Government of Singapore">Government of Singapore</a>-owned banking firm <a href="/wiki/DBS_Bank" title="DBS Bank">DBS Group Holdings Ltd</a> and <a href="/wiki/DBS_Bank_(Hong_Kong)" title="DBS Bank (Hong Kong)">DBS Bank Hong Kong Ltd</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloomberg.com_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg.com-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1999, Leung took over the position of council chairman of <a href="/wiki/Lingnan_University_(Hong_Kong)" title="Lingnan University (Hong Kong)">Lingnan University</a>. In the same year, on 16 June 1999<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> the Lingnan College received University status. Leung continued in this position for nine years, until 21 October 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2008, Leung was appointed as chairman and member of the council of the <a href="/wiki/City_University_of_Hong_Kong" title="City University of Hong Kong">City University of Hong Kong</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Leung held this position until 2011. In terms of his performance as chairman, the University staff had scored Leung less than 1 point on a scale of 10. During his tenure as chairman, Leung was accused of attempts to weaken the power of the staff association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_political_career">Early political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leung_Chun-ying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1985, Leung joined the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Basic_Law_Consultative_Committee" title="Hong Kong Basic Law Consultative Committee">Hong Kong Basic Law Consultative Committee</a>, a 180-member body nominated by the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Basic_Law_Drafting_Committee" title="Hong Kong Basic Law Drafting Committee">Hong Kong Basic Law Drafting Committee</a>, that was to consult with Hong Kong people regarding various drafts of the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Basic_Law" title="Hong Kong Basic Law">Hong Kong Basic Law</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGhai199959_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGhai199959-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELoh2010158_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELoh2010158-28">[28]</a></sup> The working of the BLCC was criticised as it had not established any formal machinery for the consultation process and did not indicate the degree of public support of views expressed. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGhai199958-59_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGhai199958-59-29">[29]</a></sup> In 1988, Leung, then aged 34, was made the Secretary-General of the Committee, replacing Mao Junnian. Former CCP member Leung Mo-han, and Leung's critics, have suggested that Leung must be a secret member of the Communist party, since, per the rules of the Chinese Communist Party, such senior positions are normally assumed only by party members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In 1990, the BLCC ceased to exist after the Basic Law was adopted by the <a href="/wiki/National_People%27s_Congress" title="National People's Congress">National People's Congress</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGhai199959_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGhai199959-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1999, Leung was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Gold_Bauhinia_Star" title="Gold Bauhinia Star">Gold Bauhinia Star</a> by the Hong Kong Government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunantv_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-hunantv-8">[8]</a></sup> He was a member of the National Standing Committee of the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_People%27s_Political_Consultative_Conference" title="Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference">CPPCC</a> and only submitted his resignation one week prior to assuming his office of the Chief Executive of Hong Kong in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gov1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-gov1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> He is currently the chairman and sits on the board of directors of the pro-Beijing <a href="/wiki/One_Country_Two_Systems_Research_Institute" title="One Country Two Systems Research Institute">One Country Two Systems Research Institute</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gov1_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-gov1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, there were confrontations between police and demonstrators after the annual <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_1_July_marches" title="Hong Kong 1 July marches">1 July march</a> amid public opposition to the government's draft legislation to eliminate by-elections for vacated Legco seats. Leung responded by saying that such rowdy rallies should be "sanctioned and restrained".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Then-Chief Executive <a href="/wiki/Tung_Chee-hwa" title="Tung Chee-hwa">Tung Chee-hwa</a> appointed Leung as the convenor of the <a href="/wiki/Executive_Council_of_Hong_Kong" title="Executive Council of Hong Kong">Executive Council</a> in 1998, replacing his predecessor <a href="/wiki/Chung_Sze-yuen" title="Chung Sze-yuen">Chung Sze-yuen</a>. During Tung's 1997 <a href="/wiki/Policy_address_of_Hong_Kong" title="Policy address of Hong Kong">policy address</a>, he proposed that the government would build no less than 85,000 flats every year, allowing 70% of the citizens to own a house within 10 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> However, the proposal was put on hold in the wake of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1997_Asian_Financial_Crisis" title="1997 Asian Financial Crisis">1997 Asian Financial Crisis</a>. As convenor of the Executive Council, CY Leung has been questioned many times regarding this policy plan over the years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The 1997 pledge was not met.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chief_Executive">Chief Executive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leung_Chun-ying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chief Executive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Election_campaign">Election campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leung_Chun-ying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Election campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Chief_Executive_election,_2012" title="Hong Kong Chief Executive election, 2012">Hong Kong Chief Executive election, 2012</a></div>
<p>On 28 November 2011, Leung officially announced his candidacy for Chief Executive of Hong Kong,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> two years after he had first hinted at his interest in the post.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The election campaign was controversial and bitterly fought. The early favourite to win was long-considered to be former Chief Secretary <a href="/wiki/Henry_Tang" title="Henry Tang">Henry Tang</a>, who was supported by the local bureaucracy, key property and business tycoons, and crucially, by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Government of the People's Republic of China">Beijing government</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phila20120321hks_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-phila20120321hks-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webcitation1_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-webcitation1-39">[39]</a></sup> However, while Tang stumbled over the revelation of an illegal structure at his home, Leung faced similar problems at his residence.</p>
<p>Leung appointed <a href="/wiki/Fanny_Law" title="Fanny Law">Fanny Law</a>, who attracted widespread criticisms for mishandling educational reforms when in office from 2002 to 2006, to his Office of the Chief-Executive Elect as Campaign Manager.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the campaign, rumours persistently resurfaced that Leung was a closet member of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party of China</a>. Section 31 of Chief Executive Election Ordinance (Chapter 569) stipulates that a CE election winner must "publicly make a statutory declaration to the effect that he is not a member of any political party".</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Martin_Lee" title="Martin Lee">Martin Lee</a>, a pro-democracy politician, questioned the survival of the '<a href="/wiki/One_country,_two_systems" title="One country, two systems">one country, two systems</a>' principle if Leung were to be elected the CE, saying that Leung must have been a loyal CCP member for him to be appointed as the Secretary General of the Basic Law Consultative Committee in 1985 at the young age of 31.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> This view was supported by a former underground communist, Florence Leung, whose memoir recorded that Leung was also a secret member of the party. She explained that, in order for Leung to succeed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mao_Junnian" title="Mao Junnian">Mao Junnian</a> (whose identity as a communist had been revealed) as the Secretary General of the Basic Law Consultative Committee, he must also have been a party member, per the tradition of the party. She also cited Leung's vague remarks about the 1989 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiananmen_massacre" title="Tiananmen massacre">Tiananmen massacre</a> as a clue to his membership, in contrast to Henry Tang's greater sympathy for the protest movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> She said that if Leung, as an underground party member, won the election then the leader of the Communist Party in Hong Kong would be in actual control. Leung consistently dismissed such claims as ungrounded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The suggestion that Leung's loyalty was more to Beijing than Hong Kong has long dogged him. In 2010, Leung had been asked whether he would support the award of the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> to jailed Chinese dissident <a href="/wiki/Liu_Xiaobo" title="Liu Xiaobo">Liu Xiaobo</a>. He replied that China's former paramount leader, <a href="/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping" title="Deng Xiaoping">Deng Xiaoping</a>, should have been the first Chinese to win the award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Towards the end of the election campaign, <a href="/wiki/James_Tien_(politician)" title="James Tien (politician)">James Tien</a>, the honorary chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(Hong_Kong)" title="Liberal Party (Hong Kong)">Liberal Party</a> and a supporter of Henry Tang during the election, stated that members of the election committee had received phone calls from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liaison_Office_of_the_Central_People%27s_Government_in_the_Hong_Kong_Special_Administrative_Region" title="Liaison Office of the Central People's Government in the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region">the Beijing government's Liaison Office</a> demanding that they vote for Leung.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 25 March 2012, Leung was declared Hong Kong's new Chief Executive,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCY-E-01_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-LCY-E-01-2">[2]</a></sup> after securing 689 votes from the 1,200-member appointed election committee. Henry Tang garnered 285 votes and the third candidate, Democrat <a href="/wiki/Albert_Ho" title="Albert Ho">Albert Ho</a>, just 76, of a total of 1,132 valid votes received.</p>
<p>Upon his selection, the online version of the <i><a href="/wiki/People%27s_Daily" title="People's Daily">People's Daily</a></i> addressed Leung as "Comrade Leung Chun-ying".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_profile_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_profile-5">[5]</a></sup> When the Chinese mass media pointed out that the title <i><a href="/wiki/Comrade" title="Comrade">Comrade</a></i> (or <i><a href="/wiki/Tongzhi" title="Tongzhi">tongzhi</a></i>, 同志) is reserved by the party for its own members, and that neither Tung Chee-hwa nor Donald Tsang had been thus addressed, the epithet "Comrade" was removed from the page.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>

<p>After his selection, a number of illegal or unauthorised structures were found at Leung's house, in a reprise of the scandal involving an illegal basement that had badly hit the campaign of his rival, Tang, and for which Leung had roundly criticised Tang.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The issue dominated the period around his taking up the post. Leung's structures were to be demolished. Chief Executive contender Albert Ho challenged Leung's legal legitimacy as the territory's new leader but his claim was rejected by the High Court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chief_Executive_.282012.E2.80.93present.29">Chief Executive (2012–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leung_Chun-ying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Chief Executive (2012–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Leung_Chun-ying_as_Chief_Executive_of_Hong_Kong" title="Leung Chun-ying as Chief Executive of Hong Kong">Leung Chun-ying as Chief Executive of Hong Kong</a></div>
<p>Leung assumed office as Chief Executive on 1 July 2012. On top of the controversy surrounding illegal structures of his house, in which he was severely criticised as a hypocrite for using the same accusation in attacking his opponent during the 2012 election, there were additional disputes regarding his appointments of officers and political judgements.</p>
<p>Leung's appointment of Chen Ran (陳冉) as the Project Officer in his Transitional Office stirred up further criticism among the public of Hong Kong. Chen has resided in Hong Kong for less than seven years, the minimum time period which foreigners are required to reside to apply for permanent residence. Chen is a former General Secretary of the pro-Beijing Hong Kong Young Elites Association (香港菁英會), of which Leung is a patron. Chen is the daughter of a middle-ranking government official in Shanghai and a former member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist_Youth_League" title="Communist Youth League">Communist Youth League</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the centuries-long history of <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">Cantonese</a> as the de facto spoken language of Hong Kong, Leung made his inaugural speech in <a href="/wiki/Standard_Chinese" title="Standard Chinese">Mandarin</a>, spoken in Mainland China. This was in stark contrast to his predecessor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Donald_Tsang" title="Sir Donald Tsang">Sir Donald Tsang</a>, who made his inaugural speech in Cantonese in July 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_relations">Public relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leung_Chun-ying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Public relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Leung's popularity ratings have been continuously low since his election. In October 2013, only 31 percent of the 1,009 participants in the HKU poll said they supported Leung as the city's leader, while 55 percent disapproved of him – an increase of 6 percentage points from the previous month's poll.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Leung accepted HKD50 million in a deal with Australian engineering firm UGL in 2011. On 8 October 2014, an Australian newspaper revealed how the contract was made, but Leung has denied having done anything morally or legally wrong. He sidestepped key questions, such as why he did not declare the payment to the Executive Council. This controversy has further worsened Leung's popularity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a media interview during the <a href="/wiki/2014_Hong_Kong_protests" title="2014 Hong Kong protests">pro-democracy occupation</a>, Leung attempted to justify the conservative electoral model for Hong Kong by stating "if it's entirely a numbers game – numeric representation – then obviously you'd be talking to half the people in Hong Kong [who] earn less than US$1,800 a month [the median wage in HK]. You would end up with that kind of politics and policies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The comments proved controversial and were considered insensitive and snobbish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> <i>South China Morning Post</i> columnist Alex Lo said of this interview: "Leung has set the gold standard on how not to do a media interview for generations of politicians to come." <a href="/wiki/Chris_Patten" title="Chris Patten">Lord Patten</a>, the last British governor of Hong Kong, questioned Leung's leadership skill and capacity as Hong Kong's incumbent Chief Executive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> In December 2014, Leung's popularity ratings plunged to a new low, down to 39.7 percent, with a net of minus 37%. This was attributed to public perception of Leung's unwillingness to heal the wounds, and his unwarranted shifting of the blame for the wrongs in society onto opponents. Leung also claimed negative effects on the economy without providing evidence, and his assertions were contradicted by official figures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Karolinska_Institutet_controversy">Karolinska Institutet controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leung_Chun-ying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Karolinska Institutet controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In February 2015, the <a href="/wiki/Karolinska_Institutet" title="Karolinska Institutet">Karolinska Institutet</a> announced it had received a record US$50 million donation from Lau Ming-wai, who chairs Hong Kong property developer Chinese Estates Holdings, and would establish a research centre in the city. On 16 February, a popular tabloid magazine <i>Next Magazine</i> revealed that Leung's son Chuen-yan had recently been awarded a fellowship from an independent foundation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> to research heart disease therapeutics at KI in Stockholm beginning that year, and raised questions about the "intricate relationship between the chief executive and powerful individuals". CY Leung had visited KI when in Sweden in 2014, and subsequently introduced KI president Anders Hamsten to Lau.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-universityworldnews_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-universityworldnews-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The Democratic Party urged the ICAC to investigate the donation, suggesting that Leung may have used his public position to further his son's career. The Chief Executive's Office strenuously denied suggestions of any quid pro quo, saying that "the admission of the [Chief Executive's] son to post-doctoral research at KI is an independent decision by KI having regard to his professional standards. He [the son] plays no role and does not hold any position at the [proposed] Ming Wai Lau Center for Regenerative Medicine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-universityworldnews_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-universityworldnews-60">[60]</a></sup> This accusation has been questioned by the <i><a href="/wiki/South_China_Morning_Post" title="South China Morning Post">South China Morning Post</a></i>. "The insinuation is that Leung Chuen-yan with a doctorate from Cambridge doesn't deserve his job at the Karolinska Institute...Leung the son probably could get similar junior posts in many other prestigious-sounding – at least to brand-obsessed Hongkongers – research institutes; it's not that big a deal."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Separation_of_powers">Separation of powers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leung_Chun-ying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Separation of powers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Under <a href="/wiki/One_country,_two_systems" title="One country, two systems">one country, two systems</a>, Hong Kong has three branches of government as codified in the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Basic_Law" title="Hong Kong Basic Law">Basic Law</a>. <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Xiaoming" title="Zhang Xiaoming">Zhang Xiaoming</a>, the director of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liaison_Office_of_the_Central_People%27s_Government_in_the_Hong_Kong_Special_Administrative_Region" title="Liaison Office of the Central People's Government in the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region">Liaison Office of the Central People's Government</a>, sparked controversy in Hong Kong after claiming in September 2015 that the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Executive_of_Hong_Kong" title="Chief Executive of Hong Kong">Chief Executive of Hong Kong</a> has a "special legal position which overrides administrative, legislative and judicial organs" and that <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_powers" title="Separation of powers">separation of powers</a> is "not suitable for Hong Kong".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-transcendent_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-transcendent-63">[63]</a></sup> Beijing had previously described, in 2012, the role of Hong Kong judges as "administrators, whose basic political requirement [...] is loving the country".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aus_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-aus-64">[64]</a></sup> After Zhang's statement, Leung Chun-ying subsequently affirmed that his position is "transcendent" of the branches of the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-transcendent_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-transcendent-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>The statements, which contradict the Basic Law, were widely criticised in Hong Kong. Chief Justice <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Ma" title="Geoffrey Ma">Geoffrey Ma</a> responded that no one is above the law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Bar_Association" title="Hong Kong Bar Association">Hong Kong Bar Association</a> stated that it "firmly believes that the common law principle of separation of powers will continue to be implemented within the constitutional framework of the Basic Law" and that the chief executive "cannot be said to be above the law [...] irrespective of the description of the political role".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aus_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-aus-64">[64]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alan_Leong" title="Alan Leong">Alan Leong</a>, leader of the <a href="/wiki/Civic_Party" title="Civic Party">Civic Party</a>, accused Zhang of trying to provoke radicalisation in Hong Kong by stirring controversy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aus_64-2"><a href="#cite_note-aus-64">[64]</a></sup> Legislator <a href="/wiki/James_Tien_(politician)" title="James Tien (politician)">James Tien</a> said that Leung should clarify his comment, because "obviously his authority is not above the executive, the legislature and the Judiciary".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>Video of Leung's speech went <a href="/wiki/Viral_video" title="Viral video">viral</a> on social media as Leung's media director, standing at his side, can be seen raising his eyebrows in visible disbelief when Leung asserts his "transcendence" of the branches of government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leung_Chun-ying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1981, Leung married <a href="/wiki/Regina_Leung" title="Regina Leung">Regina Ching-yee Higgins</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> whose father was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_police" title="Hong Kong police">Royal Hong Kong Police</a> officer. Investigating into her background, <i>Apple Daily</i> revealed that Higgins, commonly believed to have the surname Tong, actually had changed her name to Higgins at the age of three, through which she successfully acquired UK nationality. She thus qualified to study in the UK as a British national and may have benefitted paying reduced tuition fees. Her name on the couple's marriage certificate bears the name "Regina Ching Yee Higgins".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apple16327457_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-apple16327457-70">[70]</a></sup> After her marriage to Leung, she changed her name to Leung Tong Ching Yee Regina. Neither Tong nor the office of the chief executive has responded to questions as to her name changes. Despite Leung's grave unpopular ratings as Hong Kong's Chief Executive, his wife has publicly defended him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunantv_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-hunantv-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The couple has two daughters and a son. During the 1990s, Leung had publicly ruled out sending his children to school abroad, yet years later his own children studied in Britain and then worked overseas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Upon his election to chief executive in 2012, Leung declared that his wife and children were Chinese nationals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apple16327457_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-apple16327457-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of his daughters, Chai-yan Leung, regularly attracted media attention for her brazen public comportment. She has posted remarks on her Facebook account and has appeared in several magazine interviews, in which she talked about her depression and her stormy relationship with her parents, principally her mother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> In 2014, Chai-yan posted an image supposedly of her in a bathtub with wrists slashed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibt1849202_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-ibt1849202-74">[74]</a></sup> In March 2015, she posted a flurry of Facebook messages after a row with her mother in which she alleged she was kicked, slapped and sworn at; police and ambulance service were called to Government House. After her Facebook page was taken down, she posted an Instagram message to the effect that she had left home definitively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> CY Leung pleaded with the media and the public for some space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibt1849202_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-ibt1849202-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leung's younger daughter, Chung-yan, became embroiled in a controversy in the early hours of 28 March 2016 at Hong Kong International Airport. Chung-yan had reportedly left her hand baggage in the departure hall after she had passed through the security checkpoint to board a flight for San Francisco. According to <i>Apple Daily</i>, witnesses alleged that his wife Regina Tong, who accompanied their daughter to the airport, engaged in a lengthy argument with airport staff to have staff reunite her daughter with her missing bag. Staff insisted on upholding procedure for the passenger to exit the secured zone to recover any missing item and pass through security again. Leung Chung-yan reportedly telephoned her father to seek help in resolving the impasse. The phone was passed onto airline staff, who brought Leung her bag, making clear that they had made an exception; she boarded her flight minutes later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Leung denied abusing power by ordering airline staff to retrieve Chung-yan's bag in violation of security guidelines. Following the news of the incident, the Airport Authority issued a statement denying that it was against existing procedure for anyone other than the passenger to retrieve any missing baggage from airport security and bring it into the restricted area and through security check.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> In an apparent allusion to the incident, <i>People's Daily</i> wrote a commentary stating that it would be better if there was a bit less of "special procedures for special things".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Hong Kong Cabin Crew Federation called for direct talks with the Civil Aviation Department’s director general regarding the "dangerous precedent" set by Leung. Hundreds of members of the union were joined by supporters in a sit-in protest at the airport attended by between 1,000 and 2,500 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> Leung's popularity as chief executive plunged to a new record low during the month of April, after the incident was made public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nicknames">Nicknames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leung_Chun-ying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Nicknames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early in his career in early 1980, when he started earning an annual salary of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HK$" title="HK$">HK$</a>10 million, he was given the nickname "Working-class emperor" (<span lang="zh-Hant-HK" xml:lang="zh-Hant-HK">打工皇帝</span>) .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunantv_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-hunantv-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later, during his campaign for CE elections, across the territory he is nicknamed "The Wolf" by some opponents – alluding to his cunning and deviousness, and as a pun of his name and the Chinese word for wolf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-81">[81]</a></sup> Others refer to him as <a href="/wiki/Dracula" title="Dracula">Dracula</a>, given his prominent eye teeth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph11133867_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph11133867-82">[82]</a></sup> Leung has also been associated with and caricatured as <a href="/wiki/Lufsig" title="Lufsig">Lufsig</a> – an IKEA plush toy – after one was thrown at him by an anti-government protester in December 2013 during a town hall meeting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-81">[81]</a></sup> Lufsig is a plush wolf, whose original given Chinese name in mainland China "路姆西" resembles profanity in Cantonese, much to the delight of the Hong Kong citizens who dislike him.</p>
<p>Since becoming chief executive, Leung was bestowed the pejorative moniker "689", referring to the meagre number of votes that elected him into office, and is also used ironically to symbolise the lack of representation of the will of Hong Kong people at large.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-689_guardian_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-689_guardian-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc23193421_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc23193421-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20140702wsj_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-20140702wsj-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20160117hongkongfp_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-20160117hongkongfp-86">[86]</a></sup> Puma, which posted an image of a facsimile runners identification tag bearing the number "D7689" onto its Facebook page to publicise its involvement in the 2015 Hong Kong marathon received a complaint that it was disrespectful to Leung, since "D7" was perceived to be the initials of a <a href="/wiki/Cantonese_profanity" title="Cantonese profanity">Cantonese profanity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20160117hongkongfp_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-20160117hongkongfp-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LTN1215780_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-LTN1215780-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20150126sina_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-20150126sina-88">[88]</a></sup> Lampooning the complaint, members of the public scoured the city and found many examples of innocent occurrences of the irreverent number.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20160117hongkongfp_86-2"><a href="#cite_note-20160117hongkongfp-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hket529939_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-hket529939-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
