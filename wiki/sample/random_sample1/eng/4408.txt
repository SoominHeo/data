<p><b>Legong</b> is a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balinese_(people)" title="Balinese (people)">Balinese</a> <a href="/wiki/Balinese_dance" title="Balinese dance">dance</a>. It is a refined dance form characterized by intricate finger movements, complicated footwork, and expressive gestures and facial expressions.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Legong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Legong probably originated in the 19th century as royal entertainment. Legend has it that a prince of Sukwati fell ill and had a vivid dream in which two maidens danced to <a href="/wiki/Gamelan" title="Gamelan">gamelan</a> music. When he recovered, he arranged for such dances to be performed in reality. Others believe that the Legong originated with the <i><a href="/wiki/Sanghyang" title="Sanghyang">sanghyang</a> dedari</i>, a ceremony involving voluntary <a href="/wiki/Spirit_possession" title="Spirit possession">possession</a> of two little girls by beneficent spirits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Legong is also danced at public festivals. Excerpts from Legong dance dramas are put on for tourists.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dancers">Dancers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Legong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dancers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Traditionally, legong dancers were girls who have not yet reached puberty. They begin rigorous training from about the age of five. These dancers are regarded highly in the society and usually become wives of royal personages or wealthy merchants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> After marriage they would stop dancing. However, in present Indonesia dancers may be of all ages;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> performances by men in women's costumes are also recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Story">Story</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Legong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Story">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Classical Legong enacts several traditional stories. The most common is the tale of the King of Lasem from the <i>Malat</i>, a collection of heroic romances. He is at war with another king, the father (or brother) of Princess Ranjasari. Lasem wants to marry the girl, but she detests him and tries to run away. Becoming lost in the forest, she is captured by Lasem, who imprisons her and goes out for a final assault against her family. He is attacked by a monstrous raven, which foretells his death.</p>
<p>The dramatics are enacted in elaborate and stylized pantomime. The two little actresses are accompanied by a third dancer called a <i><a href="/wiki/Condong" title="Condong">condong</a></i> or attendant. She sets the scene, presents the dancers with their fans and later plays the part of the raven.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Legong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Traditionally, fifteen types of <i>legong</i> dance were known. The duration, movement, and narrative of each type differed. Some, for instance, could last for an hour. These types included:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Legong Bapang Saba</i></li>
<li><i>Legong Jebog</i></li>
<li><i>Legong Kraton</i></li>
<li><i>Legong Kuntir</i></li>
<li><i>Legong Lasem</i></li>
<li><i>Legong Raja Cina</i></li>
<li><i>Legong Semarandana</i></li>
<li><i>Legong Sudasarna</i></li>
</ul>

<p>Legong is mentioned in "I've Been To Bali Too", the single by Australian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folk-rock" title="Folk-rock">folk-rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Redgum" title="Redgum">Redgum</a> from their 1984 album <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frontline_(Redgum_album)" title="Frontline (Redgum album)">Frontline</a>.</p>
