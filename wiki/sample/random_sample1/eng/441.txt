<p><b>.cn</b> is the country code <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domain" title="Top-level domain">top-level domain</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CcTLD" title="CcTLD">ccTLD</a>) for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>.</p>
<p>Domain name administration in mainland China is managed through a branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Industry_and_Information_Technology_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Ministry of Industry and Information Technology of the People's Republic of China">Ministry of Industry and Information</a>. The registry is maintained by <a href="/wiki/China_Internet_Network_Information_Center" title="China Internet Network Information Center">China Internet Network Information Center</a> (CNNIC). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NeuStar" title="NeuStar">Neulevel</a> has entered into a partnership with CNNIC to market <b>.cn</b> outside mainland China.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Second-level_domains">Second-level domains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.cn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Second-level domains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Any individual may register for second-level domain names. However, the registry has created a set of predefined second-level domains for certain types of organizations and geographic locations. Registrations for such third-level domains were available before second-level domains became available in 2004, and registrants of third-level domains were given priority for names at the second level.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internationalized_domain_names_with_Chinese_characters">Internationalized domain names with Chinese characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=.cn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Internationalized domain names with Chinese characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Internationalized_domain_name" title="Internationalized domain name">Internationalized domain names</a> with Chinese characters may be registered at the second level under the .cn <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domain" title="Top-level domain">top-level domain</a>.</p>
<p>On June 25, 2010, <a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a> approved the use of the <a href="/wiki/Internationalized_country_code_top-level_domain" title="Internationalized country code top-level domain">internationalized country code top-level domains</a> .ä¸­å½ (<i>China</i> in <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese characters</a>, DNS name <i>xn--fiqs8s</i>) and .ä¸­å (<i>China</i> in <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese characters</a>, DNS name <i>xn--fiqz9s</i>) by CNNIC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> These two TLDs were added to the DNS in July 2010.</p>
<p>CNNIC proposes Chinese <a href="/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">domain names</a> in .å¬å¸ (".com" in Chinese) and .ç½ç» (".net" in Chinese). However, these are not recognized by <a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a> and are only available via domestic <a href="/wiki/Domain_name_registrar" title="Domain name registrar">domain name registrar</a>.</p>
