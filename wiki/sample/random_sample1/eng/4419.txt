<p>The <b>Red Line</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_rail" title="Heavy rail">heavy rail</a> <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit" title="Rapid transit">subway</a> line running between <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Los_Angeles" title="Downtown Los Angeles">Downtown Los Angeles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Hollywood,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="North Hollywood, Los Angeles, California">North Hollywood</a> via the districts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hollywood,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="Hollywood, Los Angeles, California">Hollywood</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-Wilshire" title="Mid-Wilshire">Mid-Wilshire</a>. In North Hollywood it connects with the <a href="/wiki/Orange_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)" title="Orange Line (Los Angeles Metro)">Orange Line</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bus_rapid_transit" title="Bus rapid transit">bus rapid transit</a>) service for stations to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warner_Center,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="Warner Center, Los Angeles, California">Warner Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Woodland_Hills,_Los_Angeles" title="Woodland Hills, Los Angeles">Woodland Hills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chatsworth,_Los_Angeles" title="Chatsworth, Los Angeles">Chatsworth</a>. It is operated by the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County_Metropolitan_Transportation_Authority" title="Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority">Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority</a>.</p>
<p>The Red Line, which is one of six lines forming the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County_Metro_Rail" title="Los Angeles County Metro Rail">Metro Rail rapid transit system</a> opened in stages between 1993 and 2000. Together with the <a href="/wiki/Purple_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)" title="Purple Line (Los Angeles Metro)">Purple Line</a>, these two heavy rail lines combine to form L.A. Metro Rail's busiest line. As of October 2013<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the combined Red and Purple lines averaged 169,478 boardings per weekday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ridership_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ridership-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ridership-Plot_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ridership-Plot-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_description">Service description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Service description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Route">Route</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Route">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Red Line begins at Union Station and travels southwest through <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Los_Angeles" title="Downtown Los Angeles">Downtown Los Angeles</a>, passing the Civic Center, Pershing Square (near the Historic Core) and the Financial District. At <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/7th_St/Metro_Center_station" title="7th St/Metro Center station">7th St/Metro Center</a>, travelers can connect to the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)" title="Blue Line (Los Angeles Metro)">Metro Blue Line</a> and <a href="/wiki/Expo_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)" title="Expo Line (Los Angeles Metro)">Metro Expo Line</a>. From here, the train travels between 7th Street and Wilshire Boulevard (and briefly Ingraham Street) west through Pico-Union and Westlake, arriving at <a href="/wiki/Wilshire/Vermont_station" title="Wilshire/Vermont station">Wilshire/Vermont</a> in the city's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-Wilshire" title="Mid-Wilshire">Mid-Wilshire</a>/<a href="/wiki/Koreatown,_Los_Angeles" title="Koreatown, Los Angeles">Koreatown</a> district. Up to this point, the track is shared with the <a href="/wiki/Purple_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)" title="Purple Line (Los Angeles Metro)">Metro Purple Line</a>: at Wilshire/Vermont, the two lines diverge.</p>
<p>From here, the Red Line travels north along Vermont, and then west along <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Boulevard" title="Hollywood Boulevard">Hollywood Boulevard</a>, traveling through <a href="/wiki/Koreatown,_Los_Angeles" title="Koreatown, Los Angeles">Koreatown</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hollywood,_Los_Angeles" title="Hollywood, Los Angeles">Hollywood</a>. Finally, the line turns northwest and crosses into the <a href="/wiki/San_Fernando_Valley" title="San Fernando Valley">San Fernando Valley</a>, where it terminates in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Hollywood,_California" title="North Hollywood, California">North Hollywood</a>.</p>
<p>This route matches a branch of the old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Car" title="Red Car">Red Car</a> system, dismantled during The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_American_Streetcar_Scandal" title="Great American Streetcar Scandal">Great American Streetcar Scandal</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Headways">Headways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Headways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Trains on the Red Line operate every ten minutes during peak hours Monday through Friday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> They operate every twelve minutes during the daytime weekdays and all day on the weekends after approximately 10 a.m. (with a 15-minute headway early Saturday and Sunday mornings). Night service can range between 10–20 minutes. Due to being paired with the Purple Line, the headways between Union Station and Wilshire/Vermont are cut in half, so the Red/Purple Line trunk has trains running five to ten minutes throughout service times.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planning_and_construction">Planning and construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Planning and construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Red Line project consisted of the current Red Line and Purple Line corridors, plus other corridors that were never built. Rather than building the entire project all at once, the SCRTD divided the project into "minimum operating segments" (MOS), corridor segments that could be built as distinct project phases that would provide incremental benefit as they opened.</p>
<p>While some type of mass transit rail project had been planned and considered for many decades to serve the length of <a href="/wiki/Wilshire_Boulevard" title="Wilshire Boulevard">Wilshire corridor</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Monica" title="Santa Monica">Santa Monica</a>, transit planners finally decided on a route that would travel west along Wilshire Blvd. to <a href="/wiki/Fairfax_Avenue" title="Fairfax Avenue">Fairfax Avenue</a>, then turn north along Fairfax to the <a href="/wiki/San_Fernando_Valley" title="San Fernando Valley">San Fernando Valley</a>. Following a methane explosion in 1985 at a <a href="/wiki/Ross_Dress_for_Less" title="Ross Dress for Less">Ross Dress for Less</a> clothing store near Fairfax and Third Street, Congressman <a href="/wiki/Henry_Waxman" title="Henry Waxman">Henry Waxman</a> worked to legally designate a large part of Mid-Wilshire as a "<a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> zone". This zone stretched on either side of Wilshire Boulevard from <a href="/wiki/Hancock_Park,_Los_Angeles" title="Hancock Park, Los Angeles">Hancock Park</a> to west of Fairfax (through areas of his district where subway opposition was strongest). Congress passed the ban in 1986. Subsequently, any plans for a subway west of Western Avenue diverted the line south around the methane zone, using <a href="/wiki/Crenshaw_Boulevard" title="Crenshaw Boulevard">Crenshaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pico_Boulevard" title="Pico Boulevard">Pico</a>, and <a href="/wiki/San_Vicente_Boulevard" title="San Vicente Boulevard">San Vicente Boulevards</a>, but these plans never came to fruition.</p>
<p>The passage of the legislation forced the then <a href="/wiki/Southern_California_Rapid_Transit_District" title="Southern California Rapid Transit District">SCRTD</a> (predecessor agency to the current LACMTA) to terminate the route at Western Avenue. (it would later be referred to as "the tail" for its short length from the future transfer station at Vermont Avenue) and no longer qualify for the current round of Federal funding for the remaining miles of the rail line. However, in an effort to qualify for future awards of Federal funds for miles of route that had now been canceled, the SCRTD quickly started over (the FTA requires formulas and studies before it approves funds, and then only for the precise route submitted) and proposed that one leg of the subway would deviate from the Western Ave. bound tunnel and turn north on Vermont, west on Sunset, then north to the San Fernando Valley, avoiding Waxman's "no-subway" zone.</p>
<p>However, the route from Vermont Avenue heading north was proposed to be elevated along Vermont and required the purchase and demolition of one or more of the hospitals located near the corner of Vermont &amp; Sunset in order for the trains to make the turn west onto Sunset continuing on elevated tracks, a major impact upon the community. In addition, the owners of TV and radio stations, and recording studios further west along Sunset (at that time <a href="/wiki/KTLA" title="KTLA">KTLA</a>, <a href="/wiki/KTTV" title="KTTV">KTTV</a> and <a href="/wiki/KCBS-TV" title="KCBS-TV">KCBS</a> were among several broadcast and post production facilities and music recording studios that were located along the proposed route's stretch of Sunset Blvd.) strongly protested the route claiming that the vibrations and noise from passing trains would interfere with their productions using sensitive microphones and recording equipment. The SCRTD later proposed to put trains underground along Sunset to mollify the media owners' concerns, but those same business interests strongly believed that even underground trains would still create sufficient vibrations to negatively impact their facilities and services and vowed to fight and file suit to prevent any rail line along their stretch of Sunset Blvd.</p>
<p>By then, new rounds of Federal money were available, and then-SCRTD CEO Allen Pegg announced that the transit agency was highly confident that sufficient funding for an all subway line, now proposed to travel under Hollywood Blvd to avoid conflicts with the studios on Sunset, could be secured. The line would then turn north along Highland Avenue to Universal City and North Hollywood (a Hollywood Bowl stop was determined not to have enough year-round ridership to meet FTA formulas for Federal funding, just one of the reasons for not building a station there). The FTA approved the all subway route and funding (although broken into 2 phases rather than the original 1 phase in order to spread the cost over time making it more likely to be approved by Congress as part of the Federal Budget) for the line as it exists today.</p>
<p>Building awareness to the new Metro Red Line required a massive public relations and advertising campaign. Several agencies were awarded contracts to supply information, create signage and billboards, and produce radio, newspaper and television advertising. Coronado Communications, in alliance with sub-contractor <a href="/wiki/Pangea_Corporation" title="Pangea Corporation">Pangea Corporation</a>, worked out a creative strategy to communicate to the Latino, Korean and Chinese communities. Under the direction of Coronado Communications' Fernando Oaxaca<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and Pangea's <a href="/wiki/Cheryl_Ann_Wong" title="Cheryl Ann Wong">Cheryl Ann Wong</a>, the campaign reached the target audience months before the Metro opened by utilizing traditional media and hosting special minority community events. As the Metro Red Line extended to the West Side of Los Angeles, another campaign was enacted by UltraSystems Environmental and Hintz and Balvin Communications, who put together a team to handle all the collateral and public relations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>MOS-1, which consisted of the original five stations from <a href="/wiki/Union_Station_(Los_Angeles)" title="Union Station (Los Angeles)">Union Station</a> to <a href="/wiki/Westlake/MacArthur_Park_station" title="Westlake/MacArthur Park station">Westlake/MacArthur Park</a>, opened on January 30, 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOS1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOS1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1995, during construction of the subway, a <a href="/wiki/Sinkhole" title="Sinkhole">sinkhole</a> appeared on <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Boulevard" title="Hollywood Boulevard">Hollywood Boulevard</a>, barely missing several workers and causing damage to buildings on the street. Subway construction was halted until the situation could be resolved. The contractor, Shea-Kiewit-Kenny, was replaced with a new contractor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tutor_Saliba" title="Tutor Saliba">Tutor Saliba</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>MOS-2 included three new stations between <a href="/wiki/Westlake/MacArthur_Park_station" title="Westlake/MacArthur Park station">Westlake/MacArthur Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wilshire/Western_station" title="Wilshire/Western station">Wilshire/Western</a>, which completed the branch now served by the <a href="/wiki/Purple_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)" title="Purple Line (Los Angeles Metro)">Purple Line</a> opened in 1996,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOS2A_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOS2A-10">[10]</a></sup> and five new stations from <a href="/wiki/Wilshire/Vermont_station" title="Wilshire/Vermont station">Wilshire/Vermont</a> to <a href="/wiki/Hollywood/Vine_station" title="Hollywood/Vine station">Hollywood/Vine</a> which opened in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOS2B_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOS2B-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1998, Los Angeles County Supervisor <a href="/wiki/Zev_Yaroslavsky" title="Zev Yaroslavsky">Zev Yaroslavsky</a> introduced a Los Angeles County initiative called the MTA Reform and Accountability Act of 1998 (Los Angeles County Proposition A). This initiative, passed by voters in 1998, bans the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County" title="Los Angeles County">Los Angeles County</a> revenue from existing sales taxes for subway tunneling. Supervisor <a href="/wiki/Zev_Yaroslavsky" title="Zev Yaroslavsky">Zev Yaroslavsky</a> later stated that local money could be used to cover subway-related costs, as long as it was not used directly for tunneling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In the time since this proposition became law, a new sales tax measure, <a href="/wiki/Measure_R" title="Measure R">Measure R</a>, was passed by voters which specifically provides funds for subway development.</p>
<p>MOS-3, which added new stations and extended the Red Line from <a href="/wiki/Hollywood/Vine_station" title="Hollywood/Vine station">Hollywood/Vine</a> to its final terminus at <a href="/wiki/North_Hollywood_station" title="North Hollywood station">North Hollywood</a>, opened in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOS3_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOS3-13">[13]</a></sup> Litigation over an illegally awarded contract to build the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hollywood/Highland_(Los_Angeles_Metro_station)" title="Hollywood/Highland (Los Angeles Metro station)">Hollywood/Highland</a> station and tunnels took more time to resolve than the actual construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>During construction, 2,000 fossils were discovered, including 64 extinct species of fish, the tusk of an <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">Ice Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephant</a> and the bones of an ancient <a href="/wiki/Bison_latifrons" title="Bison latifrons">longhorn bison</a>, a report funded by the MTA found. The report was authored by paleontologist Bruce Lander of Paleo Environmental Associates in <a href="/wiki/Irvine,_California" title="Irvine, California">Irvine</a>. Lander worked with a team of 28 scientists during construction of the Metro Rail Red Line. Fossil evidence showed that tens of thousands of years ago, <a href="/wiki/Ground_sloth" title="Ground sloth">ground sloths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equus_(genus)" title="Equus (genus)">horses</a>, elephants and <a href="/wiki/Camel" title="Camel">camels</a> roamed among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redwood" title="Redwood">redwood trees</a> in what is now Los Angeles, according to an MTA summary of the 300-page report. The scientists also found evidence of a great flood in the <a href="/wiki/San_Fernando_Valley" title="San Fernando Valley">San Fernando Valley</a> 9,000 years ago that swept away trees. Among the 64 extinct species of marine fish 39 had never before been discovered, the report said. The scientists found bones of an American <a href="/wiki/Mastodon" title="Mastodon">mastodon</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_camel" title="Western camel">western camel</a> and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harlan%27s_ground_sloth" title="Harlan's ground sloth">Harlan's ground sloth</a>. They found wood and pollen of land plants including incense cedar and coast redwood trees, and bones of birds, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrews" title="Shrews">shrews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cottontail_rabbit" title="Cottontail rabbit">cottontail rabbits</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gopher_(animal)" title="Gopher (animal)">gophers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mice" title="Mice">mice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kangaroo_rat" title="Kangaroo rat">kangaroo rats</a>. Some of the fossils are as much as 16.5 million years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>During construction there were allegations of corruption and safety issues, including cost overruns and tunnel walls having thicknesses less than specified or required by law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2005, the new <a href="/wiki/Orange_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)" title="Orange Line (Los Angeles Metro)">Orange Line (Los Angeles Metro)</a> began service. The busway was constructed instead of a further Red Line rail extension in the Valley. The Orange Line uses train-like two-cabin <a href="/wiki/Articulated_bus" title="Articulated bus">articulated bus</a> bodies to provide high-capacity transit service across an east-west corridor in the southern <a href="/wiki/San_Fernando_Valley" title="San Fernando Valley">San Fernando Valley</a>. The Red Line connects to the Orange Line at <a href="/wiki/North_Hollywood_station" title="North Hollywood station">North Hollywood</a> station. The Orange Line feeds about 15,000 new boardings into the Red Line at the North Hollywood terminus. Currently, little chance exists for further underground Red Line extension west from its northern terminus.</p>
<p>In August 2006, the Wilshire branch of the Red Line, operating between <a href="/wiki/Union_Station_(Los_Angeles)" title="Union Station (Los Angeles)">Union Station</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wilshire/Western_station" title="Wilshire/Western station">Wilshire/Western</a>, was redesignated by Metro as the <a href="/wiki/Purple_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)" title="Purple Line (Los Angeles Metro)">Metro Purple Line</a> to distinguish it from the Downtown-North Hollywood service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> This helps riders in the overlapping section of the routes to avoid confusion and taking the wrong train.</p>

<p>The route currently known as the Red Line was originally intended to continue beyond its eastern terminus at <a href="/wiki/Union_Station_(Los_Angeles)" title="Union Station (Los Angeles)">Union Station</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Los_Angeles_(region)" title="East Los Angeles (region)">East Los Angeles</a>. At the north end of the route, the Red Line was to turn west from <a href="/wiki/North_Hollywood_station" title="North Hollywood station">North Hollywood</a> station toward <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warner_Center" title="Warner Center">Warner Center</a>. However, a 1998 proposition was passed by voters, which banned use of county sales tax revenue for subway construction due to the high cost of construction and problems associated with tunneling under Hollywood Blvd.</p>
<p>The tunneling ban put an end to expansion of the Red Line for the then foreseeable future. The route to Warner Center was turned into the Metro Orange Line, a bus rapid transitway (BRT) service. However, in recent years, new legislation has been passed reflecting new public support for subway development, and Metro is now currently working on two subway projects (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Westside_Subway_Extension_(Los_Angeles_Metro)" title="Westside Subway Extension (Los Angeles Metro)">Purple Line Extension</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regional_Connector_(Los_Angeles_Metro)" title="Regional Connector (Los Angeles Metro)">Regional Connector</a>) in other parts of Los Angeles.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extension_to_East_Valley">Extension to East Valley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Extension to East Valley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Former Mayor Antonio Villaraigosa has mentioned extending the Red Line from its current North Hollywood Station terminus along Lankershim Boulevard to the northeastern San Fernando Valley, with a terminus in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sylmar,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="Sylmar, Los Angeles, California">Sylmar</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>One long-term possibility might be an underground extension of another mile or two to a future high-rise housing district, or to a multi-modal transportation hub station at <a href="/wiki/Bob_Hope_Airport" title="Bob Hope Airport">Bob Hope Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Burbank,_California" title="Burbank, California">Burbank</a>, a distance of approximately four miles. It would possibly go under Vineland Avenue and Vanowen Street.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2006 a large number of housing units, including a high-rise tower was completed very near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Hollywood,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="North Hollywood, Los Angeles, California">North Hollywood</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NoHo_Arts_District" title="NoHo Arts District">NoHo Arts District</a>) station. A master planned multi high-rise complex further to the north could justify a future short extension, and also allow more commuter parking to be developed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> No plan of this sort has been formally proposed, though some transit advocates have suggested that the Orange Line may be extended along the same route as mentioned above.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extension_to_Arts_District">Extension to Arts District</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Extension to Arts District">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, at the request of L.A. City Councilman <a href="/wiki/Tom_LaBonge" title="Tom LaBonge">Tom LaBonge</a>, Metro staff studied the possibility of adding a station along the west bank of the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_River" title="Los Angeles River">Los Angeles River</a> to 6th Street and Santa Fe Avenue. The study concluded that such an extension, completed at-grade along Metro-owned right-of-way, could be completed for as little as $90 million.</p>
<p>The study suggested an alternative station at the Division 20 Yard north of 4th Street and Santa Fe Avenue. This station would be closer to the residential population of the Arts District. As new turnback tracks will need to be built as part of the <a href="/wiki/Purple_Line_Extension" title="Purple Line Extension">Purple Line Extension</a> (to allow shorter headways), this Arts District extension could possibly be partially completed as part of the Purple Line Extension project, lowering the incremental cost of the station while increasing its usability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extensions_East">Extensions East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Extensions East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Plans of extending the Red Line to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Los_Angeles_(region)" title="East Los Angeles (region)">Eastside</a> have been set aside with opening of the <a href="/wiki/Gold_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)" title="Gold Line (Los Angeles Metro)">Metro Gold Line</a> East extension to the neighborhoods of Boyle Heights and East Los Angeles in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RAIL_22_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-RAIL_22-20">[20]</a></sup> Although there are no plans to do such, it is conceivable that plans for a future eastward extension could involve the <a href="/wiki/San_Gabriel_Valley" title="San Gabriel Valley">San Gabriel Valley</a> rather than the Eastside. Some citizen proposals have included the conversion of the <a href="/wiki/El_Monte_Busway" title="El Monte Busway">El Monte Busway</a> to heavy rail, although this would disrupt the existing bus and Metrolink service along that corridor. Other rights-of-way that could host a Red Line extension, whether subway or at-grade, include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Pacific" title="Union Pacific">Union Pacific</a>'s Alhambra Trench, the former <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Electric" title="Pacific Electric">Pacific Electric</a> two-track right of way extending through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_Terrace" title="City Terrace">City Terrace</a> area to <a href="/wiki/El_Monte,_California" title="El Monte, California">El Monte</a> and <a href="/wiki/Covina,_California" title="Covina, California">Covina</a>, and the median of <a href="/wiki/Huntington_Drive" title="Huntington Drive">Huntington Drive</a>, which also held a two-track Pacific Electric line extending as far as <a href="/wiki/Azusa,_California" title="Azusa, California">Azusa</a> &amp; Glendora, until 1951, when it was removed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Purple_Line_Extension">Purple Line Extension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Purple Line Extension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Metro is currently constructing the <a href="/wiki/Purple_Line_Extension" title="Purple Line Extension">Purple Line Extension</a> (formerly known as the Westside Subway Extension project) to extend the Purple Line (originally part of the Red Line) westward, to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Westside,_Los_Angeles" title="Westside, Los Angeles">Westside</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maintenance">Maintenance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Maintenance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Metro_Red_%26_Purple_Lines_Train_Car.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2432" data-file-width="4320" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Metro_Red_%26_Purple_Lines_Train_Car.JPG/220px-Metro_Red_%26_Purple_Lines_Train_Car.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Metro_Red_%26_Purple_Lines_Train_Car.JPG/330px-Metro_Red_%26_Purple_Lines_Train_Car.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Metro_Red_%26_Purple_Lines_Train_Car.JPG/440px-Metro_Red_%26_Purple_Lines_Train_Car.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Metro_Red_%26_Purple_Lines_Train_Car.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Inside the car of a Metro Red Line. Both the Metro Red and Purple Lines use the Ansaldobreda A650 cars.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Red Line operates out of the Division 20 Yard (Santa Fe Yard) located at 320 South Santa Fe Avenue in Downtown Los Angeles. This yard stores the fleet used on the Red and Purple Lines, and where heavy maintenance is performed. Cars reach this yard by continuing past Union Station, making a right turn and surfacing at the Eastern terminus of Ducommun Street. They then travel south to 1st Street, through a washing station, and enter the yard.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rolling_stock">Rolling stock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Rolling stock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Red Line uses <a href="/wiki/Breda_A650" title="Breda A650">Breda A650</a> 75-foot (23 m) electric <a href="/wiki/Multiple_unit" title="Multiple unit">multiple unit</a> cars built by <a href="/wiki/Societ%C3%A0_Italiana_Ernesto_Breda" title="Società Italiana Ernesto Breda">Breda</a> in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. Trains usually run in six-car consists during peak hours and four-car consists outside of peak hours. The acceleration for cars #530 and up is similar to that of cars used by the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Metro_rolling_stock" title="Washington Metro rolling stock">Washington Metro</a> because they both use <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> <a href="/wiki/Traction_motor" title="Traction motor">traction motors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The cars are maintained in a Metro yard on Santa Fe Avenue near 4th Street alongside the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_River" title="Los Angeles River">Los Angeles River</a> in downtown Los Angeles.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidents">Incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2006_mercury_spillage">2006 mercury spillage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: 2006 mercury spillage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On December 22, 2006, a transient accidentally spilled a vial of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> on the platform at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pershing_Square_(LACMTA_station)" title="Pershing Square (LACMTA station)">Pershing Square</a> station. He then located a passenger information intercom and told the <a href="/wiki/Operator_(profession)" title="Operator (profession)">operator</a> that he spilled mercury before boarding a train.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County_Sheriff%27s_Department" title="Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department">Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department</a> was not notified until the next day, eight hours later. Metro has responded since the incident by giving hazardous materials (Hazmat) training to its field employees and operators so they can identify hazardous substances and take correct action in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_fatal_stabbing">2011 fatal stabbing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: 2011 fatal stabbing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On August 19, 2011 an altercation between two passengers resulted in a fatal stabbing of one of the involved on the train near the Hollywood and Vine station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The suspect was caught on August 24.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2012_fatal_accident">2012 fatal accident</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Line_(Los_Angeles_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: 2012 fatal accident">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On September 4, 2012, a 54-year-old man fell onto the tracks at the North Hollywood station and was hit by an oncoming train.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> He was rescued by firefighters and paramedics, but died at the hospital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
