<p><b>Digital Negative</b> (<b>DNG</b>) is a patented, <a href="/wiki/Open_format" title="Open format">open, non-free</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lossless" title="Lossless">lossless</a> <a href="/wiki/Raw_image_format" title="Raw image format">raw image format</a> written by <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Systems" title="Adobe Systems">Adobe</a> used for <a href="/wiki/Digital_photography" title="Digital photography">digital photography</a>. It was launched on September 27, 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adobe2004_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-adobe2004-1">[1]</a></sup> The launch was accompanied by the first version of the DNG specification,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGspecification_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-DNGspecification-2">[2]</a></sup> plus various products, including a free-of-charge DNG converter utility. All Adobe photo manipulation software (such as <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Photoshop" title="Adobe Photoshop">Adobe Photoshop</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adobe_Lightroom" title="Adobe Lightroom">Adobe Lightroom</a>) released since the launch supports DNG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PEARSONadobeproducts_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-PEARSONadobeproducts-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>DNG is based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tag_Image_File_Format_/_Electronic_Photography" title="Tag Image File Format / Electronic Photography">TIFF/EP</a> <a href="/wiki/Open_standard" title="Open standard">standard format</a>, and mandates significant use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metadata_(computing)" title="Metadata (computing)">metadata</a>. Use of the file format is royalty-free; Adobe has published a license allowing anyone to exploit DNG,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNHlicense_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-DNHlicense-4">[4]</a></sup> and has also stated that there are no known intellectual property encumbrances or license requirements for DNG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CinemaDNGnoencumbrance_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CinemaDNGnoencumbrance-5">[5]</a></sup> Adobe stated that if there were a consensus that DNG should be controlled by a <a href="/wiki/Standards_organization" title="Standards organization">standards body</a>, they were open to the idea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DMDkevinconnor_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-DMDkevinconnor-6">[6]</a></sup> Adobe has submitted DNG to ISO for incorporation into their revision of TIFF/EP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DPReviewISO_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-DPReviewISO-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rationale_for_DNG">Rationale for DNG</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_Negative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rationale for DNG">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Given the existence of other <a href="/wiki/Raw_image_format" title="Raw image format">raw image formats</a>, Adobe's creation of DNG as a competing format implies that DNG is unusual and satisfies objectives that other raw image formats do not. These objectives and the associated characteristics of DNG, as well as assessments of whether these objectives are met, are described below. Increasingly, professional archivists and conservationists, working for respectable organizations, variously suggest or recommend DNG for archival purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UPDIG_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-UPDIG-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADSDA_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ADSDA-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UofC_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-UofC-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCPSR_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-IUCPSR-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JISC_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-JISC-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDEADISC_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-IDEADISC-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPGM_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-JPGM-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AIMEMG_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-AIMEMG-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AABC_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-AABC-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Objectives">Objectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_Negative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Objectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These objectives are repeatedly emphasized in Adobe documents:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adobe2004_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-adobe2004-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DMDkevinconnor_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-DMDkevinconnor-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGmain_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-DNGmain-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGintromanufacturers_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-DNGintromanufacturers-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Digital image preservation (sometimes known as "archiving")</i>: to be suitable for the purpose of preserving digital images as an authentic resource for future generations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USLOCfactors_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-USLOCfactors-19">[19]</a></sup> <i>Assessment</i>: The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Library_of_Congress" title="US Library of Congress">US Library of Congress</a> states that DNG is a recommended alternative to other raw image formats: "Less desirable file formats: RAW; Suggested alternatives: DNG".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USLOCpreferences_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-USLOCpreferences-20">[20]</a></sup> The Digital Photography Best Practices and Workflow (dpBestflow) project, funded by the United States Library of Congress and run by the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Media_Photographers" title="American Society of Media Photographers">American Society of Media Photographers (ASMP)</a>, singles out DNG, and states "DNG files have proven to be significantly more useful than the proprietary raw files in our workflow".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dpBestflowDNG_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-dpBestflowDNG-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Easy and/or comprehensive exploitation by software developers</i>: to enable software to be developed without the need for <a href="/wiki/Reverse_engineering" title="Reverse engineering">reverse engineering</a>; and to avoid the need for frequent software upgrades and re-releases to cater for new cameras. <i>Assessment</i>: Software could support raw formats without DNG, by using <a href="/wiki/Reverse_engineering" title="Reverse engineering">reverse engineering</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Dcraw" title="Dcraw">dcraw</a>; DNG makes it easier, and many software products can handle, via DNG, images from cameras that they have no specific knowledge of.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PEARSONnotnative_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-PEARSONnotnative-22">[22]</a></sup> An unresolved restriction is that any edit/development settings stored in the DNG file by a software product are unlikely to be recognized by a product from a different company. (This problem is not specific to DNG).</li>
<li><i>In-camera use by camera manufacturers</i>: to be suitable for many camera manufacturers to use as a native or optional <a href="/wiki/Raw_image_format" title="Raw image format">raw image format</a> in many cameras. <i>Assessment</i>: About 12 camera manufacturers have used DNG in-camera. About 38 camera models have used DNG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PEARSONcameramakers_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-PEARSONcameramakers-23">[23]</a></sup> Raw image formats for more than 230 camera models can be converted to DNG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PEARSONhistory_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-PEARSONhistory-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Multi-vendor interoperability</i>: to be suitable for workflows where different hardware and software components share raw image files and/or transmit and receive them.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_Negative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>All of the above objectives are facilitated or enabled by most of these characteristics:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USLOCdng_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-USLOCdng-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Freely-available specification</i>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LLtherawflaw_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-LLtherawflaw-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RawFlawLLWord_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-RawFlawLLWord-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RawFlawLLPDF_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-RawFlawLLPDF-28">[28]</a></sup> this can be downloaded from the Adobe website without negotiation or needing justification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGspecification_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-DNGspecification-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Format based on open specifications and/or standards</i>: DNG is compatible with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tag_Image_File_Format_/_Electronic_Photography" title="Tag Image File Format / Electronic Photography">TIFF/EP</a>, and various <a href="/wiki/Open_format" title="Open format">open formats</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Open_standard" title="Open standard">standards</a> are used, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exchangeable_image_file_format" title="Exchangeable image file format">Exif metadata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Extensible_Metadata_Platform" title="Extensible Metadata Platform">XMP metadata</a>, <a href="/wiki/IPTC_Information_Interchange_Model" title="IPTC Information Interchange Model">IPTC metadata</a>, <a href="/wiki/CIE_1931_color_space" title="CIE 1931 color space">CIE XYZ coordinates</a> and <a href="/wiki/JPEG" title="JPEG">JPEG</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGspecification_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-DNGspecification-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PEARSONstandards_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-PEARSONstandards-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Self-contained file format</i>: a DNG file contains the data (raw image data and <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a>) needed to render an image without needing additional knowledge of the characteristics of the camera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGspecification_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-DNGspecification-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PEARSONnotnative_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-PEARSONnotnative-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Version control scheme</i>: it has a version scheme built into it that allows the DNG specification, DNG writers, and DNG readers, to evolve at their own paces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGspecification4_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-DNGspecification4-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Version_control_in_DNG_files_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Version_control_in_DNG_files-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Freely-available source-code-based <a href="/wiki/Software_development_kit" title="Software development kit">software development kit (SDK)</a></i>: there are three aspects - there <i>is</i> an SDK; it is source-code-based (as can be verified by examination); and it can be downloaded from the Adobe website without needing justification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Documented to have no known intellectual property encumbrances or license requirements</i>: there is both a "Digital Negative (DNG) Specification Patent License" which says that anyone can exploit DNG,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNHlicense_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-DNHlicense-4">[4]</a></sup> and a statement that there are no known intellectual property encumbrances or license requirements for DNG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CinemaDNGnoencumbrance_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-CinemaDNGnoencumbrance-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Lossless and lossy compression (optional)</i>: DNG support optional lossless and (since version 1.4) also lossy compression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNG14SpecNotes_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-DNG14SpecNotes-33">[33]</a></sup> The lossy compressions losses are practically indistinguishable in real world images.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGLossyTest_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-DNGLossyTest-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_summary">Technical summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_Negative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Technical summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A DNG file always contains data for one main image, plus metadata, and optionally contains at least one JPEG preview.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGspecification_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-DNGspecification-2">[2]</a></sup> It normally has the extension "dng" or "DNG".</p>
<p>DNG conforms to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tag_Image_File_Format_/_Electronic_Photography" title="Tag Image File Format / Electronic Photography">TIFF/EP</a> and is structured according to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tagged_Image_File_Format" title="Tagged Image File Format">TIFF</a>. DNG supports various formats of metadata, (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exchangeable_image_file_format" title="Exchangeable image file format">Exif metadata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Extensible_Metadata_Platform" title="Extensible Metadata Platform">XMP metadata</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_Press_Telecommunications_Council" title="International Press Telecommunications Council">IPTC metadata</a>), and specifies a set of mandated metadata.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGspecification4_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-DNGspecification4-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>DNG is both a raw image format and a format that supports "non-raw", or partly processed, images.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGspecification_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-DNGspecification-2">[2]</a></sup> The latter (non-raw) format is known as "Linear DNG".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Linear DNG is still scene-referred<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scene-referred_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scene-referred-36">[36]</a></sup> and can still benefit from many of the operations typically performed by a raw converter, such as white balance, the application of a camera color profile, HDR compositing, etc. All images that can be supported as raw images can <i>also</i> be supported as Linear DNG. Images from the <a href="/wiki/Foveon_X3_sensor" title="Foveon X3 sensor">Foveon X3 sensor</a> or similar, hence especially <a href="/wiki/Sigma_Corporation" title="Sigma Corporation">Sigma</a> cameras, can <i>only</i> be supported as Linear DNG.</p>
<p>DNG can contain raw image data from sensors with various configurations of <a href="/wiki/Color_filter_array" title="Color filter array">color filter array (CFA)</a>. These include: conventional <a href="/wiki/Bayer_filter" title="Bayer filter">Bayer filters</a>, using three colors and rectangular pixels; four-color CFAs, for example the <a href="/wiki/RGBE_filter" title="RGBE filter">RGBE filter</a> used in the <a href="/wiki/Sony_Cyber-shot_DSC-F828" title="Sony Cyber-shot DSC-F828">Sony Cyber-shot DSC-F828</a>; rectangular (non-square) pixels, for example as used in the <a href="/wiki/Nikon_D1#D1H_and_D1X" title="Nikon D1">Nikon D1X</a>; and offset sensors (for example with octagonal pixels) such as <a href="/wiki/Super_CCD" title="Super CCD">Super CCD</a> sensors of various types, as used in various <a href="/wiki/Fujifilm" title="Fujifilm">Fujifilm</a> cameras. (Or combinations of these if necessary). DNG specifies metadata describing these individual parameters; this is one significant extension to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tag_Image_File_Format_/_Electronic_Photography" title="Tag Image File Format / Electronic Photography">TIFF/EP</a>.</p>
<p>When used in a <a href="/wiki/CinemaDNG" title="CinemaDNG">CinemaDNG</a> movie clip, each frame is encoded using the above DNG image format. The clip's image stream can then be stored in one of two formats: either as video essence using frame-based wrapping in an <a href="/wiki/Material_Exchange_Format" title="Material Exchange Format">MXF</a> file, or as a sequence of DNG files in a specified file directory.</p>
<p>Contrary to its name (Digital Negative) the DNG format doesn't distinguish negative and positive data<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> - all data is considered to be describing a positive image. While this is not an issue when working with images from digital cameras (which are always positive), working with scanned (by a <a href="/wiki/Film_scanner" title="Film scanner">film scanner</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DSLR" title="DSLR">DSLR</a> <a href="/wiki/Copy_stand" title="Copy stand">copy stand</a>) film negatives saved as raw DNG files is complicated, because the resulted image is not automatically inverted and thus impossible to be used directly. A way to get around this is using an inverted curve in the photo editing application, however this reverses the effect of the image controls (<a href="/wiki/Exposure_(photography)" title="Exposure (photography)">Exposure</a>, Shadow and Highlight details, etc) which complicates the photo editing.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_Negative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The reaction to DNG has been mixed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PEARSONhistory_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-PEARSONhistory-24">[24]</a></sup> A few camera manufacturers stated their intention to use DNG at launch. The first supported DNG about 9 months after launch. Several more niche and minority camera manufacturers added support after this (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Leica_Camera" title="Leica Camera">Leica</a>). The largest camera manufacturers have apparently never indicated an intention to use DNG (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Nikon" title="Nikon">Nikon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canon_Inc." title="Canon Inc.">Canon</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Some software products supported DNG within 5 months of launch, with many more following. Some only support DNG from cameras writing DNG, or from cameras supported via native raw image formats.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/OpenRAW" title="OpenRAW">OpenRAW</a> was an advocacy and lobby group with the motto "Digital Image Preservation Through Open Documentation". They became <a href="/wiki/OpenRAW#Limitation" title="OpenRAW">opposed to DNG</a>.</p>
<p>Some photographic competitions do not accept converted files, and some do not accept DNG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="DNG_conversion">DNG conversion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_Negative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: DNG conversion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"DNG conversion" refers to the process of generating a DNG file from a non-DNG image. (This is in contrast to "raw conversion", which typically refers to reading and processing a <a href="/wiki/Raw_image_format" title="Raw image format">raw file</a>, which might be a DNG file, and generating some other type of output from it). DNG conversion is one of the sources of DNG files, the other being direct output from cameras and <a href="/wiki/Digital_camera_back" title="Digital camera back">digital backs</a>.</p>
<p>Several software products are able to do DNG conversion. The original such product is <i>Adobe DNG Converter</i> or <i>DNG Converter</i>, a freely available stand-alone utility from Adobe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGmain_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-DNGmain-17">[17]</a></sup> Other Adobe products such as the <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Photoshop#Plugins" title="Adobe Photoshop">ACR plugin to Photoshop</a> or <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Photoshop_Lightroom" title="Adobe Photoshop Lightroom">Lightroom</a> can also generate DNG files from other image files.</p>
<p>Most DNG converters are supplied by companies other than Adobe. For example:</p>
<ul>
<li>The software that <a href="/wiki/Pentax" title="Pentax">Pentax</a> supplies with all their dSLR cameras can convert PEF raw image files from Pentax cameras into DNG files.</li>
<li>Flexcolor and Phocus from <a href="/wiki/Hasselblad" title="Hasselblad">Hasselblad</a> can convert 3FR raw image files from Hasselblad cameras and digital backs into DNG files.</li>
<li>Capture One from <a href="/wiki/Phase_One_(company)" title="Phase One (company)">Phase One</a> is a raw converter that can process not only raw image files from Phase One digital backs, but also raw image files from many other cameras too. Capture One can save images from many of those cameras to DNG.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/KDE_Image_Plugin_Interface" title="KDE Image Plugin Interface">KDE Image Plugin Interface</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Application_programming_interface" title="Application programming interface">API</a>, that can save the images it is processing to DNG. It can be used standalone or with any image processing applications of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kde" title="Kde">KDE</a> desktop environment under <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windows" title="Windows">Windows</a>.</li>
<li>A number of DNG converters have been developed by "amateurs" to enable raw images from their favored camera or digital back to be processed in a large range of raw converters. These include cases where cameras have been hacked to output raw images that have then been converted to DNG.</li>
</ul>
<p>The process of DNG conversion involves extracting raw image data from the source file and assembling it according to the DNG specification into the required TIFF format. This optionally involves compressing it. Metadata as defined in the DNG specification is also put into that TIFF assembly. Some of this metadata is based on the characteristics of the camera, and especially of its sensor. Other metadata may be image-dependent or camera-setting dependent. So a DNG converter must have knowledge of the camera model concerned, and be able to process the source raw image file including key metadata. Optionally a JPEG preview is obtained and added. Finally, all of this is written as a DNG file.</p>
<p>DNG conversion typically leaves the original raw image file intact. For safety, many photographers retain the original raw image file on one medium while using the DNG file on another, enabling them to recover from a range of hardware, software, and human, failures and errors. For example, it has been reported in user forums that some versions of the Adobe DNG Converter don't preserve all the raw data from raw images from some camera models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary_of_products_that_support_DNG_in_some_way">Summary of products that support DNG in some way</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_Negative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Summary of products that support DNG in some way">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This section summarizes other more comprehensive lists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGsupports_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-DNGsupports-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adobe_products">Adobe products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_Negative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Adobe products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>All raw image file handling products from Adobe now support DNG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PEARSONadobeproducts_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-PEARSONadobeproducts-3">[3]</a></sup> <i>Adobe DNG Converter</i> was published by <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Systems" title="Adobe Systems">Adobe Systems</a> on September 27, 2004. It converts different camera raw format files into the Digital Negative (DNG) standard. It also supports <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lossless_data_compression" title="Lossless data compression">lossless data compression</a> when converting. The program is free of charge. It can be downloaded at Adobe's site (for Microsoft Windows<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and the Apple Macintosh<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_cameras_and_related_software">Digital cameras and related software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_Negative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Digital cameras and related software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Use by camera manufacturers varies; there are about 15 camera manufacturers that use DNG, including a few that specialize in movie cameras:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PEARSONcameramakers_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-PEARSONcameramakers-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Niche camera manufacturers typically use DNG in new cameras (including a <a href="/wiki/Digiscoping" title="Digiscoping">digiscope</a>, <a href="/wiki/Panorama" title="Panorama">panorama</a> cameras, and at least one movie camera). The article on <a href="/wiki/Raw_image_format" title="Raw image format">raw image formats</a> illustrates the complicated relationship between new raw image formats and third-party software developers. Using DNG provides immediate support for these cameras by a large range of software products.</li>
<li>High-end smartphone cameras using iOS 10,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Android 5 or Windows Mobile that are capable to shoot RAW images typically use DNG (e.g. Samsung and Nokia).</li>
<li>Some low market share but conventional camera manufacturers use DNG in new cameras. Camera manufacturers that do not supply their own software for processing raw images typically, but not always, use DNG.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pentax" title="Pentax">Pentax</a> typically offers users the option of whether to use Pentax's own raw image format (PEF) or DNG, but some, for example <a href="/wiki/Pentax_Q_series" title="Pentax Q series">Pentax Q, Q10 and Q7</a>, do not support PEF. For example, the digital SLR Camera Pentax K-x does offer the ability to save PEF or DNG or even DNG+ which saves two files, a DNG and a separate JPEG file at the same time.</li>
<li>If a camera uses DNG, and that camera manufacturer supplies software, it will support DNG. It may support DNG only from their own cameras, or support it more generically.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canon_(company)" title="Canon (company)">Canon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nikon" title="Nikon">Nikon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Panasonic" title="Panasonic">Panasonic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olympus_Corporation" title="Olympus Corporation">Olympus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fujifilm" title="Fujifilm">Fuji</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sigma_Corporation" title="Sigma Corporation">Sigma</a> do not use DNG in their cameras. If a camera manufacturer's cameras do not use DNG, their software is unlikely to support DNG unless that software is also sold independently of the cameras.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Third-party_software">Third-party software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_Negative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Third-party software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Support by software suppliers varies; there are of the order of 200 software products that use DNG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGsupports_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-DNGsupports-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The majority of raw handling software products support DNG. Most provide generic support, while a few support it only if it is output directly from a camera. The type of support varies considerably. There appear to be very few third party software products that process raw images but don't support DNG. This may reflect the difficulty of discovering all of those that do not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PEARSONnotyet_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-PEARSONnotyet-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Versions_of_the_specification">Versions of the specification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_Negative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Versions of the specification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All versions of the specification remain valid, in the sense that DNG files conforming to old versions should still be read and processed by DNG readers capable of processing later versions. DNG has a version scheme built into it that allows the DNG specification, DNG writers, and DNG readers, to evolve at their own paces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Version_control_in_DNG_files_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Version_control_in_DNG_files-31">[31]</a></sup> Each version of the specification describes its compatibility with previous versions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGspecification_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-DNGspecification-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>1.0.0.0, published September, 2004</dt>
<dd>This version accompanied the launch of DNG and related products. It was a rare, possibly unique, example of a raw image format specification published by its owner. It was adequate for representing typical images, but it had a few errors and deficiencies (specifically the lack of support of "masked pixels" and an inadvertent deviation from the JPEG specification) that required it soon to be replaced by the next version.</dd>
<dt>1.1.0.0, published February, 2005</dt>
<dd>This version corrected the flaws in the first version. It has proved capable of representing raw images for a large variety of cameras (both when written in-camera or via conversion from other raw image formats) for a few years, and it is the version still typically written in-camera.</dd>
<dt>1.2.0.0, published May 2008</dt>
<dd>This version was based on experience and feedback from other companies about DNG since its launch. It introduced many new features, especially several new options for color specification under the general heading of "Camera Profiles". These are mainly of value to software products wanting their own flavor of color handling. This version permits administrative control of Camera Profiles, including calibration signatures and copyright information.</dd>
<dt>1.3.0.0, published June, 2009</dt>
<dd>This version added various improvements, but the major change was to introduce "Opcodes". A Opcode is an algorithm to be applied to some or all of the image data, described in the specification, and (optionally) implemented in the product that reads and processes the DNG file. The DNG file itself holds lists of Opcodes to be executed, together with the parameters to be applied on execution. In effect, the DNG file can contain lists of "function calls" to be executed at various stages in the raw conversion process. For example, the <i>WarpRectilinear</i> Opcode "applies a warp to an image and can be used to correct geometric distortion and lateral (transverse) chromatic aberration for rectilinear lenses". This is an example of an algorithm that cannot be applied to the raw image data itself before it is placed into the DNG file, because it should be executed <i>after</i> <a href="/wiki/Demosaicing" title="Demosaicing">demosaicing</a>. There are 13 Opcodes described in this version, and each Opcode is accompanied by a specification version so that more can be added in future.</dd>
<dt>1.4.0.0, published Sept., 2012</dt>
<dd>This version added Floating Point Image Data, Transparent Pixels, Proxy DNG Files, and additional tags. It also added SampleFormat and Predictor.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>CinemaDNG, published September 2009</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/CinemaDNG" title="CinemaDNG">CinemaDNG</a> uses DNG for each frame of a movie clip. There are additional tags specifically for movies: <i>TimeCodes</i> and <i>FrameRate</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CinemaDNGspecification_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-CinemaDNGspecification-56">[56]</a></sup> It is not clear whether these tags will be added to a later version of the DNG specification, or will remain separately described only in the CinemaDNG specification.</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standardization">Standardization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Digital_Negative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Standardization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>DNG is not (yet) a standard format, but is based on several <a href="/wiki/Open_format" title="Open format">open formats</a> or <a href="/wiki/Open_standard" title="Open standard">standards</a> and is being used by ISO in its revision of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tag_Image_File_Format_/_Electronic_Photography" title="Tag Image File Format / Electronic Photography">TIFF/EP</a>. A timeline:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>2001</i>: The ISO standard raw image format, ISO 12234-2, better known as TIFF/EP, was ratified and published. It also supports "non-raw", or "processed", images. TIFF/EP provided a basis for the raw image formats of a number of cameras, but they typically added their own proprietary data. Some cameras have sensors that cannot be described by that version of TIFF/EP.</li>
<li><i>2004, September</i>: Adobe launched DNG. Its specification states it "is compatible with the TIFF-EP standard".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGspecification_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-DNGspecification-2">[2]</a></sup> It is a TIFF/EP extension with considerably more specified metadata, brought up-to-date and made fit for purpose. DNG also exploits various other <a href="/wiki/Open_format" title="Open format">open formats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Open_standard" title="Open standard">standards</a>, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exchangeable_image_file_format" title="Exchangeable image file format">Exif metadata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Extensible_Metadata_Platform" title="Extensible Metadata Platform">XMP metadata</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_Press_Telecommunications_Council" title="International Press Telecommunications Council">IPTC metadata</a>, <a href="/wiki/CIE_1931_color_space" title="CIE 1931 color space">CIE XYZ coordinates</a>, <a href="/wiki/ICC_profile" title="ICC profile">ICC profiles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/JPEG" title="JPEG">JPEG</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PEARSONstandards_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-PEARSONstandards-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNGspecification_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-DNGspecification-2">[2]</a></sup> Although DNG supports more sensor configurations than TIFF/EP (for example, cameras from <a href="/wiki/Fujifilm" title="Fujifilm">Fujifilm</a> using <a href="/wiki/Super_CCD" title="Super CCD">Super CCD</a> sensors), it still doesn't support all sensor types as raw images, especially those using the <a href="/wiki/Foveon_X3_sensor" title="Foveon X3 sensor">Foveon X3 sensor</a> or similar, hence especially <a href="/wiki/Sigma_Corporation" title="Sigma Corporation">Sigma</a> cameras.</li>
<li><i>2006</i>: TIFF/EP began its 5-year revision cycle.</li>
<li><i>2006 to 2007</i>: Adobe offered the DNG specification to ISO to be part of ISO's revised TIFF/EP standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DPReviewISO_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-DPReviewISO-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>2008, September &amp; October</i>: Minutes of ISO/TC 130/WG2 — Prepress Data Exchange, 37th Meeting: "WG 18 is revising the two-part standard (ISO 12234), which addresses digital camera removable memory. The revision of... Part 2 will add DNG into TIFF/EP." A progress report from ISO about the revision of TIFF/EP stated that the revision "...currently includes two "interoperability-profiles," "IP 1" for processed image data, using ".TIF" extension, and "IP 2" for "raw" image data, ".DNG" extension".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>2009, September</i>: Minutes of ISO/TC 130/WG2 — Prepress Data Exchange, 39th Meeting: the revision of TIFF/EP "is comprehensive to support many different use cases, including backward compatibility with current TIFF readers and support of Adobe DNG... Profile 2 (proposed extension .dng, if Adobe is in agreement) is intended for camera raw images, including un-demosaiced images... This format will be similar to DNG 1.3, which serves as the starting point for development."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
