<p>The <b>Lerkendal Stadion</b> (<small>Norwegian pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Norwegian" title="Help:IPA for Norwegian">[ˈlærkənˈdɑːl ˈstɑːdɪʊn]</a></span>) is an <a href="/wiki/All-seater_stadium" title="All-seater stadium">all-seater</a> <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> <a href="/wiki/Stadium" title="Stadium">stadium</a> located at <a href="/wiki/Lerkendal" title="Lerkendal">Lerkendal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Trondheim" title="Trondheim">Trondheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>. The home ground of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tippeligaen" title="Tippeligaen">Tippeligaen</a> side <a href="/wiki/Rosenborg_BK" title="Rosenborg BK">Rosenborg BK</a>, it has a capacity for 21,405 spectators, making it the second-largest football stadium in the country.</p>
<p>Lerkendal opened as a <a href="/wiki/Multi-purpose_stadium" title="Multi-purpose stadium">multi-purpose stadium</a> on 10 August 1947, as the main football and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athletics_(sport)" title="Athletics (sport)">athletics</a> stadium in Trondheim. Originally the venue was mostly used by the football teams <a href="/wiki/SK_Freidig" title="SK Freidig">SK Freidig</a> and <a href="/wiki/FK_Kvik" title="FK Kvik">FK Kvik</a>, and Rosenborg did not became a tenant until 1957. A new grandstand with roof was completed in 1962, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floodlights_(sport)" title="Floodlights (sport)">floodlights</a> were installed in 1968. The oldest of the current stands were built in 1996, along with new lighting. Three more grandstands were built between 2000 and 2002, which also saw the removal of the athletics facilities and the sale of the stadium from <a href="/wiki/Trondheim" title="Trondheim">Trondheim</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_municipalities_of_Norway" title="List of municipalities of Norway">Municipality</a> to Rosenborg. Further expansions plans have been launched, to increase capacity by filling in the corners and possibly by building a <a href="/wiki/Retractable_roof" title="Retractable roof">retractable roof</a>.</p>
<p>The record attendance of 28,569 dates from the decisive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tippeligaen" title="Tippeligaen">league</a> match against <a href="/wiki/Lillestr%C3%B8m_SK" title="Lillestrøm SK">Lillestrøm</a> in <a href="/wiki/1985_Norwegian_First_Division" title="1985 Norwegian First Division">1985</a>. Ten international matches were held at Lerkendal between 1951 and 1990. One domestic cup final has been held at Lerkendal; the Women's Cup Final in 1986.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lerkendal_Stadion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lerkendal_Stadion_(1947).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2572" data-file-width="3535" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Lerkendal_Stadion_%281947%29.jpg/220px-Lerkendal_Stadion_%281947%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Lerkendal_Stadion_%281947%29.jpg/330px-Lerkendal_Stadion_%281947%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Lerkendal_Stadion_%281947%29.jpg/440px-Lerkendal_Stadion_%281947%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lerkendal_Stadion_(1947).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Construction of Lerkendal started as a public works during the 1930s, as a measure to create jobs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nod_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nod-2">[2]</a></sup> It opened on 10 August 1947, and consisted of a main grass field, two gravel fields, two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Team_handball" title="Team handball">handball</a> courts and two tennis courts. The main field had a spectator capacity for 37,000 on temporary stands, making it the second-largest stadium in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The long sides consisted of temporary wooden stands, with seating on the south stand and standing places on terraces on the north stand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nod_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-nod-2">[2]</a></sup> The end sides of the stadium consisted of grass embankments at a 30 percent angle, allowing for standing space for spectators without having to build stands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Changing rooms were located in a nearby German-built barracks dating from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nod_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-nod-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first major rebuilding of the venue took place ahead of the 1962 season, when the wooden stands were torn and replaced with concrete stands on both long sides. On the south side, a wave-shaped roof was built; originally designed to be self-supporting, the contractor, <a href="/wiki/Reinertsen" title="Reinertsen">Reinertsen</a>, did not trust their own calculations and made a last-minute decision to install support columns. The new stands were taken into use on 3 May 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dunfermline_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-dunfermline-5">[5]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floodlights_(sport)" title="Floodlights (sport)">Floodlighting</a> was installed in 1968 to allow <a href="/wiki/UEFA" title="UEFA">UEFA</a> club tournament matches to be held at the venue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbkweb_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-rbkweb-6">[6]</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/All-weather_running_track" title="All-weather running track">all-weather running track</a> was subsequently also laid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nod_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-nod-2">[2]</a></sup> On 1 December 1988, <a href="/wiki/Lerkendal_Station" title="Lerkendal Station">Lerkendal Station</a> opened, allowing train passengers a short walk to the stadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1994, the first pitch with <a href="/wiki/Desso_GrassMaster" title="Desso GrassMaster">Desso GrassMaster</a> was laid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>By the time Rosenborg had qualified for the <a href="/wiki/1995%E2%80%9396_UEFA_Champions_League" title="1995–96 UEFA Champions League">1995–96 UEFA Champions League</a>, UEFA had for security reasons introduced restrictions which reduced Lerkendal's capacity to 12,200 spectators. In addition, a total ban on standing places would be introduced from 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbkweb_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-rbkweb-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1995, a debate arose between Rosenborg and Trondheim Municipality regarding the construction of a new grandstand, to be located on the north side of the stadium. Rosenborg's <a href="/wiki/Nils_Skutle" title="Nils Skutle">Nils Skutle</a> stated that if it was not built ahead of the <a href="/wiki/1997%E2%80%9398_UEFA_Champions_League" title="1997–98 UEFA Champions League">1997–98 season</a>, Rosenborg would only be allowed to sell 2,800 tickets to their home Champions League matches, and that they instead would be forced to play their games at <a href="/wiki/Ullevaal_Stadion" title="Ullevaal Stadion">Ullevaal Stadion</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>. The new grandstand cost 32 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and opened in 1996 season. The upgrades also included a new floodlight and public announcement system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbkweb_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-rbkweb-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rosenborg started laying plans for further construction, at first looking at construction of a new grandstand on the east end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In May 1998, Lerkendal Eiendom AS, owned 44% by Rosenborg, was established to build three new grandstands,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> including removing the running track to make it a football-only stadium. An alternative was to move and build an all-new stadium at <a href="/wiki/Ranheim" title="Ranheim">Ranheim</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bor_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-bor-12">[12]</a></sup> Following the announcement of the Ranheim plans, Mayor <a href="/wiki/Anne_Kathrine_Slung%C3%A5rd" title="Anne Kathrine Slungård">Anne Kathrine Slungård</a> stated that she did not want to sell Lerkendal, as she wanted public ownership of cultural infrastructure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In April 1999, Trondheim Municipality's chief of administration, <a href="/wiki/Knut_S%C3%A6ther" title="Knut Sæther">Knut Sæther</a>, recommended that the municipality sell Lerkendal to Rosenborg, who would then along with private investors be free to construct the necessary facilities. At the time, the municipality estimated the value of Lerkendal to between NOK 50 and 100 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The football district stated they were in favor of conversion of Lerkendal, and that they feared that if Rosenborg moved Lerkendal would incur high costs on the municipality, giving <a href="/wiki/Gran%C3%A5sen" title="Granåsen">Granåsen</a> as an example of a little used skiing facility which used half the municipal funding for sports venues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bor_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-bor-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 5 October 1999, the club and the municipality signed a <a href="/wiki/Letter_of_intent" title="Letter of intent">letter of intent</a> which indicated that the two would cooperate constructing a new venue along with private investors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The final contract was made with Reinertsen on 20 April 2000, and other involved investors included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fokus_Bank" title="Fokus Bank">Fokus Bank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gjensidige_NOR" title="Gjensidige NOR">Gjensidige NOR</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I._K._Lykke" title="I. K. Lykke">I. K. Lykke</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eiendomspar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eiendomspar (page does not exist)">Eiendomspar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a> and Trondheims Næringsbygg. The club received a 47% share of the stadium company, while the municipality received 14% in exchange for the company taking over the ownership of the land and existing structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> As compensation for losing Lerkendal as an athletics venue, the municipality spent NOK 32 million upgrading <a href="/wiki/%C3%98ya_stadion" title="Øya stadion">Øya stadion</a> as the city's new main athletics stadium. NOK 20 million was financed by Rosenborg, while the remainder was spent using public funds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-malmo_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-malmo-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Construction of the end stands started in January 2001, and they were completed in November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-utbygging_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-utbygging-18">[18]</a></sup> The demolition of the 1962 stand started on 24 October 2001, and the new stand was completed on 30 September 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-utbygging_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-utbygging-18">[18]</a></sup> The cost of constructing the new Lerkendal, including all four stands from 1996 to 2002, was NOK 335 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-malmo_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-malmo-17">[17]</a></sup> of which NOK 253 million was for the 2001 and 2002 work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-utbygging_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-utbygging-18">[18]</a></sup> To give optimal conditions for the turf, the corners were not built out and the south stand was built with a transparent roof. However, the harsh climate forced the turf to be replaced two years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In a 2012 survey carried out by the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Players%27_Association" title="Norwegian Players' Association">Norwegian Players' Association</a> among away-team captains, Lerkendal was found to be the league's second-best stadium, with a score of 4,40 on a scale from one to five.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lerkendal_Stadion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lerkendal_stadion_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1152" data-file-width="2048" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Lerkendal_stadion_2.jpg/220px-Lerkendal_stadion_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Lerkendal_stadion_2.jpg/330px-Lerkendal_stadion_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Lerkendal_stadion_2.jpg/440px-Lerkendal_stadion_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lerkendal_stadion_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The EiendomsMegler1 Stand was built in 2002</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Lerkendal Stadion consists of four, three-tier grandstand without corners. The stadium has a capacity for 21,405 spectators, of which 1,338 are in <a href="/wiki/Club_seating" title="Club seating">club seating</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luxury_boxes" title="Luxury boxes">luxury boxes</a> on the center tier of all four stands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The Hent Stand on the north side of the pitch, built in 1996, is the largest and the only to have red seats. It holds a capacity of 7,457 spectators. Originally named the <a href="/wiki/Adidas" title="Adidas">Adidas</a> Stand, it was renamed before the 2013-season. Opposite lies the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EiendomsMegler1" title="EiendomsMegler1">EiendomsMegler1</a> Stand, which was completed in 2002. In addition to change rooms and an honor stand, it has a capacity for 6,194 spectators. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rema_1000" title="Rema 1000">Rema</a> Stand to the west, opened in 2001, houses the supporter shop, a pizza restaurant and commercial offices. The upper tier has since its opening been used as a family tier. While the lower tier was used by Kjernen, Rosenborg's singing supporters. Since Kjernen in 2009 moved to the upper tier of the opposite <a href="/wiki/Adresseavisen" title="Adresseavisen">Adressa</a> Stand, the whole Rema Stand has been used as a family stand. The <a href="/wiki/Adresseavisen" title="Adresseavisen">Adressa</a> Stand opened at the same time, and both have a <a href="/wiki/Seating_capacity" title="Seating capacity">seating capacity</a> for 3,810 people. The latter hosts office space for Rosenborg Arena and the football district, as well as change rooms for the training pitch. The upper tier is used by Kjernen, Rosenborg's supporter club. The lower tier was earlier used by the away fans. But after Kjernen's move to the upper tier, the away fans was moved to the western end of the Hent stand. The pitch has <a href="/wiki/Desso_GrassMaster" title="Desso GrassMaster">Desso GrassMaster</a>, a natural grass pitch with artificial fibers sown in for increase strength. This includes an automatic watering system and a built-in heating 25 centimeters (9.8 in) below the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The stadium is part of Lerkendal idrettspark, which also consists of three training pitches, two in full size and of which one has <a href="/wiki/Artificial_turf" title="Artificial turf">artificial turf</a>. The main training pitch is the natural grass Skoglunden, while the artificial turf venue is Lerkendal kunstgress. The two pitches both have flood lighting, heating and are often used for matches by Rosenborg's recruit and reserve teams. The team has a <a href="/wiki/Health_club" title="Health club">health club</a> located within the south stand. The club's offices are located in Brakka, a German-built barracks dating from <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The stadium is located at <a href="/wiki/Lerkendal" title="Lerkendal">Lerkendal</a>, 3 kilometers (2 mi) south of the city center. It is located on the southern main road into town, with good bus service. In addition, it is located next to <a href="/wiki/Lerkendal_Station" title="Lerkendal Station">Lerkendal Station</a>, the southern terminus of the <a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%B8ndelag_Commuter_Rail" title="Trøndelag Commuter Rail">Trøndelag Commuter Rail</a>. Parking near the stadium is very limited, and ad-hoc <a href="/wiki/Park_and_ride" title="Park and ride">park and ride</a> are established on matchdays at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stavset&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stavset (page does not exist)">Stavset</a> and <a href="/wiki/City_Syd" title="City Syd">City Syd</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events">Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lerkendal_Stadion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lerkendal_stadion_3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1152" data-file-width="2048" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Lerkendal_stadion_3.jpg/220px-Lerkendal_stadion_3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Lerkendal_stadion_3.jpg/330px-Lerkendal_stadion_3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Lerkendal_stadion_3.jpg/440px-Lerkendal_stadion_3.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lerkendal_stadion_3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Hent Stand was built in 1996 and is the oldest remaining part of the stadium.</div>

<p>The venue was opened with a friendly football match between the Trondheim city team and the Vestfold county team, which Trondheim won 4–3. The opening, attended by 8,000 spectators, also saw several track and field events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The original main football tenants were <a href="/wiki/SK_Freidig" title="SK Freidig">SK Freidig</a> and <a href="/wiki/FK_Kvik" title="FK Kvik">FK Kvik</a>. The latter played in the Main League in <a href="/wiki/1951%E2%80%9352_Norwegian_Main_League" title="1951–52 Norwegian Main League">1951–52</a> and <a href="/wiki/1955%E2%80%9356_Norwegian_Main_League" title="1955–56 Norwegian Main League">1955–56</a>, and otherwise remained in the Second Division throughout most of the 1950s and 60s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> A record 18,000 spectators watched Freidig lose the <a href="/wiki/1948_Norwegian_Football_Cup" title="1948 Norwegian Football Cup">1948 Norwegian Football Cup</a> semifinal against <a href="/wiki/Viking_FK" title="Viking FK">Viking</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> On 18 October 1950, Lerkendal hosted a friendly between Kvik and <a href="/wiki/FC_Spartak_Moscow" title="FC Spartak Moscow">Spartak Moscow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lerkendal hosted its first international on 26 June 1951, when <a href="/wiki/Norway_national_football_team" title="Norway national football team">Norway</a> beat <a href="/wiki/Iceland_national_football_team" title="Iceland national football team">Iceland</a> 3–1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> On 28 August 1953 it hosted a B international game against <a href="/wiki/Finland_national_football_team" title="Finland national football team">Finland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Later national games consist of 5–0 against <a href="/wiki/Malta_national_football_team" title="Malta national football team">Malta</a> on 3 July 1962,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> 2–0 against Finland on 20 August 1964,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> 4–2 against <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg_national_football_team" title="Luxembourg national football team">Luxembourg</a> on 27 May 1965, with the record international audience of 22,319,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> 2–1 against Iceland on 21 July 1969,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> 0–1 against <a href="/wiki/Denmark_national_football_team" title="Denmark national football team">Denmark</a> on 23 September 1973,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> 2–0 against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Germany_national_football_team" title="West Germany national football team">West Germany</a> on 26 September 1979,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> 1–0 against <a href="/wiki/Wales_national_football_team" title="Wales national football team">Wales</a> on 6 June 1984,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> 1–0 against <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria_national_football_team" title="Bulgaria national football team">Bulgaria</a> on 26 May 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> and 1–2 against Denmark on 6 June 1990,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Football_Association_of_Norway" title="Football Association of Norway">Football Association of Norway</a> no longer plays international matches elsewhere than <a href="/wiki/Ullevaal_Stadion" title="Ullevaal Stadion">Ullevaal Stadion</a> because of the conditions in its sponsorship agreements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> On 26 May 1977, the <a href="/wiki/Norway_national_under-21_football_team" title="Norway national under-21 football team">Norway national under-21 football team</a> lost 2–3 against <a href="/wiki/Sweden_national_under-21_football_team" title="Sweden national under-21 football team">Sweden</a> at Lerkendal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rosenborg took Lerkendal into use from the 1957–58 season, when they were promoted to the Regional League. The season ended in relegation and Rosenborg played the following season at their old home ground, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rosenborgbanen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rosenborgbanen (page does not exist)">Rosenborgbanen</a>. With the promotion ahead of the 1959–60 season, Rosenborg returned to Lerkendal and have remained there since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbkweb_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-rbkweb-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1959, Lerkendal was the venue of a neutral quarter final in the cup between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nessegutten" title="Nessegutten">Nessegutten</a> from <a href="/wiki/Levanger" title="Levanger">Levanger</a> and Viking, which attracted 25,043 spectators, mostly from <a href="/wiki/Innherred" title="Innherred">Innherred</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> setting a new stadium record. From the <a href="/wiki/1960%E2%80%9361_Norwegian_Main_League" title="1960–61 Norwegian Main League">1960–61 season</a>, Rosenborg established itself in the Main League.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dunfermline_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-dunfermline-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lerkendal_Nessegutten_Viking.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2335" data-file-width="8909" height="58" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Lerkendal_Nessegutten_Viking.jpg/220px-Lerkendal_Nessegutten_Viking.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Lerkendal_Nessegutten_Viking.jpg/330px-Lerkendal_Nessegutten_Viking.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Lerkendal_Nessegutten_Viking.jpg/440px-Lerkendal_Nessegutten_Viking.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The opening game of the new stands on 3 May 1962 was a Rosenborg friendly against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dunfermline_Athletic" title="Dunfermline Athletic">Dunfermline Athletic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dunfermline_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-dunfermline-5">[5]</a></sup> Freidig moved to Eberg idrettsanlegg when it was completed ahead of the 1965 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> A new stadium spectator record was set in the cup semifinal against <a href="/wiki/SK_Brann" title="SK Brann">Brann</a> in 1967, which was watched by 25,551 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dunfermline_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-dunfermline-5">[5]</a></sup> Rosenborg qualified for the <a href="/wiki/1968%E2%80%9369_European_Cup" title="1968–69 European Cup">1968–69 European Cup</a> and met <a href="/wiki/SK_Rapid_Wien" title="SK Rapid Wien">SK Rapid Wien</a> in the first round.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbkweb_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-rbkweb-6">[6]</a></sup> The game was spectated by 22,492 and remains the record for UEFA tournaments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spectators_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-spectators-44">[44]</a></sup> Per-season average attendance for Rosenborg's league matches lay between five and ten thousand people during the 1970s and early 1980s, with an all-time low of 2,549 in 1978, when Rosenborg played in the Second Division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snitt_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-snitt-45">[45]</a></sup> The venue hosted the Norwegian Athletics Championships four times, in 1963, 1967, 1973 and 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Lerkendal hosted the Women's Cup Final in 1986, where <a href="/wiki/SK_Sprint-Jel%C3%B8y" title="SK Sprint-Jeløy">Sprint-Jeløy</a> beat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trondheims-%C3%98rn" title="Trondheims-Ørn">Trondheims-Ørn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Strindheim_IL" title="Strindheim IL">Strindheim IL</a> played in the top division in <a href="/wiki/1984_Norwegian_First_Division" title="1984 Norwegian First Division">1984</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1995_Norwegian_Premier_League" title="1995 Norwegian Premier League">1995</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> both times playing their home games at Lerkendal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Kvik remained at Lerkendal until the upgrades started in 2000, after which they moved to <a href="/wiki/%C3%98ya_stadion" title="Øya stadion">Øya stadion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bod%C3%B8/Glimt" title="Bodø/Glimt">Bodø/Glimt</a> played their home game in the <a href="/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_UEFA_Cup" title="1999–2000 UEFA Cup">1999–2000 UEFA Cup</a> against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Werder_Bremen" title="Werder Bremen">Werder Bremen</a> at Lerkendal, attracting 1,425 spectators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>The official all-time record at Lerkendal is the final game of the <a href="/wiki/1985_Norwegian_First_Division" title="1985 Norwegian First Division">1985 season</a>, when Rosenborg won the league in the last game against Lillestrøm in a league final which attracted 28,569.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spectators_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-spectators-44">[44]</a></sup> Starting with Rosenborg's success in the late 1980s, attendance rose and lay between eleven and thirteen thousand until 2001. After the opening of the new stadium in 2002, attendance again rose, peaking at an average 19,903 in 2007, although it has fallen somewhat since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snitt_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-snitt-45">[45]</a></sup> The high of most seasons is the 16 May game held every year: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1994_Norwegian_Premier_League" title="1994 Norwegian Premier League">1994</a> edition against <a href="/wiki/Troms%C3%B8_IL" title="Tromsø IL">Tromsø</a> saw 27,661 spectators, and in 1985 as many as 30,000 may have seen the game, as the gates were opened. Since the opening of the current stadium, the attendance record is 22,330, set in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2006_Norwegian_Premier_League" title="2006 Norwegian Premier League">2006</a> edition against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Odd_Grenland" title="Odd Grenland">Odd Grenland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spectators_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-spectators-44">[44]</a></sup> Between 1995 and 2007, Rosenborg played 11 seasons and 37 home matches in <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">UEFA Champions League</a>. The venue has been used for numerous concerts, including <a href="/wiki/A-ha" title="A-ha">A-ha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron_Maiden" title="Iron Maiden">Iron Maiden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kiss_(band)" title="Kiss (band)">Kiss</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future">Future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lerkendal_Stadion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Ahead of the failed Nordic bid to host the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euro_2008" title="Euro 2008">Euro 2008</a>, Lerkendal was, along with Ullevaal Stadion, proposed as Norwegian venues. To reach the required spectator capacity of 30,000 seats, the plans called for the roof of the Adidas stand to be removed to allow for a temporary third tier. Additional seating would be created by adding a single row at the bottom of the four stands, and building capacity in the corners. The proposal would have given a spectator capacity of 30,849, although it would have been reduced after the championship by removing the extra tier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Similar plans were launched for the Norwegian–Swedish bid for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euro_2016" title="Euro 2016">Euro 2016</a>, where the cost of the stadium upgrades was estimated at NOK 800 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> In 2007, the club management stated that they wanted to sell out all matches before they made further expansions to the stadium, independent of the plans to host the Euro. Construction of stands in the corners could give 4,000 additional seats, although these would be the most costly to build and at the same time give the worst view of the pitch. Construction could be done by first building two of the corners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-framtid_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-framtid-58">[58]</a></sup> Since then, Rosenborg has experienced a significant drop in attendance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snitt_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-snitt-45">[45]</a></sup> The failed <a href="/wiki/Trondheim_bid_for_the_2018_Winter_Olympics" title="Trondheim bid for the 2018 Winter Olympics">Trondheim bid for the 2018 Winter Olympics</a>, launched in 2007, called for Lerkendal to be the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Stadium" title="Olympic Stadium">Olympic Stadium</a> and host the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games_ceremony" title="Olympic Games ceremony">opening and closing ceremonies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rosenborg had originally planned to build additional commercial facilities and a tower hotel during the 2002 construction, but this was canceled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-utbygging_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-utbygging-18">[18]</a></sup> At the 2011 annual meeting, the plans were again launched by Skutle, who stated that construction might start as early as 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> In March 2005, Rosenborg announced plans to build a retractable roof over the pitch and lay <a href="/wiki/Artificial_turf" title="Artificial turf">artificial turf</a>. The roof was estimated to cost NOK 100 million, and the club estimated that it could generate NOK 30 million per year in increased revenue from concerts and events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> By 2007, the roof plans were delayed, with management stating that it would be necessary to complete the construction of all stands before a roof was built, and that they believed the stadium would soon be expanded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-framtid_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-framtid-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 19 September 2014, Lerkendal was awarded the right to host the <a href="/wiki/2016_UEFA_Super_Cup" title="2016 UEFA Super Cup">2016 UEFA Super Cup</a>, marking the first time a Norwegian stadium would host a European final.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
