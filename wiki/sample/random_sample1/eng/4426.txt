<p><i><b>Aloysia citrodora</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plant</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Verbena" title="Verbena">verbena</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> Verbenaceae, <a href="/wiki/Native_plant" title="Native plant">native</a> to western South America. Common names include <b>lemon verbena</b> and <b>lemon beebrush</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Armada.26Barra_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Armada.26Barra-2">[2]</a></sup> It was brought to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> in the 17th century and cultivated for its oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roberts2000_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts2000-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aloysia_citrodora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lemon verbena is a <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial</a> <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a> or subshrub growing to 2–3 m high. The 8-cm-long, glossy, pointed leaves are slightly rough to the touch and emit a powerful scent reminiscent of <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemon</a> when bruised (hence the Latin <a href="/wiki/Botanical_name#Binary_name" title="Botanical name">specific epithet</a> <i>citrodora</i>—lemon-scented).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RHSLG_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-RHSLG-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sprays of tiny purple or white flowers appear in late spring or early summer. It is sensitive to cold, losing leaves at temperatures below 0 °C (32 °F), although the wood is hardy to −10 °C (14 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Due to its many culinary uses, it is widely listed and marketed as a plant for the herb garden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aloysia_citrodora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lemon verbena leaves are used to add a lemon flavor to fish and poultry dishes, vegetable marinades, salad dressings, jams, puddings, Greek yogurt and beverages. It also is used to make <a href="/wiki/Herbal_tea" title="Herbal tea">herbal teas</a>, or added to standard <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a> in place of actual lemon (as is common with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moroccan_tea_culture" title="Moroccan tea culture">Moroccan tea</a>). It can also be used to make a <a href="/wiki/Sorbet" title="Sorbet">sorbet</a>. In addition, it has anti-<i><a href="/wiki/Candida_albicans" title="Candida albicans">Candida albicans</a></i> activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In the European Union, Verbena <a href="/wiki/Essential_oil" title="Essential oil">essential oils</a> (<i>Lippia citriodora</i> Kunth.) and derivatives other than absolute are prohibited when used as a fragrance ingredient (Commission Directive 2009/164/EU of 22 December 2009).</p>
<p>Moderate <a href="/wiki/Antioxidant" title="Antioxidant">antioxidant</a> supplementation with lemon verbena extract protects <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutrophil_granulocyte" title="Neutrophil granulocyte">neutrophils</a> against oxidative damage, decreasing the signs of muscular damage in chronic running exercise without blocking the cellular adaptation to exercise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Aloysia citriodora</i> extract shows antioxidant properties that could play an important role in modulating GSH-reductase activity in lymphocytes and erythrocytes and protecting plasma from exercise oxidative damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lemon verbena extract containing 25% <a href="/wiki/Verbascoside" title="Verbascoside">verbascoside</a> showed strong antioxidant capacity, especially in a lipophilic environment, which was higher than expected as concluded from the antioxidant capacity of pure verbascoside, probably due to synergistic effects. The capacity of verbascoside to act as an effective radical scavenger in lipophilic environments was also shown. Verbascoside-enriched extracts might have interesting applications in cosmetic, nutraceuticals or functional food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Funes2009_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Funes2009-10">[10]</a></sup> Although some "in vitro" <a href="/wiki/Genotoxicity" title="Genotoxicity">genotoxicity</a> of verbascoside has been reported on human <a href="/wiki/Lymphocyte" title="Lymphocyte">lymphocytes</a> with an involvement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PARP-1" title="PARP-1">PARP-1</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P53_protein" title="P53 protein">p53 proteins</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> subsequent "in vivo" tests reported no genotoxicity for high dosage oral administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>When making teas, it is said to help with muscle spasms, stomach pain, menstrual cramping, anxiety, and a fever reducer.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aloysia_citrodora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The major isolates in lemon verbena oil are <a href="/wiki/Citral" title="Citral">citral</a> (30–35%), <a href="/wiki/Nerol" title="Nerol">nerol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geraniol" title="Geraniol">geraniol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Extracts of lemon verbena also contain <a href="/wiki/Verbascoside" title="Verbascoside">verbascoside</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synonyms">Synonyms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aloysia_citrodora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Synonyms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Synonyms for lemon verbena are <i><a href="/wiki/Verbena" title="Verbena">Verbena</a> triphylla</i> L'Hér., <i>Verbena citriodora</i> Cav., <i><a href="/wiki/Lippia" title="Lippia">Lippia</a> triphylla</i>, <i>Lippia citriodora</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Aloysia" title="Aloysia">Aloysia</a> citriodora</i> (Cav.) Ort.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Garden_history">Garden history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aloysia_citrodora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Garden history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first European botanist who publicly noticed this plant was the French <a href="/wiki/Philibert_Commerson" title="Philibert Commerson">Philibert Commerson</a>, who collected in Buenos Aires on his botanical circumnavigation with <a href="/wiki/Louis_Antoine_de_Bougainville" title="Louis Antoine de Bougainville">Bougainville</a>, about 1767. The plant had already been quietly imported directly into the <a href="/wiki/Real_Jard%C3%ADn_Bot%C3%A1nico_de_Madrid" title="Real Jardín Botánico de Madrid">Real Jardín Botánico de Madrid</a>, where in 1797 professors <a href="/wiki/Casimiro_G%C3%B3mez_Ortega" title="Casimiro Gómez Ortega">Casimiro Gómez Ortega</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Antonio_Palau_y_Verdera&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Antonio Palau y Verdera (page does not exist)">Antonio Palau y Verdera</a> named it, though they did not yet effectively publish it, <i>Aloysia citrodora</i> in Latin and "Hierba de la Princesa" in Spanish,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> to compliment Maria Louisa of Parma, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princess_of_Asturias" title="Princess of Asturias">Princess of Asturias</a> the wife of the Garden's patron <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV_of_Spain" title="Charles IV of Spain">Infante Carlos de Borbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Asturias" title="Prince of Asturias">Prince of Asturias</a> and son of king Carlos III.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The name was later effectively published in the first volume of Palau's <i>Parte Práctica de Botánica</i> in 1784.</p>
<p>Unofficial importations from Spanish America seldom fared well: when another French botanist <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Dombey" title="Joseph Dombey">Joseph Dombey</a> landed his collections at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cadiz" title="Cadiz">Cadiz</a> in 1785 they were impounded and left to rot in warehouses, while he was refused permission even to have seeds planted. Among the bare handful of plants Dombey had assembled during eight years at Lima, lemon verbena survived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, Gómez Ortega sent seeds and specimens of the plant to <a href="/wiki/Charles_Louis_L%27H%C3%A9ritier_de_Brutelle" title="Charles Louis L'Héritier de Brutelle">Charles Louis L'Héritier de Brutelle</a> in Paris; L'Héritier published it as <i>Verbena triphylla</i> in the second fascicle his <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stirpes_Novae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stirpes Novae (page does not exist)">Stirpes Novae</a></i>. published in December 1785 or January 1786. From Paris <a href="/wiki/John_Sibthorpe" title="John Sibthorpe">John Sibthorpe</a>, professor of Botany at Oxford, obtained the specimen that he introduced to British horticulture: by 1797 lemon verbena was common in greenhouses around London, and its popularity as essential in a fragrant bouquet increased through the following century.</p>
<p>This plant has gained the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Horticultural_Society" title="Royal Horticultural Society">Royal Horticultural Society</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Award_of_Garden_Merit" title="Award of Garden Merit">Award of Garden Merit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
