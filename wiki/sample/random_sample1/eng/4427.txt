<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lemon socialism</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Lemon socialism</b> is a pejorative term for a form of <a href="/wiki/Nationalization" title="Nationalization">government intervention</a> in which government <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subsidies" title="Subsidies">subsidies</a> go to weak or failing firms, often with the intent of preventing further, systemic damage to what might otherwise be considered a <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">free marketplace</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> These subsidies can even take the form of a full or partial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bail-out" title="Bail-out">bail-out</a>, as happened during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2008_financial_crisis" title="2008 financial crisis">2008 financial crisis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The pejorative comes from the perception among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free-market" title="Free-market">free-market</a> economists that failing companies are defective lemons that a working <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">free market</a> would replace with better-functioning companies in response to market demand, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public-sector" title="Public-sector">public-sector</a> involvement this type of state intervention shares with socialism.</p>
<p>Confusingly, lemon socialism may also refer to government efforts to transition from capitalism to actual <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a>; in this case it refers to a deliberate strategy of absorbing the losses entailed in saving jobs within the worst-performing sectors of the economy — the <a href="/wiki/Lemon_(automobile)" title="Lemon (automobile)">lemons</a> — before the nationalization of more profitable industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lemon_socialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mark_J._Green" title="Mark J. Green">Mark J. Green</a> coined the exact phrase in a 1974 article discussing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utility_company" title="Utility company">utility company</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Con_Ed" title="Con Ed">Con Ed</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sentiment was earlier expressed in the adage "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialism_for_the_rich_and_capitalism_for_the_poor" title="Socialism for the rich and capitalism for the poor">Socialism for the rich and capitalism for the poor</a>", which was in use by the 1960s, and the notion of privatizing profits and socializing losses dates at least to 1834 and <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Jackson" title="Andrew Jackson">Andrew Jackson</a>'s closing of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Bank_of_the_United_States" title="Second Bank of the United States">Second Bank of the United States</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_languages">Other languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lemon_socialism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a>, lemon socialism is known as "<a class="extiw" href="https://is.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%B3s%C3%ADalismi_andskotans" title="is:Sósíalismi andskotans">Sósíalismi andskotans</a>", meaning "the devil's socialism", a term coined by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vilmundur_J%C3%B3nsson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vilmundur Jónsson (page does not exist)">Vilmundur Jónsson</a> (1889–1971, Iceland's Surgeon General) in the 1930s to criticize alleged <a href="/wiki/Crony_capitalism" title="Crony capitalism">crony capitalism</a> in <a href="/wiki/Landsbanki" title="Landsbanki">Landsbanki</a>, which term has gained renewed currency in the debate over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2008%E2%80%932012_Icelandic_financial_crisis" title="2008–2012 Icelandic financial crisis">2008–2012 Icelandic financial crisis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Lemon socialism, or more precisely crony capitalism, is also referred to as <a class="extiw" href="https://is.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pilsfaldakap%C3%ADtalismi" title="is:Pilsfaldakapítalismi">Pilsfaldakapítalismi</a>, meaning "skirt capitalism", <i>pilsfaldur</i> being the hemline of the skirt, and the term referring to children hiding behind their mothers' skirts after having done something wrong, to criticize the alleged lack of transparency in dealings and reluctance to deal with bad consequences by themselves.</p>
