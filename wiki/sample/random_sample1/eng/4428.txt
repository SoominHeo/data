<p><b>Lemuel Gulliver</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ʌ/ short 'u' in 'bud'">ʌ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/ər/ 'er' in 'finger'">ər</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is the fictional protagonist and narrator of <i><a href="/wiki/Gulliver%27s_Travels" title="Gulliver's Travels">Gulliver's Travels</a></i>, a novel written by <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Swift" title="Jonathan Swift">Jonathan Swift</a>, first published in 1726.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Gulliver.27s_Travels">In <i>Gulliver's Travels</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lemuel_Gulliver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In Gulliver's Travels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Swift's novel, Gulliver was born in <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a> c. 1661, where his father had a small estate; the Gulliver family is said to have originated in <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a>, however. He supposedly studied for three years (c. 1675-1678) at <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_College,_Cambridge" title="Emmanuel College, Cambridge">Emmanuel College, Cambridge</a>, leaving to become an apprentice to an eminent London surgeon; after four years (c. 1678-1682), he left to study at the <a href="/wiki/Leiden_University" title="Leiden University">University of Leiden</a>, a prominent Dutch university and medical school. He also educated himself in navigation and mathematics, leaving the University around 1685.</p>
<p>Prior to the voyages whose adventures are recounted in the novel, he is described as having traveled less remarkably to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Levant" title="The Levant">the Levant</a> (c. 1685-1688) and later to the <a href="/wiki/East_Indies" title="East Indies">East Indies</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a> (c. 1690-1696). Between his travels he married Miss Mary Burton (c. 1688), daughter of a London hosier. In his education and travels he acquired some knowledge of "<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">High</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Low Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Lingua_Franca" title="Mediterranean Lingua Franca">Lingua Franca</a>"; he later states that he understood some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_Language" title="Greek Language">Greek</a>, and that he "understood (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>) very well".</p>
<p>Gulliver's remarkable travels begin in 1699 and end in 1715, having changed Gulliver's personality to that of a recluse. He claims to have written his memoirs five years following his last return to England, i.e., in 1720 or 1721. The frontispiece to the 1726 edition of <i>Gulliver's Travels</i> shows a fictitious engraving of Gulliver at the age of 58 (i.e., c. 1719). An additional preface, attributed to Gulliver, added to a revised version of the work is given the fictional date of April 2, 1727, at which time Gulliver would have been about 65 or 66 years old. The earliest editions of the book credited Gulliver as the author, whom many at the time believed to be a real person. Swift, an Anglican clergyman, had published much of his work anonymously or pseudonymously.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_sequels_and_spinoffs">In sequels and spinoffs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lemuel_Gulliver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In sequels and spinoffs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In the 2007 comic <i><a href="/wiki/The_League_of_Extraordinary_Gentlemen:_Black_Dossier" title="The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen: Black Dossier">The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen: Black Dossier</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Moore" title="Alan Moore">Alan Moore</a>, Gulliver is the leader of the second incarnation of <a href="/wiki/The_League_of_Extraordinary_Gentlemen" title="The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen">The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen</a> in the 18th century, which then consists of <a href="/wiki/The_Scarlet_Pimpernel" title="The Scarlet Pimpernel">The Scarlet Pimpernel</a> and his wife Lady Blakeney, <a href="/wiki/Fanny_Hill" title="Fanny Hill">Fanny Hill</a> (with whom Gulliver has been romantically involved), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dr_Syn" title="Dr Syn">Dr Syn</a> aka The Scarecrow, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Bumppo" title="Nathaniel Bumppo">Nathaniel Bumppo</a> and Orlando. Gulliver leads the League until his death of <a href="/wiki/Testicular_cancer" title="Testicular cancer">testicular cancer</a> in 1799 and is buried in Lilliput. This version of Gulliver is evidently at least a generation younger than Swift's Gulliver; his dates have evidently been shifted forward in time to allow him to interact with other fictional characters of the later 18th century.</li>
<li>Gulliver is mentioned throughout the recent <i>Malplaquet</i> trilogy of children's novels by Andrew Dalton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Taking much of their initial inspiration from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T.H._White" title="T.H. White">T.H. White</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Mistress_Masham%27s_Repose" title="Mistress Masham's Repose">Mistress Masham's Repose</a></i>, the books describe the adventures of a large colony of Lilliputians living secretly in the enormous and mysterious grounds of an English Country House (<a href="/wiki/Stowe_House" title="Stowe House">Stowe House</a> in <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>). In <i>The Temples of Malplaquet</i>, for example, Jamie Thompson (their human protector, aged 13) has a dream-like vision of the episode in which Gulliver is first captured by the Lilliputians.</li>
<li>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Gulliver%27s_Travels_(2010_film)" title="Gulliver's Travels (2010 film)">Gulliver's Travels</a></i> film released in 2010, Gulliver, played by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Black" title="Jack Black">Jack Black</a>, is a mail room worker who fancies himself a writer but plagiarizes most of his work from the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>. After Gulliver is assigned to write a story about the <a href="/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle" title="Bermuda Triangle">Bermuda Triangle</a>, his ship becomes lost at sea and he finds himself in Lilliput.</li>
</ul>
