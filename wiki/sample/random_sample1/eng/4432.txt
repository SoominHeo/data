<p>A <b>lemming</b> is a small <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodent</a>, usually found in or near the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> <a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">biomes</a>. Lemmings are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subnivean" title="Subnivean">subniveal animals</a> and together with <a href="/wiki/Vole" title="Vole">voles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muskrat" title="Muskrat">muskrats</a>, they make up the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">subfamily</a> <a href="/wiki/Arvicolinae" title="Arvicolinae">Arvicolinae</a> (also known as Microtinae), which forms part of the largest <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammal</a> <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_radiation" title="Evolutionary radiation">radiation</a> by far, the superfamily <a href="/wiki/Muroidea" title="Muroidea">Muroidea</a>, which also includes <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hamster" title="Hamster">hamsters</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gerbil" title="Gerbil">gerbils</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_habitat">Description and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lemming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lemmings weigh from 30 to 110 g (1 to 4 oz) and are about 7 to 15 cm (3 to 6 in) long. They generally have long, soft fur, and very short tails. They are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbivory" title="Herbivory">herbivorous</a>, feeding mostly on leaves and shoots, <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">grasses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cyperaceae" title="Cyperaceae">sedges</a> in particular, but also on roots and bulbs. At times, they will eat grubs and larvae.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Like other rodents, their <a href="/wiki/Incisor" title="Incisor">incisors</a> grow continuously, allowing them to exist on much tougher forage than would otherwise be possible.</p>
<p>Lemmings do not <a href="/wiki/Hibernation" title="Hibernation">hibernate</a> through the harsh northern winter. They remain active, finding food by burrowing through the snow and using grasses clipped and stored in advance. They are solitary animals by nature, meeting only to mate and then going their separate ways, but like all rodents, they have a high reproductive rate and can breed rapidly when food is plentiful.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lemming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The behavior of lemmings is much the same as that of many other rodents<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Category:Articles_contradicting_other_articles" title="Category:Articles contradicting other articles"><span title='""Further'>contradictory</span></a></i>]</sup> which have periodic population booms and then disperse in all directions, seeking the food and shelter their natural habitats cannot provide. The <a href="/wiki/Norway_lemming" title="Norway lemming">Norway lemming</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siberian_Brown_Lemming" title="Siberian Brown Lemming">brown lemming</a> are two of the few vertebrates which reproduce so quickly that their population fluctuations are chaotic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> rather than following linear growth to a carrying capacity or regular oscillations. It is not known why lemming populations fluctuate with such great variance roughly every four years, before numbers drop to near extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Lemming behavior and appearance are markedly different from those of other rodents, which are inconspicuously colored and try to conceal themselves from their predators. Lemmings, by contrast, are conspicuously colored and behave aggressively towards predators and even human observers. The lemming defense system is thought to be based on <a href="/wiki/Aposematism" title="Aposematism">aposematism</a> (warning display).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>For many years, the population of lemmings was believed to change with the <a href="/wiki/Population_cycle" title="Population cycle">population cycle</a>, but now some evidence suggests their predators' populations, particularly those of the <a href="/wiki/Stoat" title="Stoat">stoat</a>, may be more closely involved in changing the lemming population.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Misconceptions">Misconceptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lemming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Misconceptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Misconceptions about lemmings go back many centuries. In the 1530s, the geographer Zeigler of Strasbourg proposed the theory that the creatures fell out of the sky during stormy weather (also featured in the <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inupiat_people" title="Inupiat people">Inupiat</a>/<a href="/wiki/Yupik_peoples" title="Yupik peoples">Yupik</a> at <a href="/wiki/Norton_Sound" title="Norton Sound">Norton Sound</a>), and then died suddenly when the grass grew in spring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This description was contradicted by the natural historian <a href="/wiki/Ole_Worm" title="Ole Worm">Ole Worm</a>, who accepted that lemmings could fall out of the sky, but claimed they had been brought over by the wind rather than created by <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_generation" title="Spontaneous generation">spontaneous generation</a>. Worm first published dissections of a lemming, which showed they are anatomically similar to most other rodents like voles and hamsters, and the work of <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> proved they had a natural origin.</p>
<p>Lemmings have become the subject of a widely popular misconception that they commit <a href="/wiki/Mass_suicide" title="Mass suicide">mass suicide</a> when they migrate, by jumping off cliffs. It is in fact not a mass <a href="/wiki/Animal_suicide" title="Animal suicide">suicide</a> but the result of their migratory behavior. Driven by strong biological urges, some species of lemmings may migrate in large groups when population density becomes too great. Lemmings can swim and may choose to cross a body of water in search of a new habitat. In such cases, many may drown if the body of water is so wide as to stretch their physical capabilities to the limit. This fact, combined with the unexplained fluctuations in the population of Norwegian lemmings, gave rise to the misconception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alaska.gov_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-alaska.gov-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture_and_media">In popular culture and media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lemming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In popular culture and media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The misconception of lemming "mass suicide" is long-standing and has been popularized by a number of factors. It was well enough known to be mentioned in "<a href="/wiki/The_Marching_Morons" title="The Marching Morons">The Marching Morons</a>", a 1951 short story by <a href="/wiki/Cyril_M._Kornbluth" title="Cyril M. Kornbluth">Cyril M. Kornbluth</a>. In 1955, <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">Disney Studio</a> illustrator <a href="/wiki/Carl_Barks" title="Carl Barks">Carl Barks</a> drew an <a href="/wiki/Uncle_Scrooge" title="Uncle Scrooge">Uncle Scrooge</a> adventure comic with the title "The Lemming with the Locket". This comic, which was inspired by a 1953 <i><a href="/wiki/The_American_Mercury" title="The American Mercury">American Mercury</a></i> article, showed massive numbers of lemmings jumping over Norwegian cliffs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Even more influential was the 1958 Disney film <i><a href="/wiki/White_Wilderness_(film)" title="White Wilderness (film)">White Wilderness</a></i>, which won an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Documentary_Feature" title="Academy Award for Documentary Feature">Academy Award for Documentary Feature</a>, in which staged footage was shown with lemmings jumping into certain death after faked scenes of mass migration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Canadian Broadcasting Corporation">Canadian Broadcasting Corporation</a> documentary, <i>Cruel Camera</i>, found the lemmings used for <i>White Wilderness</i> were flown from <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">Hudson Bay</a> to <a href="/wiki/Calgary" title="Calgary">Calgary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, Canada, where they did not jump off the cliff, but were in fact forced off the cliff by the camera crew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Because of the limited number of lemmings at their disposal, which in any case were the wrong sub-species, the migration scenes were simulated using tight camera angles and a large, snow-covered turntable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alaska.gov_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-alaska.gov-6">[6]</a></sup> Lemmings also appear in <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">Arthur C. Clarke</a>'s 1953 short story "The Possessed", where their suicidal urges are attributed to the lingering consciousness of an alien group mind which had inhabited the species in the prehistoric past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>This same myth was also used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_Computer" title="Apple Computer">Apple Computer</a> 1985 Super Bowl commercial "<a href="/wiki/Lemmings_(advertisement)" title="Lemmings (advertisement)">Lemmings</a>" and the popular 1991 <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Lemmings_(video_game)" title="Lemmings (video game)">Lemmings</a></i>, in which the player must stop the lemmings from mindlessly marching over cliffs or into traps. In a 2010 board game by GMT games, <i>Leaping Lemmings</i>, players must maneuver lemmings across a board while avoiding hazards, and successfully launch them off a cliff. The 1994 <a href="/wiki/Short_film" title="Short film">short film</a> "Lemming Aid" portrayed a group of misfits attempting to save lemmings from mass suicide (a <a href="/wiki/Guinea_pig" title="Guinea pig">guinea pig</a> was used as a substitute for a lemming). American cartoonist <a href="/wiki/Stephan_Pastis" title="Stephan Pastis">Stephan Pastis</a>'s popular comic strip <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pearls_Before_Swine_(comic_strip)" title="Pearls Before Swine (comic strip)">Pearls Before Swine</a></i> frequently has strips starring four lemmings in humorous gossip and confessions just before they are about to jump over a cliff. A twist is put on the myth in the 2016 picture book <i>Leaping Lemmings!</i>, when one lemming refuses to jump off cliffs with the others as a lesson in thinking for yourself.</p>
<p>Because of their association with this odd behavior, lemming "suicide" is a frequently used <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphor</a> in reference to people who go along unquestioningly with popular opinion, with potentially dangerous or fatal consequences. This metaphor is seen many times in popular culture, such as in the video game <i>Lemmings</i>, in episodes of <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Dwarf" title="Red Dwarf">Red Dwarf</a></i>, and in <a href="/wiki/Adult_Swim" title="Adult Swim">Adult Swim</a>'s show <i><a href="/wiki/Robot_Chicken" title="Robot Chicken">Robot Chicken</a></i>. In the eighth episode of season 1 of Showtime's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Borgias_(2011_TV_series)" title="The Borgias (2011 TV series)">The Borgias</a></i>, the Pope's second son Juan refers to the college of cardinals as lemmings when they flee the Vatican in anticipation of an impending French invasion. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blink_182" title="Blink 182">Blink 182</a> song <i>Lemmings</i> also uses this metaphor, as does the unrelated song of the same name by English progressive rock band <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Graaf_Generator" title="Van der Graaf Generator">Van der Graaf Generator</a> (from their 1971 album <i><a href="/wiki/Pawn_Hearts" title="Pawn Hearts">Pawn Hearts</a></i>), and the 1973 stage show <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Lampoon%27s_Lemmings" title="National Lampoon's Lemmings">National Lampoon's Lemmings</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/John_Belushi" title="John Belushi">John Belushi</a> and mocking post-<a href="/wiki/Woodstock" title="Woodstock">Woodstock</a> <a href="/wiki/Groupthink" title="Groupthink">groupthink</a>. In the song <i><a href="/wiki/Synchronicity_II" title="Synchronicity II">Synchronicity II</a></i> (from their 1983 album <i><a href="/wiki/Synchronicity" title="Synchronicity">Synchronicity</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/The_Police" title="The Police">The Police</a> compare people in rush hour traffic to lemmings ("packed like lemmings into shiny metal boxes / contestants in a suicidal race").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
