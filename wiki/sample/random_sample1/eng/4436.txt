<p>The <b>President of the Lebanese Republic</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>. The president is elected by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Lebanon" title="Parliament of Lebanon">parliament</a> for a term of six years, which is not immediately renewable. By convention, the president is always a <a href="/wiki/Maronite_Christianity_in_Lebanon" title="Maronite Christianity in Lebanon">Maronite Christian</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=President_of_Lebanon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lebanese_presidential_election,_2014%E2%80%932016" title="Lebanese presidential election, 2014–2016">Lebanese presidential election, 2014–2016</a></div>
<p>From the expiration of the term of President <a href="/wiki/Michel_Suleiman" title="Michel Suleiman">Michel Suleiman</a> in May 2014 until October 31, 2016, the parliament was unable to obtain the majority required to elect a president, and the office was vacant for almost two and a half years, despite more than 30 votes being held. In response to the deadlock, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Youmna_maksoud&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Youmna maksoud (page does not exist)">youmna maksoud</a> suggested amending the constitution to make the presidency popularly elected. Under the proposal, Lebanese Christians would choose two candidates who would run in a national election in which all voters were eligible to participate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> On October 31, 2016, the parliament finally elected <a href="/wiki/Michel_Aoun" title="Michel Aoun">Michel Aoun</a> as President.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Qualifications">Qualifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=President_of_Lebanon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Qualifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The constitution requires the president hold the same qualifications as a member of <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Lebanon" title="Parliament of Lebanon">Parliament</a> (also called the Chamber of Deputies), which are <a href="/wiki/Lebanese_nationality_law" title="Lebanese nationality law">Lebanese citizenship</a> and attainment of the age of twenty-one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tom_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-tom-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though not specifically stated in the constitution, an <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_convention_(political_custom)" title="Constitutional convention (political custom)">understanding</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/National_Pact" title="National Pact">National Pact</a>, agreed in 1943, customarily limits the office to members of the <a href="/wiki/Maronite_Christianity_in_Lebanon" title="Maronite Christianity in Lebanon">Maronite Christian</a> faith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tom_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-tom-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usip_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-usip-4">[4]</a></sup> This is based on a gentlemen's agreement between Lebanon's Maronite Christian President <a href="/wiki/Bechara_El_Khoury" title="Bechara El Khoury">Bechara El Khoury</a> and his Sunni Muslim Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Riad_Al_Solh" title="Riad Al Solh">Riad Al Solh</a>, which was reached in 1943, when Lebanon became independent of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, and described that the President of the Republic was to be a <a href="/wiki/Maronite_Christianity_in_Lebanon" title="Maronite Christianity in Lebanon">Maronite Christian</a>, the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Prime_Ministers_of_Lebanon" title="List of Prime Ministers of Lebanon">Prime Minister</a> a <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam_in_Lebanon" title="Sunni Islam in Lebanon">Sunni Muslim</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Speakers_of_the_Parliament_of_Lebanon" title="List of Speakers of the Parliament of Lebanon">Speaker of Parliament</a> a <a href="/wiki/Shia_Islam_in_Lebanon" title="Shia Islam in Lebanon">Shia Muslim</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usip_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-usip-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Article 50 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Lebanon" title="Constitution of Lebanon">constitution of Lebanon</a> requires the president to take an oath upon assuming office, which is prescribed thus:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icl_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-icl-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">I swear by Almighty God to observe the Constitution and the laws of the Lebanese Nation and to maintain the independence of Lebanon and its territorial integrity.</td>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_and_responsibilities">Role and responsibilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=President_of_Lebanon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Role and responsibilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As described in the constitution, the president is <a href="/wiki/Commander-in-chief" title="Commander-in-chief">commander-in-chief</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lebanese_Armed_Forces" title="Lebanese Armed Forces">Lebanese Armed Forces</a> and security forces; may appoint and dismiss the prime minister and cabinet; promulgates laws passed by Parliament; may also veto bills; and may <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_parliament" title="Dissolution of parliament">dissolve</a> Parliament. In practice, however, Lebanon being a <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_republic" title="Parliamentary republic">parliamentary republic</a>, the president is essentially the repository of <a href="/wiki/Reserve_power" title="Reserve power">reserve powers</a> and the office is largely symbolic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tom_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-tom-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Presidential_palace" title="Presidential palace">presidential palace</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Baabda_Palace" title="Baabda Palace">Baabda Palace</a>, located southeast of <a href="/wiki/Beirut" title="Beirut">Beirut</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><br/></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Election">Election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=President_of_Lebanon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thirty to sixty days before the expiration of a president's term, the speaker of the Chamber of Deputies calls for a special session to elect a new president, which selects a candidate for a six-year term on a secret ballot in which a two-thirds majority is required to elect. If no candidate receives a two-thirds majority, a second ballot is held in which only a majority is required to elect. An individual cannot be reelected president until six years have passed from the expiration of his or her first term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tom_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-tom-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-loc-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quorum_for_an_election">Quorum for an election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=President_of_Lebanon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Quorum for an election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Constitution is silent on the issue of the quorum needed to call to order a parliamentary presidential electoral meeting. In the absence of a clear provision designating the quorum needed to elect the President, the constitution is open to differing interpretations. According to one view on the issue, a quorum constituting a majority of fifty-percent plus one (that required for any meeting of Parliament) is sufficient for a parliamentary presidential electoral meeting. Another view on the issue argues that the quorum is a two-third majority of the total members of Parliament as Article 49 of the constitution requires a two-third voting majority to elect the President in the first round and, if the quorum were half plus one, there would have been no need to require the two-third voting majority when the number of deputies present at the meeting does not exceed the quorum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-loc-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Official_State_car">Official State car</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=President_of_Lebanon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Official State car">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The president's car is a W221 Mercedes-Benz S600Guard armoured limousine and it is escorted by the Republican Guard's SUVs and other security vehicles including the preceding official state car, an armoured W140 S600 now possibly used as a back up limo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
