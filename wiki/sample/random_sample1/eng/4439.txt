<p><b>Lebanon</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Kentucky_cities" title="List of Kentucky cities">home rule-class city</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Marion_County,_Kentucky" title="Marion County, Kentucky">Marion County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The population was 6,331 at the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 census</a>. It is the <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">seat</a> of its county.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR6_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR6-2">[2]</a></sup> Lebanon is located in central Kentucky, southeast of <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Lebanon_National_Cemetery" title="Lebanon National Cemetery">national cemetery</a> is located nearby.</p>
<p>Lebanon is renowned for its Ham Days Festival and Tractor Show which is held during the last weekend of September. In the 1960s and early 1970s, it was known as an entertainment hotspot, as nationally known acts appeared at Club 68 and the Golden Horseshoe nightclubs.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lebanon,_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lebanon is located at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Lebanon%2C_Kentucky&amp;params=37_34_14_N_85_15_23_W_type:city"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">37°34′14″N</span> <span class="longitude">85°15′23″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">37.57056°N 85.25639°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">37.57056; -85.25639</span></span></span></a></span> (37.570623, -85.256263).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR1-3">[3]</a></sup> It is approximately 30 miles (48 km) from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danville,_KY" title="Danville, KY">Danville</a> and 20 miles (32 km) north of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Campbellsville,_KY" title="Campbellsville, KY">Campbellsville</a>. It is located at the junction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_68" title="US 68">US 68</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Route_55" title="Kentucky Route 55">Ky. 55</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Route_52" title="Kentucky Route 52">Ky. 52</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Route_49" title="Kentucky Route 49">Ky. 49</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Route_84" title="Kentucky Route 84">Ky. 84</a> intersects Ky. 49 and 52 just west of town.</p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city has a total area of 4.4 square miles (11.4 km<sup>2</sup>), all land.</p>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-6">[6]</a></sup> of 2000, there were 5,718 people, 2,332 households, and 1,476 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 1,296.6 per square mile (500.6/km<sup>2</sup>). There were 2,555 housing units at an average density of 579.3 per square mile (223.7/km<sup>2</sup>). The racial makeup of the city was 77.88% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 19.92% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 0.12% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 0.73% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.47% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 0.87% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 1.03% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 2,332 households out of which 29.5% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 39.5% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 20.2% had a female householder with no husband present, and 36.7% were non-families. 33.1% of all households were made up of individuals and 16.0% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.31 and the average family size was 2.92.</p>
<p>In the city the population was spread out with 23.7% under the age of 18, 9.5% from 18 to 24, 26.8% from 25 to 44, 21.5% from 45 to 64, and 18.5% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 37 years. For every 100 females there were 85.4 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 80.8 males.</p>
<p>The median income for a household in the city was $21,860, and the median income for a family was $26,552. Males had a median income of $25,889 versus $18,680 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $14,311. About 26.7% of families and 30.3% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 42.8% of those under age 18 and 20.9% of those age 65 or over.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lebanon,_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In historical context, it is important to note that prior to the establishment of the city now known as Lebanon, the nearby town of <a href="/wiki/Georgetown,_Kentucky" title="Georgetown, Kentucky">Georgetown</a> was also named "Lebanon" during its first few years of establishment. It was renamed in 1790 in honor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_U.S._presidents" title="List of U.S. presidents">President</a> <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>.</p>
<p>Present-day Lebanon was established in 1814 and named for the Biblical <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a> because of its abundant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cedar_of_Lebanon#history" title="Cedar of Lebanon">cedar trees</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The founding community traces back to the <b>Hardin's Creek Meeting House</b>, built by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterians" title="Presbyterians">Presbyterians</a> from <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>. It was incorporated as a city on January 28, 1815,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sos_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-sos-8">[8]</a></sup> and became the county seat of Marion County in 1835. Because of its style and beauty, elegant homes, and flourishing businesses, Lebanon had the reputation of being Kentucky's Philadelphia and was considered for the site of the state capitol.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the 19th century, Lebanon was one of the stops along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Turnpike" title="National Turnpike">National Turnpike</a> from <a href="/wiki/Maysville,_Kentucky" title="Maysville, Kentucky">Maysville</a> to <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville</a>. In 1819, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay" title="Henry Clay">Henry Clay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Jackson" title="Andrew Jackson">Andrew Jackson</a> met here after having crossed paths on their journeys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Many of its brick homes date from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antebellum_South" title="Antebellum South">antebellum period</a>, including Hollyhill and Myrtledene Bed and Breakfast. Much of Lebanon's downtown business district was recently placed on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Historic_Register" title="National Historic Register">National Historic Register</a>.</p>
<p>A branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louisville_%26_Nashville_Railroad" title="Louisville &amp; Nashville Railroad">Louisville &amp; Nashville Railroad</a> was built to Lebanon in 1857, but growth of the town was halted by the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>. Three battles were fought nearby, and control over the railroad branch passed between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_(Civil_War)" title="Union (Civil War)">Union</a> and <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederate</a> hands several times. After the death of his brother Tom during a local battle, Confederate <a href="/wiki/John_Hunt_Morgan" title="John Hunt Morgan">John Hunt Morgan</a>'s cavalry burned the railroad depot, a hotel, and several residences on July 5, 1863 during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lebanon" title="Battle of Lebanon">Battle of Lebanon</a>.</p>
<p>Lebanon's Historic Homes and Landmarks Tour is also part of the Kentucky's Civil War Heritage Trail and includes twenty-four listings. On the Civil War Discovery Trail, three landmarks stand out. The Commissary Building, which is the old Sunnyside Dispensary Building, was in place during the Civil War and supplied dry goods and food stuffs to the Union Garrison here. The Shuck building, which is now Henning's Restaurant, was the office of General George H. Thomas, when he gathered an army of several thousand to go to Mill Springs to defend the Cumberland Valley. Myrtledene Bed and Breakfast was where General John Hunt Morgan rode his horse in the house and started up the stairs. General Morgan used the property as his headquarters while he was in Lebanon. On the southern limits of Lebanon is the National Cemetery, where many of the Union Soldiers who fell in the 1862 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Perryville" title="Battle of Perryville">Battle of Perryville</a> were laid to rest. The cemetery is the site of many military funerals and hosts annual Memorial Day celebrations.</p>
<p>The town rebounded after the war and became a trade center, but declined as railroads became less important to commerce in the 1900s. The tracks were abandoned, then eventually removed by <a href="/wiki/CSX_Transportation" title="CSX Transportation">CSX Transportation</a> in the mid-1980s.</p>
<p>In the 1950s, 1960s, and early 1970s, Lebanon was known as an entertainment hotspot, as nationally known acts appeared at The Plantation, Club Cherry, Club 68, and the Golden Horseshoe nightclubs.</p>

<p>Marion County is the home of Maker's Mark Distillery, a small batch bourbon whiskey distillery that is in Loretto, Marion County, Kentucky and owned by Beam Inc. It is sold in distinctively squarish bottles, which are sealed with red wax. The distillery offers tours, and is part of the American Whiskey Trail and the Kentucky Bourbon Trail. The distillery was listed on the National Register of Historic Places on December 31, 1974, and designated a National Historic Landmark on December 16, 1980, listed as "Burks' Distillery". It was the first distillery in America to be so recognized where the landmark buildings were in active use for distilling.</p>
<p>The Loretto Motherhouse is located in <a href="/wiki/Nerinx,_Kentucky" title="Nerinx, Kentucky">Nerinx</a>, Marion County, Kentucky. A working farm and place of extraordinary natural beauty and historical buildings, it is the residence of active and retired members. Belgian priest <a href="/wiki/Charles_Nerinckx" title="Charles Nerinckx">Charles Nerinckx</a> (2 October 1761, Herfelingen – 12 August 1824) and three Kentucky frontier women founded the Sisters of Loretto, the first native American order of Roman Catholic nuns, in 1812. A dozen years later the order moved its headquarters to Saint Stephen's farm, now called Nerinx, where <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Badin" title="Stephen Badin">Stephen Badin</a>, first priest ordained in the United States, had centered the missionary activity which earned for him the nickname "Apostle of Kentucky".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Loretto Community publishes Loretto Magazine, In Brief, a newsletter of the Education Committee, Loretto Earth News, and the Justice and Peace Newsletter.</p>
<p>Penn's Store is the oldest country store in America being run continuously by the same family.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It has been in the Penn family since 1850. Nestled near Marion County's Western border in Boyle County Ky, Penn's Store has become a popular site for visitors seeking living history in an ever-changing, modern world. The store remains today much as it has throughout its past 150+ years. It is not a restored landmark; it is an authentic landmark.</p>
<p>Kentucky Classic ARTS at Centre Square is a Community and Cultural Performing Arts Center located in Lebanon bringing first class in-house and travelling Broadway style musicals, concerts, plays, movies, artists and variety shows to Central Kentucky. Most shows are held in the beautifully renovated Angelic Hall, an intimate 323 seat air-conditioned theater. Kentucky Classic ARTS at Centre Square is home to Kentucky Classic Theatre, Kentucky Classic Orchestra, Centre Saxes, an active arts education program, and Friends of Live Music which hosts a Music Fest featuring local musicians on the outdoor stage each June. Angelic Hall is also home to World Renowned Elvis Tribute Artist, Eddie Miles, who performs there several times each year. Current event listings can be found at www.mykct.com or www.visitlebanonky.com.</p>
<p>Lebanon hosts the Heart Of Kentucky Bluegrass Music Kickoff in January. Special guests along with local groups perform old favorites while other bluegrass musicians are invited to bring their favorite song for an opportunity to come on stage and perform.</p>
<p>Lebanon holds an annual Ham Days Festival featuring a county ham breakfast, craft and food booths, 2 parades, lots of games and contests, free outdoor concerts, a carnival, a Car Show and Tractor Show, plus much more. Ham Days is sponsored by the Marion County Chamber of Commerce and held during the last weekend of September.</p>
