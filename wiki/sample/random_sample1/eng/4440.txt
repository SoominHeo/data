<p><b>Mark Lavon "Levon" Helm</b> (May 26, 1940 – April 19, 2012)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levon_Helm_Dies_at_71_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levon_Helm_Dies_at_71-1">[1]</a></sup> was an American musician and actor who achieved fame as the drummer and regular lead vocalist for <a href="/wiki/The_Band" title="The Band">the Band</a>. Helm was known for his deeply soulful, <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a>-accented voice, <a href="/wiki/Multi-instrumentalist" title="Multi-instrumentalist">multi-instrumental</a> ability, and creative drumming style, highlighted on many of the Band's recordings, such as "<a href="/wiki/The_Weight" title="The Weight">The Weight</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Up_on_Cripple_Creek" title="Up on Cripple Creek">Up on Cripple Creek</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/The_Night_They_Drove_Old_Dixie_Down" title="The Night They Drove Old Dixie Down">The Night They Drove Old Dixie Down</a>".</p>
<p>Helm also had a successful career as a film actor, appearing as <a href="/wiki/Loretta_Lynn" title="Loretta Lynn">Loretta Lynn</a>'s father in <i><a href="/wiki/Coal_Miner%27s_Daughter_(film)" title="Coal Miner's Daughter (film)">Coal Miner's Daughter</a></i>, as <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Yeager" title="Chuck Yeager">Chuck Yeager</a>'s friend and colleague Captain <a href="/wiki/Jack_Ridley_(pilot)" title="Jack Ridley (pilot)">Jack Ridley</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Right_Stuff_(film)" title="The Right Stuff (film)">The Right Stuff</a></i>, and as a Tennessee firearms expert in <i><a href="/wiki/Shooter_(2007_film)" title="Shooter (2007 film)">Shooter</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1998, Helm was diagnosed with throat cancer, which caused him to lose his singing voice. After treatment, his cancer eventually went into remission, and he gradually regained the use of his voice. His 2007 comeback album <i><a href="/wiki/Dirt_Farmer" title="Dirt Farmer">Dirt Farmer</a></i> earned the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Contemporary_Folk_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Contemporary Folk Album">Grammy Award for Best Traditional Folk Album</a> in February 2008, and in November of that year, <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine ranked him No. 91 in its list of 100 Greatest Singers of All Time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2010, <i><a href="/wiki/Electric_Dirt" title="Electric Dirt">Electric Dirt</a></i>, his 2009 follow-up to <i><a href="/wiki/Dirt_Farmer" title="Dirt Farmer">Dirt Farmer</a></i>, won the first <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Americana_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Americana Album">Grammy Award for Best Americana Album</a>, a category inaugurated in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2011, his live album <i><a href="/wiki/Ramble_at_the_Ryman" title="Ramble at the Ryman">Ramble at the Ryman</a></i> won the Grammy in the same category.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Best_Americana_Album_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Best_Americana_Album-4">[4]</a></sup> On April 17, 2012, his wife and daughter announced on Helm's website that he was "in the final stages of his battle with cancer" and thanked fans while requesting prayers. Two days later, Helm died at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memorial_Sloan-Kettering_Cancer_Center" title="Memorial Sloan-Kettering Cancer Center">Memorial Sloan-Kettering Cancer Center</a> in New York City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levon_Helm_Dies_at_71_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Levon_Helm_Dies_at_71-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levon_Helm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levon_Helm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Elaine,_Arkansas" title="Elaine, Arkansas">Elaine, Arkansas</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Helm grew up in <a href="/wiki/Turkey_Scratch,_Arkansas" title="Turkey Scratch, Arkansas">Turkey Scratch</a>, a hamlet west of <a href="/wiki/Helena,_Arkansas" title="Helena, Arkansas">Helena, Arkansas</a>. His parents, Nell and Diamond Helm, cotton farmers and also great lovers of music, encouraged their children to play and sing. Young Lavon (as he was christened) began playing the guitar at the age of eight and also played drums during his formative years. He saw <a href="/wiki/Bill_Monroe" title="Bill Monroe">Bill Monroe and His Blue Grass Boys</a> at the age of six and decided then to become a musician.</p>
<p>Arkansas in the 1940s and 50s stood at the confluence of a variety of musical styles—<a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a>, <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">R&amp;B</a>—that later became known as <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a>. Helm was influenced by all these styles, which he heard on the <i><a href="/wiki/Grand_Ole_Opry" title="Grand Ole Opry">Grand Ole Opry</a></i> on radio station <a href="/wiki/WSM_(AM)" title="WSM (AM)">WSM</a> and R&amp;B on radio station <a href="/wiki/WLAC" title="WLAC">WLAC</a> out of <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville, Tennessee</a>. He also saw traveling shows such as <a href="/wiki/The_Rabbit%27s_Foot_Company" title="The Rabbit's Foot Company">F.S. Walcott's Rabbit's Foot Minstrels</a> that featured top African-American artists of the time.</p>
<p>Another early influence on Helm was the work of the harmonica player, guitarist and singer <a href="/wiki/Sonny_Boy_Williamson_II" title="Sonny Boy Williamson II">Sonny Boy Williamson II</a>, who played <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> and early <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">rhythm and blues</a> on the <i><a href="/wiki/King_Biscuit_Time" title="King Biscuit Time">King Biscuit Time</a></i> radio show on <a href="/wiki/KFFA_(AM)" title="KFFA (AM)">KFFA</a> in Helena and performed regularly in Marvell with blues guitarist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Lockwood,_Jr." title="Robert Lockwood, Jr.">Robert Lockwood, Jr.</a> In his 1993 autobiography, <i><a href="/wiki/This_Wheel%27s_on_Fire:_Levon_Helm_and_the_Story_of_The_Band" title="This Wheel's on Fire: Levon Helm and the Story of The Band">This Wheel's on Fire: Levon Helm and the Story of the Band</a></i>, Helm describes watching Williamson's drummer, James "Peck" Curtis, intently during a live performance in the early 1950s and later imitating this R&amp;B drumming style. Helm established his first band, the Jungle Bush Beaters, while in high school.</p>
<p>Helm also witnessed some of the earliest performances by southern <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country music</a>, blues and <a href="/wiki/Rockabilly" title="Rockabilly">rockabilly</a> artists such as <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conway_Twitty" title="Conway Twitty">Conway Twitty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bo_Diddley" title="Bo Diddley">Bo Diddley</a> and a fellow Arkansan, <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Hawkins" title="Ronnie Hawkins">Ronnie Hawkins</a>. At age 17, Helm began playing in clubs and bars around Helena.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Hawks">The Hawks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levon_Helm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Hawks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While he was still in high school, Helm was invited to join <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Hawkins" title="Ronnie Hawkins">Ronnie Hawkins</a>'s band, the Hawks, a popular bar and club act in the South, which was also successful in Canada, where rockabilly acts were very popular. Helm's mother insisted that he graduate from high school before touring with Hawkins, but he was able to play with the Hawks locally on weekends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> After his graduation in 1958, Helm joined the Hawks as a full-time member, and they moved to <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, Canada, where they signed with <a href="/wiki/Roulette_Records" title="Roulette Records">Roulette Records</a> in 1959 and released several singles, including a few hits.</p>
<p>Helm reported in his autobiography, <i>This Wheel's on Fire</i>, that fellow Hawks band members had difficulty pronouncing "Lavon" correctly and started calling him "Levon" (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">LEE</span></span></b>-von</i></a></span>) because it was easier to pronounce.</p>
<p>In the early 1960s, Helm and Hawkins recruited an all-Canadian lineup of musicians: guitarist <a href="/wiki/Robbie_Robertson" title="Robbie Robertson">Robbie Robertson</a>, bassist <a href="/wiki/Rick_Danko" title="Rick Danko">Rick Danko</a>, pianist <a href="/wiki/Richard_Manuel" title="Richard Manuel">Richard Manuel</a>, and organist <a href="/wiki/Garth_Hudson" title="Garth Hudson">Garth Hudson</a>, all of whom were multi-instrumentalists. In 1963, the band parted ways with Hawkins and started touring as Levon and the Hawks and later as the Canadian Squires, before changing back to the Hawks. They recorded two singles, but remained mostly a popular touring bar band in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, Arkansas, Canada, and on the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a> of the United States, where they found regular summer club gigs on the <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> shore.</p>

<p>By the mid-1960s, songwriter and musician <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a> was interested in performing electric rock music and asked the Hawks to be his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backing_band" title="Backing band">backing band</a>. Disheartened by fans' negative response to Dylan's new sound, Helm returned to Arkansas for what turned out to be a two-year layoff, being replaced by other drummers, including <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Jones" title="Mickey Jones">Mickey Jones</a>. With the completion of Dylan's world tour, which included the other four members of the Hawks, Helm went back to Arkansas—to home, to the "woodshed", as he called it, to consider his options. The eventual result was a return to Woodstock to rejoin his group.</p>
<p>After the Hawks toured Europe with Dylan, they followed him back to the U.S. and settled near <a href="/wiki/Woodstock,_New_York" title="Woodstock, New York">Woodstock, New York</a>, remaining under salary to him. The Hawks recorded a large number of demo and practice tapes in Woodstock, playing almost daily with Dylan, who had completely withdrawn from public life the previous year. These recordings were widely <a href="/wiki/Bootleg_recording" title="Bootleg recording">bootlegged</a> and were partially released officially in 1975 as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Basement_Tapes" title="The Basement Tapes">The Basement Tapes</a></i>. The songs and themes developed during this period played a crucial role in the group's future direction and style. The Hawks members also began writing their own songs. <a href="/wiki/Rick_Danko" title="Rick Danko">Rick Danko</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Manuel" title="Richard Manuel">Richard Manuel</a> also shared writing credits with Dylan on a few songs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Band">The Band</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levon_Helm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Band">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/The_Band" title="The Band">The Band</a></div>
<p>Helm returned to the group, then referred to simply as "the band", as it was known around Woodstock. While contemplating a recording contract, Helm had dubbed the band "the Crackers." However, when Robertson and their new manager <a href="/wiki/Albert_Grossman" title="Albert Grossman">Albert Grossman</a> worked out the contracts, the group's name was given as "the Band." Under these contracts, the Band was contracted to Grossman, who in turn contracted their services to <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a>. This arrangement allowed the Band to release recordings on other labels if the work was done in support of Dylan.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Thus the Band was able to play on Dylan's <i><a href="/wiki/Planet_Waves" title="Planet Waves">Planet Waves</a></i> album and to release <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Waltz" title="The Last Waltz">The Last Waltz</a></i>, both on other labels. The Band also recorded their own album <i><a href="/wiki/Music_from_Big_Pink" title="Music from Big Pink">Music from Big Pink</a></i> (1968), which catapulted them into stardom.</p>

</div>
</div>
<p>On <i>Music from Big Pink</i>, Manuel was the most prominent vocalist and Helm sang backup and harmony, with the exception of "<a href="/wiki/The_Weight" title="The Weight">The Weight</a>." However, as Manuel's health deteriorated and <a href="/wiki/Robbie_Robertson" title="Robbie Robertson">Robbie Robertson</a>'s songwriting increasingly looked to the South for influence and direction, subsequent albums relied more and more on Helm's vocals, alone or in harmony with Danko. Helm was primarily a drummer and vocalist and increasingly sang lead, although, like all his bandmates, he was also a multi-instrumentalist. On occasion, Manuel switched to drums while Helm played <a href="/wiki/Mandolin" title="Mandolin">mandolin</a>, guitar, or bass guitar (while Danko played <a href="/wiki/Fiddle" title="Fiddle">fiddle</a>) on some songs. Helm played the 12-string guitar backdrop to "Daniel and the Sacred Harp".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Helm remained with the Band until their farewell performance on American Thanksgiving Day, November 25, 1976, which was recorded for the documentary film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Waltz" title="The Last Waltz">The Last Waltz</a></i> by director <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">Martin Scorsese</a>. Helm repudiated his involvement with <i>The Last Waltz</i> shortly after the completion of its final scenes. In his autobiography, Helm criticized the film and Robertson, who produced it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solo.2C_acting_and_the_reformed_Band">Solo, acting and the reformed Band</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levon_Helm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Solo, acting and the reformed Band">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Band_2005710037.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3472" data-file-width="2338" height="252" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/The_Band_2005710037.jpg/170px-The_Band_2005710037.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/The_Band_2005710037.jpg/255px-The_Band_2005710037.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/The_Band_2005710037.jpg/340px-The_Band_2005710037.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Band_2005710037.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Helm playing mandolin in 1971</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>With the breakup of the Band in its original form, Helm began working on a solo album <i>Levon Helm and the RCO All-Stars</i>, with <a href="/wiki/Paul_Butterfield" title="Paul Butterfield">Paul Butterfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fred_Carter,_Jr." title="Fred Carter, Jr.">Fred Carter, Jr.</a>, Emmeretta Marks, <a href="/wiki/Howard_Johnson_(jazz_musician)" title="Howard Johnson (jazz musician)">Howard Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Cropper" title="Steve Cropper">Steve Cropper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donald_%22Duck%22_Dunn" title='Donald "Duck" Dunn'>Donald "Duck" Dunn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Booker_T._Jones" title="Booker T. Jones">Booker T. Jones</a> and others, followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Levon_Helm_(1978_album)" title="Levon Helm (1978 album)">Levon Helm</a></i>. He recorded solo albums in 1980 and 1982 entitled <i><a href="/wiki/American_Son_(album)" title="American Son (album)">American Son</a></i> and (once again) <i><a href="/wiki/Levon_Helm_(1982_album)" title="Levon Helm (1982 album)">Levon Helm</a></i>, both produced by <a href="/wiki/Fred_Carter,_Jr." title="Fred Carter, Jr.">Fred Carter, Jr.</a>. He also participated in musician <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kennerley" title="Paul Kennerley">Paul Kennerley</a>'s 1980 <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country music</a> <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Jesse_James" title="The Legend of Jesse James">The Legend of Jesse James</a></i>, singing the role of <a href="/wiki/Jesse_James" title="Jesse James">Jesse James</a>, alongside <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Cash" title="Johnny Cash">Johnny Cash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emmylou_Harris" title="Emmylou Harris">Emmylou Harris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Daniels" title="Charlie Daniels">Charlie Daniels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Lee" title="Albert Lee">Albert Lee</a> and others.</p>
<p>In addition to his work as musician, Helm also acted in several dramatic films after the breakup of the Band. His first acting role was the 1980 film <i><a href="/wiki/Coal_Miner%27s_Daughter_(film)" title="Coal Miner's Daughter (film)">Coal Miner's Daughter</a></i>, in which he portrayed <a href="/wiki/Loretta_Lynn" title="Loretta Lynn">Loretta Lynn</a>'s father, followed three years later when he appeared as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> test pilot and engineer Capt. <a href="/wiki/Jack_Ridley_(pilot)" title="Jack Ridley (pilot)">Jack Ridley</a>, in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Right_Stuff_(film)" title="The Right Stuff (film)">The Right Stuff</a></i>. Helm was also the latter film's narrator. 1987's under-appreciated <a href="/wiki/End_of_the_Line_(1987_film)" title="End of the Line (1987 film)">End of the Line</a> featured Levon as a small-town railroad employee alongside <a href="/wiki/Wilford_Brimley" title="Wilford Brimley">Wilford Brimley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Bacon" title="Kevin Bacon">Kevin Bacon</a>. He played a Kentucky backwoods preacher along with <a href="/wiki/Steven_Seagal" title="Steven Seagal">Steven Seagal</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Fire_Down_Below_(1997_film)" title="Fire Down Below (1997 film)">Fire Down Below</a></i>. He played an eccentric old man in the 2005 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Three_Burials_of_Melquiades_Estrada" title="The Three Burials of Melquiades Estrada">The Three Burials of Melquiades Estrada</a></i> and appeared as Gen. <a href="/wiki/John_Bell_Hood" title="John Bell Hood">John Bell Hood</a> in the 2009 film <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Electric_Mist" title="In the Electric Mist">In the Electric Mist</a></i>. He also had a brief cameo as a weapons expert in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Shooter_(2007_film)" title="Shooter (2007 film)">Shooter</a></i>, with <a href="/wiki/Mark_Wahlberg" title="Mark Wahlberg">Mark Wahlberg</a>.</p>
<p>In 1983, the Band reunited without Robbie Robertson, with <a href="/wiki/Jim_Weider" title="Jim Weider">Jim Weider</a> on guitar. In 1986, while on tour, Manuel committed suicide. Helm, Danko and Hudson continued in the Band, releasing the album <i><a href="/wiki/Jericho_(album)" title="Jericho (album)">Jericho</a></i> in 1993 and <i><a href="/wiki/High_on_the_Hog_(The_Band_album)" title="High on the Hog (The Band album)">High on the Hog</a></i> in 1996. The final album from The Band was the 30th anniversary album, <i><a href="/wiki/Jubilation_(The_Band_album)" title="Jubilation (The Band album)">Jubilation</a></i>, released in 1998.</p>
<p>In 1989, Helm and Danko toured with drummer <a href="/wiki/Ringo_Starr" title="Ringo Starr">Ringo Starr</a> as part of his <a href="/wiki/Ringo_Starr_and_His_All-Starr_Band" title="Ringo Starr and His All-Starr Band">All-Starr Band</a>. Other musicians in the band included singer and guitarist <a href="/wiki/Joe_Walsh" title="Joe Walsh">Joe Walsh</a>, singer and pianist <a href="/wiki/Dr._John" title="Dr. John">Dr. John</a>, guitarist <a href="/wiki/Nils_Lofgren" title="Nils Lofgren">Nils Lofgren</a>, singer <a href="/wiki/Billy_Preston" title="Billy Preston">Billy Preston</a>, saxophonist <a href="/wiki/Clarence_Clemons" title="Clarence Clemons">Clarence Clemons</a> and drummer <a href="/wiki/Jim_Keltner" title="Jim Keltner">Jim Keltner</a>. <a href="/wiki/Garth_Hudson" title="Garth Hudson">Garth Hudson</a> was a guest on <a href="/wiki/Accordion" title="Accordion">accordion</a> on some dates. Helm played drums and harmonica and sang "The Weight" and "Up on Cripple Creek" each night.</p>
<p>In the televised <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1989_Juno_Awards" title="1989 Juno Awards">1989 Juno Awards</a> celebration, the Band was inducted into the Juno Awards' Hall of Fame. Helm was not present at the ceremony, but a taped segment of him saying his thanks was broadcast after the acceptance speeches by Rick Danko and Robbie Robertson. Richard Manuel's children accepted the award on behalf of their father. To conclude the televised special, Rick Danko, Garth Hudson, and Robbie Robertson performed "The Weight" with <a href="/wiki/Blue_Rodeo" title="Blue Rodeo">Blue Rodeo</a>.</p>
<p>Helm performed with Danko and Hudson as the Band in 1990 at <a href="/wiki/Roger_Waters" title="Roger Waters">Roger Waters</a>'s epic <a href="/wiki/The_Wall_%E2%80%93_Live_in_Berlin" title="The Wall – Live in Berlin">The Wall – Live in Berlin</a> Concert in Germany to an estimated 300,000 to half a million people.</p>
<p>In 1993, Helm published an autobiography, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/This_Wheel%27s_on_Fire:_Levon_Helm_and_the_Story_of_The_Band" title="This Wheel's on Fire: Levon Helm and the Story of The Band">This Wheel's on Fire: Levon Helm and the Story of the Band</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Midnight_Ramble">The Midnight Ramble</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levon_Helm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Midnight Ramble">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Helm's performance career in the 2000s revolved mainly around the Midnight Ramble at his home and studio, "The Barn," in <a href="/wiki/Woodstock,_New_York" title="Woodstock, New York">Woodstock, New York</a>. These concerts, featuring Helm and various musical guests, allowed him to raise money for his medical bills and to resume performing after a bout with cancer that nearly ended his career.</p>
<p>In the late 1990s, Helm was diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Head_and_neck_cancer" title="Head and neck cancer">throat cancer</a> after suffering <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hoarseness" title="Hoarseness">hoarseness</a>. Advised to undergo a <a href="/wiki/Laryngectomy" title="Laryngectomy">laryngectomy</a>, he instead underwent an arduous regimen of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_treatment" title="Radiation treatment">radiation treatments</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memorial_Sloan%E2%80%93Kettering_Cancer_Center" title="Memorial Sloan–Kettering Cancer Center">Memorial Sloan–Kettering Cancer Center</a> in New York City. The tumor was then successfully removed, but Helm's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vocal_cords" title="Vocal cords">vocal cords</a> were damaged, and his clear, powerful <a href="/wiki/Tenor" title="Tenor">tenor</a> voice was replaced by a quiet rasp. Initially Helm only played drums and relied on guest vocalists at the Rambles, but eventually his singing voice grew stronger. On January 10, 2004, he sang again at his Ramble sessions. In 2007, during production of <i><a href="/wiki/Dirt_Farmer" title="Dirt Farmer">Dirt Farmer</a></i>, Helm estimated that his singing voice was 80% recovered.</p>
<p>The Levon Helm Band featured his daughter <a href="/wiki/Amy_Helm" title="Amy Helm">Amy Helm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Larry_Campbell_(musician)" title="Larry Campbell (musician)">Larry Campbell</a>, Teresa Williams, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Weider" title="Jim Weider">Jim Weider</a> (the Band's last guitarist), <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Vivino" title="Jimmy Vivino">Jimmy Vivino</a>, Mike Merritt, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Mitchell_(musician)" title="Brian Mitchell (musician)">Brian Mitchell</a>, Erik Lawrence, <a href="/wiki/Steven_Bernstein_(musician)" title="Steven Bernstein (musician)">Steven Bernstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Howard_Johnson_(jazz_musician)" title="Howard Johnson (jazz musician)">Howard Johnson</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tuba" title="Tuba">tuba</a> player in the horn section on the Band's <i>Rock of Ages</i> and <i>The Last Waltz</i> live public at), Jay Collins (Helm's now former son-in-law), Byron Isaacs, and blues harmonica player <a href="/wiki/Little_Sammy_Davis" title="Little Sammy Davis">Little Sammy Davis</a>. Helm hosted Midnight Rambles that were open to the public at his home in Woodstock.</p>

<p>The Midnight Ramble was an outgrowth of an idea Helm explained to Martin Scorsese in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Waltz" title="The Last Waltz">The Last Waltz</a>.</i> Earlier in the 20th century, Helm recounted, traveling <a href="/wiki/Medicine_show" title="Medicine show">medicine shows</a> and music shows such as F.S. Walcott Rabbit's Foot Minstrels, featuring African-American blues singers and dancers, would put on titillating performances in rural areas. (This was also turned into a song by the Band, "The W.S. Walcott Medicine Show," with the name altered so the lyric was easier to sing.)</p>
<p>"After the finale, they'd have the midnight ramble," Helm told Scorsese. With young children off the premises, the show resumed: "The songs would get a little bit juicier. The jokes would get a little funnier and the prettiest dancer would really get down and shake it a few times. A lot of the rock and roll <a href="/wiki/Duckwalk" title="Duckwalk">duck walks</a> and moves came from that."</p>

Costello</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emmylou_Harris" title="Emmylou Harris">Emmylou Harris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dr._John" title="Dr. John">Dr. John</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mavis_Staples" title="Mavis href="/wiki/Donald_Fagen" title="Donald Fagen">Donald Fagen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jon_Herington" title="Jon Herington">Jon Herington</a> of <a href="/wiki/Steely_Dan" title="Steely Dan">Steely Dan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Vivino" title="Jimmy Vivino">Jimmy Vivino</a> (of the house band on "<a href="/wiki/Late_Night_with_Conan_O%27Brien" title="Late Night with Conan O'Brien">Late Night with Conan O'Brien</a>"), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Max_Weinberg_7" title="Max Weinberg 7">Max Weinberg 7</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sean_Costello" title="Sean Costello">Sean Costello</a>, the Muddy Waters Tribute Band, <a href="/wiki/Pinetop_Perkins" title="Pinetop Perkins">Pinetop Perkins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hubert_Sumlin" title="Hubert Sumlin">Hubert Sumlin</a>, <a Earle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bow_Thayer" title="Bow Thayer">Bow Thayer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luther_Johnson_(Guitar_Junior)" title="Luther Johnson (Guitar Junior)">Luther "Guitar Junior" Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rickie_Lee_Jones" title="Rickie Lee Jones">Rickie Lee Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kate_Taylor" title="Kate Taylor">Kate Taylor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ollabelle" title="Ollabelle">Ollabelle</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holmes_Brothers" title="Holmes Brothers">Holmes Brothers</a>, Catherine Russell, <a href="/wiki/Norah_Jones" title="Norah Jones">Norah Jones</a>, <a <p>During this period, Helm switched to the <a href="/wiki/Matched_grip" title="Matched grip">matched grip</a> and adopted a less busy, greatly simplified drumming style, as opposed to the <a href="/wiki/Traditional_grip" title="Traditional grip">traditional grip</a> he used during his years with the Band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Helm was busy touring every year during the 2000s, generally traveling by tour bus to venues in eastern Canada and the eastern United States. After 2007, he performed in large venues such the <a href="/wiki/Beacon_Theatre_(New_York_City)" title="Beacon Theatre (New York City)">Beacon Theater</a> in New York. <a href="/wiki/Dr._John" title="Dr. John">Dr. John</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warren_Haynes" title="Warren Haynes">Warren Haynes</a> (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allman_Brothers_Band" title="Allman Brothers Band">Allman Brothers Band</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gov%27t_Mule" title="Gov't Mule">Gov't Mule</a>) and Garth Hudson played at the concerts along with several other guests. At a show in <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>, Canada, <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Costello" title="Elvis Costello">Elvis Costello</a> joined to sing "Tears of Rage." The <a href="/wiki/Alexis_P._Suter" title="Alexis P. Suter">Alexis P. Suter</a> Band was a frequent opening act. Helm was a favorite of radio personality <a href="/wiki/Don_Imus" title="Don Imus">Don Imus</a> and was frequently featured on <i><a href="/wiki/Imus_in_the_Morning" title="Imus in the Morning">Imus in the Morning</a>.</i> In the summer of 2009, it was reported that a reality television series centering on the Midnight Ramble was in development.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dirt_Farmer_and_comeback"><i>Dirt Farmer</i> and comeback</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levon_Helm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dirt Farmer and comeback">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The fall of 2007 saw the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Dirt_Farmer" title="Dirt Farmer">Dirt Farmer</a></i>, Helm's first studio solo album since 1982. Dedicated to his parents and co-produced by his daughter Amy, the album combines traditional tunes Levon recalled from his youth with newer songs (by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Earle" title="Steve Earle">Steve Earle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kennerley" title="Paul Kennerley">Paul Kennerley</a> and others) which flow from similar historical streams. The album was released to almost immediate critical acclaim, and earned him a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> in the Traditional Folk Album category for 2007.</p>
<p>Helm declined to attend the Grammy Awards ceremony, instead holding a "Midnight Gramble" and celebrating the birth of his grandson, Lavon (Lee) Henry Collins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, Helm performed at <a href="/wiki/Warren_Haynes" title="Warren Haynes">Warren Haynes</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mountain_Jam_(festival)" title="Mountain Jam (festival)">Mountain Jam</a> Music Festival in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hunter_(town),_New_York" title="Hunter (town), New York">Hunter, New York</a>, playing alongside Haynes on the last day of the three-day festival. Helm also joined guitarist <a href="/wiki/Bob_Weir" title="Bob Weir">Bob Weir</a> and his band <a href="/wiki/RatDog" title="RatDog">RatDog</a> on stage as they closed out the festival. Helm performed to great acclaim at the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Bonnaroo_Music_Festival" title="Bonnaroo Music Festival">Bonnaroo Music Festival</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manchester,_Tennessee" title="Manchester, Tennessee">Manchester, Tennessee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Helm drummed on a couple of tracks for <a href="/wiki/Jorma_Kaukonen" title="Jorma Kaukonen">Jorma Kaukonen</a>'s February 2009 album <i><a href="/wiki/River_of_Time_(Jorma_Kaukonen_album)" title="River of Time (Jorma Kaukonen album)">River of Time</a></i>, recorded at the Levon Helm studio.</p>
<p>Helm released the album <i><a href="/wiki/Electric_Dirt" title="Electric Dirt">Electric Dirt</a></i> on his own label on June 30, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The album won a best album Grammy for the newly created Americana category in 2010. Helm performed on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CBS_Television" title="CBS Television">CBS television</a> program <a href="/wiki/Late_Show_with_David_Letterman" title="Late Show with David Letterman">Late Show with David Letterman</a> on July 9, 2009. He also toured that same year in a supporting role with the band <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Crowes" title="Black Crowes">Black Crowes</a>.</p>
<p>A documentary on Helm's day-to-day life, entitled <a class="external text" href="http://www.imdb.com/title/tt1362518/" rel="nofollow"><i>Ain't in It for My Health: A Film About Levon Helm</i></a> was released in March 2010. Directed by Jacob Hatley, it made its debut at the <a href="/wiki/South_by_Southwest" title="South by Southwest">South by Southwest</a> film festival in <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin, Texas</a> and went on to be screened at the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Film_Festival" title="Los Angeles Film Festival">Los Angeles Film Festival</a> in June 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The film had a limited release in select theaters in the United States in the spring of 2013 and was released on DVD and Blu-ray later that year.</p>
<p>On May 11, 2011, Helm released <i><a href="/wiki/Ramble_at_the_Ryman" title="Ramble at the Ryman">Ramble at the Ryman</a></i>, a live album recorded during his performance of September 17, 2008, at the <a href="/wiki/Ryman_Auditorium" title="Ryman Auditorium">Ryman Auditorium</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville, Tennessee</a>. The album features Helm's band playing six songs by the <a href="/wiki/The_Band" title="The Band">Band</a> and other cover material, including some songs from previous Helm solo releases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The album won the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Americana_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Americana Album">Grammy Award for Best Americana Album</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Best_Americana_Album_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Best_Americana_Album-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Illness_and_death">Illness and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levon_Helm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Illness and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 2012, during the <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">Rock and Roll Hall of Fame</a> induction ceremonies in Cleveland, Robbie Robertson sent "love and prayers" to Helm, fueling speculation about Helm's health. Helm had previously cancelled several performances, citing poor health or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slipped_disk" title="Slipped disk">slipped disk</a> in his back;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> his final concert was held in Tarrytown, New York at <a href="/wiki/Tarrytown_Music_Hall" title="Tarrytown Music Hall">Tarrytown Music Hall</a> on March 24.</p>
<p>On April 17, 2012, Helm's wife Sandy and daughter Amy revealed that he had end-stage cancer. They posted the following message on Helm's website:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"Dear Friends,</dd>
<dd>Levon is in the final stages of his battle with cancer. Please send your prayers and love to him as he makes his way through this part of his journey.</dd>
<dd>Thank you fans and music lovers who have made his life so filled with joy and celebration... he has loved nothing more than to play, to fill the room up with music, lay down the back beat, and make the people dance! He did it every time he took the stage...</dd>
<dd>We appreciate all the love and support and concern.</dd>
<dd>From his daughter Amy, and wife Sandy"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>On April 18, Robertson revealed on his Facebook page that he had a long visit with Helm at the hospital the previous Sunday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> On the same day, Garth Hudson posted on his personal website that he was "too sad for words". He then left a link for a video of himself and the <a href="/wiki/Alexis_P._Suter" title="Alexis P. Suter">Alexis P. Suter</a> Band performing Bob Dylan's song "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knocking_on_Heaven%27s_Door" title="Knocking on Heaven's Door">Knocking on Heaven's Door</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Surrounded by family and friends, Helm died on April 19, 2012 at 1:30 p.m. EST (17:30 UTC) in New York City's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memorial_Sloan-Kettering_Cancer_Center" title="Memorial Sloan-Kettering Cancer Center">Memorial Sloan-Kettering Cancer Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fans were invited to a public wake at Helm's Barn studio complex on April 26. Approximately 2,000 fans came to pay their respects to the rock icon. The following day, after a private funeral service and a procession through the streets of Woodstock, Helm was interred in the Woodstock Cemetery, within sight of the grave of his longtime bandmate and friend <a href="/wiki/Rick_Danko" title="Rick Danko">Rick Danko</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levon_Helm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/George_Harrison" title="George Harrison">George Harrison</a> said that while writing his 1970 song "<a href="/wiki/All_Things_Must_Pass_(song)" title="All Things Must Pass (song)">All Things Must Pass</a>", he imagined Levon Helm singing it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a>'s lyricist, <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Taupin" title="Bernie Taupin">Bernie Taupin</a>, named the song "<a href="/wiki/Levon_(song)" title="Levon (song)">Levon</a>" after Helm, although the song is not actually about him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elton-john-song_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-elton-john-song-26">[26]</a></sup> Both John and Taupin cited that they were inspired by Helm, Taupin saying in various interviews that they would "go down to their favourite record stores to buy the Band's records" along with Elton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1994, Helm was <a href="/wiki/List_of_Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame_inductees" title="List of Rock and Roll Hall of Fame inductees">inducted</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">Rock and Roll Hall of Fame</a> as a member of the <a href="/wiki/The_Band" title="The Band">Band</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marc_Cohn" title="Marc Cohn">Marc Cohn</a> wrote the song "<a href="/wiki/Join_the_Parade" title="Join the Parade">Listening to Levon</a>" in 2007.</p>
<p>"The Man behind the Drums," written by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Earl_Keen" title="Robert Earl Keen">Robert Earl Keen</a> and Bill Whitbeck, appeared on Keen's 2009 album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rose_Hotel" title="The Rose Hotel">The Rose Hotel</a>.</i></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tracy_K._Smith" title="Tracy K. Smith">Tracy K. Smith</a>'s 2011 poem "Alternate Take", included in her Pulitzer Prize–winning collection <i>Life on Mars</i>, is dedicated to Helm.</p>
<p>On the day of Helm's death, April 19, 2012, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tom_Petty_%26_the_Heartbreakers" title="Tom Petty &amp; the Heartbreakers">Tom Petty &amp; the Heartbreakers</a>, in a concert at the First Bank Center in Broomfield, Colorado, paid tribute to Levon by performing their song "The Best of Everything" and dedicating it to him.</p>
<p>At a concert on May 2, 2012, at the <a href="/wiki/Prudential_Center" title="Prudential Center">Prudential Center</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newark,_NJ" title="Newark, NJ">Newark, NJ</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bruce_Springsteen_and_the_E_Street_Band" title="Bruce Springsteen and the E Street Band">Bruce Springsteen and the E Street Band</a> performed "The Weight" as a tribute to Helm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bruce-springsteen-levon_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-bruce-springsteen-levon-28">[28]</a></sup> Springsteen called Helm "one of the greatest, greatest voices in country, rockabilly and rock 'n' roll ... staggering ... while playing the drums. Both his voice and his drumming were so incredibly personal. He had a feel on the drums that comes out of certain place in the past and you can't replicate it." Springsteen also said it was one of the songs that he had played with drummer <a href="/wiki/Max_Weinberg" title="Max Weinberg">Max Weinberg</a> in Weinberg's audition with for the band.</p>
<p>On June 2, 2012, at Mountain Jam, Gov't Mule, along with the Levon Helm Band (with Lukas Nelson coming on stage for the closing song) played a tribute set, including "The Night They Drove Old Dixie Down," "Up on Cripple Creek,""It Makes No Difference," and closing with "The Weight."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>A tribute concert called <a href="/wiki/Love_for_Levon" title="Love for Levon">Love for Levon</a> took place at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Izod_Center" title="Izod Center">Izod Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Rutherford,_New_Jersey" title="East Rutherford, New Jersey">East Rutherford, New Jersey</a>, on October 3, 2012. The concert featured many special guests who had collaborated with and were inspired by Helm and the Band, including Roger Waters, Garth Hudson, Joe Walsh, <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Allman" title="Gregg Allman">Gregg Allman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Hornsby" title="Bruce Hornsby">Bruce Hornsby</a>, Jorma Kaukonen, <a href="/wiki/John_Mayer" title="John Mayer">John Mayer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mavis_Staples" title="Mavis Staples">Mavis Staples</a>, <a href="/wiki/My_Morning_Jacket" title="My Morning Jacket">My Morning Jacket</a>, Marc Cohn, <a href="/wiki/John_Hiatt" title="John Hiatt">John Hiatt</a>, Allen Toussaint, <a href="/wiki/Jakob_Dylan" title="Jakob Dylan">Jakob Dylan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Gordon" title="Mike Gordon">Mike Gordon</a> and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Proceeds from the concert were to "help support the lasting legacy of Levon Helm by helping his estate keep ownership of his home, barn and studio, and to continue the Midnight Ramble Sessions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the 2013 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammy_Awards" title="Grammy Awards">Grammy Awards</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Zac_Brown_Band" title="Zac Brown Band">Zac Brown Band</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mumford_%26_Sons" title="Mumford &amp; Sons">Mumford &amp; Sons</a>, Elton John, Mavis Staples, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-Bone_Burnett" title="T-Bone Burnett">T-Bone Burnett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alabama_Shakes" title="Alabama Shakes">Alabama Shakes</a> singer Brittany Howard performed "The Weight" as a tribute to Levon and other recently deceased musicians. They also dedicated the song to the victims of the <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Hook_Elementary_School_shooting" title="Sandy Hook Elementary School shooting">Sandy Hook Elementary School shooting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2013, the New York State Legislature approved a resolution to name State Route 375—the road which connects State Route 28 with the town of Woodstock—"Levon Helm Memorial Boulevard". Governor Andrew Cuomo signed the bill on June 20, 2013.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levon_Helm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Helm met singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Libby_Titus" title="Libby Titus">Libby Titus</a> in April 1969, while the Band was recording its second album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> They began a lengthy relationship, which produced daughter <a href="/wiki/Amy_Helm" title="Amy Helm">Amy Helm</a> (born December 3, 1970).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Amy formed the band <a href="/wiki/Ollabelle" title="Ollabelle">Ollabelle</a> and performed with her father's band at the Midnight Rambles and other concerts.</p>
<p>Helm met his future wife, Sandra Dodd, in 1975 in California, while he was still involved with Titus. Helm and Dodd were married on September 7, 1981. They had no children together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
