<p><i><b>Rhesus</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ῥῆσος</span>, <i>Rhēsos</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Classical_Athens" title="Classical Athens">Athenian</a> <a href="/wiki/Tragedy" title="Tragedy">tragedy</a> that belongs to the transmitted plays of <a href="/wiki/Euripides" title="Euripides">Euripides</a>. Its authorship has been disputed since antiquity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and the issue has invested modern scholarship since the 17th century when the play's authenticity was challenged, first by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Scaliger" title="Joseph Scaliger">Joseph Scaliger</a> and subsequently by others, partly on aesthetic grounds and partly on peculiarities in the play's vocabulary, style and technique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WRvii_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-WRvii-2">[2]</a></sup> The conventional attribution to Euripides remains controversial.</p>
<p><i>Rhesus</i> takes place during the <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">Trojan War</a>, on the night when <a href="/wiki/Odysseus" title="Odysseus">Odysseus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diomedes" title="Diomedes">Diomedes</a> sneak into the Trojan camp. The same event is narrated in book 10 of <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic poem</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_synopsis">Plot synopsis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhesus_(play)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot synopsis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the middle of the night Trojan guards on the lookout for suspicious enemy activity sight bright fires in the Greek camp. They promptly inform <a href="/wiki/Hector" title="Hector">Hector</a>, who almost issues a general call to arms before <a href="/wiki/Aeneas" title="Aeneas">Aeneas</a> makes him see how ill-advised this would be. Their best bet, <a href="/wiki/Aeneas" title="Aeneas">Aeneas</a> argues, would be to send someone to spy on the Greek camp and see what the enemy is up to. <a href="/wiki/Dolon_(mythology)" title="Dolon (mythology)">Dolon</a> volunteers to spy on the Greeks in exchange for <a href="/wiki/Achilles" title="Achilles">Achilles</a>'s horses when the war is won. <a href="/wiki/Hector" title="Hector">Hector</a> accepts the deal and sends him out. Dolon leaves wearing the skin of a wolf, and plans on deceiving the Greeks by walking on all fours. <a href="/wiki/Rhesus_of_Thrace" title="Rhesus of Thrace">Rhesus</a>, the neighboring king of <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a>, arrives to assist the Trojans soon after Dolon sets out. Hector berates him for coming so many years late, but decides better late than never. Rhesus says he intended on coming in the beginning, but was sidetracked defending his own land from an attack by <a href="/wiki/Scythians" title="Scythians">Scythians</a>.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, on their way into the Trojan encampment, Odysseus and Diomedes run into Dolon and kill him. When they reach the encampment with the intention of killing <a href="/wiki/Hector" title="Hector">Hector</a>, <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a> guides them to Rhesus' sleeping quarters instead, pointing out that they are not destined to kill Hector. Diomedes slays Rhesus and others while Odysseus takes his prized horses before making their escape. Rumors spread from Rhesus' men that it was an inside job, and that Hector was responsible. Hector arrives to cast blame on the sentinels for, due to the sly tactics, the guilty party could only be Odysseus. The mother of Rhesus, one of the nine <a href="/wiki/Muse" title="Muse">muses</a>, then arrives and lays blame on all those responsible: Odysseus, Diomedes, and Athena. She also announces the imminent resurrection of Rhesus, who will become immortal but will be sent to live in an underground cave.</p>
<p>This short play is most notable in comparison with the Iliad. The part with Dolon is pushed to the background, and much more is revealed about Rhesus and the reactions of the Trojans to his murder.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhesus_(play)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first to fully dispute that the <i>Rhesus</i> was a play by Euripides was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L._C._Valckenaer" title="L. C. Valckenaer">L. C. Valckenaer</a> in his <i>Phoenissae</i> (1755) and <i>Diatribe in Euripidis deperditorum dramatum reliquias</i> (1767).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In an introduction to <i>Rhesus</i>, classical scholar <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Murray" title="Gilbert Murray">Gilbert Murray</a> wrote that passages from the play were quoted by early Alexandrian writers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The ancient hypotheses transmitted along with the play, however, show that its authenticity was attacked by a number of scholars whose names are not given.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WRvii_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-WRvii-2">[2]</a></sup> Cases against Euripides' authorship generally center on stylistic differences. Murray argued that this may be attributable simply to a younger or less-developed Euripides. Murray also raised the possibility of its being a reproduction of a Euripides play, perhaps undergone by a contemporary or by Euripides' son. Professor <a href="/wiki/Edith_Hall" title="Edith Hall">Edith Hall</a> argued in an introduction that modern readers "will be struck in particular by the un-Euripidean lack of interest in women," and noted Euripides' son having borne the tragedian's name, as an argument against the conventional attribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1964, William Ritchie defended the play's authenticity in a book-length study. His conclusions were opposed, however, by <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Fraenkel" title="Eduard Fraenkel">Eduard Fraenkel</a>. Before Ritchie, <a href="/wiki/Richmond_Lattimore" title="Richmond Lattimore">Richmond Lattimore</a> had also stated that the <i>Rhesus</i> had been written by Euripides, probably at some point before 440 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latt_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-latt-6">[6]</a></sup> Professor and translator Michael Walton has claimed that modern scholarship agrees with the classical authorities in ascribing the play to Euripides,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-w97_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-w97-7">[7]</a></sup> but admitted in a successive work that the attribution is still disputed by a number of scholars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
