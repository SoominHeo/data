<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lecithin</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p><b>Lecithin</b> (from the Greek <i>lekithos</i>, "egg yolk") is a generic term to designate any group of yellow-brownish fatty substances occurring in animal and plant tissues, which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphiphilic" title="Amphiphilic">amphiphilic</a> - they attract both water and fatty substances (and so are both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophilic" title="Hydrophilic">hydrophilic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipophilic" title="Lipophilic">lipophilic</a>), and are used for smoothing food textures, dissolving powders (emulsifying), homogenizing liquid mixtures, and repelling sticking materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lecithins are usually <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">phospholipids</a>, composed of <a href="/wiki/Phosphoric_acid" title="Phosphoric acid">phosphoric acid</a> with <a href="/wiki/Choline" title="Choline">choline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">glycerol</a> or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatty_acids" title="Fatty acids">fatty acids</a> usually <a href="/wiki/Glycolipid" title="Glycolipid">glycolipids</a> or <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">triglyceride</a>. <a href="/wiki/Glycerophospholipid" title="Glycerophospholipid">Glycerophospholipids</a> in lecithin include <a href="/wiki/Phosphatidylcholine" title="Phosphatidylcholine">phosphatidylcholine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphatidylethanolamine" title="Phosphatidylethanolamine">phosphatidylethanolamine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphatidylinositol" title="Phosphatidylinositol">phosphatidylinositol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphatidylserine" title="Phosphatidylserine">phosphatidylserine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phosphatidic_acid" title="Phosphatidic acid">phosphatidic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lecithin was first isolated in 1845 by the French chemist and pharmacist <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Nicolas_Gobley" title="Theodore Nicolas Gobley">Theodore Gobley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1850, he named the phosphatidylcholine <i>lécithine</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Gobley originally isolated lecithin from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_yolk" title="Egg yolk">egg yolk</a>—λέκιθος <i>lekithos</i> is "egg yolk" in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>—and established the complete chemical formula of phosphatidylcholine in 1874;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> in between, he had demonstrated the presence of lecithin in a variety of biological matters, including venous blood, bile, human brain tissue, fish eggs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_roe" title="Fish roe">fish roe</a>, and chicken and sheep brain.</p>
<p>Lecithin can easily be extracted chemically using any non-polar solvent such as <a href="/wiki/Hexane" title="Hexane">hexane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_ether" title="Petroleum ether">petroleum ether</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>, etc., or extraction can be done mechanically. It is usually available from sources such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soy" title="Soy">soybeans</a>, eggs, milk, marine sources, <a href="/wiki/Rapeseed" title="Rapeseed">rapeseed</a>, cottonseed, and sunflower. It has low solubility in water, but is an excellent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emulsifier" title="Emulsifier">emulsifier</a>. In aqueous solution, its phospholipids can form either <a href="/wiki/Liposome" title="Liposome">liposomes</a>, bilayer sheets, <a href="/wiki/Micelle" title="Micelle">micelles</a>, or lamellar structures, depending on hydration and temperature. This results in a type of <a href="/wiki/Surfactant" title="Surfactant">surfactant</a> that usually is classified as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphipathic" title="Amphipathic">amphipathic</a>. Lecithin is sold as a food additive and dietary supplement. In cooking, it is sometimes used as an emulsifier and to prevent sticking, for example in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-stick_cooking_spray" title="Non-stick cooking spray">nonstick cooking spray</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lecithin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lecithin, as a food additive, is also a dietary source of several active compounds: <a href="/wiki/Choline" title="Choline">Choline</a> and its metabolites are needed for several physiological purposes, including cell membrane signaling and cholinergic neurotransmission,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fischer_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fischer-7">[7]</a></sup> although its exact function has not been determined, and the involvement of choline in long-term health and development of <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">clinical</a> disorders, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_diseases" title="Cardiovascular diseases">cardiovascular diseases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">cognitive decline</a> in aging and regulation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_lipid" title="Blood lipid">blood lipid</a> levels, has not been well defined, and remains under research in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>While lecithin is also a rich source of a variety of types of dietary fats, the small amounts of lecithin typically used for food additive purposes mean it is not a significant source of fats. Lecithin is a source for methyl groups via its <a href="/wiki/Metabolite" title="Metabolite">metabolite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trimethylglycine" title="Trimethylglycine">trimethylglycine</a> (betaine) although this is mostly consumed from plants (and is abundant in sugar beets for example).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Phosphatidylcholine" title="Phosphatidylcholine">Phosphatidylcholine</a> occurs in all cellular organisms, being one of the major components of the phospholipid portion of the cell membrane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lecithin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Commercial lecithin, as used by food manufacturers, is a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">phospholipids</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vegetable_fats_and_oils" title="Vegetable fats and oils">oil</a>. The lecithin can be obtained by water degumming the extracted oil of seeds. It is a mixture of various phospholipids, and the composition depends on the origin of the lecithin. A major source of lecithin is <a href="/wiki/Soybean_oil" title="Soybean oil">soybean oil</a>. Because of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> requirement to declare additions of allergens in foods, in addition to regulations regarding <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_crops" title="Genetically modified crops">genetically modified crops</a>, a gradual shift to other sources of lecithin (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Sunflower_oil" title="Sunflower oil">sunflower oil</a>) is taking place. The main phospholipids in lecithin from soya and sunflower are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphatidyl_choline" title="Phosphatidyl choline">phosphatidyl choline</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphatidyl_inositol" title="Phosphatidyl inositol">phosphatidyl inositol</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphatidyl_ethanolamine" title="Phosphatidyl ethanolamine">phosphatidyl ethanolamine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phosphatidic_acid" title="Phosphatidic acid">phosphatidic acid</a>. They often are abbreviated to PC, PI, PE, and PA, respectively. Purified phospholipids are produced by companies commercially.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrolysed_lecithin">Hydrolysed lecithin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lecithin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hydrolysed lecithin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To modify the performance of lecithin to make it suitable for the product to which it is added, it may be hydrolysed enzymatically. In hydrolysed lecithins, a portion of the phospholipids have one fatty acid removed by <a href="/wiki/Phospholipase" title="Phospholipase">phospholipase</a>. Such phospholipids are called lysophospholipids. The most commonly used phospholipase is phospholipase A2, which removes the <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">fatty acid</a> at the C2 position of glycerol. Lecithins may also be modified by a process called <a href="/wiki/Fractionation" title="Fractionation">fractionation</a>. During this process, lecithin is mixed with an alcohol, usually <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>. Some phospholipids, such as phosphatidylcholine, have good solubility in ethanol, whereas most other phospholipids do not dissolve well in ethanol. The ethanol is separated from the lecithin sludge, after which the ethanol is removed by evaporation to obtain a phosphatidylcholine-enriched lecithin fraction.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetically_modified_crops_as_a_source_of_lecithin">Genetically modified crops as a source of lecithin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lecithin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Genetically modified crops as a source of lecithin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As described above, lecithin is highly processed. Therefore, genetically modified (GM) <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> or <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> from the original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetically-modified_crops" title="Genetically-modified crops">GM crop</a> from which it is derived often is undetectable – in other words, it is not substantially different from lecithin derived from non-GM crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MarxDissertation_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-MarxDissertation-11">[11]</a></sup> Nonetheless, consumer concerns about <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_food" title="Genetically modified food">genetically modified food</a> have extended to highly purified derivatives from GM food, such as lecithin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Navigator_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Navigator-12">[12]</a></sup> This concern led to policy and regulatory changes in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> in 2000, when Commission Regulation (<a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">EC</a>) 50/2000 was passed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> which required <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regulation_of_the_release_of_genetic_modified_organisms#Labeling_and_traceability" title="Regulation of the release of genetic modified organisms">labelling of food containing additives derived from GMOs</a>, including lecithin. Because it is nearly impossible to detect the origin of derivatives such as lecithin, the European regulations require those who wish to sell lecithin in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> to use a meticulous, but essential system of <a href="/wiki/Identity_preservation" title="Identity preservation">identity preservation</a> (IP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MarxDissertation_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-MarxDissertation-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_and_applications">Properties and applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lecithin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Properties and applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lecithin has <a href="/wiki/Emulsion" title="Emulsion">emulsification</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lubricant" title="Lubricant">lubricant</a> properties, and is a <a href="/wiki/Surfactant" title="Surfactant">surfactant</a>. It can be totally <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolized</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Inositol" title="Inositol">Inositol</a>) by humans, so is well tolerated by humans and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nontoxic" title="Nontoxic">nontoxic</a> when ingested; some other emulsifiers can only be excreted via the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidneys</a>.</p>
<p>The major components of commercial soybean-derived lecithin are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>33–35% Soybean oil</li>
<li>20–21% <a href="/wiki/Inositol" title="Inositol">Inositol</a> phosphatides</li>
<li>19–21% <a href="/wiki/Phosphatidylcholine" title="Phosphatidylcholine">Phosphatidylcholine</a></li>
<li>8–20% <a href="/wiki/Phosphatidylethanolamine" title="Phosphatidylethanolamine">Phosphatidylethanolamine</a></li>
<li>5–11% Other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphatide" title="Phosphatide">phosphatides</a></li>
<li>5% Free <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a></li>
<li>2–5% <a href="/wiki/Sterol" title="Sterol">Sterols</a></li>
<li>1% Moisture</li>
</ul>
<p>Lecithin is used for applications in human food, animal feed, pharmaceuticals, paints, and other industrial applications.</p>
<p>Applications include:</p>
<ul>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_industry" title="Pharmaceutical industry">pharmaceutical industry</a>, it acts as a wetting, stabilizing agent and a choline enrichment carrier, helps in emulsifications and encapsulation, and is a good dispersing agent. It can be used in manufacture of intravenous fat infusions and for therapeutic use.</li>
<li>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_feed" title="Compound feed">animal feed</a>, it enriches fat and protein and improves pelletization.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">paint</a> industry, it forms protective coatings for surfaces with painting and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Printing_ink" title="Printing ink">printing ink</a>, has <a href="/wiki/Antioxidant" title="Antioxidant">antioxidant</a> properties, helps as a <a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">rust</a> inhibitor, is a colour-intensifying agent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>, conditioning aid modifier, and dispersing aid; it is a good stabilizing and suspending agent, emulsifier, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wetting_agent" title="Wetting agent">wetting agent</a>, helps in maintaining uniform mixture of several <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigments</a>, helps in grinding of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_oxide" title="Metal oxide">metal oxide</a> pigments, is a spreading and mixing aid, prevents hard settling of pigments, eliminates <a href="/wiki/Foam" title="Foam">foam</a> in water-based paints, and helps in fast dispersion of <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a>-based paints.</li>
<li>Lecithin also may be used as a <a href="/wiki/Release_agent" title="Release agent">release agent</a> for <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastics</a>, an antisludge additive in motor lubricants, an antigumming agent in gasoline, and an emulsifier, spreading agent, and antioxidant in textile, rubber, and other industries.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_additive">Food additive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lecithin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Food additive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The nontoxicity of lecithin leads to its use with food, as an additive or in food preparation. It is used commercially in foods requiring a natural emulsifier or lubricant.</p>
<p>In confectionery, it reduces viscosity, replaces more expensive ingredients, controls sugar crystallization and the flow properties of <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolate</a>, helps in the homogeneous mixing of ingredients, improves shelf life for some products, and can be used as a coating. In emulsions and fat spreads, it stabilizes emulsions, reduces spattering during frying, improves texture of spreads and flavour release. In <a href="/wiki/Dough" title="Dough">doughs</a> and bakery, it reduces fat and egg requirements, helps even distribution of ingredients in dough, stabilizes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">fermentation</a>, increases volume, protects <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a> cells in dough when frozen, and acts as a releasing agent to prevent sticking and simplify cleaning. It improves wetting properties of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophilic" title="Hydrophilic">hydrophilic</a> powders (e.g., low-fat proteins) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipophilic" title="Lipophilic">lipophilic</a> powders (e.g., cocoa powder), controls dust, and helps complete dispersion in water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-applications_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-applications-16">[16]</a></sup> Lecithin keeps <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_solids" title="Cocoa solids">cocoa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_butter" title="Cocoa butter">cocoa butter</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Candy_bar" title="Candy bar">candy bar</a> from separating. It can be used as a component of <a href="/wiki/Cooking_spray" title="Cooking spray">cooking sprays</a> to prevent sticking and as a releasing agent. In margarines, especially those containing high levels of <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a> (&gt;75%), lecithin is added as an 'antispattering' agent for shallow <a href="/wiki/Frying" title="Frying">frying</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lecithin is approved by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> for <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> consumption with the status "<a href="/wiki/Generally_recognized_as_safe" title="Generally recognized as safe">generally recognized as safe</a>". Lecithin is admitted by the EU as a <a href="/wiki/Food_additive" title="Food additive">food additive</a>, designated as <a href="/wiki/E_number" title="E number">E322</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FSA5_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FSA5-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dietary_supplement">Dietary supplement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lecithin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dietary supplement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because it contains <a href="/wiki/Phosphatidylcholine" title="Phosphatidylcholine">phosphatidylcholines</a>, lecithin is a source of <a href="/wiki/Choline" title="Choline">choline</a>, an essential nutrient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zeisel_SH.2C_da_Costa_KA_2009_615.E2.80.9323_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zeisel_SH.2C_da_Costa_KA_2009_615.E2.80.9323-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMR_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMR-20">[20]</a></sup> Clinical studies have shown benefit in acne, in improving liver function, and in lowering cholesterol, but clinical studies in dementia and dyskinesias have found no benefit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMR_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-AMR-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Altmedex_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Altmedex-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> An earlier study using a small sample (20 men divided in 3 groups) did not detect statistically significant short term (2–4 weeks) effects on cholesterol in hyperlipidaemic men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/La_Leche_League" title="La Leche League">La Leche League</a> recommends its use to prevent blocked or plugged milk ducts which can lead to <a href="/wiki/Mastitis" title="Mastitis">mastitis</a> in breastfeeding women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Egg_lecithin" title="Egg lecithin">Egg-derived lecithin</a> is not usually a concern for those allergic to eggs since commercially available egg lecithin is highly purified and devoid of allergy-causing egg proteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Egg lecithin is not a concern for those on low-<a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a> diets, because the lecithin found in eggs markedly inhibits the absorption of the cholesterol contained in eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_restrictions">Religious restrictions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lecithin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Religious restrictions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Soy-derived lecithin is considered by some to be <i><a href="/wiki/Kitniyot" title="Kitniyot">kitniyot</a></i> and prohibited on <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">Passover</a> for <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jews" title="Ashkenazi Jews">Ashkenazi Jews</a> when many grain-based foods are forbidden, but not at other times. This does not necessarily affect <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">Sephardi Jews</a>, who do not have the same restrictions on rice and <i>kitniyot</i> during <i>Pesach</i>/Passover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslims</a> are not forbidden to eat lecithin <i>per se</i>; however, since it may be derived from animal as well as plant sources, care must be taken to ensure this source is <a href="/wiki/Halal#Dietary_laws" title="Halal">halal</a>. Lecithin derived from plants and egg yolks is permissible, as is that derived from animals slaughtered according to the rules of <i><a href="/wiki/Dhabihah" title="Dhabihah">dhabihah</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lecithin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Research studies claim to show soy-derived lecithin has significant effects on lowering serum <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">triglycerides</a>, while increasing <a href="/wiki/High-density_lipoprotein" title="High-density lipoprotein">HDL</a> ("good cholesterol") levels in the blood of rats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>A growing body of evidence indicates lecithin is converted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gut_bacteria" title="Gut bacteria">gut bacteria</a> into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TMAO" title="TMAO">trimethylamine-N-oxide</a> (TMAO), which is released into circulation, and may with time contribute to <a href="/wiki/Atherosclerosis" title="Atherosclerosis">atherosclerosis</a> and heart attacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
