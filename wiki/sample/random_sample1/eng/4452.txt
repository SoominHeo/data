<p><i><b>Leaellynasaura</b></i> (meaning "Leaellyn's lizard") is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbivorous" title="Herbivorous">herbivorous</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ornithischian" title="Ornithischian">ornithischian</a> <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Albian" title="Albian">Albian</a> stage of the Early <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> (dated to between 118 and 110 million years ago<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holtz2008_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holtz2008-1">[1]</a></sup>), first discovered in <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur_Cove" title="Dinosaur Cove">Dinosaur Cove</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. The only known species is <i>Leaellynasaura amicagraphica</i>. It was described in 1989, and named after <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Leaellyn_Rich&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Leaellyn Rich (page does not exist)">Leaellyn Rich</a>, the daughter of the Australian palaeontologist couple <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tom_Rich&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tom Rich (page does not exist)">Tom Rich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Patricia_Vickers-Rich" title="Patricia Vickers-Rich">Patricia Vickers-Rich</a> who discovered it. The specific name <i>L. amicagraphica</i> translates to "friend writing" and honours both the Friends of the Museum of Victoria and the <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic Society</a> for their support of Australian paleontology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rich.26rich1988_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-rich.26rich1988-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leaellynasaura&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leaellynasaura_fossils.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Leaellynasaura_fossils.jpg/220px-Leaellynasaura_fossils.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Leaellynasaura_fossils.jpg/330px-Leaellynasaura_fossils.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Leaellynasaura_fossils.jpg/440px-Leaellynasaura_fossils.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leaellynasaura_fossils.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Fossils</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Leaellynasaura</i> is a relatively small dinosaur, about 90 centimeters (3 feet) in length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holtz2008_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Holtz2008-1">[1]</a></sup> It is known from several specimens including two nearly complete skeletons and two fragmentary skulls. It has been variously described as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypsilophodontid" title="Hypsilophodontid">hypsilophodontid</a>, a primitive <a href="/wiki/Iguanodontia" title="Iguanodontia">iguanodontian</a> and primitive ornithischian (<a href="/wiki/Genasauria" title="Genasauria">Genasauria</a>). The most recent assessment describes it as a non-<a href="/wiki/Iguanodontia" title="Iguanodontia">iguanodontian</a> <a href="/wiki/Ornithopod" title="Ornithopod">ornithopod</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Herne (2009) argued that, unlike more advanced ornithischians, <i>Leaellynasaura</i> lacked ossified tendons in its tail. He also argued that the tail is noteworthy as among the longest relative to its body size of any ornithischian: the tail was three times as long as the rest of the body combined; it also has more tail vertebrae than any other ornithischians except for some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadrosaur" title="Hadrosaur">hadrosaurs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-herne2009_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-herne2009-4">[4]</a></sup> However, in a subsequent revision of fossil material attributed to <i>Leaellynasaura</i> Herne (2013) could not confidently assign the postcranial skeletons with long tails (or indeed any fossils other than the holotype incomplete cranium MV P185991, right maxilla MV P186352 and left maxillary tooth MV P186412, all from late Aptian-early Albian <a href="/wiki/Eumeralla_Formation" title="Eumeralla Formation">Eumeralla Formation</a>) to <i>Leaellynasaura amicagraphica</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology_and_ecology">Biology and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leaellynasaura&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biology and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leaellynasaura.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="971" data-file-width="3092" height="69" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Leaellynasaura.jpg/220px-Leaellynasaura.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Leaellynasaura.jpg/330px-Leaellynasaura.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Leaellynasaura.jpg/440px-Leaellynasaura.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leaellynasaura.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Completely feathered illustration</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Leaellynasaura</i> was an Australian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Polar_dinosaurs" title="South Polar dinosaurs">polar dinosaur</a>. At this period in time, <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a> would have been within the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">Antarctic Circle</a>. Although this latitude is very cold today, it was significantly warmer during the mid-Cretaceous. Because of the Earth's tilt, <i>Leaellynasaura</i> and its contemporaries would still have been living under conditions with extended periods of both daylight and night. Depending on <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>, it is possible that the sun might not have risen for several weeks or months in the winter, which means that <i>Leaellynasaura</i> would have had to live in the dark for perhaps months at a time. A skull fragment interpreted as being from <i>Leaellynasaura</i> has been reported as showing enlarged <a href="/wiki/Eye" title="Eye">eyes</a> and the suggestion of proportionally large <a href="/wiki/Midbrain#Corpora_quadrigemina" title="Midbrain">optic lobes</a>, implying an adaptation to low-light conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rich.26rich2002_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-rich.26rich2002-6">[6]</a></sup> However, the relatively large orbits of this specimen were more recently interpreted as representing characteristically large eyes of a juvenile individual,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> rather than any low-light adaptation.</p>
