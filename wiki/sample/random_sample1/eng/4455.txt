<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Leo Lionni</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Leo Lionni</b> (May 5, 1910 – October 11, 1999) was an author and illustrator of children's books. Born in the Netherlands, he moved to Italy and lived there before moving to the United States in 1939, where he worked as an art director for several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advertising_agencies" title="Advertising agencies">advertising agencies</a>, and then for <i><a href="/wiki/Fortune_(magazine)" title="Fortune (magazine)">Fortune</a></i> magazine. He returned to Italy in 1962 and started writing and illustrating children's books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1962 his book <i>Inch by Inch</i> was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll_Shelf_Award" title="Lewis Carroll Shelf Award">Lewis Carroll Shelf Award</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leo_Lionni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lionni was born in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a> but spent two years in Philadelphia before moving to Italy during his teens. His father worked as an accountant and his mother was an opera singer. His father was assigned to an office in Italy part way through Leo's time in High School. He married Nora Maffi, the daughter of Fabrizio Maffi, a founder of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Communist_Party" title="Italian Communist Party">Italian Communist Party</a>, and they had two sons, Louis and Paolo, grandchildren Pippo and Annie and Sylvan, and great-grandchildren Madeline, Luca, Sam, Nick, Alix, Henry and Theo.</p>
<p>Leo Lionni died October 11, 1999, at his home in <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a>, Italy, at the age of 89.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leo_Lionni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From 1931 to 1939 he was a well-known and respected painter in Italy, where he worked in the <a href="/wiki/Futurism" title="Futurism">Futurism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> <a href="/wiki/Style_(visual_arts)" title="Style (visual arts)">styles</a>. In 1935 he received a degree in economics from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Genoa" title="University of Genoa">University of Genoa</a>. During the later part of this period, Lionni devoted himself more and more to advertising design.</p>
<p>In 1939 he moved to <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> and began full-time work in advertising, at which he was extremely successful, acquiring accounts from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ford_Motors" title="Ford Motors">Ford Motors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">Chrysler</a> <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_(automobile)" title="Plymouth (automobile)">Plymouth</a>, among others. He commissioned art from <a href="/wiki/Saul_Steinberg" title="Saul Steinberg">Saul Steinberg</a>, the then neophyte <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Andy Warhol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Calder" title="Alexander Calder">Alexander Calder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willem_de_Kooning" title="Willem de Kooning">Willem de Kooning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fernand_L%C3%A9ger" title="Fernand Léger">Fernand Léger</a>. He was a member of the Advertising Art Hall of Fame.</p>
<p>In 1948 he accepted a position as art director for <a href="/wiki/Fortune_(magazine)" title="Fortune (magazine)"><i>Fortune</i></a>, which he held until 1960. He also maintained outside clients, designing <a href="/wiki/The_Family_of_Man" title="The Family of Man">The Family of Man</a> catalogue design for the Museum of Modern Art, and was design director for Olivetti, for whom he produced ads, brochures and showroom design.</p>
<p>In 1960 he moved back to Italy, and began his career as a children's book author and illustrator. Lionni produced more than 40 children's books. He received the 1984 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Graphic_Arts" title="American Institute of Graphic Arts">American Institute of Graphic Arts</a> (A.I.G.A.) Gold Medal and was a four-time <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caldecott_Honor" title="Caldecott Honor">Caldecott Honor</a> Winner—for <i>Inch by Inch</i> (1961), <i>Swimmy</i> (1964), <i>Frederick</i> (1968), and <i>Alexander and the Wind-Up Mouse</i> (1970).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He also won the <a href="/wiki/Deutscher_Jugendliteraturpreis" title="Deutscher Jugendliteraturpreis">Deutscher Jugendliteraturpreis</a> in 1965.</p>
<p>Lionni always thought of himself as an artist. He worked in many disciplines including, especially, drawing, painting, sculpture and photography. He had one-man shows in the United States, Europe, Asia and the Middle East. He continued to work as an artist until just before his death in 1999.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Children.27s_author_and_illustrator">Children's author and illustrator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leo_Lionni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Children's author and illustrator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lionni became the first children's author/illustrator to use <a href="/wiki/Collage" title="Collage">collage</a> as the main medium for his illustrations. Reviewers such as <i><a href="/wiki/Booklist" title="Booklist">Booklist</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/School_Library_Journal" title="School Library Journal">School Library Journal</a></i> have said that Lionni's illustrations are "bold, sumptuous collages" that include "playful patches of color" and that his "beautifully simple [and] boldly graphic art [is] perfect to share with very young children." <i><a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Book_World" title="Book World">Book World</a></i><sup class="plainlinks noprint Inline-Template" style="vertical-align:text-top;white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="external text" href="http://dispenser.homenet.org/~dispenser/cgi-bin/dab_solver.py?page=Leo_Lionni&amp;editintro=Template:Disambiguation_needed/editintro&amp;client=Template:Dn" rel="nofollow"><span title="Link needs disambiguation. (August 2016)">disambiguation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> said that "the translucent color of the pictures and the simplicity of the text make a perfect combination." Many of Lionni's books deal with issues of community and creativity, and the existential condition, rendered as fables which appealed to children. He participated in workshops with children and even after his death school children continue to honor him by making their own versions of his books.</p>
<p>Leo Lionni would usually draw pictures as he told stories to his grandchildren, but one time he found himself on a long train ride with no drawing materials. Instead, he tore out circles of yellow and blue from a magazine to help him tell the story he had in mind. This experience led him to create his first book for children, <i>Little Blue and Little Yellow</i> (1959).</p>
<p>Lionni uses earth tones in his illustrations that are close to the actual colors of the objects found in nature. In his book <i>Inch by Inch</i>, for example, he uses realistic shades of brown and burnt orange in his collage of a robin, while the tree branches are shades of brown with dark green leaves. Mice are consistently found as characters in Lionni's books, such as the star character in <i>Frederick</i> and the title character in the Caldecott Honor Book <i>Alexander and the Wind-Up Mouse</i>. Lionni's illustrations have been compared to those of <a href="/wiki/Eric_Carle" title="Eric Carle">Eric Carle</a> as both often employ animals, birds, insects, and other creatures to tell a story about what it is to be human.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parallel_Botany"><i>Parallel Botany</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leo_Lionni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Parallel Botany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among Lionni's books that were not intended for children, the best known is probably <i>Parallel Botany</i> (1978; first published in Italian as <i>La botanica parallela</i>, 1976). This detailed treatise on plants that lack materiality—in other words, imaginary plants—is richly illustrated with drawings of plants in charcoal or pencil and photographs of "parallel botanists". The text is a rich mix of plant descriptions, travel tales, "ancient" myths, and folk etymologies, leavened with historical facts and grounded in actual science. As an imaginary taxonomy, it is invoked by <a href="/wiki/Italo_Calvino" title="Italo Calvino">Italo Calvino</a> as a precursor to the <i><a href="/wiki/Codex_Seraphinianus" title="Codex Seraphinianus">Codex Seraphinianus</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Serafini_(artist)" title="Luigi Serafini (artist)">Luigi Serafini</a>.</p>
