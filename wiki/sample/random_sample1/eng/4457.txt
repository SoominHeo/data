<p><b>Leonard Clemence</b> "<b>Leo</b>" <b>Tindemans</b> (<small>Dutch: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Dutch" title="Help:IPA for Dutch">[ˈleːjoː ˈtɪndəmɑns]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:Nl-be_leo_tindemands.ogg" title="File:Nl-be leo tindemands.ogg"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/ff/Nl-be_leo_tindemands.ogg" title="Nl-be leo tindemands.ogg">listen</a>)</small>; 16 April 1922 – 26 December 2014) was a Belgian politician. He served as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Prime_Ministers_of_Belgium" title="List of Prime Ministers of Belgium">43rd</a> <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Belgium" title="Prime Minister of Belgium">Prime Minister of Belgium</a> serving from 25 April 1974 until he resigned as minister on 20 October 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-revolution_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-1">[1]</a></sup> He was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Christen-Democratisch_en_Vlaams" title="Christen-Democratisch en Vlaams">Christian Democratic and Flemish</a> party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-revolution_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leo_Tindemans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tindemans was born in <a href="/wiki/Zwijndrecht,_Belgium" title="Zwijndrecht, Belgium">Zwijndrecht</a>, Belgium, to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> family.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">Political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leo_Tindemans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leo_Tindemans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tindemans was affiliated with the <a href="/wiki/Christen-Democratisch_en_Vlaams" title="Christen-Democratisch en Vlaams">CVP</a>. At the time, the party was strong in the northern region of <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>. Tindemans was elected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgian_Chamber_of_Representatives" title="Belgian Chamber of Representatives">Belgian Chamber of Representatives</a> in 1961 and re-elected in 1965, 1968, 1971, 1974, 1977 and 1978. From 1965 to 1973 Tindemans also served as the mayor of <a href="/wiki/Edegem" title="Edegem">Edegem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pact_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pact-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1968 Tindemans became minister tasked with the relations between the communities (1968–1972) during which he prepared the first constitutional reform which saw Belgium start transforming into a federal state. In 1972 he became minister for agriculture (1972–1973). In 1973 he became deputy Prime Minister and minister for the budget (1973–1974).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pact_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-pact-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prime_Minister_.281974.E2.80.931978.29">Prime Minister (1974–1978)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leo_Tindemans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Prime Minister (1974–1978)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tindemans served as Prime Minister of two Belgian governments, from 25 April 1974 to 20 October 1978. His first cabinet was a minority government formed by the Christian-democrats and liberals. When his first government fell in 1977, Tindemans won the snap general election with 983,000 votes, still a record for any election in Belgium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This formed his second cabinet with the Christian-democrats, socialists and Flemish nationalists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pact_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-pact-2">[2]</a></sup> His second government (1977–1978) fell due to the controversy surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Egmont_pact" title="Egmont pact">Egmont pact</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-revolution_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pact_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-pact-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne_Prize" title="Charlemagne Prize">Charlemagne Prize</a> 1976.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tindemans_Report">Tindemans Report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leo_Tindemans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tindemans Report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the conclusion of the Paris Summit in 1974, Tindemans was tasked with devising a report to define what was meant by the term ‘<a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.’<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tindemans_Report_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tindemans_Report-4">[4]</a></sup> Consulting not only reports drawn up by the European Parliament, European Commission and the European Court of Justice, Tindemans also sought advice from members of European governments and “other powerful forces in the various States.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tindemans_Report_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tindemans_Report-4">[4]</a></sup> Tindemans deliberately sought to avoid using the term constitution, and instead referred to his proposals as “a new phase in the history of the unification of Europe which can only be achieved by a continuous process.” Four major areas were outlined in the report: European foreign policy, European economic and social policies, European citizen rights, and the strengthening of existing European institutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tindemans_Report_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tindemans_Report-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In regards to a common foreign policy, Tindemans argues that Europe must present itself united outward not only in security, tariffs and trade, but also in an economic sense. He advocated for the creation of a single decision-making centre to handle these issues, and making foreign policy cooperation between member states a legal obligation – Tindemans felt that this role largely would lie within a strengthened Council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tindemans_Report_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Tindemans_Report-4">[4]</a></sup> He argued for placing the interest of joint action above each country’s own interests, and advocated for placing a delegate responsible for representing a collective Europe’s decisions. In addition, Tindemans placed particular emphasis on strengthening Europe-United States relations, proposing that a delegate be assigned to represent European Union to the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tindemans_Report_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Tindemans_Report-4">[4]</a></sup> Lastly on the foreign policy front, Tindemans advocated for the eventual creation of a common defense policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tindemans_Report_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Tindemans_Report-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the economic and social policy front, Tindemans advocated for reigniting discussions about a common economic and monetary policy, which had stalled in Europe during the early 1970s recession. As part of this revival of talks, he also advocated for the consolidation and modification of <a href="/wiki/Snake_in_the_tunnel" title="Snake in the tunnel">the snake</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tindemans_Report_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Tindemans_Report-4">[4]</a></sup> He proposed expanding the scope of monetary policy by establishing an internal monetary policy, budgetary policy, and plans for the control of inflation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tindemans_Report_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Tindemans_Report-4">[4]</a></sup> Tindemans supported abolishing the remaining obstacles to free trade of capital that existed within the European Economic Community. Finally, Tindemans hoped for a citizen’s Europe; he advocated for European civil rights, consumer rights, and protection of the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tindemans_Report_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-Tindemans_Report-4">[4]</a></sup> He also pushed for a European passport union, and creation of integrated educational systems. Finally, Tindemans encouraged broad institutional reform, pushing for increased powers to the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>, and overall reform for the <a href="/wiki/European_Council" title="European Council">European Council</a>, the Council of Ministers, and the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tindemans_Report_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-Tindemans_Report-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to economic conditions at the time, the Tindemans report failed to make an immediate impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CVCE_report_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CVCE_report-5">[5]</a></sup> Despite this the report generated a request from the Council of Foreign Ministers and the Commission to create an annual progress report on the European Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CVCE_report_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-CVCE_report-5">[5]</a></sup> In addition, though quite optimistic and federalist in scope, several items which Tindemans advocated for eventually found themselves in the European Union, such as a common economic and foreign policy, as well as symbols for the European Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CVCE_report_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-CVCE_report-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_career">Later career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leo_Tindemans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1976, during the founding Congress of the <a href="/wiki/European_People%27s_Party" title="European People's Party">European People's Party</a> in Brussels, he was elected first President of the new party, a role which gave him the important tasks of harmonising and finding consensus between the different leaders and member parties of the EPP and of leading the party during the first direct elections to the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> in 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pact_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-pact-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tindemans received an <a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">Honorary Doctorate</a> from <a href="/wiki/Heriot-Watt_University" title="Heriot-Watt University">Heriot-Watt University</a> in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tindemans was elected to the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> with a record number of votes (983.000 votes, which is still a record for any election in Belgium) and was a member of that parliament from 1979 to 1981 (during which time he also was chairman of the CVP). With the general elections of 1981 Tindemans returned to the Belgian politics and became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_of_Foreign_Affairs_(Belgium)" title="Minister of Foreign Affairs (Belgium)">minister of foreign affairs</a> (1981–1989).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> With the European elections in 1989 Tindemans went back to the European Parliament where he served two terms until he retired in 1999. During 1994–1995 he was chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Tindemans_group" title="Tindemans group">Tindemans group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-revolution_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leo_Tindemans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tindemans died on 26 December 2014 in <a href="/wiki/Edegem" title="Edegem">Edegem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a>, Belgium, aged 92.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
