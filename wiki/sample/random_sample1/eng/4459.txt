<p><b>Leonid Borisovich Kogan</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">ÐÐµÐ¾Ð½Ð¸ÌÐ´ ÐÐ¾ÑÐ¸ÌÑÐ¾Ð²Ð¸Ñ ÐÐ¾ÌÐ³Ð°Ð½</span>; <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a>: <span lang="uk" xml:lang="uk">ÐÐµÐ¾Ð½ÑÐ´ ÐÐ¾ÑÐ¸ÑÐ¾Ð²Ð¸Ñ ÐÐ¾Ð³Ð°Ð½</span>; November 14, 1924 â December 17, 1982<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) was a preeminent <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violinist</a> during the 20th century. He is considered to have been one of the greatest representatives of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> School of violin playing. Many violinists considered him to be the greatest violinist of all time, and violin connoisseurs also have a great respect for his style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leonid_Kogan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kogan was born in <a href="/wiki/Dnipropetrovsk" title="Dnipropetrovsk">Dnipropetrovsk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, the son of a photographer who was an amateur violinist. After showing an early interest and ability for violin playing, his family moved to <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, where he was able to further his studies. From age ten he studied there with the noted violin pedagogue Abram Yampolsky. In 1934, <a href="/wiki/Jascha_Heifetz" title="Jascha Heifetz">Jascha Heifetz</a> played concerts in Moscow. "I attended every one," Kogan later said, "and can remember until now every note he played. He was the ideal artist for me." When Kogan was 12, <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Thibaud" title="Jacques Thibaud">Jacques Thibaud</a> was in Moscow and heard him play. The French virtuoso predicted a great future for Kogan.</p>
<p>Kogan studied at the Central Music School in Moscow (1934â43), then at the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Conservatory" title="Moscow Conservatory">Moscow Conservatory</a> (1943â48), where he studied as a postgraduate (1948â51).</p>
<p>At the age of 17, and while still a student, he performed throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR">USSR</a>. He was co-winner of the first prize at the World Youth Festival in <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>. In 1951 Kogan won first prize at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Elisabeth_Music_Competition" title="Queen Elisabeth Music Competition">Queen Elizabeth Competition</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> with a dazzling performance of <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Paganini" title="NiccolÃ² Paganini">Paganini</a>'s first concerto that included an outstanding interpretation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sauret" title="Sauret">Sauret</a>'s cadenza.</p>
<p>His official debut was in 1941, playing the <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Brahms</a> <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Brahms)" title="Violin Concerto (Brahms)">Concerto</a> with the Moscow Philharmonic in the Great Hall of the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Conservatory" title="Moscow Conservatory">Moscow Conservatory</a>.</p>
<p>His international solo tours took him to <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> and <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> in 1955, and then <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and the United States in the following years. Kogan had a repertoire of over 18 concerti and a number of concerti by modern composers were dedicated to him.</p>
<p>In 1952, Kogan began teaching at the Moscow Conservatory, and in 1980 he was invited to teach at the <a href="/wiki/Accademia_Musicale_Chigiana" title="Accademia Musicale Chigiana">Accademia Musicale Chigiana</a> in <a href="/wiki/Siena" title="Siena">Siena</a>, Italy.</p>

<p>Kogan, a brilliant and compelling violinist, shunned publicity. His career was always overshadowed by that of <a href="/wiki/David_Oistrakh" title="David Oistrakh">David Oistrakh</a>, who was strongly promoted by the Soviet authorities. Kogan was made an Honoured Artist in 1955 and a <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Artist_of_the_USSR" title="People's Artist of the USSR">People's Artist of the USSR</a> in 1964. He received the <a href="/wiki/Lenin_Prize" title="Lenin Prize">Lenin Prize</a> in 1965.</p>
<p>Kogan married <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Gilels" title="Elizabeth Gilels">Elizabeth Gilels</a> (sister of pianist <a href="/wiki/Emil_Gilels" title="Emil Gilels">Emil Gilels</a>), also a concert violinist. His son, <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Kogan_(conductor)" title="Pavel Kogan (conductor)">Pavel Kogan</a> (b. 1952) became a famous violinist and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conductor_(music)" title="Conductor (music)">conductor</a>. His daughter, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nina_Kogan_(pianist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nina Kogan (pianist) (page does not exist)">Nina Kogan</a> (b. 1954), is a concert pianist and became the accompanist and sonata partner of her father at an early age.</p>
<p>Kogan was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jewish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kogan died of a heart attack in the city of <a href="/wiki/Mytishchi" title="Mytishchi">Mytishchi</a>, while travelling by train between <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yaroslavl" title="Yaroslavl">Yaroslavl</a> to a concert he was to perform with his son. Two days before, he had played the Beethoven Concerto in Vienna. He was buried in <a href="/wiki/Novodevichy_Cemetery" title="Novodevichy Cemetery">Novodevichy Cemetery</a>.</p>
<p>Many speculate that Kogan played on all steel strings, though there is not an outright confirmation. While his close associates indicate he played on gut strings with a steel 'e', it is most likely that he used different combinations over the course of his career.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments">Instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leonid_Kogan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kogan used two <a href="/wiki/Guarneri" title="Guarneri">Guarneri</a> del GesÃ¹ violins: the 1726 ex-Colin and the 1733 ex-Burmester. He used French bows by <a href="/wiki/Dominique_Peccatte" title="Dominique Peccatte">Dominique Peccatte</a>. Kogan never actually owned these instruments; they were provided on loan from the Soviet government. Today they are worth more than $4 million USD.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recordings">Recordings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leonid_Kogan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recordings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kogan formed a Trio with pianist <a href="/wiki/Emil_Gilels" title="Emil Gilels">Gilels</a> and cellist <a href="/wiki/Mstislav_Rostropovich" title="Mstislav Rostropovich">Rostropovich</a>. Their recordings include <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piano_Trio_No._7_(Beethoven)" title="Piano Trio No. 7 (Beethoven)">Archduke Trio</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Robert_Schumann" title="Robert Schumann">Schumann</a> D minor, the <a href="/wiki/Piano_Trio_(Tchaikovsky)" title="Piano Trio (Tchaikovsky)">Tchaikovsky</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Camille_Saint-Sa%C3%ABns" title="Camille Saint-SaÃ«ns">Saint-SaÃ«ns</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Brahms</a> Horn Trio with Yakov Shapiro (horn), and the <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Faur%C3%A9" title="Gabriel FaurÃ©">FaurÃ©</a> C minor Quartet with <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Barshai" title="Rudolf Barshai">Rudolf Barshai</a> (viola). Kogan later formed another Trio with the conductor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evgeny_Svetlanov" title="Evgeny Svetlanov">Svetlanov</a> on piano and cellist Luzanov. Kogan was the first Soviet violinist to play and record <a href="/wiki/Alban_Berg" title="Alban Berg">Berg</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Berg)" title="Violin Concerto (Berg)">Violin concerto</a>. He also made a famous recording of <a href="/wiki/Khachaturian" title="Khachaturian">Khachaturian</a>'s violin concerto with <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Monteux" title="Pierre Monteux">Pierre Monteux</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Boston Symphony Orchestra">Boston Symphony Orchestra</a> for RCA Victor (his America debut recording), a version still considered the most exciting reading of the work. Kogan recorded violin concerti by other Soviet composers, including the two by <a href="/wiki/Tikhon_Khrennikov" title="Tikhon Khrennikov">Tikhon Khrennikov</a>. With Karl Richter Kogan recorded J.S Bach's 6 Violin Sonatas in 1972. There are more than 30 albums of his performances on the Arlecchino label. In 2006, EMI France issued a 4-CD box set ("Les Introuvables de Leonid Kogan") containing his concerto recordings for that label, all digitally remastered the same year.</p>
<p>The EMI Kogan recordings from 1950s and 1960s used to belong to Columbia, who released about five stereo recordings of Kogan in the vinyl period: Beethoven Violin Concerto (SAX 2386), Brahms Violin Concerto (SAX 2307), Tchaikovsky Violin Concerto (SAX 2323), Lalo Symphonie espagnole (SAX 2329) and Leclar/Telemann/Ysaye Sonatas for duo Violins (SAX 2531). Nowadays, these Kogan records are among the most sought-after records for classical vinyl collectors. For example, the price of the Beethoven Violin Concerto (SAX2386) record soars up to 10,000 dollars in eBay auctions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
