<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Leonid Kreutzer</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Leonid Kreutzer</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Petersburg" title="St. Petersburg">St. Petersburg</a>, 13 March 1884 – 30 October 1953, <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>) was a classical pianist.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leonid_Kreutzer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kreutzer was born in St. Petersburg into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Jews" title="German Jews">German-Jewish</a> family. He studied composition under <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Glazunov" title="Alexander Glazunov">Alexander Glazunov</a> and piano under <a href="/wiki/Anna_Yesipova" title="Anna Yesipova">Anna Yesipova</a>. He was a highly influential piano teacher at the Berlin Academy of Music (Berliner Hochschule für Musik), together with <a href="/wiki/Egon_Petri" title="Egon Petri">Egon Petri</a>. Amongst Kreutzer's students were <a href="/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_Szpilman" title="Władysław Szpilman">Władysław Szpilman</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hans-Erich_Riebensahm&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hans-Erich Riebensahm (page does not exist)">Hans-Erich Riebensahm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Horbowski" title="Vladimir Horbowski">Vladimir Horbowski</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karl-Ulrich_Schnabel" title="Karl-Ulrich Schnabel">Karl-Ulrich Schnabel</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Franz_Osborn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Franz Osborn (page does not exist)">Franz Osborn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ignace_Strasfogel" title="Ignace Strasfogel">Ignace Strasfogel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grete_Sultan" title="Grete Sultan">Grete Sultan</a>. Leonid Kreutzer also gave musically and technically demanding solo recitals, mostly dedicated to specific composers or themes. At some of these, notably in June 1925, he performed works of contemporaries or modern, <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> composers of his time or of the recent past such as <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Franck" title="César Franck">César Franck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claude_Debussy" title="Claude Debussy">Claude Debussy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hindemith" title="Paul Hindemith">Paul Hindemith</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Juon" title="Paul Juon">Paul Juon</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazis</a> targeted him prominently as a cultural enemy: Together with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Frieda_Loebenstein&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Frieda Loebenstein (page does not exist)">Frieda Loebenstein</a> he is the one of two pianists whose name appears in a list of "tidy-up tasks" ("Aufräumungsarbeiten") compiled by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Rosenberg" title="Alfred Rosenberg">Rosenberg</a>'s "Kampfbund für deutsche Kultur" (Battle-Union for German Culture). He emigrated in 1933 to Tokyo, Japan. He is also known as editor of Chopin's works at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ullstein-Verlag" title="Ullstein-Verlag">Ullstein-Verlag</a>. He wrote one of the first works on systematic use of the <a href="/wiki/Piano#Pedals" title="Piano">piano pedal</a> ("Das normale Klavierpedal vom akustischen und ästhetischen Standpunkt", 1915).</p>
<p>There are pianos which are built under his name in Japan.</p>
