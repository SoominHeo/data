<p><b>Leopold III</b> (3 November 1901 – 25 September 1983) reigned as <a href="/wiki/List_of_Belgian_monarchs" title="List of Belgian monarchs">King of the Belgians</a> from 1934 until 1951, when he abdicated in favour of the <a href="/wiki/Heir_apparent" title="Heir apparent">heir apparent</a>, his son <a href="/wiki/Baudouin_of_Belgium" title="Baudouin of Belgium">Baudouin</a>. From 1944 until 1950, Leopold's brother, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Charles,_Count_of_Flanders" title="Prince Charles, Count of Flanders">Charles</a>, served as <a href="/wiki/Prince_regent" title="Prince regent">prince regent</a> while Leopold was declared unable to rule. In 1950, the debate about whether Leopold could resume his royal duties provoked a political crisis known as the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Question" title="Royal Question">Royal Question</a>.</p>
<p>Leopold III was born in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> as Prince Leopold of Belgium, Prince of <a href="/wiki/Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" title="Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">Saxe-Coburg and Gotha</a>, and succeeded to the throne of Belgium on 23 February 1934 following the death of his father, <a href="/wiki/Albert_I_of_Belgium" title="Albert I of Belgium">King Albert I</a>. He was invested as <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">Knight</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Golden_Fleece" title="Order of the Golden Fleece">Order of the Golden Fleece</a> in Spain in 1923, <a href="/wiki/Grand_Cross" title="Grand Cross">Grand Cross</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Tower_and_Sword" title="Order of the Tower and Sword">Order of the Tower and Sword</a> (Portugal) in 1927 and Knight of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Garter" title="Order of the Garter">Order of the Garter</a> (United Kingdom) in 1935.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_family">Early life and family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leopold_III_of_Belgium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prince Leopold, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Brabant" title="Duke of Brabant">Duke of Brabant</a>, was sent by his father to <a href="/wiki/Eton_College" title="Eton College">Eton College</a> in the United Kingdom in 1915. After the war, in 1919, the Duke visited the Old Mission and Saint Anthony Seminary in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Barbara,_California" title="Santa Barbara, California">Santa Barbara, California</a>. He married <a href="/wiki/Astrid_of_Sweden" title="Astrid of Sweden">Princess Astrid of Sweden</a> in a civil ceremony in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> on 4 November 1926, followed by a religious ceremony in Brussels on 10 November. The marriage produced three children:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Princess_Jos%C3%A9phine_Charlotte_of_Belgium" title="Princess Joséphine Charlotte of Belgium">Joséphine-Charlotte</a>, Princess of Belgium, born at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Palace_of_Brussels" title="Royal Palace of Brussels">Royal Palace of Brussels</a> on 11 October 1927, Grand Duchess of <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>. She was married on 9 April 1953 to <a href="/wiki/Jean,_Grand_Duke_of_Luxembourg" title="Jean, Grand Duke of Luxembourg">Prince Jean</a>, later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand-Duke" title="Grand-Duke">Grand-Duke</a> of <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>. She died at <a href="/wiki/Fischbach_Castle" title="Fischbach Castle">Fischbach Castle</a> on 10 January 2005.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Baudouin_of_Belgium" title="Baudouin of Belgium">Baudouin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Brabant" title="Duke of Brabant">Duke of Brabant</a>, Count of Hainaut, Prince of Belgium, who became the fifth King of the Belgians as <b>Baudouin</b>, born at Stuyvenberg on the outskirts of Brussels on 7 September 1930, and died at <a href="/wiki/Motril" title="Motril">Motril</a> in <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, on 31 July 1993.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Albert_II_of_Belgium" title="Albert II of Belgium">Albert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Li%C3%A8ge" title="Prince of Liège">Prince of Liège</a>, Prince of Belgium, who became the sixth King of the Belgians as <b>Albert II</b>, born at Stuyvenberg on 6 June 1934. He abdicated in July 2013.</li>
</ul>
<p>On 29 August 1935, while the king and queen were driving along the winding, narrow roads near their villa at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%BCssnacht_am_Rigi" title="Küssnacht am Rigi">Küssnacht am Rigi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schwyz" title="Schwyz">Schwyz</a>, Switzerland, on the shores of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_of_Lucerne" title="Lake of Lucerne">Lake Lucerne</a>, Leopold lost control of the car which plunged into the lake, killing Queen Astrid.</p>
<p>Leopold married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lilian_Baels" title="Lilian Baels">Lilian Baels</a> on 11 September 1941 in a secret, religious ceremony, with no validity under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgian_law" title="Belgian law">Belgian law</a>. They originally intended to wait until the end of the war for the civil marriage, but as the new <i>Princesse de Réthy</i> was soon expecting their first child, the ceremony took place on 6 December 1941. They had three children in total:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prince_Alexander_of_Belgium" title="Prince Alexander of Belgium">Alexander</a>, Prince of Belgium, born in Brussels on 18 July 1942. In 1991, he married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princess_Lea_of_Belgium" title="Princess Lea of Belgium">Lea Inga Dora Wohlman</a>, a marriage revealed only seven years later. He died on 29 November 2009.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Princess_Marie-Christine_of_Belgium" title="Princess Marie-Christine of Belgium">Marie-Christine</a>, Princess of Belgium, born in Brussels on 6 February 1951. Her first marriage, to Paul Drucker in 1981, lasted 40 days (though they were not formally divorced until 1985); she subsequently married Jean-Paul Gourges in 1989.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Princess_Marie-Esm%C3%A9ralda_of_Belgium" title="Princess Marie-Esméralda of Belgium">Maria-Esmeralda</a>, Princess of Belgium, born in Brussels on 30 September 1956, a journalist, her professional name is Esmeralda de Réthy. She married <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Moncada" title="Salvador Moncada">Salvador Moncada</a>, a noted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmacologist" title="Pharmacologist">pharmacologist</a>, in 1998. They have a son and a daughter.</li>
</ul>

<p>When World War II broke out in September 1939, the French and British governments immediately sought to persuade Belgium to join them. Leopold and his government refused, maintaining Belgium's neutrality. Belgium considered itself well-prepared against a possible invasion by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_powers_of_World_War_II" title="Axis powers of World War II">Axis</a> forces, for during the 1930s the Belgian government had made extensive preparations to deter and repel an invasion of the country by Germany such as the one that had occurred in 1914.</p>
<p>On 10 May 1940, the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a> invaded Belgium. On the first day of the offensive, the principal Belgian strong point of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Eben-Emael" title="Fort Eben-Emael">Fort Eben-Emael</a> was overwhelmed by a daring paratroop operation and the defensive perimeter thus penetrated before any French or British troops could arrive. After a short running battle that eventually involved the armies of all four belligerents, Belgium was overwhelmed by the numerically superior and better-prepared Germans.</p>
<p>Nevertheless, the Belgian perseverance prevented the <a href="/wiki/British_Expeditionary_Force_(World_War_II)" title="British Expeditionary Force (World War II)">British Expeditionary Force</a> from being outflanked and cut off from the coast, enabling the <a href="/wiki/Dunkirk_evacuation" title="Dunkirk evacuation">evacuation from Dunkirk</a>. After his military surrender, Leopold (unlike <a href="/wiki/Wilhelmina_of_the_Netherlands" title="Wilhelmina of the Netherlands">Queen Wilhelmina of the Netherlands</a>, in a similar predicament) remained in Brussels to face the victorious invaders, while his entire civil government fled to Paris and later to London.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surrender_and_constitutional_crisis">Surrender and constitutional crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leopold_III_of_Belgium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Surrender and constitutional crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 24 May 1940, Leopold, having assumed command of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgian_Army" title="Belgian Army">Belgian Army</a>, met with his ministers for the final time. The ministers urged the king to leave the country with the government. Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Hubert_Pierlot" title="Hubert Pierlot">Hubert Pierlot</a> reminded him that capitulation was a decision for the Belgian government, not the king. The king indicated that he had decided to remain in Belgium with his troops, whatever the outcome. The ministers took this to mean that he would establish a new government under the direction of Hitler, potentially a treasonous act. Leopold thought that he might be seen as a deserter if he were to leave the country: "Whatever happens, I have to share the same fate as my troops."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royalarticles_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-royalarticles-1">[1]</a></sup> Leopold had long had a difficult and contentious relationship with his ministers, acting independently of government influence whenever possible, and seeking to circumvent and even limit the ministers' powers, while expanding his own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royalarticles_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-royalarticles-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>French, British, and Belgian troops were encircled by German forces at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dunkirk" title="Battle of Dunkirk">Battle of Dunkirk</a>. Leopold notified King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_VI_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George VI of the United Kingdom">George VI of the United Kingdom</a> by telegram on 25 May 1940 that Belgian forces were being crushed, saying "assistance which we give to the Allies will come to an end if our army is surrounded".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Two days later (27 May 1940), Leopold surrendered the Belgian forces to the Germans.</p>
<p>Prime Minister Pierlot spoke on French radio, saying that the king's decision to surrender went against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgian_Constitution" title="Belgian Constitution">Belgian Constitution</a>. The decision, he said, was not only a military decision but also a political decision, and the king had acted without his ministers' advice, and therefore contrary to the Constitution. Pierlot and his Government believed this created an <i>impossibilité de régner</i>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Should the king find himself unable to reign, the ministers, having observed this inability, immediately summon the Chambers. Regency and guardianship are to be provided by the united Chambers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>It was impossible, however, to summon the Belgian <a href="/wiki/Chamber_of_Representatives_(Belgium)" title="Chamber of Representatives (Belgium)">Chamber of Representatives</a> or Belgian <a href="/wiki/Senate_(Belgium)" title="Senate (Belgium)">Senate</a> at this time, or to appoint a <a href="/wiki/Regent" title="Regent">regent</a>. After the liberation of Belgium in September 1944, the government asked Leopold's brother, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Charles,_Count_of_Flanders" title="Prince Charles, Count of Flanders">Prince Charles</a>, to serve as regent.</p>
<p>After Leopold's surrender, the British press denounced him as "Traitor King" and "King Rat"; the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mirror" title="Daily Mirror">Daily Mirror</a></i> published a picture of Leopold with the headline "The Face That Every Woman Now Despises". A group of Belgian refugees in Paris placed a message at <a href="/wiki/Albert_I_of_Belgium" title="Albert I of Belgium">King Albert</a>'s statue denouncing his son as "your unworthy successor".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atkin1990_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-atkin1990-4">[4]</a></sup> French Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Paul_Reynaud" title="Paul Reynaud">Paul Reynaud</a> accused Leopold of treason. Flemish historians Valaers and Van Goethem wrote that Leopold III had become "The scapegoat of Reynaud",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> because Reynaud was likely already aware that the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_France" title="Battle of France">Battle of France</a> was lost.</p>
<p>Leopold's surrender was also decried by Winston Churchill. In the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> on 4 June 1940 he said:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>At the last moment when Belgium was already invaded, King Leopold called upon us to come to his aid, and even at the last moment we came. He and his brave, efficient army, nearly half a million strong, guarded our left flank and thus kept open our only line of retreat to the sea. Suddenly, without prior consultation, with the least possible notice, without the advice of his ministers and upon his own personal act, he sent a plenipotentiary to the German Command, surrendered his army and exposed our whole flank and means of retreat."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In 1949, Churchill's comments about the events of May 1940 were published in <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Soir" title="Le Soir">Le Soir</a></i> (12 February 1949). Leopold's former secretary sent a letter to Churchill saying that Churchill was wrong. Churchill sent a copy of this letter to the king's brother, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Charles,_Count_of_Flanders" title="Prince Charles, Count of Flanders">Prince Charles</a>, via his secretary André de Staercke. In his own letter Churchill wrote,</p>
<blockquote>
<p>With regards to King Leopold, the words which I used at the time in the House of Commons are upon record and after careful consideration I do not see any reason to change them (...) it seemed to me and many others that the king should have been guided by the advice of his ministers and should not have favoured a course which identified the capitulation of the Belgian Army with the submission of the Belgian State to Herr Hitler and consequently taking them out of the war. Happily this evil was averted, and in the end, all came right. I need scarcely say that nothing I said at the time could be interpreted as a reflection upon the personal courage or honour of King Leopold."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>De Staercke replied that Churchill was right: "The Prince, Monsieur Spaak [Belgian Foreign Minister <a href="/wiki/Paul-Henri_Spaak" title="Paul-Henri Spaak">Paul-Henri Spaak</a>] and I read your text, which states the precise truth and seems perfect to us."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Belgian historian Francis Balace wrote that capitulation was inevitable because the Belgian Army was not able to fight any longer against the German army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Even Churchill admitted that their position was perilous. In a telegram to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_Marshal_(United_Kingdom)" title="Field Marshal (United Kingdom)">Field Marshal</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Vereker,_6th_Viscount_Gort" title="John Vereker, 6th Viscount Gort">Lord Gort</a> on 27 May, only one day before the Belgian capitulation, he wrote, "We are asking them to sacrifice themselves for us."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_fall_of_France">After the fall of France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leopold_III_of_Belgium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: After the fall of France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Upon Leopold's surrender, the government ministers left for exile, mostly in France. When France fell at the end of June 1940, several ministers sought to return to Belgium. They made an overture to Leopold but were rebuffed:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Pierlot and his government saw that Western Europe had been conquered by the Germans completely and tried to make amends to their king. Would it be possible for them to return to Belgium and form a new government? Leopold showed his stubborn nature; he was insulted by his ministers... His reply was short: "The situation of the king is unaltered; he does not engage in politics and does not receive politicians."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royalarticles_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-royalarticles-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Because of the great popularity of the king, and the unpopularity of the civil government from the middle of 1940,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> the government crisis persisted. <i>The Royal Articles</i> state:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>This refusal [of the king to reconcile with the ministers] left the ministers with no other option than to move to London, where they could continue their work representing the independent Belgium. From the time of their arrival in London, they were confident about an Allied victory and soon were treated with respect by the Allies.... Pierlot and Spaak helped to build Leopold's reputation as a heroic prisoner of war and even said that the Belgians should support their king. But they had no idea what Leopold was doing in [the Royal Palace at] Laeken. He refused to reply to their messages and stayed cool toward them. What was he doing in the castle? Was he collaborating, did he oppose the Germans, or had he decided to just shut his mouth and wait to see how things would go?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royalarticles_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-royalarticles-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>On 2 August 1940, several ministers conferred in <a href="/wiki/Le_Perthus" title="Le Perthus">Le Perthus</a> in France near the Spanish border. Prime Minister Pierlot and Foreign Minister Spaak were persuaded to go to London, but they were able to start out for London only at the end of August and could travel only via neutral Spain and Portugal. When they reached Spain, they were arrested and detained by the regime of <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Francisco Franco</a>; they finally arrived in London on 22 October.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meeting_with_Hitler">Meeting with Hitler</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leopold_III_of_Belgium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Meeting with Hitler">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Leopold rejected cooperation with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazis" title="Nazis">Nazis</a> and refused to administer Belgium in accordance with their dictates, thus the Germans implemented a military government. Leopold attempted to assert his authority as monarch and head of the Belgian government although he was a prisoner of the Germans. Despite his defiance of the Germans, the Belgian government-in-exile in London maintained that the king did not represent the Belgian government and was unable to reign. The Germans held him at first under <a href="/wiki/House_arrest" title="House arrest">house arrest</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Palace_of_Laeken" title="Royal Palace of Laeken">Royal Palace of Laeken</a> in Brussels. Having desired a meeting with Adolf Hitler since June 1940 in respect of the situation of Belgian prisoners of war, Leopold III finally met with him on 19 November 1940. Leopold wanted Hitler to release Belgian POW, and issue a public statement about Belgium's future independence. Hitler refused to speak about the independence of Belgium or issue a statement about it. In refusing to publish a statement, Hitler unintentionally preserved the king from being seen as cooperating with Germany, and thus engaged in treasonous acts, which would likely have obliged him to abdicate upon liberation of Belgium. "The [German] Chancellor saved the king two times."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_marriage">Second marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leopold_III_of_Belgium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Second marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 11 September 1941, while a prisoner of the Germans, Leopold secretly married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lilian_Baels" title="Lilian Baels">Lilian Baels</a> in a religious ceremony that had no validity under Belgian law, as Belgian law required a religious marriage to be preceded by a legal or <a href="/wiki/Civil_marriage" title="Civil marriage">civil marriage</a>. On 6 December, they were married under <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a>. The reason for the out-of-order marriages was never officially made public.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jozef-Ernest_Cardinal_van_Roey" title="Jozef-Ernest Cardinal van Roey">Jozef-Ernest Cardinal van Roey</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Mechelen" title="Archbishop of Mechelen">Archbishop of Mechelen</a>, wrote an open letter to parish priests throughout the country announcing Leopold's second marriage on 7 December. The letter from the Cardinal revealed that the king's new wife would be known as Princesse de Réthy, not Queen Lilian, and that any children they had would have no claim to the throne (though they would be Princes or Princesses of Belgium with the style <a href="/wiki/Royal_Highness" title="Royal Highness">Royal Highness</a>). Leopold's new marriage damaged his reputation further in the eyes of many of his subjects.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Political_Testament">The <i>Political Testament</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leopold_III_of_Belgium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Political Testament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:183px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Belgium_5_francs_1941_obverse.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="179" data-file-width="181" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a6/Belgium_5_francs_1941_obverse.jpg" width="181"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Belgium_5_francs_1941_obverse.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The face of Leopold III on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/5_francs_(World_War_II_Belgian_coin)" title="5 francs (World War II Belgian coin)">zinc 5 franc coin</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The ministers made several efforts during the war to work out a suitable agreement with Leopold III. They sent Pierlot's son-in-law as an emissary to Leopold in January 1944, carrying a letter offering reconciliation from the Belgian government-in-exile. The letter never reached its destination, however, as the son-in-law was killed by the Germans en route. The ministers did not know what happened either to the message or the messenger and assumed that Leopold was ignoring them.</p>
<p>Leopold wrote his <i>Political Testament</i> in January 1944, shortly after this failed attempt at reconciliation. The testament was to be published in case he was not in Belgium when Allied forces arrived. The testament, which had an imperious and negative tone, considered the potential Allied movement into Belgium an "occupation", not a "liberation". It gave no credit to the active <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgian_resistance" title="Belgian resistance">Belgian resistance</a>. The Belgian government-in-exile in London did not like Leopold's demand that the government ministers involved in the 1940 crisis be exonerated. The Allies did not like Leopold's repudiation of the treaties concluded by the Belgian government-in-exile in London. The United States was particularly concerned about the economic treaty it had reached with the government-in-exile that enabled it to obtain <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Congo" title="Belgian Congo">Congolese</a> <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> for America's secret <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atom_bomb" title="Atom bomb">atom bomb</a> program, which had been developed for use against Germany (although, as it turned out, Germany surrendered before the first bomb was ready).</p>
<p>The Belgian government did not publish the <i>Political Testament</i> and tried to ignore it, partly for fear of increased support for the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Belgium" title="Communist Party of Belgium">Belgian Communist party</a>. When Pierlot and Spaak learned of its contents in September 1944, they were astonished and felt deceived by the king. According to André de Staercke, they were dismayed "in the face of so much blindness and unawareness".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Churchill's reaction to the Testament was simply, "It stinks."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In a sentence inspired by a quote of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles-Maurice_de_Talleyrand-P%C3%A9rigord" title="Charles-Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord">Talleyrand</a> about the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bourbons" title="Bourbons">Bourbons</a> after the restoration of the French monarchy in 1815, Churchill declared, "He is like the Bourbons, he has learned nothing and forgotten everything."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exile_and_abdication">Exile and abdication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leopold_III_of_Belgium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Exile and abdication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deportation_and_exile">Deportation and exile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leopold_III_of_Belgium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Deportation and exile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1944, <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Himmler" title="Heinrich Himmler">Heinrich Himmler</a> ordered Leopold deported to Germany. Princess Lilian followed with the family in another car the following day under an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS" title="SS">SS</a> armed guard. The Nazis held the family in a fort at <a href="/wiki/Hirschstein" title="Hirschstein">Hirschstein</a> in <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a> from June 1944 to March 1945, and then at <a href="/wiki/Strobl" title="Strobl">Strobl</a>, Austria.</p>
<p>The British and American governments worried about the return of the king. <a href="/wiki/Charles_W._Sawyer" title="Charles W. Sawyer">Charles W. Sawyer</a>, US Ambassador to Belgium, warned his government that an immediate return by the king to Belgium would "precipitate serious difficulties". "There are deep differences even in the royal family and the situation holds dynamite for Belgium and perhaps for Europe".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> "The Foreign Office feared that an increasing minority in French-speaking Wallonia would demand either autonomy or annexation to France. Winant, the American Ambassador to the Court of Saint James, reported a Foreign Office official's concern regarding irredentist propaganda in Wallonia."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and that "the French Ambassador in Brussels... is believed to have connived in the spreading of this propaganda".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leopold and his companions were freed by members of the United States <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/106th_Cavalry_Group_(United_States)" title="106th Cavalry Group (United States)">106th Cavalry Group</a> in early May 1945. Because of the controversy about his conduct during the war, Leopold III and his wife and children were unable to return to Belgium and spent the next six years in exile at <a href="/wiki/Pregny-Chamb%C3%A9sy" title="Pregny-Chambésy">Pregny-Chambésy</a> near <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>, Switzerland. A regency under his brother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Charles_of_Belgium" title="Prince Charles of Belgium">Prince Charles</a> had been established by the Belgian legislature in 1944.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resistance_to_Leopold.27s_return">Resistance to Leopold's return</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leopold_III_of_Belgium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Resistance to Leopold's return">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Van den Dungen, the rector of the <a href="/wiki/Free_University_of_Brussels" title="Free University of Brussels">Free University of Brussels</a>, wrote to Leopold on 25 June 1945 about concerns for serious disorder in <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Wallonia</a>, "The question is not if the accusations against you are right or not [but that...] You are no more a symbol of the Belgian unity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gillon, the President of the Belgian Senate, told the king that there was a threat of serious disorder: "If there are only ten or twenty people killed, the situation would become terrible for the king."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The president of the Belgian Chamber of Representatives, <a href="/wiki/Frans_Van_Cauwelaert" title="Frans Van Cauwelaert">Frans Van Cauwelaert</a>, was concerned that there would be a <a href="/wiki/General_strike" title="General strike">general strike</a> in Wallonia and revolt in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge_(city)" title="Liège (city)">Liège</a>. He wrote, "The country is not able to put down the disorders because of the insufficient forces of the police and a lack of weapons."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1946, a commission of inquiry exonerated Leopold of treason. Nonetheless, controversy concerning his loyalty continued, and in 1950, a referendum was held about his future. Fifty-seven per cent of the voters favoured his return. The divide between Leopoldists and anti-Leopoldists ran along the lines of socialists and Walloons who were mostly opposed (42% favourable votes in Wallonia) and Christian Democrats and Flemish who were more in favour of the King (70% favourable votes in Flanders).</p>

<p>On his return to Belgium in 1950, Leopold was met with one of the most violent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_strike_against_Leopold_III_of_Belgium" title="General strike against Leopold III of Belgium">general strikes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Belgium" title="History of Belgium">history of Belgium</a>. Three protesters were killed when the <a href="/wiki/Gendarmerie" title="Gendarmerie">gendarmerie</a> opened automatic fire upon the protesters. The country stood on the brink of civil war, and <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Belgium" title="Flag of Belgium">Belgian banners</a> were replaced by <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Wallonia" title="Flag of Wallonia">Walloon flags</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge_(city)" title="Liège (city)">Liège</a> and other municipalities of <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Wallonia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> To avoid tearing the country apart, and to preserve the monarchy, Leopold decided on 1 August 1950 to withdraw in favour of his 20-year-old son <a href="/wiki/Baudouin_of_Belgium" title="Baudouin of Belgium">Baudouin</a>. His abdication took effect on 16 July 1951, though in reality the government had already forced the issue on 1 August 1950. In this postponed abdication<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> the king was, in effect, forced by the government of <a href="/wiki/Jean_Duvieusart" title="Jean Duvieusart">Jean Duvieusart</a> to offer to abdicate in favour of his son.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-abdication_life">Post-abdication life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leopold_III_of_Belgium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Post-abdication life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Leopold and his wife continued to advise King Baudouin until the latter's marriage in 1960. Some Belgian historians, such as Vincent Delcorps, speak of there having been a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyarchy" title="Dyarchy">dyarchy</a>" during this period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In retirement, he followed his passion as an amateur social <a href="/wiki/Anthropologist" title="Anthropologist">anthropologist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entomologist" title="Entomologist">entomologist</a> and travelled the world. He went to <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> and strongly criticized the French decolonization process,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Please clarify the following statement or statements with a good explanation from a reliable source. (July 2014)">how?</span></a></i>]</sup> and he explored the Orinoco and the Amazon with <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Harrer" title="Heinrich Harrer">Heinrich Harrer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leopold died in 1983 in <a href="/wiki/Woluwe-Saint-Lambert" title="Woluwe-Saint-Lambert">Woluwe-Saint-Lambert</a> (<i>Sint-Lambrechts-Woluwe</i>) following emergency heart surgery. He was interred next to Queen Astrid in the royal vault at the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Our_Lady_of_Laeken" title="Church of Our Lady of Laeken">Church of Our Lady of Laeken</a>. Leopold's second wife, the Princess de Réthy, was later interred with them.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_royal_descendants">Notable royal descendants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leopold_III_of_Belgium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Notable royal descendants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2017, two of Leopold's grandsons are reigning monarchs: Grand Duke <a href="/wiki/Henri,_Grand_Duke_of_Luxembourg" title="Henri, Grand Duke of Luxembourg">Henri of Luxembourg</a> since 2000, and King <a href="/wiki/Philippe_of_Belgium" title="Philippe of Belgium">Philippe of Belgium</a> since 2013.</p>
