<p><b>Léon Bakst</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Леон (Лев) Николаевич Бакст</span>, Leon (Lev) Nikolaevich Bakst) – born as <b>Leyb-Khaim Izrailevich</b> (later <b>Samoylovich</b>) <b>Rosenberg</b>, Лейб-Хаим Израилевич (Самойлович) Розенберг (27 January (8 February) 1866<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> – 28 December 1924) was a <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scenic_design" title="Scenic design">scene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Costume_design" title="Costume design">costume designer</a>. He was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Diaghilev" title="Sergei Diaghilev">Sergei Diaghilev</a> circle and the <a href="/wiki/Ballets_Russes" title="Ballets Russes">Ballets Russes</a>, for which he designed exotic, richly coloured <a href="/wiki/Set_construction" title="Set construction">sets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Costume" title="Costume">costumes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%A9on_Bakst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Leyb-Khaim Izrailevich (later Samoylovich) Rosenberg was born in <a href="/wiki/Grodno" title="Grodno">Grodno</a> (now in <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>), into a middle-class Jewish family. As his grandfather was an exceptional tailor, the Tsar gave him a very good position, and he had a huge and wonderful house in Saint Petersburg. Later, when Leyb's parents moved to the capital, the boy Leyb would visit his grandfather's house every Saturday. He said that he had been very impressed as a youth by that house, always returning with pleasure. At the young age of twelve, Lejb won a drawing contest and decided to become a painter, but his parents did not approve of this.</p>
<p>After graduating from <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)">gymnasium</a>, he studied at the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Academy_of_Arts" title="Imperial Academy of Arts">St. Petersburg Academy of Arts</a> as a noncredit student, because he had failed the entry. He also worked part-time as a book illustrator, gaining admission into the Imperial Academy in 1883.</p>
<p>At the time of his first exhibition (1889) he took the surname of "Bakst," based on his mother's maiden name. The surname "Rosenberg" was thought to be "too Jewish" and not good for business. At the beginning of the 1890s, Bakst exhibited his works with the Society of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watercolour" title="Watercolour">Watercolourists</a>. From 1893 to 1897 he lived in Paris, where he studied at the <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_Julian" title="Académie Julian">Académie Julian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He still often visited <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a>. After the mid-1890s, Bakst became a member of the circle of writers and artists formed by <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Diaghilev" title="Sergei Diaghilev">Sergei Diaghilev</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Benois" title="Alexandre Benois">Alexandre Benois</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-k-5">[5]</a></sup> who in 1899 founded the influential periodical <i><a href="/wiki/Mir_iskusstva" title="Mir iskusstva">Mir iskusstva</a>,</i> meaning "World of Art." His graphics for this publication brought him fame.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_to_fame">Rise to fame</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%A9on_Bakst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rise to fame">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bakst continued painting, producing portraits of <a href="/wiki/Filipp_Malyavin" title="Filipp Malyavin">Filipp Malyavin</a> (1899), <a href="/wiki/Vasily_Rozanov" title="Vasily Rozanov">Vasily Rozanov</a> (1901), <a href="/wiki/Andrei_Bely" title="Andrei Bely">Andrei Bely</a> (1905), <a href="/wiki/Zinaida_Gippius" title="Zinaida Gippius">Zinaida Gippius</a> (1906). He also worked as an art teacher for the children of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duke_Vladimir_Alexandrovich_of_Russia" title="Grand Duke Vladimir Alexandrovich of Russia">Grand Duke Vladimir Alexandrovich of Russia</a>. In 1902, he took a commission from <a href="/wiki/Tsar" title="Tsar">Tsar</a> <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_II_of_Russia" title="Nicholas II of Russia">Nicholas II</a> to paint <i>Admiral Avellan and Russian sailors meeting</i> in Paris, a painting he started there, during the celebrations from the 17th to the 25th of October 1893. It took him 8 years to finish this work. , but that he only finished after 8 years.</p>
<p>In 1898, he showed his works in the Diaghilev-organized <i>First Exhibition of Russian and Finnish Artists</i>; in <i>World of Art</i> exhibitions, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sezession" title="Sezession">Secession</a>,</i> exhibitions of the <i>Union of Russian Artists</i>, etc.</p>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_of_1905" title="Russian Revolution of 1905">Russian Revolution of 1905</a>, Bakst worked for the magazines <i>Zhupel</i>, <i>Adskaja Pochta</i>, and <i>Satyricon</i>, then for an art magazine called <i>Apollon.</i></p>
<p>Beginning in 1909, Bakst worked mostly as a stage-designer, designing <a href="/wiki/Set_construction" title="Set construction">sets</a> for <a href="/wiki/Greek_tragedy" title="Greek tragedy">Greek tragedies</a>. In 1908, he gained attention as a scene-painter for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diaghilev" title="Diaghilev">Diaghilev</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Ballets_Russes" title="Ballets Russes">Ballets Russes</a>. He produced scenery for <i>Cleopatra</i> (1909), <i><a href="/wiki/Scheherazade_(Rimsky-Korsakov)" title="Scheherazade (Rimsky-Korsakov)">Scheherazade</a></i> (1910), <i><a href="/wiki/Carnaval_(Schumann)" title="Carnaval (Schumann)">Carnaval</a></i> (1910), <i>Narcisse</i> (1911), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_Spectre_de_la_Rose" title="Le Spectre de la Rose">Le Spectre de la Rose</a></i> (1911), <i><a href="/wiki/Afternoon_of_a_Faun_(Nijinsky)" title="Afternoon of a Faun (Nijinsky)">L'après-midi d'un faune</a></i> (1912) and <i><a href="/wiki/Daphnis_et_Chlo%C3%A9" title="Daphnis et Chloé">Daphnis et Chloé</a></i> (1912).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Remsen_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Remsen-6">[6]</a></sup> During this time, Bakst lived in western Europe because, as a Jew, he did not have the right to live permanently outside the <a href="/wiki/Pale_of_Settlement" title="Pale of Settlement">Pale of Settlement</a> in the Russian Empire.</p>
<p>During his visits to Saint Petersburg, he taught in <i>Zvantseva's school,</i> where one of his students was <a href="/wiki/Marc_Chagall" title="Marc Chagall">Marc Chagall</a> (1908–1910). Bakst described Chagall as a favorite, because when told to do something, he would listen carefully, but then he would take his paint and his brushes and do something completely different from the assignment.</p>
<p>In 1914, Bakst was elected a member of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Academy_of_Arts" title="Imperial Academy of Arts">Imperial Academy of Arts</a>.</p>
<p>In 1922, Bakst broke off his relationship with Diaghilev and the Ballets Russes. During this year, he visited <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a> and, specifically <a href="/wiki/Evergreen_Museum_%26_Library" title="Evergreen Museum &amp; Library">Evergreen House</a> - the residence of his American friend and patron, art philanthropist Alice Warder Garrett (1877–1952). Having met in Paris in 1914, when Mrs. Garrett was accompanying her diplomat husband in Europe, Bakst soon depended upon Garrett as both a confidante and agent. Alice Garrett became Bakst's representative in the United States upon her return home in 1920, organizing two exhibitions of the artist's work at New York's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knoedler_Gallery" title="Knoedler Gallery">Knoedler Gallery</a>, as well as subsequent traveling shows. When in Baltimore, Bakst re-designed the dining room of Evergreen into a shocking acidic yellow and 'Chinese' red confection. The artist transformed the house's small c. 1885 gymnasium into a colorfully Modernist private theatre. This is believed to be the only extant private theatre designed by Bakst.</p>
<p>Bakst died on the 27th of December 1924, in a clinic in Rueil Malmaison, near Paris, from <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lung</a> problems (<a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_edema" title="Pulmonary edema">oedema</a>). His many admirers amongst the most famous artists of the time, poets, musicians, dancers and critiques, formed a funeral procession to accompany his body to his final resting place, in the Cimetière des Batignolles, in Paris, during a very moving ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-k-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">Victoria and Albert Museum</a> in London presented an exhibit of Bakst's costumes and prints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
