<p><b>Levi</b> (or <b>Levy</b>) (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he" xml:lang="he">לֵּוִי</span>‎‎; <small><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_Hebrew_language" title="Modern Hebrew language">Standard</a></small> <i>Levy</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Tiberian_vocalization" title="Tiberian vocalization">Tiberian</a></small> <i>Lēwî</i>) was, according to the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Book of Genesis</a>, the third son of <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">Jacob</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leah" title="Leah">Leah</a>, and the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Israelites" title="Israelites">Israelite Tribe</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Levites" title="Levites">Levi</a> (the <i>Levites</i>). Certain religious and political functions were reserved for the Levites.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> suggests that the name of <i>Levi</i> refers to Leah's hope for Jacob to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coitus" title="Coitus">join</a></i> with her, implying a derivation from <i>yillaweh</i>, meaning <i>he will join</i>, but some <a href="/wiki/Biblical_criticism" title="Biblical criticism">Biblical scholars</a> have proposed quite different origins of the name. These scholars suspect that it may simply mean <i>priest</i>, either as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loan_word" title="Loan word">loan word</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Minaeans" title="Minaeans">Minaean</a> <i>lawi'u</i>, meaning <i>priest</i>, or by referring to those people who were <i>joined</i> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ark_of_the_covenant" title="Ark of the covenant">ark of the covenant</a>. Another possibility is that the Levites originated as migrants, and that the name Levites indicates their <i>joining</i> with either the Israelites in general, or the earlier Israelite priesthood in particular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jewish_Encyclopedia_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jewish_Encyclopedia-1">[1]</a></sup> In the Book of Jubilees 28:14-15; it says that Levi was born "in the new moon of the first month" which means that he was born on 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nissan_(Hebrew_month)" title="Nissan (Hebrew month)">Nissan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Levi_and_the_.22Blessing_of_Jacob.22">Levi and the "Blessing of Jacob"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title='Edit section: Levi and the "Blessing of Jacob"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the narratives found in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Book of Genesis</a>, Levi’s youth is marked by impetuosity. He and his brother, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simeon_(Hebrew_Bible)" title="Simeon (Hebrew Bible)">Simeon</a>, destroy the city of <a href="/wiki/Shechem" title="Shechem">Shechem</a> in revenge for the rape of <a href="/wiki/Dinah" title="Dinah">Dinah</a>, seizing the wealth of the city and killing the men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The brothers had earlier misled the inhabitants of Shechem by consenting to Dinah's rapist marrying her, and when Jacob hears about their destruction of Shechem, he castigates them for it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Blessing_of_Jacob" title="Blessing of Jacob">Blessing of Jacob</a>, Jacob is described as imposing a curse on the Levites, by which they would be scattered, in punishment for Levi's actions in Shechem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Some textual scholars date the <i>Blessing of Jacob</i> to a period between just one and two centuries prior to the <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_captivity" title="Babylonian captivity">Babylonian captivity</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wwb_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-wwb-6">[6]</a></sup> and some Biblical scholars regard this <i>curse</i>, and Dinah herself as an aetiological postdiction to explain the fates of the tribe of Simeon and the Levites, with one possible explanation of the Levites' scattered nature being that the priesthood was originally open to any tribe, but gradually became seen as a distinct tribe itself (the Levites).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jewish_Encyclopedia_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jewish_Encyclopedia-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_family_of_Levi">The family of Levi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The family of Levi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Book of Genesis</a>, Levi is described as having fathered three sons—<a href="/wiki/Gershon" title="Gershon">Gershon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kohath" title="Kohath">Kohath</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Merari" title="Merari">Merari</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> A similar genealogy is given in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Exodus" title="Book of Exodus">Book of Exodus</a>, where it is added that among Kohath's sons was one—<a href="/wiki/Amram" title="Amram">Amram</a>—who married a woman named <a href="/wiki/Jochebed" title="Jochebed">Jochebed</a>, who was closely related to his father, and they were the biological parents of <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aaron" title="Aaron">Aaron</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Miriam" title="Miriam">Miriam</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> though some Greek and Latin manuscripts of the Torah state that Jochebed was Amram's father's cousin, the <a href="/wiki/Masoretic_Text" title="Masoretic Text">Masoretic Text</a> states that she was his father's sister,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a> mentions that she was <i>one of</i> his <i>father's sisters</i>. The Masoretic Text's version of Levi's genealogy thus implies (and in Numbers 26:59, explicitly states) that Levi also had a daughter (Jochebed), and the Septuagint implies further daughters. The names of Levi's sons, and possible daughter, are interpreted in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_rabbinical_literature" title="Classical rabbinical literature">classical rabbinical literature</a> as being reflections on their future destiny.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In some <a href="/wiki/Apocrypha" title="Apocrypha">apocryphal</a> texts such as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Testament_of_Levi" title="Testament of Levi">Testament of Levi</a></i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Jubilees" title="Book of Jubilees">Book of Jubilees</a>, Levi's wife, his children's mother, is named as <i>Milkah</i>, a daughter of <a href="/wiki/Aram,_son_of_Shem" title="Aram, son of Shem">Aram</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some scholars attribute the genealogy to a hypothetical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_of_Generations" title="Book of Generations">Book of Generations</a>, a document originating from a similar <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/religiopolitical" title="wiktionary:religiopolitical">religiopolitical</a> group and date to the <a href="/wiki/Priestly_source" title="Priestly source">priestly source</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wwb_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-wwb-6">[6]</a></sup> According to some Biblical scholars, the Torah's genealogy for Levi's descendants, is actually an aetiological myth reflecting the fact that there were four different groups among the levites – the Gershonites, <a href="/wiki/Kohathites" title="Kohathites">Kohathites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merarites" title="Merarites">Merarites</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aaronids&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aaronids (page does not exist)">Aaronids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-13">[13]</a></sup> Aaron—the eponymous ancestor of the Aaronids—couldn't be portrayed as a brother to Gershon, Kohath, and Merari, as<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="why so? (July 2014)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the narrative about the birth of Moses (brother of Aaron), which textual scholars attribute to the earlier <a href="/wiki/Elohist" title="Elohist">Elohist</a> source, mentions only that both his parents were Levites (without identifying their names).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Some Biblical scholars suspect that the Elohist account offers both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matrilinear_descent" title="Matrilinear descent">matrilinial</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patrilinear_descent" title="Patrilinear descent">patrilinial</a> descent from Levites in order to magnify the religious credentials of Moses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_post-Torah_tradition">In post-Torah tradition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In post-Torah tradition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Levi_by_V.Osipov.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1028" data-file-width="356" height="491" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Levi_by_V.Osipov.jpg/170px-Levi_by_V.Osipov.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Levi_by_V.Osipov.jpg/255px-Levi_by_V.Osipov.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Levi_by_V.Osipov.jpg/340px-Levi_by_V.Osipov.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Levi_by_V.Osipov.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>In accordance with his role as founder of the Levites, Levi is referred to as being particularly <a href="/wiki/Piety" title="Piety">pious</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Blessing_of_Moses" title="Blessing of Moses">Blessing of Moses</a>, which some textual scholars attribute to a period just before the <a href="/wiki/Deuteronomist" title="Deuteronomist">deuteronomist</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wwb_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-wwb-6">[6]</a></sup> speaks about Levi via an <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegorical</a> comparison to <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> himself,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hagaddah" title="Hagaddah">hagaddah</a> take to support the characterisation of Levi (and his progeny) as being by far the greatest of his brothers in respect to piety. The <a href="/wiki/Apocrypha" title="Apocrypha">apocryphal</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Joseph_and_Aseneth" title="Joseph and Aseneth">Prayer of Asenath</a></i>, which textual scholars believe dates from some time after the first century AD (scholarship in regards to the dating is currently quite contentious, with dates ranging from near the first century, to the fourth or fifth centuries),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jewish_Encyclopedia_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jewish_Encyclopedia-1">[1]</a></sup> describes Levi as a prophet and saint, able to forecast the future, understand <i>heavenly writings</i> (<a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">astrology</a>? <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> trends?), and someone who admonishes the people to be forgiving, as well as in awe of <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Malachi" title="Book of Malachi">Book of Malachi</a> argues that the Levites were chosen by <a href="/wiki/Yahweh" title="Yahweh">Yahweh</a> to be the priests, because Levi was always accurate, having never lied, specified only the true religious regulations, was reverent, revered Yahweh, was in awe of the <a href="/wiki/Tetragrammaton" title="Tetragrammaton">Tetragrammaton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pacifism" title="Pacifism">upheld peace</a>, was a model of good <a href="/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">morality</a>, and turned many people from sin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Testament_of_Levi" title="Testament of Levi">Testament of Levi</a></i>, Levi is described as having had two visions. The first vision covered <a href="/wiki/Jewish_eschatology" title="Jewish eschatology">eschatological</a> issues, portraying the seven heavens, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_Messiah" title="Jewish Messiah">Jewish Messiah</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Last_Judgment" title="Last Judgment">Judgement Day</a>. The second vision portrays seven angels bringing Levi seven insignia signifying priesthood, prophecy, and judgement; in the vision, after the angels anoint Levi, and initiate him as a priest, they tell him of the future of his descendants, mentioning <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a>, the Aaronid priesthood, and a time when there would be priest-kings; this latter point was of particular interest to the <a href="/wiki/Maccabees" title="Maccabees">Maccabean</a> period of <a href="/wiki/John_Hyrcanus" title="John Hyrcanus">John Hyrcanus</a>, who was both a high priest, and warrior-king. According to some textual scholars this is to be expected, since the Testament of Levi is believed to have been written during Maccabean rule, between 153BC and 107BC, and closer to the latter date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Jubilees" title="Book of Jubilees">Book of Jubilees</a> similarly has Isaac telling Levi of the future of his descendants, again predicting priesthood, prophets, and political power,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and additionally describes Jacob as entrusting Levi with <i>the secrets of the ancients</i>, so that they would be known only to the Levites;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> however, like the Testament of Levi, the Book of Jubilees is regarded as a Maccabean-era document by some scholars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jewish_Encyclopedia_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jewish_Encyclopedia-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
