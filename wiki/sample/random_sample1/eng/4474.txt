<p><b>Raynaud syndrome</b>, also known as <b>Raynaud's</b>, is a medical condition in which <a href="/wiki/Vasospasm" title="Vasospasm">spasm of arteries</a> cause episodes of reduced blood flow. Typically the fingers, and less commonly the toes, are involved. Rarely, the nose, ears, or lips are affected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2014What_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2014What-1">[1]</a></sup> The episodes result in the affected part turning <a href="/wiki/Pallor" title="Pallor">white</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Cyanosis" title="Cyanosis">blue</a>. Often there is numbness or pain. As blood flow returns, the area turns red and burns. The episodes typically last minutes but can last up to several hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2014Sym_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2014Sym-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Episodes are often triggered by cold or emotional stress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2014Sym_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2014Sym-2">[2]</a></sup> There are two main types: primary Raynaud's when the cause is unknown and secondary Raynaud's which occurs as a result of another condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM2016_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NEJM2016-3">[3]</a></sup> Secondary Raynaud's can occur due a <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue_disease" title="Connective tissue disease">connective tissue disorder</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Scleroderma" title="Scleroderma">scleroderma</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lupus" title="Lupus">lupus</a>, injuries to the hands, smoking, and certain medications such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birth_control_pills" title="Birth control pills">birth control pills</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Diagnosis is typically based on the symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM2016_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NEJM2016-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The primary treatment is avoiding the cold. Other measures include the discontinuation of nicotine or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stimulants" title="Stimulants">stimulants</a> use. Medications for treatment of cases that do not improve include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calcium_channel_blockers" title="Calcium channel blockers">calcium channel blockers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iloprost" title="Iloprost">iloprost</a>. Little evidence supports <a href="/wiki/Alternative_medicine" title="Alternative medicine">alternative medicine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM2016_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-NEJM2016-3">[3]</a></sup> Severe disease may rarely be complicated by <a href="/wiki/Ulcer_(dermatology)" title="Ulcer (dermatology)">skin sores</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ischemic_gangrene" title="Ischemic gangrene">gangrene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2014Sym_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2014Sym-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>About 4% of people have the condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM2016_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-NEJM2016-3">[3]</a></sup> Onset of the primary form is typically between 15 and 30 and occurs more frequently in females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM2016_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-NEJM2016-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2014Epi_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2014Epi-5">[5]</a></sup> The secondary form usually affects older people. Both forms are more common in cold <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2014Epi_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2014Epi-5">[5]</a></sup> It is named after French physician <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Raynaud" title="Maurice Raynaud">Maurice Raynaud</a> who described the condition in 1862.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM2016_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-NEJM2016-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raynaud_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The condition can cause pain within the affected extremities, discoloration (paleness), and sensations of cold and/or numbness. This can often be distressing to those who are not diagnosed, and sometimes it can be obstructive. If someone with Raynaud's is placed into a cold climate, it could potentially become dangerous.</p>
<ol>
<li>When exposed to cold temperatures, the blood supply to the fingers or toes, and in some cases the nose or earlobes, is markedly reduced; the skin turns pale or white (called <a href="/wiki/Pallor" title="Pallor">pallor</a>) and becomes cold and numb.</li>
<li>When the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> supply is depleted, the skin color turns blue (called <a href="/wiki/Cyanosis" title="Cyanosis">cyanosis</a>).</li>
<li>These events are episodic, and when the episode subsides or the area is warmed, the blood flow returns, and the skin color first turns red (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubor" title="Rubor">rubor</a>), and then back to normal, often accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Swelling_(medical)" title="Swelling (medical)">swelling</a>, tingling, and a painful "pins and needles" sensation.</li>
</ol>
<p>All three color changes are observed in classic Raynaud's. However, not all patients see all of the aforementioned color changes in all episodes, especially in milder cases of the condition. Symptoms are thought to be due to reactive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperemia" title="Hyperemia">hyperemias</a> of the areas deprived of blood flow.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a>, this sign normally disappears owing to increased surface blood flow. Raynaud's has also occurred in breastfeeding mothers, causing nipples to turn white and become extremely painful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nifedipine" title="Nifedipine">Nifedipine</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Calcium_channel_blocker" title="Calcium channel blocker">calcium channel blocker</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vasodilator" title="Vasodilator">vasodilator</a>, was recommended to increase blood flow to the extremities and noticeably relieved pain in the breast in an extremely small study group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">Cause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raynaud_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary">Primary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raynaud_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Primary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Raynaud's disease, or "Primary Raynaud's", is diagnosed if the symptoms are <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idiopathic" title="Idiopathic">idiopathic</a></i>, that is, if they occur by themselves and not in association with other diseases. Some refer to Primary Raynaud's disease as "being allergic to coldness". It often develops in young women in their teens and early adulthood. Primary Raynaud's is thought to be at least partly <a href="/wiki/Heredity" title="Heredity">hereditary</a>, although specific genes have not yet been identified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16609626_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16609626-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoking" title="Tobacco smoking">Smoking</a> increases frequency and intensity of attacks, and there is a hormonal component. <a href="/wiki/Caffeine" title="Caffeine">Caffeine</a> also worsens the attacks. Sufferers are more likely to have <a href="/wiki/Migraine" title="Migraine">migraines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Angina_pectoris" title="Angina pectoris">angina</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secondary">Secondary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raynaud_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Secondary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Raynaud's phenomenon, or "Secondary Raynaud's", occurs <i>secondary to</i> a wide variety of other conditions.</p>
<p>Secondary Raynaud's has a number of associations:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Connective tissue disorders</b>:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Scleroderma" title="Scleroderma">scleroderma</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17218139_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17218139-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Systemic_lupus_erythematosus" title="Systemic lupus erythematosus">systemic lupus erythematosus</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rheumatoid_arthritis" title="Rheumatoid arthritis">rheumatoid arthritis</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sj%C3%B6gren%27s_syndrome" title="Sjögren's syndrome">Sjögren's syndrome</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dermatomyositis" title="Dermatomyositis">dermatomyositis</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Polymyositis" title="Polymyositis">polymyositis</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mixed_connective_tissue_disease" title="Mixed connective tissue disease">mixed connective tissue disease</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cold_agglutinin_disease" title="Cold agglutinin disease">cold agglutinin disease</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ehlers-Danlos_syndrome" title="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome">Ehlers-Danlos syndrome</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Eating disorders</b>:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anorexia_nervosa" title="Anorexia nervosa">anorexia nervosa</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Obstructive disorders</b>:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Atherosclerosis" title="Atherosclerosis">atherosclerosis</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buerger%27s_disease" title="Buerger's disease">Buerger's disease</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Takayasu%27s_arteritis" title="Takayasu's arteritis">Takayasu's arteritis</a></li>
<li>subclavian <a href="/wiki/Aneurysm" title="Aneurysm">aneurysms</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thoracic_outlet_syndrome" title="Thoracic outlet syndrome">thoracic outlet syndrome</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Drugs</b>:
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta-blocker" title="Beta-blocker">beta-blockers</a></li>
<li>cytotoxic drugs – particularly <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">chemotherapeutics</a> and most especially <a href="/wiki/Bleomycin" title="Bleomycin">bleomycin</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ciclosporin" title="Ciclosporin">ciclosporin</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bromocriptine" title="Bromocriptine">bromocriptine</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ergotamine" title="Ergotamine">ergotamine</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sulfasalazine" title="Sulfasalazine">sulfasalazine</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anthrax_vaccines" title="Anthrax vaccines">anthrax vaccines</a> whose primary ingredient is the Anthrax Protective Antigen</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CNS_stimulant" title="CNS stimulant">stimulant medications</a> such as those used to treat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ADHD" title="ADHD">ADHD</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>OTC psedoephedrine medications (Chlor-Trimeton, Sudafed, others)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Occupation</b>:
<ul>
<li>jobs involving vibration, particularly drilling and prolonged use of a <a href="/wiki/String_trimmer" title="String trimmer">String trimmer</a> (weed whacker), suffer from <a href="/wiki/Vibration_white_finger" title="Vibration white finger">vibration white finger</a></li>
<li>exposure to <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_chloride" title="Vinyl chloride">vinyl chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a></li>
<li>exposure to the cold (e.g., by working as a frozen food packer)</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Others</b>:
<ul>
<li>physical trauma, such as that sustained in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auto_accident" title="Auto accident">auto accidents</a> or other traumatic events</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lyme_disease" title="Lyme disease">Lyme disease</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hypothyroidism" title="Hypothyroidism">hypothyroidism</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cryoglobulinemia" title="Cryoglobulinemia">cryoglobulinemia</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">malignancy</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chronic_fatigue_syndrome" title="Chronic fatigue syndrome">chronic fatigue syndrome</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Complex_regional_pain_syndrome" title="Complex regional pain syndrome">reflex sympathetic dystrophy</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carpal_tunnel_syndrome" title="Carpal tunnel syndrome">carpal tunnel syndrome</a></li>
<li>magnesium deficiency</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Multiple_sclerosis" title="Multiple sclerosis">multiple sclerosis</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Erythromelalgia" title="Erythromelalgia">erythromelalgia</a> (clinically presenting as the opposite of Raynaud's, with hot and warm extremities) often co-exists in patients with Raynaud's<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>)</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Raynaud's can <i>herald</i> these diseases by periods of more than twenty years in some cases, making it effectively their first presenting symptom. This may be the case in the <a href="/wiki/CREST_syndrome" title="CREST syndrome">CREST syndrome</a>, of which Raynaud's is a part.</p>
<p>Patients with Secondary Raynaud's can also have symptoms related to their underlying diseases. Raynaud's phenomenon is the initial symptom that presents for 70% of patients with <a href="/wiki/Scleroderma" title="Scleroderma">scleroderma</a>, a skin and joint disease.</p>
<p>When Raynaud's phenomenon is limited to one hand or one foot, it is referred to as Unilateral Raynaud's. This is an uncommon form, and it is always secondary to local or regional vascular disease. It commonly progresses within several years to affect other limbs as the vascular disease progresses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raynaud_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Its pathophysiology includes hyperactivation of the <a href="/wiki/Sympathetic_nervous_system" title="Sympathetic nervous system">sympathetic nervous system</a> causing extreme <a href="/wiki/Vasoconstriction" title="Vasoconstriction">vasoconstriction</a> of the peripheral blood vessels, leading to tissue <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">hypoxia</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raynaud_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_thermal_image_demonstrating_the_loss_of_heat_in_a_Reynaud%27s_sufferer.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="347" data-file-width="323" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/A_thermal_image_demonstrating_the_loss_of_heat_in_a_Reynaud%27s_sufferer.jpg/220px-A_thermal_image_demonstrating_the_loss_of_heat_in_a_Reynaud%27s_sufferer.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8b/A_thermal_image_demonstrating_the_loss_of_heat_in_a_Reynaud%27s_sufferer.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_thermal_image_demonstrating_the_loss_of_heat_in_a_Reynaud%27s_sufferer.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Heat signatures of a Raynaud's hand (top) and a healthy hand (bottom). Red indicates warm areas whilst green indicates cool areas. Image taken with a thermographic camera.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Consensus_Diagnostic_Criteria_Raynaud%27s_Phenomenon.pdf"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1039" data-file-width="727" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Consensus_Diagnostic_Criteria_Raynaud%27s_Phenomenon.pdf/page1-220px-Consensus_Diagnostic_Criteria_Raynaud%27s_Phenomenon.pdf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Consensus_Diagnostic_Criteria_Raynaud%27s_Phenomenon.pdf/page1-330px-Consensus_Diagnostic_Criteria_Raynaud%27s_Phenomenon.pdf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Consensus_Diagnostic_Criteria_Raynaud%27s_Phenomenon.pdf/page1-440px-Consensus_Diagnostic_Criteria_Raynaud%27s_Phenomenon.pdf.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Consensus_Diagnostic_Criteria_Raynaud%27s_Phenomenon.pdf" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Consensus diagnostic criteria Raynaud's phenomenon</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It is important to distinguish Raynaud's <i>disease</i> (primary Raynaud's) from <i>phenomenon</i> (secondary Raynaud's). Looking for signs of <a href="/wiki/Arthritis" title="Arthritis">arthritis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vasculitis" title="Vasculitis">vasculitis</a> as well as a number of laboratory tests may separate them. If suspected to be secondary to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_sclerosis" title="Systemic sclerosis">systemic sclerosis</a>, one tool which may help aid in the prediction of systemic sclerosis is thermography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17018538_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17018538-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>A careful medical history will often reveal whether the condition is primary or secondary. Once this has been established, an examination is largely to identify or exclude possible secondary causes.</p>
<ul>
<li>Digital artery pressure: pressures are measured in the arteries of the fingers before and after the hands have been cooled. A decrease of at least 15 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MmHg" title="MmHg">mmHg</a> is diagnostic (positive).</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_ultrasound" title="Doppler ultrasound">Doppler ultrasound</a>: to assess blood flow.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_blood_count" title="Full blood count">Full blood count</a>: this may reveal a normocytic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaemia" title="Anaemia">anaemia</a> suggesting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaemia_of_chronic_disease" title="Anaemia of chronic disease">anaemia of chronic disease</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal_failure" title="Renal failure">renal failure</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blood_test" title="Blood test">Blood test</a> for <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytes</a>: this may reveal renal impairment.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thyroid_function_tests" title="Thyroid function tests">Thyroid function tests</a>: this may reveal <a href="/wiki/Hypothyroidism" title="Hypothyroidism">hypothyroidism</a>.</li>
<li>An <a href="/wiki/Autoantibody" title="Autoantibody">autoantibody</a> screen, tests for <a href="/wiki/Rheumatoid_factor" title="Rheumatoid factor">rheumatoid factor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erythrocyte_sedimentation_rate" title="Erythrocyte sedimentation rate">Erythrocyte sedimentation rate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/C-reactive_protein" title="C-reactive protein">C-reactive protein</a>, which may reveal specific causative illnesses or a generalised inflammatory process.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nailfold_capillaroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nailfold capillaroscopy (page does not exist)">Nail fold vasculature</a>: this can be examined under the <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">microscope</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>To aid in the diagnosis of Raynaud's phenomenon, multiple sets of diagnostic criteria have been proposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Table 1 below provides a summary of these various diagnostic criteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maverakis_E.2C_Patel_F.2C_Kronenberg_D_60.E2.80.9365_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maverakis_E.2C_Patel_F.2C_Kronenberg_D_60.E2.80.9365-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recently, International Consensus Criteria were developed for the diagnosis of primary Raynaud's phenomenon by a panel of multiple experts in the fields of rheumatology and dermatology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maverakis_E.2C_Patel_F.2C_Kronenberg_D_60.E2.80.9365_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Maverakis_E.2C_Patel_F.2C_Kronenberg_D_60.E2.80.9365-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raynaud_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Secondary Raynaud's is managed primarily by treating the underlying cause and as primary Raynaud's, avoiding triggers, such as cold, emotional and environmental stress, vibrations and repetitive motions, and avoiding smoking (including passive smoking) and <a href="/wiki/Sympathomimetic_drug" title="Sympathomimetic drug">sympathomimetic drugs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mikuls_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mikuls-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medications">Medications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raynaud_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Medications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Medications can be helpful for moderate or severe RP.</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vasodilator" title="Vasodilator">Vasodilators</a> – <a href="/wiki/Calcium_channel_blocker" title="Calcium channel blocker">calcium channel blockers</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Dihydropyridine" title="Dihydropyridine">dihydropyridines</a> <a href="/wiki/Nifedipine" title="Nifedipine">nifedipine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amlodipine" title="Amlodipine">amlodipine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Diltiazem" title="Diltiazem">diltiazem</a>, preferably slow release preparations – are often first line treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mikuls_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mikuls-20">[20]</a></sup> They have the common side effects of headache, flushing, and ankle <a href="/wiki/Edema" title="Edema">edema</a>; but these are not typically of sufficient severity to require cessation of treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid3881908_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid3881908-21">[21]</a></sup> The limited evidence available shows that calcium channel blockers are only slightly effective in reducing how often the attacks happen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid24482037_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid24482037-22">[22]</a></sup> Peoples whose RP is secondary to <a href="/wiki/Erythromelalgia" title="Erythromelalgia">erythromelalgia</a> often cannot use vasodilators for therapy as they trigger 'flares' causing the extremities to become burning red due to there being too much blood.</li>
<li>People with severe RP prone to ulceration or large artery thrombotic events may be prescribed aspirin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mikuls_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mikuls-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sympatholytic" title="Sympatholytic">Sympatholytic</a> agents, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha-adrenergic_blocker" title="Alpha-adrenergic blocker">alpha-adrenergic blocker</a> <a href="/wiki/Prazosin" title="Prazosin">prazosin</a> may provide temporary relief.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mikuls_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-Mikuls-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Losartan" title="Losartan">Losartan</a> can, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Topical_nitrate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Topical nitrate (page does not exist)">topical nitrates</a> may, reduce the severity and frequency of attacks, and the <a href="/wiki/Phosphodiesterase_inhibitor" title="Phosphodiesterase inhibitor">phosphodiesterase inhibitors</a> <a href="/wiki/Sildenafil" title="Sildenafil">sildenafil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tadalafil" title="Tadalafil">tadalafil</a> may reduce their severity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mikuls_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-Mikuls-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiotensin_receptor_blocker" title="Angiotensin receptor blocker">Angiotensin receptor blockers</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiotensin-converting_enzyme_inhibitor" title="Angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitor">ACE inhibitors</a> may aid blood flow to the fingers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mikuls_20-5"><a href="#cite_note-Mikuls-20">[20]</a></sup> and there is some evidence that angiotensin receptor blockers (often <a href="/wiki/Losartan" title="Losartan">losartan</a>) reduce frequency and severity of attacks,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid9408065_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9408065-23">[23]</a></sup> and possibly better than nifedipine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10616013_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10616013-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid762741_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid762741-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Prostaglandin" title="Prostaglandin">prostaglandin</a> <a href="/wiki/Iloprost" title="Iloprost">iloprost</a> is used to manage critical ischemia and <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_hypertension" title="Pulmonary hypertension">pulmonary hypertension</a> in RP, and the <a href="/wiki/Endothelin_receptor_antagonist" title="Endothelin receptor antagonist">endothelin receptor antagonist</a> <a href="/wiki/Bosentan" title="Bosentan">bosentan</a> is used to manage severe pulmonary hypotension and prevent finger ulcers in <a href="/wiki/Scleroderma" title="Scleroderma">scleroderma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mikuls_20-6"><a href="#cite_note-Mikuls-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Statin" title="Statin">Statins</a> have a protective effect on blood vessels, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SSRI" title="SSRI">SSRIs</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Fluoxetine" title="Fluoxetine">fluoxetine</a> may help RP symptoms but the data is weak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mikuls_20-7"><a href="#cite_note-Mikuls-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surgery">Surgery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raynaud_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Surgery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>In severe cases, a <a href="/wiki/Endoscopic_thoracic_sympathectomy" title="Endoscopic thoracic sympathectomy">Endoscopic thoracic sympathectomy</a> (ETS) procedure can be performed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17098681_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17098681-26">[26]</a></sup> Here, the nerves that signal the blood vessels of the fingertips to constrict are surgically cut. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microvascular_surgery" title="Microvascular surgery">Microvascular surgery</a> of the affected areas is another possible therapy. This procedure should be considered as a last resort however.</li>
<li>Infusions of <a href="/wiki/Prostaglandin" title="Prostaglandin">prostaglandins</a>, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Prostacyclin" title="Prostacyclin">prostacyclin</a>, may be tried, with <a href="/wiki/Amputation" title="Amputation">amputation</a> in exceptionally severe cases.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>A more recent treatment for severe Raynaud's is the use of <a href="/wiki/Botulinum_toxin" title="Botulinum toxin">botulinum toxin</a>. The 2009 article<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> studied 19 patients ranging in age from 15 to 72 years with severe Raynaud's phenomenon of which 16 patients (84%) reported pain reduction at rest. 13 patients reported immediate pain relief, 3 more had gradual pain reduction over 1–2 months. All 13 patients with chronic finger ulcers healed within 60 days. Only 21% of the patients required repeated injections. A 2007 article<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> describes similar improvement in a series of 11 patients. All patients had significant relief of pain.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_medicine">Alternative medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raynaud_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Alternative medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Evidence does not support the use of alternative medicine including <a href="/wiki/Acupuncture" title="Acupuncture">acupuncture</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laser_therapy" title="Laser therapy">laser therapy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM2016_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-NEJM2016-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
