<p><b>Leiden</b> (<small>Dutch pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Dutch" title="Help:IPA for Dutch">[ˈlɛi̯.də(n)]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:Nl-Leiden.ogg" title="File:Nl-Leiden.ogg"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/79/Nl-Leiden.ogg" title="Nl-Leiden.ogg">listen</a>)</small>; in English and <a href="/wiki/Archaism" title="Archaism">archaic</a> <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> also <b>Leyden</b>) is a city and municipality in the Dutch province of <a href="/wiki/South_Holland" title="South Holland">South Holland</a>. The municipality of Leiden has a population of 122,915, but the city forms one densely connected agglomeration with its suburbs <a href="/wiki/Oegstgeest" title="Oegstgeest">Oegstgeest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leiderdorp" title="Leiderdorp">Leiderdorp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Voorschoten" title="Voorschoten">Voorschoten</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zoeterwoude" title="Zoeterwoude">Zoeterwoude</a> with 206,647 inhabitants. The Netherlands Central Bureau of Statistics (CBS) further includes <a href="/wiki/Katwijk" title="Katwijk">Katwijk</a> in the agglomeration which makes the total population of the Leiden urban agglomeration 270,879, and in the larger Leiden urban area also <a href="/wiki/Teylingen" title="Teylingen">Teylingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Noordwijk" title="Noordwijk">Noordwijk</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Noordwijkerhout" title="Noordwijkerhout">Noordwijkerhout</a> are included with in total 348,868 inhabitants. Leiden is located on the <a href="/wiki/Oude_Rijn_(Utrecht_and_South_Holland)" title="Oude Rijn (Utrecht and South Holland)">Oude Rijn</a>, at a distance of some 20 kilometres (12 miles) from <a href="/wiki/The_Hague" title="The Hague">The Hague</a> to its south and some 40 km (25 mi) from <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a> to its north. The recreational area of the Kaag Lakes (<a href="/wiki/Kagerplassen" title="Kagerplassen">Kagerplassen</a>) lies just to the northeast of Leiden.</p>
<p>A university city since 1575, Leiden houses <a href="/wiki/Leiden_University" title="Leiden University">Leiden University</a>, the oldest university of the Netherlands, and <a href="/wiki/Leiden_University_Medical_Center" title="Leiden University Medical Center">Leiden University Medical Center</a>. Leiden is a city with a rich cultural heritage, not only in science, but also in the arts. One of the world's most famous painters, Rembrandt, was born and educated in Leiden. Other famous Leiden painters include Lucas van Leyden, Jan van Goyen and Jan van Steen. The city has been one of Europe's most prominent scientific centres for more than four centuries. Modern scientific medical research and teaching started in the early 18th century in Leiden with Boerhaave. Many important scientific discoveries have been made here, giving rise to Leiden’s motto: ‘City of Discoveries’.</p>
<p>Leiden University is one of Europe’s top universities, it boasts thirteen Nobel Prize winners, it is a member of the League of European Research Universities and positioned highly in all international academic rankings. It is twinned with <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>, the location of the United Kingdom's oldest <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">university</a>. Leiden University and Leiden University of Applied Sciences (Leidse Hogeschool) together have around 35,000 students. Leiden is a typical university city, university buildings are scattered throughout the city and the many students from all over the world give the city a bustling, vivid and international atmosphere.</p>

<p>Leiden was formed on an artificial hill (today called the <a href="/wiki/Burcht_van_Leiden" title="Burcht van Leiden">Burcht van Leiden</a>) at the confluence of the rivers Oude and Nieuwe Rijn (Old and New Rhine). In the oldest reference to this, from circa 860, the settlement was called <i>Leithon</i>. The name is said to be from Germanic *leitha- "canal".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leiden has in the past erroneously been associated with the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Castellum" title="Castellum">outpost</a> <a href="/wiki/Brittenburg" title="Brittenburg">Lugdunum Batavorum</a>. This particular <i>castellum</i> was thought to be located at the Burcht of Leiden, and the city's name was thought to be derived of the Latin name Lugdunum. However the castellum was in fact closer to the town of <a href="/wiki/Katwijk" title="Katwijk">Katwijk</a>, whereas the Roman settlement near modern-day Leiden was called <a href="/wiki/Matilo" title="Matilo">Matilo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>The landlord of Leiden, situated in a stronghold on the hill (motte), was initially subject to the Bishop of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Utrecht" title="Bishopric of Utrecht">Utrecht</a> but around 1100 the <a href="/wiki/Burgrave" title="Burgrave">burgraves</a> became subject to the county of Holland. This county got its name in 1101 from a domain near the stronghold: <i>Holtland</i> or <i>Holland</i>.</p>
<p>Leiden was sacked in 1047 by Emperor <a href="/wiki/Henry_III,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry III, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry III</a>. Early 13th century, <a href="/wiki/Ada,_Countess_of_Holland" title="Ada, Countess of Holland">Ada, Countess of Holland</a> took refuge here when she was fighting in a civil war against her uncle, <a href="/wiki/William_I,_Count_of_Holland" title="William I, Count of Holland">William I, Count of Holland</a>. He besieged the stronghold and captured Ada.</p>
<p>Leiden received <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_rights_in_the_Netherlands" title="City rights in the Netherlands">city rights</a> in 1266. In 1389, its population had grown to about 4,000 persons.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Siege_of_1420">Siege of 1420</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leiden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Siege of 1420">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1420, during the <a href="/wiki/Hook_and_Cod_wars" title="Hook and Cod wars">Hook and Cod wars</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_III,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="John III, Duke of Bavaria">Duke John III of Bavaria</a> along with his army marched from <a href="/wiki/Gouda,_South_Holland" title="Gouda, South Holland">Gouda</a> in the direction of Leiden in order to conquer the city since Leiden did not pay the new <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Holland" title="Count of Holland">Count of Holland</a> <a href="/wiki/Jacqueline,_Countess_of_Hainaut" title="Jacqueline, Countess of Hainaut">Jacqueline, Countess of Hainaut</a>, his niece and only daughter of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_II,_Duke_of_Bavaria-Straubing" title="William II, Duke of Bavaria-Straubing">Count William VI of Holland</a>.</p>
<p>Burgrave Filips of Wassenaar and the other local noblemen of the Hook faction assumed that the duke would besiege Leiden first and send small units out to conquer the surrounding citadels. But John of Bavaria chose to attack the citadels first.</p>
<p>He rolled the cannons with his army but one which was too heavy went by ship. By firing at the walls and gates with iron balls the citadels fell one by one. Within a week John of Bavaria conquered the castles of Poelgeest, Ter Does, Hoichmade, de Zijl, ter Waerd, Warmond and de Paddenpoel.</p>
<p>On 24 June the army appeared before the walls of Leiden. On 17 August 1420, after a two-month siege the city surrendered to John of Bavaria. The burgrave Filips of Wassenaar was stripped of his offices and rights and lived out his last years in captivity.</p>

<p>Leiden flourished in the 16th and 17th century. At the close of the 15th century the <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">weaving</a> establishments (mainly <a href="/wiki/Broadcloth" title="Broadcloth">broadcloth</a>) of Leiden were very important, and after the expulsion of the Spaniards Leiden cloth, Leiden <a href="/wiki/Baize" title="Baize">baize</a> and Leiden <a href="/wiki/Camlet" title="Camlet">camlet</a> were familiar terms.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the same period, Leiden developed an important printing and publishing industry. The influential printer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christoffel_Plantijn" title="Christoffel Plantijn">Christoffel Plantijn</a> lived there at one time. One of his pupils was <a href="/wiki/Lodewijk_Elzevir" title="Lodewijk Elzevir">Lodewijk Elzevir</a> (1547–1617), who established the largest bookshop and printing works in Leiden, a business continued by <a href="/wiki/House_of_Elzevir" title="House of Elzevir">his descendants</a> through 1712 and the name subsequently adopted (in a variant spelling) by contemporary publisher <a href="/wiki/Elsevier" title="Elsevier">Elsevier</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1572, the city sided with the Dutch revolt against Spanish rule and played an important role in the <a href="/wiki/Eighty_Years%27_War" title="Eighty Years' War">Eighty Years' War</a>. <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Leiden" title="Siege of Leiden">Besieged</a> from May until October 1574 by the Spanish, Leiden was relieved by the cutting of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dike_(construction)" title="Dike (construction)">dikes</a>, thus enabling ships to carry provisions to the inhabitants of the flooded town. As a reward for the heroic defence of the previous year, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Leiden" title="University of Leiden">University of Leiden</a> was founded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_I_of_Orange" title="William I of Orange">William I of Orange</a> in 1575. Yearly on 3 October, the end of the siege is still celebrated in Leiden. Tradition tells that the citizens were offered the choice between a university and a certain exemption from taxes and chose the university. The siege is notable also for being the first instance in Europe of the issuance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paper_money" title="Paper money">paper money</a>, with paper taken from prayer books being stamped using coin dies when silver ran out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leiden is also known as the place where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilgrims_(Plymouth_Colony)" title="Pilgrims (Plymouth Colony)">Pilgrims</a> (as well as some of the first settlers of <a href="/wiki/New_Amsterdam" title="New Amsterdam">New Amsterdam</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> lived (and operated a printing press)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> for a time in the early 17th century before their departure to Massachusetts and New Amsterdam in the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 17th century, Leiden prospered, in part because of the impetus to the textile industry by refugees from <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>. While the city had lost about a third of its 15,000 citizens during the siege of 1574, it quickly recovered to 45,000 inhabitants in 1622, and may have come near to 70,000 circa 1670. During the Dutch Golden Era, Leiden was the second largest city of Holland, after Amsterdam.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Particularly due to the work by <a href="/wiki/Herman_Boerhaave" title="Herman Boerhaave">Herman Boerhaave</a> (1668–1738), it played a crucial role in the establishment of modern chemistry and medicine.</p>
<p>From the late 17th century onwards Leiden slumped, mainly due to the decline of the cloth industries. In the beginning of the 19th century the baize manufacture was altogether given up, although industry remained central to Leiden economy. This decline is painted vividly by the fall in population. The population of Leiden had sunk to 30,000 between 1796 and 1811, and in 1904 was 56,044.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>From the 17th to the early 19th century, Leiden was the publishing place of one of the most important contemporary journals, <i><a href="/wiki/Nouvelles_Extraordinaires_de_Divers_Endroits" title="Nouvelles Extraordinaires de Divers Endroits">Nouvelles Extraordinaires de Divers Endroits</a></i>, known also as <i>Gazette de Leyde</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_and_20th_centuries">19th and 20th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leiden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 19th and 20th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 12 January 1807, a catastrophe struck the city when a boat loaded with 17,400 kilograms (38,360 pounds) of gunpowder blew up in the middle of Leiden. 151 persons were killed, over 2,000 were injured and some 220 homes were destroyed. King <a href="/wiki/Louis_Bonaparte" title="Louis Bonaparte">Louis Bonaparte</a> personally visited the city to provide assistance to the victims. Although located in the centre of the city, the area destroyed remained empty for many years. In 1886 the space was turned into a public park, the Van der Werff park.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1842, the railroad from Leiden to <a href="/wiki/Haarlem" title="Haarlem">Haarlem</a> was inaugurated and one year later the railway to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Den_Haag" title="Den Haag">Den Haag</a> was completed, resulting in some social and economic improvement. Perhaps the most important piece of Dutch history contributed by Leiden was the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Netherlands" title="Constitution of the Netherlands">Constitution of the Netherlands</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johan_Rudolf_Thorbecke" title="Johan Rudolf Thorbecke">Johan Rudolf Thorbecke</a> (1798–1872) wrote the Dutch Constitution in April 1848 in his house at Garenmarkt 9 in Leiden.</p>
<p>Leiden's reputation as the "city of books" continued through the 19th century with the establishment of publishing dynasties by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evert_Jan_Brill" title="Evert Jan Brill">Evert Jan Brill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albertus_Willem_Sijthoff" title="Albertus Willem Sijthoff">Albertus Willem Sijthoff</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BN-History_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-BN-History-13">[13]</a></sup> Sijthoff, who rose to prominence in the trade of translated books, wrote a letter in 1899 to <a href="/wiki/Wilhelmina_of_the_Netherlands" title="Wilhelmina of the Netherlands">Queen Wilhelmina</a> regarding his opposition to becoming a signatory to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berne_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_Literary_and_Artistic_Works" title="Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works">Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works</a>. He felt that international copyright restrictions would stifle the Dutch publishing industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Publishers-Circular-1899_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Publishers-Circular-1899-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leiden_Annexaties.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1398" data-file-width="1243" height="247" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Leiden_Annexaties.jpg/220px-Leiden_Annexaties.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Leiden_Annexaties.jpg/330px-Leiden_Annexaties.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Leiden_Annexaties.jpg/440px-Leiden_Annexaties.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Leiden began to expand beyond its 17th-century moats around 1896 and the number of citizens surpassed 50,000 in 1900. After 1920, new industries were established in the city, such as the <a href="/wiki/Canning" title="Canning">canning</a> and metal industries. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Leiden was hit hard by Allied bombardments. The areas surrounding the railway station and Marewijk were almost completely destroyed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leiden_today">Leiden today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leiden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Leiden today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city's biggest and most popular annual festival is celebrated at 3 October and is called simply <a href="/wiki/3_October_Festival" title="3 October Festival">3 Oktober</a>. The people of Leiden celebrate the end of the Spanish siege of 1574.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It typically takes place over the course of two to three days (usually two but three if there's a Sunday involved) and includes parades, a hutspot feast, historical reenactments, a funfair and other events. The city has recently started to host the <a href="/wiki/Leiden_International_Film_Festival" title="Leiden International Film Festival">Leiden International Film Festival</a>, the fastest growing festival of its type in the Netherlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dawson_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-dawson-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leiden has important functions as a shopping and trade centre for communities around the city. The University of Leiden is famous for its many discoveries including Snells law (by <a href="/wiki/Willebrord_Snellius" title="Willebrord Snellius">Willebrord Snellius</a>), the famous <a href="/wiki/Leyden_jar" title="Leyden jar">Leyden jar</a>, a capacitor made from a glass jar, invented in Leiden by <a href="/wiki/Pieter_van_Musschenbroek" title="Pieter van Musschenbroek">Pieter van Musschenbroek</a> in 1746. Another development was in <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">cryogenics</a>: <a href="/wiki/Heike_Kamerlingh_Onnes" title="Heike Kamerlingh Onnes">Heike Kamerlingh Onnes</a> (1913 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel prize winner in physics</a>) liquefied <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> for the first time (1908) and later managed to reach a temperature of less than one degree above the <a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">absolute minimum</a>. Albert Einstein also spent some time at Leiden University during his early to middle career.</p>
<p>The city also houses the <a href="/wiki/Eurotransplant" title="Eurotransplant">Eurotransplant</a>, the international organization responsible for the mediation and allocation of <a href="/wiki/Organ_donation" title="Organ donation">organ donation</a> procedures in Austria, Belgium, Croatia, Germany, Luxembourg, the Netherlands and Slovenia. Leiden also houses the headquarters of <a href="/wiki/Airbus_Group" title="Airbus Group">Airbus Group</a>, a global pan-European aerospace and defence corporation and a leading defence and military contractor worldwide. The group includes <a href="/wiki/Airbus" title="Airbus">Airbus</a>, the leading manufacturer of commercial aircraft worldwide.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rivers.2C_canals_and_parks">Rivers, canals and parks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leiden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rivers, canals and parks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The two branches of the <a href="/wiki/Oude_Rijn_(Utrecht_and_South_Holland)" title="Oude Rijn (Utrecht and South Holland)">Oude Rijn</a>, which enter Leiden on the east, unite in the centre of the city. The city is further intersected by numerous small <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canals</a> with tree-bordered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quay" title="Quay">quays</a>. On the west side of the city, the <a href="/wiki/Hortus_Botanicus_Leiden" title="Hortus Botanicus Leiden">Hortus Botanicus</a> and other gardens extend along the old <i><a href="/wiki/Singel" title="Singel">Singel</a></i>, or outer canal. The <a href="/wiki/Leidse_Hout" title="Leidse Hout">Leidse Hout</a> park, which contains a small deer park, lies on the northwest border with <a href="/wiki/Oegstgeest" title="Oegstgeest">Oegstgeest</a>. The <i>Van der Werf Park</i> is named after the mayor <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pieter_Adriaansz._van_der_Werff&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pieter Adriaansz. van der Werff (page does not exist)">Pieter Adriaansz. van der Werff</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pieter_Adriaansz._van_der_Werff" title="nl:Pieter Adriaansz. van der Werff">nl</a>)</span>, who defended the city against the Spaniards in 1574. The city was beleaguered for months and many died from famine. The open space for the park was formed by the accidental <a href="/wiki/Explosion" title="Explosion">explosion</a> of a ship loaded with <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder" title="Gunpowder">gunpowder</a> in 1807, which destroyed hundreds of houses, including that of the Elsevier family of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Printer_(publisher)" title="Printer (publisher)">printers</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings_of_interest">Buildings of interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leiden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Buildings of interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of the economic decline from the end of the 17th until the middle of the 19th century, much of the 16th- and 17th-century city centre is still intact. It is the second largest 17th-century town centre in the Netherlands, the largest being Amsterdam's city centre.</p>

<p>At the strategically important junction of the two arms of the Oude Rijn stands the old <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a> <i>de Burcht</i>, a circular tower built on an earthen mound. The mound probably was a refuge against high water before a small wooden fortress was built on top of it in the 11th century. The citadel is a so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motte-and-bailey" title="Motte-and-bailey">motte-and-bailey</a> castle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Of Leiden's old <a href="/wiki/City_gate" title="City gate">city gates</a> only two are left, the <i><a href="/wiki/Zijlpoort_(Leiden)" title="Zijlpoort (Leiden)">Zijlpoort</a></i> and the <i>Morspoort</i>, both dating from the end of the 17th century. Apart from one small watch tower on the Singel nothing is left of the town's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_wall" title="City wall">city walls</a>. Another former <a href="/wiki/Fortification" title="Fortification">fortification</a> is the <i>Gravensteen</i>. Built as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fortress" title="Fortress">fortress</a> in the 13th century it has since served as house, library and prison. Presently it is one of the University's buildings.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Churches">Churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leiden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hooglandse_Kerk_1671.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2128" data-file-width="2832" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Hooglandse_Kerk_1671.jpg/220px-Hooglandse_Kerk_1671.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Hooglandse_Kerk_1671.jpg/330px-Hooglandse_Kerk_1671.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Hooglandse_Kerk_1671.jpg/440px-Hooglandse_Kerk_1671.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hooglandse_Kerk_1671.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Hooglandse_Kerk" title="Hooglandse Kerk">Hooglandse Kerk</a>, Leiden</div>
</div>

<p>The chief of Leiden's numerous churches are the <a href="/wiki/Hooglandse_Kerk" title="Hooglandse Kerk">Hooglandse Kerk</a> (or the church of <a href="/wiki/Pancras_of_Rome" title="Pancras of Rome">St Pancras</a>, built in the 15th century and containing a monument to Pieter Adriaansz. van der Werff) and the <i><a href="/wiki/Pieterskerk,_Leiden" title="Pieterskerk, Leiden">Pieterskerk</a></i> (church of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Peter" title="St Peter">St Peter</a> (1315)) with monuments to <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Justus_Scaliger" title="Joseph Justus Scaliger">Scaliger</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boerhaave" title="Boerhaave">Boerhaave</a> and other famous scholars. From a historical perspective the <a href="/wiki/Marekerk" title="Marekerk">Marekerk</a> is interesting too. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Arent_van_%27s_Gravesande&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Arent van 's Gravesande (page does not exist)">Arent van 's Gravesande</a> designed that church in 1639. Other fine examples of his work in Leiden are in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stedelijk_Museum_De_Lakenhal" title="Stedelijk Museum De Lakenhal">Stedelijk Museum De Lakenhal</a></i> (the municipal museum of fine arts), and the <i><a href="/wiki/Leiden_University_Library#Bibliotheca_Thysiana" title="Leiden University Library">Bibliotheca Thysiana</a></i>. The growing city needed another church and the <i>Marekerk</i> was the first Protestant church to be built in Leiden (and in Holland) after the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a>. It is an example of <a href="/wiki/Classicism" title="Classicism">Dutch Classicism</a>. In the drawings by Van 's Gravesande the <a href="/wiki/Pulpit" title="Pulpit">pulpit</a> is the centrepiece of the church. The pulpit is modelled after the one in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nieuwe_Kerk_(Haarlem)" title="Nieuwe Kerk (Haarlem)">Nieuwe Kerk</a></i> at Haarlem (designed by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_van_Campen" title="Jacob van Campen">Jacob van Campen</a>). The building was first used in 1650, and is still in use. The <a href="/wiki/Heilige_Lodewijkkerk" title="Heilige Lodewijkkerk">Heilige Lodewijkkerk</a> is first catholic church in Leiden that was built after the Reformation. This church was given to the Catholics after the gunpowder explosion in 1807, which killed 150 inhabitants and destroyed a large part of the city centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The 'Waalse Kerk' (Breestraat 63) was originally part of the Katharina Hospital. In 1584 it became the church of Protestant refugees from the Southern Netherlands (Brugge) and France. Later churches in the centre include the <a href="/wiki/St._Joseph,_Leiden" title="St. Joseph, Leiden">St. Joseph</a> in <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">expressionistic</a> style.</p>

<p>The city centre contains many buildings that are in use by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Leiden" title="University of Leiden">University of Leiden</a>. The <i>Academy Building</i> is housed in a former 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Convent" title="Convent">convent</a>. Among the institutions connected with the university are the national institution for East Indian languages, <a href="/wiki/Ethnology" title="Ethnology">ethnology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>; the botanical gardens, founded in 1587; the <a href="/wiki/Leiden_Observatory" title="Leiden Observatory">observatory</a> (1860); the museum of antiquities (<i><a href="/wiki/Rijksmuseum_van_Oudheden" title="Rijksmuseum van Oudheden">Rijksmuseum van Oudheden</a></i>); and the <a href="/wiki/Ethnography" title="Ethnography">ethnographical</a> museum, of which <a href="/wiki/Philipp_Franz_von_Siebold" title="Philipp Franz von Siebold">P.<span class="nowrap"> </span>F. von Siebold's</a> Japanese collections was the nucleus (<i><a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Ethnology_(Netherlands)" title="National Museum of Ethnology (Netherlands)">Rijksmuseum voor Volkenkunde</a></i>). The Bibliotheca Thysiana occupies an old <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> building of the year 1655. It is especially rich in legal works and <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">vernacular</a> chronicles. Noteworthy are also the many special collections at <a href="/wiki/Leiden_University_Library" title="Leiden University Library">Leiden University Library</a> among which those of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Society_of_Dutch_Literature" title="Society of Dutch Literature">Society of Dutch Literature</a> (1766) and the collection of casts and engravings. In recent years the university has built the Bio Science Park at the city's outskirts to accommodate the Science departments.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

The building has been used as a military hospital, prison, national asylum and army museum. It now serves as the entrance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturalis" title="Naturalis">Naturalis</a>, one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">natural history</a> museums in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>

<ul>
<li>The coat of arms of Leiden is two red keys, crossed in an X-shape on a white background. These keys are those to the gates of heaven held by St.Peter, for whom a large church in the city centre is named. Because of this coat of arms, Leiden is referred to as the <i>"Sleutelstad"</i> ("the key city").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>For a time Leiden held the title "The Coldest Place on Earth" because of the developments in <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">cryogenics</a> in a laboratory there. <a href="/wiki/Heike_Kamerlingh_Onnes" title="Heike Kamerlingh Onnes">Heike Kamerlingh Onnes</a> (1913 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_prize" title="Nobel prize">Nobel prize</a> winner in physics) liquefied <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> for the first time (1908), and later managed to reach a temperature of less than one degree above <a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">Absolute zero</a>.</li>
<li>The Norwegian cheese "<a href="/wiki/N%C3%B8kkelost" title="Nøkkelost">nøkkelost</a>" ("key cheese") is named after the keys in coat of arms of Leyden, as it is a variation of <a href="/wiki/Leyden_cheese" title="Leyden cheese">Leyden cheese</a>.</li>
<li>The following places and things are named after this city:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leyden,_New_York" title="Leyden, New York">Leyden, New York</a>, USA</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leyden,_Massachusetts" title="Leyden, Massachusetts">Leyden, Massachusetts</a>, USA<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leyden_High_School_District_212" title="Leyden High School District 212">Leyden High School District 212</a> in <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Park,_Illinois" title="Franklin Park, Illinois">Franklin Park, Illinois</a>, USA.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leiden_scale" title="Leiden scale">Leiden scale</a>, for measuring extreme low temperatures.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Factor_V_Leiden" title="Factor V Leiden">Factor V Leiden</a> is named after the city of Leiden where it was discovered in 1994.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Leyden_jar" title="Leyden jar">Leyden jar</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Capacitor" title="Capacitor">capacitor</a> made from a glass jar, was invented here by <a href="/wiki/Pieter_van_Musschenbroek" title="Pieter van Musschenbroek">Pieter van Musschenbroek</a> in 1746. It was actually first invented by <a href="/wiki/Ewald_Georg_von_Kleist" title="Ewald Georg von Kleist">Ewald Georg von Kleist</a> the year before, but the name "Leyden jar" stuck.</li>
</ul>

<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zorg_en_Zekerheid_Leiden" title="Zorg en Zekerheid Leiden">Zorg en Zekerheid Leiden</a> is the basketball club of Leiden. The club also played in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIBA_EuroChallenge" title="FIBA EuroChallenge">FIBA EuroChallenge</a> and reached the Second Round (Best 16) in 2011/2012.</li>
