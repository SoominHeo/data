<p><b>Léi Fēng</b> (18 December 1940  – 15 August 1962) was a soldier of the Chinese army in Communist legend. After his death, Lei was characterized as a selfless and modest person devoted to the Communist Party, <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a>, and the people of China. In 1963, he became the subject of a nationwide posthumous propaganda campaign, "Follow the examples of Comrade Lei Feng."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Lei was portrayed as a model citizen, and the masses were encouraged to emulate his selflessness, modesty, and devotion to Mao. After Mao's death, Lei Feng remained a <a href="/wiki/Cultural_icon" title="Cultural icon">cultural icon</a> representing earnestness and service. His name entered daily speech and his imagery appeared on T-shirts and memorabilia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although someone named Lei Feng probably existed, the accounts of his life as depicted by Party propaganda are heavily disputed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fraser_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fraser-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cull_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cull-4">[4]</a></sup> leading him to become a source of cynicism and subject of derision among segments of the Chinese population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Nevertheless, Lei's image as a role model serviceman has survived decades of political change in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Military_Celebrity_in_China_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Military_Celebrity_in_China-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lei_Feng&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Wangcheng" title="Wangcheng">Wangcheng</a> (near the town of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leifeng,_Hunan" title="Leifeng, Hunan">Leifeng</a>, <a href="/wiki/Changsha" title="Changsha">Changsha</a>, Hunan, named in his honour), Lei was orphaned at a young age. According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CNTV" title="CNTV">CNTV</a>, Lei lost all of his family prior to the establishment of the People's Republic. His father died when he was just 5 (killed by the invading Japanese Army),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> his elder brother, who was exploited as a child labourer, died a year later, and his younger brother passed soon afterwards. Finally, his mother committed suicide after being "dishonored by a landlord."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>He became a member in the Communist youth corps when he was young and joined a transportation unit of the People's Liberation Army at the age of twenty. According to his official biography, Lei died in 1962 at the age of 21 (22 by <a href="/wiki/East_Asian_age_reckoning" title="East Asian age reckoning">East Asian age reckoning</a>, by which a newborn is one year old at birth), when a telephone pole, struck by an army truck, hit him as he was directing the truck in backing up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_image">Popular image</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lei_Feng&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Popular image">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_propaganda_campaigns">Initial propaganda campaigns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lei_Feng&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Initial propaganda campaigns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lei Feng was not widely known until after his death. In 1963, <i>Lei Feng's Diary</i> was first presented to the public by <a href="/wiki/Lin_Biao" title="Lin Biao">Lin Biao</a> in the first of many "Learn from Lei Feng" propaganda campaigns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tanner_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tanner-10">[10]</a></sup> The diary was full of accounts of Lei's admiration for Mao Zedong, his selfless deeds, and his desire to foment revolutionary spirit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cull_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cull-4">[4]</a></sup> Lin's use of Lei's diary was part of a larger effort to improve Mao's image, which had suffered after the <a href="/wiki/Great_Leap_Forward" title="Great Leap Forward">Great Leap Forward</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Scholars generally believe that the diary was forged by Party propagandists under Lin's direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cull_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cull-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tanner_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tanner-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The diary contains about 200,000 words describing selfless thoughts with enthusiastic comments on Mao and the inspiring nature of the Party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The campaign began at a time when the Chinese economy was recovering from the <a href="/wiki/Great_Leap_Forward" title="Great Leap Forward">Great Leap Forward</a> campaign. During 1964 the Lei Feng campaign shifted gradually from doing good deeds to a cult of Mao.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>When Lei Feng died in the line of duty, he was only 22, but his short life gives concentrated expression to the noble ideals of a new people, nurtured with the communist spirit, and also to the noble moral integrity and values of the Chinese people in the new period. These are firm faith in communist ideals, political warmheartedness for the party and the socialist cause, the revolutionary will to work arduously for self-improvement, the moral quality and self-cultivation of showing fraternal unity and taking pleasure in assisting others, the heroic spirit of being ready to take up cudgels for a just cause without caring for one's safety, the attitude of seeking advancement and studying hard, and the genuine spirit of matching words with deeds and enthusiastically carrying out one's duties.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Editorial, <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Daily" title="People's Daily">People's Daily</a>, <i>5 March 1993</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ms1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ms1-13">[13]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Chinese leaders have praised Lei Feng as the personification of altruism. Leaders who have written about Lei Feng include <a href="/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping" title="Deng Xiaoping">Deng Xiaoping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zhou_Enlai" title="Zhou Enlai">Zhou Enlai</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jiang_Zemin" title="Jiang Zemin">Jiang Zemin</a>. His cultural importance is still reproduced and reinforced by the media and cultural apparatus of the Chinese party-state, including emphasizing the importance of moral character during Mao's era. Lei Feng's prominence in school textbooks has since declined, although he remains part of the national curriculum. The term "活雷锋/Huó Léi Fēng" (literally "living Lei Feng") has become a noun (or adjective) for anyone who is seen as selfless, or anyone who goes out of their way to help others.</p>
<p>The CCP's construction of Lei Feng as a celebrity soldier is unique to the PRC and differs from the more typical creation of military heroes by governments during times of war. In the PRC, Lei Feng was part of continuing public promotion of soldiers as exemplary models, and evidence of the People's Liberation Army's role as social and political support to the Communist government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Military_Celebrity_in_China_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Military_Celebrity_in_China-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy_among_scholars">Controversy among scholars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lei_Feng&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Controversy among scholars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Details of Lei Feng's life, as presented in the official propaganda campaign, have been subject to dispute. While someone named Lei Feng may have existed, scholars generally believe the person depicted in the campaign was almost certainly a fabrication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fraser_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fraser-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cull_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cull-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tanner_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tanner-10">[10]</a></sup> Some observers noted, for instance, that the campaign presented a collection of twelve photographs of Lei Feng performing good deeds. The photographs were of exceptionally high professional quality, and depicted Lei—supposedly an obscure and unknown young man—engaging in mundane tasks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fraser_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fraser-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobs2012_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobs2012-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The lauded details of Lei Feng's life according to official propaganda led him to become a subject of derision and cynicism among segments of the Chinese populace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fraser_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Fraser-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobs2012_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobs2012-14">[14]</a></sup> As <a href="/wiki/John_Fraser_(journalist)" title="John Fraser (journalist)">John Fraser</a> recalled, "Any Chinese I ever spoke to outside of official occasions always snorted about Lei Feng."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fraser_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Fraser-3">[3]</a></sup> In a 2012 interview with the New York Review of Books, Chinese dissident blogger <a href="/wiki/Ran_Yunfei" title="Ran Yunfei">Ran Yunfei</a> remarked on the moral and educational implications of the Lei Feng campaigns, noting the counterproductive nature of teaching virtues with a fabricated character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2008 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xinhua" title="Xinhua">Xinhua</a> survey noted that a large number elementary school students have vague knowledge of Lei Feng's life, with only 32 percent of the surveyed having read Lei's diary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_cultural_importance">Contemporary cultural importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lei_Feng&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Contemporary cultural importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Lei Feng is an icon who continues to resonate in mainland China. 5 March has become the official "Learn from Lei Feng Day" (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%AD%A6" title="wikt:学">学</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9B%B7" title="wikt:雷">雷</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%94%8B" title="wikt:锋">锋</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%97%A5" title="wikt:日">日</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Xué Léi Fēng Rì</span></i>). This day involves various community and school events where people go to clean up parks, schools, and other community locations. Local news on that day usually has footage from these events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lei Feng is especially honoured in <a href="/wiki/Changsha" title="Changsha">Changsha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hunan" title="Hunan">Hunan</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Fushun" title="Fushun">Fushun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liaoning" title="Liaoning">Liaoning</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leifeng,_Hunan#Attractions" title="Leifeng, Hunan">Lei Feng Memorial Hall</a> (in his birthplace, now named for him, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leifeng,_Hunan" title="Leifeng, Hunan">Leifeng</a>) and Lei Feng statue are located in <a href="/wiki/Changsha" title="Changsha">Changsha</a>. The local hospital carries his name. There is also a Lei Feng Memorial Hall, with a museum, in Fushun. Lei Feng's military unit was based in Fushun, and it was here where he met his death. His tomb is located on the memorial grounds. To commemorate Lei Feng, the city of Fushun named several landmarks in honor of him. There is a Lei Feng Road, a Lei Feng Elementary School, a Lei Feng Middle School and a Leifeng bank office.</p>
<p>Lei Feng's story continues to be referenced in popular culture. A popular song by <a href="/wiki/Jilin" title="Jilin">Jilin</a> singer Xue Cun (雪村) is called "All Northeasterners are Living Lei Fengs" (Chinese: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">东北人都是活雷锋</span>; pinyin: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Dōngběi Rén Dōu Shì Huó Léifēng</span></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[i]</a></sup> A 1995 release<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, originally notable only for its use of <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_Mandarin" title="Northeastern Mandarin">Northeastern Mandarin</a>, it shot to nationwide fame when it was combined with <a href="/wiki/Kitsch" title="Kitsch">kitsch</a> animations on the Internet in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cw2_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-cw2-19">[18]</a></sup> In March 2006, a Chinese organization released an online game titled <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Learn_From_Lei_Feng_Online&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Learn From Lei Feng Online (page does not exist)">Learn From Lei Feng Online</a></i> (《学雷锋》) in which the player has to do good deeds, fight spies, and collect parts of Mao Zedong's collection. If the player wins, he or she gets to meet Chairman Mao in the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> In the 21st century his image has been used to sell items including, in one case, condom packaging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of March 5, 2013 popular interest in Lei Feng was minimal as ticket sales to feature-length biographical films, <i>Young Lei Feng,</i> <i>Lei Feng’s Smile</i> and <i>Lei Feng 1959,</i> released on Learn from Lei Feng Day failed to produce any takers at all in some cities. Reportedly, party cadres in rural areas have been charged by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Administration_of_Radio,_Film,_and_Television" title="State Administration of Radio, Film, and Television">State Administration of Radio, Film, and Television</a> with organizing group viewings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT031113_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT031113-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
