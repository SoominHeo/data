<p><b>Rainbow</b> (also known as <b>Ritchie Blackmore's Rainbow</b> or <b>Blackmore's Rainbow</b>) are a British <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band led by guitarist <a href="/wiki/Ritchie_Blackmore" title="Ritchie Blackmore">Ritchie Blackmore</a>, active from 1975 to 1984 and 1993 to 1997, and again since 2015. They were originally established with <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_James_Dio" title="Ronnie James Dio">Ronnie James Dio</a>'s American rock band <a href="/wiki/Elf_(band)" title="Elf (band)">Elf</a>, but after the first album, Blackmore fired the backing members and continued with Dio until 1979. Three British musicians joined in 1979, singer <a href="/wiki/Graham_Bonnet" title="Graham Bonnet">Graham Bonnet</a>, keyboardist <a href="/wiki/Don_Airey" title="Don Airey">Don Airey</a>, former Deep Purple bassist <a href="/wiki/Roger_Glover" title="Roger Glover">Roger Glover</a>, and this line-up gave the band their commercial breakthrough with the single "<a href="/wiki/Since_You_Been_Gone" title="Since You Been Gone">Since You Been Gone</a>". Over the years Rainbow went through many line-up changes with no two studio albums featuring the same line-up. Other lead singers <a href="/wiki/Joe_Lynn_Turner" title="Joe Lynn Turner">Joe Lynn Turner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doogie_White" title="Doogie White">Doogie White</a> would follow, and the project consisted of numerous backing musicians. The band's early work primarily featured mystical lyrics with a <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_metal" title="Neoclassical metal">neoclassical metal</a> style, but went in a more streamlined, commercial direction following Dio's departure from the group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eduardo_Rivadavia_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eduardo_Rivadavia-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rainbow were ranked No. 90 on VH1's <i>100 Greatest Artists of Hard Rock</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The band has sold over 28 million albums worldwide.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbow_(rock_band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_.281975.29">Formation (1975)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbow_(rock_band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation (1975)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dio.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="454" data-file-width="245" height="315" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Dio.JPG/170px-Dio.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4e/Dio.JPG 1.5x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dio.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Ronnie_James_Dio" title="Ronnie James Dio">Ronnie James Dio</a> in 2006</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>By 1973, Blackmore had steered <a href="/wiki/Deep_Purple" title="Deep Purple">Deep Purple</a> through a significant personnel change, with <a href="/wiki/Ian_Gillan" title="Ian Gillan">Ian Gillan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roger_Glover" title="Roger Glover">Roger Glover</a> being replaced by <a href="/wiki/David_Coverdale" title="David Coverdale">David Coverdale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Hughes" title="Glenn Hughes">Glenn Hughes</a>. However, the new members were keen to add new musical styles and Blackmore found his request to record the <a href="/wiki/Fat_Mattress" title="Fat Mattress">Steve Hammond</a>-penned "Black Sheep of the Family" with "Sixteenth Century Greensleeves" turned down by the band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-robinson_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-robinson-3">[3]</a></sup> He decided to record the song with Dio instead, using Dio's band <a href="/wiki/Elf_(band)" title="Elf (band)">Elf</a> as additional musicians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He enjoyed the results, and a full album, billed as <i><a href="/wiki/Ritchie_Blackmore%27s_Rainbow" title="Ritchie Blackmore's Rainbow">Ritchie Blackmore's Rainbow</a></i> was recorded between February and March 1975 at <a href="/wiki/Musicland_Studios" title="Musicland Studios">Musicland Studios</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1stalbum_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-1stalbum-5">[5]</a></sup> The band name was inspired by the Rainbow Bar and Grill in <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBloom2007193_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBloom2007193-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rainbow's music was partly inspired by classical music since Blackmore started playing cello to help him construct interesting chord progressions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and Dio wrote lyrics about medieval themes. Dio possessed a versatile vocal range capable of singing both hard rock and lighter ballads, and, according to Blackmore, "I felt shivers down my spine."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBloom2007186_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBloom2007186-9">[9]</a></sup> Although Dio never played a musical instrument on any Rainbow album, he is credited with writing and arranging the music with Blackmore, in addition to writing all the lyrics himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1stalbum_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-1stalbum-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Blackmore and Dio also found a common ground in their sense of humour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBloom2007187_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBloom2007187-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the positive experience of recording with Dio, Blackmore decided to leave Deep Purple, playing his last show in Paris in April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-robinson_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-robinson-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBloom2007184_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBloom2007184-13">[13]</a></sup> The album had a positive critical reception and was a top 20 UK and top 30 US hit. Blackmore's departure from Deep Purple was publicly announced on 21 June.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson2004176_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson2004176-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_world_tour_and_initial_success_.281975.E2.80.931978.29">First world tour and initial success (1975–1978)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbow_(rock_band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First world tour and initial success (1975–1978)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rainbowonstage.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="227" data-file-width="294" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/72/Rainbowonstage.jpg/220px-Rainbowonstage.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/7/72/Rainbowonstage.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rainbowonstage.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rainbow performing in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Munich,_Germany" title="Munich, Germany">Munich, Germany</a>, in 1977<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Blackmore was unhappy about carrying the Elf line-up along for live performances, and so he fired everybody except Dio shortly after the album was recorded, due to Driscoll's style of drumming and the funky bass playing of Gruber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carey_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-carey-16">[16]</a></sup> Blackmore would continue to dictate personnel for the remainder of the band's lifetime, with drummer and former bandmate Ricky Munro remarking "he was very difficult to get on with because you never knew when he would turn around and say 'You're sacked'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBloom2007189_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBloom2007189-17">[17]</a></sup> Blackmore recruited bassist <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Bain" title="Jimmy Bain">Jimmy Bain</a>, American keyboard player <a href="/wiki/Tony_Carey" title="Tony Carey">Tony Carey</a> and drummer <a href="/wiki/Cozy_Powell" title="Cozy Powell">Cozy Powell</a>, who had previously worked with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeff_Beck_Group" title="Jeff Beck Group">Jeff Beck</a> and had some solo success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carey_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-carey-16">[16]</a></sup> Powell also greatly appealed to Blackmore in their mutual fondness for <a href="/wiki/Practical_joke" title="Practical joke">practical jokes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBloom2007190_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBloom2007190-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>This line-up also commenced the first world tour for the band, with the first date in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a> on 10 November 1975. The centrepiece of the band's live performance was a computer-controlled rainbow including 3,000 lightbulbs, which stretched 40 feet across the stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson2004195-196_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson2004195-196-19">[19]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBloom2007190_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBloom2007190-18">[18]</a></sup> A second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Rising_(Rainbow_album)" title="Rising (Rainbow album)">Rising</a></i>, was recorded in February at Musicland. By the time of the European dates in the summer of 1976, Rainbow's reputation as a blistering live act had been established. The band added Deep Purple's "<a href="/wiki/Mistreated_(song)" title="Mistreated (song)">Mistreated</a>" to their setlist, and song lengths were stretched to include improvisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson2004196_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson2004196-20">[20]</a></sup> Carey recalls rehearsing the material was fairly straightforward, saying "We didn't work anything out, except the structure, the ending ... very free-form, really progressive rock."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBloom2007194_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBloom2007194-21">[21]</a></sup> The album art was designed by famed fantasy artist <a href="/wiki/Ken_Kelly_(artist)" title="Ken Kelly (artist)">Ken Kelly</a>, who had drawn <a href="/wiki/Tarzan" title="Tarzan">Tarzan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conan_the_Barbarian" title="Conan the Barbarian">Conan the Barbarian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 1976, following a gig at Newcastle City Hall, Blackmore decided to fire Carey, believing his playing style to be too complicated for the band. Unable to find a suitable replacement quickly, Carey was quickly reinstated,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBloom2007201-202_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBloom2007201-202-24">[24]</a></sup> but as the world tour progressed onto Japan, he found himself regularly being the recipient of Blackmore's pranks and humour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBloom2007203_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBloom2007203-25">[25]</a></sup> Blackmore subsequently decided that Bain was substandard and fired him in January 1977. The same fate befell Carey shortly after. Blackmore, however, had difficulty finding replacements he liked. On keyboards, after auditioning several high-profile artists, including <a href="/wiki/Vanilla_Fudge" title="Vanilla Fudge">Vanilla Fudge</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mark_Stein_(musician)" title="Mark Stein (musician)">Mark Stein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Procol_Harum" title="Procol Harum">Procol Harum</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Fisher" title="Matthew Fisher">Matthew Fisher</a> and ex-<a href="/wiki/Curved_Air" title="Curved Air">Curved Air</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roxy_Music" title="Roxy Music">Roxy Music</a> man <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Jobson" title="Eddie Jobson">Eddie Jobson</a>, Blackmore finally selected Canadian <a href="/wiki/David_Stone_(keyboardist)" title="David Stone (keyboardist)">David Stone</a>, from the little-known band Symphonic Slam. For a bass player, Blackmore originally chose <a href="/wiki/Mark_Clarke" title="Mark Clarke">Mark Clarke</a>, formerly of <a href="/wiki/Jon_Hiseman" title="Jon Hiseman">Jon Hiseman</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Colosseum_(band)" title="Colosseum (band)">Colosseum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uriah_Heep_(band)" title="Uriah Heep (band)">Uriah Heep</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tempest_(UK_band)" title="Tempest (UK band)">Tempest</a>, but once in the studio for the next album, <i><a href="/wiki/Long_Live_Rock_%27n%27_Roll" title="Long Live Rock 'n' Roll">Long Live Rock 'n' Roll</a></i>, Blackmore disliked Clarke's fingerstyle method of playing so much that he fired him on the spot and played bass himself on all but four songs: the album's title track, "Gates of Babylon", "Kill the King", and "Sensitive to Light". Former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Widowmaker_(band)" title="Widowmaker (band)">Widowmaker</a> bassist <a href="/wiki/Bob_Daisley" title="Bob Daisley">Bob Daisley</a> was hired to record these tracks, completing the band's next line-up.</p>
<p>After the release and extensive world tour in 1977–78, Blackmore decided that he wanted to take the band in a new commercial direction away from the "sword and sorcery" theme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Dio did not agree with this change and left Rainbow.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_success_.281978.E2.80.931984.29">Commercial success (1978–1984)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbow_(rock_band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Commercial success (1978–1984)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Graham_Bonnet.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Graham_Bonnet" title="Graham Bonnet">Graham Bonnet</a> in 2008</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Blackmore asked <a href="/wiki/Ian_Gillan" title="Ian Gillan">Ian Gillan</a>, also formerly of Deep Purple, to replace Dio, but Gillan turned him down. After a series of auditions, former singer/guitarist of <a href="/wiki/The_Marbles_(duo)" title="The Marbles (duo)">The Marbles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graham_Bonnet" title="Graham Bonnet">Graham Bonnet</a> was recruited. Powell stayed, but Daisley and Stone were both fired, the latter replaced by keyboardist <a href="/wiki/Don_Airey" title="Don Airey">Don Airey</a>. At first the band auditioned bass players, but at Cozy Powell's suggestion Blackmore hired another former Deep Purple member, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Glover" title="Roger Glover">Roger Glover</a>, as a producer, bassist and lyricist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The first album from the new line-up, <i><a href="/wiki/Down_to_Earth_(Rainbow_album)" title="Down to Earth (Rainbow album)">Down to Earth</a></i>, featured the band's first major singles chart successes, "All Night Long" and the <a href="/wiki/Russ_Ballard" title="Russ Ballard">Russ Ballard</a>-penned "<a href="/wiki/Since_You_Been_Gone" title="Since You Been Gone">Since You Been Gone</a>". In 1980, the band headlined the inaugural <a href="/wiki/Monsters_of_Rock" title="Monsters of Rock">Monsters of Rock</a> festival at <a href="/wiki/Castle_Donington" title="Castle Donington">Castle Donington</a> in England. However, this was Powell's last Rainbow gig: he had already given his notice to quit, disliking Blackmore's increasingly <a href="/wiki/Pop_rock" title="Pop rock">pop rock</a> direction. Then Bonnet resigned to pursue a solo project.</p>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Joe_Lynn_Turner_in_2008.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Joe_Lynn_Turner" title="Joe Lynn Turner">Joe Lynn Turner</a> in 2008</div>

<p>For the next album, Bonnet and Powell were replaced by Americans <a href="/wiki/Joe_Lynn_Turner" title="Joe Lynn Turner">Joe Lynn Turner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Rondinelli" title="Bobby Rondinelli">Bobby Rondinelli</a>, respectively. The title track from <i><a href="/wiki/Difficult_to_Cure" title="Difficult to Cure">Difficult to Cure</a></i> was a version of <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._9_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 9 (Beethoven)">Beethoven's Ninth Symphony</a>. The album spawned their most successful UK single, "I Surrender" (another Ballard song), which reached No.3. After the supporting tour, Don Airey quit over musical differences and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/David_Rosenthal_(musician)" title="David Rosenthal (musician)">David Rosenthal</a>.</p>
<p>The band attained significant airplay on <a href="/wiki/Album-oriented_rock" title="Album-oriented rock">Album-oriented rock</a> radio stations in the US with the track "<a href="/wiki/Jealous_Lover" title="Jealous Lover">Jealous Lover</a>", reaching No. 13 on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_Magazine" title="Billboard Magazine">Billboard Magazine</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mainstream_Rock_Tracks" title="Mainstream Rock Tracks">Rock Tracks</a> chart. Originally issued as the B-side to "Can't Happen Here", "Jealous Lover" subsequently became the title track to an EP issued in the US that featured very similar cover art to <i>Difficult to Cure</i>.</p>
<p>Rainbow's next full-length studio album was <i><a href="/wiki/Straight_Between_the_Eyes" title="Straight Between the Eyes">Straight Between the Eyes</a></i>. The album was more cohesive than <i>Difficult to Cure</i>, and had more success in the United States. The band, however, was alienating some of its earlier fans with its more AOR sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eduardo_Rivadavia_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eduardo_Rivadavia-1">[1]</a></sup> The single "Stone Cold" was a ballad that had some chart success (No. 1 on <i>Billboard</i> Magazine's Rock Tracks chart) and its video received heavy airplay on <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a>. The successful supporting tour skipped the UK completely and focused on the American market. A date in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Antonio,_Texas" title="San Antonio, Texas">San Antonio, Texas</a>, on this tour was filmed, and the resulting "Live Between the Eyes" also received repeated showings on MTV.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Bent_Out_of_Shape" title="Bent Out of Shape">Bent Out of Shape</a></i> saw drummer Rondinelli fired in favour of former <a href="/wiki/Balance_(band)" title="Balance (band)">Balance</a> drummer <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Burgi" title="Chuck Burgi">Chuck Burgi</a>. The album featured the single "<a href="/wiki/Street_of_Dreams_(Rainbow_song)" title="Street of Dreams (Rainbow song)">Street of Dreams</a>". Blackmore claims on his website that the song's video was banned by MTV for its supposedly controversial hypnotic video clip,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> but Dr. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Radecki" title="Thomas Radecki">Thomas Radecki</a> of the National Coalition on Television Violence criticised MTV for airing the video, contradicting Blackmore's claim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inside_MTV_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Inside_MTV-29">[29]</a></sup> The resulting tour saw Rainbow return to the UK, and also to Japan in March 1984 where the band performed "Difficult to Cure" with a full orchestra. The concert was also filmed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dissolution_and_temporary_revival_.281993.E2.80.931997.29">Dissolution and temporary revival (1993–1997)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbow_(rock_band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dissolution and temporary revival (1993–1997)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Doogiewhite.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="1152" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e6/Doogiewhite.jpeg/170px-Doogiewhite.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e6/Doogiewhite.jpeg/255px-Doogiewhite.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e6/Doogiewhite.jpeg/340px-Doogiewhite.jpeg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Doogiewhite.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Doogie_White" title="Doogie White">Doogie White</a> in 2009</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Rainbow's management Thames Talent co-ordinated attempts to successfully reform Deep Purple MK. II. By April 1984, Rainbow was disbanded. A then-final Rainbow album, <i><a href="/wiki/Finyl_Vinyl" title="Finyl Vinyl">Finyl Vinyl</a></i>, was pieced together from live tracks and B-sides of singles, including the instrumental "Weiss Heim" (All Night Long B-side), "Bad Girl" (Since You Been Gone B-side), and "Jealous Lover" (Can't Happen Here B-side).</p>
<p>In 1988, after joining the band <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impelliteri" title="Impelliteri">Impelliteri</a>, Graham Bonnet covered "Since You Been Gone" on the group's debut album, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stand_In_Line" title="Stand In Line">Stand In Line</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1993 Blackmore left Deep Purple permanently due to "creative differences" with other members, and reformed Rainbow with all-new members featuring Scottish singer <a href="/wiki/Doogie_White" title="Doogie White">Doogie White</a>. The band released <i><a href="/wiki/Stranger_in_Us_All" title="Stranger in Us All">Stranger in Us All</a></i> in 1995, and embarked on a lengthy world tour.</p>
<p>The tour proved very successful, and the show in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a>, Germany, was professionally filmed for the <i><a href="/wiki/Rockpalast" title="Rockpalast">Rockpalast</a></i> TV show. This show, initially heavily bootlegged (and considered by many collectors to be the best Rainbow bootleg of the era), was officially released by <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Records" title="Eagle Records">Eagle Records</a> on CD and DVD as <i>Black Masquerade</i> in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The live shows featured frequent changes in set lists, and musical improvisations that proved popular with bootleggers and many shows are still traded over a decade later.</p>
<p>However, Blackmore turned his attention to his long-time musical passion, Renaissance and medieval music. Rainbow was put on hold once again after playing its final concert in Esbjerg, Denmark in 1997. Blackmore, together with his partner <a href="/wiki/Candice_Night" title="Candice Night">Candice Night</a> as vocalist then formed the Renaissance-influenced <a href="/wiki/Blackmore%27s_Night" title="Blackmore's Night">Blackmore's Night</a>. Around the same time as production of <i>Stranger in Us All</i> (1995), they were already gearing up their debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Shadow_of_the_Moon" title="Shadow of the Moon">Shadow of the Moon</a></i> (1997).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<p>Many Rainbow songs have been performed live by former members of the band since the group's split in 1984 and then in 1997, particularly former frontmen <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_James_Dio" title="Ronnie James Dio">Ronnie James Dio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graham_Bonnet" title="Graham Bonnet">Graham Bonnet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Lynn_Turner" title="Joe Lynn Turner">Joe Lynn Turner</a> in recent years. Also, <a href="/wiki/Don_Airey" title="Don Airey">Don Airey</a> often plays 1979-1981 era songs during his solo shows. <a href="/wiki/Blackmore%27s_Night" title="Blackmore's Night">Blackmore's Night</a> occasionally performs one or two Rainbow songs live, namely "Ariel", "Rainbow Eyes", "Street of Dreams" and "Temple of the King". The latter three were also re-recorded by Blackmore's Night in studio.</p>
<p>In 2002–2004 the <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Turner_Project" title="Hughes Turner Project">Hughes Turner Project</a> played a number of Rainbow songs at their concerts. On 9 August 2007 <a href="/wiki/Joe_Lynn_Turner" title="Joe Lynn Turner">Joe Lynn Turner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graham_Bonnet" title="Graham Bonnet">Graham Bonnet</a> played a tribute to Rainbow show in Helsinki, Finland. The concert consisted of songs from the 1979-1983 era.</p>
<p>In 2009, Joe Lynn Turner, Bobby Rondinelli, Greg Smith and Tony Carey created the touring tribute band <a href="/wiki/Over_the_Rainbow_(band)" title="Over the Rainbow (band)">Over The Rainbow</a> with Jürgen Blackmore (Ritchie's son) as the guitarist. Over The Rainbow performed songs from every era of the band's history. After the first tour Tony Carey had to leave the band due to health concerns; he was replaced by another former Rainbow member, Paul Morris.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reunion_shows_.282015.E2.80.93present.29">Reunion shows (2015–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbow_(rock_band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reunion shows (2015–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2015, Blackmore announced that he would play "all rock" concerts in the summer of 2016 under the banner of "Ritchie Blackmore's Rainbow", his first rock shows since 1997. The new Rainbow line-up was announced on November 6, 2015. It featured <a href="/wiki/Lords_of_Black" title="Lords of Black">Lords of Black</a> singer Ronnie Romero, <a href="/wiki/Stratovarius" title="Stratovarius">Stratovarius</a> keyboardist <a href="/wiki/Jens_Johansson" title="Jens Johansson">Jens Johansson</a>, Blackmore's Night drummer <a href="/wiki/David_Keith_(drummer)" title="David Keith (drummer)">David Keith</a> and bassist Bob Nouveau (Bob Curiano).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClassicRock1_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-ClassicRock1-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The band headlined German edition of the legendary "Monsters of Rock" festival. They debuted on 17 June 2016 at the picturesque Loreley Freilichtbuhne, an open-air show in front of an audience of estimated 15,000. On 18 June they played another open-air date in front of 30,000 fans in Bietigheim-Bissingen (Festplatz am Viadukt). The third and final show took place at the Birmingham <a href="/wiki/Genting_Arena" title="Genting Arena">Genting Arena</a> in England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> During the three shows the band played for estimated 60,000 people.</p>
<p>Asked if Rainbow were planning to record a new album, bassist Bob Curiano replied, "I'd love to go into the studio with this Rainbow. All we need is Ritchie to say, 'Let's go!' I think all of us feel under pressure, because of the fans' expectations. For me, the pressure makes me work harder and get better results."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> However, Blackmore said that they do not have plans for a new album or world tour, and that the reunion is "just a few dates for fun."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Blackmore also said that Rainbow have received many offers to do "few more shows again" in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rainbow will embark on a four-date UK tour in June 2017. The tour will kick off with the band's first show in London since 1995 at the second annual Stone Free Festival at the <a href="/wiki/The_O2_Arena" title="The O2 Arena">The O2</a>, followed by shows in Manchester, Glasgow and Birmingham.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discography">Discography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainbow_(rock_band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Discography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
