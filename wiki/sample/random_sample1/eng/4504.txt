<p><b>Layla Murad</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar"><big>ليلى مراد</big></span>‎‎; <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he" xml:lang="he">לילה מוראד</span>‎‎; February 17, 1918- November 21, 1995) was an <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egyptian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Singer" title="Singer">singer</a> and actress, and one of the most prominent superstars in the Arabic-speaking world in her era. Born "Lillian Zaki Murad Mordechai" to a Jewish Family in Al Daher District, Cairo, Egypt, she later changed her name to Layla Murad as a stage-name. Layla married three times and converted to Islam in 1964.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leila_Mourad&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Layla Murad was born in <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Cairo</a> on February 17, 1918 to Ibrahim Zaki Mordechai and Gamilah Salmon. Her father was a respected singer, musician, and religious cantor, (<a href="/wiki/Hazzan" title="Hazzan">Hazzan</a>). Her mother was a Jewish Egyptian of Polish origins. One of her brothers, <a href="/wiki/Mounir_Mourad" title="Mounir Mourad">Mounir Mourad</a>, was an <a href="/wiki/Actor" title="Actor">actor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Composer" title="Composer">composer</a>.</p>
<p>She made her first stage appearance, aged nine, at the <i>Saalat Badi'a</i>, one of <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>'s most successful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Music_Hall" title="Music Hall">music halls</a>. The theatre had been founded in 1926 by the actress and dancer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Badi%27a_Masabni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Badi'a Masabni (page does not exist)">Badi'a Masabni</a>, who became Mourad's patron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Her first film appearance, aged fifteen, was in the 1932 <i>al-Dahaya</i> (The Victims) which had originally been made as a silent film. Her song, "The Day of Departure", was added as part of the transformation of the production into a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Talkie" title="Talkie">talkie</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>She was trained by her father and <a href="/wiki/Dawood_Hosni" title="Dawood Hosni">Dawood Hosni</a>, who was also Jewish. Hosni had composed the first <a href="/wiki/Operetta" title="Operetta">Operetta</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arabic</a> language, and he composed two songs for Leila: "Hairana Leh Bein El-Eloub" (Why can't you choose from among lovers), and "Howa el dala'a ya'ani khessam" (Does daliance mean avoiding me?). Further success came when the prominent Egyptian composer <a href="/wiki/Mohammed_Abdel_Wahab" title="Mohammed Abdel Wahab">Mohammed Abdel Wahab</a> heard her singing and gave her a role in his film <i>Yahia el Hob</i> (<i>Viva Love!</i>) in 1938. In the six years following the success of <i>Yahia el Hob</i> she made five best selling films with director <a href="/wiki/Togo_Mizrahi" title="Togo Mizrahi">Togo Mizrahi</a>, becoming Egypt's top actress. In 1945 she made <i>Layla Bint al-Fuqara</i> (Layla, daughter of the poor) directed by <a href="/wiki/Anwar_Wagdi" title="Anwar Wagdi">Anwar Wagdi</a> whom she married shortly after. She went on to make a further 20 films of which the most outstanding is <i><a href="/wiki/Ghazal_Al_Banat" title="Ghazal Al Banat">Ghazal al-Banat</a></i> (The Flirtation of Girls), also directed and co-starring Wagdi. It also featured <a href="/wiki/Naguib_el-Rihani" title="Naguib el-Rihani">Nagib al-Rihani</a> and Abdel Wahab in their last appearances on film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1953, she was selected, over <a href="/wiki/Umm_Kulthum" title="Umm Kulthum">Umm Kulthum</a>, as the official singer of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_Revolution_of_1952" title="Egyptian Revolution of 1952">Egyptian revolution</a>. Shortly thereafter, a rumor that Mourad had visited <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, where she had family, and donated money to its military, raised suspicions of spying and caused some Arab radio stations to <a href="/wiki/Boycott" title="Boycott">boycott</a> her. She denied these allegations and when called for judicial investigations, maintained her innocence all along, declaring, "I am an Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beinin.2C_Joel_1998_Page_84_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beinin.2C_Joel_1998_Page_84-4">[4]</a></sup> No proof was found that she had contributed money to Israel's military; the Egyptian government investigated and concluded that the charges against the singer were without foundation.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beinin.2C_Joel_1998_Page_84_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beinin.2C_Joel_1998_Page_84-4">[4]</a></sup> but there was no mention of the fact that she did not contribute to Israel as a country, or at least send money to her relatives in Israel .</p>
<p>Some historians claim that Egyptian President Gamal Abdel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasser" title="Nasser">Nasser</a> insisted that <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> end their <a href="/wiki/Boycott" title="Boycott">boycott</a> of her songs and films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beinin.2C_Joel_1998_Page_84_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Beinin.2C_Joel_1998_Page_84-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her decision to retire, aged 38, came with the failure of her last film, <i>Al Habib al Majhoul</i> (The Unknown Lover), the banning of her song, "With Unity, Order, and Work", praising the <a href="/wiki/Free_Officers_Movement_(Egypt)" title="Free Officers Movement (Egypt)">Free Officers 1952 revolution</a> and the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Suez_Crisis" title="Suez Crisis">1956 war</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Despite the immense popularity of her films her singing career was over-shadowed by Um Kulthum who dominated Egypt's musical landscape and, in 1949, had become president of the Musicians' Union. In the early 1950s other singers also popular with younger audiences, such as <a href="/wiki/Abdel_Halim_Hafez" title="Abdel Halim Hafez">Abdel al Halim Hafez</a>, did not get the same exposure on the radio as Um Kulthum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leila Mourad's relationship with her family was not an easy one, possibly due to her conversion to Islam. Between 1967 and 1970, hundreds of Egyptian Jewish males were deported to the prisons of <a href="/wiki/Abu_Zaabal" title="Abu Zaabal">Abu Zaabal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tura,_Egypt" title="Tura, Egypt">Tura</a>, including Leila's brother, Isak Zaki. Families of the detainees were allowed to visit beginning in 1968, and some noted that Leila was never seen visiting her brother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leila Mourad made a few brief reappearances during Ramadan in 1970, when she was scheduled to read Salah Jaheen's "Fawazeer Ramadan" (Ramadan' puzzles), a daily traditional radio program held during the Holy month of Ramadan.</p>
<p>Leila Mourad died in a hospital in Cairo in 1995.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriages">Marriages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leila_Mourad&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marriages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Leila Mourad married <a href="/wiki/Anwar_Wagdi" title="Anwar Wagdi">Anwar Wagdi</a> (1947–1954), over the objection of her father. They were married and divorced three times. Leila gives the reason for her divorces as the fact that she was not fully aware of the seriousness of Wagdi's illness, one that made him constantly irritable and difficult to live with. Later she married Waguih Abaza, and then film director <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fatin_Abdul_Wahab&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fatin Abdul Wahab (page does not exist)">Fatin Abdul Wahab</a> and she gave birth to their son Zaki Fatin Abdul Wahab, and finally divorced in 1969.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Her <b>movies</b> include:</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leila_Mourad&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ramadan" title="Ramadan">Ramadan</a> television series "Ana Albi Dalili", about Leila Mourad, debuted in 2009. It is an Egyptian production headed by Syrian director <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Muhamad_Zuhair_Rajab&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Muhamad Zuhair Rajab (page does not exist)">Muhamad Zuhair Rajab</a>. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Safa_Sultan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Safa Sultan (page does not exist)">Safa Sultan</a>, a Syrian actress, plays Leila Mourad. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ahmed_Flukhs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ahmed Flukhs (page does not exist)">Ahmed Flukhs</a> portrays <a href="/wiki/Anwar_Wagdi" title="Anwar Wagdi">Anwar Wagdi</a>. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Izat_Abu_Oof&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Izat Abu Oof (page does not exist)">Izat Abu Oof</a>, an Egyptian actor, portrays <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zaki_Mourad&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zaki Mourad (page does not exist)">Zaki Mourad</a> and Hala Fahr portrays Miriam, the aunt of Leila Mourad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
