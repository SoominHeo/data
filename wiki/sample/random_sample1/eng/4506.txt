<p>In <a href="/wiki/Fluid_mechanics" title="Fluid mechanics">fluid mechanics</a>, the <b>Rayleigh number</b> (<b>Ra</b>) for a fluid is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimensionless_number" title="Dimensionless number">dimensionless number</a> associated with buoyancy-driven flow, also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_convection" title="Free convection">free convection</a> or natural convection. When the Rayleigh number is below a critical value for that fluid, heat transfer is primarily in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_conduction" title="Heat conduction">conduction</a>; when it exceeds the critical value, heat transfer is primarily in the form of <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>.</p>
<p>The Rayleigh number is named after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Strutt,_3rd_Baron_Rayleigh" title="John Strutt, 3rd Baron Rayleigh">Lord Rayleigh</a> and is defined as the product of the <a href="/wiki/Grashof_number" title="Grashof number">Grashof number</a>, which describes the relationship between <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> within a fluid, and the <a href="/wiki/Prandtl_number" title="Prandtl number">Prandtl number</a>, which describes the relationship between momentum diffusivity and thermal diffusivity. Hence the Rayleigh number itself may also be viewed as the ratio of buoyancy and viscosity forces multiplied by the ratio of momentum and thermal diffusivities.</p>

<dd><i>R</i> is the solidification or isotherm speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>A-segregates are predicted to form when the Rayleigh number exceeds a certain critical value. This critical value is independent of the composition of the alloy, and this is the main advantage of the Rayleigh number criterion over other criteria for prediction of convectional instabilities, such as Suzuki criterion.</p>
<p>Torabi Rad et. Al. showed that for steel alloys the critical Rayleigh number is 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Pickering et. al explored Torabi Rad's criterion, and further verified its effectiveness. Critical Rayleigh numbers for lead–tin and nickel-based super-alloys were also developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geophysical_applications">Geophysical applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rayleigh_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geophysical applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">geophysics</a>, the Rayleigh number is of fundamental importance: it indicates the presence and strength of convection within a fluid body such as the Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" title="Earth's mantle">mantle</a>.
