<p><b>Reza Pahlavi, Crown Prince of Iran</b> (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">رضا پهلوی</span>‎‎, born 31 October 1960) was the last heir apparent to the defunct throne of the <a href="/wiki/Pahlavi_dynasty" title="Pahlavi dynasty">Imperial State of Iran</a> and is the current head of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Pahlavi" title="House of Pahlavi">House of Pahlavi</a>. He is the older son of the late <a href="/wiki/Shah" title="Shah">Shah</a> <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_Reza_Pahlavi" title="Mohammad Reza Pahlavi">Mohammad Reza Pahlavi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shahbanu" title="Shahbanu">Shahbanu</a> <a href="/wiki/Farah_Pahlavi" title="Farah Pahlavi">Farah Pahlavi</a>. A resident of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, he is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heir" title="Heir">heir</a> to the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persian</a> throne. Reza Pahlavi is the founder and leader of <a href="/wiki/National_Council_of_Iran" title="National Council of Iran">National Council of Iran</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Government_in_exile" title="Government in exile">government in exile</a> of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. As Crown Prince of Iran, Reza Pahlavi left Iran at the age of 17 for air force training at Reese Air Force Base near Lubbock, Texas,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> two years before the <a href="/wiki/Iranian_Revolution" title="Iranian Revolution">Iranian Revolution</a>. According to <a href="/wiki/Afshin_Ellian" title="Afshin Ellian">Afshin Ellian</a>, an Iranian-born philosopher of law and expert in international law, "in Iran, there are two names known to virtually all, even in the most remote villages. The first name is Khamenei and the second one is Reza Pahlavi."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2011, Reza Pahlavi was named <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>'s Person Of The Year by an online poll conducted by <a href="/wiki/Radio_Farda" title="Radio Farda">Radio Farda</a> that included thousands of Iranian respondents inside and outside Iran.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In November 2014, Reza Pahlavi founded his own <a href="/wiki/Television_network" title="Television network">television</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radio_network" title="Radio network">radio network</a> called OfoghIran.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reza_Pahlavi,_Crown_Prince_of_Iran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cyrus_small.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="325" height="271" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Cyrus_small.JPG/220px-Cyrus_small.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fa/Cyrus_small.JPG 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cyrus_small.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Reza Pahlavi in 1973 as Crown Prince of Iran</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Reza Pahlavi was born in <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, the eldest <a href="/wiki/Legitimacy_(family_law)" title="Legitimacy (family law)">legitimate</a> son of <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_Reza_Pahlavi" title="Mohammad Reza Pahlavi">Mohammad Reza Pahlavi</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shah_of_Iran" title="Shah of Iran">Shah of Iran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Farah_Pahlavi" title="Farah Pahlavi">Farah Pahlavi</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Shahbanu" title="Shahbanu">Shahbanu</a> of Iran. Reza Pahlavi's siblings include his sister <a href="/wiki/Farahnaz_Pahlavi" title="Farahnaz Pahlavi">Princess Farahnaz Pahlavi</a> (born 12 March 1963), brother <a href="/wiki/Ali-Reza_Pahlavi" title="Ali-Reza Pahlavi">Prince Ali-Reza Pahlavi</a> (28 April 1966 – 4 January 2011), and sister <a href="/wiki/Leila_Pahlavi" title="Leila Pahlavi">Princess Leila Pahlavi</a> (27 March 1970 – 10 June 2001), as well as a half-sister, <a href="/wiki/Shahnaz_Pahlavi" title="Shahnaz Pahlavi">Princess Shahnaz Pahlavi</a> (born 27 October 1941).</p>
<p>Accepted into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Iranian_Air_Force" title="Imperial Iranian Air Force">Imperial Iranian Air Force</a> as a junior officer following secondary schooling, he left Iran in 1977 at the age of 17 for air force flight training in the United States.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He spent a year at <a href="/wiki/Williams_College" title="Williams College">Williams College</a> in the United States, but was forced to leave because of the turmoil in Iran.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> With the monarchy overthrown and an Islamic Republic established, Reza Pahlavi did not return to Iran.</p>
<p>He obtained a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_science" title="Bachelor of science">BSc</a> degree in <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a> by correspondence from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a>, because Williams did not offer that option.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Crown Prince successfully completed the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undergraduate_Pilot_Training" title="Undergraduate Pilot Training">Undergraduate Pilot Training</a> (UPT) Program at the former <a href="/wiki/Reese_Air_Force_Base" title="Reese Air Force Base">Reese Air Force Base</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lubbock,_Texas" title="Lubbock, Texas">Lubbock, Texas</a>. Shortly thereafter, at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93Iraq_War" title="Iran–Iraq War">Iran–Iraq War</a>, Reza Pahlavi, wrote to General <a href="/wiki/Valiollah_Fallahi" title="Valiollah Fallahi">Valiollah Fallahi</a>, Chief Commander of the Armed Forces of the Islamic Republic, offering to fly and fight as a pilot for the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Republic_of_Iran_Air_Force" title="Islamic Republic of Iran Air Force">Islamic Republic of Iran Air Force</a>. His offer was rebuffed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the death of his father on 27 July 1980, Reza Pahlavi became the Head of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Pahlavi" title="House of Pahlavi">House of Pahlavi</a>.</p>
<p>Crown Prince Reza Pahlavi has written three books on the state of Iran.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>Reza married <a href="/wiki/Yasmine_Pahlavi" title="Yasmine Pahlavi">Yasmine Etemad-Amini</a> on 12 June 1986. Yasmine, a graduate of the <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_University" title="George Washington University">George Washington University</a> School of Law, worked for ten years as a lawyer for the Children’s Law Center as a legal advocate for at-risk youth. Yasmine also founded the Foundation for the Children of Iran in 1991, a non-profit foundation that provides health care services to Iranian children or children of Iranian origin.</p>
<p>In 2004, Reza Pahlavi was named as the "unofficial <a href="/wiki/Godparent" title="Godparent">godfather</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Princess_Louise_of_Belgium_(b._2004)" title="Princess Louise of Belgium (b. 2004)">Princess Louise of Belgium</a>, the eighth granddaughter of King <a href="/wiki/Albert_II_of_Belgium" title="Albert II of Belgium">Albert II of Belgium</a>. The decision to choose him was criticized by the Foreign Ministry of the Islamic Republic of Iran.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reza_Pahlavi,_Crown_Prince_of_Iran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Reza Pahlavi II has used his high profile as an Iranian abroad to campaign for <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a>, democracy and unity among Iranians in and outside Iran.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> On his website he calls for a separation of religion and state in Iran and for free and fair elections "for all freedom-loving individuals and political ideologies". He exhorts all groups dedicated to a democratic agenda to work together for a democratic and secular Iranian government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rezapahlavi.com_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rezapahlavi.com-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Reza Bayegan, Reza Pahlavi believes in the separation of religion from politics. However, he avoids the "<a href="/wiki/Islamophobia" title="Islamophobia">Islam bashing</a>" that Bayegan writes occurs in some circles of the Iranian opposition. Rather, he believes that religion has a humanizing and ethical role in shaping individual character and infusing society with greater purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-payvand_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-payvand-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of Iranian clergy, such as <a href="/wiki/Hossein_Kazemeyni_Boroujerdi" title="Hossein Kazemeyni Boroujerdi">Hossein Kazemeyni Boroujerdi</a>, who oppose the politicization of Islam, support Reza Pahlavi.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Mujahedin_of_Iran" title="People's Mujahedin of Iran">People's Mujahedin of Iran</a>'s most important competitor among Iranian exiles is Reza Pahlavi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Massoud_Rajavi" title="Massoud Rajavi">Massoud Rajavi</a>, leader of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mujahedin" title="Mujahedin">Mujahedin</a>, who once spoke against US then-president <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> for visiting Reza Pahlavi in a restaurant, asked Reza Pahlavi for his support. <a href="/wiki/Massoud_Rajavi" title="Massoud Rajavi">Massoud Rajavi</a> directly asked Reza Pahlavi to help the People's Mujahedin of Iran for its removal from terror list of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">United States Department of State</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The European Union, Canada and the United States formerly listed the MEK as a terrorist organization, but this designation has since been lifted, first by the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_the_European_Union" title="Council of the European Union">Council of the European Union</a> on 26 January 2009 (following what the group called a "seven-year-long legal and political battle"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Runner_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Runner-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UPI2009_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-UPI2009-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters2009_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters2009-15">[15]</a></sup> then by a decision by U.S. Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_2012_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_2012-16">[16]</a></sup> on 21 September 2012 and lastly by a decision by the Canadian government on 20 December 2012.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reza_Pahlavi,_Crown_Prince_of_Iran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When interviewed about religion, Pahlavi said, "That's a private matter; but if you must know, I am, of course, by education and by conviction, a Shia Muslim. I am very much a man of faith."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Iranian writer Reza Bayegan also notes that Crown Prince Reza is deeply attached to his <a href="/wiki/Shia_Islam" title="Shia Islam">Shi'a Muslim</a> faith. He has performed the <a href="/wiki/Hajj" title="Hajj">Hajj</a> (pilgrimage) to <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-payvand_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-payvand-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_comments">2011 comments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reza_Pahlavi,_Crown_Prince_of_Iran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2011 comments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In February 2011, after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2011_Iranian_protests" title="2011 Iranian protests">violence erupted</a> in Tehran, Reza Pahlavi II said that Iran's youth were determined to get rid of an authoritarian government tainted by corruption and misrule in the hope of installing a democracy. "Fundamental and necessary change is long overdue for our region and we have a whole generation of young Egyptians and Iranians not willing to take no for an answer", he told <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i>. "Democratisation is now an imperative that cannot be denied. It is only a matter of time before the whole region can transform itself."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Monarchy">Monarchy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reza_Pahlavi,_Crown_Prince_of_Iran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Monarchy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Following in a line of Persian dynasties stretching back 3,000 years, the <a href="/wiki/Pahlavi_dynasty" title="Pahlavi dynasty">Pahlavi dynasty</a> was founded early in the twentieth century. The 1979 revolution replaced the monarchy with an <a href="/wiki/Islamic_republic" title="Islamic republic">Islamic republic</a>. After the death of his father, <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_Reza_Pahlavi" title="Mohammad Reza Pahlavi">Mohammad Reza Pahlavi</a>, he symbolically declared himself <i>Shāhanshāh</i> (literally <i><a href="/wiki/King_of_Kings" title="King of Kings">King of Kings</a></i> in Persian) at the age of 21.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> He remains crown prince according to the former Constitution of 1906, as he is required to take the oath in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iranian_Parliament" title="Iranian Parliament">Iranian Parliament</a> first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> His press releases refer to him as either "Reza Pahlavi" or "the former Crown Prince of Iran".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On his website, Reza Pahlavi has said that the state of Iran should become democratic and secular, and <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> should be respected. Whether the form of government would be that of a constitutional monarchy or a republic is something that he would like to leave up to the people of Iran.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Succession">Succession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reza_Pahlavi,_Crown_Prince_of_Iran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Succession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Line_of_succession_to_the_Iranian_throne" title="Line of succession to the Iranian throne">Line of succession to the Iranian throne</a></div>
<p>Reza Pahlavi II is first in the line of succession to his late father, while his younger brother <a href="/wiki/Ali-Reza_Pahlavi" title="Ali-Reza Pahlavi">Ali-Reza Pahlavi II</a> was second in line until his death in January 2011. His first cousin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Patrick_Ali_Pahlavi" title="Prince Patrick Ali Pahlavi">Prince Patrick Ali Pahlavi</a> is now next in line to the throne.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_and_legal_issues">Business and legal issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reza_Pahlavi,_Crown_Prince_of_Iran&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Business and legal issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Reza Pahlavi is the owner of Medina Development Company. He and his company were engaged in a civil lawsuit against a family member in the 1990s culminating in a favorable judgment in May 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
