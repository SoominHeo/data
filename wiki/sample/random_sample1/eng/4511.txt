<p><b>Lechuguilla Cave</b> is, as of June 2013, with 138.3 miles (222.6 km) the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longest_cave" title="Longest cave">seventh-longest</a> explored <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">cave</a> in the world<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gulden_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gulden-1">[1]</a></sup> and the second deepest (1,604 feet or 489 meters) in the continental <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> after the Tears of the Turtle Cave in the <a href="/wiki/Bob_Marshall_Wilderness" title="Bob Marshall Wilderness">Bob Marshall Wilderness</a> of <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deep_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-deep-2">[2]</a></sup> but it is most famous for its unusual <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, rare <a href="/wiki/Speleothem" title="Speleothem">formations</a>, and pristine condition.</p>
<p>The cave is named for <i><a href="/wiki/Agave_lechuguilla" title="Agave lechuguilla">Agave lechuguilla</a></i>, a species of plant found near its entrance. Lechuguilla is in <a href="/wiki/Carlsbad_Caverns_National_Park" title="Carlsbad Caverns National Park">Carlsbad Caverns National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>. Access to the cave is limited to approved scientific researchers, survey and exploration teams, and <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> management-related trips.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration_history">Exploration history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lechuguilla_Cave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Exploration history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lechuguilla Cave was known until 1986 as a small, insignificant historic site in the park's back country. Small amounts of bat <a href="/wiki/Guano" title="Guano">guano</a> were mined from the entrance passages for a year under a mining claim filed in 1914. The historic cave contained a 90-foot (27 m) entrance pit known as Misery Hole, which led to 400 feet (122 m) of dry dead-end passages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps-lech_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-nps-lech-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cave was visited infrequently after mining activities ceased. However, in the 1950s, cavers heard wind roaring up from the rubble-choked cave floor. Although there was no obvious route, people concluded that cave passages lay below the rubble. Led by Dave Allured, a group of cavers from the Colorado <a href="/wiki/Grotto_(National_Speleological_Society)" title="Grotto (National Speleological Society)">Grotto</a> gained permission from the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> and began digging in 1984. Since the breakthrough into large walking passages occurred on May 26, 1986,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps-lech_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-nps-lech-3">[3]</a></sup> explorers have mapped over 138 miles (222 km) of passages,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gulden_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gulden-1">[1]</a></sup> making Lechuguilla the 7th longest cave in the world, and 4th longest in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gulden_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gulden-1">[1]</a></sup> Lechugilla was also the deepest cave in the continental United States 1,604 feet (489 m)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deep_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-deep-2">[2]</a></sup> until the exploration of Tears of the Turtle Cave in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Drawn by the cave's pristine condition and rare beauty, cavers come from around the world to explore and map its passages and geology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps-lech_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-nps-lech-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2012 a team led by Derek Bristol of Colorado climbed over 410 feet (120 m) into a dome and discovered several new, unexplored passages, pits and large rooms. This new section was named "Oz" and many of its features were named after items from <a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">The Wizard of Oz</a>. The discovery included a large room measuring 600 feet (180 m) long, up to 150 feet (46 m) wide and up to 150 feet (46 m) high. It was named "Munchkinland". A pit, named "Kansas Twister", at over 510 feet (160 m) from floor to ceiling, is the deepest pit yet discovered in the park. The team spent eight days mapping Oz, adding the largest distance to the survey since 1989, and bringing the total length to 134.6 miles (216.6 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lechuguilla_Cave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lechuguilla_Cave_Pearlsian_Gulf.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="681" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Lechuguilla_Cave_Pearlsian_Gulf.jpg/220px-Lechuguilla_Cave_Pearlsian_Gulf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Lechuguilla_Cave_Pearlsian_Gulf.jpg/330px-Lechuguilla_Cave_Pearlsian_Gulf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Lechuguilla_Cave_Pearlsian_Gulf.jpg/440px-Lechuguilla_Cave_Pearlsian_Gulf.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Lechuguilla Cave offers more than extreme size. It holds a variety of rare <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speleothems" title="Speleothems">speleothems</a>, including lemon-yellow <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> deposits, 20 feet (6.1 m) <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> chandeliers, 20 feet (6.1 m) gypsum hairs and beards, 15 feet (4.6 m) <a href="/wiki/Soda_straw" title="Soda straw">soda straws</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydromagnesite" title="Hydromagnesite">hydromagnesite</a> balloons, <a href="/wiki/Cave_pearl" title="Cave pearl">cave pearls</a>, subaqueous <a href="/wiki/Helictite" title="Helictite">helictites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rusticle" title="Rusticle">rusticles</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=U-loop&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="U-loop (page does not exist)">U-loops</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J-loop&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="J-loop (page does not exist)">J-loops</a>. Lechuguilla Cave surpasses its nearby sister, <a href="/wiki/Carlsbad_Caverns_National_Park" title="Carlsbad Caverns National Park">Carlsbad Caverns</a>, in size, depth, and variety of speleothems, though no room has been discovered yet in Lechuguilla Cave that is larger than Carlsbad's Big Room.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps-lech_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-nps-lech-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scientific exploration has been conducted. For the first time, a <a href="/wiki/Guadalupe_Mountains" title="Guadalupe Mountains">Guadalupe Mountains</a> cave extends deep enough that scientists may study five separate geologic formations from the inside. The profusion of gypsum and sulfur lends support to <a href="/wiki/Speleogenesis" title="Speleogenesis">speleogenesis</a> by <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> dissolution. The sulfuric acid is believed to be derived from <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> that migrated from nearby oil deposits. Therefore this cavern formed from the bottom up, in contrast to the normal top-down <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> dissolution mechanism of cave formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps-lech_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-nps-lech-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lechuguilla Cave lies beneath a park wilderness area. The cave's passages may extend out of the park into adjacent <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Land_Management" title="Bureau of Land Management">Bureau of Land Management</a> (BLM) land. A major threat to the cave is proposed gas and oil drilling on BLM land. Any leakage of gas or fluids into the cave's passages could kill cave life or cause explosions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps-lech_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-nps-lech-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microbiology">Microbiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lechuguilla_Cave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Microbiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rare, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemolithoautotroph" title="Chemolithoautotroph">chemolithoautotrophic</a> bacteria are believed to occur in the cave. These bacteria feed on the <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> minerals and may assist in enlarging the cave and determining the shapes of unusual speleothems. The claim in the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> documentary series <a href="/wiki/Planet_Earth_(TV_series)" title="Planet Earth (TV series)"><i>Planet Earth</i></a> that these bacteria do not derive any energy from the sun is incorrect, as the majority are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfur-oxidizing_bacteria" title="Sulfur-oxidizing bacteria">sulfur-oxidizing bacteria</a> that utilize <i>primarily</i> atmospheric oxygen (derived from sunlight-driven photosynthesis) as an electron acceptor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other studies indicate that some microbes may have medicinal qualities that are beneficial to humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps-lech_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-nps-lech-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of the bacteria strains isolated from the cave have been found to harbor novel pathways for antibiotic resistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bhullar_2012_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bhullar_2012-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pawlowski_2016_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pawlowski_2016-9">[9]</a></sup> A 4 million year-old strain of <i><a href="/wiki/Paenibacillus" title="Paenibacillus">Paenibacillus</a></i> isolated from soil samples in Lechuguilla Cave was found to be naturally resistant to many modern antibiotics, including <a href="/wiki/Daptomycin" title="Daptomycin">daptomycin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pawlowski_2016_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pawlowski_2016-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lechuguilla_Cave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lechuguilla Cave was shown in the BBC documentary series <a href="/wiki/Planet_Earth_(TV_series)" title="Planet Earth (TV series)"><i>Planet Earth</i></a>. The fourth episode, titled "Caves", airing on April 22, 2007, documented scientists and filmmakers exploring Lechuguilla Cave, including the Chandelier Ballroom, which has high-quality crystals. It took the team two years to get permission to film, and local authorities are unlikely to allow another film crew to enter in the foreseeable future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planetearth_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-planetearth-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Denver_Museum_of_Nature_and_Science" title="Denver Museum of Nature and Science">Denver Museum of Nature and Science</a> (then known as the Denver Museum of Natural History) filmed one of the first documentaries in the cave, titled <i>Lechuguilla Cave: The Hidden Giant</i>, in 1987, featuring many of the cavers responsible for the breakthrough and initial survey work. The video was broadcast on the Denver PBS station KRMA in 1989 and was available for purchase at the Denver Museum until the early 1990s.</p>
<p>The 1992 <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic Society</a> program titled <i>Mysteries Underground</i> was also filmed extensively in Lechuguilla Cave.</p>
