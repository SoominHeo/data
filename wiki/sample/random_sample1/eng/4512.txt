<p><b>Recitative</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span></span>/</a></span></span>, also known by its Italian name "recitativo" (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[retʃitaˈtiːvo]</a></span>)) is a style of delivery (much used in <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">operas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oratorio" title="Oratorio">oratorios</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cantata" title="Cantata">cantatas</a>) in which a singer is allowed to adopt the rhythms of ordinary speech. Recitative does not repeat lines as formally composed songs do. It resembles sung ordinary speech more than a formal musical composition.</p>
<p>Recitative can be distinguished on a continuum from more speech-like to more musical. The mostly syllabic <b>recitativo secco</b> ("dry", accompanied only by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basso_continuo" title="Basso continuo">continuo</a>) is at one end of a spectrum through <b>recitativo accompagnato</b> (using orchestra), the more <a href="/wiki/Melisma" title="Melisma">melismatic</a> <a href="/wiki/Arioso" title="Arioso">arioso</a>, and finally the full-blown <a href="/wiki/Aria" title="Aria">aria</a> or ensemble, where the pulse is entirely governed by the music.</p>
<p>The term recitative (or occasionally liturgical recitative) is also applied to the simpler <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Reciting_%CE%98yy_tone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Reciting Θyy tone (page does not exist)">formulas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_chant" title="Gregorian chant">Gregorian chant</a>, such as the tones used for the <a href="/wiki/Epistle" title="Epistle">Epistle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">Gospel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Preface_(liturgy)" title="Preface (liturgy)">preface</a> and <a href="/wiki/Collect" title="Collect">collects</a>; see <a href="/wiki/Accentus" title="Accentus">accentus</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Recitative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first use of recitative in <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a> was preceded by the <a href="/wiki/Monody" title="Monody">monodies</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Florentine_Camerata" title="Florentine Camerata">Florentine Camerata</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Vincenzo_Galilei" title="Vincenzo Galilei">Vincenzo Galilei</a>, father of the astronomer <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a>, played an important role. The elder Galilei, influenced by his correspondence with <a href="/wiki/Girolamo_Mei" title="Girolamo Mei">Girolamo Mei</a> on the writings of the ancient Greeks and with <a href="/wiki/Erycius_Puteanus" title="Erycius Puteanus">Erycius Puteanus</a> on the writings of <a href="/wiki/Hucbald" title="Hucbald">Hucbald</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and wishing to recreate the old manner of storytelling and drama, pioneered the use of a single melodic line to tell the story, accompanied by simple chords from a harpsichord or lute.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">baroque</a> era, recitatives were commonly rehearsed on their own by the stage director, the singers frequently supplying their own favourite <a href="/wiki/Insertion_aria" title="Insertion aria">baggage arias</a> which might be by a different composer (some of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozart" title="Mozart">Mozart</a>'s so-called concert arias fall into this category). This division of labour persisted in some of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rossini" title="Rossini">Rossini</a>'s most famous works; the recitatives for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Barber_of_Seville" title="The Barber of Seville">The Barber of Seville</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/La_Cenerentola" title="La Cenerentola">La Cenerentola</a></i> were composed by assistants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Secco">Secco</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Recitative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Secco">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Secco recitative, popularized in Florence though the proto-opera music dramas of <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Peri" title="Jacopo Peri">Jacopo Peri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giulio_Caccini" title="Giulio Caccini">Giulio Caccini</a> during the late 16th century, formed the substance of <a href="/wiki/Claudio_Monteverdi" title="Claudio Monteverdi">Claudio Monteverdi</a>'s operas during the 17th century, and continued to be used into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanticism_(music)" title="Romanticism (music)">Romantic era</a> by such composers as <a href="/wiki/Gaetano_Donizetti" title="Gaetano Donizetti">Gaetano Donizetti</a>, reappearing in Stravinsky's <a href="/wiki/The_Rake%27s_Progress" title="The Rake's Progress">The Rake's Progress</a>. It also influenced areas of music outside opera from the outset; the recitatives of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Johann Sebastian Bach</a>, found in his passions and <a href="/wiki/Cantata" title="Cantata">cantatas</a>, are especially notable.</p>
<p>In the early operas and cantatas of the Florentine school, secco recitative was accompanied by a variety of instruments, mostly plucked strings including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chitarrone" title="Chitarrone">chitarrone</a> with perhaps a small organ to provide sustained tone. Later, in the operas of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vivaldi" title="Vivaldi">Vivaldi</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H%C3%A4ndel" title="Händel">Händel</a>, the accompaniment was standardised as a <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichord</a> and a bass <a href="/wiki/Viol" title="Viol">viol</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Violoncello" title="Violoncello">violoncello</a>. When the harpsichord went out of use in the early 19th century, many opera-houses did not replace it with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pianoforte" title="Pianoforte">piano</a>; instead the violoncello was left to carry on alone or with reinforcement from a <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double bass</a>. A 1919 recording of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rossini" title="Rossini">Rossini</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barber_of_Seville" title="Barber of Seville">Barber of Seville</a></i>, issued by Italian <a href="/wiki/HMV" title="HMV">HMV</a>, gives a unique glimpse of this technique in action, as do <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cello</a> methods of the period and some scores of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meyerbeer" title="Meyerbeer">Meyerbeer</a>. There are examples of the revival of the harpsichord for this purpose as early as the 1890s (e.g. by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Richter_(conductor)" title="Hans Richter (conductor)">Hans Richter</a> for a production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozart" title="Mozart">Mozart</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Giovanni" title="Don Giovanni">Don Giovanni</a></i> at the London <a href="/wiki/Royal_Opera_House" title="Royal Opera House">Royal Opera House</a>, the instrument being supplied by <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Dolmetsch" title="Arnold Dolmetsch">Arnold Dolmetsch</a>), but it was not until the 1950s that the 18th-century method was consistently observed once more.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accompagnato_.28or_obbligato.29">Accompagnato (or obbligato)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Recitative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Accompagnato (or obbligato)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Accompanied recitative, known as <i>accompagnato</i> or <i>stromentato</i>, employs the <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">orchestra</a> as an accompanying body. As a result, it is less improvisational and declamatory than <i>recitativo secco</i>, and more <a href="/wiki/Song" title="Song">song</a>-like. This form is often employed where the orchestra can underscore a particularly dramatic text, as in <i><a href="/wiki/Messiah_Part_I#5" title="Messiah Part I">Thus saith the Lord</a></i> from Händel's <i><a href="/wiki/Messiah_(Handel)" title="Messiah (Handel)">Messiah</a></i>; <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Joseph Haydn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart</a> were also fond of it. A more inward intensification calls for an <a href="/wiki/Arioso" title="Arioso">arioso</a>; the opening of <i><a href="/wiki/Messiah_Part_I#2" title="Messiah Part I">Comfort ye</a></i> from the same work is a famous example, while the ending of it ("The voice of him that crieth in the wilderness") is secco.</p>
<p>Sometimes a distinction is made between the more dramatic, expressive, or interjecting <i>orchestral recitative</i> (<i>recitativo obbligato</i> or <i>stromentato</i>) and a more passive and sustained <i>accompanied recitative</i> (<i>recitativo accompagnato</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Wagner_uses">Post-Wagner uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Recitative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post-Wagner uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Later operas, under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>, favored <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Through-composition" title="Through-composition">through-composition</a>, where recitatives, arias, choruses and other elements were seamlessly interwoven into a whole. Many of Wagner's operas employ sections which are analogous to accompanied recitative.</p>
<p>Recitative is also occasionally used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musicals" title="Musicals">musicals</a>, being put to ironic use in the finale of <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Weill" title="Kurt Weill">Kurt Weill</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Threepenny_Opera" title="The Threepenny Opera">The Threepenny Opera</a></i>. It also appears in <i><a href="/wiki/Carousel_(musical)" title="Carousel (musical)">Carousel</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Of_Thee_I_Sing" title="Of Thee I Sing">Of Thee I Sing</a></i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/George_Gershwin" title="George Gershwin">George Gershwin</a> used it in his opera <i><a href="/wiki/Porgy_and_Bess" title="Porgy and Bess">Porgy and Bess</a></i>, though sometimes the recitative in that work is changed to spoken dialogue. <i>Porgy and Bess</i> has also been staged as a musical rather than as an opera.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instrumental_recitative">Instrumental recitative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Recitative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Instrumental recitative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Recitative has also sometimes been used to refer to parts of purely instrumental works which resemble vocal recitatives. One of the earliest examples is found in the slow movement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vivaldi" title="Vivaldi">Vivaldi</a>'s violin concerto in D, RV 208 which is marked 'Recitative', although it is perhaps more virtuosic and flashy than most operatic recitative. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C._P._E._Bach" title="C. P. E. Bach">C. P. E. Bach</a> included instrumental recitative in his "Prussian" piano sonatas of 1742, composed at <a href="/wiki/Frederick_the_Great" title="Frederick the Great">Frederick the Great</a>'s court in Berlin. In 1761, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Joseph Haydn</a> took his post at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esterhazy_Palace" title="Esterhazy Palace">Esterhazy Palace</a> and soon after composed his Symphony No. 7 ("Le Midi") in concertante style (i.e. with soloists). In the second movement of that work, the violinist is the soloist in an instrumental recitative.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Ludwig van Beethoven</a> used the instrumental recitative in at least three works, including <i><a href="/wiki/Piano_Sonata_No._17_(Beethoven)" title="Piano Sonata No. 17 (Beethoven)">Piano Sonata No. 17</a></i> (<i>The Tempest</i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Piano_Sonata_No._31_(Beethoven)" title="Piano Sonata No. 31 (Beethoven)">Piano Sonata No. 31</a></i>, and perhaps most famously in the opening section of the Finale of his <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._9_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 9 (Beethoven)">Ninth Symphony</a>. Here, Beethoven inscribed on the score (in French) "In the manner of a recitative, but in tempo." Leon Plantinga argues that the second movement of Beethoven's <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_No._4_(Beethoven)" title="Piano Concerto No. 4 (Beethoven)">Fourth Piano Concerto</a> is also an instrumental recitative,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> although Owen Jander interprets it as a dialogue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Other <a href="/wiki/Romantic_music" title="Romantic music">Romantic</a> composers to employ instrumental recitative include <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Rimsky-Korsakov" title="Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov">Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov</a> (who composed a lyrical, virtuosic recitative for solo violin with <a href="/wiki/Harp" title="Harp">harp</a> accompaniment to represent the <a href="/wiki/Scheherazade" title="Scheherazade">title character</a> in his orchestral <i><a href="/wiki/Scheherazade_(Rimsky-Korsakov)" title="Scheherazade (Rimsky-Korsakov)">Scheherazade</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Hector_Berlioz" title="Hector Berlioz">Hector Berlioz</a> (whose <a href="/wiki/Choral_symphony" title="Choral symphony">choral symphony</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Rom%C3%A9o_et_Juliette_(Berlioz)" title="Roméo et Juliette (Berlioz)">Roméo et Juliette</a></i> contains a <a href="/wiki/Trombone" title="Trombone">trombone</a> recitative as part of its Introduction).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schoenberg" title="Arnold Schoenberg">Arnold Schoenberg</a> labeled the last of his <a href="/wiki/Five_Pieces_for_Orchestra" title="Five Pieces for Orchestra">Five Pieces for Orchestra</a>, Op. 16, as "<i>Das obligate Rezitativ</i>" (English: "The <a href="/wiki/Obbligato" title="Obbligato">obbligato</a> recitative"), and also composed a piece for <a href="/wiki/Organ_(music)" title="Organ (music)">organ</a>, <i>Variations on a Recitative</i>, Op. 40. Other examples of instrumental recitative in twentieth century music include the third movement of <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Moore" title="Douglas Moore">Douglas Moore</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Quintet_for_clarinet_and_strings_(Moore)" title="Quintet for clarinet and strings (Moore)">Quintet for Clarinet and Strings</a> (1946), the first of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Rodney_Bennett" title="Richard Rodney Bennett">Richard Rodney Bennett</a>'s <i>Five <a href="/wiki/Impromptu" title="Impromptu">Impromptus</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a> (1968), the opening section of the last movement of <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Britten" title="Benjamin Britten">Benjamin Britten</a>'s <a href="/wiki/String_Quartet_No._3_(Britten)" title="String Quartet No. 3 (Britten)">String Quartet No. 3</a> (1975), and the second of <a href="/wiki/William_Bolcom" title="William Bolcom">William Bolcom</a>'s <i>12 New Etudes for Piano</i> (1977–86).</p>
