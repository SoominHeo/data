<p><b>Georg Joachim de Porris</b>, also known as <b>Rheticus</b> (16 February 1514 – 4 December 1574), was a <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartographer" title="Cartographer">cartographer</a>, navigational-instrument maker, medical practitioner, and teacher. He is perhaps best known for his <a href="/wiki/Trigonometry" title="Trigonometry">trigonometric</a> tables and as <a href="/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus" title="Nicolaus Copernicus">Nicolaus Copernicus</a>'s sole pupil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d3_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-d3-1">[1]</a></sup> He facilitated the publication of his master's <i><a href="/wiki/De_revolutionibus_orbium_coelestium" title="De revolutionibus orbium coelestium">De revolutionibus orbium coelestium</a></i> (<i>On the Revolutions of the Heavenly Spheres</i>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surname">Surname</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Joachim_Rheticus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Surname">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rheticus was born at <a href="/wiki/Feldkirch,_Vorarlberg" title="Feldkirch, Vorarlberg">Feldkirch</a>, in present-day <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>. Both his parents, Georg Iserin and Thomasina de Porris, possessed considerable wealth, his father being the town physician. However, Georg (Iserin) abused the trust of many of his patients, stealing belongings and money from their homes. In 1528 he was convicted and executed for his crimes, and as a result his family was stripped of their <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The family adopted the mother's maiden name: de Porris. Later as a student in Wittenberg Georg Joachim adopted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toponym" title="Toponym">toponym</a> Rheticus, a form of the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> name for his home region, <a href="/wiki/Raetia" title="Raetia">Rhaetia</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman province</a> that had included parts of Austria, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. In the matriculation list for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Leipzig" title="University of Leipzig">University of Leipzig</a> his family name, de Porris, is translated into German as von Lauchen. The lunar crater <a href="/wiki/Rhaeticus_(crater)" title="Rhaeticus (crater)">Rhaeticus</a> is named for him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patrons">Patrons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Joachim_Rheticus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Patrons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After Georg Iserin's death, <a href="/wiki/Achilles_Gasser" title="Achilles Gasser">Achilles Gasser</a> took over his medical practice, helping Rheticus to continue his studies and supporting him. Rheticus studied at Feldkirch, <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_University_of_Halle-Wittenberg" title="Martin Luther University of Halle-Wittenberg">University of Wittenberg</a>, where he received his M.A. in 1536.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a> the theologian and educator <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philipp_Melanchthon" title="Philipp Melanchthon">Philipp Melanchthon</a> reorganized the whole educational system of the Lutheran Protestant parts of Germany, reforming and founding several new universities. In 1536 Melanchthon appointed Rheticus as professor of the lower mathematics, arithmetic and geometry, at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Halle-Wittenberg" title="University of Halle-Wittenberg">Wittenberg University</a>.</p>
<p>Two years later, Melanchthon arranged a two-year leave for Rheticus to study with noted astronomers. Leaving Wittenberg in October 1538, he first went to <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> to visit the professor of mathematics at the Eigidien Oberschule <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Sch%C3%B6ner" title="Johannes Schöner">Johannes Schöner</a>. In Nuremberg he also made the acquaintance of other mathematicians such as <a href="/wiki/Georg_Hartmann" title="Georg Hartmann">Georg Hartmann</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Venatorius&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thomas Venatorius (page does not exist)">Thomas Venatorius</a> as well as the printer-publisher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petreius" title="Petreius">Petreius</a>. During his journey, probably in Nuremberg, Rheticus heard of Copernicus and decided to seek him out. From <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petreius" title="Petreius">Petreius</a> Rheticus was given works by <a href="/wiki/Regiomontanus" title="Regiomontanus">Regiomontanus</a> and others, intended as presents for Copernicus. He went on to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_Apian" title="Peter Apian">Peter Apian</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ingolstadt" title="Ingolstadt">Ingolstadt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joachim_Camerarius" title="Joachim Camerarius">Joachim Camerarius</a> in <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BCbingen" title="Tübingen">Tübingen</a>, then to Gasser in his hometown. From Feldkirch he set out on his journey to visit Copernicus in <a href="/wiki/Frombork" title="Frombork">Frombork</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Copernicus">Copernicus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Joachim_Rheticus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Copernicus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In May 1539, Rheticus arrived in <a href="/wiki/Frombork" title="Frombork">Frombork</a> (Frauenburg), where he spent two years with Copernicus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d3_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-d3-1">[1]</a></sup> It is unknown whether he had prior access to Copernicus' <a href="/wiki/Commentariolus" title="Commentariolus">Commentariolus</a>, an unsigned, unpublished outline of Copernicus' revolutionary <a href="/wiki/Copernican_heliocentrism" title="Copernican heliocentrism">heliocentric theory</a> that Copernicus distributed to friends and colleagues three decades before he published <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/De_revolutionibus" title="De revolutionibus">De revolutionibus</a></i>.</p>
<p>In September 1539, Rheticus went to <a href="/wiki/History_of_Gda%C5%84sk" title="History of Gdańsk">Danzig</a> (Gdańsk) to visit the mayor, who gave him financial assistance to publish his <i><a href="/wiki/Narratio_Prima" title="Narratio Prima">Narratio Prima</a></i> (<i>First Report</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> of Copernicus' forthcoming treatise. <a href="/wiki/Franz_Rhode" title="Franz Rhode">Rhode</a> in Danzig published Narratio Prima in 1540. While in Danzig, Rheticus interviewed <a href="/wiki/Maritime_pilot" title="Maritime pilot">maritime pilots</a> to learn about their problems in <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigation</a>. Rheticus also visited Copernicus' friend, <a href="/wiki/Tiedemann_Giese" title="Tiedemann Giese">Tiedemann Giese</a>, who was <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Culm_(Che%C5%82mno)" title="Bishopric of Culm (Chełmno)">Bishop of Culm</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Che%C5%82mno" title="Chełmno">Chełmno</a>).</p>
<p>In August 1541, Rheticus presented a copy of his <i>Tabula chorographica auff Preussen und etliche umbliegende lender</i> (<i>Map of Prussia and Neighboring Lands</i>) to <a href="/wiki/Albert,_Duke_of_Prussia" title="Albert, Duke of Prussia">Albert, Duke of Prussia</a>, who had been trying to compute the exact time of sunrise. Rheticus made an instrument for him that determined the length of the day. Rheticus obtained the duke's permission to publish <i>De revolutionibus</i>. Albrecht asked Rheticus to end his travels and return to his teaching position. Rheticus returned to the University of Wittenberg in October 1541. In May 1542, he traveled to Nürnberg to supervise the printing by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Petreius" title="Johannes Petreius">Johannes Petreius</a> of the first edition of <i>De revolutionibus</i>, but had to leave in fall to take a position in <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Osiander" title="Andreas Osiander">Andreas Osiander</a> replaced him. Copernicus' major work was published shortly before his death in 1543.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a work tentatively titled <i>Epistolae de Terrae Motu</i>, published posthumously and anonymously in 1651,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Rheticus attempts to reconcile Copernicanism with scripture by employing <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">St. Augustine</a>'s principle of <a href="/wiki/Accommodation_(religion)" title="Accommodation (religion)">accommodation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-west_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-west-7">[7]</a></sup> According to historian Robert Westman, the <i>Epistolae</i>, also known as the <i>Opusculum</i>, demonstrates that Copernicus and Rheticus recognized the problem of conflict between their finding of earthly motion and biblical scripture, and had therefore developed a systematic defense of compatibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-west_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-west-7">[7]</a></sup> Rheticus argues that biblical language was written in terms meant to be readily comprehensible to a wide audience:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It borrows a kind of discourse, a habit of speech, and a method of teaching from popular usage.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <small>Rheticus describing scripture's apparent conflict with planetary motion in his <i>Epistolae de Terrae Motu</i> (posthumous).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-west_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-west-7">[7]</a></sup></small></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>While relying heavily upon citations to appease religious authorities, Rheticus may have nevertheless refrained from publishing the work in his life in order to avoid angering more conservative Christians such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philipp_Melanchthon" title="Philipp Melanchthon">Melanchthon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-west_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-west-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criminal_history">Criminal history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Joachim_Rheticus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Criminal history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1552, Rheticus was found guilty of raping the son of Hans Meusel, a merchant. According to Meusel, Rheticus "plied him with a strong drink, until he was inebriated; and finally did with violence overcome him and practice upon him the shameful and cruel vice of sodomy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Rheticus was consequently exiled from Leipzig for 101 years.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Joachim_Rheticus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The canon of <a href="/wiki/Warmia" title="Warmia">Warmia</a> <a href="/wiki/Georg_Rafael_Donner" title="Georg Rafael Donner">Georg Donner</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Warmia" title="Bishop of Warmia">bishop of Warmia</a> <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Dantiscus" title="Johannes Dantiscus">Johannes Dantiscus</a> were both patrons of Rheticus. Rheticus was also commissioned to make a staff for king Sigismund II of Poland, while he held a position as teacher in <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a> for many years. From there he went to <a href="/wiki/Ko%C5%A1ice" title="Košice">Košice</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Kingdom of Hungary</a>, where he died.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trigonometry">Trigonometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Joachim_Rheticus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Trigonometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For much of his life, Rheticus displayed a passion for the study of triangles, the branch of mathematics now called <a href="/wiki/Trigonometry" title="Trigonometry">trigonometry</a>. In 1542 he had the trigonometric sections of Copernicus' <i>De revolutionibis</i> published separately under the title <i>De lateribus et angulis triangulorum</i> (<i>On the Sides and Angles of Triangles</i>). In 1551 Rheticus produced a tract titled <i>Canon of the Science of Triangles,</i> the first publication of six-function trigonometric tables (although the word <i>trigonometry</i> was not yet coined). This pamphlet was to be an introduction to Rheticus' greatest work, a full set of tables to be used in angular astronomical measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>At his death, the <i>Science of Triangles</i> was still unfinished. However, paralleling his own relationship with Copernicus, Rheticus had acquired a student who devoted himself to completing his teacher's work. <a href="/wiki/Valentinus_Otho" title="Valentinus Otho">Valentinus Otho</a> oversaw the hand computation of approximately 100,000 ratios to at least ten decimal places. When completed in 1596, the volume, <i>Opus palatinum de triangulis,</i> filled nearly 1,500 pages. Its tables were accurate enough to be used in astronomical computation into the early twentieth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Joachim_Rheticus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rheticus narrates the third part of <a href="/wiki/John_Banville" title="John Banville">John Banville</a>'s 1975 novel <i>Doctor Copernicus</i>, relating how he convinced Copernicus to publish the book. The novel itself is less about Copernicus's work than about his life and the 16th century world in which he lived.</p>
<p>The episode <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudia_(Warehouse_13)" title="Claudia (Warehouse 13)">"Claudia"</a> of the U.S. science fiction series <i><a href="/wiki/Warehouse_13" title="Warehouse 13">Warehouse 13</a></i> referencing a teleportation device in the form of a compass said to have been built by Rheticus.</p>
<p>Rheticus is referenced several times in the song "Like Rheticus" on the 2004 album <i>Place</i> by British songwriter <a href="/wiki/Owen_Tromans" title="Owen Tromans">Owen Tromans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dava_Sobel" title="Dava Sobel">Dava Sobel</a>'s 2011 book, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A_More_Perfect_Heaven:_How_Copernicus_Revolutionized_the_Cosmos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A More Perfect Heaven: How Copernicus Revolutionized the Cosmos (page does not exist)">A More Perfect Heaven: How Copernicus Revolutionized the Cosmos</a></i>, features a fictional play about Rheticus' visit to Copernicus, sandwiched between chapters about the visit's pre-history and post-history.</p>
