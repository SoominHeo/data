<p><b>Lev Semyonovich Vygotsky</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Лев Семёнович Вы́готский or Выго́тский</span>, born Лев Симхович Выгодский [Lev Simkhovich Vygodskiy], November 17 [<a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">O.S.</a> November 5] 1896 – June 11, 1934) was a Soviet psychologist, the founder of a theory of human cultural and bio-social development commonly referred to as <a href="/wiki/Cultural-historical_psychology" title="Cultural-historical psychology">cultural-historical psychology</a>, and leader of the <a href="/wiki/Vygotsky_Circle" title="Vygotsky Circle">Vygotsky Circle</a> (also referred to as 'Vygotsky-Luria Circle').</p>
<p>Vygotsky's main work was in developmental psychology, and he proposed a theory of the development of higher cognitive functions in children that saw reasoning as emerging through practical activity in a social environment. During the earlier period of his career he argued that the development of reasoning was mediated by signs and symbols, and therefore contingent on cultural practices and language as well as on universal cognitive processes. He identified play, especially for young children, as the leading source of development in terms of emotional, social, physical, language or cognitive development.</p>
<p>During his lifetime, Vygotsky's ideas were controversial within the Soviet Union. As early as in mid-1920s Vygotsky was introduced in the West where he remained virtually unknown until the early 1980s when the popularity of the developmental psychology of <a href="/wiki/Jean_Piaget" title="Jean Piaget">Jean Piaget</a> (1896-1980) among the educators started to decline and, in contrast, Vygotsky's notion of the "<a href="/wiki/Zone_of_proximal_development" title="Zone of proximal development">zone of proximal development</a>" became a central component of the development of new paradigms in developmental and educational psychology. A <i><a href="/wiki/Review_of_General_Psychology" title="Review of General Psychology">Review of General Psychology</a></i> study, published in 2002, ranked Vygotsky as the 83rd top psychologist of the 20th century and the third (and the last) Russian on the top-100 list after <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Pavlov" title="Ivan Pavlov">Ivan Pavlov</a> and Vygotsky's longtime collaborator <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Luria" title="Alexander Luria">Alexander Luria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The early 21st century has seen scholarly reevaluations of the popular version of Vygotsky's legacy (sometimes termed "Vygotsky cult", "the cult of Vygotsky" or even "the cult of personality around Vygotsky"), which is referred to as the "revisionist revolution in Vygotsky Studies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-revBook_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-revBook-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vygRevisitado_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-vygRevisitado-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lev_Vygotsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lev Vygotsky was born in the town of <a href="/wiki/Orsha" title="Orsha">Orsha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a> (then part of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a>) into a non-religious middle class <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Jews" title="Russian Jews">Russian Jewish</a> family. His father was a banker. He was raised in the city of <a href="/wiki/Gomel" title="Gomel">Gomel</a>, Belarus, where he obtained both public and private education. In 1913 Vygotsky was admitted to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moscow_University" title="Moscow University">Moscow University</a> through a "<a href="/wiki/Jewish_quota#Countries_legislating_limitations_on_the_admission_of_Jewish_students" title="Jewish quota">Jewish Lottery</a>" to meet a three percent Jewish student quota for entry in Moscow and Saint Petersburg universities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wertsch_1985_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wertsch_1985-4">[4]</a></sup> He had interest in humanities and social sciences, but at the insistence of his parents he applied to the Medical School in Moscow University. During the first semester of study he transfers to the Law school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kozulin1986_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kozulin1986-5">[5]</a></sup> There he studied law and, in parallel, he attended lectures at fully official, but privately funded and non degree granting Shaniavskii <a class="extiw" href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%9C%D0%BE%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%BE%D0%B2%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%B9_%D0%B3%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%BE%D0%B4%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%BE%D0%B9_%D0%BD%D0%B0%D1%80%D0%BE%D0%B4%D0%BD%D1%8B%D0%B9_%D1%83%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%B2%D0%B5%D1%80%D1%81%D0%B8%D1%82%D0%B5%D1%82_%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B8_%D0%90._%D0%9B._%D0%A8%D0%B0%D0%BD%D1%8F%D0%B2%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%BE%D0%B3%D0%BE" title="ru:Московский городской народный университет имени А. Л. Шанявского">Moscow City People's University</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> His early interests were in the arts and he might have aspired to be a literary critic, fascinated with the <a href="/wiki/Russian_formalism" title="Russian formalism">formalism</a> of his time.</p>
<p>Upon graduation in 1917, Vygotsky returned to Gomel, where he lived after the <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">October Socialist Revolution</a> of 1917 happened. There is virtually no information about his life during the years of the German occupation and the Civil War until the Bolsheviks captured the town in 1919. Subsequently Vygotsky was an active participant of major social transformation under the Bolshevik rule and a fairly prominent representative of the Bolshevik government in Gomel from 1919 to 1923. For unclear reasons, around the early 1920s, he changed his birth name from Vygodskii (with "d") into Vygotskii (with middle "t") and his patronymic from original Jewish "Simkhovich" to Slavic "Semenovich".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 1924, Vygotsky took part in the Second All-Russian Psychoneurological Congress in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leningrad" title="Leningrad">Leningrad</a>. Soon thereafter, Vygotsky received an invitation to become a research fellow at the Psychological Institute in Moscow. Vygotsky moved to Moscow with new wife Roza Smekhova. He began his career at the Psychological Institute as a "staff scientist, second class".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wertsch_1985g_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wertsch_1985g-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> By the end of 1925, Vygotsky completed his dissertation in 1925 on "The Psychology of Art" (not published until the 1960s) and a book "Pedagogical Psychology" that was apparently created on the basis of lecture notes that he prepared back in Gomel as a psychology instructor at local educational establishments. In summer 1925 he made his first and only trip abroad to a London congress on the education of the deaf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Upon return to the Soviet Union, he was hospitalized due to relapse of <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> and, having miraculously survived, remained an invalid and out of job until the end of 1926.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> His dissertation was accepted as the prerequisite of scholarly degree, which was awarded to Vygotsky in fall 1925 <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graduation_in_absentia" title="Graduation in absentia">in absentia</a></i>.</p>
<p>After his release from hospital Vygotsky did theoretical and methodological work on the crisis in psychology, but never finished the draft of the manuscript and interrupted his work on it around mid-1927. The manuscript was later published with notable editorial interventions and distortions in 1982 and presented by the editors as one of the most important Vygotsky's works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zavershneva.2C_E_2009_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zavershneva.2C_E_2009-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In this early manuscript, Vygotsky argued for the formation of a general psychology that could unite the naturalist objectivist strands of psychological science with the more philosophical approaches of Marxist orientation. However, he also harshly criticized those of his colleagues who attempted to build a "<a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxist</a> Psychology" as an alternative to the naturalist and philosophical schools. He argued that if one wanted to build a truly Marxist Psychology, there were no shortcuts to be found by merely looking for applicable quotes in <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Marx</a>' writings. Rather one should look for a methodology that was in accordance with the Marxian spirit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kozulin1986_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kozulin1986-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1926-30 Vygotsky worked on a research programme investigating the development of higher cognitive functions of logical memory, selective attention, decision making and language comprehension, from early forms of primal psychological functions. During this period he gathered a group of collaborators including <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Luria" title="Alexander Luria">Alexander Luria</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Leonid_Sakharov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Leonid Sakharov (page does not exist)">Leonid Sakharov</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Boris_Varshava&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Boris Varshava (page does not exist)">Boris Varshava</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexei_Leontiev" title="Alexei Leontiev">Alexei Leontiev</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Zankov" title="Leonid Zankov">Leonid Zankov</a> and several others. Vygotsky guided his students in researching this phenomenon from three different angles:</p>
<ul>
<li>The instrumental angle, which tried to understand the ways humans use objects as mediation aids in memory and reasoning</li>
<li>A developmental approach, focused on how children acquire higher cognitive functions during development</li>
<li>A culture-historical approach, studying how social and cultural patterns of interaction shape forms of mediation and developmental trajectories<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kozulin1986_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kozulin1986-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>In early 1930s Vygotsky experienced deep crisis, personal and theoretical, and after a period of massive self-criticism made an attempt at a radical revision of his theory. The work of the representatives of the <a href="/wiki/Gestalt_psychology" title="Gestalt psychology">Gestalt psychology</a> and other holistic scholars was instrumental in this theoretical shift. In 1932-1934 Vygotsky was aiming at establishing a psychological theory of consciousness, but this theory because of his death remained only in a very sketchy and unfinished form.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_scientific_legacy">Life and scientific legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lev_Vygotsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life and scientific legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vygotsky was a pioneering psychologist and his major works span six separate volumes, written over roughly 10 years, from <i>Psychology of Art</i> (1925) to <i>Thought and Language</i> [or <i>Thinking and Speech</i>] (1934). Vygotsky's interests in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Developmental_psychology" title="Developmental psychology">developmental psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Child_development" title="Child development">child development</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a> were extremely diverse. His philosophical framework includes interpretations of the cognitive role of mediation tools, as well as the re-interpretation of well-known concepts in psychology such as <a href="/wiki/Internalization" title="Internalization">internalization</a> of knowledge. Vygotsky introduced the notion of <a href="/wiki/Zone_of_proximal_development" title="Zone of proximal development">zone of proximal development</a>, a metaphor capable of describing the potential of human cognitive development. His work covered topics such as the origin and the <a href="/wiki/Psychology_of_art" title="Psychology of art">psychology of art</a>, development of higher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mental_function" title="Mental function">mental functions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_science" title="Philosophy of science">philosophy of science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methodology" title="Methodology">methodology of psychological research</a>, the relation between <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">learning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Developmental_psychology" title="Developmental psychology">human development</a>, concept formation, interrelation between <a href="/wiki/Language_and_thought" title="Language and thought">language and thought</a> development, play as a psychological phenomenon, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Learning_disabilities" title="Learning disabilities">learning disabilities</a>, and abnormal human development (aka <i>defectology</i>). His scientific thinking underwent several major transformations throughout his career, but generally Vygotsky's legacy can be divided into two fairly distinct periods<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and the transitional phase between the two during which Vygotsky experienced the crisis in his theory and personal life. These are the <a href="/wiki/Mechanism_(philosophy)" title="Mechanism (philosophy)">mechanistic</a> "instrumental" period of the 1920s, integrative "<a href="/wiki/Holism" title="Holism">holistic</a>" period of the 1930s, and the transitional years of, roughly, 1929-1931. Each of these periods is characterized by its distinct themes and theoretical innovations.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_mediation_and_internalization">Cultural mediation and internalization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lev_Vygotsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cultural mediation and internalization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Vygotsky studied child development and the significant roles of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_mediation" title="Cultural mediation">cultural mediation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interpersonal_communication" title="Interpersonal communication">interpersonal communication</a>. He observed how higher mental functions developed through these interactions, and also represented the shared knowledge of a culture. This process is known as <a href="/wiki/Internalization" title="Internalization">internalization</a>. <i>Internalization</i> can be understood in one respect as "knowing how". For example, the practices of riding a bicycle or pouring a cup of milk are initially outside and beyond the child. The mastery of the skills needed for performing these practices occurs through the activity of the child within society. A further aspect of internalization is <i>appropriation</i>, in which the child takes a tool and makes it his own, perhaps using it in a way unique to himself. Internalizing the use of a pencil allows the child to use it very much for his own ends rather than drawing exactly what others in society have drawn previously.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_period_of_crisis.2C_criticism.2C_and_self-criticism_.281929.E2.80.931932.29">The period of crisis, criticism, and self-criticism (1929–1932)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lev_Vygotsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The period of crisis, criticism, and self-criticism (1929–1932)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1930s Vygotsky was engaged in massive reconstruction of his theory of his "instrumental" period of the 1920s. Around 1929-1930 he realized numerous deficiencies and imperfections of the earlier work of the Vygotsky Circle and criticized it on a number of occasions: in 1929,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> 1930,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> in 1931,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and in 1932.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Specifically, Vygotsky criticized his earlier idea of radical separation between the "lower" and "higher" psychological functions and, around 1932, appears to abandon it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>This Vygotsky's self-criticism was complemented by external criticism for a number of issues, including the separation between the "higher" and the "lower" psychological functions, impracticality and inapplicability of his theory in social practices (such as industry or education) during the time of rapid social change, and vulgar Marxist interpretation of human psychological processes. Critics also pointed to his overemphasis on the role of language and, on the other hand, the ignorance of the emotional factors in human development. Major figures in Soviet psychology such as <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Rubinstein" title="Sergei Rubinstein">Sergei Rubinstein</a> criticized Vygotsky's notion of mediation and its development in the works of students. Following criticism and in response to generous offer from the highest officials in Soviet Ukraine, a major group of Vygotsky's associates, the members of the <a href="/wiki/Vygotsky_Circle" title="Vygotsky Circle">Vygotsky Circle</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Luria" title="Alexander Luria">Luria</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Lebedinsky&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mark Lebedinsky (page does not exist)">Mark Lebedinsky</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aleksei_N._Leontiev" title="Aleksei N. Leontiev">Leontiev</a>, moved from Moscow to Ukraine to establish the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kharkov_School_of_Psychology" title="Kharkov School of Psychology">Kharkov school of psychology</a>. In the second half of the 1930s, Vygotsky would be yet again criticized for his involvement in the cross-disciplinary study of the child known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paedology" title="Paedology">paedology</a> and uncritical borrowings from contemporary "bourgeois" science. Considerable critique came from the alleged Vygotsky's followers, such as Leontiev and members of his research group in Kharkov. Much of this early criticism was later discarded by these Vygotskian scholars themselves.</p>

<p>The period of major revision of Vygotsky's theory and its transition from mechanist orientation of his 1920s to integrative holistic science of the 1930s. During this period Vygotsky was under particularly strong influence of holistic theories of German-American group of proponents of <a href="/wiki/Gestalt_psychology" title="Gestalt psychology">Gestalt psychology</a>, most notably, the peripheral participants of the Gestalt movement <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Goldstein" title="Kurt Goldstein">Kurt Goldstein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Lewin" title="Kurt Lewin">Kurt Lewin</a>. However, Vygotsky's work of this period remained largely fragmentary and unfinished and, therefore, unpublished.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Zone_of_proximal_development">Zone of proximal development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lev_Vygotsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Zone of proximal development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Zone_of_proximal_development" title="Zone of proximal development">Zone of proximal development</a>" (ZPD) is Vygotsky’s term for the range of tasks that a child is in the process of learning to complete. In the original Vygotsky's writings this phrase is used in three different meanings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Vygotsky viewed the ZPD as a way to better explain the relation between children’s learning and cognitive development. Prior to the ZPD, the relation between learning and development could be boiled down to the following three major positions: 1) Development always precedes learning (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constructivism_(learning_theory)" title="Constructivism (learning theory)">constructivism</a>): children first need to meet a particular maturation level before learning can occur; 2) Learning and development cannot be separated but instead occur simultaneously (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Behaviorism" title="Behaviorism">behaviorism</a>): essentially, learning is development; and 3) learning and development are separate but interactive processes (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gestaltism" title="Gestaltism">gestaltism</a>): one process always prepares the other process, and vice versa. Vygotsky rejected these three major theories because he believed that learning should always precede development in the ZPD. According to Vygotsky, through the assistance of a more capable person, a child is able to learn skills or aspects of a skill that go beyond the child’s actual developmental or maturational level.The lower limit of ZPD is the level of skill reached by the child working independently (also referred to as the child’s actual developmental level). The upper limit is the level of potential skill that the child is able to reach with the assistance of a more capable instructor. In this sense, the ZPD provides a prospective view of cognitive development, as opposed to a retrospective view that characterizes development in terms of a child’s independent capabilities.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Thinking_and_speech"><i>Thinking and speech</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lev_Vygotsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Thinking and speech">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Perhaps Vygotsky's most important contribution concerns the inter-relationship of language development and thought. This problem was explored in Vygotsky's book <i>Thinking and speech</i>, titled, in Russian, <i>Myshlenie i rech</i> published in 1934. In fact, this book was a mere collection of essays and scholarly papers that Vygotsky wrote during different periods of his thought development and included writings of his "instrumental" and "holistic" periods. Vygotsky never saw the book published: it was published posthumously, edited by his closest associates (Kolbanovskii, Zankov, and Shif) not sooner than December, 1934, i.e., half a year after his death. First English translation was published in 1962 (with several later re-editions) heavily abbreviated and under an alternative and incorrect translation of the title <i>Thought and Language</i> for the Russian title <i>Mysl' i iazyk</i>. The book establishes the explicit and profound connection between speech (both silent inner speech and oral language), and the development of mental concepts and cognitive awareness. Vygotsky described inner speech as being qualitatively different from verbal external speech. Although Vygotsky believed inner speech developed from external speech via a gradual process of "internalization" (i.e., transition from the external to the internal), with younger children only really able to "think out loud," he claimed that in its mature form inner speech would not resemble spoken language as we know it (in particular, being greatly compressed). Hence, thought itself develops socially.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_.281934.29_and_posthumous_fame">Death (1934) and posthumous fame</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lev_Vygotsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Death (1934) and posthumous fame">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Vygotsky died of <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> on June 11, 1934, at the age of 37, in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. One of the last Vygotsky's private notebook entries gives a proverbial, yet very pessimistic self-assessment of his contribution to psychological theory:</p>
<pre>
"This is the final thing I have done in psychology – and I will like Moses die at the summit, having glimpsed the promised land but without setting foot on it. Farewell, dear creations. The rest is silence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-revBook_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-revBook-2">[2]</a></sup> 
</pre>
<p>Immediately after his death Vygotsky was proclaimed one of the leading psychologists in the Soviet Union although his stellar reputation was somewhat undermined by the decree of the Central Committee of the Communist Party of 1936 that denounced the mass movement, discipline and related social practice of the so-called <a href="/wiki/Pedology_(children_study)" title="Pedology (children study)">pedology</a>. Yet, even despite some criticisms and even censorship of his works—most notably, in the post-Stalin era in the Soviet Union of 1960s-1980s by his Russian alleged and self-proclaimed best students and followers,—Vygotsky always remained among the most quoted scholars in the field and has become a <a href="/wiki/Cult_following" title="Cult following">cult figure</a> for a number of contemporary intellectuals and practitioners in Russia and the international psychological and educational community alike.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_worldwide">Influence worldwide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lev_Vygotsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Influence worldwide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Europe">Eastern Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lev_Vygotsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Eastern Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, the work of the group of Vygotsky's students known as the <a href="/wiki/Vygotsky_Circle" title="Vygotsky Circle">Vygotsky Circle</a> was vital for preserving and, in many respects, distorting the scientific legacy of Lev Vygotsky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kozulin_1986_264.E2.80.93274_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kozulin_1986_264.E2.80.93274-25">[25]</a></sup> The members of the group subsequently laid a foundation for Vygotskian psychology's systematic development in such diverse fields as the psychology of memory (<a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Zinchenko" title="Pyotr Zinchenko">P. Zinchenko</a>), perception, sensation and movement (<a href="/wiki/Alexander_Zaporozhets" title="Alexander Zaporozhets">Zaporozhets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Asnin" title="Vladimir Asnin">Asnin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexei_Nikolaevich_Leont%27ev" title="Alexei Nikolaevich Leont'ev">A. N. Leont'ev</a>), personality (Lidiya Bozhovich, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Asnin" title="Vladimir Asnin">Asnin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexei_Nikolaevich_Leont%27ev" title="Alexei Nikolaevich Leont'ev">A. N. Leont'ev</a>), will and volition (<a href="/wiki/Alexander_Zaporozhets" title="Alexander Zaporozhets">Zaporozhets</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexei_Nikolaevich_Leont%27ev" title="Alexei Nikolaevich Leont'ev">A. N. Leont'ev</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Zinchenko" title="Pyotr Zinchenko">P. Zinchenko</a>, L. Bozhovich, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Asnin" title="Vladimir Asnin">Asnin</a>), psychology of play (G. D. Lukov, Daniil El'konin) and psychology of learning (<a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Zinchenko" title="Pyotr Zinchenko">P. Zinchenko</a>, L. Bozhovich, D. El'konin), as well as the theory of step-by-step formation of mental actions (Pyotr Gal'perin), general psychological <a href="/wiki/Activity_theory" title="Activity theory">activity theory</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexei_Nikolaevich_Leont%27ev" title="Alexei Nikolaevich Leont'ev">A. N. Leont'ev</a>) and psychology of action (<a href="/wiki/Alexander_Zaporozhets" title="Alexander Zaporozhets">Zaporozhets</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kozulin_1986_264.E2.80.93274_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kozulin_1986_264.E2.80.93274-25">[25]</a></sup> Andrey Puzyrey elaborated the ideas of Vygotsky in respect of psychotherapy and even in the broader context of deliberate psychological intervention (psychotechnique), in general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Puzyrey_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Puzyrey-26">[26]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laszlo_Garai" title="Laszlo Garai">Laszlo Garai</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> founded a Vygotskian research group.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lev_Vygotsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In North America, Vygotsky's work was known from the end of the 1920s through a series of publications in English, but it did not have a major impact on research in general. In 1962 a translation of his posthumous 1934 book <i>Thinking and Speech</i> published with the title <i>Thought and Language</i> did not seem to considerably change the situation. It was only after an eclectic compilation of partly rephrased and partly translated works of Vygotsky and his collaborators published in 1978 under Vygotsky's name in <i>Mind in Society</i> that the Vygotsky boom started in the West: originally, in North America, and later, following the North American example, spread to other regions of the world. This version of Vygotskian science is typically associated with the names of its chief proponents <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Cole_(psychologist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Michael Cole (psychologist) (page does not exist)">Michael Cole</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Wertsch" title="James Wertsch">James Wertsch</a>, their associates and followers, and is relatively well-known under the names of "<a href="/wiki/Cultural-historical_activity_theory" title="Cultural-historical activity theory">cultural-historical activity theory</a>" (aka CHAT) or, yet more distant from Vygotsky's legacy, "<a href="/wiki/Activity_theory" title="Activity theory">activity theory</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toomela1_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-toomela1-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toomela2_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-toomela2-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Instructional_scaffolding" title="Instructional scaffolding">Scaffolding</a>, a concept introduced by Wood, <a href="/wiki/Jerome_Bruner" title="Jerome Bruner">Bruner</a> and Ross in 1976, is somewhat related to the idea of ZPD, although Vygotsky never actually used the term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms_of_North_American_.22Vygotskian.22_legacy">Criticisms of North American "Vygotskian" legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lev_Vygotsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title='Edit section: Criticisms of North American "Vygotskian" legacy'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A critique of the North American interpretation of Vygotsky's ideas and, somewhat later, its global spread and dissemination appeared in the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The early 1980s criticism of Russian and Western "Vygotskian" scholars<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> continued throughout the 1990s. Thus, different authors emphasized the biased and fragmented interpretations of Vygotsky by representatives of what was termed "neo-Vygotskian fashions in contemporary psychology"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> or "selective traditions" in Vygotskian scholarship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Characteristically, the most fashionable "Vygotskian" phraseology in wide circulation in Western scholarly and educational discourse—such as the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Zone_of_proximal_development" title="Zone of proximal development">zone of proximal development</a>"—in the critical literature of this period were referred to as "one of the most used and least understood constructs to appear in contemporary educational literature",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> the construct that was "used as little more than a fashionable alternative to Piagetian terminology or the concept of IQ for describing individual differences in attainment or potential".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Other authors also suggest clearly distinguishing between original Vygotsky's notion of "zona blizhaishego razvitiia" (<i>ZBR</i>) and its later Western superficial interpretations known under the umbrella term "zone of proximal development" (<i>ZPD</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The criticism continued and reached a peak in the 2000s. Most often these critiques address numerous distortions of Vygotsky's ideas, mere "declarations of faith",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> "versions of Vygotsky",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> the "concepts and inferences curiously attributed to Lev Vygotsky",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> "multiple readings of Vygotsky",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> some of which—for instance, "activity theory"—are referred to as "dead end” for cultural-historical psychology<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toomela1_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-toomela1-29">[29]</a></sup> and, moreover, for methodological thinking in cultural psychology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toomela2_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-toomela2-30">[30]</a></sup> Some publications question "if anyone actually reads Vygotsky’s words",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> whether it is "too late to understand Vygotsky for the classroom",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> and suggest "turning Vygotsky on his head."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Inconsistencies, contradictions, and at times fundamental flaws in "Vygotskian" literature were revealed in the ocean of critical publications on this subject and are typically associated with—but certainly not limited to—the North American legacy of Michael Cole and James Wertsch and their associates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> These criticisms contributed significantly to the increasing awareness of numerous "challenges of claiming a Vygotskian perspective".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revisionist_movement_in_Vygotsky_Studies">Revisionist movement in Vygotsky Studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lev_Vygotsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Revisionist movement in Vygotsky Studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The revisionist movement in Vygotsky Studies was termed a "revisionist revolution"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-revBook_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-revBook-2">[2]</a></sup> to describe a relatively recent trend that emerged in the 1990s. This trend is typically associated with growing dissatisfaction with the quality and scholarly integrity of available texts of Vygotsky and members of <a href="/wiki/Vygotsky_Circle" title="Vygotsky Circle">Vygotsky Circle</a>, including their English translations made from largely mistaken, distorted, and even in a few instances falsified Soviet editions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> which raises serious concerns about the reliability of Vygotsky's texts available in English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-html_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-html-51">[51]</a></sup> However, unlike critical literature that discusses Western interpretations of Vygotsky's legacy, the target of criticism and the primary object of research in the studies of the revisionist strand are Vygotsky's texts proper: the manuscripts, original lifetime publications, and Vygotsky's posthumous Soviet editions that most often were subsequently uncritically translated into other languages. The revisionist strand is solidly grounded in a series of studies in Vygotsky's archives that uncovered the previously unknown and unpublished Vygotsky's materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zavershneva.2C_E_2009_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zavershneva.2C_E_2009-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thus, some studies of the revisionist strand show that certain phrases, terms, and expressions typically associated with Vygotskian legacy as its core notions and concepts—such as "cultural-historical psychology", "cultural-historical theory", "cultural-historical school", "higher psychical/mental functions", "internalization", "zone of proximal development", etc., -- in fact, either occupy not more than just a few dozen pages within the six-volume collection of Vygotsky’s works<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> or even never occur in Vygotsky's own writings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Another series of studies revealed the questionable quality of Vygotsky's published texts that, in fact, were never finished and intended for publication by their author,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> but were nevertheless posthumously published without giving proper editorial acknowledgement of their unfinished, transitory nature<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> and with numerous editorial interventions and distortions of Vygotsky's text.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Another series of publications reveals that another well-known Vygotsky's text that is often presented as the foundational work was back-translated into Russian from an English translation of a lost original and passed for the original Vygotsky's writing. This episode was referred to as "benign forgery".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Complete_Works_of_L.S._Vygotsky"><i>Complete Works of L.S. Vygotsky</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lev_Vygotsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Complete Works of L.S. Vygotsky">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scholars associated with the revisionist movement in Vygotsky Studies propose returning to Vygotsky's original uncensored works, critically revising the available discourse, and republishing them in both Russian and translation with a rigorous scholarly commentary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-html_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-html-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Therefore, an essential part of this revisionist strand is the ongoing work on "<i>PsyAnima</i> Complete Vygotsky" project<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> that for the first time ever exposes full collections of Vygotsky's texts, uncensored and cleared from numerous mistakes, omissions, insertions, and blatant distortions and falsifications of the author's text made in Soviet editions and uncritically transferred in virtually all foreign translated editions of Vygotsky's works. This project is carried out by an international team of volunteers—researchers, archival workers, and library staff—from Belarus, Brazil, Canada, Israel, Italy, the Netherlands, Russia, and Switzerland, who joined their efforts and put together a collection of L.S. Vygotsky’s texts. This publication work is supported by a stream of critical scholarly studies and publications on textology, history, theory and methodology of Vygotskian research that cumulatively contributes to the first ever edition of <i>The Complete Works of L.S. Vygotsky</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
