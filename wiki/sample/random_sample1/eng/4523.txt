<p><b>Lexicon</b> is an American company that engineers, manufactures, and markets audio equipment as a brand of <a href="/wiki/Harman_International_Industries" title="Harman International Industries">Harman International Industries</a> (HII), with offices in Salt Lake City. The company was founded in 1971 and headquartered in Waltham, Massachusetts. It was acquired by HII in 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref5_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref5-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lexicon traces its history to the 1969 founding of American Data Sciences by MIT professor Dr. <a href="/wiki/Francis_F._Lee" title="Francis F. Lee">Francis F. Lee</a> and engineer Chuck Bagnaschi, developers of digital audio devices for medical heart monitoring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company is widely known for the design and development of the multi-speaker audio system for the <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Phantom_(2003)" title="Rolls-Royce Phantom (2003)">Rolls-Royce Phantom</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref8_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref8-3">[3]</a></sup> — in addition to the <a href="/wiki/Hyundai_Genesis" title="Hyundai Genesis">Hyundai Genesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyundai_Equus" title="Hyundai Equus">Hyundai Equus</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kia_K900" title="Kia K900">Kia K900</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_audio_equipment">Professional audio equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lexicon_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Professional audio equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_delay_systems">Digital delay systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lexicon_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Digital delay systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lexicon is sometimes credited as the inventor of commercial digital delay products. The first product to market was the popular Delta T-101 delay in 1971, followed by the Delta T-102 in 1972.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reverb_and_effects">Reverb and effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lexicon_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reverb and effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lexicon is considered "the godfather of digital reverb",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> as one of the early players on the <a href="/wiki/Reverberation" title="Reverberation">reverb/reverberation</a> market. The company was among the first to produce commercially available digital reverb equipment, beginning in 1978<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> with the Model <i>224</i>. In 1986, Lexicon released the <i>480L</i> (costing more than some cars), a successor of the 224XL.</p>
<p>The PCM series was introduced as a smaller, more economical option particularly in live situations where the 480L was too cumbersome for a rack rider. First in the series was the PCM-60 (1984), followed a few years later by the Lexicon PCM-70, the latter adding multi-effects and a digital screen interface. <a href="/wiki/David_Gilmour" title="David Gilmour">David Gilmour</a> from <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a> used a Lexicon PCM-70 to store the circular delay sounds in songs such as "<a href="/wiki/Shine_On_You_Crazy_Diamond" title="Shine On You Crazy Diamond">Shine On You Crazy Diamond</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Time_(Pink_Floyd_song)" title="Time (Pink Floyd song)">Time</a>" in the 1994 <a href="/wiki/The_Division_Bell_Tour" title="The Division Bell Tour">The Division Bell Tour</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1990s Lexicon continued the PCM series with two new units, the PCM-80 multi-effects unit and PCM-90 digital reverb. In 1997. they released Model <i>300</i> as another iconic multi-effect unit. Lexicon continued the PCM series in the 2000s with new mid-level units including the PCM-96 and PCM-96 Surround, standalone reverb units that easily integrate into <a href="/wiki/Digital_audio_workstation" title="Digital audio workstation">DAWs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also from the 1990s comes the consumer-level LXP series including the LXP-1, LXP-5, LXP-15 and the LXP-15II, and later the affordable MPX1. A new low-priced reverb series, the MX series, was introduced in the 2000s, with the <a href="/wiki/Lexicon_MX200" title="Lexicon MX200">Lexicon MX200</a> as the entrance model.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="HD_recording">HD recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lexicon_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: HD recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lexicon was a pioneer in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_disk_recording" title="Hard disk recording">hard disk recording</a> market, introducing the Opus system in 1988. This system feature 8 channels of disk I/O along with an integrated 12 channel digital mixer. In the following years, Opus was upgraded with EQ and console automation. In the mid 1990s <i>Lexicon Studio</i> and <i>Core2</i> audio interfaces were introduced. They were notable in that they could be expanded with a Lexicon reverb daughterboard that was then accessible to the recording software.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electroacoustic_enhancement">Electroacoustic enhancement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lexicon_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Electroacoustic enhancement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1988, Lexicon developed <a href="/wiki/LARES" title="LARES">LARES</a>, an electronic processing system intended to give performance spaces a tailored acoustic experience. LARES uses microphones to pick up the sound, central processing units to apply time-variant anti-feedback, delay and reverberation algorithms, and banks of loudspeakers to bring the enhanced audio signal back into the performance space. LARES Associates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spin_out" title="Spin out">split away</a> from Lexicon in 1995. Lexicon continues to benefit from its initial LARES research and development with the company offering a scaled-down and simplified microprocessor controller, the MC-12,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> intended for auditory enhancement within home and professional listening spaces. The system is called Lexicon LIVE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_theater_equipment">Home theater equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lexicon_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Home theater equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lexicon's first foray into home theater equipment was with its surround processor, the <i>CP-1</i>. Later, a <i>CP-2</i> was released, followed by the <i>CP-3</i> and the <i>CP-3+</i>. The CP-3/CP-3+ were the first of its home theater products to be <a href="/wiki/THX" title="THX">THX</a> certified.</p>
<p>With the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Dolby_Digital" title="Dolby Digital">Dolby Digital</a>, the CP line had to be discontinued. It was replaced with the DC line, namely the <i>DC-1</i> [1996] and the <i>DC-2</i>. It was at this point that the company introduced its revolutionary surround processing algorithm <i>Logic 7</i>. Logic 7 was notable for generating a convincing soundfield from seven loudspeakers when presented with either a <a href="/wiki/Stereophonic_sound" title="Stereophonic sound">stereo</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/5.1" title="5.1">5.1</a> input.</p>
<p>After a while, Lexicon added the <i>MC-1</i> to its lineup of the DC-1 and DC-2, and the MC-1 became its new flagship.</p>
<p>A few years later, Lexicon introduced the <i>MC-12</i> and the <i>MC-12b</i>. The MC-12b was in all respects identical to the MC-12 except that it had <a href="/wiki/Balanced_audio" title="Balanced audio">balanced outputs</a> in addition to the standard unbalanced ones. Shortly after this, Lexicon filled in the lower end of its product line by providing an MC-8 and an MC-4. They also produced a receiver, the <i>RV-8</i>.</p>
<p>As of this writing (8/2016), its current flagship is the MC-14. Conspicuously absent from this model is the room correction offered by its recent predecessors such as the MC-12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to surround processors, Lexicon also sells the LX and CX multi-channel home theater amplifiers and the RT-20 DVD player. Its discontinued NT line of amplifiers were rebadged <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bryston&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bryston (page does not exist)">Bryston</a> amplifiers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lexicon_BD-30.2FTHX_certification_controversy">Lexicon BD-30/THX certification controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lexicon_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lexicon BD-30/THX certification controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2010, an <a href="/wiki/Audioholics" title="Audioholics">Audioholics</a>.com review revealed that the chassis and internal components of Lexicon's BD-30 Blu-ray player (retail price $3500) was identical to that of the <a href="/wiki/OPPO_Digital#DVD_Players" title="OPPO Digital">Oppo BDP-83</a> (retail price $500); audiovisual testing indicated no changes were made in performance either.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-audioholics_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-audioholics-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Furthermore, it appears that this player was given THX certification (the first Blu-ray player with this distinction), despite failing fundamental THX tests. Currently, evidence of its certification has largely disappeared from the THX website after this was revealed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-audioholics2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-audioholics2-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
