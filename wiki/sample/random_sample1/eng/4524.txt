<p><b>Lexington Avenue</b>, often colloquially abbreviated as "<b>Lex</b>," is an avenue on the East Side of the <a href="/wiki/Borough_(New_York_City)" title="Borough (New York City)">borough</a> of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> that carries southbound one-way traffic from East 131st Street to <a href="/wiki/Gramercy_Park" title="Gramercy Park">Gramercy Park</a> at East 21st Street. Along its 5.5-mile (8.9-kilometer), 110-block route, Lexington Avenue runs through <a href="/wiki/Harlem" title="Harlem">Harlem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Hill" title="Carnegie Hill">Carnegie Hill</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Upper_East_Side" title="Upper East Side">Upper East Side</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midtown_(Manhattan)" title="Midtown (Manhattan)">Midtown</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Murray_Hill,_Manhattan" title="Murray Hill, Manhattan">Murray Hill</a> to a point of origin that is centered on Gramercy Park. South of Gramercy Park, the axis continues as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irving_Place" title="Irving Place">Irving Place</a> from 20th Street to East <a href="/wiki/14th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="14th Street (Manhattan)">14th Street</a>.</p>
<p>Lexington Avenue was not one of the streets included in the <a href="/wiki/Commissioners%27_Plan_of_1811" title="Commissioners' Plan of 1811">Commissioners' Plan of 1811</a> street grid, so the addresses for cross streets do not start at an even hundred number, as they do with avenues that were originally part of the plan.</p>
<p>The portion of Lexington Avenue between 14th and 20th Streets is known as <b>Irving Place</b>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lexington_Avenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:New_york_lexington_ave_50_street.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="280" height="313" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/New_york_lexington_ave_50_street.jpg/175px-New_york_lexington_ave_50_street.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/New_york_lexington_ave_50_street.jpg/263px-New_york_lexington_ave_50_street.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d1/New_york_lexington_ave_50_street.jpg 2x" width="175"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:New_york_lexington_ave_50_street.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
View of Lexington Avenue south from 50th street, with the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Building" title="Chrysler Building">Chrysler Building</a> in the background</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Both Lexington Avenue and Irving Place began in 1832 when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samuel_Ruggles" title="Samuel Ruggles">Samuel Ruggles</a>, a lawyer and real-estate developer, petitioned the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Legislature" title="New York State Legislature">New York State Legislature</a> to approve the creation of a new north/south avenue between the existing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="Third Avenue (Manhattan)">Third</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Park_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="Park Avenue (Manhattan)">Fourth Avenues</a>, between <a href="/wiki/14th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="14th Street (Manhattan)">14th</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/30th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="30th Street (Manhattan)">30th Streets</a>. Ruggles had purchased land in the area, and was developing it as a planned community of townhouses around a private park, which he called <a href="/wiki/Gramercy_Park" title="Gramercy Park">Gramercy Park</a>. He was also developing property around the planned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Square_(New_York_City)" title="Union Square (New York City)">Union Square</a>, and wanted the new road to improve the value of these tracts. The legislation approved, and, as the owner of most of the land along the route of the new street, Ruggles was assessed for the majority of its cost. Ruggles named the southern section, below 20th Street, which opened in 1833, after his friend <a href="/wiki/Washington_Irving" title="Washington Irving">Washington Irving</a>. The northern section, which opened three years later, in 1836, was named after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lexington" title="Battle of Lexington">Battle of Lexington</a> in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">Revolutionary War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STREETBOOK_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-STREETBOOK-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-desrep_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-desrep-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1899, Lexington would see the first arrest in New York for speeding, when a bicycle patrolman overtook <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxicab_driver" title="Taxicab driver">cabdriver</a> Jacob German, who had been racing down the avenue at the "reckless" speed of 12 mph (19 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The portion of Lexington Avenue above East 42nd Street was reconstructed at the same time as the <a href="/wiki/IRT_Lexington_Avenue_Line" title="IRT Lexington Avenue Line">IRT Lexington Avenue Line</a> of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Subway" title="New York City Subway">New York City Subway</a>. The widened street and the subway line both opened on July 17, 1918.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Parallel to Lexington Avenue lies <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Park_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="Park Avenue (Manhattan)">Park Avenue</a> to its west and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="Third Avenue (Manhattan)">Third Avenue</a> to its east. The avenue is largely commercial at ground level, with offices above. There are clusters of hotels in the 30s and 40s, roughly from the avenue's intersection with 30th Street through to its intersection with 49th Street, and apartment buildings farther north.</p>
<p>In 1955, portions of the avenue were widened, which required eminent domain takings of the facades of some structures along Lexington.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lexington Avenue has carried one-way (downtown) traffic since July 17, 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The July 18, <a href="/wiki/2007_New_York_City_steam_explosion" title="2007 New York City steam explosion">2007 New York City steam explosion</a> sent a geyser of hot steam up from beneath the avenue at 41st Street resulting in one death and more than 40 injuries.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Irving_Place">Irving Place</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lexington_Avenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Irving Place">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Petes-tavern-2007.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="562" data-file-width="750" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Petes-tavern-2007.jpg/275px-Petes-tavern-2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Petes-tavern-2007.jpg/413px-Petes-tavern-2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Petes-tavern-2007.jpg/550px-Petes-tavern-2007.jpg 2x" width="275"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Petes-tavern-2007.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Pete%27s_Tavern" title="Pete's Tavern">Pete's Tavern</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In contrast to Lexington Avenue, the five-block stretch of Irving Place, from <a href="/wiki/14th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="14th Street (Manhattan)">14th</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/20th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="20th Street (Manhattan)">20th Street</a> at <a href="/wiki/Gramercy_Park" title="Gramercy Park">Gramercy Park</a> carries two-way traffic and is decidedly local in nature. After the opening of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Square_(New_York_City)" title="Union Square (New York City)">Union Square</a> in 1839, the Irving Place area became one of the most sought-after residential neighborhoods in the city, a situation which was only enhanced by the development of <a href="/wiki/Gramercy_Park" title="Gramercy Park">Gramercy Park</a> to the north and <a href="/wiki/Stuyvesant_Square" title="Stuyvesant Square">Stuyvesant Square</a> to the east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-desrep_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-desrep-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>An assortment of restaurants and bars line Irving Place, including <a href="/wiki/Pete%27s_Tavern" title="Pete's Tavern">Pete's Tavern</a>, New York's oldest surviving saloon, where <a href="/wiki/O._Henry" title="O. Henry">O. Henry</a> supposedly conceived of "<a href="/wiki/The_Gift_of_the_Magi" title="The Gift of the Magi">The Gift of the Magi</a>", and which survived <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_in_the_United_States" title="Prohibition in the United States">Prohibition</a> disguised as a flower shop. <a href="/wiki/Irving_Plaza" title="Irving Plaza">Irving Plaza</a>, on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/15th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="15th Street (Manhattan)">East 15th Street</a> and Irving, hosts numerous concerts for both well-known and indie bands and draws a crowd almost every night. Another component of the avenue are the large apartment buildings which line the street from Gramercy Park to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/17th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="17th Street (Manhattan)">17th Street</a>. Also at 17th, a small bed-and-breakfast, the Inn at Irving Place, occupies two <a href="/wiki/Greek_Revival_architecture" title="Greek Revival architecture">Greek Revival architecture</a> townhouses built in 1840–1841 and renovated in 1991–1995.</p>
<p>Historically and architecturally significant are 47 and 49 Irving Place – the latter where Washington Irving is said to have lived, but did not<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-desrep_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-desrep-4">[4]</a></sup> – which are part of the <a href="/wiki/East_17th_Street/Irving_Place_Historic_District" title="East 17th Street/Irving Place Historic District">East 17th Street/Irving Place Historic District</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/19_Gramercy_Park" title="19 Gramercy Park">19 Gramercy Park</a> on the corner of 20th Street, part of the <a href="/wiki/Gramercy_Park" title="Gramercy Park">Gramercy Park Historic District</a>.</p>
<p>Offices located on Irving Place include those of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nation" title="The Nation">The Nation</a></i> magazine, the New York branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosicrucian_Order" title="Rosicrucian Order">Rosicrucian Order</a> and the Seafarers and International House mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-song_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-song-9">[9]</a></sup> There are also a number of clinics and official city buildings along the street, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Irving_High_School_(New_York_City)" title="Washington Irving High School (New York City)">Washington Irving High School</a> and the headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Human_Resources_Administration" title="New York City Human Resources Administration">New York City Human Resources Administration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-song_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-song-9">[9]</a></sup> The bottom of the street is anchored by the rear of the <a href="/wiki/Zeckendorf_Towers" title="Zeckendorf Towers">Zeckendorf Towers</a> condominium apartment complex on the west side, and the <a href="/wiki/Consolidated_Edison_Building" title="Consolidated Edison Building">Consolidated Edison Building</a> on the east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-song_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-song-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_transportation">Public transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lexington_Avenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Public transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Surface:</b></p>
<p>The following buses use Lexington Avenue between the following streets (northbound buses run along Third Avenue):</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M98_(New_York_City_bus)" title="M98 (New York City bus)">M98</a>: Between East 120th and East 67th Streets</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M101_(New_York_City_bus)" title="M101 (New York City bus)">M101</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M102_(New_York_City_bus)" title="M102 (New York City bus)">M102</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M103_(New_York_City_bus)" title="M103 (New York City bus)">M103</a> (Third and Lexington Avenues Line): Between Harlem and East 24th Streets; turns east at East 24th Street. M101 and M103 run to 125th Street; M102 runs to 116th Street and turns west there.</li>

<p><b>Subway:</b></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/IRT_Lexington_Avenue_Line" title="IRT Lexington Avenue Line">IRT Lexington Avenue Line</a> (<span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/4_(New_York_City_Subway_service)" title="4 (New York City Subway service)">4</a></span> <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/5_(New_York_City_Subway_service)" title="5 (New York City Subway service)">5</a></span> <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/6_(New_York_City_Subway_service)" title="6 (New York City Subway service)">6</a></span> <span class="nowrap"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/6d_(New_York_City_Subway_service)" title="6d (New York City Subway service)">&lt;6&gt;</a></span> trains) of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Subway" title="New York City Subway">New York City Subway</a> runs under Lexington Avenue north of <a href="/wiki/42nd_Street_(Manhattan)" title="42nd Street (Manhattan)">42nd Street</a> (at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Central_%E2%80%93_42nd_Street_(IRT_Lexington_Avenue_Line)" title="Grand Central – 42nd Street (IRT Lexington Avenue Line)">Grand Central</a>) to <a href="/wiki/125th_Street_(IRT_Lexington_Avenue_Line)" title="125th Street (IRT Lexington Avenue Line)">125th Street</a>; south of Grand Central, this subway line runs under <a href="/wiki/Park_Avenue" title="Park Avenue">Park Avenue</a>, Park Avenue South, and Fourth Avenue until <a href="/wiki/Astor_Place_(IRT_Lexington_Avenue_Line)" title="Astor Place (IRT Lexington Avenue Line)">Astor Place</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lexington_Avenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lexington Avenue became part of a classic American cinematic moment, in the 1955 movie <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seven_Year_Itch" title="The Seven Year Itch">The Seven Year Itch</a></i>, the scene in which <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Monroe" title="Marilyn Monroe">Marilyn Monroe</a> shot what would become her most famous scene. Standing on a subway grating outside the Loew's Lexington theatre, her skirt billows up from the wind underneath. While the footage showing the theatre in the background appeared in the finished film, the footage featuring the subway grate shot on September 15, 1954, on the corner of Lexington Avenue and <a href="/wiki/51st_Street_(Manhattan)" title="51st Street (Manhattan)">51st Street</a> was more of a publicity stunt, retakes were shot on a studio soundstage, and shots from both are seen in the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
