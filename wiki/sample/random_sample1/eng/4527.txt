<p><i><b>Rendition</b></i> is a 2007 American <a href="/wiki/Kidnapping" title="Kidnapping">abduction</a> <a href="/wiki/Thriller_film" title="Thriller film">thriller film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Hood" title="Gavin Hood">Gavin Hood</a> and starring <a href="/wiki/Reese_Witherspoon" title="Reese Witherspoon">Reese Witherspoon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meryl_Streep" title="Meryl Streep">Meryl Streep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sarsgaard" title="Peter Sarsgaard">Peter Sarsgaard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Arkin" title="Alan Arkin">Alan Arkin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jake_Gyllenhaal" title="Jake Gyllenhaal">Jake Gyllenhaal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Omar_Metwally" title="Omar Metwally">Omar Metwally</a>. It centers on the controversial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIA" title="CIA">CIA</a> practice of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraordinary_rendition_by_the_United_States" title="Extraordinary rendition by the United States">extraordinary rendition</a> and is based on the true story of <a href="/wiki/Khalid_El-Masri" title="Khalid El-Masri">Khalid El-Masri</a>, who was mistaken for <a href="/wiki/Khalid_al-Masri" title="Khalid al-Masri">Khalid al-Masri</a>. The movie also has similarities to the case of <a href="/wiki/Maher_Arar" title="Maher Arar">Maher Arar</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rendition_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CIA analyst Douglas Freeman (<a href="/wiki/Jake_Gyllenhaal" title="Jake Gyllenhaal">Jake Gyllenhaal</a>) is briefing a newly arrived CIA agent in a square in an unnamed country in North Africa (filmed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marrakech" title="Marrakech">Marrakech</a>), when a <a href="/wiki/Suicide_attack" title="Suicide attack">suicide attack</a> kills the CIA agent and 18 other people. The target was a high-ranking police official, Abbas-i "Abasi" Fawal (<a href="/wiki/Yigal_Naor" title="Yigal Naor">Yigal Naor</a>), who acts as a liaison for the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and whose tasks include conducting interrogations and overseeing the application of techniques amounting to torture. Fawal escapes unscathed.</p>
<p>Egyptian-born Anwar El-Ibrahimi (<a href="/wiki/Omar_Metwally" title="Omar Metwally">Omar Metwally</a>), a chemical engineer who lives in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> with his mother, his pregnant wife Isabella (<a href="/wiki/Reese_Witherspoon" title="Reese Witherspoon">Reese Witherspoon</a>) and their young son, is linked to a violent organization by telephone records indicating that known terrorist Rashid placed several calls to Anwar's cellphone. Returning to the United States from a conference in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, he is detained by American officials and sent to a secret detention facility near the location of the suicide attack depicted earlier, where he is interrogated and tortured. Isabella is not informed and all records of his being on the flight from South Africa are erased, although records remain of his boarding the plane at <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town_International_Airport" title="Cape Town International Airport">Cape Town International Airport</a> and making a purchase en route.</p>
<p>For lack of more experienced staff, Freeman is assigned to observe the interrogation of Anwar, whose interrogator is Fawal himself. After Freeman briefly questions Anwar, he is doubtful of Anwar's guilt, but his boss, Corrine Whitman (<a href="/wiki/Meryl_Streep" title="Meryl Streep">Meryl Streep</a>), insists that the detention continue, justifying such treatments as necessary to save thousands from becoming victims of terrorism.</p>
<p>Growing worried, Isabella travels to Washington, D.C. to see old friend Alan Smith (<a href="/wiki/Peter_Sarsgaard" title="Peter Sarsgaard">Peter Sarsgaard</a>), who now works as an aide to Senator Hawkins (<a href="/wiki/Alan_Arkin" title="Alan Arkin">Alan Arkin</a>), and plead with him to find out what has happened to her husband. She is first informed that there had been a mistake in South Africa and Anwar was not on the flight, but she presents his credit card record, which shows that he purchased something at the in-flight duty-free shop; this confirms that he was on the flight. Smith slowly pieces together details of Anwar's detention. He is unable to convince either the senator or Corrine Whitman, who had ordered the <a href="/wiki/Rendition_(law)" title="Rendition (law)">rendition</a>, to release him, or to openly acknowledge that the rendition had taken place. After the senator advises Smith to let it go (rationale: it's not the right time to start debating an <a href="/wiki/Extraordinary_rendition" title="Extraordinary rendition">extraordinary rendition</a> because he's fighting to have a bill passed in Congress), Smith advises Isabella to get an excellent lawyer he knows on the case, but she refuses. Upon hearing the confrontation from her office, his sympathetic secretary quietly tips Isabella off as to when Whitman will be in her office next. The next day Isabella confronts Whitman, who avoids her questions and pretends to know nothing. Frustrated, Isabella storms out of the office—and goes into labor in the hallway.</p>
<p>Eventually, Anwar confesses that he advised Rashid how to make more powerful bombs and was promised $40,000 in return. Suspicious that it's a <a href="/wiki/False_confession" title="False confession">false confession</a>, Freeman asks where the money is and Anwar replies that it should have been delivered to him in South Africa but the courier never arrived. Freeman's suspicions are confirmed when he has the names Anwar gives traced by <a href="/wiki/Interpol" title="Interpol">Interpol</a> and draws a blank. He then <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Googles</a> the names and learns that they are the names of the Egyptian soccer team from the year Anwar left Egypt. He also expresses doubt as to whether Anwar would be willing to put his life, family and job in danger for $40,000 when his annual salary is $200,000. He quotes <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Merchant_of_Venice" title="The Merchant of Venice">The Merchant of Venice</a></i> in a discussion with the minister of the interior on the value of intelligence gathered through torture:</p>
<dl>
<dd>I fear you speak upon the rack</dd>
<dd>Where men enforced do speak anything.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Without the consent of his superiors, and not caring what happens to him, Freeman gets a warrant for Anwar's release and sends him back to America via a clandestine ship to Spain. When his immediate boss calls and orders him to return Anwar to Fawal, Freeman simply hangs up the phone. Angered by the injustice Anwar has suffered, Freeman leaks the details of Anwar's detention to the American press, creating a scandal that humiliates Whitman and Hawkins. Anwar officially went back home and reunite with his wife Isabella and his two children since he is now a free man.</p>
<p>In a parallel storyline, Abasi's daughter Fatima (<a href="/wiki/Zineb_Oukach" title="Zineb Oukach">Zineb Oukach</a>) has run away from home with her boyfriend Khalid (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Moa_Khouas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Moa Khouas (page does not exist)">Moa Khouas</a>). Fatima sees a picture of Khalid's brother, but he does not tell her what has happened to him. Abasi is told that Khalid's brother was an inmate at his prison and later died. Fatima is unaware that Khalid is a member of a terrorist group until his friends are arrested at a planned march and he leads her to the terrorist group's base. Near the end of the movie, Fatima discovers a notebook that contains pictures of Khalid and his brother together, showing that they were extremely close, as well as a picture of the two brandishing AK-47s, then some pictures of a grief-stricken Khalid standing over his brother's corpse, some pictures of her father, and finally a statement saying that Khalid is doing a deed to avenge his brother's death. Realizing that Khalid's brother met his death at the hands of her father and that Khalid is about to assassinate him, she runs off. It is then revealed that this second story took place before the suicide attack. (From the briefing with the CIA agent in the beginning we know that the first story took place after the suicide attack.) At the town square Fatima begs him not to do it, arguing that the target is her father. After removing the pin of his <a href="/wiki/Detonator" title="Detonator">detonator</a> he hesitates, and is therefore killed by the organizers of the attack. As a result, he releases the handle of the <a href="/wiki/Dead_man%27s_switch" title="Dead man's switch">detonator</a> and the bomb explodes, killing Fatima. In the present, Abasi rushes to Khalid's apartment and discovers Khalid's grandmother grief-stricken over the loss of both her grandsons and Fatima. Abasi then realizes that his daughter died trying to protect him and is filled with grief himself.</p>
<p>The record of a phone call supposedly made by Rashid to Anwar is not explained in the film. However, earlier it was mentioned that phones are sometimes passed on from one person to another in order to avoid phone tracing (the DVD extras explain that there was a subplot dropped from the film that elaborated on this concept). The film's director, Gavin Hood, stated in an interview that the lack of explanation on the call was deliberate, so as to create ambiguity about whether Anwar was guilty or innocent, and to let the viewer decide whether the possibility of his guilt was enough to justify kidnapping and torturing him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rendition_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Reviews for <i>Rendition</i> were mixed. Review aggregator <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> reports a 47% approval rating from critics, based on 152 reviews. On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, the film averaged a score of 55 based on 34 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> awarded the film four stars out of four, saying, "<i>Rendition</i> is valuable and rare. As I wrote from Toronto: 'It is a movie about the theory and practice of two things: torture and personal responsibility. And it is wise about what is right, and what is wrong.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In contrast, Peter Travers of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> applauded the cast, but noted that the film was a "bust as a persuasive drama".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Travers declared the film the year's Worst Anti-War Film on his list of the Worst Movies of 2007.</p>
