<p><i><b>Rent</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Rock_musical" title="Rock musical">rock musical</a> with music, lyrics and book by <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Larson" title="Jonathan Larson">Jonathan Larson</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rent:_Bible_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rent:_Bible-1">[1]</a></sup> loosely based on <a href="/wiki/Giacomo_Puccini" title="Giacomo Puccini">Giacomo Puccini</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Boh%C3%A8me" title="La Bohème">La Bohème</a></i>. It tells the story of a group of impoverished young artists struggling to survive and create a life in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>'s <a href="/wiki/East_Village,_Manhattan" title="East Village, Manhattan">East Village</a> in the thriving days of <a href="/wiki/Bohemianism" title="Bohemianism">Bohemian</a> <a href="/wiki/Alphabet_City,_Manhattan" title="Alphabet City, Manhattan">Alphabet City</a>, under the shadow of <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a>.</p>
<p>The musical was first seen in a <a href="/wiki/Workshop_production" title="Workshop production">workshop production</a> at <a href="/wiki/New_York_Theatre_Workshop" title="New York Theatre Workshop">New York Theatre Workshop</a> in 1993. This same <a href="/wiki/Off-Broadway" title="Off-Broadway">Off-Broadway</a> theatre was also the musical's initial home following its official 1996 opening. The show's creator, Jonathan Larson, died suddenly of an <a href="/wiki/Aortic_dissection" title="Aortic dissection">aortic dissection</a>, believed to have been caused by undiagnosed <a href="/wiki/Marfan_syndrome" title="Marfan syndrome">Marfan syndrome</a>, the night before the Off-Broadway premiere. The show won a <a href="/wiki/1996_Pulitzer_Prize" title="1996 Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>, and the production was a hit. The musical moved to Broadway's larger <a href="/wiki/Nederlander_Theatre" title="Nederlander Theatre">Nederlander Theatre</a> on April 29, 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rent:_Glory_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rent:_Glory-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>On Broadway, <i>Rent</i> gained critical acclaim and won a <a href="/wiki/Tony_Award_for_Best_Musical" title="Tony Award for Best Musical">Tony Award for Best Musical</a> among other awards. The Broadway production closed on September 7, 2008 after a 12-year run of 5,123 performances. On February 14, 2016, the musical <i><a href="/wiki/Wicked_(musical)" title="Wicked (musical)">Wicked</a></i> surpassed <i>Rent'</i>s number of performances with a 2pm matinee, pushing <i>Rent</i> from the <a href="/wiki/List_of_the_longest-running_Broadway_shows" title="List of the longest-running Broadway shows">tenth- to eleventh-longest-running Broadway show</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The production grossed over $280 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Time-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The success of the show led to several national tours and numerous foreign productions. In 2005, it was adapted into a <a href="/wiki/Rent_(film)" title="Rent (film)">motion picture</a> featuring most of the original cast members.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concept_and_genesis">Concept and genesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Concept and genesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1988, playwright <a href="/wiki/Billy_Aronson" title="Billy Aronson">Billy Aronson</a> wanted to create "a musical based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puccini" title="Puccini">Puccini</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Boh%C3%A8me" title="La Bohème">La Bohème</a></i>, in which the luscious splendor of Puccini's world would be replaced with the coarseness and noise of modern New York."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aronson_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aronson-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1989, Jonathan Larson, a 29-year-old composer, began collaborating with Aronson on this project, and the two composed together "Santa Fe", "Splatter" (later re-worked into the song "Rent"), and "I Should Tell You". Larson suggested setting the play "amid poverty, homelessness, spunky gay life, drag queens and punk" in the <a href="/wiki/East_Village,_Manhattan" title="East Village, Manhattan">East Village</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, which happened to be down the street from his <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Village" title="Greenwich Village">Greenwich Village</a> apartment. He also came up with the show's ultimate title (a decision that Aronson was unhappy with, at least until Larson pointed out that "rent" also means torn apart). In 1991, he asked Aronson if he could use Aronson's original concept and make <i>Rent</i> his own. Larson had ambitious expectations for <i>Rent</i>; his ultimate dream was to write a rock opera "to bring musical theater to the <a href="/wiki/MTV_Generation" title="MTV Generation">MTV generation</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odyssey_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Odyssey-7">[7]</a></sup> Aronson and Larson made an agreement that if the show went to Broadway, Aronson would share in the proceeds and be given credit for "original concept &amp; additional lyrics".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odyssey_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Odyssey-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jonathan Larson focused on composing <i>Rent</i> in the early 1990s, waiting tables at the <a href="/wiki/Moondance_Diner" title="Moondance Diner">Moondance Diner</a> to support himself. Over the course of years, Larson wrote hundreds of songs and made many drastic changes to the show, which in its final incarnation contained 42 songs. In the fall of 1992, Larson approached James Nicola, artistic director of New York Theatre Workshop, with a tape and copy of <i>Rent's</i> script. When <i>Rent</i> had its first staged reading at <a href="/wiki/New_York_Theatre_Workshop" title="New York Theatre Workshop">New York Theatre Workshop</a> in March 1993, it became evident that, despite its very promising material and moving musical numbers, many structural problems needed to be addressed, including its cumbersome length and overly complex plot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odyssey_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Odyssey-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 1994, the New York Theatre Workshop version of <i>Rent</i> featured songs that never made it to the final version, such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>"You're A Fool"</li>
<li>"Do A Little Business", the predecessor of "You'll See," featuring Benny, Mark, Roger, Collins, and Angel</li>
<li>"Female to Female A &amp; B," featuring Maureen and Joanne</li>
<li>"He's A Fool"</li>
<li>"He Says"</li>
<li>"Right Brain", later rewritten as "One Song Glory", featuring Roger</li>
<li>"You'll Get Over It", the predecessor of "Tango: Maureen," featuring Mark and Maureen</li>
<li>"Real Estate", a number wherein Benny tries to convince Mark to become a real estate agent and drop his filmmaking</li>
<li>"Open Road", the predecessor of "What You Own", with a backing track similar to this in "Your Eyes"</li>
</ul>
<p>This workshop version of <i>Rent</i> starred <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Rapp" title="Anthony Rapp">Anthony Rapp</a> as Mark and <a href="/wiki/Daphne_Rubin-Vega" title="Daphne Rubin-Vega">Daphne Rubin-Vega</a> as Mimi. Larson continued to work on <i>Rent</i>, gradually reworking its flaws and staging more workshop productions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memoir_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Memoir-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 24, 1996, after the musical's final dress rehearsal before its off-Broadway opening, Larson had his first (and only) newspaper interview with music critic <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Tommasini" title="Anthony Tommasini">Anthony Tommasini</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, attracted by the coincidence that the show was debuting exactly 100 years after Puccini's opera. Larson would not live to see <i>Rent's</i> success; he died from an undiagnosed <a href="/wiki/Aortic_aneurysm" title="Aortic aneurysm">aortic aneurysm</a> (believed to have resulted from <a href="/wiki/Marfan_syndrome" title="Marfan syndrome">Marfan syndrome</a>) in the early morning of January 25, 1996. The first preview of <i>Rent</i> was canceled and instead, friends and family gathered at the theater where the actors performed a sing-through of <i>Rent</i> in Larson's memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odyssey_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Odyssey-7">[7]</a></sup> The show premiered as planned and quickly gained popularity fueled by enthusiastic reviews and the recent death of its composer. It proved extremely successful during its off-Broadway run, selling out all its shows at the 150-seat New York Theater Workshop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rent:_Glory_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rent:_Glory-2">[2]</a></sup> Due to such overwhelming popularity and a need for a larger theater, <i>Rent</i> moved to Broadway's recently remodeled Nederlander Theatre on 41st Street on April 29, 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rent:_Glory_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rent:_Glory-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_and_inspiration">Sources and inspiration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sources and inspiration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Larson's inspiration for <i>Rent</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s content came from several different sources. Many of the characters and plot elements are drawn directly from Giacomo Puccini's opera <i>La bohème</i>, the world premiere of which was in 1896, a century before <i>Rent</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s premiere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rent:_Leap_of_Faith_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rent:_Leap_of_Faith-9">[9]</a></sup> <i>La bohème</i> was also about the lives of poor young artists. <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">Tuberculosis</a>, the plague of Puccini's opera, is replaced by HIV/AIDS in <i>Rent</i>; 1800s Paris is replaced by New York's East Village in the late 1980s or early 1990s. The names and identities of <i>Rent</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s characters also heavily reflect Puccini's original characters, though they are not all direct adaptations. For example, Joanne in <i>Rent</i> represents the character of Alcindoro in <i>Bohème</i>, but is also partially based on Marcello. Also, Joanne is the only <i>Rent</i> character whose predecessor in <i>La bohème</i> is the opposite sex.</p>

<p>Other examples of parallels between Larson's and Puccini's work include Larson's song "Light My Candle", which draws melodic content directly from "Che gelida manina";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Quando_me%27n_vo%27" title="Quando me'n vo'">Quando me'n vo</a>'" ("Musetta's Waltz"), a melody taken directly from Puccini's opera; and "Goodbye Love", a long, painful piece that reflects a confrontation and parting between characters in both Puccini's and Larson's work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Libretto_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Libretto-11">[11]</a></sup> "Quando me'n vo'" is paralleled in the first verse of "Take Me or Leave Me," when Maureen describes the way people stare when she walks in the street. It is also directly referred to in the scene where the characters are celebrating their bohemian life. Mark says, "Roger will attempt to write a bittersweet, evocative song..." Roger plays a quick piece, and Mark adds, "...that <i>doesn't</i> remind us of 'Musetta's Waltz'." This part of "Musetta's Waltz" is also later used in "Your Eyes", a song Roger writes.</p>
<p><i>Rent</i> is also a somewhat autobiographical work, as Larson incorporated many elements of his life into his show. Larson lived in New York for many years as a starving artist with an uncertain future. He sacrificed a life of stability for his art, and shared many of the same hopes and fears as his characters. Like his characters he endured poor living conditions, and some of these conditions (e.g. illegal wood-burning stove, bathtub in the middle of his kitchen, broken buzzer [his guests had to call from the pay phone across the street and he would throw down the keys, as in "Rent"]) made their way into the play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beals_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beals-12">[12]</a></sup> Part of the motivation behind the storyline in which Maureen leaves Mark for a woman (Joanne) is based on the fact that Larson's own girlfriend left him for a woman. The Mark Cohen character is based on Larson's friends, cinematographer and producer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Burkhart&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jonathan Burkhart (page does not exist)">Jonathan Burkhart</a> and documentary filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Rosenstein" title="Eddie Rosenstein">Eddie Rosenstein</a>.</p>
<p>Playwright <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Schulman" title="Sarah Schulman">Sarah Schulman</a> alleged that <i>Rent</i> bore striking similarities to her novel <i>People in Trouble</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulman_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schulman-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The line, "I'm more of a man than you'll ever be... and more of a woman than you'll ever get!", attributed to Angel Dumott Schunard at her funeral, was previously used by the character Hollywood Montrose, who appeared in the films <i><a href="/wiki/Mannequin_(1987_film)" title="Mannequin (1987 film)">Mannequin</a></i> (1987) and <i><a href="/wiki/Mannequin_Two:_On_the_Move" title="Mannequin Two: On the Move">Mannequin Two: On the Move</a></i> (1991). Like Angel, Hollywood performs a song and dance number and sometimes wears women's clothing, although Angel is a trans woman and Hollywood is a homosexual man; however, the line was originally in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Car_Wash_(film)" title="Car Wash (film)">Car Wash</a></i> (1976), delivered by <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Fargas" title="Antonio Fargas">Antonio Fargas</a> as a flamboyant homosexual cross dresser.</p>
<p>The earliest concepts of the characters differ largely from the finished products. Everyone except Mark had AIDS, including Maureen and Joanne; Maureen was a serious, angry character who played off <a href="/wiki/Oedipus" title="Oedipus">Oedipus</a> in her performance piece instead of <a href="/wiki/Hey_Diddle_Diddle" title="Hey Diddle Diddle">Hey Diddle Diddle</a>; Mark was, at one point, a painter instead of a filmmaker; Roger was named Ralph and wrote musical plays; Angel was a <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosopher</a>, while Collins was a street performer; Angel and Collins were both originally described as Caucasian; and Benny had a somewhat enlarged role in the story, taking part in songs like "Real Estate", which was later cut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>Many actual locations and events are included in, or are the inspiration for, elements of the musical. Life Café, where the "La Vie Bohème" numbers are set, was an actual restaurant (closed 2013) on 10th Street and Avenue B in the East Village of New York City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life_Cafe_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Life_Cafe-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rent:_Connection_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rent:_Connection-16">[16]</a></sup> The riot at the end of the first act is based on the East Village <a href="/wiki/Tompkins_Square_Park_riot_(1988)" title="Tompkins Square Park riot (1988)">riot in 1988</a> that arose as a result of the city-imposed curfew in <a href="/wiki/Tompkins_Square_Park" title="Tompkins Square Park">Tompkins Square Park</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rent:_Connection_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rent:_Connection-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Will I?", a song which takes place during a Life Support meeting and expresses the pain and fear of living a life with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a>, was inspired by a real event. Larson attended a meeting of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friends_in_deed" title="Friends in deed">Friends in Deed</a>, an organization that helps people deal with illness and grief, much like Life Support. After that first time, Larson attended the meetings regularly. During one meeting, a man stood up and said that he was not afraid of dying. He did say, however, that there was one thing of which he was afraid: Would he lose his dignity? From this question stemmed the first line in the single stanza of this song. The people present at the Life Support meeting in the show, such as Gordon, Ali, and Pam carry the names of Larson's friends who died of AIDS. In the Broadway show, the names of the characters in that particular scene (they introduce themselves) are changed nightly to honor the friends of the cast members who are living with or have died from AIDS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rent:_Friends_In_Deed_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rent:_Friends_In_Deed-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The scene and song "Life Support" were also based on Friends in Deed, as well as on Gordon, Pam, and Ali. Originally, the members of Life Support had a solid block of the "forget regret" refrain, and they talked about remembering love. When Jonathan's HIV positive friends heard this scene, they told him that having AIDS was not so easy to accept: it made you angry and resentful too, and the song did not match that. Jonathan then added a part where Gordon says that he has a problem with this "credo...my T-cells are low, I regret that news, okay?" Paul, the leader of the meeting, replies, "Okay...but, Gordon, how do you feel today?" Gordon admits that he is feeling the best that he has felt all year. Paul asks, "Then why choose fear?" Gordon says, "I'm a New Yorker. Fear's my life."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lynn_Thomson_controversy">Lynn Thomson controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lynn Thomson controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lynn Thomson was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dramaturg" title="Dramaturg">dramaturg</a> who was hired by New York Theatre Workshop to help rework <i>Rent</i>. She claimed that between early May and the end of October 1995, she and Larson co-wrote a "new version" of the musical. She sued the Larson estate for $40 million USD and sought 16% of the show's royalties, claiming she had written a significant portion of the lyrics and the libretto of the "new version" of <i>Rent</i>.</p>
<p>During the trial, Thomson could not recall the lyrics to the songs that she allegedly wrote, nor the structures of the libretto she claimed to have created. The judge ruled against her and gave the Jonathan Larson Estate full credit and right to <i>Rent</i>. A federal appellate court upheld the original ruling on appeal. In August 1998, the case was settled out of court. The terms of the settlement were not disclosed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dramaturgy_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-dramaturgy-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Act_One">Act One</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Act One">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On Christmas Eve in Manhattan's East Village, two roommates—Mark, a filmmaker, and Roger, a rock musician—struggle to stay warm and produce their art during Roger's <i>First Tune Up</i>. Their friend Collins, a gay anarchist professor, leaves a <i>First Voicemail</i> and plans to surprise them at their apartment, but is mugged before entering. At the same time, Roger and Mark's former roommate Benny, who has since become their harsh new landlord, has reneged on an earlier agreement and now demands last year's rent, before shutting down their electrical power during Roger's <i>Second Tune Up</i>. However, Mark and Roger rebel and pledge "We're not gonna pay last year's <i>Rent</i>...". Meanwhile, a <a href="/wiki/Cross-dressing" title="Cross-dressing">cross-dressing</a> street drummer (currently out of drag) named Angel finds Collins wounded in an alley and tends to him, asking <i>"You Okay, Honey?"</i>. The two are immediately attracted to each other, and learn that they are both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HIV_positive" title="HIV positive">HIV positive</a>. Roger also has HIV, along with his last girlfriend, who committed <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a>. Mark goes looking for Collins <i>("Tune Up #3")</i>, while Roger dreams of writing "<i>One Song Glory</i>, one song before I go...". An exotic dancer and neighbor, Mimi, walks in to flirt with Roger, who is hesitant to start a new relationship. She enters asking to <i>"Light My Candle..."</i>. Meanwhile, a <i>Second Voicemail</i> reveals that Joanne, a lawyer, is the new girlfriend of Maureen, a <a href="/wiki/Protest_art" title="Protest art">protest artist</a> who recently dumped Mark.</p>
<p>At last, the missing Collins enters the apartment, presenting Angel, who is now in <a href="/wiki/Drag_queen" title="Drag queen">full drag</a> and shares the money she made and the amusing story of how she attained it, boasting that it's "<i>Today 4 U</i>, tomorrow for me...". Benny arrives, speaking of Maureen's upcoming protest against his plans to evict the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeless" title="Homeless">homeless</a> from a lot where he is hoping to build a cyber arts studio. Benny offers that, if they convince Maureen to cancel the protest, then they can officially remain rent-free tenants. However, the two rebuff Benny's offer and he leaves, telling them <i>"You'll See..."</i>. Mark must leave to fix Maureen's sound equipment for the protest, but he meets Joanne at the stage. They overcome their awkwardness by connecting over their shared distrust of Maureen's promiscuous behaviors. They imagine how this might look like in the form of the <i>"Tango: Maureen"</i>. Mark joins his friends to film their HIV <i>"Life Support"</i> meeting, while Mimi attempts to "...go <i>Out Tonight</i>" and seduce Roger alone in the apartment, though he coldly tells her to "Come back <i>Another Day</i>...". After Mimi leaves, Roger reflects on his fear of dying from AIDS, while the life-support group echoes his thoughts. Everyone ends up asking "<i>Will I</i> lose my dignity?".</p>
<p>"<i>On the Street...</i>", Collins, Mark, and Angel protect a homeless woman from police harassment, but she chastises them. Collins talks about his dream of escaping New York City to "Open up a restaurant in <i>Santa Fe</i>...", and, soon, Collins and Angel confess their love for each other, proclaiming <i>"I'll Cover You..."</i>. Joanne hectically prepares for Maureen's show, telling everyone <i>"We're Okay"</i>, and Roger apologizes to Mimi, inviting her to come to the protest and the dinner afterwards. Police, vendors, and homeless people prepare for the protest, saying that "<i>Christmas Bells</i> are ringing...", and Maureen begins her avant-garde, if not over the top, performance, <i>"Over the Moon"</i>, based on "<a href="/wiki/Hey_Diddle_Diddle" title="Hey Diddle Diddle">Hey Diddle Diddle</a>". At Life Café after the show, Benny criticizes the protest and the group's <a href="/wiki/Bohemianism" title="Bohemianism">bohemian</a> lifestyle. In response, Mark and all the café's bohemian patrons defiantly rise up to celebrate their <i>"La Vie Bohème"</i> lifestyle. Mimi and Roger each discover that the other is HIV-positive and decide to move forward with their relationship, struggling to say <i>"I Should Tell You"</i>. Joanne explains that Mark and Roger's building has been padlocked and a riot has broken out, just before Roger and Mimi share their first kiss. Everyone proclaims to live "<i>Viva La Vie Bohème</i>".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Act_Two">Act Two</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Act Two">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The cast lines up to sing <i>"Seasons of Love"</i>, before the plot resumes with Mark and Roger gathering to break back into their locked apartment with their friends, believing "It's gonna be a <i>Happy New Year</i>...". A "<i>Third Voicemail</i>" reveals that Mark's footage of the riot has earned him a job offering at a <a href="/wiki/Tabloid_journalism" title="Tabloid journalism">tabloid</a> news company. The others continue their <i>Happy New Year</i>..." and finally break through the door just as Benny arrives, saying he wants to call a truce, revealing that Mimi, a former girlfriend of his, convinced him to change his mind. Mimi denies rekindling her relationship with Benny, but Roger is upset, and Mimi goes to her drug dealer for a fix.</p>
<p>Around Valentine's Day, Mark tells the audience that Roger and Mimi have been living together, but they are tentative with each other. It is also told that Maureen and Joanne are preparing another protest, and during rehearsal, Maureen cites Joanne's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Controlling_behavior" title="Controlling behavior">controlling behavior</a> and Joanne cites Maureen's promiscuous mannerisms, and they break up dramatically coming up with the ultimatum <i>"Take Me or Leave Me"</i>. Time speeds on to spring <i>("Seasons of Love B")</i>, but Roger and Mimi's relationship is strained by her escalating <a href="/wiki/Heroin" title="Heroin">heroin</a> usage and Roger's lasting jealousy and suspicion of Benny. Each alone, Roger and Mimi sing of love and loneliness, telling each other how they feel <i>"Without You"</i> as they watch Collins nurse Angel, whose health is declining from AIDS. Mark continues to receive calls offering a corporate job at a tabloid television show, as revealed by a "<i>Fourth Voicemail</i>". The couples have devolved into on-and-off relationships. A dance is performed representing all the couples' sex-lives <i>("Contact")</i>. At the climax of the number, the two former couples break up, and Angel suddenly dies. At the funeral, the friends briefly come together to share their memories with Collins being the last to reminisce <i>("I'll Cover You [Reprise]")</i>. On <i>"Halloween"</i> Mark expresses his fear of being the only one left surviving when the rest of his friends die of AIDS, and he finally accepts the corporate job offer. Roger reveals that he is leaving for Santa Fe, which sparks an argument about commitment between him and Mimi, and between Maureen and Joanne. Collins arrives and admonishes the entire group for fighting on the day of Angel's funeral, causing Maureen and Joanne to reconcile, but not Mimi and Roger. Collins is forcibly removed from the church for being unable to pay for Angel's funeral. Benny shows compassion by paying, causing him and Collins to recuperate their old friendship. The group shares a sad moment, knowing that between deaths and leaving, their close-knit friendships will be breaking up. Everyone tells each other <i>"Goodbye Love"</i>.</p>
<p>Months later, both Mark and Roger are simultaneously reaching an artistic <a href="/wiki/Epiphany_(feeling)" title="Epiphany (feeling)">epiphany</a>, as Roger finds his song in Mimi and Mark finds his film in Angel's memory. Roger returns to New York just in time for Christmas, and Mark quits his job to work on his own film once more. They both come to the conclusion "You are <i>What You Own</i>...". The characters' parents leave several messages on their phones <i>("Voicemail #5")</i>, and on Christmas Eve, exactly one year having passed, Mark prepares to screen his now-completed film to his friends. Roger has written his song, but no one can find Mimi for him to play it to. Benny's wife, discovering Benny's relationship with Mimi, has pulled Benny out of the East Village; the power suddenly blows and Collins enters with handfuls of cash, revealing that he reprogrammed an <a href="/wiki/Automated_teller_machine" title="Automated teller machine">ATM</a> at a grocery store to provide money to anybody with the code (A-N-G-E-L). Maureen and Joanne abruptly enter carrying Mimi, who has been homeless and is now weak and close to death. She begins to fade, but not before telling Roger that she loves him <i>("Finale A")</i>. Roger tells her to hold on as he plays her the song he wrote for her, <i>"Your Eyes"</i> which reveals the depths of his feelings for her. Mimi appears to die, but abruptly awakens, claiming to have been heading into a <a href="/wiki/Near-death_experience" title="Near-death experience">white light</a>, except that a vision of Angel told her to go back. The remaining friends gather together in a final moment of shared happiness and resolve to enjoy whatever time they have left with each other, affirming that there is "no day but today" <i>("Finale B")</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rent:_No_Day_but_Today_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rent:_No_Day_but_Today-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roles">Roles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Roles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_characters">Main characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Main characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><b>Mark Cohen</b> (Lead): A struggling Jewish-American documentary filmmaker and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narrator" title="Narrator">narrator</a> of the show. He is Roger's roommate; at the start of the show, he was recently dumped by Maureen.</li>
<li><b>Roger Davis</b> (Lead): A once-successful-but-now-struggling musician and ex-lead singer and rock guitarist who is HIV-positive and an ex-junkie. He hopes to write one last meaningful song before he dies. He is having a hard time coping with the fact that he, along with many others around him, knows that he is going to die. His girlfriend, April, killed herself after finding out that she was HIV-positive. He is roommates with Mark.</li>
<li><b>Mimi Márquez</b> (Lead): A Hispanic-American club dancer and drug addict. She lives downstairs from Mark and Roger, is Roger's love interest, and, like him, has HIV. She is also Benny's ex-lover.</li>
<li><b>Tom Collins</b> (Support): An <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchist</a> professor with AIDS. He is described by Mark as a "computer genius, teacher, and vagabond anarchist who ran naked through the <a href="/wiki/Parthenon" title="Parthenon">Parthenon</a>." Collins dreams of opening a restaurant in Santa Fe, where the problems in New York will not affect him and his friends. He was formerly a roommate of Roger, Mark, Benny, and Maureen, then just Roger and Mark, until he moves out.</li>
<li><b>Angel Dumott Schunard</b> (Support): A young transgender woman/drag queen who uses both she/her and he/him pronouns. Angel is a street percussionist with a generous disposition, who has AIDS; Collins' love interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><span id="Maureen_Johnson">Maureen Johnson</span></b> (Support): A <a href="/wiki/Performance_art" title="Performance art">performance artist</a> who is Mark's ex-girlfriend and Joanne's current girlfriend. She is very flirtatious and cheated on Mark (presumably with Joanne). Larson considered Maureen a lesbian, despite her relationships with men, and he specifically identified her as "lesbian" in the script itself. However, an old lesbian friend of Larson told him it was wrong to call Maureen a lesbian because of her attraction to men, and that is where the idea of her being a bisexual started.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rapp19_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rapp19-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Joanne Jefferson</b> (Support): An Ivy League-educated public interest <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">lawyer</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Lesbian" title="Lesbian">lesbian</a>. Joanne is the woman for whom Maureen left Mark. Joanne has very politically powerful parents (one is undergoing confirmation to be a judge, the other is a government official).</li>
<li><b>Benjamin "Benny" Coffin III</b> (Support): <a href="/wiki/Landlord" title="Landlord">Landlord</a> of Mark, Roger, and Mimi's apartment building and ex-roommate of Mark, Collins, Roger, and Maureen. Now married to Alison Grey of the Westport Greys, a very wealthy family involved in real estate, and he is considered <a href="/wiki/Yuppie" title="Yuppie">yuppie</a> scum and a sell-out by his ex-roommates. He at one time had a relationship with Mimi.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minor_characters">Minor characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Minor characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><b>Mrs. Cohen</b>: Mark's stereotypical Jewish mother. Her voicemail messages are the basis for the songs Voicemail #1, Voicemail #3, and Voicemail #5.</li>
<li><b>Alexi Darling</b>: The producer of Buzzline, a sleazy tabloid company that tries to employ Mark after his footage of the riot makes primetime. Sings Voicemail #3 and Voicemail #4.</li>
<li><b>Mr. and Mrs. Jefferson</b>: The wealthy parents of Joanne Jefferson, they leave her Voicemail #2. Mr. Jefferson is also one of the a cappella singers in Voicemail #5. Mr. and Mrs. Jefferson usually sing the solos in Seasons of Love.</li>
<li><b>Mrs. Davis</b>: Roger's confused mother who calls in Voicemail #5, asking continuously, "Roger, where are you?"</li>
<li><b>Mrs. Marquez</b>: Mimi's Spanish-speaking mother who sings in Voicemail #5, wondering, in Spanish, where she is.</li>
<li><b>Mr. Grey</b>: Benny's father-in-law who wants to buy out the lot.</li>
<li><b>The Man</b>: The local drug dealer whom Mimi buys from and Roger used to buy from. Based on the character Parpignol from <i>La Bohème</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Paul</b>: The man in charge of the Life Support group.</li>
<li><b>Gordon</b>: One of the Life Support members.</li>
<li><b>Steve</b>: One of the Life Support members.</li>
<li><b>Ali</b>: One of the Life Support members</li>
<li><b>Pam</b>: One of the Life Support members</li>
<li><b>Sue</b>: One of the Life Support members.</li>
<li>As notated in the script by Larson, the roles of all of the Life Support members are encouraged to take on the name that someone in the cast (or production) knows or has known to have succumbed to AIDS. In the final Broadway performance, Sue is renamed Lisa.</li>
<li><b>Squeegee Man</b>: A homeless person who chants "Honest living!" over and over during "Christmas Bells".</li>
<li><b>The Waiter</b>: A waiter at Life Cafe.</li>
<li><b>The Woman With Bags</b> or <b>Homeless Woman</b>: A woman who attacks Mark for trying to help her during "On The Street".</li>
<li><b>The Preacher</b> or <b>The Pastor</b>: The Preacher kicks Collins out of the church because he can't pay for Angel's funeral.</li>
</ul>
<p>There are also many other non-named roles such as Cops, Bohemians, Vendors, Homeless People.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Critical reception of <i>Rent</i> was positive not only for its acting and musical components, but for its representation of HIV positive individuals. Many critics praised the portrayal of characters such as Angel and Collins as being happy, with positive outlooks on life, rather than being resigned to death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> While critics and theatre patrons had largely positive reviews of the show, criticism was given to the show for the stereotypically negative portrayal of lesbian characters and the "glamourization" of the East Village in the late 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Billy Aronson said, "For the record, although I was ambivalent about Jonathan’s ideas for <i>Rent</i> when we were working together on it, I have come to love the show. And as tragic as it is that he didn’t live to see his work become a huge success, I believe he knew it would be. In our last conversation I asked how the show was going and he said, with complete assurance, that it was incredible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aronson_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aronson-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_impact_and_legacy">Cultural impact and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cultural impact and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song "Seasons of Love" became a successful pop song and often is performed on its own. Because of its connection to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Years" title="New Years">New Years</a> and looking back at times past, it is sometimes performed at graduations or school holiday programs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="RENT-heads">RENT-heads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: RENT-heads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Rent</i> gathered a following of fans who refer to themselves as "RENT-heads." The name originally referred to people who would camp out at the Nederlander Theater for hours in advance for the discounted $20 rush tickets to each show, though it generally refers to anyone who is obsessed with the show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_for_.27Rent.27_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Time_for_.27Rent.27-25">[25]</a></sup> These discounted tickets were for seats in the first two rows of the theater reserved for sale by lottery two hours prior to each show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_for_.27Rent.27_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Time_for_.27Rent.27-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reidel_Party_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reidel_Party-26">[26]</a></sup> Other Broadway shows have followed <i>Rent'</i>s example and now also offer cheaper tickets in efforts to make Broadway theater accessible to people who would otherwise be unable to afford the ticket prices.</p>
<p>The term originated in <i>Rent'</i>s first months on <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a>. The show's producers offered 34 seats in the front two rows of the orchestra for $20 each, two hours before the performance. Fans and others interested in tickets would camp out for hours in front of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nederlander_Theater" title="Nederlander Theater">Nederlander Theater</a> – which is on 41st Street, just outside <a href="/wiki/Times_Square" title="Times Square">Times Square</a> – to buy these tickets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Productions">Productions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Productions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_York_workshops_and_off-Broadway_production">New York workshops and off-Broadway production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: New York workshops and off-Broadway production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Rent</i> had its first staged reading at New York Theatre Workshop in March 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odyssey_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Odyssey-7">[7]</a></sup> A further two-week <a href="/wiki/New_York_Theatre_Workshop" title="New York Theatre Workshop">New York Theatre Workshop</a> version was performed in 1994 starring Anthony Rapp as Mark and Daphne Rubin-Vega as Mimi, and more workshops followed. The show opened on 1996, again at New York Theatre Workshop, and quickly gained popularity off-Broadway, receiving enthusiastic reviews. <i>The New York Times</i> theater critic <a href="/wiki/Ben_Brantley" title="Ben Brantley">Ben Brantley</a> called it an "exhilarating, landmark rock opera" with a "glittering, inventive score" that "shimmers with hope for the future of the American musical."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Another reviewer wrote, "<i>Rent</i> speaks to <a href="/wiki/Generation_X" title="Generation X">Generation X</a> the way that the musical <i><a href="/wiki/Hair_(musical)" title="Hair (musical)">Hair</a></i> spoke to the <a href="/wiki/Baby_boomers" title="Baby boomers">baby boomers</a> or those who grew up in the 1960s," while the <i>New York Times</i> similarly called it "a rock opera for our time, a <i>Hair</i> for the 90s."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comet_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Comet-44">[44]</a></sup> The show proved extremely successful off-Broadway, selling out all of its performances at the 150-seat theatre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rent:_Glory_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Rent:_Glory-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_Broadway_production">Original Broadway production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Original Broadway production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to its overwhelming popularity and the need for a larger theater, <i>Rent</i> moved to Broadway's previously derelict Nederlander Theatre on 41st Street on April 29, 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rent:_Glory_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Rent:_Glory-2">[2]</a></sup> On Broadway, the show achieved critical acclaim and word-of-mouth popularity. The production's ethnically diverse principal cast originally included <a href="/wiki/Taye_Diggs" title="Taye Diggs">Taye Diggs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilson_Jermaine_Heredia" title="Wilson Jermaine Heredia">Wilson Jermaine Heredia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jesse_L._Martin" title="Jesse L. Martin">Jesse L. Martin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Idina_Menzel" title="Idina Menzel">Idina Menzel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Pascal" title="Adam Pascal">Adam Pascal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Rapp" title="Anthony Rapp">Anthony Rapp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daphne_Rubin-Vega" title="Daphne Rubin-Vega">Daphne Rubin-Vega</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fredi_Walker" title="Fredi Walker">Fredi Walker</a>.</p>
<p>The production's controversial topics and innovative pricing, including same day-of-performance $20 tickets, helped to increase the popularity of musical theater amongst the younger generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marks_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marks-45">[45]</a></sup> The production was nominated for ten <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tony_Awards" title="Tony Awards">Tony Awards</a> in 1996 and won four: Best Musical, Best Book, Best Original Score and Best Performance by a Featured Actor in a Musical (Heredia)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonys_Search_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tonys_Search-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 24, 2006, the original Broadway cast reunited for a one-night performance of the musical at the Nederlander Theatre. This performance raised over $2,000,000 for the <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Larson_Performing_Arts_Foundation" title="Jonathan Larson Performing Arts Foundation">Jonathan Larson Performing Arts Foundation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friends_In_Deed" title="Friends In Deed">Friends In Deed</a> and New York Theatre Workshop. Former cast members were invited, and many from prior tours and former Broadway casts appeared, performing an alternate version of "Seasons of Love" as the finale of the performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Playbill_10th_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Playbill_10th-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Rent</i> closed on September 7, 2008, after a 12-year run and 5,123 performances,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP0907_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP0907-48">[48]</a></sup> making it the <a href="/wiki/List_of_the_longest-running_Broadway_shows" title="List of the longest-running Broadway shows">eleventh-longest-running Broadway show</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The production grossed over $280 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Time-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Original cast <a href="/wiki/Musical_ensemble" title="Musical ensemble">ensemble</a> members Rodney Hicks and Gwen Stewart returned to the cast at the time of the Broadway closing. Hicks played Benny and Stewart played the role she created, the soloist in the song "Seasons of Love". In addition, actress <a href="/wiki/Tracie_Thoms" title="Tracie Thoms">Tracie Thoms</a> joined the cast at the end of the run playing Joanne, the role she portrayed in the 2005 film version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP0907_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-AP0907-48">[48]</a></sup> The last Broadway performance was filmed and screened in movie theaters as <i>Rent: Filmed Live on Broadway</i> in September 2008. It was released on DVD and Blu-ray formats on February 3, 2009.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_North_American_tours">Early North American tours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Early North American tours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Successful United States national tours, the "Angel Tour" and the "Benny Tour", launched in the 1990s. Later, the non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Actors%27_Equity" title="Actors' Equity">Equity</a> tour started its run. There was also a Canadian tour (often referred to as the "Collins Tour").</p>
<p>The Angel tour began in November 1996 in Boston. It then went on to St. Paul, Minnesota, Washington, DC, Chicago (where Anthony Rapp temporarily joined the cast), Detroit, Cleveland, Philadelphia, Baltimore, Salt Lake City, Denver, and Los Angeles (where Daphne Rubin-Vega temporarily joined the cast), before finishing in San Francisco in September 1999. Cast members appearing in the Angel Cast included Simone, Manley Pope, Christian Anderson, <a href="/wiki/Carrie_Hamilton" title="Carrie Hamilton">Carrie Hamilton</a>, Amy Spanger, Joshua J. Greene, Evan D'Angeles, Cheri Smith, J. Marshall Evans, Julie Danao, Sylvia MacCalla, Kamilah Martin, Jonathon Morgan, Luther Creek, Kristoffer Cusick, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Vincent" title="Tony Vincent">Tony Vincent</a>, Courtney Corey, and Shaun Earl.</p>
<p>The Benny Tour began in July 1997 in San Diego, CA at the LaJolla Playhouse. Michael Grief, the original director of the Broadway show was also the artistic director of the LaJolla Playhouse and was instrumental in arranging for the Benny tour to begin in the smaller city of San Diego rather than Los Angeles, CA. It originally featured <a href="/wiki/Neil_Patrick_Harris" title="Neil Patrick Harris">Neil Patrick Harris</a> in the role of Mark Cohen. The Benny tour generally played shorter stops and often-smaller markets than the Angel Tour did. Cast members appearing in the Benny Cast included Eric Reed, Wilson Cruz, Julia Santana as Mimi, Keely Snelson, <a href="/wiki/D%27Monroe" title="D'Monroe">d'Monroe</a>, Mark Lull, Courtney Corey, Pierre Angelo Bayuga and Jonathon Morgan. 1999 – 2001 tenure included Julia Santana, Maggie Benjamin (Maureen, September 2000 – July 15, 2001), Scott Hunt (Mark), Tricia Young (Alexi), Haven Burton (Mrs. Cohen), Joshua J. Greene, etc...</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000_Irish_production">2000 Irish production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: 2000 Irish production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> production had an extended run at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympia_Theatre,_Dublin" title="Olympia Theatre, Dublin">Olympia Theatre, Dublin</a> in 2000 it starred <a href="/wiki/Sean_Pol_McGreevy" title="Sean Pol McGreevy">Sean Pol McGreevy</a> as Mark, Samuel Nicholl as Roger <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Tucker" title="Rachel Tucker">Rachel Tucker</a> as Maureen, Jacqui Dubois as Joanne, Mykal Rand as Collins, Thomas Goodridge as Angel and Allyson Brown as Mimi under the direction of Phil Willmot. The show returned to Dublin in 2009, this time performed by students of Dublin City University's Drama Society at The Helix Theatre, which then moved to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympia_Theatre,_Dublin" title="Olympia Theatre, Dublin">Olympia Theatre, Dublin</a> for a short run due to high demand. DCU's Drama Society mounted another production of the show in April 2013 at The Helix Theatre.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UK_productions">UK productions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: UK productions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The show made its UK premiere on April 21, 1998 at the West End's <a href="/wiki/Shaftesbury_Theatre" title="Shaftesbury Theatre">Shaftesbury Theatre</a> and officially opened on May 12, 1998. The original cast included Krysten Cummings as Mimi Marquez, Wilson Jermaine Heredia as Angel Schunard, Bonny Lockhart as Benjamin Coffin III, Jesse L. Martin as Tom Collins, Adam Pascal as Roger Davis, Anthony Rapp as Mark Cohen, and Jessica Tezier as Maureen Johnson. The show closed on October 30, 1999 after one-and-a-half years. Limited revivals took place at the <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales_Theatre" title="Prince of Wales Theatre">Prince of Wales Theatre</a> from December 4, 2001 to January 6, 2002; December 6, 2002 to March 1, 2003 (featuring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adam_Rickett" title="Adam Rickett">Adam Rickett</a> as Mark and <a href="/wiki/Caprice_Bourret" title="Caprice Bourret">Caprice</a> as Maureen). There was also a successful production for a limited run in Manchester in 2006 with an additional 'goodbye' performance in 2008 from the Manchester cast.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rent:_School_Edition"><i>Rent: School Edition</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Rent: School Edition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2007, a modified edition of <i>Rent</i> was made available to five non-professional acting groups in the United States for production. Billed as <span class="nowrap"><i>Rent: School Edition</i></span>, this version omits the song "Contact" and eliminates some of the coarse language and tones down some public displays of affection of the original.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-School_Edition_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-School_Edition-51">[51]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Shorewood_High_School_(Wisconsin)" title="Shorewood High School (Wisconsin)">Shorewood High School</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shorewood,_WI" title="Shorewood, WI">Shorewood, WI</a> became the first high school to perform an early version of the adaptation in May 2006. The high school was selected to present a workshop performance as part of <a href="/wiki/Music_Theatre_International" title="Music Theatre International">Music Theatre International</a>'s work to adapt the musical for younger actors and potentially more conservative audiences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>There were four test shows of the <i>Rent: School Edition</i>, the first of which premiered at the Stuart, Florida's Lyric Theater in June, 2007, produced by StarStruck Performing Arts Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The original cast of the finalized version of the Rent: School Edition was in summer of 2007 at Stagedoor Manor, a performing arts camp.</p>
<p>In November 2007, Harry S Truman High School in Levittown, PA performed Rent: School Edition on the high school stage, directed by Lou Volpe. Jonathan Larson's father was in attendance on closing night of the show.</p>
<p>In June 2009, California Youth Conservatory Theatre, a <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>-based youth theatre company which was originally licensed to perform <i>Rent: School Edition</i>, converted their performance license (through Music Theatre International) to the full-score version of Rent. This was the first <a class="external text" href="http://cyctheatre.webs.com/apps/photos/album?albumid=6599808" rel="nofollow">production</a> of the unedited <i>Rent</i> licensed and performed by a youth theatre group. This production was directed by Shaun T. Evans and co-directed by Karole Foreman. <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Hicks" title="Rodney Hicks">Rodney Hicks</a> (from the original Broadway cast) was a guest artistic contributor to this production. On December 26, 2015, CYC began another run of the full version of <i>Rent</i>, again directed by Shaun T. Evan who co-starred as Collins.</p>
<p>In June 2009, Verdugo Hills High School in Tujunga, CA became one of the first high schools in Los Angeles County to perform the School Edition successfully, though faced with many challenges and inquiries from the community. In June 2010, <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_High_School" title="Hollywood High School">Hollywood High School</a> in Hollywood, CA was the first high school to perform the original broadway version of <i>Rent</i>, including the song "Contact", which is omitted in the <i>School Edition</i>. In May 2012 the Pinellas County Center for the Arts at Gibbs High School, St Petersburg Florida performed the original broadway version of Rent.</p>
<p>In March 2013, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hillsboro_Comprehensive_High_School" title="Hillsboro Comprehensive High School">Hillsboro Comprehensive High School</a> became the first high school in the Southeastern United States to perform the edited edition of "Rent", eighth in the nation overall.</p>
<p>In November 2008, <a href="/wiki/Sudbury_Secondary_School" title="Sudbury Secondary School">Sudbury Secondary School</a> became the first high school to put on this production in a Canadian high school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2015, Las Vegas Academy of the Arts had Fredi Walker-Browne perform "Seasons of Love" with the cast during previews and had a workshop with the cast.</p>
<p>In 2016, <a href="/wiki/Strathcona_High_School" title="Strathcona High School">Strathcona High School</a> become the first high school to put on the original broadway production of Rent. It was performed without any omissions or changes to the original script.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rent_Remixed"><i>Rent Remixed</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Rent Remixed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On October 16, 2007, the production <i>Rent Remixed</i> opened at the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_York%27s_Theatre" title="Duke of York's Theatre">Duke of York's Theatre</a> in London's West End. Directed by <a href="/wiki/William_Baker_(fashion_designer)" title="William Baker (fashion designer)">William Baker</a>, it was set in the present day. The cast included <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Thornton" title="Oliver Thornton">Oliver Thornton</a> (Mark), <a href="/wiki/Luke_Evans_(actor)" title="Luke Evans (actor)">Luke Evans</a> (Roger), Craig Stein (Benny), <a href="/wiki/Leon_Lopez" title="Leon Lopez">Leon Lopez</a> (Collins), <a href="/wiki/Francesca_Jackson" title="Francesca Jackson">Francesca Jackson</a> (Joanne), Jay Webb (Angel), <a href="/wiki/Siobh%C3%A1n_Donaghy" title="Siobhán Donaghy">Siobhán Donaghy</a> (Mimi), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denise_Van_Outen" title="Denise Van Outen">Denise Van Outen</a> (Maureen). From December 24, 2007, the role of Maureen was played by <a href="/wiki/Jessie_Wallace" title="Jessie Wallace">Jessie Wallace</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> The production received generally unfavorable reviews. The Guardian gave it only one out of five stars, writing, "They call this 'Rent Remixed'. I'd dub it 'Rent Reduced', in that the late Jonathan Larson's reworking of La Bohème, while never a great musical, has been turned into a grisly, synthetic, pseudo pop concert with no particular roots or identity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The production closed on February 2, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>The production radically altered elements of the musical including defining the characters of Mimi, Angel and Mark as British. Songs were reordered (including Maureen's first appearance as the Act I finale). The rehaul of the score was masterminded by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Anderson_(musician)" title="Steve Anderson (musician)">Steve Anderson</a> and featured radically rearranged versions of Out Tonight, Today 4 U, Over the Moon and Happy New Year.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australian_productions">Australian productions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Australian productions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1999, an Australian production featured <a href="/wiki/Justin_Smith_(Australian_actor)" title="Justin Smith (Australian actor)">Justin Smith</a> as Mark, <a href="/wiki/Rodger_Corser" title="Rodger Corser">Rodger Corser</a> as Roger, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Opell_Ross&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Opell Ross (page does not exist)">Opell Ross</a> as Angel and Australian ARIA Award winner <a href="/wiki/Christine_Anu" title="Christine Anu">Christine Anu</a> as Mimi. The tour began in Sydney and wrapped in Melbourne.</p>
<p>A production in <a href="/wiki/Perth" title="Perth">Perth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a> was mounted in 2007 and featured <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Callea" title="Anthony Callea">Anthony Callea</a> as Mark, <a href="/wiki/Tim_Campbell_(actor)" title="Tim Campbell (actor)">Tim Campbell</a> as Roger, Jaya Henderson as Mimi, <a href="/wiki/Courtney_Act" title="Courtney Act">Courtney Act</a> as Angel, Shai Yammanne as Tom Collins, Sharon Wisniewski as Joanne, Andrew Conaghan as Benny and <a href="/wiki/Nikki_Webster" title="Nikki Webster">Nikki Webster</a> as Maureen.</p>
<p>In 2015, Hayes Theatre Co.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> in Sydney staged a sell-out season at the Hayes Theatre under the direction of Shaun Rennie and musical direction of Andrew Worboys. The production starred Stephen Madsen as Mark, Linden Furnell as Roger, Loren Hunter as Mimi, Christopher Scalzo as Angel, Nana Matapule as Collins, Laura Bunting as Maureen and <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Casey_Donovan" title="Casey Donovan">Casey Donovan</a> as Joanne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> An encore season of this production has been proposed for March–April 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2005.E2.80.932006_International_tour">2005–2006 International tour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: 2005–2006 International tour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The international tour, which played from 2005 to 2006, started in Singapore in 2005 and ended in Taipei in 2006. It also visited Hong Kong, Beijing, Shanghai, and Seoul.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2009_U.S._National_tour">2009 U.S. National tour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: 2009 U.S. National tour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A national tour starring Adam Pascal and Anthony Rapp, reprising their original Broadway (and film) roles, launched in January 2009 in Cleveland, OH. After having a reprise performance in the summer of 2007, they signed on to be part of the 2009 tour. Original "Seasons of Love" soloist Gwen Stewart signed on to this tour as well. Joining them were Nicolette Hart as Maureen, Justin Johnston as Angel, Lexi Lawson as Mimi, Michael McElroy as Collins, Jacques C. Smith as Benny, and Haneefah Wood as Joanne. Ensemble members were Karmine Alers, Toby Blackwell, Adam Halpin, Trisha Jeffrey, Joshua Kobak, Telly Leung, Caren Tackett, Jed Resnick, Andy Senor, Cary Shields, Yuka Takara, and John Watson.</p>
<p>At the tour stop in <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>, Pascal herniated two discs in his neck and was put on medical leave. Cary Shields, an understudy who had replaced Pascal when <i>Rent</i> first opened on Broadway, filled in. Pascal made a full recovery and returned to the show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2009 National Tour ended on February 7, 2010, in Sacramento, CA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Tour stops included: Los Angeles, Seattle, Costa Mesa, San Diego, Toronto, Phoenix, Sacramento, Orlando, Tampa, Miami, St. Louis, Milwaukee, Chicago, Des Moines, Tokyo, Cincinnati, Pittsburgh, Norfolk, and Houston.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_Hollywood_Bowl">2010 Hollywood Bowl</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: 2010 Hollywood Bowl">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

His <i><a href="/wiki/Beastly_(film)" title="Beastly (film)">Beastly</a></i> co-star <a href="/wiki/Vanessa_Hudgens" title="Vanessa Hudgens">Vanessa Hudgens</a> played Mimi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 30, 2010, it was confirmed that <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Brady" title="Wayne Brady">Wayne Brady</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Tveit" title="Aaron Tveit">Aaron Tveit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skylar_Astin" title="Skylar Astin">Skylar Astin</a>, Collins Pennie, <a href="/wiki/Tracie_Thoms" title="Tracie Thoms">Tracie Thoms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telly_Leung" title="Telly Leung">Telly Leung</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gwen_Stewart" title="Gwen Stewart">Gwen Stewart</a> had also been cast as Collins, Roger, Mark, Benny, Joanne, Angel, and the Season of Love soloist (and additional roles) respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bowl_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-bowl-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 4, 2010, it was confirmed that <a href="/wiki/Nicole_Scherzinger" title="Nicole Scherzinger">Nicole Scherzinger</a> would round out the cast of <i>Rent</i> playing Maureen. Additionally the show's ensemble included Yassmin Alers, Eric B. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_Off-Broadway_revival">2011 Off-Broadway revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: 2011 Off-Broadway revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The show was revived <a href="/wiki/Off-Broadway" title="Off-Broadway">Off-Broadway</a> at Stage 1 of <a href="/wiki/New_World_Stages" title="New World Stages">New World Stages</a> with previews starting July 14, 2011 and a scheduled opening of August 11, 2011. This was the first New York Revival of the show since the original production closed less than three years earlier. The production was directed by <i>Rent'</i>s original director <a href="/wiki/Michael_Greif" title="Michael Greif">Michael Greif</a>. Joining Greif were several Tony Award winning/nominated designers. Almost the entire show was different from the original yet the reinvention did not please the critics, who complained that the new actors did not have a feel for the characters they were playing and it made the show feel contrived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The Off-Broadway production of RENT closed on September 9, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Audio_recordings">Audio recordings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Audio recordings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2005_film">2005 film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rent_(musical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: 2005 film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rent_(film)" title="Rent (film)">Rent (film)</a></div>
<p><i>Rent</i> was adapted into a movie directed by <a href="/wiki/Chris_Columbus_(filmmaker)" title="Chris Columbus (filmmaker)">Chris Columbus</a> with a screenplay by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Chbosky" title="Stephen Chbosky">Stephen Chbosky</a>. With the exception of Daphne Rubin-Vega and Fredi Walker, the original Broadway cast members reprised the principal roles. Rosario Dawson played Mimi and Tracie Thoms was cast as Joanne, as Rubin-Vega (Mimi) was pregnant at the time of filming and Walker (Joanne) felt she was too old for the part. Released on November 23, 2005, the film remained in the box office top ten for three weeks. Several plot elements were changed slightly, and some of the songs were changed to spoken dialogue in the film. The soundtrack was produced by <a href="/wiki/Rob_Cavallo" title="Rob Cavallo">Rob Cavallo</a>, engineered by Doug McKean and features renowned session musicians <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Muhoberac" title="Jamie Muhoberac">Jamie Muhoberac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tim_Pierce" title="Tim Pierce">Tim Pierce</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dorian_Crozier" title="Dorian Crozier">Dorian Crozier</a>. The film received mixed reviews.</p>
