<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lefschetz fixed-point theorem</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, the <b>Lefschetz fixed-point theorem</b> is a formula that counts the <a href="/wiki/Fixed_point_(mathematics)" title="Fixed point (mathematics)">fixed points</a> of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuous_function_(topology)" title="Continuous function (topology)">continuous mapping</a> from a <a href="/wiki/Compact_space" title="Compact space">compact</a> <a href="/wiki/Topological_space" title="Topological space">topological space</a> <i>X</i> to itself by means of <a href="/wiki/Trace_(linear_algebra)" title="Trace (linear algebra)">traces</a> of the induced mappings on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homology_group" title="Homology group">homology groups</a> of <i>X</i>. It is named after <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Lefschetz" title="Solomon Lefschetz">Solomon Lefschetz</a>, who first stated it in 1926.</p>
<p>The counting is subject to an imputed <a href="/wiki/Multiplicity_(mathematics)" title="Multiplicity (mathematics)">multiplicity</a> at a fixed point called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fixed_point_index" title="Fixed point index">fixed point index</a>. A weak version of the theorem is enough to show that a mapping without <i>any</i> fixed point must have rather special topological properties (like a rotation of a circle).</p>

<p>then <i>f</i> has at least one fixed point, i.e. there exists at least one <i>x</i> in <i>X</i> such that <i>f</i>(<i>x</i>) = <i>x</i>. In fact, since the Lefschetz number has been defined at the homology level, the conclusion can be extended to say that any map <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homotopic" title="Homotopic">homotopic</a> to <i>f</i> has a fixed point as well.</p>
<p>Note however that the converse is not true in general: Λ<sub><i>f</i></sub> may be zero even if <i>f</i> has fixed points.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sketch_of_a_proof">Sketch of a proof</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lefschetz_fixed-point_theorem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sketch of a proof">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>First, by applying the <a href="/wiki/Simplicial_approximation_theorem" title="Simplicial approximation theorem">simplicial approximation theorem</a>, one shows that if <i>f</i> has no fixed points, then (possibly after subdividing <i>X</i>) <i>f</i> is homotopic to a fixed-point-free <a href="/wiki/Simplicial_map" title="Simplicial map">simplicial map</a> (i.e., it sends each simplex to a different simplex). This means that the diagonal values of the matrices of the linear maps induced on the <a href="/wiki/Simplicial_homology" title="Simplicial homology">simplicial chain complex</a> of <i>X</i> must be all be zero. Then one notes that, in general, the Lefschetz number can also be computed using the alternating sum of the matrix traces of the aforementioned linear maps (this is true for almost exactly the same reason that the <a href="/wiki/Euler_characteristic#Topological_definition" title="Euler characteristic">Euler characteristic has a definition in terms of homology groups</a>; see <a href="#Relation_to_the_Euler_characteristic">below</a> for the relation to the Euler characteristic). In the particular case of a fixed-point-free simplicial map, all of the diagonal values are zero, and thus the traces are all zero.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_the_Brouwer_fixed_point_theorem">Relation to the Brouwer fixed point theorem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lefschetz_fixed-point_theorem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relation to the Brouwer fixed point theorem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Lefschetz fixed point theorem generalizes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brouwer_fixed_point_theorem" title="Brouwer fixed point theorem">Brouwer fixed point theorem</a>, which states that every continuous map from the <i>n</i>-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Unit_disk" title="Unit disk">closed unit disk</a> <i>D</i><sup><i>n</i></sup> to <i>D</i><sup><i>n</i></sup> must have at least one fixed point.</p>
<p>This can be seen as follows: <i>D</i><sup><i>n</i></sup> is compact and triangulable, all its homology groups except H<sub>0</sub> are 0, and every continuous map <i>f</i> : <i>D</i><sup><i>n</i></sup> → <i>D</i><sup><i>n</i></sup> induces the identity map <i>f</i><sub>*</sub> : H<sub>0</sub>(<i>D</i><sup><i>n</i></sup>, <b>Q</b>) → H<sub>0</sub>(<i>D</i><sup><i>n</i></sup>, <b>Q</b>) whose trace is one; all this together implies that Λ<sub><i>f</i></sub> is non-zero for any continuous map <i>f</i> : <i>D</i><sup><i>n</i></sup> → <i>D</i><sup><i>n</i></sup>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_context">Historical context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lefschetz_fixed-point_theorem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Historical context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
