<p><b>Roguelike</b> is a subgenre of <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video games</a> characterized by a <a href="/wiki/Dungeon_crawl" title="Dungeon crawl">dungeon crawl</a> through <a href="/wiki/Procedural_generation" title="Procedural generation">procedurally generated</a> <a href="/wiki/Level_(video_gaming)" title="Level (video gaming)">game levels</a>, turn-based gameplay, tile-based graphics, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permanent_death" title="Permanent death">permanent death</a> of the player-character. Most roguelikes are based on a <a href="/wiki/High_fantasy" title="High fantasy">high fantasy</a> narrative, reflecting their influence from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tabletop_role_playing_game" title="Tabletop role playing game">tabletop role playing games</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i>. Though the roguelikes <i><a href="/wiki/Beneath_Apple_Manor" title="Beneath Apple Manor">Beneath Apple Manor</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sword_of_Fargoal" title="Sword of Fargoal">Sword of Fargoal</a></i> predate it, the 1980 game <i><a href="/wiki/Rogue_(video_game)" title="Rogue (video game)">Rogue</a></i> is considered the forerunner and the namesake of the genre, with derivative games mirroring <i>Rogue</i><span style="padding-left:0.2em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/Text-based_game" title="Text-based game">character-</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sprite_(computer_graphics)" title="Sprite (computer graphics)">sprite-based</a> graphics. These games were popularized among college students and computer programmers of the 1980s and 1990s, leading to a large number of variants but adhering to these common gameplay elements, often titled the "Berlin Interpretation". Some of the better-known variants include <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hack_(Unix_video_game)" title="Hack (Unix video game)">Hack</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/NetHack" title="NetHack">NetHack</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Domains_of_Mystery" title="Ancient Domains of Mystery">Ancient Domains of Mystery</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Moria_(video_game)" title="Moria (video game)">Moria</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Angband_(video_game)" title="Angband (video game)">Angband</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Tales_of_Maj%27Eyal" title="Tales of Maj'Eyal">Tales of Maj'Eyal</a></i>. The Japanese series of <i><a href="/wiki/Mystery_Dungeon" title="Mystery Dungeon">Mystery Dungeon</a></i> games by <a href="/wiki/Chunsoft" title="Chunsoft">Chunsoft</a>, inspired by <i>Rogue</i>, also fall within the concept of roguelike games.</p>
<p>More recently, with more powerful home computers and gaming systems, new variations of roguelikes incorporating other gameplay genres, thematic elements and graphical styles have become popular, typically retaining the notion of procedural generation and permanent death of the player-character. <a href="/wiki/Independent_video_game_development" title="Independent video game development">Indie games</a> like <i><a href="/wiki/Weird_Worlds:_Return_to_Infinite_Space" title="Weird Worlds: Return to Infinite Space">Weird Worlds: Return to Infinite Space</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Spelunky" title="Spelunky">Spelunky</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Binding_of_Isaac_(video_game)" title="The Binding of Isaac (video game)">The Binding of Isaac</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/FTL:_Faster_Than_Light" title="FTL: Faster Than Light">FTL: Faster Than Light</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Rogue_Legacy" title="Rogue Legacy">Rogue Legacy</a></i> helped to establish the use of roguelike elements in other genres. These titles are sometimes labeled as "roguelike-like", "rogue-lite", or "procedural death labyrinths" to reflect the variation from titles which mimic the gameplay of traditional roguelikes more faithfully. Other games, like <i><a href="/wiki/Diablo_(video_game)" title="Diablo (video game)">Diablo</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/UnReal_World" title="UnReal World">UnReal World</a></i>, key titles in the <a href="/wiki/Action_role-playing_game" title="Action role-playing game">action role-playing</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Survival_game" title="Survival game">survival game</a> genres respectively, took inspiration from roguelikes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay_and_design">Gameplay and design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roguelike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gameplay and design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_gameplay">General gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roguelike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: General gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Deriving from the concepts of <a href="/wiki/Tabletop_role-playing_game" title="Tabletop role-playing game">tabletop role-playing games</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i>, nearly all roguelikes give the player control of a character, which they may customize by selecting a <a href="/wiki/Character_class" title="Character class">class</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(fantasy)" title="Race (fantasy)">race</a>, and gender, and adjusting <a href="/wiki/Attribute_(role-playing_games)" title="Attribute (role-playing games)">attributes</a> points and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skill_(role-playing_games)" title="Skill (role-playing games)">skills</a>. At the start of the game, the character is placed at the top-most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Level_(computer_and_video_games)" title="Level (computer and video games)">level</a> of a dungeon, with basic equipment such as a simple weapon, armor, torches, and food. Following along the role-playing concept of a <a href="/wiki/Dungeon_crawl" title="Dungeon crawl">dungeon crawl</a>, the player moves the character through the dungeon, collecting treasure which can include new weapons, armors, magical devices, potions, scrolls, food, and in-game money, while having to fight <a href="/wiki/Mob_(video_gaming)" title="Mob (video gaming)">monsters</a> that roam the dungeon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorker_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker-1">[1]</a></sup> Most combat is performed simply by attempting to move the character into the same space as the monster, which the game then calculates the damage that the character and monster deal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp2-2">[2]</a></sup> Other types of attacks, such as firing an arrow or performing an offensive magic spell, can often be performed as well.</p>
<p>Defeating monsters earns the character <a href="/wiki/Experience_point" title="Experience point">experience points</a>, and after earning enough points, the character will gain an experience level, improving their <a href="/wiki/Health_(gaming)" title="Health (gaming)">hit points</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magic_(gaming)" title="Magic (gaming)">magic capability</a>, and other attributes. Monsters may drop treasure to be looted. On the other hand, the character dies if they lose all their hit points. As most roguelikes feature the concept of <a href="/wiki/Permadeath" title="Permadeath">permadeath</a>, this represents the end of the game, and the player will need to restart the game with a newly made character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_intro_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_intro-3">[3]</a></sup> Roguelikes are nearly always <a href="/wiki/Turn-based_strategy" title="Turn-based strategy">turn-based</a>, with the game only reacting when the player makes an action with the character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_intro_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_intro-3">[3]</a></sup> This allows players to evaluate a difficult situation, such as being cornered by several monsters, at their own pace and determine the best strategy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorker_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The player generally has to explore the dungeon to reveal its contents similar to a <a href="/wiki/Fog_of_war#in_video_games" title="Fog of war">fog of war</a>. Many roguelikes include factors related to light sources such as a torch to provide illumination to see monsters in nearby squares, and <a href="/wiki/Line_of_sight_(gaming)" title="Line of sight (gaming)">line of sight</a> can also limit what monsters are visible. Dungeons tend to be connected by stairs; lower dungeon levels generally are more difficult than higher ones, so that a character that is underdeveloped may have difficulty by progressing too fast. Dungeon levels and the population of monsters and treasure within them are generated randomly using <a href="/wiki/Procedural_generation" title="Procedural generation">procedural generation</a>, so that no game is the same on subsequent playthroughs. Most roguelikes have an ultimate goal of either claiming an item located at the deepest level of the dungeon, or to defeat a specific monster that lives on that level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_intro_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_intro-3">[3]</a></sup> Typical roguelikes include a scoreboard, ranking the player's performance once their game is over (be it through permadeath or completing the objective) based on the amount of treasure, money, and experience earned, and how fast the player completed the game in such cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-play_80_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-play_80-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The genre of roguelike broadly encompasses the gameplay that was popularized by the <a href="/wiki/Text-based_game" title="Text-based game">text-based</a> game <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rogue_(computer_game)" title="Rogue (computer game)">Rogue</a></i> (1980), which bore out many variations due to its success;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorker_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcgamer_50_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-pcgamer_50-5">[5]</a></sup> As of 2015, several hundred games claiming to be roguelikes were available through the <a href="/wiki/Steam_(software)" title="Steam (software)">Steam</a> game catalog,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_intro_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_intro-3">[3]</a></sup> and the user-run <a href="/wiki/Wiki" title="Wiki">wiki</a> RogueBasin tracks hundreds of roguelikes and their development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-makeuseof_spin_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-makeuseof_spin-6">[6]</a></sup> Because of the expansion of numerous variations on the roguelike theme, the gameplay elements characterizing the roguelike genre were explicitly defined at the International Roguelike Development Conference 2008 held in Berlin, Germany; these factors encompass what is known as the "<b>Berlin Interpretation</b>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespy_roguelikes_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamespy_roguelikes-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsw_berlin_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-gsw_berlin-9">[9]</a></sup> Some of the factors used in this definition include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Roguelike games create random <a href="/wiki/Tile-based_video_game" title="Tile-based video game">tile-based</a> levels, monster encounters, and treasure through procedural generation on each new game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1up_essential_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-1up_essential-10">[10]</a></sup> Preferably roguelikes adhering to the Berlin Interpretation use ASCII-based graphics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsw_berlin_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-gsw_berlin-9">[9]</a></sup> They may include pre-determined levels as well, such as a town level common to the <i><a href="/wiki/Moria_(video_game)" title="Moria (video game)">Moria</a></i> family where the player can buy and sell equipment. Generated layouts typically incorporate rooms connected by corridors, some of which may be preset to some degree (e.g., monster lairs or treasuries). Open areas or natural features, such as rivers, may occur, though these are considered against the Berlin Interpretation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsw_berlin_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-gsw_berlin-9">[9]</a></sup> While early roguelikes would have levels confined to the bounds of the terminal screen, other roguelikes enabled a much larger level size, only a portion shown to the player as they moved around it.</li>
<li>Roguelikes traditionally implement permadeath. Once a character dies, the player must begin a new game, which will regenerate the game's levels anew due to procedural generation. A "save game" feature will only provide suspension of gameplay and not a limitlessly recoverable state; the <a href="/wiki/Saved_game" title="Saved game">stored session</a> is deleted upon resumption or character death. Players can circumvent this by backing up stored game data ("save scumming"), an act that is usually considered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cheating_(video_games)" title="Cheating (video games)">cheating</a>; the developers of <i>Rogue</i> introduced the permadeath feature after introducing a save function, finding that players were repeatedly loading saved games to achieve the best results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp2-2">[2]</a></sup> According to <i>Rogue</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Michael Toy, they saw their approach to permadeath not as a means to make the game painful or difficult but to put weight on every decision the player made as to create a more immersive experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>The combat system is <a href="/wiki/Turn-based_strategy" title="Turn-based strategy">turn-based</a> instead of <a href="/wiki/Real-time_strategy" title="Real-time strategy">real-time</a>. Gameplay is usually step-based, where player actions are performed serially and take a variable measure of in-game time to complete. Game processes (e.g., monster movement and interaction, progressive effects such as poisoning or starvation) advance based on the passage of time dictated by these actions. This requires the player to learn and apply tactics as they progress in the dungeon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsw_berlin_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-gsw_berlin-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Roguelikes generally have complex interacting systems that allow the player to complete certain goals in multiple ways, creating <a href="/wiki/Emergent_gameplay" title="Emergent gameplay">emergent gameplay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsw_berlin_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-gsw_berlin-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_heart_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_heart-12">[12]</a></sup> For example, to get through a locked door, the player may attempt to pick the lock, kick it down, burn down the door, or even tunnel around it, depending on their current situation and inventory. A common phrase associated with <i>NetHack</i> is "The Dev Team Thinks of Everything" in that the developers seem to have anticipated every possible combination of actions that a player may attempt to try in their gameplay strategy, such as using gloves to protect one's character while wielding the corpse of a <a href="/wiki/Cockatrice" title="Cockatrice">cockatrice</a> as a weapon to petrify enemies by its touch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp6_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp6-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>Roguelike games are focused on <a href="/wiki/Hack_and_slash" title="Hack and slash">hack and slash</a>-based gameplay, where the goal is to kill lots of monsters, and where other peaceful options do not exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsw_berlin_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-gsw_berlin-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>The identity of <a href="/wiki/Magic_(gaming)" title="Magic (gaming)">magical</a> items, including magically enchanted items, varies across games. Newly discovered objects only offer a vague physical description that is randomized between games, with purposes and capabilities left unstated. For example, a "bubbly" potion might heal wounds one game, then poison the player character in the next. Items are often subject to alteration, acquiring specific traits, such as a curse, or direct player modification. Exploration and discovery to learn the identity and function of items is of high value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsw_berlin_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-gsw_berlin-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>The player must use resource management to survive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsw_berlin_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-gsw_berlin-9">[9]</a></sup> Items that help sustain the player, such as food and healing items, are in limited supply, and the player must figure out how to use these most advantageously in order to survive in the dungeon. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USGamer" title="USGamer">USGamer</a></i> further considers "stamina decay" as another feature related to resource management. The player's character constantly needs to find food to survive or will die from hunger, which prevents the player from exploiting health regeneration by simply either passing turns for a long period of time or fighting very weak monsters at low level dungeons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgamer_guide_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgamer_guide-14">[14]</a></sup> Rich Carlson, one of the creators of an early roguelike-like <i><a href="/wiki/Strange_Adventures_in_Infinite_Space" title="Strange Adventures in Infinite Space">Strange Adventures in Infinite Space</a></i>, called this aspect a sort of "clock", imposing some type of deadline or limitation on how much the player can explore and creating tension in the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digitaleel_interview_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-digitaleel_interview-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>Most are single-player games. On <a href="/wiki/Multi-user" title="Multi-user">multi-user</a> systems, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_score" title="High score">leaderboards</a> are often shared between players. Some roguelikes allow traces of former player characters to appear in later game sessions in the form of <a href="/wiki/Ghost" title="Ghost">ghosts</a> or <a href="/wiki/Headstone" title="Headstone">grave markings</a>. Some games such as NetHack even have the player's former characters reappear as enemies within the dungeon. Multi-player turn-based derivatives such as <i>TomeNET</i>, <i>MAngband</i>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crossfire_(computer_game)" title="Crossfire (computer game)">Crossfire</a></i> do exist and are playable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/On-line_and_off-line" title="On-line and off-line">online</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_bonus_7day_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_bonus_7day-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_roguelikes">Early roguelikes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roguelike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early roguelikes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NeXTSTEP_Nethack.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/NeXTSTEP_Nethack.png/220px-NeXTSTEP_Nethack.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/NeXTSTEP_Nethack.png/330px-NeXTSTEP_Nethack.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/NeXTSTEP_Nethack.png/440px-NeXTSTEP_Nethack.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NeXTSTEP_Nethack.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Nethack</i> running in multiple windows on a NeXT computer</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Early roguelikes were developed to be played on <a href="/wiki/Text-based_user_interface" title="Text-based user interface">text-based user interfaces</a>, commonly UNIX-based computer mainframes and terminals used at colleges and universities before transitioning to personal computers. Games used a mix of <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ANSI_escape_codes" title="ANSI escape codes">ANSI</a> characters to represent elements of the dungeon levels, creatures, and items on the level. These games typically included one or two text lines presenting the player's current status at the bottom of the screen, and text-based menu screens to manage inventory, statistics, and other details. The player's character was nearly always represented by the <code>@</code> across text-based roguelikes, which had been chosen by the developers of <i>Rogue</i> to stand for "where you're at".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp2_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp2-2">[2]</a></sup> Other examples would include <code>$</code> for monetary treasure and <code>D</code> for a dragon. Later games would take advantage of color-based text graphics to increase the variation of creature types, such as a red <code>D</code> for a red dragon that would shoot fire, while a green <code>D</code> could indicate a green dragon that would shoot acid. Players would use the <a href="/wiki/Computer_keyboard" title="Computer keyboard">keyboard</a>, using one keypress to enter a <a href="/wiki/Command_(computing)" title="Command (computing)">command</a>. With modern computer systems, users developed alternate means of displaying the game, such as graphical tilesets and <a href="/wiki/Isometric_projection" title="Isometric projection">Isometric</a>-based graphical front ends, as well as interfaces that took advantage of keyboard and mouse UI controls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>As computers offered more advanced user interfaces, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windows_(computing)" title="Windows (computing)">windows</a> and point-and-click <a href="/wiki/Menu_(computing)" title="Menu (computing)">menus</a>, many traditional roguelikes were modified to include support for having multiple windows. This was useful to not only show the character-based dungeon, but details on the character's inventory, the monster they were in battle with, and other status messages, in separate windows. Having access to multiple windows also allowed having menus to complete more complex commands.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roguelike-likes_and_procedural_death_labyrinths">Roguelike-likes and procedural death labyrinths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roguelike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Roguelike-likes and procedural death labyrinths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Abyss_Odyssey_-_Screenshot_06.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1022" data-file-width="1816" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Abyss_Odyssey_-_Screenshot_06.jpg/260px-Abyss_Odyssey_-_Screenshot_06.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Abyss_Odyssey_-_Screenshot_06.jpg/390px-Abyss_Odyssey_-_Screenshot_06.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Abyss_Odyssey_-_Screenshot_06.jpg/520px-Abyss_Odyssey_-_Screenshot_06.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Abyss_Odyssey_-_Screenshot_06.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Abyss_Odyssey" title="Abyss Odyssey">Abyss Odyssey</a></i> combines roguelike elements with <a href="/wiki/Beat_%27em_up" title="Beat 'em up">beat 'em up</a> gameplay</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>With computers and video game consoles capable of more advanced graphics and gameplay, numerous games have emerged that are loosely based on the classic roguelike design but diverge in one or more features. Many of these games use the concepts of procedurally generated maps and permadeath, while moving away from tile-based movement and turn-based gameplay, often using another gameplay genre such as <a href="/wiki/Action_game" title="Action game">action games</a> or <a href="/wiki/Platform_game" title="Platform game">platformers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Other titles deriving from roguelike games are based on the observation that the traditional roguelikes are difficult with a steep <a href="/wiki/Learning_curve" title="Learning curve">learning curve</a>, and a player may never complete these games over numerous play sessions, making these titles difficult to sell to a broader audience. These new games would include elements to reduce the difficulty as to draw in a larger audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieee_insight_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-ieee_insight-20">[20]</a></sup> Many games with some of the Berlin Interpretation elements call themselves "roguelike", but bear little resemblance to the original <i>Rogue</i>, causing confusion and dilution of the term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>As such, the term "roguelike-like" or "rogue-lite" has been used to distinguish these games that possess some, but not all, of the Berlin Interpretation features from those that exactly meet the Berlin roguelike definition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The phrase "procedural death labyrinth" has also been applied to such games, as they retain the notion of permadeath and random level generation but lack the other high-value factors normally associated with roguelike games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Roguelike-likes are generally much shorter games intended to be winnable within a single gaming session, in contrast to traditional roguelikes that are designed for multiple sessions of gameplay. Associated with their short length, many roguelike-likes feature a <a href="/wiki/Metagaming" title="Metagaming">metagame</a>, whereby achieving certain goals will unlock features such as the ability to select a new character at the start of the game or the addition of new items and monsters in the procedural generation of the game's levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Several roguelike-likes feature daily challenges, in which a preset <a href="/wiki/Random_seed" title="Random seed">random seed</a> is used to generate the game's levels in a deterministic fashion so that each player will have the same encounters; players attempt to complete the game through those levels or otherwise get the highest score through online leaderboards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Roguelike-likes may also allow the player to enter the random seed directly as to be able to rechallenge the same set of levels or share a difficult set of levels with other players.</p>
<p><i>US Gamer</i> further identified games they consider <a href="/wiki/Edge_case" title="Edge case">edge cases</a> of being roguelikes or roguelike-likes, as they are inspired by <i>Rogue</i>, and "that stray a bit further from the genre but still manage to scratch the same itch as a great roguelike". These include games such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Diablo_(series)" title="Diablo (series)">Diablo</a></i> series, <i><a href="/wiki/ToeJam_%26_Earl" title="ToeJam &amp; Earl">ToeJam &amp; Earl</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Dwarf_Fortress" title="Dwarf Fortress">Dwarf Fortress</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgamer_guide_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-usgamer_guide-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roguelike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronology_of_roguelike_video_games" title="Chronology of roguelike video games">Chronology of roguelike video games</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history_.281975.E2.80.931980.29">Early history (1975–1980)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roguelike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Early history (1975–1980)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The creation of roguelike games came from hobbyist programmers and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hack_(computing)" title="Hack (computing)">computer hackers</a>, attempting to create games for the nascent computer field in the early 1980s, particularly influenced by the 1975 <a href="/wiki/Adventure_game" title="Adventure game">text adventure game</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Colossal_Cave_Adventure" title="Colossal Cave Adventure">Colossal Cave Adventure</a></i> (often simply titled <i>Adventure</i>), and from the <a href="/wiki/High_fantasy" title="High fantasy">high fantasy</a> setting of the tabletop game <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i>. Some elements of the roguelike genre were present in dungeon crawlers written for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PLATO_system" title="PLATO system">PLATO system</a>. This includes <i><a href="/wiki/Pedit5" title="Pedit5">pedit5</a></i> (1975) believed to be the first dungeon crawl game, and featured random monster encounters, though only used a single fixed dungeon level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieee_insight_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-ieee_insight-20">[20]</a></sup> <i>pedit5</i> inspired similar PLATO-based dungeon crawlers <i><a href="/wiki/Dnd_(video_game)" title="Dnd (video game)">dnd</a></i> (1975), <i>orthanc</i> (1978), <i><a href="/wiki/Moria_(PLATO)" title="Moria (PLATO)">Moria</a></i> (1978), and <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar_(1979_video_game)" title="Avatar (1979 video game)">avatar</a></i> (1979).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieee_insight_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-ieee_insight-20">[20]</a></sup> It is unclear if these PLATO games inspired the roguelike genre as there is no evidence that the early roguelike creators had access to these games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>The core roguelike games were developed independently of each other, many of the developers not learning about their respective projects until several years after the genre took off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp4_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp4-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Roguelike games were initially developed for computing environments with limited memory, including shared mainframe systems and early home computers; this limitation prevented developers from retaining all but a few dungeon levels in memory while the game was running, leading to procedural generation to avoid the memory storage issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Roguelikes were also often written in languages like <a href="/wiki/BASIC" title="BASIC">BASIC</a>, which are not as efficient in memory and data management compared to modern languages, further limiting the scope the game could cover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp1-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Concurrent_variants">Concurrent variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roguelike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Concurrent variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Though the term "roguelike" derives from the 1980 game <i>Rogue</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-essential_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-essential-32">[32]</a></sup> the first known game with the core roguelike gameplay elements was <i><a href="/wiki/Beneath_Apple_Manor" title="Beneath Apple Manor">Beneath Apple Manor</a></i> (1978), written by Don Worth for the <a href="/wiki/Apple_II" title="Apple II">Apple II</a>; <i>Beneath Apple Manor</i> is also recognized as the first commercial roguelike game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp1_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp1-31">[31]</a></sup> The game, inspired by Worth's enjoyment of <i>Dungeons &amp; Dragons</i> roleplaying, included procedural generation using a modification of the random maze generator from the game <i>Dragon Maze</i>, role-playing elements for the characters, tile-based movement and turn-based combat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp1_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp1-31">[31]</a></sup> Though <i>Beneath Apple Manor</i> predated <i>Rogue</i>, it was not as popular as <i>Rogue</i>: <i>Rogue</i> had advantage of being distributed over <a href="/wiki/ARPANET" title="ARPANET">ARPANET</a> which many college students had easy access to, while <i>Beneath Apple Manor</i> was packaged and sold by hand by Worth either at local stores or through mail fulfillment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp1_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp1-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another early roguelike whose development pre-dated <i>Rogue</i> was <i><a href="/wiki/Sword_of_Fargoal" title="Sword of Fargoal">Sword of Fargoal</a></i> (1982), developed by Jeff McCord starting in 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp4_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp4-29">[29]</a></sup> The game was based on <i>GammaQuest</i>, an earlier title McCord had created on the <a href="/wiki/Commodore_PET" title="Commodore PET">Commodore PET</a> which he shared locally with friends while a student at <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay_High_School" title="Henry Clay High School">Henry Clay High School</a> in Kentucky; the game itself was based on a <i>Dungeons &amp; Dragons</i> campaign he had run himself in the prior years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp4_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp4-29">[29]</a></sup> Before graduating and attending the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tennessee" title="University of Tennessee">University of Tennessee</a> in 1981, he had started work on <i>GammaQuest II</i>, which required the player to navigate through randomly generated dungeon levels, acquire a sword, and make it back to the surface with that sword through more randomly generated levels. The more advanced computers available at the school, such as the <a href="/wiki/Commodore_VIC-20" title="Commodore VIC-20">Commodore VIC-20</a>, enabled him to expand out the game further from the highly limited memory on the PET. On seeing the prospects of selling computer software, he eventually got a publication deal with <a href="/wiki/Epyx" title="Epyx">Epyx</a>, where they helped him to refine the marketing of the game, renaming it <i>Sword of Fargoal</i>, and giving him access to the more powerful <a href="/wiki/Commodore_64" title="Commodore 64">Commodore 64</a>, enabling him to use graphics and sound as part of the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp4_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp4-29">[29]</a></sup> The game was considered a success, and when it was ported to the PC in 1983, it out-shone <i>Rogue</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s PC release the same year due to <i>Sword of Fargoal</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s superior graphics and sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp4_29-4"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp4-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rogue"><i>Rogue</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roguelike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rogue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rogue_(video_game)" title="Rogue (video game)">Rogue (video game)</a></div>
<p><i>Rogue</i> was written by <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Wichman" title="Glenn Wichman">Glenn Wichman</a> and Michael Toy in 1980 while students at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz" title="University of California, Santa Cruz">University of California, Santa Cruz</a>. The game was inspired by Toy's prior experience in playing the 1971 <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek_(text_game)" title="Star Trek (text game)">Star Trek</a></i> game and programming clones of it for various other computer systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp2_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp2-2">[2]</a></sup> It was also inspired by interactive fiction <i>Adventure</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp2_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp2-2">[2]</a></sup> While looking for a way to randomize the experience of <i>Adventure</i>, they came across <a href="/wiki/Ken_Arnold" title="Ken Arnold">Ken Arnold</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Curses_(programming_library)" title="Curses (programming library)">curses</a> library that enabled them to better manipulate characters on the terminal screen, prompting Toy and Wichman to create a graphical-like randomized adventure game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp2_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp2-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_history_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_history-35">[35]</a></sup> They created the story of the game by having the player seek out the "Amulet of Yendor", "Yendor" being "Rodney" spelled backwards, the name of the wizard they envisioned had created the dungeon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp2_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp2-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>Rogue</i> was originally executed on a <a href="/wiki/VAX-11" title="VAX-11">VAX-11/780</a> computer; its limited memory forced them to use a simple text-based interface for the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Toy eventually dropped out of school but got a job at the computing labs at <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>, where he met with Arnold. Arnold helped to optimize the curses code and implement more features into the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp3_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp3-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Rogue</i> proved popular with college students and computer researchers at the time, including <a href="/wiki/Ken_Thompson" title="Ken Thompson">Ken Thompson</a>; <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Ritchie" title="Dennis Ritchie">Dennis Ritchie</a> had joked at the time that <i>Rogue</i> was "the biggest waste of CPU cycles in history".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp3_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp3-37">[37]</a></sup> Its popularity led to the game's inclusion on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BSD_UNIX" title="BSD UNIX">BSD UNIX</a> v4.2 in 1984, though at that time, without its <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp3_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp3-37">[37]</a></sup> Toy and Arnold had anticipated selling <i>Rogue</i> commercially and were hesitant about releasing it; Toy would go on to meet Jon Lane at <a href="/wiki/Olivetti" title="Olivetti">Olivetti</a>, and together they would go on to create the company A.I. Design to port the games for various home systems along with publishing support by Epyx, later bringing Wichman back to help.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp3_37-3"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp3-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

<p>The popularity of <i>Rogue</i> led developers to create their own versions of the game, though their efforts were originally limited by the lack of access to <i>Rogue</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s source, which was not released until BSD v4.3 in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chapter_5_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chapter_5-38">[38]</a></sup> These developers resorted to building games from scratch similar to <i>Rogue</i> but with features that they wanted to see.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chapter_5_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chapter_5-38">[38]</a></sup> These versions would be distributed with source code, and along with the original <i>Rogue</i> source, other developers were able to create <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_fork" title="Software fork">software forks</a> of the games, adding in new monsters, items, and gameplay features, creating several dozen variants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chapter_5_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chapter_5-38">[38]</a></sup> This process was aided by switching code to languages with better <a href="/wiki/Strong_and_weak_typing" title="Strong and weak typing">data typing</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Object-oriented_programming" title="Object-oriented programming">object-oriented</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scripting_language" title="Scripting language">scripting</a> languages, and cleaning up and modularizing the code so that contributors can better follow where changes can be made.</p>
<p>While there are some direct variants of <i>Rogue</i>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Brogue_(video_game)" title="Brogue (video game)">Brogue</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brogue_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-brogue-39">[39]</a></sup> most variants of <i>Rogue</i> could be classified into two branches based on two key games, <i>Moria</i> and <i>Hack</i>, that were developed in the spirit of <i>Rogue</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Moria-based"><i>Moria</i>-based</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roguelike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Moria-based">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Moria_(video_game)" title="Moria (video game)">Moria</a></i> (1983) was developed by Robert Alan Koeneke while a student at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oklahoma" title="University of Oklahoma">University of Oklahoma</a>, inspired by both <i>Adventure</i> and <i>Rogue</i>. Having access to a <a href="/wiki/VAX-11" title="VAX-11">VAX-11/780</a>, but without the source to <i>Rogue</i> due to computer administrator restrictions, he began trying to recreate <i>Rogue</i> but specifically flavored with the <a href="/wiki/Moria_(Middle-earth)" title="Moria (Middle-earth)">complex underground cave maze</a> of the same name in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.R.R._Tolkien" title="J.R.R. Tolkien">J.R.R. Tolkien</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Earth" title="Middle Earth">Middle Earth</a> stories. Following Tolkien's fiction, the player's goal was to descend to the depths of Moria to defeat the <a href="/wiki/Balrog" title="Balrog">Balrog</a>, akin to a <a href="/wiki/Boss_(video_gaming)" title="Boss (video gaming)">boss battle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp7_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp7-41">[41]</a></sup> As with <i>Rogue</i>, levels were not persistent: when the player left the level and then tried to return, a new level would be procedurally generated. Among other improvement to <i>Rogue</i>, Koeneke included a persistent town at the highest level where players could buy and sell equipment, and the use of <a href="/wiki/Data_structure" title="Data structure">data structures</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(programming_language)" title="Pascal (programming language)">Pascal</a> language allowed him to create a more diverse <a href="/wiki/Bestiary" title="Bestiary">bestiary</a> within the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp7_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp7-41">[41]</a></sup> He got help from several playtesters as well as another student, Jimmey Wayne Todd, who helped to program a deeper character generation system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp7_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp7-41">[41]</a></sup> <i>UMoria</i> (short for <i>UNIX Moria</i>) is a close variation on <i>Moria</i> by Jim E. Wilson, making the game more portable to a larger variety of computers while fixing various bugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp7_41-3"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp7-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<p><i><a href="/wiki/Angband_(video_game)" title="Angband (video game)">Angband</a></i> (1990) was developed by Alex Cutler and Andy Astrand while attending the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Warwick" title="University of Warwick">University of Warwick</a>. Having played <i>UMoria</i>, they wanted to expand the game even further. Working from <i>UMoria</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s code, they increased the number of levels and monsters, flavored the game based on <a href="/wiki/Angband" title="Angband">Angband</a>, the massive fortress controlled by <a href="/wiki/Morgoth" title="Morgoth">Morgoth</a> from Tolkien's fiction, and incorporated more of the deadlier creatures described within the Middle Earth mythology. They kept the Balrog as a difficult creature that must be overcome at a mid-game level, while Morgoth became the final boss the player must defeat to win the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp8_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp8-42">[42]</a></sup> Following Cutler and Astrand's graduation, Sean March and Geoff Hill took over the development to see the game through to a public release outside of the university, adding in elements such as giving the player a sense of the rewards and dangers of a level when they entered it the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp8_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp8-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once <i>Angband</i> was released to the public via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USENET" title="USENET">USENET</a>, there were efforts to have code maintainers (the "devteam") to fix bugs, clean up the code and implement suggestions into the code.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp8_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp8-42">[42]</a></sup> Due to numerous shifts in those maintaining the code (due to other obligations), and the number of potential user suggestions to include, <i>Angband</i> would become highly <a href="/wiki/Fork_(software_development)" title="Fork (software development)">forked</a>, leading to a number of <i>Angband</i> variants; at least sixty known variants exist with about a half dozen still under active development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-engadget_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-engadget-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variants_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-variants-44">[44]</a></sup> One significant fork was <i>ZAngband</i> (1994) (short for <i>Zelazny Angband</i>), which expanded on <i>Angband</i> and altered the theme towards <a href="/wiki/Roger_Zelazny" title="Roger Zelazny">Roger Zelazny</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronicles_of_Amber" title="The Chronicles of Amber">The Chronicles of Amber</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The <i>ZAngband</i> codebase would be used to create <i>Tales of Middle Earth</i> (<i>TOME</i>) in 2002, which later swapped out the Tolkien and Zelazny fiction setting for a new original one to become <i><a href="/wiki/Tales_of_Maj%27Eyal" title="Tales of Maj'Eyal">Tales of Maj'Eyal</a></i> (2009).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The vanilla <i>Angband</i> remains in development today by the devteam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp8_42-3"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp8-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hack-based"><i>Hack</i>-based</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roguelike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Hack-based">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hack_(Unix_video_game)" title="Hack (Unix video game)">Hack</a></i> (1982) was developed by Jay Fenlason with help from Kenny Woodland, Mike Thome, and Jonathan Payne, students at <a href="/wiki/Lincoln-Sudbury_Regional_High_School" title="Lincoln-Sudbury Regional High School">Lincoln-Sudbury Regional High School</a> at the time, while participating in the school's computer lab overseen by Brian Harvey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chapter_5_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chapter_5-38">[38]</a></sup> Harvey had been able to acquire a <a href="/wiki/PDP-11" title="PDP-11">PDP-11/7</a> mainframe for the school and instituted a course curriculum that allowed students to do whatever they wanted on the computers, including playing games, as long as they had completed assignments by the end of each semester. Fenlason, Woodland, Thome, and Payne met through these courses and became a close-group of friends and competent programmers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chapter_5_38-4"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chapter_5-38">[38]</a></sup> Harvey had invited the group to the computer labs at UC Berkeley where they had the opportunity to use the mainframe systems there, and were introduced to <i>Rogue</i>, inspiring them to create their own version as their class project. Fenlason had created a list of features they wanted to improve upon in <i>Rogue</i> such as having a level's layout saved once the player moved off that level. They approached Toy and Arnold at a local <a href="/wiki/USENIX" title="USENIX">USENIX</a> conference for the source code to <i>Rogue</i>, but were refused, forcing them to develop the routines from scratch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chapter_5_38-5"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chapter_5-38">[38]</a></sup> The resulting program, <i>Hack</i>, stayed true to the original <i>Dungeons and Dragons</i> influences, and derived its name from being both a "hack and slash" game as well as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hack_(computer_code)" title="Hack (computer code)">programming hack</a> to recreate <i>Rogue</i> without having access to its source code.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chapter_5_38-6"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chapter_5-38">[38]</a></sup> Fenlason was not able to include all the desired features, and his involvement in <i>Hack</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s development concluded after the students had left the school. Fenlason had provided the source code to <i>Hack</i> to the USENIX conferences to be distributed on their digital tapes, from which it was later discovered and built upon through USENET newsgroups, porting it to various systems. Like <i>Angband</i>, the maintainership of the <i>Hack</i> code passed through several hands, and some variants were created by different forks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp6_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp6-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>Hack</i> would eventually be dropped in favor of <i><a href="/wiki/NetHack" title="NetHack">NetHack</a></i> (1987).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-engadget_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-engadget-43">[43]</a></sup> When Mike Stephenson, an analyst at a computer hardware manufacture, took maintainership of <i>Hack</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s code, he took the opportunity to improve upon it along with suggestions for game improvements from <a href="/wiki/Izchak_Miller" title="Izchak Miller">Izchak Miller</a>, a philosophy professor at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>, and Janet Walz, another computer hacker. Calling themselves the DevTeam, they began to make major modifications to <i>Hack</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s code. They named their new version <i>NetHack</i>, in part for the collaboration of the game over USENET.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp6_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp6-13">[13]</a></sup> <i>NetHack</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s major deviations from <i>Hack</i> were the introduction of a wider variety of monsters, borrowing from other mythologies and lores, including anachronistic and contemporary cultural elements (such as a tourist class with a flash-bulb camera inspired by <a href="/wiki/Terry_Pratchett" title="Terry Pratchett">Terry Pratchett</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Discworld" title="Discworld">Discworld</a></i> series)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> in the high fantasy setting, and the use of pre-defined levels with some procedural elements that the player would encounter deeper in the dungeons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp6_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp6-13">[13]</a></sup> Further iterations of the game included branching pathways through the dungeon and optional character-based quests that could grant the player an extremely useful item to complete the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp6_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp6-13">[13]</a></sup> Though the DevTeam released the code publicly, they carefully maintained who could contribute to the code base to avoid excessive forking of the vanilla game, and remain relatively quiet about suggested improvements to each release, working in relatively secrecy from its player base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp6_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp6-13">[13]</a></sup> There are separate forks of <i>NetHack</i>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Slash%27EM" title="Slash'EM">Slash'EM</a></i>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Domains_of_Mystery" title="Ancient Domains of Mystery">Ancient Domains of Mystery</a></i> (1994), or <i>ADOM</i> for short, derived from concepts presented in <i>NetHack</i>. <i>ADOM</i> was originally developed by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Biskup" title="Thomas Biskup">Thomas Biskup</a> while a student at <a href="/wiki/Technical_University_of_Dortmund" title="Technical University of Dortmund">Technical University of Dortmund</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp9_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp9-48">[48]</a></sup> After playing through <i>Rogue</i> and <i>Hack</i>, he came to <i>NetHack</i> and was inspired by the game but dismayed at the complexity and elements he found unnecessary or distracting. Biskup created <i>ADOM</i> from scratch with the aim of creating a more story-driven game than <i>NetHack</i> that kept the depth of gameplay with a focused theme and setting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp9_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp9-48">[48]</a></sup> The resulting game featured several different dungeons, many generated procedurally, connected through an <a href="/wiki/Overworld" title="Overworld">overworld</a> map of the fictional realm of Ancardia, and would have the player complete various quests in those dungeons to progress the game. A major feature was the influence of Chaos forces through unsealed portals, which the player would have to close. While in areas affected by Chaos, the player's character would become tainted, causing mutations that could be either detrimental or beneficial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp9_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp9-48">[48]</a></sup> <i>ADOM</i>, like <i>NetHack</i> and <i>Angband</i>, would gain a devteam to maintain the code and implement updates and patches to avoid excessive forking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp9_48-3"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp9-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_variants">Other variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roguelike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Not all early roguelikes were readily classified as <i>Hack</i> or <i>Moria</i> descendants. <i><a href="/wiki/Larn_(video_game)" title="Larn (video game)">Larn</a></i> (1986), developed by Noah Morgan, borrowed concepts from both <i>Hack</i> (in that there are persistent and fixed levels) and <i>Moria</i> (in the availability of a shop level and general difficulty increasing with dungeon level), but while these two games have spiraled in size to take multiple play sessions to complete, <i>Larn</i> was aimed to be completed in a single session. <i>Larn</i> also uses a fixed-time feature, in that the player had only so many turns to complete a goal, though there were ways to jump back in time as to extend play. This was further limited by the stamina decay created by the need to feed the character, as failure to find food would result in the character's death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> <i>Omega</i>, developed by the Laurence Brothers in the late 1980s, is credited with introducing an overworld concept to the roguelike genre, prior to the feature's appearance in <i>ADOM</i>. <i>Omega</i> was often remembered for its odd inventory approach in which the player would have to pick up an object, considering it being held, and then moving that object to a bag or an equipment slot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-makeuseof_spin_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-makeuseof_spin-6">[6]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Linley%27s_Dungeon_Crawl" title="Linley's Dungeon Crawl">Linley's Dungeon Crawl</a></i> (1995) was created by Linley Henzell and featured a skill-based character progression system, in which <a href="/wiki/Experience_point" title="Experience point">experience points</a> could be used to improve specific skills, such as weapon proficiency or trap detection. One fork of this would form the basis for <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeon_Crawl_Stone_Soup" title="Dungeon Crawl Stone Soup">Dungeon Crawl Stone Soup</a></i> (2006).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mystery_Dungeon_games_.281995.E2.80.93onward.29"><i>Mystery Dungeon</i> games (1995–onward)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roguelike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Mystery Dungeon games (1995–onward)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A variation of the roguelike genre came from Japan, primarily through the <i><a href="/wiki/Mystery_Dungeon" title="Mystery Dungeon">Mystery Dungeon</a></i> series by <a href="/wiki/Chunsoft" title="Chunsoft">Chunsoft</a>. The first game in this series was <i><a href="/wiki/Torneko_no_Daib%C5%8Dken:_Fushigi_no_Dungeon" title="Torneko no Daibōken: Fushigi no Dungeon">Torneko no Daibōken: Fushigi no Dungeon</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">トルネコの大冒険 不思議のダンジョン</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Torneko's Great Adventure: Mystery Dungeon</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> based on the <i><a href="/wiki/Dragon_Quest" title="Dragon Quest">Dragon Quest</a></i> series. Producer <a href="/wiki/Koichi_Nakamura" title="Koichi Nakamura">Koichi Nakamura</a> stated the game was directly inspired by <i>Rogue</i>, looking to make a "more understandable, more easy-to-play version" of the title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The developers had developed ways to reduce the difficulty of the roguelike by using progressively more difficult dungeons that were randomly generated, and made permadeath an option by selection of difficulty level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgamer_guide_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-usgamer_guide-14">[14]</a></sup> To date, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Mystery_Dungeon_video_games" title="List of Mystery Dungeon video games">over 25 games</a> in the <i>Mystery Dungeon</i> series have been made across various platforms,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgamer_guide_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-usgamer_guide-14">[14]</a></sup> and spanning various franchises, including <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chocobo_(series)" title="Chocobo (series)">Chocobo</a></i> series based on <i><a href="/wiki/Final_Fantasy" title="Final Fantasy">Final Fantasy</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Mystery_Dungeon" title="Pokémon Mystery Dungeon">Pokémon Mystery Dungeon</a></i> based on <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon" title="Pokémon">Pokémon</a></i>. Several titles in the <i>Mystery Dungeon</i> series were popular, and would become a staple of the Japanese video game market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-play_80_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-play_80-4">[4]</a></sup> The games were not as successful in Western markets when published there, as the target players – younger players who likely had not experienced games like <i>Rogue</i> – found the lack of a traditional role-playing game save system odd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other Japanese role-playing games would incorporate random dungeon generation as part of their design, mimicking part of the nature of roguelikes, and were considered roguelike titles when published in Western markets. Such titles include <i><a href="/wiki/Vagrant_Story" title="Vagrant Story">Vagrant Story</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Shining_Soul" title="Shining Soul">Shining Soul</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Baroque_(video_game)" title="Baroque (video game)">Baroque</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1up_essential_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-1up_essential-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Massively_multiplayer_online_role_playing_game" title="Massively multiplayer online role playing game">massively multiplayer online role playing game</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Final_Fantasy_XIV" title="Final Fantasy XIV">Final Fantasy XIV</a></i> added a randomly-generated Deep Dungeon that was inspired by the procedural generation of roguelikes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continued_development_in_Western_markets_.282002.E2.80.93onward.29">Continued development in Western markets (2002–onward)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roguelike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Continued development in Western markets (2002–onward)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dungeon_crawl_stone_soup.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="481" data-file-width="800" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Dungeon_crawl_stone_soup.png/220px-Dungeon_crawl_stone_soup.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Dungeon_crawl_stone_soup.png/330px-Dungeon_crawl_stone_soup.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Dungeon_crawl_stone_soup.png/440px-Dungeon_crawl_stone_soup.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dungeon_crawl_stone_soup.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A screenshot of <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeon_Crawl_Stone_Soup" title="Dungeon Crawl Stone Soup">Dungeon Crawl Stone Soup</a></i>, incorporating user interface elements more common to other hack-and-slash games such as a mini-map and a persistent inventory window</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Though new classical roguelike variants would continue to be developed within the Western market, the genre languished as more advanced personal computers capable of improved graphics capabilities and games that utilized these features became popular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> However, some of these new graphical games drew influence for roguelike concepts, notably <a href="/wiki/Action_role-playing_game" title="Action role-playing game">action role-playing games</a> like <a href="/wiki/Blizzard_Entertainment" title="Blizzard Entertainment">Blizzard Entertainment</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Diablo_(video_game)" title="Diablo (video game)">Diablo</a></i> (1996). <i>Diablo</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s creator, <a href="/wiki/David_Brevik" title="David Brevik">David Brevik</a>, acknowledged that games like <i>Rogue</i>, <i>Nethack</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Telengard" title="Telengard">Telengard</a></i> and other roguelikes influenced the design of <i>Diablo</i>, including the nature of randomly generated dungeons and loot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>Existing roguelikes continue to be developed: a sequel to <i>ADOM</i> successfully received <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crowd_funding" title="Crowd funding">crowd funding</a> in 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craddock_chp9_48-4"><a href="#cite_note-craddock_chp9-48">[48]</a></sup> while <i>NetHack</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s first major release in ten years in 2015 is set to help the DevTeam expand the game further.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> New roguelikes that adhere to core Berlin Interpretation rules are still being created, including <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeon_Crawl_Stone_Soup" title="Dungeon Crawl Stone Soup">Dungeon Crawl Stone Soup</a></i> (2006), <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_of_Dredmor" title="Dungeons of Dredmor">Dungeons of Dredmor</a></i> (2011), and <i><a href="/wiki/Dragon_Fin_Soup" title="Dragon Fin Soup">Dragon Fin Soup</a></i> (2015).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgamer_guide_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-usgamer_guide-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> A subclass of "coffeebreak roguelikes" that could be completed in a short period of time have developed, often derived from entries in the <a href="#Seven_Day_Roguelike_Challenge">Seven Day Roguelike Challenge</a>; examples include such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DoomRL" title="DoomRL">DoomRL</a></i> (2013) and <i><a href="/wiki/Desktop_Dungeons" title="Desktop Dungeons">Desktop Dungeons</a></i> (2013)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Some games would also take advantage of the ease of developing in the tile-based ASCII interfaces common to roguelikes. For example, the highly popular <i><a href="/wiki/Dwarf_Fortress" title="Dwarf Fortress">Dwarf Fortress</a></i> (2006) uses the roguelike interface atop a <a href="/wiki/Construction_and_management_simulation" title="Construction and management simulation">construction and management simulation</a>, and would serve as a major inspiration for <i><a href="/wiki/Minecraft" title="Minecraft">Minecraft</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgamer_guide_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-usgamer_guide-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> while <i><a href="/wiki/SanctuaryRPG" title="SanctuaryRPG">SanctuaryRPG</a></i> (2014) is a more traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Console_role_playing_game" title="Console role playing game">turn-based role playing game</a> featuring a scripted story that uses an ASCII interface and roguelike gameplay elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/UnReal_World" title="UnReal World">UnReal World</a></i> (1992), the game that is considered to be the forerunner of the <a href="/wiki/Survival_game" title="Survival game">survival game</a> genre,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> and which frequently uses procedural generation to create the worlds that players must survive in, was developed by Sami Maaranen and was influenced by roguelikes, with its initial interface being similar to that of NetHack<i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></i></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_of_the_roguelike-like_.282005-onward.29">Growth of the roguelike-like (2005-onward)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roguelike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Growth of the roguelike-like (2005-onward)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The roguelike genre saw a resurgence in Western markets after 2000 through <a href="/wiki/Independent_video_game_development" title="Independent video game development">independent developers</a> that created a new subgenre designated the roguelike-like. Indie developers began to incorporate roguelike elements into genres not normally associated with roguelikes, creating games that would form the basis of this new subgenre. Two of the earliest cited examples of roguelike-likes are <i><a href="/wiki/Strange_Adventures_in_Infinite_Space" title="Strange Adventures in Infinite Space">Strange Adventures in Infinite Space</a></i> (2002) and its sequel <i><a href="/wiki/Weird_Worlds:_Return_to_Infinite_Space" title="Weird Worlds: Return to Infinite Space">Weird Worlds: Return to Infinite Space</a></i> (2005) by <a href="/wiki/Digital_Eel" title="Digital Eel">Digital Eel</a>, both space exploration games that included randomly generated planets and encounters, and permadeath. Digital Eel based their work on the space exploration game <i><a href="/wiki/Starflight" title="Starflight">Starflight</a></i> along with roguelikes like <i>Nethack</i> but wanted to provide a shorter experience that would be easier to replay, akin to tabletop <a href="/wiki/Beer_and_pretzels_game" title="Beer and pretzels game">beer and pretzels games</a> like <i><a href="/wiki/Deathmaze" title="Deathmaze">Deathmaze</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sorcerer%27s_Cave" title="The Sorcerer's Cave">The Sorcerer's Cave</a></i> that has elements in common with roguelikes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digitaleel_interview_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-digitaleel_interview-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crawl_screenshot_6.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1080" data-file-width="1920" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Crawl_screenshot_6.png/220px-Crawl_screenshot_6.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Crawl_screenshot_6.png/330px-Crawl_screenshot_6.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Crawl_screenshot_6.png/440px-Crawl_screenshot_6.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crawl_screenshot_6.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Crawl_(video_game)" title="Crawl (video game)">Crawl</a></i> (2014) is a multiplayer, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brawler" title="Brawler">brawler</a>-based roguelike-like that has up to four players playing in a mixed cooperative/competitive gameplay to explore procedurally generated dungeons.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Spelunky" title="Spelunky">Spelunky</a></i> (2008), released shortly after the formation of the Berlin Interpretation, is considered to be a major contribution to the growth of indie-developed roguelike-likes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgamer_guide_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-usgamer_guide-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcgamer_50_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-pcgamer_50-5">[5]</a></sup> <i>Spelunky</i> was developed by Derek Yu, who wanted to take the deep gameplay that is offered by roguelikes and combine it with the ease and pick-up-and-play of a <a href="/wiki/Platform_game" title="Platform game">platformer</a>. The result was a platform game incorporating the notion of permadeath in which the player takes an explorer character through randomly-generated underground caves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespy_roguelikes_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamespy_roguelikes-8">[8]</a></sup> The intent was to create "deep" gameplay in which the game could be replayed over and over again, with the randomly generated situations driving the need for the player to develop novel, emergent strategies on the fly. Developer <a href="/wiki/Jason_Rohrer" title="Jason Rohrer">Jason Rohrer</a> stated that <i>Spelunky</i> "totally revamped my thinking about single-player videogame design".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>The formula and success of both <i>Weird Worlds</i> and <i>Spelunky</i> would influence other developers in creating new roguelike-likes. <a href="/wiki/Edmund_McMillen" title="Edmund McMillen">Edmund McMillen</a>, the developer of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Binding_of_Isaac_(video_game)" title="The Binding of Isaac (video game)">The Binding of Isaac</a></i> (2011), and Kenny and Teddy Lee, the co-developers of <i><a href="/wiki/Rogue_Legacy" title="Rogue Legacy">Rogue Legacy</a></i> (2012), credit Yu's approach with <i>Spelunky</i> as showing how to distill down the nature of a traditional roguelike to apply it to other gaming genres which they had done for their roguelike-likes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespy_roguelikes_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-gamespy_roguelikes-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polygon_spelunky_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-polygon_spelunky-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Justin Ma and Matthew Davis, the co-developers of <i><a href="/wiki/FTL:_Faster_Than_Light" title="FTL: Faster Than Light">FTL: Faster Than Light</a></i> (2012), credited both <i>Weird Worlds: Return to Infinite Space</i> and <i>Spelunky</i> as part of their influence for <i>FTL</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polygon_spelunky_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-polygon_spelunky-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> All of these games earned critical praise, and their success has led to a more modern resurgence in roguelike-likes since their release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_heart_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_heart-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgamer_guide_14-7"><a href="#cite_note-usgamer_guide-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign_rebirth_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-ign_rebirth-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>The newfound success in roguelike-likes is considered part of a larger trend in those that play both board and computer games, looking for "rich play experiences", as described by <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=100_Rogues&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="100 Rogues (page does not exist)">100 Rogues</a></i> developer Keith Burgun, that more popular titles may not always offer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_heart_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_heart-12">[12]</a></sup> David Bamguart of Gaslight Games stated that there is a thrill of the risk inherent in roguelike-likes with random generation and permadeath, helping the player become more invested in the fate of their player character: "The deadly precariousness inherent to the unknown environments of roguelikes gives that investment a great deal of meaning."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gi.biz_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-gi.biz-80">[80]</a></sup> Additionally, many of these newer roguelike-likes strive to address the apparent high difficulty and ruthlessness that traditional roguelikes were known for, and newer players will be able to find more help through user-generated game guides and walkthroughs made possible through wide Internet accessibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gi.biz_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-gi.biz-80">[80]</a></sup> Fabien Fischer offers that players have taken to independently-developed roguelike-likes as they have tired from "superficial gameplay, whitewashing spectacle, the content craze, and <a href="/wiki/Operant_conditioning_chamber" title="Operant conditioning chamber">Skinner Box</a> design" in titles produced by <a href="/wiki/AAA_(video_game_industry)" title="AAA (video game industry)">AAA</a> developers and publishers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>Particularly for smaller indie developers, the nature of the procedural-generated world allows teams to deliver many hours worth of game content without having to spend resources and development time on fleshing out detailed worlds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_heart_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_heart-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign_rebirth_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-ign_rebirth-79">[79]</a></sup> This also allows developers to devote more time in building out complex gameplay features and their interacting systems that are part of the enjoyment of roguelike and roguelike-like games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gi.biz_80-2"><a href="#cite_note-gi.biz-80">[80]</a></sup> McMillen of <i>The Binding of Isaac</i> stated that incorporating roguelike elements into other game genres can be difficult due to overcoming the complex interfaces roguelikes tend to have, but once a means to do so is found, "it becomes an increasingly beautiful, deep, and everlasting design that allows you to generate a seemingly dynamic experience for players, so that each time they play your game they're getting a totally new adventure".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Community">Community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roguelike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="Seven_Day_Roguelike_Challenge"></span> The roguelike genre has developed with the expansion of both classical roguelikes and modern roguelike-like titles, a dedicated fan community has come about to not only discuss games within it but to craft their own tales of near-death adventures or amusing stories in roguelikes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_future_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_future-83">[83]</a></sup> Within this community, there is strong interest in developing roguelikes. The 7 Day Roguelike challenge (7DRL) was born out of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USENET" title="USENET">USENET</a> newsgroup in 2005 for roguelike developers, informally challenging them to create the core of a novel roguelike within 7 days to be submitted for judging and play by the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorker_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker-1">[1]</a></sup> The competition has continued annually each year, since growing from 5–6 entries in 2005 to over 130 in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> In the spirit of the 2008 International Roguelike Conference, the "Roguelike Celebration" was held in September 2016 in San Francisco where several past and present roguelike developers gathered to discuss the history and future direction of the genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_future_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_future-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
