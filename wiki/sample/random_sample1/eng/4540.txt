<p><b>Ronald Myles Dworkin</b>, <a href="/wiki/British_Academy" title="British Academy">FBA</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/ɔːr/ 'or' in 'abhor'">ɔːr</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>; December 11, 1931 – February 14, 2013) was an American<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosopher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jurist" title="Jurist">jurist</a>, and scholar of <a href="/wiki/United_States_constitutional_law" title="United States constitutional law">United States constitutional law</a>. At the time of his death, he was Frank Henry Sommer Professor of Law and Philosophy at <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a> and Professor of Jurisprudence at <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a>, and had taught previously at <a href="/wiki/Yale_Law_School" title="Yale Law School">Yale Law School</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a>, where he was the Professor of Jurisprudence, successor to <a href="/wiki/H._L._A._Hart" title="H. L. A. Hart">Herbert Lionel Hart</a>. An influential contributor to both <a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence" title="Jurisprudence">philosophy of law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a>, Dworkin received the 2007 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holberg_International_Memorial_Prize" title="Holberg International Memorial Prize">Holberg International Memorial Prize</a> in the Humanities for "his pioneering scholarly work" of "worldwide impact."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> According to a survey in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Journal_of_Legal_Studies" title="The Journal of Legal Studies">The Journal of Legal Studies</a></i>, Dworkin was the second most-cited American legal scholar of the twentieth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> After his death, the Harvard legal scholar <a href="/wiki/Cass_Sunstein" title="Cass Sunstein">Cass Sunstein</a> said Dworkin was "one of the most important legal philosophers of the last 100 years. He may well head the list."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>His theory of <a href="/wiki/Law_as_integrity" title="Law as integrity">law as integrity</a> as presented in his book titled <i><a href="/wiki/Law%27s_Empire" title="Law's Empire">Law's Empire</a></i>, in which <a href="/wiki/Judge" title="Judge">judges</a> interpret the law in terms of consistent moral principles, especially justice and fairness, is among the most influential contemporary theories about the nature of law. Dworkin advocated a "moral reading" of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and an <a href="/wiki/Interpretivism_(legal)" title="Interpretivism (legal)">interpretivist</a> approach to law and morality. He was a frequent commentator on contemporary political and legal issues, particularly those concerning the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court of the United States</a>, often in the pages of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Review_of_Books" title="The New York Review of Books">The New York Review of Books</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_Dworkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ronald Dworkin was born in 1931 in <a href="/wiki/Providence,_Rhode_Island" title="Providence, Rhode Island">Providence, Rhode Island</a>, United States, the son of Madeline (Talamo) and David Dworkin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> His family was Jewish. He studied at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> and at <a href="/wiki/Magdalen_College,_Oxford" title="Magdalen College, Oxford">Magdalen College, Oxford</a>, where he was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhodes_Scholar" title="Rhodes Scholar">Rhodes Scholar</a> and a student of <a href="/wiki/Rupert_Cross" title="Rupert Cross">Sir Rupert Cross</a>. After he completed his final year's exams at Oxford, the examiners were so impressed with his script that the Professor of Jurisprudence (then <a href="/wiki/H._L._A._Hart" title="H. L. A. Hart">H. L. A. Hart</a>) was summoned to read it. Dworkin then attended <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_School" title="Harvard Law School">Harvard Law School</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liptak_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liptak-7">[7]</a></sup> and subsequently <a href="/wiki/Law_clerk" title="Law clerk">clerked</a> for Judge <a href="/wiki/Learned_Hand" title="Learned Hand">Learned Hand</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Second_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit</a>. Judge Hand would later call Dworkin "the law clerk to beat all law clerks"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liptak_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Liptak-7">[7]</a></sup>—and Dworkin would recall Judge Hand as an enormously influential mentor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_Dworkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After clerking for Judge Learned Hand, Dworkin was offered the opportunity to clerk for Justice <a href="/wiki/Felix_Frankfurter" title="Felix Frankfurter">Felix Frankfurter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liptak_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Liptak-7">[7]</a></sup> He turned down the offer and joined <a href="/wiki/Sullivan_%26_Cromwell" title="Sullivan &amp; Cromwell">Sullivan &amp; Cromwell</a>, a prominent law firm in New York City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liptak_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Liptak-7">[7]</a></sup> After working at the firm, Dworkin became a Professor of Law at <a href="/wiki/Yale_Law_School" title="Yale Law School">Yale Law School</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liptak_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Liptak-7">[7]</a></sup> where he became the holder of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wesley_N._Hohfeld" title="Wesley N. Hohfeld">Wesley N. Hohfeld</a> Chair of Jurisprudence.</p>
<p>In 1969, Dworkin was appointed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professor_of_Jurisprudence_(University_of_Oxford)" title="Professor of Jurisprudence (University of Oxford)">Chair of Jurisprudence at Oxford</a>, a position in which he succeeded H. L. A. Hart and was elected Fellow of <a href="/wiki/University_College,_Oxford" title="University College, Oxford">University College, Oxford</a>. After retiring from Oxford, Dworkin became the <a href="/wiki/Quain_Professor" title="Quain Professor">Quain Professor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence" title="Jurisprudence">Jurisprudence</a> at <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a>, where he subsequently became the Bentham Professor of Jurisprudence. He was Frank Henry Sommer Professor of Law at <a href="/wiki/New_York_University_School_of_Law" title="New York University School of Law">New York University School of Law</a> and professor of Philosophy at <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a> (NYU),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> where he taught since the late 1970s. He co-taught a colloquium in legal, political, and social philosophy with <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Nagel" title="Thomas Nagel">Thomas Nagel</a>. Dworkin had regularly contributed, for several decades, to <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Review_of_Books" title="The New York Review of Books">The New York Review of Books</a></i>. He delivered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oliver_Wendell_Holmes,_Jr." title="Oliver Wendell Holmes, Jr.">Oliver Wendell Holmes</a> Lecture at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard</a>, the Storrs Lectures at <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale</a>, the Tanner Lectures on Human Values at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford</a>, and the Scribner Lectures at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton</a>. In June 2011, he joined the professoriate of <a href="/wiki/New_College_of_the_Humanities" title="New College of the Humanities">New College of the Humanities</a>, a private college in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jurisprudence_and_philosophy">Jurisprudence and philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_Dworkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Jurisprudence and philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Law_as_rule_and_principle">Law as rule and principle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_Dworkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Law as rule and principle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dworkin as a critic of HLA Hart's <a href="/wiki/Legal_positivism" title="Legal positivism">legal positivism</a> has been summarized by the <i>Stanford Encyclopedia</i> which has stated that:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Dworkin, as positivism's most significant critic, rejects the positivist theory on every conceivable level. Dworkin denies that there can be any general theory of the existence and content of law; he denies that local theories of particular legal systems can identify law without recourse to its moral merits, and he rejects the whole institutional focus of positivism. A theory of law is for Dworkin a theory of how cases ought to be decided and it begins, not with an account of the political organization of a legal system, but with an abstract ideal regulating the conditions under which governments may use coercive force over their subjects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RonaldDworkin.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="505" data-file-width="392" height="283" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/RonaldDworkin.jpg/220px-RonaldDworkin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/RonaldDworkin.jpg/330px-RonaldDworkin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/43/RonaldDworkin.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RonaldDworkin.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ronald Dworkin in 2008</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Dworkin is most famous for his critique of <a href="/wiki/H._L._A._Hart" title="H. L. A. Hart">Hart's</a> <a href="/wiki/Legal_positivism" title="Legal positivism">legal positivism</a>; he sets forth the fullest statement of his critique in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Law%27s_Empire" title="Law's Empire">Law's Empire</a></i>. Dworkin's theory is '<a href="/wiki/Interpretivism_(legal)" title="Interpretivism (legal)">interpretive</a>': the law is whatever follows from a constructive interpretation of the institutional history of the legal system.</p>
<p>Dworkin argues that moral principles that people hold dear are often wrong, even to the extent that certain crimes are acceptable if one's principles are skewed enough. To discover and apply these principles, courts interpret the legal data (legislation, cases etc.) with a view to articulating an interpretation that best explains and justifies past legal practice. All interpretation must follow, Dworkin argues, from the notion of "<a href="/wiki/Law_as_integrity" title="Law as integrity">law as integrity</a>" to make sense.</p>
<p>Out of the idea that law is 'interpretive' in this way, Dworkin argues that in every situation where people's legal rights are controversial, the best interpretation involves the right answer thesis, the thesis that there exists a right answer as a matter of law that the judge must discover. Dworkin opposes the notion that judges have a discretion in such difficult cases.</p>
<p>Dworkin's model of legal principles is also connected with Hart's notion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rule_of_Recognition" title="Rule of Recognition">Rule of Recognition</a>. Dworkin rejects Hart's conception of a master rule in every legal system that identifies valid laws, on the basis that this would <a href="/wiki/Logical_consequence" title="Logical consequence">entail</a> that the process of identifying law must be uncontroversial, whereas (Dworkin argues) people have legal rights even in cases where the correct legal outcome is open to reasonable dispute.</p>
<p>Dworkin moves away from <a href="/wiki/Legal_positivism" title="Legal positivism">positivism's</a> separation of law and morality, since constructive interpretation implicates moral judgments in every decision about what the law is.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_right_answer_thesis">The right answer thesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_Dworkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The right answer thesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Dworkin's own words, his "right answer thesis" may be interpreted through the following words:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Suppose the legislature has passed a statute stipulating that "sacrilegious contracts shall henceforth be invalid." The community is divided as to whether a contract signed on Sunday is, for that reason alone, sacrilegious. It is known that very few of the legislators had that question in mind when they voted, and that they are now equally divided on the question of whether it should be so interpreted. Tom and Tim have signed a contract on Sunday, and Tom now sues Tim to enforce the terms of the contract, whose validity Tim contests. Shall we say that the judge must look for the right answer to the question of whether Tom's contract is valid, even though the community is deeply divided about what the right answer is? Or is it more realistic to say that there simply is no right answer to the question?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>One of Dworkin's most interesting and controversial theses states that the law as properly interpreted will give an answer. This is not to say that everyone will have the same answer (a consensus of what is "right"), or if it did, the answer would not be justified exactly in the same way for every person; rather it means that there will be a necessary answer for each individual if he applies himself correctly to the legal question. For the correct method is that encapsulated by the metaphor of Judge Hercules, an ideal judge, immensely wise and with full knowledge of legal sources. Hercules (the name comes from a classical mythological <a href="/wiki/Hercules" title="Hercules">hero</a>) would also have plenty of time to decide. Acting on the premise that the law is a seamless web, Hercules is required to construct the theory that best fits and justifies the law as a whole (<a href="/wiki/Law_as_integrity" title="Law as integrity">law as integrity</a>) in order to decide any particular case. Hercules, Dworkin argues, would always come to the one right answer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Dworkin does not deny that competent lawyers often disagree on what is the solution to a given case. On the contrary, he claims that they are disagreeing about <i>the</i> right answer to the case, the answer Hercules would give.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Dworkin's critics argue not only that <i>law proper</i> (that is, the legal sources in a positivist sense) is full of gaps and inconsistencies, but also that other legal standards (including principles) may be insufficient to solve a hard case. Some of them are <a href="/wiki/Commensurability_(philosophy_of_science)" title="Commensurability (philosophy of science)">incommensurable</a>. In any of these situations, even Hercules would be in a <a href="/wiki/Dilemma" title="Dilemma">dilemma</a> and none of the possible answers would be the <i>right</i> one.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discussion_of_the_right_answer_thesis">Discussion of the right answer thesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_Dworkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Discussion of the right answer thesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dworkin's metaphor of judge Hercules bears some resemblance to <a href="/wiki/John_Rawls" title="John Rawls">Rawls</a>' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veil_of_ignorance_(philosophy)" title="Veil of ignorance (philosophy)">veil of ignorance</a> and <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Habermas" title="Jürgen Habermas">Habermas</a>' ideal speech situation, in that they all suggest idealized methods of arriving at somehow valid normative propositions. The key difference with respect to the former is that Rawls' veil of ignorance translates almost seamlessly from the purely ideal to the practical. In relation to <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democratic</a> society, for example, it is a way of saying that those in power should treat the <a href="/wiki/Opposition_(politics)" title="Opposition (politics)">political opposition</a> consistently with how they would like to be treated when in opposition, because their present position offers no guarantee as to what their position will be in the political landscape of the future (i.e. they will inevitably form the opposition at some point). Dworkin's Judge Hercules, on the other hand, is a purely idealized construct, that is <i>if</i> such a figure existed, he would arrive at a right answer in every <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moral_dilemma" title="Moral dilemma">moral dilemma</a>. For a critique along these lines see Lorenzo Zucca's <i>Constitutional Dilemmas</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dworkin's right answer thesis turns on the success of his attack on the skeptical argument that right answers in legal-moral dilemmas cannot be determined. Dworkin's anti-skeptical argument is essentially that the properties of the skeptic's claim are analogous to those of substantive moral claims, that is, in asserting that the truth or falsity of "legal-moral" dilemmas cannot be determined, the skeptic makes not a metaphysical claim about the way things are, but a <i>moral</i> claim to the effect that it is, in the face of epistemic uncertainty, unjust to determine legal-moral issues to the detriment of any given individual.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moral_reading_of_the_Constitution">Moral reading of the Constitution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_Dworkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Moral reading of the Constitution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In her recent book on <a href="/wiki/Hans_Kelsen" title="Hans Kelsen">Hans Kelsen</a>, Sandrine Baume<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> identified Ronald Dworkin as a leading defender of the "compatibility of judicial review with the very principles of democracy." Baume identified John Hart Ely alongside Dworkin as the foremost defenders of this principle in recent years, while the opposition to this principle of "compatibility" was identified as Bruce Ackerman and Jeremy Waldron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Dworkin has been a long-time advocate of the principle of the moral reading of the Constitution whose lines of support he sees as strongly associated with enhanced versions of judicial review in the federal government.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory_of_equality">Theory of equality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_Dworkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Theory of equality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dworkin has also made important contributions to what is sometimes called the <i><a href="/wiki/Egalitarianism" title="Egalitarianism">equality of what</a></i> debate. In a famous pair of articles and his book <i>Sovereign Virtue</i> he advocates a theory he calls 'equality of resources'. This theory combines two key ideas. Broadly speaking, the first is that human beings are <a href="/wiki/Moral_responsibility" title="Moral responsibility">responsible</a> for the life choices they make. The second is that natural endowments of intelligence and talent are <a href="/wiki/Moral_luck" title="Moral luck">morally arbitrary</a> and ought not to affect the distribution of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">resources</a> in society. Like the rest of Dworkin's work, his theory of equality is underpinned by the core principle that every person is entitled to <a href="/wiki/Social_equality" title="Social equality">equal</a> concern and respect in the design of the structure of society. Dworkin's theory of equality is said to be one variety of so-called <a href="/wiki/Luck_egalitarianism" title="Luck egalitarianism">luck egalitarianism</a>, but he rejects this statement (<i>Philosophy and Public Affairs</i>, v. 31: 2).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Positive_and_negative_liberty">Positive and negative liberty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_Dworkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Positive and negative liberty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the essay "Do Values Conflict? A Hedgehog's Approach" (<i>Arizona Law Review</i>, Vol 43:2), Dworkin contends that the values of liberty and equality do not necessarily conflict. He criticizes <a href="/wiki/Isaiah_Berlin" title="Isaiah Berlin">Isaiah Berlin</a>'s conception of liberty as "flat" and proposes a new, "dynamic" conception of liberty, suggesting that one cannot say that one's liberty is infringed when one is prevented from committing murder. Thus, liberty cannot be said to have been infringed when no wrong has been done. Put in this way, liberty is only liberty to do whatever we wish so long as we do not infringe upon the rights of others.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life_and_death">Personal life and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_Dworkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Personal life and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While working for Judge Learned Hand, Dworkin met his future wife, Betsy Ross, with whom he would have twins Anthony and Jennifer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodgson_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hodgson-16">[16]</a></sup> Betsy was the daughter of a successful New York businessman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodgson_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hodgson-16">[16]</a></sup> They were married from 1958 until Betsy died of cancer in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodgson_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hodgson-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williamson_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williamson-17">[17]</a></sup> Dworkin later married Irene Brendel, the former wife of pianist <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Brendel" title="Alfred Brendel">Alfred Brendel</a>.</p>
<p>Dworkin died of <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a> in London on February 14, 2013 at the age of 81.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-obit-19">[19]</a></sup> He was survived by his second wife, two children, and two grandchildren.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodgson_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hodgson-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_Dworkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In September 2007, Dworkin was awarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holberg_International_Memorial_Prize" title="Holberg International Memorial Prize">Holberg International Memorial Prize</a>. The award citation of the Holberg Prize Academic Committee recognized that Dworkin has "elaborated a liberal egalitarian theory" and stressed Dworkin's effort to develop "an original and highly influential legal theory grounding law in morality, characterized by a unique ability to tie together abstract philosophical ideas and arguments with concrete everyday concerns in law, morals, and politics".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a> <a href="/wiki/NYU_Annual_Survey_of_American_Law" title="NYU Annual Survey of American Law">Annual Survey of American Law</a> honored Dworkin with its 2006 dedication.</p>
<p>In 2006, the Legal Research Institute of the National Autonomous University of Mexico honored Dworkin with the International Prize of legal Research "Dr. Héctor Fix-Zamudio".</p>
<p>In June 2000, he was awarded an honorary doctorate by the University of Pennsylvania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In June 2009, he was awarded an honorary doctorate of law by Harvard University.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In August 2011, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Buenos_Aires" title="University of Buenos Aires">University of Buenos Aires</a> awarded Dworkin an honorary doctorate. The resolution noted that he "has tirelessly defended the rule of law, democracy and human rights." These were among a number of honorary doctorates conferred upon him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-otago.ac.nz_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-otago.ac.nz-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>On November 14, 2012, he received the <a href="/wiki/Balzan_Prize" title="Balzan Prize">Balzan Prize</a> for Jurisprudence in Quirinale Palace, Rome, from the President of the Italian Republic. The Balzan Prize was awarded "for his fundamental contributions to Jurisprudence, characterized by outstanding originality and clarity of thought in a continuing and fruitful interaction with ethical and political theories and with legal practices".</p>
<p>He was an honorary <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Counsel" title="Queen's Counsel">Queen's Counsel</a> (QC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-otago.ac.nz_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-otago.ac.nz-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
