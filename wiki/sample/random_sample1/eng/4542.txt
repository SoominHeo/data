<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lone Scherfig</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Lone Scherfig</b> (born 2 May 1959) is a <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">director</a>, writer and producer who has been involved with the <a href="/wiki/Dogme_95" title="Dogme 95">Dogme 95</a> film movement and who has been widely critically acclaimed for several of her movies, including the Oscar-nominated film <i><a href="/wiki/An_Education" title="An Education">An Education</a></i> (2009). Scherfig's movies are generally <a href="/wiki/Romantic_comedy_film" title="Romantic comedy film">romantic comedies</a>, including her film <i><a href="/wiki/One_Day_(2011_film)" title="One Day (2011 film)">One Day</a></i> (2011), based on the <a href="/wiki/David_Nicholls_(writer)" title="David Nicholls (writer)">David Nicholls</a> novel. Through both experimenting with creative constraints and her astute attention to detail, she has come to be recognized as a blossoming talent in the film industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suntimes1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-suntimes1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-google1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lone_Scherfig&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s_-_1990s:_Early_beginnings">1980s - 1990s: Early beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lone_Scherfig&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1980s - 1990s: Early beginnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scherfig graduated from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Danish_Film_School&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Danish Film School (page does not exist)">Danish Film School</a> in 1984, and began her career as a director with the television film <i>Margrethes elsker</i> in 1985. Previously, she had been involved in the advertising business and had won awards (including the Lion d'Argent) at the Cannes International Advertising Film Festival. Her directorial debut in film came with <i>Kaj's fodselsdag</i>. The film was critically successful and garnered her the Grand Jury prize and the Club Espace Award at the <a href="/wiki/Rouen_Nordic_Film_Festival" title="Rouen Nordic Film Festival">Rouen Nordic FIlm Festival</a>. For a period of time following such success, Scherfig wrote and directed a few short films and worked with both radio shows and the stage. In 1998, she directed the film <i>Nar mor kommer hjem</i>, which was the recipient of the Grand Prix at the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_World_Film_Festival" title="Montreal World Film Festival">Montreal Film Festival</a> and the Cinekid Award in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s:_International_breakthrough.2C_Italian_for_Beginners">2000s: International breakthrough, <i>Italian for Beginners</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lone_Scherfig&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2000s: International breakthrough, Italian for Beginners">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scherfig found her international breakthrough with the film <i><a href="/wiki/Italian_for_Beginners" title="Italian for Beginners">Italian for Beginners</a></i> (2000), which was critically acclaimed and won several awards, including the Jury Grand Prix <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silver_Berlin_Bear" title="Silver Berlin Bear">Silver Bear</a> award at the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="Berlin International Film Festival">Berlin International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Hailed as a feel-good movie, the film is consistently preoccupied with themes of hope, happiness, and choice. It is credited as the most profitable Scandinavian film to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film, following the creative constraints of the <a href="/wiki/Dogme_95" title="Dogme 95">Dogme 95</a> movement, is set almost entirely on location within a small space, uses sound only found at the source, and it's shot on video. The film involves several characters and their various romantic or otherwise interactions that unfold across this limited setting. As opposed to many other <i><a href="/wiki/Dogme_95" title="Dogme 95">Dogme 95</a></i> films, Scherfig's is rather upbeat and comedic, and has been noted for its rather amusing tone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following <i>Italian for Beginners</i>, Scherfig made the deadpan comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Wilbur_Wants_to_Kill_Himself" title="Wilbur Wants to Kill Himself">Wilbur Wants to Kill Himself</a></i>, released in 2002. This film, not considered a part of the <i>Dogme 95</i> canon, is a touching movie centered on a suicidal man who is constantly saved and cared for by his brother. Noted by critics to be a surprisingly lighthearted affair, the movie was praised for Scherfig's ability to craft deep and interesting characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suntimes1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-suntimes1-1">[1]</a></sup> A.O. Scott mused that the film's tone "ranges from stoic to diffident to quizzical, at least on the surface. But there is an undercurrent of deep and complicated feeling beneath the Scottish reserve; it is signaled by the music, and by Ms. Scherfig's exquisite sense of nuance." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This film found Scherfig working closely with the prolific writer <a href="/wiki/Anders_Thomas_Jensen" title="Anders Thomas Jensen">Anders Thomas Jensen</a> in developing a screenplay. It also allowed her to align her work with the production companies Sigma Films and Zentropa. Although well received, <i>Wilbur</i> was not as commercially successful as <i>Italian for Beginners</i>, although it did act as a catalyst for a <i>Dogme 95</i> related project called the "Advance Party," in which both Scherfig and Jensen helped write characters for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lars_Von_Trier" title="Lars Von Trier">Lars Von Trier</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-google1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>As with <i>Wilbur Wants to Kill Himself</i>, Scherfig wrote and directed another character focused film: <i><a href="/wiki/Just_like_Home_(2007_film)" title="Just like Home (2007 film)">Just Like Home</a></i>, released in 2007. In this comedic endeavor, set in a little town, several characters unite to discover who could be causing commotion in the streets at night. The movie concerns itself primarily with the various citizens of the town and their interactions with each other. In the style of constraints learned by being involved in the <i>Dogme 95</i>, Scherfig claims that the film was written piece by piece every day it was shot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Further proving Scherfig's fascination with lighthearted and sweet affairs, she has claimed the story is "about trust...the fundamental belief that people you hardly know will want the best for you." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The film received very little commercial or critical spotlight, instead acting as a bridge between Scherfig's earlier experiments and her more American successes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s:_Further_success.2C_An_Education">2010s: Further success, <i>An Education</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lone_Scherfig&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2010s: Further success, An Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2009, <i><a href="/wiki/An_Education" title="An Education">An Education</a></i>, Scherfig's most critically lauded film, was released. Written by esteemed fiction author <a href="/wiki/Nick_Hornby" title="Nick Hornby">Nick Hornby</a>, the movie was based on journalist Lynn Barber's experiences as a teenager in post-war Britain. The film's story follows 16-year-old Jenny (played by <a href="/wiki/Carey_Mulligan" title="Carey Mulligan">Carey Mulligan</a>) as she is picked up one rainy night by David (played by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sarsgaard" title="Peter Sarsgaard">Peter Sarsgaard</a>), and brought into the bustling and exciting London society. Centered on this tender and somewhat morally ambiguous romantic relationship between David and Jenny, the movie was hailed as being subtle and deliberate in its pacing and tone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Many reviewers noted the exceptional performance of Carey Mulligan as the protagonist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> She went on to gain a nomination from the Academy Awards, as did the film itself (for Best Picture) and Nick Hornby (for his Adapted Screenplay).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imdb-awards_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-imdb-awards-11">[11]</a></sup> On making the film, Scherfig has talked about her focus on the theme of being an American teenager examined throughout the story, saying, "my guess is about America is that it’s this combination of innocence and freedom that attracts you. Here in Denmark, as well, it was more liberated than it is now, and was definitely more innocent and less dangerous. I mean, when I was a teenager, the world was a lot safer than it is now for my daughter as a teenager, which meant that I could have a lot more fun. It wasn’t risky the way it is now." She has also discussed the pleasures of working in a more collaborative spirit for this movie, commenting that the movie was "the same piece that we [were] all working on, and that was really important to me as a director that everyone was making the same film, that everyone contributed to the package and tried to strengthen it and get as many facets as possible but not be over-inventive, [to] just tell the story as well as we possibly could." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Coming off the success of <i>An Education</i>, Scherfig had many opportunities to develop a more expansive American career in filmmaking.</p>
<p>Scherfig's next film titled <i><a href="/wiki/One_Day_(2011_film)" title="One Day (2011 film)">One Day</a></i>, released in 2011, follows the lives of two romantically engaged individuals as they intersect one day each year. Based on a novel and then adapted by <a href="/wiki/David_Nicholls_(writer)" title="David Nicholls (writer)">David Nicholls</a>, the movie marks a more obvious turning point for Scherfig's career in reaching a mildly larger audience than any of her previous films. The plot and story of the movie has been commented on being somewhat simple and predictable, but critics were also aware of Scherfig's ability to give the dialogue and tone of the film a distinct freshness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Leading actors <a href="/wiki/Anne_Hathaway" title="Anne Hathaway">Anne Hathaway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Sturgess" title="Jim Sturgess">Jim Sturgess</a> both lent the film access to a wider audience. Scherfig herself had stated expressly her interest in working with Hathaway on this project. She has also commented on the difficulties of working with someone else's screenplay, saying in an interview that:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<blockquote>
<p>It’s so much easier to work with something you’ve written, because you can cut things or add things on the spot. You can be much more at home and at ease with what you do. You don’t feel unfaithful to the writer because there is no writer. Most of the other films I’ve done I’ve co-written, and I prefer it. But having said that, when you work with someone else’s characters, you get a lot of gifts. You get an entire world, you get to portray people that you couldn’t have made up, and entire worlds that are fascinating because they are not yours. It’s much harder, I really think it’s much harder <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Ultimately, the film was a moderate success commercially, grossing over $56 million worldwide on a $15 million budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It also furthered Scherfig's career and her capacity to reach a broader audience.</p>
<p>Scherfig is currently at work as a consultant writer for the Danish film <i>Alting</i>, and has worked on other projects (such as <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Road_(film)" title="Red Road (film)">Red Road</a></i> and <i>Donkeys</i>) in helping write and develop characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Her most recent work is <i><a href="/wiki/The_Riot_Club" title="The Riot Club">The Riot Club</a></i>, based on the stage play <i><a href="/wiki/Posh_(play)" title="Posh (play)">Posh</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Laura_Wade" title="Laura Wade">Laura Wade</a>, released in 2014. The film follows two first year students amongst the privileged elite of Oxford University, determined to join the infamous Riot Club, where reputations can be made or destroyed over the course of a single evening. Universal Pictures distributed the film in the UK and Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_nominations">Awards and nominations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lone_Scherfig&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Awards and nominations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Kaj's fodselsdag</i> won both the Club Espace Award and the Grand Jury Prize at the Rouen Nordic Film Festival in 1991. <i>Nar mor kommer hjem</i> won the Cinekid Film Award in 1998.</p>
<p>At the Berlin International Film Festival, <i>Italian for Beginners</i> won the FIPRESCI Prize, the Prize of Ecumenical Jury, the Reader Jury of the "Berliner Morgenpost," and the Silver Berlin Bear, and was nominated for the Golden Berlin Bear. It was also nominated for a Bodil (as was <i>Wilbur Wants to Kill Himself</i> and <i>An Education</i>). <i>Italian for Beginners</i> was nominated at the Chicago International Film Festival, the European Film Awards, the Goya Awards, and picked up awards at Bordeaux International Festival of Women in Cinema, the Festróia-Tróia International Film Festival, the Flaiano Film Festival, the Hamptons International Film Festival, the Paris Film Festival, the Robert Festival, the Valladolid Film Festival, and the Warsaw International Film Festival.</p>
<p><i>Wilbur Wants to Kill Himself</i> garnered nominations from the British Independent Film Awards, the Chlotudis Awards, the Robert Festival, the Valladolid Film Festival, and came in second place at the Emden International Film Festival. It took home awards at the Festróia-Tróia International Film Festival, the Hamptons International Film Festival, the Skip City International D-Cinema Festival, the U.S. Comedy Arts Festival, and the Victoria Independent Film &amp; Video Festival in Canada.</p>
<p>Although <i>Just Like Home</i> was shunned by critics, Scherfig's fourth film was offered the most recognition. <i>An Education</i> was nominated at the BAFTA Awards, the British Independent Film Awards, the European Film Awards, the Film Critics Circle of Australia Awards, the Satellite Awards, and the Academy Awards. It won various awards at the Chicago International Film Festival, the Independent Spirit Awards, the Mill Valley Film Festival, the Robert Festival, and the Sundance Film Festival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
