<p><b>Rotherham</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="noexcerpt"><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5a/En-uk-Rotherham.ogg" title="Listen"><img alt="Listen" data-file-height="11" data-file-width="11" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/11px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/17px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/22px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a><sup><span class="IPA" style="color:#00e;font:bold 80% sans-serif;padding:0 .1em"><a href="/wiki/File:En-uk-Rotherham.ogg" title="File:En-uk-Rotherham.ogg">i</a></span></sup></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="/ð/ 'th' in 'thy'">ð</span><span title="/ᵊ/ optional 'ə' in 'jewelry'">ᵊ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a large town in <a href="/wiki/South_Yorkshire" title="South Yorkshire">South Yorkshire</a>, England, which together with its conurbation and outlying settlements to the north, south and south-east forms the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Rotherham" title="Metropolitan Borough of Rotherham">Metropolitan Borough of Rotherham</a>, with a recorded population of 257,280 in the 2011 census. <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">Historically</a> in the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>, its central area is on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/River_Don,_South_Yorkshire" title="River Don, South Yorkshire">River Don</a> below its confluence with the <a href="/wiki/River_Rother,_South_Yorkshire" title="River Rother, South Yorkshire">Rother</a> on the traditional road between <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doncaster" title="Doncaster">Doncaster</a>. Rotherham is today the largest town in <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_urban_area" title="Sheffield urban area">a contiguous area with Sheffield, informally known as the Sheffield Urban Area</a> and is as such an economic centre for many of Sheffield's suburbs — <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_City_Centre" title="Sheffield City Centre">Sheffield City Centre</a> is 5.6 miles (9.0 km) from Rotherham town centre.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotherham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotherham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Old_Rotherham.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="548" data-file-width="734" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Old_Rotherham.jpg/220px-Old_Rotherham.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Old_Rotherham.jpg/330px-Old_Rotherham.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Old_Rotherham.jpg/440px-Old_Rotherham.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Rotherham in the late Medieval period</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman</a> settlements dot the area covered by the district, including a small Roman fort to the south-west in the upper flood meadow of the Don. Rotherham was founded in the very early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. Its name is from Old English <i>hām</i> 'homestead, estate', meaning 'homestead on the Rother'. The river name was carried into Old English from Brittonic branch of Celtic words: <i>ro-</i> 'over, chief' and <i>duβr</i> 'water', thus 'main river'; a similar size namesake is in <a href="/wiki/East_Sussex" title="East Sussex">East Sussex</a>, see <a href="/wiki/Rother" title="Rother">Rother</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It established itself as a <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Saxon</a> market town, on a Roman road near a <a href="/wiki/Ford_(crossing)" title="Ford (crossing)">forded</a> part of the River Don.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the late Saxon period, Rotherham was at the centre of a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_parish" title="Ecclesiastical parish">parish</a> on the Don's banks.</p>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman Conquest</a> an absentee lord held the most inhabited <a href="/wiki/Manor" title="Manor">manor</a>, Nigel Fossard (however today's city proper takes in eight outyling Domesday estates). The Domesday 'Book' or Survey records this lord of the manor with a Norman name took the place of the Saxon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feudal_system" title="Feudal system">lord</a> Hakon holding 20 years before in 1066 and was tenant of an overlord of hundreds of such manors, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_de_Mortain" title="Robert de Mortain">Robert de Mortain</a>, the Conqueror's half-brother. The central assets at the time were medium in rank among manors: eight adult male householders were counted as villagers, three were smallholders and one the priest, three ploughlands were tilled by one lord's plough team and two and a half men's plough teams were active. The manor's other resources were a church, four loosely called 'acres' of meadow, and seven of woodland. Rotherham had a mill valued at an ordinary half of one pound sterling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hey_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hey-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>His successors, the De Vesci family, rarely visited the town and did not build a castle but maintained a Friday <a href="/wiki/Market_(place)" title="Market (place)">market</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Fair" title="Fair">fair</a>. In the mid 13th century, John de Vesci and Ralph de Tili gave all their possessions in Rotherham to <a href="/wiki/Rufford_Abbey" title="Rufford Abbey">Rufford Abbey</a>, a period of growing wealth in the church. The monks collected <a href="/wiki/Tithe" title="Tithe">tithes</a> from the town and gained rights to an extra market day on Monday and to extend the annual fair from two to three days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hey_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hey-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The townsmen of Rotherham formed the "Greaves of Our Lady's Light", an organisation which worked with the town's three <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guilds</a>. It was suppressed in 1547 but revived in 1584 as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feoffees" title="Feoffees">feoffees</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Common_land" title="Common land">common lands</a> of Rotherham, and remains in existence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hey_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hey-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1480s the Rotherham-born <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_York" title="Archbishop of York">Archbishop of York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Rotherham" title="Thomas Rotherham">Thomas Rotherham</a>, instigated the building of a College of Jesus or Jesus College, Rotherham to rival the colleges of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">Oxford</a>. It was the first brick building in what is now South Yorkshire and taught <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Church_music" title="Church music">religious chant and hymns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">writing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hey_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hey-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The College and new parish <a href="/wiki/All_Saints_Church,_Rotherham" title="All Saints Church, Rotherham">church of All Saints</a> made Rotherham an enviable and modern town at the turn of the 16th century. The college was dissolved in 1547 in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Edward_VI_of_England" title="Edward VI of England">Edward VI</a>, its assets stripped for the crown to grant to its supporters. Very little remains of the original building in College Street. Walls of part of the College of Jesus are encased within number 23 and Nos 2, 2A, 4 (later for a time <i>Old College Inn</i>, a beerhouse), 6 and 8 Effingham Street. Its fragments of walls are the earliest surviving brick structure in South Yorkshire and are remains of the key institution to Rotherham's growth into a town of regional significance. Sixty years after the College's dissolution Rotherham was described by a wealthy visitor as falling from a fashionable college town to having admitted gambling and vice. The history of Thomas Rotherham and education in the town are remembered in the name of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Rotherham_College" title="Thomas Rotherham College">Thomas Rotherham College</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_Revolution">Industrial Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotherham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Industrial Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The region had been exploited for <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> since Roman times, but it was <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> that first brought the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> to Rotherham. Exploitation of the coal seams was the driving force behind the improvements to navigation on the River Don, which eventually formed the <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_and_South_Yorkshire_Navigation" title="Sheffield and South Yorkshire Navigation">Sheffield and South Yorkshire Navigation</a> system of navigable inland waterways.</p>
<p>In the early Industrial Revolution major uses of iron demanded good local <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">ore</a> and established processing skills for iron strength, qualities found in Rotherham's smelting plants and foundries. Iron, and later <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, became the principal industry in Rotherham, surviving into the 20th century. The Walker family built an iron and steel empire in the 18th century, their foundries producing high quality <a href="/wiki/Cannon" title="Cannon">cannon</a>, including some for the ship of the line <a href="/wiki/HMS_Victory" title="HMS Victory">HMS <i>Victory</i></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a> <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridges</a>, one of which was commissioned by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Paine" title="Thomas Paine">Thomas Paine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rotherham's cast iron industry expanded rapidly in the early 19th century, the Effingham Ironworks, later Yates, Haywood &amp; Co, opened in 1820. Other major <a href="/wiki/Iron_founder" title="Iron founder">iron founders</a> included William Corbitt and Co; George Wright and Co of Burton Weir; Owen and Co of Wheathill Foundry; Morgan Macauley and Waide of the Baths Foundry; the Masbro’ Stove Grate Co belonging to Messrs. Perrot, W. H. Micklethwait and John and Richard Corker of the Ferham Works.</p>
<p>The Parkgate Ironworks was established in 1823 by Sanderson and Watson, and changed ownership several times. In 1854, Samuel Beal &amp; Co produced wrought iron plates for <a href="/wiki/Isambard_Kingdom_Brunel" title="Isambard Kingdom Brunel">Isambard Kingdom Brunel</a>'s famous steamship the <a href="/wiki/SS_Great_Eastern" title="SS Great Eastern">SS <i>Great Eastern</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1864, the ironworks was taken over by the Parkgate Iron Co. Ltd, becoming the <a href="/wiki/Park_Gate_Iron_and_Steel_Company" title="Park Gate Iron and Steel Company">Park Gate Iron and Steel Company</a> in 1888. The company was purchased by Tube Investments Ltd in 1956 and closed in 1974. <a href="/wiki/Steel,_Peech_and_Tozer" title="Steel, Peech and Tozer">Steel, Peech and Tozer</a>'s massive <a href="/wiki/Templeborough" title="Templeborough">Templeborough</a> steelworks (now the <a href="/wiki/Magna_Science_Adventure_Centre" title="Magna Science Adventure Centre">Magna Science Adventure Centre</a>) was, at its peak, over a mile (1.6 km) long, employing 10,000 workers, and housing six <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc_furnace" title="Electric arc furnace">electric arc furnaces</a> producing 1.8 million tonnes of steel a year. The operation closed down in 1993.</p>
<p>Joseph Foljambe established a factory to produce his Rotherham plough, the first commercially successful iron <a href="/wiki/Plough" title="Plough">plough</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Glassblowing" title="Glassblowing">glass works</a> was set up in Rotherham in 1751, and became Beatson Clark &amp; Co, one of the town's largest manufacturers, exporting glass medicine bottles worldwide. Beatson Clark &amp; Co was a family business until 1961, when it became a <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">public company</a>. The glass works operated on the same site, although the family connection ceased and the company is owned by Newship Ltd, a holding company linked to the industrialist John Watson Newman. It continues to the manufacture <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_container_industry" title="Glass container industry">glass containers</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical" title="Pharmaceutical">pharmaceutical</a>, food and drinks industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beatson_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-beatson-10">[10]</a></sup> In the 19th century, other successful industries included <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a> making and the manufacture of cast iron fireplaces. Precision manufacturing companies in the town include AESSEAL, Newburgh Engineering, Precision Magnetics, Orkot Composites and Darron Oil Tools SBO. Rotherham is the location of the <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Manufacturing_Park" title="Advanced Manufacturing Park">Advanced Manufacturing Park</a> (AMP).</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Mill_(grinding)" title="Mill (grinding)">Milling</a> grain into flour was a traditional industry in Rotherham, formerly in the <a href="/wiki/Millmoor" title="Millmoor">Millmoor</a> area, hence <a href="/wiki/Rotherham_United_F.C." title="Rotherham United F.C.">Rotherham United F.C.</a>'s nickname "The Millers". <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Floods_of_2007">Floods of 2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotherham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Floods of 2007">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ulleyres.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ulleyres.jpg/220px-Ulleyres.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ulleyres.jpg/330px-Ulleyres.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ulleyres.jpg/440px-Ulleyres.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ulleyres.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulley_reservoir" title="Ulley reservoir">Ulley reservoir</a> between Ulley and Aughton is in the south of the district.</div>

<p>Rotherham was affected by <a href="/wiki/2007_United_Kingdom_floods" title="2007 United Kingdom floods">flooding in the summer of 2007</a>, which caused the closure of central roads, schools, transport services and damaged residential and commercial property, including the Parkgate Shopping complex and the <a href="/wiki/Meadowhall_Centre" title="Meadowhall Centre">Meadowhall Centre</a>, which suffered considerable internal water damage. <a href="/wiki/Ulley_Reservoir" title="Ulley Reservoir">Ulley Reservoir</a> caused major concern and forced the evacuation of thousands of homes when its dam showed signs of structural damage, threatening to break and release water into the suburbs of <a href="/wiki/Treeton" title="Treeton">Treeton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brinsworth" title="Brinsworth">Brinsworth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canklow" title="Canklow">Canklow</a> as well as potentially flooding the Junction 33 electrical sub-station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rother_FM" title="Rother FM">Rother FM</a> evacuated its studios, passing its frequency temporarily to neighbouring station <a href="/wiki/Trax_FM" title="Trax FM">Trax FM</a>. A stretch of the <a href="/wiki/M1_motorway" title="M1 motorway">M1 motorway</a> was closed for three days owing to the flood risk in the event of a breach of the reservoir. Fire service and police officers used thirteen high-powered pumps to lower the water level in the reservoir and reduce pressure on the dam wall, which was damaged but held. By summer 2008, the reservoir and surrounding country park reopened.</p>
<p>A new wetland and flood storage area, Centenary Washlands, has since been built by Rotherham Council and the Environment Agency to prevent flooding in the future. Sheffield Wildlife Trust manages the site as a local nature reserve.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Child_sexual_exploitation_scandal">Child sexual exploitation scandal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotherham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Child sexual exploitation scandal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Rotherham_child_sexual_exploitation_scandal" title="Rotherham child sexual exploitation scandal">Rotherham child sexual exploitation scandal</a> and <a href="#Governance">§ Governance</a></div>
<p>Following a 2012 article published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> alleging the cover-up of organised, large-scale sexual abuse of young children by gangs of people of Pakistani origin in Rotherham,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-14">[14]</a></sup> Rotherham Council commissioned Professor <a href="/wiki/Alexis_Jay" title="Alexis Jay">Alexis Jay</a>, a former chief social work adviser to the Scottish government, to lead an independent inquiry about the handling of the cases and a suspected child exploitation network. She issued an <a href="/wiki/Rotherham_child_sexual_exploitation_scandal" title="Rotherham child sexual exploitation scandal">exploitation report</a> stretching beyond police-level investigated cases. Her report of August 2014 revealed an unprecedented scale of reported <a href="/wiki/Child_sexual_abuse" title="Child sexual abuse">child sexual abuse</a> within an urban area of this size over a 16-year period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her report, and a subsequent Best Value/Fit for Purpose report by <a href="/wiki/Louise_Casey" title="Louise Casey">Louise Casey</a>, stated that a majority of the known perpetrators were of <a href="/wiki/British_Pakistanis" title="British Pakistanis">Pakistani</a> heritage, and reported a denial of severity which was to an extent the responsibility of Councillors. The report concluded that at the time of her inspection the Council was not fit for the purpose, and identified some necessary measures for preventing further repetition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caseyandinspectors_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-caseyandinspectors-18">[18]</a></sup> On 4 February 2015, after receiving Casey's report, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Pickles" title="Eric Pickles">Eric Pickles</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Communities_and_Local_Government" title="Secretary of State for Communities and Local Government">Secretary of State for Communities and Local Government</a>, said that commissioners would be appointed to run the council pending new elections, and the council leader and cabinet resigned en masse to allow for a 'fresh start'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/National_Crime_Agency" title="National Crime Agency">National Crime Agency</a> was called in to investigate whether Rotherham councillors were complicit in hiding the depth and scale of the child abuse (the figure of 1,400 children is now said to be conservative) due to a 'fear of losing their jobs and pensions' following a concern that they might be considered 'racist' if they spoke out. Also, according to the new report, the councillors were driven by "misplaced <a href="/wiki/Political_correctness" title="Political correctness">political correctness</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jayne_Senior&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jayne Senior (page does not exist)">Jayne Senior</a>, a former youth town worker,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> was reported to have worked for more than a decade to expose rampant child sexual abuse in Rotherham, but she was met with "indifference and scorn".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Senior was awarded an <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">MBE</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2016_Birthday_Honours" title="2016 Birthday Honours">2016 Birthday Honours</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotherham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_schools_in_Rotherham" title="List of schools in Rotherham">List of schools in Rotherham</a></div>
<p>Rotherham has three further education institutions and colleges. These are <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Rotherham_College" title="Thomas Rotherham College">Thomas Rotherham College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dearne_Valley_College" title="Dearne Valley College">Dearne Valley College</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rotherham_College_of_Arts_and_Technology" title="Rotherham College of Arts and Technology">Rotherham College of Arts and Technology</a>. The Rotherham College of Arts and Technology has a campus in the Rotherham town centre and a second site in <a href="/wiki/Dinnington,_South_Yorkshire" title="Dinnington, South Yorkshire">Dinnington</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_governance">Local governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotherham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Local governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Labour Party, who have controlled the authority since its 1974 incorporation currently hold 74% of local government seats. Rotherham's shadow cabinet local opposition is currently <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Independence_Party" title="United Kingdom Independence Party">UKIP</a> with 20% of the seats, no longer the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> who went from 8% to 4% of seats in 2014, Independents account for 2% of seats and having had elections by thirds every other year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The method of election is changing to <i>whole council elections</i> every four years, from 2016.</p>
<p>In 2013, Professor <a href="/wiki/Alexis_Jay" title="Alexis Jay">Alexis Jay</a> published a report about the <a href="/wiki/Rotherham_child_sexual_exploitation_scandal" title="Rotherham child sexual exploitation scandal">Rotherham child sexual exploitation scandal</a> (1997–2013). Following the report's publication, the council leader, Roger Stone of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a>, resigned - an act of contrition the report said should have been made years earlier<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peachey_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-peachey-28">[28]</a></sup> - saying he would take full responsibility for "the historic failings described so clearly in the report."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC26Aug2014_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC26Aug2014-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnson_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-johnson-30">[30]</a></sup> Labour Councillors Gwendoline Russell, Shaukat Ali and former council leader Roger Stone were suspended from the Labour Party, as was former Deputy Council Leader Jahangir Akhtar, who had lost his council seat in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Chief Executive, Martin Kimber, said no council officers would face disciplinary action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnson_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-johnson-30">[30]</a></sup> Kimber announced on 8 September that he intended to step down in December 2014, and offered his "sincere apology to those who were let down".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The council's director of children's services, Joyce Thacker, also left the authority by mutual agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Malcolm Newsam was appointed as Children's Social Care Commissioner in October 2014,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and subsequently Ian Thomas was appointed as interim director of children's services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gdn171214_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-gdn171214-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Shaun_Wright" title="Shaun Wright">Shaun Wright</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Police_and_Crime_Commissioner" title="Police and Crime Commissioner">Police and Crime Commissioner</a> (PCC) for <a href="/wiki/South_Yorkshire_Police" title="South Yorkshire Police">South Yorkshire</a> from 2012, was the Labour councillor in charge of child safety at the council for five years from 2005-10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> He initially refused demands to resign as PCC from the <a href="/wiki/Home_Secretary" title="Home Secretary">Home Secretary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theresa_May" title="Theresa May">Theresa May</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-statesmanpast_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-statesmanpast-37">[37]</a></sup> as well as members of his own party and local Labour MP <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Champion_(politician)" title="Sarah Champion (politician)">Sarah Champion</a>, saying: "I believe I am the most appropriate person to hold this office at this current time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> He resigned from the Labour Party on 27 August 2014,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcquit_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbcquit-39">[39]</a></sup> after an ultimatum by the party to either resign or face suspension from the party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Wright stood down as PCC on 16 September, saying that the prominence given to his role distracted from "the important issue, which should be everybody's focus - the 1,400 victims outlined in the report - and in providing support to victims and bringing to justice the criminals responsible for the atrocious crimes committed against them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc160914_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc160914-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>The former Chief Constable, <a href="/wiki/Meredydd_Hughes" title="Meredydd Hughes">Meredydd Hughes</a>, who served from 2004 to 2011 and who had unsuccessfully stood for the Labour Party nomination in the Police Crime Commissioner elections, was told by Labour MP <a href="/wiki/Keith_Vaz" title="Keith Vaz">Keith Vaz</a> that he had 'failed' abuse victims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The inspector, Louise Casey aided by seven assistant inspectors produced the Inspection Report on 4 February 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caseyandinspectors_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-caseyandinspectors-18">[18]</a></sup> Following its conclusion that the Council was not fit for purpose the minister directed that the powers of the Council (RMBC) be transferred to his department and the cabinet would need to resign unless RMBC made sufficient representations within 14 days to contradict the report. The Secretary of State empowered a team of five Commissioners to replace councillors before a full election in 2016 and on the Report's strength, stated that as the authority was not currently fit for purpose its powers would not revert until the dis-empowered councillors could prove their fitness to carry out all of the Council's duties without intervention. One of these commissioners was appointed to specialise in child protection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Representation_in_the_national_legislature">Representation in the national legislature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotherham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Representation in the national legislature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like all of South Yorkshire bar <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_Hallam_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Sheffield Hallam (UK Parliament constituency)">Sheffield Hallam</a>, the area consists of representatives of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> at the Parliamentary level whose seats have been almost universally cast among analysts as '<a href="/wiki/Safe_seat" title="Safe seat">safe</a>', that is having enjoyed 'substantial' majorities over a 'long' period of time; a typecast which heightens the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incumbency" title="Incumbency">incumbency factor</a> present in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_past_the_post" title="First past the post">first past the post</a> elections. The town's seat, including all its near suburbs, has been held by Labour MPs since a by-election in 1933. After the resignation and jailing of <a href="/wiki/Denis_MacShane" title="Denis MacShane">Denis MacShane</a> in November 2012 due to expenses abuse, this area required a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotherham_by-election_2012" title="Rotherham by-election 2012">by-election in 2012</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Champion_(politician)" title="Sarah Champion (politician)">Sarah Champion</a> for Labour became the MP at that by-election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotherham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The town in great part occupies the slopes of two hills; that in the west is the start of a 3 miles (4.8 km) north-west crest topped by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keppels_Column" title="Keppels Column">Keppels Column</a>, that in the east is a narrower crest alongside the Rother known as Canklow Hill, topped by a <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">protected</a> formally laid out public area, Boston Park, less than 500 east of and 80 metres above the Rother. The Rother here is between 32 and 34 metres above sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The south scarp here is slightly higher still, the Canklow Hill Earthworks, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scheduled_Ancient_Monument" title="Scheduled Ancient Monument">Scheduled Ancient Monument</a>, one of relatively few in the borough, as pre-dating recorded history. This compares to 524 listed buildings and structures by English Heritage in the district. <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Lewis_(publisher)" title="Samuel Lewis (publisher)">Samuel Lewis</a> describing the town with its townships as having nearly 14,000 people in 1848, described the southern green slope by saying the town lies "partly on the acclivities of an eminence...great improvements have been made within the last twenty years; in the immediate neighbourhood, several substantial and respectable dwellings have been recently built".</p>
<p>Rotherham's commercial town centre occupies the valley in between these hills on the navigable part of the <a href="/wiki/River_Don,_South_Yorkshire" title="River Don, South Yorkshire">River Don</a> flowing from the south-west after it has turned approximately due north. The town centre is less than 0.5 miles (0.80 km) below and north of the confluence of the <a href="/wiki/River_Rother,_South_Yorkshire" title="River Rother, South Yorkshire">Rother</a> flowing from the south. The Mid Don Valley continues adjoining towns in the north of the Metropolitan Borough.</p>
<p>Beyond the town centre and away from the Don Valley, the Rotherham district is largely rural, containing a mixture of retired people, larger properties, some farming and tourism and the landscaped <a href="/wiki/Wentworth_Woodhouse" title="Wentworth Woodhouse">Wentworth Woodhouse</a> estate, where the last surviving <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">kiln</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Rockingham_Pottery" title="Rockingham Pottery">Rockingham Pottery</a> can be seen.</p>
<p>Aside from two regular roads and two bypasses (one being the motorway network), <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a> is connected directly by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trans-Pennine_Trail" title="Trans-Pennine Trail">Trans-Pennine Trail</a> which passes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meadowhall_Shopping_Centre" title="Meadowhall Shopping Centre">Meadowhall Shopping Centre</a> on both sides (which between the two places) as it includes Sheffield as southern detour.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rotherham_Central_railway_station" title="Rotherham Central railway station">Rotherham Central railway station</a> has frequent trains connecting to Sheffield in a time of 14 minutes; <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> through a change in Sheffield is accessible in a similar circa 70 minutes to nearer <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> and <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> as many towns and suburbs in South Yorkshire and West Yorkshire are all stops on Rotherham's railway – it is <a href="/wiki/Doncaster" title="Doncaster">Doncaster</a> which has the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_Main_Line" title="East Coast Main Line">East Coast Main Line</a> providing express intercity services.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demography">Demography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotherham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Demography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2011, Rotherham had a population of 109,691, this figure is for an urban subdivision and roughly corresponds with ward and output area boundaries. The population in 2001 was 117,262 but the figure includes <a href="/wiki/Catcliffe" title="Catcliffe">Catcliffe</a> which was a separate subdivision a decade later, so there may not have been an actual decrease in population.</p>

<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, 14.4% of Rotherham's population were non-white British compared with 8.1% for the surrounding borough. Rotherham town has over double the percentage of <a href="/wiki/Asian_people" title="Asian people">Asian people</a> compared with the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Rotherham" title="Metropolitan Borough of Rotherham">Metropolitan Borough of Rotherham</a> and a slightly larger percentage of black people. Rotherham is about as ethnically diverse as <a href="/wiki/Middlesbrough" title="Middlesbrough">Middlesbrough</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doncaster" title="Doncaster">Doncaster</a> and in 2001, 93.0% of Rotherham's residents were <a href="/wiki/White_British" title="White British">White British</a>, so the town's foreign population has increased considerably since then.</p>
<p>The population of Rotherham is increasing slightly because 110,550 people lived in the town in 2014. This means an extra 859 people moved into Rotherham in just over 3 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landmarks">Landmarks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotherham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Landmarks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rotherham_Minster.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Rotherham_Minster.JPG/220px-Rotherham_Minster.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Rotherham_Minster.JPG/330px-Rotherham_Minster.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Rotherham_Minster.JPG/440px-Rotherham_Minster.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rotherham_Minster.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rotherham Minster</div>

<p>Rotherham Minster or All Saints' Church in All Saints Square built largely of <a href="/wiki/Ashlar" title="Ashlar">neat-cut</a> pieces of sandstone and low-pitch lead roofs dates from the 15th century and includes parts from earlier Saxon and Norman structures. Clayton and Bell working to <a href="/wiki/George_Gilbert_Scott" title="George Gilbert Scott">George Gilbert Scott</a>'s designs constructed the east window. Stained glass makers and designers A. Gibbs, Camm Brothers, Heaton, Butler and Bayne and James Bell are known makers of the other windows. Gargoyles flank its clock on each face. It has a "recessed octagonal spire with crocketed arrises and pinnacled shafts rising from corner faces and a gilded weathervane." Architectural critics <a href="/wiki/Nikolaus_Pevsner" title="Nikolaus Pevsner">Pevsner</a> and Simon Jenkins considered it "the best perpendicular [style] church in the country" and "the best work in the county", respectively. It is a <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a> in the highest category of architecture, Grade I.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Close to the town centre is the 15th-century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chapel_of_Our_Lady_of_Rotherham_Bridge" title="Chapel of Our Lady of Rotherham Bridge">Chapel of Our Lady of Rotherham Bridge</a> (or "Chapel on the Bridge"), beside <a href="/wiki/River_Don_Navigation#Bridge_Street_Bridge_and_Chantry_Bridge" title="River Don Navigation">Chantry Bridge</a> (a road bridge opened in the 1930s). It is one of four surviving bridge chapels in the country. The chapel was restored in 1923, having been used as the town jail and a <a href="/wiki/Tobacconist" title="Tobacconist">tobacconist</a>'s shop.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boston_Castle_-_Rotherham_-_geograph.org.uk_-_191088.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Boston_Castle_-_Rotherham_-_geograph.org.uk_-_191088.jpg/220px-Boston_Castle_-_Rotherham_-_geograph.org.uk_-_191088.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Boston_Castle_-_Rotherham_-_geograph.org.uk_-_191088.jpg/330px-Boston_Castle_-_Rotherham_-_geograph.org.uk_-_191088.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Boston_Castle_-_Rotherham_-_geograph.org.uk_-_191088.jpg/440px-Boston_Castle_-_Rotherham_-_geograph.org.uk_-_191088.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Boston_Castle_-_Rotherham_-_geograph.org.uk_-_191088.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Built in the 18th century, Clifton House houses <a href="/wiki/Clifton_Park_Museum" title="Clifton Park Museum">Clifton Park Museum</a>. The remains of the 16th-century College of Jesus are in the town centre. Boston Castle, in the grounds of Boston Park, was built as a hunting lodge by Thomas, 3rd <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Effingham" title="Earl of Effingham">Earl of Effingham</a> between 1773 and 1774 to mark his opposition to British attempts to crush the Americans in their <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">war for independence</a>. It is named after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a>, the scene of the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Tea_Party" title="Boston Tea Party">Boston Tea Party</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the outskirts of Rotherham, a brick-built glass making furnace, the <a href="/wiki/Catcliffe_Glass_Cone" title="Catcliffe Glass Cone">Catcliffe Glass Cone</a>, is the oldest surviving structure of its type in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a> and one of four remaining in the United Kingdom – the others being the <a href="/wiki/Red_House_Cone" title="Red House Cone">Red House Cone</a> in the Wordsley centre of the Dudley Glassworks in the West Midlands, <a href="/wiki/Lemington_Glass_Works" title="Lemington Glass Works">Lemington Glass Works</a> west of Newcastle upon Tyne and <a href="/wiki/Alloa" title="Alloa">Alloa</a> in Scotland. Threatened with demolition in the 1960s, it has been preserved as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scheduled_Ancient_Monument" title="Scheduled Ancient Monument">Scheduled Ancient Monument</a> and stands as a focal point in a sheltered housing complex and close to the path leading up the Rother valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>South of <a href="/wiki/Maltby,_South_Yorkshire" title="Maltby, South Yorkshire">Maltby</a> in the east of the district, half-way to <a href="/wiki/Worksop" title="Worksop">Worksop</a> are the ruins of <a href="/wiki/Roche_Abbey" title="Roche Abbey">Roche Abbey</a>, among the small minority in the United Kingdom bearing multi-storey walls, as most others are no more than foundations or a single storey of ruins following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_monasteries" title="Dissolution of the monasteries">dissolution of the monasteries</a> in the 1530s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Events">Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotherham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rotherham holds several public event through the year:- A fashion show <b>Rotherham Rocks</b> in July, takes place in 'All Saints Square' and <b>Rotherham by the Sea</b>, in August, is held in the same location, where it is transformed into a seaside beach with sand, deckchairs and other traditional seaside attractions. <b>Rotherham Show</b> is an annual event, held in Clifton Park, with stalls from all sectors of the community, shows and live bands.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Freedom_of_the_borough">Freedom of the borough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotherham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Freedom of the borough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On Monday 3 August 2009 Rotherham became the first town to bestow the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_City" title="Freedom of the City">Freedom of the Borough</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Regiment" title="Yorkshire Regiment">Yorkshire Regiment</a>, giving it the right to march through the town with "flags flying, bands playing and bayonets fixed". At a ceremony outside the Town Hall, the Regiment paraded two Guards of soldiers who had recently returned from <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Colours,_standards_and_guidons" title="Colours, standards and guidons">Colours</a> of the 3rd Battalion Yorkshire Regiment (Duke of Wellington's), led by the Kings Division Band, under the command of Lieutenant Colonel Vallings, the battalion <a href="/wiki/Commanding_officer" title="Commanding officer">commanding officer</a>. The Mayor of Rotherham, Councillor <a href="/wiki/Shaukat_Ali" title="Shaukat Ali">Shaukat Ali</a>, on behalf of the borough, presented the Freedom Scroll to Colonel Simon Newton, who accepted the honour for the regiment. The regiment is the only military unit to become Honorary Freemen of the Borough.</p>
