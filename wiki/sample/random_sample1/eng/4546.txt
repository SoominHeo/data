<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Rodeo</h1>

<p><b>Rodeo</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a competitive sport that arose out of the working practices of cattle herding in Spain, Mexico, and later Central America, the United States, Canada, South America, Australia and New Zealand. It was based on the skills required of the working <a href="/wiki/Vaquero" title="Vaquero">vaqueros</a> and later, <a href="/wiki/Cowboy" title="Cowboy">cowboys</a>, in what today is the western United States, western Canada, and northern Mexico. Today it is a sporting event that involves <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>, designed to test the skill and speed of the cowboys and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cowgirls" title="Cowgirls">cowgirls</a>. American style professional rodeos generally comprise the following events: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tie-down_roping" title="Tie-down roping">tie-down roping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Team_roping" title="Team roping">team roping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steer_wrestling" title="Steer wrestling">steer wrestling</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saddle_bronc_riding" title="Saddle bronc riding">saddle bronc riding</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bareback_bronc_riding" title="Bareback bronc riding">bareback bronc riding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bull_riding" title="Bull riding">bull riding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barrel_racing" title="Barrel racing">barrel racing</a>. The events are divided into two basic categories: the rough stock events and the timed events. Depending on sanctioning organization and region, other events such as <a href="/wiki/Breakaway_roping" title="Breakaway roping">breakaway roping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goat_tying" title="Goat tying">goat tying</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Pole_bending" title="Pole bending">pole bending</a> may also be a part of some rodeos.</p>
<p>American rodeo, particularly popular today within the Canadian province of <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a> and throughout the western United States, is the official state sport of Wyoming, South Dakota, and Texas. The iconic silhouette image of a "Bucking Horse and Rider" is a federal and state-registered <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademark</a> of the State of Wyoming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_of_Alberta" title="Legislative Assembly of Alberta">Legislative Assembly of Alberta</a> has considered making American rodeo the official sport of that province. However, enabling legislation has yet to be passed.</p>
<p>In the United States, professional rodeos are governed and sanctioned by the <a href="/wiki/Professional_Rodeo_Cowboys_Association" title="Professional Rodeo Cowboys Association">Professional Rodeo Cowboys Association</a> (PRCA) and <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Professional_Rodeo_Association" title="Women's Professional Rodeo Association">Women's Professional Rodeo Association</a> (WPRA), while other associations govern children's, high school, collegiate, and senior rodeos. Associations also exist for Native Americans and other minority groups. The traditional season for competitive rodeo runs from spring through fall, while the modern professional rodeo circuit runs longer, and concludes with the PRCA <a href="/wiki/National_Finals_Rodeo" title="National Finals Rodeo">National Finals Rodeo</a> (NFR) in <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Valley" title="Las Vegas Valley">Las Vegas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>, now held in December.</p>
<p>Rodeo has provoked opposition from <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">animal rights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal_welfare" title="Animal welfare">animal welfare</a> advocates, who argue that various competitions constitute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_cruelty" title="Animal cruelty">animal cruelty</a>. The American rodeo industry has made progress in improving the welfare of rodeo animals, with specific requirements for veterinary care and other regulations that protect rodeo animals. However, rodeo is opposed by a number of animal welfare organizations in the United States and Canada. Some local and state governments in North America have banned or restricted rodeos, certain rodeo events, or types of equipment. Internationally, rodeo is banned in the United Kingdom and the Netherlands,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vancouverhumanesociety.bc.ca_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-vancouverhumanesociety.bc.ca-2">[2]</a></sup> with other European nations placing restrictions on certain practices.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The American English word "rodeo" is taken directly from Spanish <i>rodeo</i> (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[roˈðe.o]</a></span>), which roughly translates into English as "round up."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Spanish word is derived from the verb <i>rodear,</i> meaning "to surround" or "go around," used to refer to "a pen for cattle at a fair or market," derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>rota</i> or <i>rotare,</i> meaning to rotate or go around.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Spanish America, the <i>rodeo</i> was the process that was used by vaqueros to <a href="/wiki/Muster_(livestock)" title="Muster (livestock)">gather</a> cattle for various purposes, such as moving them to new pastures, separating the cattle owned by different ranchers, or gathering in preparation for slaughter (<i>matanza</i>). The yearly rodeos for separating the cattle were overseen by the "<a href="/wiki/Judges_of_the_Plains" title="Judges of the Plains">Juez del Campo</a>," who decided all questions of ownership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The term was also used to refer to exhibitions of skills used in the working <i>rodeo</i>. This evolved from these yearly gatherings where festivities were held and horsemen could demonstrate their equestrian skills. It was this latter usage which was adopted into the cowboy tradition of the United States and Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <i>rodeo</i> was first used in English in approximately 1834 to refer to a cattle round-up. Today the word is used primarily to refer to a public exhibition of cowboy skills, usually in the form of a competitive event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_rodeo">History of rodeo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of rodeo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Many rodeo events were based on the tasks required by cattle ranching. The working <a href="/wiki/Cowboy" title="Cowboy">cowboy</a> developed skills to fit the needs of the terrain and climate of the American west, and there were many regional variations. The skills required to manage cattle and horses date back to the Spanish traditions of the <i><a href="/wiki/Cowboy" title="Cowboy">vaquero</a>.</i></p>
<p>Early rodeo-like affairs of the 1820s and 1830s were informal events in the western United States and northern Mexico with cowboys and <i><a href="/wiki/Cowboy" title="Cowboy">vaqueros</a></i> testing their work skills against one another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllen199817_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllen199817-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGroves20063_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGroves20063-10">[10]</a></sup> Following the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, rodeo competitions emerged, with the first held in <a href="/wiki/Cheyenne,_Wyoming" title="Cheyenne, Wyoming">Cheyenne, Wyoming</a> in 1872.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGroves20063_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGroves20063-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Prescott,_Arizona" title="Prescott, Arizona">Prescott, Arizona</a> claimed the distinction of holding the first professional rodeo, as it charged admission and awarded trophies in 1888.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllen199818_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllen199818-11">[11]</a></sup> Between 1890 and 1910, rodeos became public entertainment, sometimes combined <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wild_West_show" title="Wild West show">Wild West shows</a> featuring individuals such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buffalo_Bill_Cody" title="Buffalo Bill Cody">Buffalo Bill Cody</a>, <a href="/wiki/Annie_Oakley" title="Annie Oakley">Annie Oakley</a>, and other charismatic stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGroves20063_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGroves20063-10">[10]</a></sup> By 1910, several major rodeos were established in western North America, including the <a href="/wiki/Calgary_Stampede" title="Calgary Stampede">Calgary Stampede</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pendleton_Round-Up" title="Pendleton Round-Up">Pendleton Round-Up</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Cheyenne_Frontier_Days" title="Cheyenne Frontier Days">Cheyenne Frontier Days</a>.</p>
<p>Rodeo-type events also became popular for a time in the big cities of the Eastern United States, with large venues such as <a href="/wiki/Madison_Square_Garden" title="Madison Square Garden">Madison Square Garden</a> playing a part in popularizing them for new crowds. There was no standardization of events for a rodeo competition until 1929, when associations began forming.</p>
<p>In the 1970s, rodeo saw unprecedented growth. Contestants referred to as "the new breed" brought rodeo increasing media attention. These contestants were young, often from an urban background, and chose rodeo for its athletic rewards. By 1985, one third of PRCA members had a college education and one half of the competitors had never worked on a cattle ranch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllen199832_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllen199832-12">[12]</a></sup> Today, some professional rodeos are staged in large, air-conditioned arenas; offer large purses, and are often telecast. Many other professional rodeos are held outside, under the same conditions of heat, cold, dust or mud as were the original events.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Women">Women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Women_in_rodeo" title="Women in rodeo">Women in rodeo</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FannieSperrySteele.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="183" data-file-width="300" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/FannieSperrySteele.jpg/220px-FannieSperrySteele.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/da/FannieSperrySteele.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FannieSperrySteele.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Fannie_Sperry_Steele" title="Fannie Sperry Steele">Fannie Sperry Steele</a>, Champion Lady Bucking Horse Rider, Winnipeg Stampede, 1913</div>

<p>Historically, women have long participated in rodeo. "Prairie Rose" Henderson debuted at the Cheyenne rodeo in 1901, and, by 1920, women were competing in rough stock events, relay races and trick riding. But after Bonnie McCarrol died in the Pendleton Round-Up in 1929 and Marie Gibson died in a horse wreck in 1933, women's competitive participation was curbed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Rodeo women organized into various associations and staged their own rodeos. Today, women's barrel racing is included as a competitive event in professional rodeo, with breakaway roping and goat tying added at collegiate and lower levels. They compete equally with men in team roping, sometimes in mixed-sex teams. Women also compete in traditional roping and rough stock events at women-only rodeos.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competitive_events">Competitive events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Competitive events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Barrel-Racing-Szmurlo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Professional rodeos in the United States and Canada usually incorporate both timed events and "rough stock" events, most commonly <a href="/wiki/Calf_roping" title="Calf roping">calf roping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Team_roping" title="Team roping">team roping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steer_wrestling" title="Steer wrestling">steer wrestling</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saddle_bronc_and_bareback_riding" title="Saddle bronc and bareback riding">saddle bronc and bareback bronc riding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bull_riding" title="Bull riding">bull riding</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Barrel_racing" title="Barrel racing">barrel racing</a>. Additional events may be included at the collegiate and high school level, including <a href="/wiki/Breakaway_roping" title="Breakaway roping">breakaway roping</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goat_tying" title="Goat tying">goat tying</a>. Some events are based on traditional ranch practices; others are modern developments and have no counterpart in ranch practice.</p>
<p>Rodeos may also offer western-themed entertainment at intermission, including music and novelty acts, such as trick riding.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Roping">Roping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Roping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Roping competitions are based on the tasks of a working cowboy, who often had to capture calves and adult cattle for branding, medical treatment and other purposes. The cowboy must throw a type of rope with a loop, known as a <a href="/wiki/Lasso" title="Lasso">lariat</a>, <i>riata</i> or <i>reata</i>, or lasso, over the head of a calf or onto the horns and around the hind legs of adult cattle, and secure the animal in a fashion dictated by its size and age.</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calf_Roping" title="Calf Roping">Calf Roping</a>, also called Tie-down roping, is based on ranch work in which calves are roped for branding, medical treatment, or other purposes. It is the oldest of rodeo's timed events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECurnutt2001268.E2.80.93269_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECurnutt2001268.E2.80.93269-14">[14]</a></sup> The cowboy ropes a running calf around the neck with a lariat, and his horse stops and sets back on the rope while the cowboy dismounts, runs to the calf, throws it to the ground and ties three feet together. (If the calf falls when roped, the cowboy must lose time waiting for the calf to get back to its feet so that the cowboy can do the work.) The job of the horse is to hold the calf steady on the rope. A well-trained calf-roping horse will slowly back up while the cowboy ties the calf, to help keep the lariat snug.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Breakaway_roping" title="Breakaway roping">Breakaway roping</a> - a form of calf roping where a very short lariat is used, tied lightly to the saddle horn with string and a flag. When the calf is roped about the neck, the horse stops, the flagged rope breaks free of the saddle, and the calf runs on without being thrown or tied. In most of the United States, this event is primarily for women of all ages and boys under 12. In places where traditional "tie-down" calf roping is not allowed, riders of both genders compete.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Team_roping" title="Team roping">Team roping</a>, also called "heading and heeling," is the only rodeo event where men and women riders compete together. Two people capture and restrain a full-grown steer. One horse and rider, the "header," lassos a running steer's horns, while the other horse and rider, the "heeler," lassos the steer's two hind legs. Once the animal is captured, the riders face each other and lightly pull the steer between them, so that it loses its balance and lies down. This technique originated from methods of capture and restraint for treatment used on a ranch.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_timed_events">Other timed events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other timed events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Barrel_racing" title="Barrel racing">Barrel racing</a> - is a timed speed and agility event. In barrel racing, horse and rider gallop around a cloverleaf pattern of barrels, making agile turns without knocking the barrels over.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBroyles20064_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBroyles20064-15">[15]</a></sup> In professional, collegiate and high school rodeo, barrel racing is an exclusively women's sport, though men and boys occasionally compete at local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gymkhana_(Equestrian)" title="Gymkhana (Equestrian)">O-Mok-See</a> competition.</li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rodeo3b2004-12-21.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1362" data-file-width="1193" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Rodeo3b2004-12-21.jpg/170px-Rodeo3b2004-12-21.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Rodeo3b2004-12-21.jpg/255px-Rodeo3b2004-12-21.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Rodeo3b2004-12-21.jpg/340px-Rodeo3b2004-12-21.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rodeo3b2004-12-21.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Steer_wrestling" title="Steer wrestling">Steer wrestling</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Steer_wrestling" title="Steer wrestling">Steer wrestling</a> - Also known as "Bulldogging," is a rodeo event where the rider jumps off his horse onto a <a href="/wiki/Corriente_cattle" title="Corriente cattle">Corriente</a> <a href="/wiki/Cattle#Terminology" title="Cattle">steer</a> and 'wrestles' it to the ground by grabbing it by the horns. This is probably the single most physically dangerous event in rodeo for the cowboy, who runs a high risk of jumping off a running horse head first and missing the steer, or of having the thrown steer land on top of him, sometimes horns first.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Goat_tying" title="Goat tying">Goat tying</a> is usually an event for women or pre-teen girls and boys; a goat is staked out while a mounted rider runs to the goat, dismounts, grabs the goat, throws it to the ground and ties it in the same manner as a calf. The horse must not come into contact with the goat or its tether. This event was designed to teach smaller or younger riders the basics of calf roping without requiring the more complex skill of roping the animal. This event is not part of professional rodeo competition.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Rough_stock.22_competition">"Rough stock" competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title='Edit section: "Rough stock" competition'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2006-07-28_-_United_States_-_Wyoming_-_Cody_-_Rodeo_-_Cowboy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1691" data-file-width="2539" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/2006-07-28_-_United_States_-_Wyoming_-_Cody_-_Rodeo_-_Cowboy.jpg/220px-2006-07-28_-_United_States_-_Wyoming_-_Cody_-_Rodeo_-_Cowboy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/2006-07-28_-_United_States_-_Wyoming_-_Cody_-_Rodeo_-_Cowboy.jpg/330px-2006-07-28_-_United_States_-_Wyoming_-_Cody_-_Rodeo_-_Cowboy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/2006-07-28_-_United_States_-_Wyoming_-_Cody_-_Rodeo_-_Cowboy.jpg/440px-2006-07-28_-_United_States_-_Wyoming_-_Cody_-_Rodeo_-_Cowboy.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2006-07-28_-_United_States_-_Wyoming_-_Cody_-_Rodeo_-_Cowboy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saddle_bronc" title="Saddle bronc">Saddle bronc</a> riding; in rough stock events, the animal usually "wins."</div>

<p>In spite of popular myth, most modern "broncs" are not in fact wild horses, but are more commonly spoiled riding horses or horses bred specifically as bucking stock. Rough stock events also use at least two well-trained riding horses ridden by "pick up men" (or women), tasked with assisting fallen riders and helping successful riders get safely off the bucking animal.</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saddle_bronc_and_bareback_riding" title="Saddle bronc and bareback riding">Bronc riding</a> - there are two divisions in rodeo, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bareback_bronc" title="Bareback bronc">bareback bronc</a> riding, where the rider is only allowed to hang onto a bucking horse with a type of <a href="/wiki/Surcingle" title="Surcingle">surcingle</a> called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodeo_bareback_rigging" title="Rodeo bareback rigging">"rigging"</a>; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saddle_bronc" title="Saddle bronc">saddle bronc</a> riding, where the rider uses a specialized western saddle without a horn (for safety) and hangs onto a heavy lead rope, called a bronc rein, which is attached to a halter on the horse.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bull_riding" title="Bull riding">Bull riding</a> - an event where the cowboys ride full-grown bulls instead of horses. Although skills and equipment similar to those needed for bareback bronc riding are required, the event differs considerably from horse riding competition due to the danger involved. Because bulls are unpredictable and may attack a fallen rider, <a href="/wiki/Rodeo_clown" title="Rodeo clown">rodeo clowns</a>, now known as "bullfighters", work during bull-riding competition to distract the bulls and help prevent injury to competitors.</li>
</ul>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Less_common_events">Less common events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Less common events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Several other events may be scheduled on a rodeo program depending upon the rodeo's governing association.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Steer_roping" title="Steer roping">Steer roping</a> —Not listed as an official PRCA event,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Events_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Events-16">[16]</a></sup> and banned in several states, but quietly recognized by the PRCA in some areas. It is rarely seen in the United States today because of the tremendous risk of injury to all involved, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_cruelty" title="Animal cruelty">animal cruelty</a> concerns. A single roper ropes the steer around the horns, throws the rope around the steer's back hip, dallies, and rides in a ninety-degree angle to the roped steer (opposite side from the aforementioned hip). This action brings the steer's head around toward the legs in such a manner as to redirect the steer's head towards its back legs. This causes the steer to "trip". Steers are too big to tie in the manner used for calves. Absent a "heeler," it is very difficult for one person to restrain a grown steer once down. However, the steer's "trip" causes it to be temporarily incapacitated allowing its legs to be tied in a manner akin to calf roping. The event has roots in ranch practices north of the Rio Grande, but is no longer seen at the majority of American rodeos. However, it is practiced at some rodeos in Mexico, and may also be referred to as "steer tripping."</li>
<li>Steer daubing—Usually seen at lower levels of competition, an event to help young competitors learn skills later needed for steer wrestling. A rider carrying a long stick with a paint-filled dauber at the end attempts to run up alongside a steer and place a mark of paint inside a circle that has been drawn on the side of the animal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStrickland1998_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStrickland1998-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pole_bending" title="Pole bending">Pole bending</a> is a speed and agility competition sometimes seen at local and high school rodeos. It is more commonly viewed as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gymkhana_(Equestrian)" title="Gymkhana (Equestrian)">gymkhana</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gymkhana_(Equestrian)" title="Gymkhana (Equestrian)">O-Mok-See</a> competition. In pole bending, the horse and rider run the length of a line of six upright poles, turn sharply and weave through the poles, turn again and weave back, then return to the start.</li>
<li>Chute Dogging is an event to teach pre-teen boys how to steer wrestle. The competitor enters a bucking chute with a small steer. The boy will then place his right arm around the steer's neck and left hand on top of its neck. When ready, the gate is opened and steer and contestant exit the chute. Once they cross over a designated line, the competitor will grab onto the horns of the steer (colloquially, to "hook-up" to the steer) and wrestle it to the ground.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_activities">Other activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RodeoNatives.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1053" data-file-width="1123" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/RodeoNatives.jpg/220px-RodeoNatives.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/RodeoNatives.jpg/330px-RodeoNatives.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/RodeoNatives.jpg/440px-RodeoNatives.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RodeoNatives.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Grand Entry at the <a href="/wiki/Pendleton_Round-Up" title="Pendleton Round-Up">Pendleton Round-Up</a></div>

<p>Outside of competitive events, other activities are often associated with rodeos, particularly at local levels. A typical rodeo begins with a "Grand Entry", in which mounted riders, many carrying flags, including the American flag, state flags, banners representing sponsors, and others enter the arena at a gallop, circle once, come to the center of the arena and stop while the remaining participants enter. The grand entry is used to introduce some of the competitors, officials, and sponsors. It is capped by the presentation of the American flag, usually with a rendition of "The Star-Spangled Banner," and, depending on region, other ceremonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> If a <a href="/wiki/Rodeo_queen" title="Rodeo queen">rodeo queen</a> is crowned, the contestants or winner and runners-up may also be presented.</p>
<p>Variety acts, which may include musicians, trick riders or other entertainment may occur halfway through the rodeo at intermission. Some rodeos may also include novelty events, such as steer riding for preteens or "<a href="/wiki/Mutton_busting" title="Mutton busting">mutton busting</a>" for small children. In some places, various types of novelty races or events such as <a href="/wiki/Wild_cow_milking" title="Wild cow milking">wild cow milking</a> are offered for adults. Such contests often are unregulated, with a higher risk of injury to human participants and poor treatment of animals than in traditionally-sanctioned events, particularly if consumption of alcoholic beverages by participants is permitted.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governing_organizations_in_the_United_States">Governing organizations in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Governing organizations in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Formal organizations and detailed rules came late to rodeo. Until the mid-1930s, every rodeo was independent and selected its own events from among nearly one hundred different contests. Until World War I, there was little difference between rodeo and <i><a href="/wiki/Charreada" title="Charreada">charreada</a></i>. Athletes from the US, Mexico and Canada competed freely in all three countries. Subsequently, <i>charreada</i> was formalized as an amateur team sport and the international competitions ceased. It remains popular in Mexico and Hispanic communities of the U.S. today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Numerous organizations govern rodeo in the United States, each with slightly different rules and different events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The oldest and largest sanctioning body of professional rodeo is the <a href="/wiki/Professional_Rodeo_Cowboys_Association" title="Professional Rodeo Cowboys Association">Professional Rodeo Cowboys Association</a> (PRCA) which governs about a third of all rodeos staged in the US annually. It was originally named the Cowboys Turtle Association, later became the Rodeo Cowboys Association, and finally the Professional Rodeo Cowboys Association in 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGroves20063_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGroves20063-10">[10]</a></sup> The PRCA crowns the World Champions at the <a href="/wiki/National_Finals_Rodeo" title="National Finals Rodeo">National Finals Rodeo</a> (NFR), in Las Vegas on the UNLV campus, featuring the top fifteen money-winners in seven events.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Professional_Bull_Riders" title="Professional Bull Riders">Professional Bull Riders</a> (PBR) is a more recent organization dedicated solely to bull riding. Rodeo gender bias was a problem for cowgirls, and in response women formed the Girls Rodeo Association in 1948 (now the Women's Professional Rodeo Association (WPRA)) and held their own rodeos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllen199824.E2.80.9325_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllen199824.E2.80.9325-21">[21]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Professional_Rodeo_Association" title="Women's Professional Rodeo Association">Women's Professional Rodeo Association</a> (WPRA) is open exclusively to women. Women's barrel racing is governed by the WPRA, which holds finals for barrel racing along with the PRCA with the cowboys at the NFR.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pbrnow.com_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-pbrnow.com-22">[22]</a></sup> There are associations governing children's, teen, and college level rodeos as well as associations governing rodeo for gays, seniors, Native Americans and others.</p>
<p>There are also high-school rodeos, sponsored by the <a href="/wiki/National_High_School_Rodeo_Association" title="National High School Rodeo Association">National High School Rodeo Association</a> (NHSRA). Many colleges, particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_grant_college" title="Land grant college">land grant colleges</a> in the west, have rodeo teams. The <a href="/wiki/National_Intercollegiate_Rodeo_Association" title="National Intercollegiate Rodeo Association">National Intercollegiate Rodeo Association</a> (NIRA) is responsible for the College National Finals Rodeo (CNFR) held each June in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casper,_WY" title="Casper, WY">Casper, WY</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Other rodeo governing bodies in the United States include American Junior Rodeo Association (AJRA) for contestants under twenty years of age; National Little Britches Rodeo Association (NLBRA), for youths ages five to eighteen; Senior Pro Rodeo (SPR), for people forty years old or over; and the <a href="/wiki/International_Gay_Rodeo_Association" title="International Gay Rodeo Association">International Gay Rodeo Association</a>. Each organization has its own regulations and its own method of determining champions. Athletes participate in rodeos sanctioned by their own governing body or one that has a mutual agreement with theirs and their points count for qualification to their Association Finals. Rodeo committees must pay sanctioning fees to the appropriate governing bodies, and employ the needed <a href="/wiki/Stock_contractor" title="Stock contractor">stock contractors</a>, judges, announcers, bull fighters, and barrel men from their approved lists. Other nations have similar sanctioning organizations.</p>
<p>Until recently, the most important was PRCA, which crowns the World Champions at the <a href="/wiki/National_Finals_Rodeo" title="National Finals Rodeo">National Finals Rodeo</a> (NFR), held since 1985 at Las Vegas, Nevada, featuring the top fifteen money-winners in seven events. The athletes who have won the most money, including NFR earnings, in each event are the World's Champions. However, since 1992, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professional_Bull_Riders,_Inc." title="Professional Bull Riders, Inc.">Professional Bull Riders, Inc.</a> (PBR) has drawn many top bull riders, and holds its own multimillion-dollar finals in Las Vegas prior to the NFR. Women's barrel racing is governed by the WPRA, and holds its finals along with the PRCA with the cowboys at the NFR.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pbrnow.com_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-pbrnow.com-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Contemporary rodeo is a lucrative business. More than 7,500 cowboys compete for over thirty million dollars at 650 rodeos annually. Women's barrel racing, sanctioned by the WRPA, has taken place at most of these rodeos. Over 2,000 barrel racers compete for nearly four million dollars annually. Professional cowgirls also compete in bronc and bull riding, team roping and calf roping under the auspices of the PWRA, a WPRA subsidiary. However, numbers are small, about 120 members, and these competitors go largely unnoticed, with only twenty rodeos and seventy individual contests available annually. The total purse at the PWRA National Finals is $50,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Meanwhile, the PBR has 700 members from three continents and ten million dollars in prize money.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Calgarystampede.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="864" data-file-width="1152" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Calgarystampede.jpg/220px-Calgarystampede.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Calgarystampede.jpg/330px-Calgarystampede.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Calgarystampede.jpg/440px-Calgarystampede.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The first rodeo in Canada was held in 1902 in <a href="/wiki/Raymond,_Alberta" title="Raymond, Alberta">Raymond, Alberta</a> when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O._Raymond_Knight" title="O. Raymond Knight">Raymond Knight</a> funded and promoted a rodeo contest for bronc riders and steer ropers called the <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Stampede" title="Raymond Stampede">Raymond Stampede</a>. Knight also coined the rodeo term "stampede" and built rodeo's first known shotgun style bucking chute. In 1903, Knight built Canada's first rodeo arena and grandstand and became the first rodeo producer and rodeo stock contractor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raymond_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Raymond-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1912, <a href="/wiki/Guy_Weadick" title="Guy Weadick">Guy Weadick</a> and several investors put up $100,000 to create what today is the <a href="/wiki/Calgary_Stampede" title="Calgary Stampede">Calgary Stampede</a>. The Stampede also incorporated mythical and historical elements, including <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">Native Indians</a> in full regalia, chuckwagon races, the Mounted Police, and marching bands. From its beginning, the event has been held the second week in July, and since 1938, attendees were urged to dress for the occasion in western hats to add to the event's flavour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 2003, it was estimated that 65 professional rodeos involving 700 members of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_Professional_Rodeo_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Canadian Professional Rodeo Association (page does not exist)">Canadian Professional Rodeo Association</a> (CPRA) took place in Western Canada, along with professionals from the United States. Many Canadian contestants were part-timers who did not earn a significant living from rodeo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArmstrongBotzler2003_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArmstrongBotzler2003-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Canadians made several significant contributions to the sport of rodeo. In 1916, at the Bascom Ranch in <a href="/wiki/Welling,_Alberta" title="Welling, Alberta">Welling, Alberta</a>, John W. Bascom and his sons Raymond, Mel, and Earl designed and built rodeo's first side-delivery <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cattle_race" title="Cattle race">bucking chute</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Ranch_rodeo" title="Ranch rodeo">ranch rodeos</a> they were producing. In 1919, Earl and John made rodeo's first reverse-opening side-delivery bucking chute at the Bascom Ranch in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lethbridge,_Alberta" title="Lethbridge, Alberta">Lethbridge, Alberta</a>. This Bascom-style bucking chute is now rodeo's standard design. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earl_Bascom" title="Earl Bascom">Earl Bascom</a> also continued his innovative contributions to the sport of rodeo by designing and making rodeo's first hornless bronc saddle in 1922, rodeo's first one-hand <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodeo_bareback_rigging" title="Rodeo bareback rigging">bareback rigging</a> in 1924, and the first high-cut rodeo <a href="/wiki/Chaps" title="Chaps">chaps</a> in 1928. Earl and his brother Weldon also produced rodeo's first night rodeo held outdoors under electric lights in 1935.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Pro_Rodeo_Hall_Of_Fame" title="Canadian Pro Rodeo Hall Of Fame">Canadian Pro Rodeo Hall Of Fame</a> is located in <a href="/wiki/Ponoka,_Alberta" title="Ponoka, Alberta">Ponoka, Alberta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Mexican_Americans" title="Mexican Americans">Mexican Americans</a> have had a long history with both rodeo and <i><a href="/wiki/Charreada" title="Charreada">charreada</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECandelariaGarc.C3.ADaAldama2004126.E2.80.93128_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECandelariaGarc.C3.ADaAldama2004126.E2.80.93128-30">[30]</a></sup> In spite of long association with southwestern culture, there has been significant assimilation and cross-acculturation — Mexican Americans are so integrated into the southwestern cowboy culture that they are not visibly distinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllen199817_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllen199817-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Native American and Hispanic cowboys compete in modern rodeos in small numbers. African Americans constitute a smaller minority of rodeo contestants, though many early rodeo champions, such as <a href="/wiki/Nat_Love" title="Nat Love">Nat Love</a>, were African American. <a href="/wiki/Bill_Pickett" title="Bill Pickett">Bill Pickett</a> and bronc rider Bill Stahl were both elected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cowboy_Hall_of_Fame" title="Cowboy Hall of Fame">Cowboy Hall of Fame</a>. During the 1940s and 1950s, African Americans created the Southwestern Colored Cowboys Association. Although the PRCA never formally excluded people of color, pre-1960s racism effectively kept many minority participants, particularly African Americans, out of white competitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKirschHarrisElaine_Nolte2000390_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKirschHarrisElaine_Nolte2000390-31">[31]</a></sup> In the 1960s, bull rider Myrtis Dightman vied for national honors and qualified for the National Finals Rodeo. In the 1990s, the Black World Championship Rodeo was held in New York City and other locations across the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKirschHarrisElaine_Nolte2000390_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKirschHarrisElaine_Nolte2000390-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1976, the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gay_rodeo" title="Gay rodeo">gay rodeo</a> was held in <a href="/wiki/Reno,_Nevada" title="Reno, Nevada">Reno, Nevada</a> as a charity fundraiser. Several regional gay rodeo organizations were formed in the following years, and, in 1985, the existing organizations formed the <a href="/wiki/International_Gay_Rodeo_Association" title="International Gay Rodeo Association">International Gay Rodeo Association</a> as a national sanctioning body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The melding of homosexuality and straight cowboy culture in gay rodeo simultaneously embraces archetypal Cowboy Code traits and contemporary gay identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllen1998170.E2.80.93173_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllen1998170.E2.80.93173-33">[33]</a></sup> Openly gay competitors stage their own rodeos because they are not welcomed in the straight circuit. "We can ride with the best of them," one person stated, "But they don't want us around."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllen1998170.E2.80.93173_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllen1998170.E2.80.93173-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_America">Latin America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Latin America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Charreada" title="Charreada">Charreada</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Jaripeo" title="Jaripeo">Jaripeo</a></div>

<p>The <i>charreada</i> is the national sport of Mexico. It is a display and contest of roping and riding with origins tracing to the cattle ranching life and culture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonial_Mexico" title="Colonial Mexico">colonial Mexico</a>. Over time, it became an event that included games, parades, foods, and contests involving humans, cattle, and horses. Following the Mexican Revolution of 1910, many rural Mexicans were displaced and took up residence in cities, where urban-based <a href="/wiki/Charro" title="Charro">charros</a> and others formed associations to establish and refine the charreada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECandelariaGarc.C3.ADaAldama2004126.E2.80.93128_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECandelariaGarc.C3.ADaAldama2004126.E2.80.93128-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the "Chicano Movement" of the 1970s, Mexican Americans revitalized their heritage by establishing the event in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECandelariaGarc.C3.ADaAldama2004126.E2.80.93128_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECandelariaGarc.C3.ADaAldama2004126.E2.80.93128-30">[30]</a></sup> The event historically enjoys greater prestige in Mexico, however, and due to animal cruelty concerns, some <i>charreada</i> events have been banned in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECandelariaGarc.C3.ADaAldama2004126.E2.80.93128_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECandelariaGarc.C3.ADaAldama2004126.E2.80.93128-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike rodeos, most <i>charreadas</i> do not award money to the winners as <i>charreada</i> is considered an amateur sport, but trophies may be distributed. Until recently, the <i>charreada</i> was confined to men but a women's precision equestrian event called the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Escaramuza&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Escaramuza (page does not exist)">escaramuza</a></i> is now the tenth and final event in a <i>charreada</i>. Unlike American rodeo, events are not timed, but judged and scored based on finesse and grace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECandelariaGarc.C3.ADaAldama2004126.E2.80.93128_30-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECandelariaGarc.C3.ADaAldama2004126.E2.80.93128-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colombia_and_Venezuela">Colombia and Venezuela</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Colombia and Venezuela">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Coleo is a traditional Venezuelan and Colombian sport, very similar to American rodeo, where a small group of llaneros (cowboys) on horseback pursue cattle at high speeds through a narrow pathway (called a manga de coleo) in order to drop or tumble them. Coleos are usually presented as a side attraction to a larger event, such as a religious festival. They are very popular in Venezuela and in parts of Colombia, mostly in the plains (llanos). A coleo starts with the participants and a calf or bull (this depends on the age and stature of the competitors) locked behind a trap door. The trap door leads to a narrow earthen pathway about 100 metres long with high guard rails, open at the other end. When a judge gives a signal, the calf is set loose and starts running. A couple of seconds later, the riders are released and they race to grab the calf by its tail. The rider who accomplishes this first will increase speed, dragging the calf until it finally stumbles. The object is to accomplish this in the shortest time.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Brazilian "rodeios" can be traced to the town of <a href="/wiki/Barretos" title="Barretos">Barretos</a> where the primary economic activities involved livestock and the transporting the livestock to other locations, where one of the ways the cowboys found to get some entertainment was riding the animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In 1956 the first ever <a href="/wiki/Festa_do_Pe%C3%A3o_de_Boiadeiro" title="Festa do Peão de Boiadeiro">Festa do Peão de Boiadeiro</a> was created and as the years went by this rodeo became the biggest in Brazil and in Latin America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Barretos is the most famous rodeo in Brazil. However, rodeos are very common in inner state towns in Brazil, especially in <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_do_Sul" title="Rio Grande do Sul">Rio Grande do Sul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mato_Grosso_do_Sul" title="Mato Grosso do Sul">Mato Grosso do Sul</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo_state" title="São Paulo state">São Paulo state</a>. Bull riding has become a significant niche sport in the country in recent years; PBR now runs a national circuit in Brazil, and Brazilian riders are a major presence on the main PBR circuit in the United States.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Argentina">Argentina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Argentina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the twentieth century, rodeo's popularity increased in Argentina. <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>, Rosario, and other major cities hosted rodeos. In 1909, the Sociedad Sportiva Argentina (Argentina Sports Society) announced a rodeo competition in which the winners would eventually compete in the United States against rodeo performers from other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chile">Chile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Chile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chilean_rodeo" title="Chilean rodeo">Chilean rodeo</a></div>
<p>Second to soccer, rodeo is the most popular sport in Chile, and became the national sport of Chile on January 10, 1962 by decree Nº269 of the National Council of Sports and the Comité Olímpico de Chile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chilean rodeo traces to the 16th century, beginning with the gathering together lost or stray cattle in the Plaza de Armas de Santiago for branding and selection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Rodeo began to see regulation in the 17th century and talented riders received honors and awards.</p>
<p>In Chilean rodeo, a team of two mounted men (called a <i>collera</i>) attempt to pin a calf against large cushions lining the arena (<a href="/wiki/Medialuna" title="Medialuna">medialuna</a>). Points are earned for proper technique. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chilean_Horse" title="Chilean Horse">Chilean Horses</a> are employed to the exclusion of others and riders wear traditional <i><a href="/wiki/Huaso" title="Huaso">huaso</a></i> garb as a requirement. The sport has become so popular that in 2004, more spectators attended rodeo events than professional <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> matches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Chilean rodeo has experienced financial woes, lack of political support and poor promotion. Unlike other Chilean sports, rodeo does not receive any of the revenue from <i>Chiledeportes</i> because only sports that represent Chile overseas receive funds. The Chilean Rodeo Federation has criticized the lack of governmental funding and has pointed out that rodeo reaches a part of the population that does not have access to other Chilean sports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Australian_rodeo" title="Australian rodeo">Australian rodeo</a></div>
<p>Rodeos have long been a popular competitor and spectator sport in Australia, but were not run on an organized basis until the 1880s. The National Agricultural Society of Victoria ran one of the earliest recorded events in 1888, when a roughriding competition was held at their annual show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartin1959_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartin1959-41">[41]</a></sup> Travelling tent rodeo shows increased the popularity of roughriding throughout much of Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> However, by 1930, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">depression</a> left only a few of these travelling shows on the road.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartin1959_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartin1959-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bushmen's Carnivals, the Australian equivalent of American rodeos, originated in Northern New South Wales in the 1920s and were well established by the 1930s. Australian rodeo continued to grow following WWII, and by September 1978 riders from the USA, Canada, New Zealand and Australia competed in the World Rodeo Titles there for prize money totaling $60,000. In 1982, an Australian Bushmen's Carnival Association team competed in the North American Rodeo Commission's championships in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denver,_Colorado" title="Denver, Colorado">Denver, Colorado</a>, finishing sixth overall.</p>
<p>In August 1944 the Australian Bushmen's Carnival Association (ABCA) was formed by the Royal Agricultural Society of New South Wales, as a result of the increase in the number of bushmen's carnivals. The purpose of this formation was to standardize regulations and rules, but insufficient support was given and the association was terminated in 1947. The <a href="/wiki/Australian_Professional_Rodeo_Association" title="Australian Professional Rodeo Association">Australian Professional Rodeo Association</a> (APRA) was also formed in 1944 and is the national governing body for professional rodeo competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Also formed in 1944 was the Australian Rough-Riders Association (ARRA) in South Australia. On 28 March 1946 the Northern (N.S.W.) Bushmen's Carnival Association was founded at <a href="/wiki/Maitland,_New_South_Wales" title="Maitland, New South Wales">Maitland, New South Wales</a>. These two associations are now the Australian Bushmen's Campdraft &amp; Rodeo Association (ABCRA). The ABCRA is the largest rodeo and campdraft organization in Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABCRA_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABCRA-44">[44]</a></sup> In May 1992 the National Rodeo Council of Australia (NRCA) was formed to further the sport of rodeo and has represented ABCRA and several other associations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Original events included buckjumping (saddle broncs), bullock riding, <a href="/wiki/Campdrafting" title="Campdrafting">campdrafting</a>, bulldogging, wild-cow milking, wild bullock races, wild horse races and releasing the surcingle. Other common sporting events such as flag and bending races (similar to <a href="/wiki/Pole_bending" title="Pole bending">pole bending</a>) were held for the competitors' horses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShaw1984_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShaw1984-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later the term "rodeo" became more commonly used, with American saddles used and the events took on American naming patterns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1963213_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1963213-47">[47]</a></sup> The ABCRA now affiliates the sports of <a href="/wiki/Campdrafting" title="Campdrafting">campdrafting</a>, roughriding (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saddle_bronc_and_bareback_riding" title="Saddle bronc and bareback riding">saddle bronc and bareback riding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steer_riding" title="Steer riding">steer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bull_riding" title="Bull riding">bull riding</a>) and timed rodeo events: <a href="/wiki/Barrel_racing" title="Barrel racing">barrel races</a> (ladies and junior), <a href="/wiki/Calf_roping" title="Calf roping">rope and tie</a>, steer undecorating (ladies), <a href="/wiki/Steer_wrestling" title="Steer wrestling">steer wrestling</a>, junior calf riding, <a href="/wiki/Team_roping" title="Team roping">team roping</a> and <a href="/wiki/Breakaway_roping" title="Breakaway roping">breakaway roping</a> (ladies).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABCRA_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-ABCRA-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are strict standards for the selection, care and treatment of rodeo livestock, arenas, plus equipment requirements and specifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992 the National Rodeo Queen Quest was founded by the National Rodeo Council of Australia to promote and encourage young women into the sport of Rodeo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>The carnivals and rodeos typically take place during the spring and summer, and are usually arranged to avoid date clashes, so that competitors may take part in as many events as possible. The prize money is obtained from donations and entry fees, with the main prize money being for the open campdraft event.</p>
<p>The biggest rodeos are in Queensland. Some large events are also held in New South Wales, where Sydney has the rodeo during the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Agricultural_Society_of_New_South_Wales" title="Royal Agricultural Society of New South Wales">Royal Agricultural Society</a> show and <a href="/wiki/Walcha,_New_South_Wales" title="Walcha, New South Wales">Walcha</a> holds a four-day campdrafting and rodeo competition annually. There also is a <a href="/wiki/National_Finals_Rodeo_(Australia)" title="National Finals Rodeo (Australia)">National Finals Rodeo</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_treatment_controversies">Animal treatment controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Animal treatment controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Animal_treatment_in_rodeo" title="Animal treatment in rodeo">Animal treatment in rodeo</a></div>
<p>Protests were first raised regarding rodeo <a href="/wiki/Cruelty_to_animals" title="Cruelty to animals">animal cruelty</a> in the 1870s, and, beginning in the 1930s, some states enacted laws curtailing rodeo activities and other events involving animals. In the 1950s, the then Rodeo Cowboys Association (RCA, later the PRCA) worked with the American Humane Association (AHA) to establish regulations protecting the welfare of rodeo animals that were acceptable to both organizations. The PRCA realized that public education regarding rodeo and the welfare of animals was needed to keep the sport alive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over the years, conditions for animals in rodeo and many other sporting events improved. Today, the PRCA and other rodeo sanctioning organizations have stringent regulations to ensure rodeo <a href="/wiki/Animal_welfare" title="Animal welfare">animals' welfare</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> For example, these rules require, among other things, provisions for injured animals, a veterinarian's presence at all rodeos (a similar requirement exists for other equine events), padded flank straps, horn protection for steers, and <a href="/wiki/Spur" title="Spur">spurs</a> with dulled, free-spinning rowels. Rodeo competitors in general value and provide excellent care to the animals with which they work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMillerLamb2005135.E2.80.93144_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMillerLamb2005135.E2.80.93144-52">[52]</a></sup> Animals must also be protected with fleece-lined flank straps for bucking stock and horn wraps for roping steers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AVMA2000_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-AVMA2000-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Laws governing rodeo vary widely. In the American west, some states incorporate the regulations of the PRCA into their statutes as a standard by which to evaluate if animal cruelty has occurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> On the other hand, some events and practices are restricted or banned in other states, including California, Rhode Island, and Ohio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECurnutt2001_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECurnutt2001-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:268–269</sup> St. Petersburg, Florida is the only locality in the United States with a complete ban on rodeo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECurnutt2001_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECurnutt2001-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:268–269</sup> Canadian Humane Societies are careful in criticizing Canadian rodeo as the event has become so indigenous to Western Canada that criticism may jeopardize support for the organization's other humane goals. The Calgary Humane Society itself is wary of criticizing the famous <a href="/wiki/Calgary_Stampede" title="Calgary Stampede">Calgary Stampede</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArmstrongBotzler2003_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArmstrongBotzler2003-28">[28]</a></sup> Internationally rodeo itself is banned in the United Kingdom and the Netherlands,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vancouverhumanesociety.bc.ca_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-vancouverhumanesociety.bc.ca-2">[2]</a></sup> and other European nations have placed restrictions on certain practices.</p>
<p>However, a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humane_Society" title="Humane Society">humane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">animal rights</a> organizations have policy statements that oppose many rodeo practices and often the events themselves. Some also claim that regulations vary from vague to ineffective and are frequently violated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>In response to these concerns, a number of cities and states, mostly in the eastern half of the United States, have passed ordinances and laws governing rodeo. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh</a>, for example, specifically prohibits electric prods or shocking devices, flank or bucking straps, wire tie-downs, and sharpened or fixed spurs or rowels. Pittsburgh also requires humane officers be provided access to any and all areas where animals may go—specifically pens, chutes, and injury pens. The state of Rhode Island has banned tie-down roping and certain other practices.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>Other locales have similar ordinances and laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Positions_taken_by_animal_welfare_organizations">Positions taken by animal welfare organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Positions taken by animal welfare organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are three basic areas of concern to various groups. The first set of concerns surround relatively common rodeo practices, such as the use of bucking straps, also known as flank straps,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> the use of metal or electric cattle prods,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> and tail-twisting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The second set of concerns surround non-traditional rodeo events that operate outside the rules of sanctioning organizations. These are usually <a href="/wiki/Amateur" title="Amateur">amateur</a> events such as <a href="/wiki/Mutton_busting" title="Mutton busting">mutton busting</a>, calf dressing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wild_cow_milking" title="Wild cow milking">wild cow milking</a>, calf riding, chuck wagon races, and other events designed primarily for publicity, half-time entertainment or crowd participation. Finally, some groups consider some or all rodeo events themselves to be cruel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Groups such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PETA" title="PETA">PETA</a>, SHARK,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humane_Society_of_the_United_States" title="Humane Society of the United States">Humane Society of the United States</a> generally take a position of opposition to all rodeos and rodeo events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> A more general position is taken by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASPCA" title="ASPCA">ASPCA</a>, only opposing rodeo events that "involve cruel, painful, stressful and potentially harmful treatment of livestock, not only in performance but also in handling, transport and prodding to perform." The group singles out children's rodeo events such as goat tying, calf riding and sheep riding (“<a href="/wiki/Mutton_busting" title="Mutton busting">mutton busting</a>”), "which do not promote humane care and respect for animals."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Humane_Association" title="American Humane Association">American Humane Association</a> (AHA) does not appear to oppose rodeos per se, though they have a general position on events and contests involving animals, stating that "when animals are involved in entertainment, they must be treated humanely at all times."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> The AHA also has strict requirements for the treatment of animals used for rodeo scenes in movies, starting with the rules of the PRCA and adding additional requirements consistent with the association's other policies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unique among animal protection groups, the ASPCA specifically notes that practice sessions are often the location of more severe abuses than competitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-65">[65]</a></sup> However, many state animal cruelty laws provide specific exemptions for "training practices." The American Humane Association is the only organization addressing the legislative issue, advocating the strengthening of animal cruelty laws in general, with no exceptions for "training practices."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Myths_and_actual_modern_practice">Myths and actual modern practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Myths and actual modern practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some accusations of cruelty are based on misunderstanding. For example, it is a myth that a bucking horse is a wild, terrified animal. The modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronc" title="Bronc">bronc</a> is not a truly <a href="/wiki/Feral_horse" title="Feral horse">feral horse</a>. A significant number of bucking horses are riding horses that learned to buck off their riders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AVMA2000_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-AVMA2000-53">[53]</a></sup> Many bucking horses today are specifically bred for use in rodeos. A proven bucking horse can be sold for $8000 to $10,000, making "rough stock" a valuable investment worth caring for and keeping in good health for many years. Likewise, bucking bulls are also <a href="/wiki/Selective_breeding" title="Selective breeding">selectively bred</a>. Most are allowed to grow up in a natural, semi-wild condition on the open range, but also have to be trained in order to be managed from the ground, safely loaded into trailers, vaccinated and wormed, and be loaded in and out of bucking chutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diamond_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Diamond-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>Young bucking horses are initially introduced to work with cloth dummies attached to the saddle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diamond_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-Diamond-69">[69]</a></sup> Others are already well-trained on the ground. Some champion bucking horses got their start as spoiled riding horses that learned to quickly and effectively unseat riders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronc_Riding_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bronc_Riding-70">[70]</a></sup> Due to the rigors of travel and the short bursts of high intensity work required, most horses in a bucking string are at least 6 or 7 years old before they are used extensively, and are expected to be sound performers for many years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diamond_69-2"><a href="#cite_note-Diamond-69">[69]</a></sup> Awards are given to the owners of the best bucking horses, who are respected as equine athletes and perform for many years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Many are retired to pasture at the end of their careers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AVMA2000_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-AVMA2000-53">[53]</a></sup> Many bucking horses understand their job well and reduce or stop their bucking, even while still wearing a flank strap, as soon as they either unseat the rider or hear the buzzer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Likewise, some bulls appear to understand that their "job" is to throw the rider; they learned not to buck when in the chute and buck far less once the rider is thrown.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry_position">Industry position</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Industry position">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Modern rodeos in the United States are closely regulated and have responded to accusations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_cruelty" title="Animal cruelty">animal cruelty</a> by instituting a number of rules to guide how rodeo animals are to be managed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRCApdf_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-PRCApdf-72">[72]</a></sup> In 1994, a survey of 28 sanctioned rodeos was conducted by on-site independent veterinarians. Reviewing 33,991 animal runs, the injury rate was documented at 16 animals or 0.047 percent, less than five-hundredths of one percent or one in 2000 animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> A study of rodeo animals in Australia found a similar injury rate. Basic injuries occurred at a rate of 0.072 percent, or one in 1405, with injuries requiring veterinary attention at 0.036 percent, or one injury in every 2810 times the animal was used, and transport, yarding and competition were all included in the study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> A later PRCA survey of 60,971 animal performances at 198 rodeo performances and 73 sections of "slack" indicated 27 animals were injured, again approximately five-hundredths of 1 percent—0.0004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRCApdf_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-PRCApdf-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, accusations of cruelty in the USA persist. The PRCA acknowledges that they only sanction about 30 percent of all rodeos, while another 50 percent are sanctioned by other organizations and 20 percent are completely unsanctioned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRCApdf_72-2"><a href="#cite_note-PRCApdf-72">[72]</a></sup> The PRCA opposes the general concept of <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">animal rights</a>, but supports <a href="/wiki/Animal_welfare" title="Animal welfare">animal welfare</a>—the view that humans have the right to use animals but are responsible for their humane treatment and care. The PRCA takes the position that the organization does this and even goes beyond expectation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Regan:_150_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-Regan:_150-76">[76]</a></sup> Not all rodeos are governed by the PRCA however, though organizations governing collegiate and high school rodeos base their rules on those of the PRCA. Nonetheless, certain amateur and "backyard" rodeos are unregulated,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> and do not follow PRCA rules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Regan:_150_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-Regan:_150-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>Advocates for rodeo state that sick, injured, hungry, or severely abused animals cannot perform well in a given event. Rough stock must be healthy and well fed to give the cowboy a powerful and challenging ride sufficient to obtain a high score. The bucking strap has to be an incentive to an animal that already wants to buck off a rider, not a prod, or the animal will either flee the pain, not buck, quickly sour and refuse to work, regardless of any pain that might be inflicted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronc_Riding_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bronc_Riding-70">[70]</a></sup> Steers and roping calves will not break from the chute fast enough for ropers to achieve a fast time if they are lame or weak, and they are not generally used for more than a single season.</p>
<p>Health regulations mandate vaccinations and blood testing of animals crossing state lines, so rodeo stock receives routine care. An injured animal will not buck well and hence a cowboy cannot obtain a high score for his ride, so sick or injured animals are not run through the chutes, but instead are given appropriate veterinary care so they can be returned to their usual level of strength and power. PRCA regulations require veterinarians to be available at all rodeos to treat both bucking stock and other animals as needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rules_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rules-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>The PRCA emphasizes that they first promulgated rules for proper and humane treatment of livestock in 1947, a full 7 years before the founding of the Humane Society of the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronc_Riding_70-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bronc_Riding-70">[70]</a></sup> Participants are fined for animal abuse, and a study of 21 PRCA rodeos found only 15 animals injured in 26,584 performances, a 0.06 percent rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AVMA2001_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-AVMA2001-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the other hand, there are occasions of rule violations and animal mistreatment at sanctioned rodeos. However, the major national rodeos are also under the most intense scrutiny and are the most likely to rigorously follow the rules. Rodeos not subject to the rules of the PRCA or other organizations, and rodeos outside of the United States and Canada, where animal cruelty laws are weaker, are more likely to be the sites of abusive practices. However, animal rights groups are less likely to target these cases.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_mainstream_culture">In mainstream culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rodeo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: In mainstream culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The largest state-of-the-art rodeos are professional, commercial athletic contests held in climate-controlled stadiums, with broadcasting by <a href="/wiki/CBS_Sports_Network" title="CBS Sports Network">CBS Sports Network</a> and other television networks.</p>
<p>Outside of the rodeo world itself, there is disagreement about exactly what rodeo "is." Professional competitors, for example, view rodeo as a sport and call themselves professional athletes while also using the title of cowboy. Fans view rodeo as a spectator sport with animals, having aspects of pageantry and theater unlike other professional sport. Non-westerners view the spectacle as a quaint but exciting remnant of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wild_West" title="Wild West">Wild West</a> while animal activists view rodeo as a cruel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_circus" title="Roman circus">Roman circus</a> spectacle, or an Americanized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bullfight" title="Bullfight">bullfight</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllen199817_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllen199817-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anthropologists studying the sport of rodeo and the culture surrounding it have commented that it is "a blend of both performance and contest", and that rodeo is far more expressive in blending both these aspects than attempting to stand alone on one or the other. Rodeo's performance level permits pageantry and ritual which serve to "revitalize the spirit of the Old West" while its contest level poses a man-animal opposition that articulates the transformation of nature and "dramatizes and perpetuates the conflict between the wild and the tame."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> "On its deepest level, rodeo is essentially a ritual addressing itself to the dilemma of man's place in nature."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rodeo is a popular topic in country-western music, such as the 1991 <a href="/wiki/Garth_Brooks" title="Garth Brooks">Garth Brooks</a> hit single "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodeo_(song)" title="Rodeo (song)">Rodeo</a>", and has also been featured in numerous movies, television programs and in literature. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodeo_(Copland)" title="Rodeo (Copland)">Rodeo</a></i> is a ballet score written by <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Copland" title="Aaron Copland">Aaron Copland</a> in 1942, and choreographer <a href="/wiki/Agnes_de_Mille" title="Agnes de Mille">Agnes de Mille</a>'s ballet, <i><a href="/wiki/Rodeo_(ballet)" title="Rodeo (ballet)">Rodeo</a></i> was commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Ballet_Russe_de_Monte_Carlo" title="Ballet Russe de Monte Carlo">Ballet Russe de Monte Carlo</a> in 1942 with the Copeland score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllen1998175.E2.80.93176_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllen1998175.E2.80.93176-82">[82]</a></sup> The late country singer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chris_Ledoux" title="Chris Ledoux">Chris Ledoux</a> competed in bareback riding and wrote many of his songs based on his experiences.</p>
