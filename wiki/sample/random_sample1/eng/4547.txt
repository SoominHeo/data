<p><i><b>Rodeleros</b></i> ("shield bearers"), also called <i><b>espadachines</b></i> ("swordsmen") and colloquially known as "Sword and Buckler Men", were <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Spain" title="Habsburg Spain">Spanish</a> troops in the early 16th (and again briefly in the 17th) century, equipped with steel shields or <a href="/wiki/Buckler" title="Buckler">bucklers</a> known as <i>rodela</i> and <a href="/wiki/Sword" title="Sword">swords</a> (usually of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Side-sword" title="Side-sword">side-sword</a> type). Originally conceived as an Italian attempt to revive the <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">legionary</a> swordsman, they were adopted by the Spanish and used with great efficiency in the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a> during the 1510s and 1520s, but discontinued in the 1530s.</p>
<p>The majority of <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Hernán Cortés">Hernán Cortés</a>'s troops during his campaigns in the New World were rodeleros: in 1520, over 1000 of his 1300 men were so equipped, and in 1521 he had 700 rodeleros, but only 118 <a href="/wiki/Arquebus" title="Arquebus">arquebusiers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crossbow" title="Crossbow">crossbowmen</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernal_D%C3%ADaz" title="Bernal Díaz">Bernal Díaz</a>, the author of an account of Cortés' <a href="/wiki/Spanish_conquest_of_the_Aztec_Empire" title="Spanish conquest of the Aztec Empire">conquest</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aztecs" title="Aztecs">Aztecs</a>, served as a rodelero under Cortés.</p>
<p>When the Spanish adopted the <i>colunella</i> (the first of the mixed <a href="/wiki/Pike_and_shot" title="Pike and shot">pike and shot</a> formations), they used small groups of sword and buckler men to break the deadlock of the <a href="/wiki/Push_of_pike" title="Push of pike">push of pike</a>, as the Swiss and Germans used <a href="/wiki/Halberd" title="Halberd">halberdiers</a>, comparable to the role of the German <i><a href="/wiki/Doppels%C3%B6ldner" title="Doppelsöldner">Doppelsöldner</a></i> during the same period. At the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ravenna_(1512)" title="Battle of Ravenna (1512)">Battle of Ravenna</a> in 1512, they proved to very effective with this tactic; however, when facing a fresh, well ordered <a href="/wiki/Pike_square" title="Pike square">pike square</a>, they were vulnerable, as at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Seminara" title="Battle of Seminara">Battle of Seminara</a>. They were also very vulnerable to attack by <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalry</a>. As battlefield tactics evolved during the early 16th century, the Spanish ultimately concluded that the vulnerability of the rodeleros on the battlefield outweighed their strengths, and they were dropped as a troop type when the Spanish infantry were reorganized into <a href="/wiki/Tercio" title="Tercio">tercios</a> in the 1530s.</p>
<p>Occasional attempts were made to revive them, such as by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maurice_of_Nassau,_Prince_of_Orange" title="Maurice of Nassau, Prince of Orange">Maurice of Nassau</a>, who armed his guard troops with a sword and buckler in addition to a <a href="/wiki/Pike_(weapon)#Renaissance_heyday" title="Pike (weapon)">pike</a>. Later during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thirty_Years_War" title="Thirty Years War">Thirty Years War</a>, some military theorists proposed deploying swordsmen equipped with large iron shields in front of the pikemen to protect them from being shot by enemy <a href="/wiki/Musketeer" title="Musketeer">musketeers</a>, but it is doubtful whether this fanciful tactic was either successful or much employed in practice.</p>
