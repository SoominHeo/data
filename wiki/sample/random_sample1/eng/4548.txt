<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>The Rhodes Colossus</i></h1>

<p><i><b>The Rhodes Colossus</b></i> is an iconic <a href="/wiki/Editorial_cartoon" title="Editorial cartoon">editorial cartoon</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Scramble_for_Africa" title="Scramble for Africa">Scramble for Africa</a> period, part of the <a href="/wiki/New_Imperialism" title="New Imperialism">New Imperialism</a>, depicting <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British colonialist</a> <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Rhodes" title="Cecil Rhodes">Cecil Rhodes</a> as a giant standing over the continent.</p>
<p>The cartoon was drawn by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Linley_Sambourne" title="Edward Linley Sambourne">Edward Linley Sambourne</a>, and first appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/Punch_(magazine)" title="Punch (magazine)">Punch</a></i> magazine in 1892. It was widely reprinted in its time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and has since become a standard illustration in history texts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cartoon was published in the 10 December 1892 edition of <i>Punch</i>, appearing beside a recent excerpt from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> about a Rhodes plan to extend an <a href="/wiki/Electrical_telegraph" title="Electrical telegraph">electrical telegraph</a> line from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>.</p>
<p>It was led by a piece of satirical verse on the character and ambitions of Rhodes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-punch1892_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-punch1892-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="poem">
<p><a href="/wiki/Brobdingnag" title="Brobdingnag">Brobdingnagian</a> Bagman, big Dreamer of Dreams.<br/>
<span class="mw-poem-indented" style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 1em;">A Titan of tact and shrewd trader—shrewd trader!</span><br/>
A diplomat full of <i>finesse</i> and sharp schemes,<br/>
<span class="mw-poem-indented" style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 1em;">With a touch of the pious Crusader—Crusader!</span><br/>
A "Dealer" with despots, a "Squarer" of Kings,<br/>
<span class="mw-poem-indented" style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 1em;">A jumper of mountain, lake, wilderness, wady,</span><br/>
And manager 'cute of such troublesome things<br/>
<span class="mw-poem-indented" style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 1em;">As <a href="/wiki/Lobengula" title="Lobengula">L<small>OBENGULA</small></a> or the <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ahmad" title="Muhammad Ahmad">M<small>AHDI</small></a>—the M<small>AHDI</small>.</span><br/>
<br/>
Well may <a href="/wiki/James_Hamilton,_2nd_Duke_of_Abercorn" title="James Hamilton, 2nd Duke of Abercorn">A<small>BERCORN</small></a> wonder and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Duff,_1st_Duke_of_Fife" title="Alexander Duff, 1st Duke of Fife">F<small>IFE</small></a> tootle praise,<br/>
<span class="mw-poem-indented" style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 1em;">His two thousand hearers raise cheering—raise cheering.</span><br/>
Of wild would-be Scuttlers he proves the mad craze,<br/>
<span class="mw-poem-indented" style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 1em;">And of Governments prone to small-beering—small-beering.</span><br/>
Sullen <a href="/wiki/Boer" title="Boer">Boers</a> may prove bores to a man of less tact,<br/>
<span class="mw-poem-indented" style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 1em;">A duffer funk wiles Portuguesy—tuguesy;</span><br/>
But Dutchmen, black potentates, all sorts, in fact,<br/>
<span class="mw-poem-indented" style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 1em;">To R<small>HODES</small> the astute come quite easy—quite easy.</span><br/>
<br/>
The <a href="/wiki/British_South_Africa_Company" title="British South Africa Company">British South-African Company</a>'s shares<br/>
<span class="mw-poem-indented" style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 1em;"><i>May</i> be at a discount—(Trade-martyrs!—trade-martyrs!)—</span><br/>
But he, our Colossus, strides on, he declares,<br/>
<span class="mw-poem-indented" style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 1em;">Whether with or without chums or charters—or charters.</span><br/>
Hooray! We brave Britons are right now to the front—<br/>
<span class="mw-poem-indented" style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 1em;">Provided we've someone to boss us—to boss us;</span><br/>
And Scuttlers will have their work cut out to shunt<br/>
<span class="mw-poem-indented" style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 1em;">This stalwart, far-striding Colossus—Colossus!</span></p>
</div>

</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Iconography_and_influence">Iconography and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Rhodes_Colossus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Iconography and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Colossus_of_the_Pacific.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="401" data-file-width="339" height="260" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Colossus_of_the_Pacific.png/220px-Colossus_of_the_Pacific.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Colossus_of_the_Pacific.png/330px-Colossus_of_the_Pacific.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6f/Colossus_of_the_Pacific.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Colossus_of_the_Pacific.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An 1898 American <i>Colossus of the Pacific</i>, in the style of the Rhodes cartoon</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Rhodes is shown in a <a href="/wiki/Visual_pun" title="Visual pun">visual pun</a> as the ancient Greek statue the <a href="/wiki/Colossus_of_Rhodes" title="Colossus of Rhodes">Colossus of Rhodes</a>, following the traditional (and architecturally unlikely) depiction of the Colossus with wide-set legs across Rhodes harbour (above).</p>
<p>Rhodes measures with the telegraphic line the distance from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a> (at his right foot) in South Africa to <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a> (at his left foot) in Egypt, illustrating his broader "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_to_Cairo_Red_Line" title="Cape to Cairo Red Line">Cape to Cairo</a>" concept for <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British domination</a> of Africa.</p>
<p>The cartoon is recognised today as a standard illustration in history texts of the Scramble for Africa, and of Colonialism as a whole. The original context of a proposed telegraph line is rarely mentioned in such reproductions, which take the "Cape to Cairo" concept more generally.</p>
<p>In 2009, the South African cartoonist <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Shapiro" title="Jonathan Shapiro">Jonathan Shapiro</a> parodied the famous cartoon by placing Chinese premier <a href="/wiki/Wen_Jiabao" title="Wen Jiabao">Wen Jiabao</a> in place of Rhodes holding up <a href="/wiki/Nkosazana_Dlamini-Zuma" title="Nkosazana Dlamini-Zuma">Nkosazana Dlamini-Zuma</a>, the then Minister of Foreign Affairs, like a <a href="/wiki/Marionette" title="Marionette">marionette</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/14th_Dalai_Lama" title="14th Dalai Lama">Dalai Lama</a> looks on from Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The cartoon satirised <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sino-African_relations" title="Sino-African relations">Sino-African relations</a> in general, and recent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/China-South_Africa_relations" title="China-South Africa relations">China-South Africa relations</a> in particular, after the Dalai Lama was denied a visa to attend an international peace conference in <a href="/wiki/Johannesburg" title="Johannesburg">Johannesburg</a>, a move that was perceived to be the result of Chinese pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Rhodes cartoon also influenced political cartoonist <a href="/wiki/Martin_Rowson" title="Martin Rowson">Martin Rowson</a>, who published on 1 February 2013 a drawing<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> on British prime minister David Cameron's policy regarding Algeria and the French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Op%C3%A9ration_Serval" title="Opération Serval">intervention</a> in Mali.</p>
