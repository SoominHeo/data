<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Paektusan (rocket)</h1>

<p>The <b>Paektusan</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chosongul" title="Chosongul">Chosongul</a>: 백두산, <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a>: 白头山), also transliterated <b>Baekdusan</b> or <b>Pekdosan</b> is a <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korean</a> <a href="/wiki/Expendable_launch_system" title="Expendable launch system">expendable</a> <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">carrier rocket</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> derived from the <b>Taepodong-1</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Intermediate-range_ballistic_missile" title="Intermediate-range ballistic missile">intermediate-range ballistic missile</a>. It is a three-stage rocket, consisting of the two-stage Taepodong-1, fuelled by <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unsymmetrical_dimethylhydrazine" title="Unsymmetrical dimethylhydrazine">unsymmetrical dimethylhydrazine</a>, with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid-fuel_rocket" title="Solid-fuel rocket">solid-fuelled</a> upper stage to perform orbital insertion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-EA-3">[3]</a></sup> The name refers to <a href="/wiki/Paektu_Mountain" title="Paektu Mountain">Paektu Mountain</a>, the highest mountain in North Korea.</p>
<p>The only reported launch of a Paektusan was conducted at 03:07 UTC on 31 August 1998, with the <a href="/wiki/Kwangmy%C5%8Fngs%C5%8Fng-1" title="Kwangmyŏngsŏng-1">Kwangmyŏngsŏng-1</a> satellite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EA-k1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-EA-k1-4">[4]</a></sup> Although North Korea claimed that the launch was successful, no objects were tracked in orbit, and the United States government claimed that the rocket's third stage failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EA-k1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-EA-k1-4">[4]</a></sup> Subsequent North Korean orbital launch attempts have used the <a href="/wiki/Taepodong-2" title="Taepodong-2">Taepodong-2</a> derived <a href="/wiki/Unha" title="Unha">Unha</a> rocket, and it is unclear whether the Paektusan is still in service. On this launch, the designation Paektusan-1 was used; however, it is unclear whether this was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serial_number" title="Serial number">serial number</a>, or part of the rocket's designation. According to experts North Korea spent approximately $20 million test-firing the Taepodong-1 missile in 1998, and experts guess it cost more this time since performance and capabilities including the airframe and range of the Taepodong-2 missile have been improved by more than 10 percent since the rocket fizzled ignominiously in a test in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a nominal launch, the first stage burns for 95 seconds, before separating, and landing about 250 kilometres (160 mi) downrange. The payload fairing separates 144 seconds after launch. This is followed by the depletion and separation of the second stage, 266 seconds into the flight, resulting in an impact about 1,650 kilometres (1,030 mi) downrange. The third stage, which is spin-stabilised, then burns for 27 seconds to insert the payload into Earth orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EA-k1_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-EA-k1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
