<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lóðurr</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Lóðurr</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%81ss" title="Áss">god</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>. In the <i><a href="/wiki/Poetic_Edda" title="Poetic Edda">Poetic Edda</a></i> poem <i><a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lusp%C3%A1" title="Völuspá">Völuspá</a></i> he is assigned a role in animating the first humans, but apart from that he is hardly ever mentioned, and remains obscure. Scholars have variously identified him with <a href="/wiki/Loki" title="Loki">Loki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vili_and_V%C3%A9" title="Vili and Vé">Vé</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vili" title="Vili">Vili</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freyr" title="Freyr">Freyr</a>, but consensus has not been reached on any one theory.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_and_etymology">Name and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%B3%C3%B0urr&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name's meaning is unknown. It has been speculatively linked to various <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> words, such as <i>lóð</i>, "fruit, land", <i>ljóðar</i>, "people" and <i>laða</i>, "to attract". The <a href="/wiki/Gothic_language" title="Gothic language">Gothic</a> words <i>liudan</i>, "to grow" and <i>laudi</i>, "shape", as well as the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> word <i>lodern</i>, "to blaze", have also been mentioned in this context.</p>
<p>The metrical position of Lóðurr's name in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skaldic_poem" title="Skaldic poem">skaldic poem</a> <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%8Dslendingadr%C3%A1pa" title="Íslendingadrápa">Íslendingadrápa</a></i>, composed in the strict <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dr%C3%B3ttkv%C3%A6tt" title="Dróttkvætt">dróttkvætt</a> metre, indicates that it contains the sound value /ó/ rather than /o/. This evidence, while strong, is not incontrovertible and some scholars have held out for a <i>Loðurr</i> reading. (Lóðurr's name can be furtherly <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse_orthography" title="Old Norse orthography">represented or anglicized</a> as <i>Lóður</i>, <i>Lódurr</i>, <i>Lódur</i>, <i>Lóthurr</i>, <i>Lóthur</i>, <i>Lódhurr</i>, <i>Lódhur</i>, <i>Lodurr</i>, <i>Lodur</i>, <i>Lothurr</i>, <i>Lothur</i>, <i>Lodhurr</i>, <i>Loðurr</i>, <i>Loður</i> or <i>Lodhur</i>.)</p>
<p>Danish and Norwegian <i>lørdag</i>, Swedish <i>lördag</i>, as well as Finnish <i>lauantai</i> may possibly derive from Lóður Dag, meaning "Saturday",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rausing_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rausing-1">[1]</a></sup> although more typically the etymology is proposed to originate from <a href="/wiki/Saturday#Name_and_associations_in_European_cultures" title="Saturday">"washing day"</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attestations">Attestations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%B3%C3%B0urr&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Attestations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="V.C3.B6lusp.C3.A1">Völuspá</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%B3%C3%B0urr&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Völuspá">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Poetic_Edda" title="Poetic Edda">Poetic Edda</a> the name <i>Lóðurr</i> occurs only once; in <i><a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lusp%C3%A1" title="Völuspá">Völuspá</a></i>, where the gods animate the first humans.</p>

<p>The precise meaning of these strophes and their context in <i>Völuspá</i> is debated. Most relevant for the present discussion are Lóðurr's gifts of <i>lá</i> and <i>litu góða</i>. The word <i>lá</i> is obscure and the translations "film of flesh" and "blood" are just two of the many possibilities that have been suggested. The phrase "litu góða" is somewhat less difficult and traditionally interpreted as "good colours", "good shape" or even "good looks".</p>
<p>The 19th-century Swedish scholar <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Rydberg" title="Viktor Rydberg">Viktor Rydberg</a> proposed a reading of <i>litu goða</i>, meaning "shape of gods", and saw the line as indication that the gods created human beings in their own image. While the manuscripts do not distinguish between the <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phonemes</a> /o/ and /ó/, most other scholars have preferred the /ó/ reading for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meter_(poetry)" title="Meter (poetry)">metrical</a> reasons. The metrical structure of <i>Völuspá'</i>s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fornyr%C3%B0islag" title="Fornyrðislag">fornyrðislag</a> is, however, not very rigid and in 1983 Rydberg's theory was championed again by <a href="/wiki/Gro_Steinsland" title="Gro Steinsland">Gro Steinsland</a>. It remains debated.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_attestations">Other attestations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%B3%C3%B0urr&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other attestations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Apart from the strophe in <i>Völuspá</i>, Lóðurr's name occurs only twice in the original sources. The name is found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skaldic_poetry" title="Skaldic poetry">skaldic poems</a> <i><a href="/wiki/H%C3%A1leygjatal" title="Háleygjatal">Háleygjatal</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%8Dslendingadr%C3%A1pa" title="Íslendingadrápa">Íslendingadrápa</a></i> where "Lóðurr's friend" is used as a <a href="/wiki/Kenning" title="Kenning">kenning</a> for <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a>. This seems consistent with Lóðurr's role in <i>Völuspá</i>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Snorri_Sturluson" title="Snorri Sturluson">Snorri Sturluson</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Prose_Edda" title="Prose Edda">Prose Edda</a></i>, Lóðurr is conspicuously absent. Here the creation of humans is attributed to the sons of <a href="/wiki/Borr" title="Borr">Borr</a>, whom Snorri names elsewhere as Odin, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vili" title="Vili">Vili</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V%C3%A9" title="Vé">Vé</a>.</p>

<p>Snorri often quotes <i>Völuspá</i> in his work, but in this case he does not. We cannot know whether he knew the strophes above or whether he was working entirely from other sources.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nordendorf_fibula">Nordendorf fibula</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%B3%C3%B0urr&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nordendorf fibula">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another source sometimes brought into the discussion is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nordendorf_fibula" title="Nordendorf fibula">Nordendorf fibula</a>. This artifact, dating from about 600 <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">CE</a>, contains the <a href="/wiki/Runes" title="Runes">runic</a> inscription <b>logaþorewodanwigiþonar</b>. This is usually interpreted as <i>Logaþore Wodan Wigiþonar</i>, where <i>Wodan</i> is <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a> and <i>Wigiþonar</i> probably is <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">Thor</a>. It would be natural for <i>logaþore</i> to be the name of a third god, but there is no obvious identification in Norse mythology as we know it. Both Lóðurr and Loki have been proposed, but the etymological reasoning is tenuous, and firm conclusions cannot be reached.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories">Theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%B3%C3%B0urr&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the <i>Prose Edda</i> mentions the sons of Borr in the same context as <i>Völuspá</i> does Hœnir and Lóðurr, some scholars have reasoned that Lóðurr might be another name for either Vili or Vé. <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Rydberg" title="Viktor Rydberg">Viktor Rydberg</a> was an early proponent of this theory, but recently it has received little attention.</p>
<p>A more popular theory proposed by the scholar <a href="/wiki/Ursula_Dronke" title="Ursula Dronke">Ursula Dronke</a> is that <i>Lóðurr</i> is "a third name of <a href="/wiki/Loki" title="Loki">Loki/Loptr</a>". The main argument for this is that the gods Odin, Hœnir and Loki occur as a trio in <i><a href="/wiki/Haustl%C3%B6ng" title="Haustlöng">Haustlöng</a></i>, in the prose prologue to <i><a href="/wiki/Reginsm%C3%A1l" title="Reginsmál">Reginsmál</a></i> and also in the <i><a href="/wiki/Loka_T%C3%A1ttur" title="Loka Táttur">Loka Táttur</a></i> a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faroese_people" title="Faroese people">Faroese</a> ballad which is a rare example of the occurrence of Norse gods in folklore. The Odin-kenning "Lóðurr's friend" furthermore appears to parallel the kenning "Loptr's friend" and Loki is similarly referred to as "Hœnir's friend" in Haustlöng, strengthening the trio connection. While many scholars agree with this identification, it is not universally accepted. One argument against it is that Loki appears as a malevolent being later in <i>Völuspá</i>, seemingly conflicting with the image of Lóðurr as a "mighty and loving" figure. Many scholars, including <a href="/wiki/Jan_de_Vries_(linguist)" title="Jan de Vries (linguist)">Jan de Vries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georges_Dum%C3%A9zil" title="Georges Dumézil">Georges Dumézil</a>, have also identified Lóðurr as being the same deity as <a href="/wiki/Loki" title="Loki">Loki</a>.</p>
<p>Recently, Haukur Þorgeirsson of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iceland" title="University of Iceland">University of Iceland</a> suggested that Loki and Lóðurr were different names of the same deity based on that Loki is referred to as Lóður in the rimur <i><a href="/wiki/Lokrur" title="Lokrur">Lokrur</a></i>. Haukur argues that whatever if the rimur is based on Snorri's <i><a href="/wiki/Gylfaginning" title="Gylfaginning">Gylfaginning</a></i> or a folksource the writer must have had the information about the identification from either a tradition or drawing the conclusion based on Edda poems, since Snorri does not mention Lóðurr in his Edda. Since the contents of the Poetic Edda are assumed to have been forgotten around 1400 when the rimur was written Haukur argues for a traditional identification. Haukur also points to <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%9Erymlur" title="Þrymlur">Þrymlur</a></i> where the same identification is made with Loki and Lóðurr. Haukur Þorgeirsson says that unless the possible but unlikely idea that the 14th and 15th century poets possessed lost written sources unknown to us, the idea must have come from either an unlikely amount of sources from where the poets could have drawn a similar conclusion that Loki and Lóðurr are identical like some recent scholars or that there still were remnants of an oral tradition. Haukur concludes that if Lóðurr was historically considered an independent deity from Loki, then a discussion of when and why he became identified with Loki is appropriate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>An identification with <a href="/wiki/Freyr" title="Freyr">Freyr</a> has also been proposed. This theory emphasizes the possible fertility-related meanings of Lóðurr's name but otherwise has little direct evidence to support it.</p>
