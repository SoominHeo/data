<p><b>Hywel Rhodri Morgan</b> (born 29 September 1939) is a <a href="/wiki/Welsh_people" title="Welsh people">Welsh</a> <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> politician who was the <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Wales" title="First Minister of Wales">First Minister of Wales</a> and the Leader of <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Labour" title="Welsh Labour">Welsh Labour</a> from 2000 to 2009. He was also the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">Assembly Member</a> for <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_West_(Assembly_constituency)" title="Cardiff West (Assembly constituency)">Cardiff West</a> from 1999 to 2011 and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" title="Member of Parliament">Member of Parliament</a> for <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_West_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Cardiff West (UK Parliament constituency)">Cardiff West</a> from 1987 to 2001. He is, as of 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rhodri_Morgan&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the longest-serving First Minister of Wales. He was elected Chancellor of <a href="/wiki/Swansea_University" title="Swansea University">Swansea University</a> on 24 October 2011.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodri_Morgan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a>, Hywel Rhodri Morgan is the son of Professor <a href="/wiki/T._J._Morgan" title="T. J. Morgan">T. J. Morgan</a> and the brother of the historian, <a href="/wiki/Prys_Morgan" title="Prys Morgan">Prys Morgan</a>. His second cousin is Professor <a href="/wiki/Garel_Rhys" title="Garel Rhys">Garel Rhys</a>.</p>
<p>Morgan was educated at Whitchurch Grammar School (merged with Whitchurch County Secondary School to become the comprehensive <a href="/wiki/Whitchurch_High_School" title="Whitchurch High School">Whitchurch High School</a> in 1972) in <a href="/wiki/Whitchurch,_Cardiff" title="Whitchurch, Cardiff">Whitchurch</a>, Cardiff; <a href="/wiki/St_John%27s_College,_Oxford" title="St John's College, Oxford">St John's College, Oxford</a> studying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philosophy,_politics,_and_economics" title="Philosophy, politics, and economics">PPE</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, where he gained an <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">MA</a> in Government.</p>
<p>In 1967, he married <a href="/wiki/Julie_Morgan" title="Julie Morgan">Julie Morgan</a>, who was later the Labour MP for <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_North_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Cardiff North (UK Parliament constituency)">Cardiff North</a> between <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1997" title="United Kingdom general election, 1997">1997</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">2010</a>. The couple live in <a href="/wiki/Michaelston-le-Pit" title="Michaelston-le-Pit">Michaelston-le-Pit</a> (a village situated outside their constituencies), and are supporters of the <a href="/wiki/British_Humanist_Association" title="British Humanist Association">British Humanist Association</a>. They have a son and two daughters.</p>
<p>In July 2007, Morgan was admitted to hospital where he underwent heart surgery. Even though he left hospital within the week, doctors said he would not be fully recovered for a few weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodri_Morgan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodri_Morgan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before entering politics, Morgan worked as an Industrial Development Officer for <a href="/wiki/South_Glamorgan" title="South Glamorgan">South Glamorgan</a> County Council from 1974 to 1980. He then served as Head of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Community's</a> office in Wales from 1980 to 1987.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Westminster">Westminster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodri_Morgan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Westminster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Morgan was elected as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" title="Member of Parliament">Member of Parliament</a> (MP) for <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_West_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Cardiff West (UK Parliament constituency)">Cardiff West</a> in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1987" title="United Kingdom general election, 1987">1987</a>. From 1988 to 1994, he was a Shadow Environment Spokesman. He was also Chairman of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> <a href="/wiki/Public_Administration_Select_Committee" title="Public Administration Select Committee">Select Committee on Public Administration</a> (1997–1999), and <a href="/wiki/Opposition_(politics)" title="Opposition (politics)">Opposition</a> <a href="/wiki/Frontbencher" title="Frontbencher">Front Bench</a> Spokesman on <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">Energy</a> (1988–92) and Welsh Affairs (1992–1997). He stepped down from the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2001" title="United Kingdom general election, 2001">2001 General Election</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Assembly_for_Wales">National Assembly for Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodri_Morgan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: National Assembly for Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Assembly_.281999.29">First Assembly (1999)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodri_Morgan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: First Assembly (1999)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A committed supporter of Welsh <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolution</a>, Morgan contested the position of Labour's nominee for the (then titled) First Secretary for Wales. He lost to the then <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Wales" title="Secretary of State for Wales">Secretary of State for Wales</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ron_Davies_(British_politician)" title="Ron Davies (British politician)">Ron Davies</a>. Davies was then forced to resign his position after an alleged sex scandal, whereupon Morgan again ran for the post. His opponent, <a href="/wiki/Alun_Michael" title="Alun Michael">Alun Michael</a>, the new Secretary of State for Wales, was seen as a reluctant participant despite also having a long-standing commitment to Welsh devolution, and was widely regarded as being the choice of the UK leadership of the Labour Party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Michael was duly elected to the leadership but resigned a little more than a year later, amid threats of an imminent no-confidence vote and alleged plotting against him by members of not only his own party, but also Assembly groups and Cabinet members. Morgan became Labour's new nominee for First Secretary, and was elected in February 2000, later becoming First Minister on 16 October 2000 when the position was retitled. He was also appointed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Most_Honourable_Privy_Council" title="Her Majesty's Most Honourable Privy Council">Privy Council</a> in July 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Morgan stepped down from the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2001" title="United Kingdom general election, 2001">2001 General Election</a>.</p>
<p>Morgan's leadership has been characterised by a willingness to distance himself from a number of aspects of UK Labour party policy, particularly in relation to plans to introduce choice and competition into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_services" title="Public services">public services</a>, which he has argued do not fit Welsh attitudes and values, and would not work effectively in a smaller and more rural country. In a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speech_(public_address)" title="Speech (public address)">speech</a> given in <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Swansea_University" title="Swansea University">National Centre for Public Policy</a> in November 2002, Morgan stated his opposition to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foundation_hospitals" title="Foundation hospitals">foundation hospitals</a> (a UK Labour proposal), and referred to the "clear red water"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> separating policies in Wales and in <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Westminster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_Assembly_.282003.29">Second Assembly (2003)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodri_Morgan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Second Assembly (2003)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>On 1 May 2003, Labour under Morgan's leadership <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales_election,_2003" title="National Assembly for Wales election, 2003">was re-elected in the Assembly elections</a>. Morgan managed to win enough seats to form a Labour-only administration (the election was held under <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a>, and Labour won 30 of the 60 seats in the Assembly and the overall majority was achieved when <a href="/wiki/Dafydd_Elis-Thomas" title="Dafydd Elis-Thomas">Dafydd Elis-Thomas</a> AM was elected Presiding Officer of the Assembly) and named his cabinet on 9 May. In that election, Labour easily took back all of the former strongholds they lost to <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a> at <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales_election,_1999" title="National Assembly for Wales election, 1999">the height of Alun Michael's unpopularity in 1999</a>.</p>
<p>In his second term, Morgan's administration continued its theme of "Welsh solutions for Welsh problems", a marked contrast to the Blairite public service reform agenda.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Instead of competition, <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Labour" title="Welsh Labour">Welsh Labour</a> emphasised the need for <a href="/wiki/Collaboration" title="Collaboration">collaboration</a> between public service providers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_Assembly_.282007.29">Third Assembly (2007)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodri_Morgan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Third Assembly (2007)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales_election,_2007" title="National Assembly for Wales election, 2007">Labour was the biggest party</a> with 26 out of the 60 seats, five short of an overall majority. After one month of <a href="/wiki/Minority_government" title="Minority government">minority government</a>, Morgan signed a <a href="/wiki/Coalition_agreement" title="Coalition agreement">coalition agreement</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/One_Wales" title="One Wales">One Wales</a></i>) with <a href="/wiki/Ieuan_Wyn_Jones" title="Ieuan Wyn Jones">Ieuan Wyn Jones</a>, leader of <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a>, on 27 June 2007. Rhodri Morgan became the first modern political leader of Wales to lead an Assembly with powers to pass primary legislation (subject to consent from Westminster).</p>
<p>As head of the civil service in Wales, Morgan's administration was faced with a number of legal issues including an employment tribunal when the service dismissed blogger Christopher Glamorganshire in the Autumn of 2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and a civil court action from Christine Davies after an accusation of sexual harassment from a senior manager was ignored. Davies went on to say that "sexual harassment and bullying is widespread in the Welsh Assembly – and that women are intimidated by some male bosses on a daily basis".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodri_Morgan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In July 2005, Morgan announced his intention to lead the Welsh Labour party into the 2007 general election, but retire as leader and First Minister sometime in 2009, when he would be 70.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> On his 70th birthday (29 September) he set the exact date as immediately following the Assembly's budget session on 8 December 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Counsel_General_for_Wales" title="Counsel General for Wales">Counsel General</a> <a href="/wiki/Carwyn_Jones" title="Carwyn Jones">Carwyn Jones</a>, Health Minister <a href="/wiki/Edwina_Hart" title="Edwina Hart">Edwina Hart</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merthyr_Tydfil_and_Rhymney_(National_Assembly_for_Wales_constituency)" title="Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney (National Assembly for Wales constituency)">Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney</a> <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Member of the National Assembly for Wales">AM</a> <a href="/wiki/Huw_Lewis" title="Huw Lewis">Huw Lewis</a> entered a leadership contest to elect a new Labour leader in Wales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> On 1 December 2009 the winner was declared as Carwyn Jones,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IcWales_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-IcWales-18">[18]</a></sup> who assumed office as First Minister on 10 December 2009. Morgan remained a backbench AM until April 2011, when the third Assembly was dissolved prior to the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales_election,_2011" title="National Assembly for Wales election, 2011">general election on 5 May 2011</a>.</p>
