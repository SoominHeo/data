<p><b>Laura Mvula</b> (née <b>Douglas</b>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ones_to_watch_in_2013_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ones_to_watch_in_2013-1">[1]</a></sup> born 23 April 1986)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is a British soul singer-songwriter from <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>. Her debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Sing_to_the_Moon" title="Sing to the Moon">Sing to the Moon</a></i>, was released on 4 March 2013 with an orchestral re-recording released on 11 August 2014.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_and_personal_life">Early and personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laura_Mvula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early and personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mvula grew up in the Birmingham suburbs of <a href="/wiki/Selly_Park" title="Selly Park">Selly Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kings_Heath" title="Kings Heath">Kings Heath</a> with two younger siblings and was influenced by the girl band <a href="/wiki/Eternal_(band)" title="Eternal (band)">Eternal</a>. In her teen years she attended Swanshurst School for girls. Her mother is from <a href="/wiki/Saint_Kitts_and_Nevis" title="Saint Kitts and Nevis">Saint Kitts</a> and her father is from <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>. In 2005, Mvula sang with Black Voices, an <a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">a cappella</a> group set up by her aunt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2008, she formed a jazz/neo-soul group called Judyshouse, singing lead vocals and writing material for the band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Mvula was Director of the Lichfield Community Gospel Choir, founded by Black Voices and <a href="/wiki/The_Lichfield_Festival" title="The Lichfield Festival">Lichfield Festival</a> in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> She has also previously directed the Alvechurch Community Choir.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Mvula graduated from the <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Conservatoire" title="Birmingham Conservatoire">Birmingham Conservatoire</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_City_University" title="Birmingham City University">Birmingham City University</a> with a degree in composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-counteract_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-counteract-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cosyns_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cosyns-9">[9]</a></sup> While she was working as a supply teacher in a Birmingham secondary school, she started writing songs on her laptop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-counteract_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-counteract-8">[8]</a></sup> She was working as a receptionist when she sent out two demos to several people in the music industry. One of them, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Brown_(composer)" title="Steve Brown (composer)">Steve Brown</a>, heard the songs and sent them to his manager, <a href="/wiki/Kwame_Kwaten" title="Kwame Kwaten">Kwame Kwaten</a>, who would become Mvula's manager.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cosyns_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cosyns-9">[9]</a></sup> In a 2013 podcast for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i>, Mvula admitted to suffering from "crippling stage fright".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laura_Mvula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2012.E2.80.9314:_Sing_to_the_Moon">2012–14: <i>Sing to the Moon</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laura_Mvula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2012–14: Sing to the Moon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sing_to_the_Moon" title="Sing to the Moon">Sing to the Moon</a></div>
<p>After several showcases, Mvula was signed by Colin Barlow to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_Music_Entertainment" title="Sony Music Entertainment">Sony</a> subsidiary <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">RCA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cosyns_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cosyns-9">[9]</a></sup> She released her debut <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">extended play</a>, <i>She</i>, on 16 November 2012. The title track is the first song Mvula ever wrote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sound_of_Boston_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sound_of_Boston-11">[11]</a></sup> Her debut studio album, <i>Sing to the Moon</i>, was released on 4 March 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cosyns_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cosyns-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> She worked on the album with producer <a href="/wiki/Steve_Brown_(composer)" title="Steve Brown (composer)">Steve Brown</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and mix engineer <a href="/wiki/Tom_Elmhirst" title="Tom Elmhirst">Tom Elmhirst</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Paul Lester from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> described her music as "gospeldelia", calling it a new musical genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ones_to_watch_in_2013_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ones_to_watch_in_2013-1">[1]</a></sup> The album was preceded by the single "Green Garden",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cosyns_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Cosyns-9">[9]</a></sup> an elegy to her home in Kings Heath.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-3">[3]</a></sup> On 6 December 2012, Mvula was shortlisted for the Critics' Choice award at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2013_BRIT_Awards" title="2013 BRIT Awards">2013 BRIT Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cchoice_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-cchoice-16">[16]</a></sup> On 9 December, she was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Sound_of..." title="Sound of...">Sound of 2013</a> poll and finished in fourth position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-so13_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-so13-17">[17]</a></sup> On 1 February 2013, she gave her first live TV performance on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Graham_Norton_Show" title="The Graham Norton Show">The Graham Norton Show</a></i> on <a href="/wiki/BBC_One" title="BBC One">BBC One</a>, singing "Green Garden".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Mvula stated her influences include <a href="/wiki/Nina_Simone" title="Nina Simone">Nina Simone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jill_Scott" title="Jill Scott">Jill Scott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erykah_Badu" title="Erykah Badu">Erykah Badu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lauryn_Hill" title="Lauryn Hill">Lauryn Hill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Sing to the Moon</i> reached number 9 on the UK Albums Chart and within the top 100 in seven other countries and has so far reached 173 in the US Billboard 200. In October 2013, Mvula won awards for best female act and best R&amp;B or soul artist at the 2013 <a href="/wiki/MOBO_Awards" title="MOBO Awards">MOBO Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>In March 2014, Mvula re-recorded an orchestral version of her debut album <i>Sing to the Moon</i> in collaboration with the <a href="/wiki/Metropole_Orkest" title="Metropole Orkest">Metropole Orkest</a> and conducted by <a href="/wiki/Jules_Buckley" title="Jules Buckley">Jules Buckley</a>. This was released on 23 June 2014 as a high quality download via <a href="/wiki/Bowers_%26_Wilkins" title="Bowers &amp; Wilkins">Bowers &amp; Wilkins' Society of Sound</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and on CD on 11 August 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> On 19 August 2014, Mvula performed with the <a href="/wiki/Metropole_Orkest" title="Metropole Orkest">Metropole Orkest</a> at the Albert Hall as part of the 2014 BBC Proms Season, supported by Esperanza Spalding and ElectricVocals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2016.E2.80.93present:_The_Dreaming_Room">2016–present: <i>The Dreaming Room</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laura_Mvula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2016–present: The Dreaming Room">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 2016, Mvula released "Overcome", a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Nile_Rodgers" title="Nile Rodgers">Nile Rodgers</a>, and the lead single from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dreaming_Room" title="The Dreaming Room">The Dreaming Room</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Mvula recorded "Sing to the Moon" with <a href="/wiki/Snarky_Puppy" title="Snarky Puppy">Snarky Puppy</a> for the album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Family_Dinner_-_Volume_2" title="Family Dinner - Volume 2">Family Dinner - Volume 2</a></i>, which was released on 12 February 2016. She began promotion for <i>The Dreaming Room</i> by performing "Overcome" on <i>The Graham Norton Show</i> on 29 January 2016 and on <i>The Andrew Marr Show</i> on 14 February 2016. On 19 March 2016, Mvula played the first live show of the album at the Jazz Maastricht Festival. On 22 March 2016, she previewed the entire album at Islington Assembly Hall. On 7 April 2016, Mvula released "People" from the album, a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Wretch_32" title="Wretch 32">Wretch 32</a>. On 19 April 2016, Mvula released the second single from <i>The Dreaming Room</i>, "Phenomenal Woman". On 27 May 2016, Mvula released "Show Me Love". <i>The Dreaming Room</i> was released on 17 June 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2016 Mvula performed, with <a href="/wiki/Tom_Odell" title="Tom Odell">Tom Odell</a>, at the first UK event for "Global Citizen" and "Chime For Change", at <a href="/wiki/The_View_from_the_Shard" title="The View from the Shard">The View from the Shard</a> in London. The evening followed the launch of the #SheWill campaign, aimed at breaking down the barriers that prevent millions of girls worldwide from attending school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 30 October 2016 Mvula appeared on BBC's <i><a href="/wiki/Strictly_Come_Dancing_(series_14)" title="Strictly Come Dancing (series 14)">Strictly Come Dancing</a></i> singing her new single "<a href="/wiki/Ready_or_Not_(Fugees_song)" title="Ready or Not (Fugees song)">Ready or Not</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Laura will write the music for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Shakespeare_Company" title="Royal Shakespeare Company">Royal Shakespeare Company</a>'s 2017 show, <a href="/wiki/Antony_and_Cleopatra" title="Antony and Cleopatra">Antony and Cleopatra</a>.</p>
