<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Laurence McKeown</b> (born 1956) is an Irish author, playwright, screenwriter, and former <a href="/wiki/Volunteer_(Irish_republican)" title="Volunteer (Irish republican)">volunteer</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Provisional Irish Republican Army">Provisional Irish Republican Army</a> (IRA) who took part in the <a href="/wiki/1981_Irish_hunger_strike" title="1981 Irish hunger strike">1981 Irish hunger strike</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_IRA_activity">Background and IRA activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laurence_McKeown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and IRA activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McKeown was born in 1956 in <a href="/wiki/Randalstown" title="Randalstown">Randalstown</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Antrim" title="County Antrim">County Antrim</a>, Northern Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-searc_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-searc-1">[1]</a></sup> As a teenager, McKeown had ambitions of becoming an architect and when aged 16 he started working in the offices of a <a href="/wiki/Quantity_surveyor" title="Quantity surveyor">quantity surveyor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-2">[2]</a></sup> When aged 17 he joined the IRA, and he was arrested in August 1976 and charged with causing explosions and the attempted murder of a member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Ulster_Constabulary" title="Royal Ulster Constabulary">Royal Ulster Constabulary</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-searc_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-searc-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-2">[2]</a></sup> At his trial in April 1977, McKeown was found guilty and sentenced to life imprisonment in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maze_Prison" title="Maze Prison">Maze Prison</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-searc_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-searc-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Imprisonment_and_hunger_strike">Imprisonment and hunger strike</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laurence_McKeown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Imprisonment and hunger strike">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While in prison McKeown took part in the <a href="/wiki/Blanket_protest" title="Blanket protest">blanket</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dirty_protest" title="Dirty protest">dirty protests</a> attempting to secure the return of <a href="/wiki/Special_Category_Status" title="Special Category Status">Special Category Status</a> for convicted paramilitary prisoners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-searc_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-searc-1">[1]</a></sup> In late 1980 the protest escalated and seven prisoners took part in a fifty-three-day <a href="/wiki/Hunger_strike" title="Hunger strike">hunger strike</a>, aimed at restoring political status by securing what were known as the "Five Demands":</p>
<ol>
<li>The right not to wear a prison uniform;</li>
<li>The right not to do prison work;</li>
<li>The right of free association with other prisoners, and to organise educational and recreational pursuits;</li>
<li>The right to one visit, one letter and one parcel per week;</li>
<li>Full restoration of remission lost through the protest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>The strike ended before any prisoners had died and without political status being secured, and <a href="/wiki/1981_Irish_hunger_strike" title="1981 Irish hunger strike">a second hunger strike</a> began on 1 March 1981 led by <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Sands" title="Bobby Sands">Bobby Sands</a>, the IRA's former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Officer_Commanding" title="Officer Commanding">Officer Commanding</a> (OC) in the prison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cainhsc_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-cainhsc-4">[4]</a></sup> McKeown joined the strike on 29 June, after Sands and three other prisoners had died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cainlist_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-cainlist-5">[5]</a></sup> Following the deaths of six other prisoners, McKeown's family authorised medical intervention to save his life on 6 September, the 70th day of his hunger strike.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cainlist_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-cainlist-5">[5]</a></sup> He described his recollection of the events in an interview:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>You're very sleepy and very, very tired and you're sort of nodding off to sleep but something's telling you to keep waking up. This was the thing that kept everybody going through the hunger strike in trying to live or last out as long as possible. I knew death was close but I wasn't afraid to die – and it wasn't any sort of courageous or glorious thing. I think death would have been a release. You can never feel that way again. It's not like tiredness. It's an absolute, total, mental and physical exhaustion. It's literally like slipping into death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Freedom">Freedom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laurence_McKeown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Freedom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McKeown completed a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">bachelor's degree</a> in social science from the <a href="/wiki/Open_University" title="Open University">Open University</a> while in prison before being released in 1992, and subsequently obtained a PhD in Sociology from <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_University_Belfast" title="Queen's University Belfast">Queen's University Belfast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-irb_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-irb-8">[8]</a></sup> In the mid-1990s he co-founded the <a href="/wiki/Belfast_Film_Festival" title="Belfast Film Festival">Belfast Film Festival</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-2">[2]</a></sup> and has written two books about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_republican" title="Irish republican">Irish republican</a> prisoners in the Maze Prison; <i>Nor Meekly Serve My Time: The H-Block Struggle 1976–1981</i> (co-written with Brian Campbell and Felim O'Hagan) was published in 1994, and <i>Out Of Time: Irish Republican Prisoners, Long Kesh, 1972–2000</i> was published in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-irb_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-irb-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>McKeown and Brian Campbell co-wrote a film about the 1981 hunger strike called <i><a href="/wiki/H3_(film)" title="H3 (film)">H3</a></i> which was directed by <a href="/wiki/Les_Blair" title="Les Blair">Les Blair</a>, and premiered in cinemas on 28 September 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Before the death of Campbell in 2005, he and McKeown also wrote two plays together, <i>The Laughter of Our Children</i> which debuted in 2001, and <i>A Cold House</i> which debuted in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> McKeown's first solo play, <i>The Official Version</i>, debuted on 18 September 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 2006 he appeared in a two-part documentary titled <i>Hunger Strike</i>, which was shown on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RT%C3%89" title="RTÉ">RTÉ</a> to mark the 25th anniversary of the 1981 hunger strike.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> McKeown also works as a Development Officer for <i><a href="/wiki/Coiste_na_n-Iarchim%C3%AD" title="Coiste na n-Iarchimí">Coiste na n-Iarchimí</a></i>, an umbrella organisation of republican ex-prisoners groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
