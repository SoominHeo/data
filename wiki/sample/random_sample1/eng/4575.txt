<p><b>Roman art</b> refers to the visual arts made in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Ancient Rome</a> and in the territories of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. Roman art includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_architecture" title="Roman architecture">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_sculpture" title="Roman sculpture">sculpture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_mosaic" title="Roman mosaic">mosaic work</a>. Luxury objects in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal-work" title="Metal-work">metal-work</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engraved_gem" title="Engraved gem">gem engraving</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivory_carving" title="Ivory carving">ivory carvings</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roman_glass" title="Roman glass">glass</a> are sometimes considered in modern terms to be minor forms of Roman art,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> although this would not necessarily have been the case for contemporaries. Sculpture was perhaps considered as the highest form of art by Romans, but figure painting was also very highly regarded. The two forms have had very contrasting rates of survival, with a very large body of sculpture surviving from about the 1st century BC on wards, though very little from before, but very little painting at all remains, and probably nothing that a contemporary would have considered to be of the highest quality.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman_pottery" title="Ancient Roman pottery">Ancient Roman pottery</a> was not a luxury product, but a vast production of "fine wares" in <i><a href="/wiki/Terra_sigillata" title="Terra sigillata">terra sigillata</a></i> were decorated with reliefs that reflected the latest taste, and provided a large group in society with stylish objects at what was evidently an affordable price. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_coins" title="Roman coins">Roman coins</a> were an important means of propaganda, and have survived in enormous numbers. Other perishable forms of art have not survived at all.</p>

<p>While the traditional view of the ancient Roman artists is that they often borrowed from, and copied Greek precedents (much of the Greek sculptures known today are in the form of Roman marble copies), more recent analysis has indicated that Roman art is a highly creative pastiche relying heavily on Greek models but also encompassing <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_art" title="Etruscan art">Etruscan</a>, native Italic, and even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_art" title="Egyptian art">Egyptian</a> visual culture. Stylistic eclecticism and practical application are the hallmarks of much Roman art.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny</a>, Ancient Rome’s most important historian concerning the arts, recorded that nearly all the forms of art – sculpture, landscape, portrait painting, even genre painting – were advanced in Greek times, and in some cases, more advanced than in Rome. Though very little remains of Greek wall art and portraiture, certainly Greek sculpture and vase painting bears this out. These forms were not likely surpassed by Roman artists in fineness of design or execution. As another example of the lost "Golden Age", he singled out <a href="/wiki/Peiraikos" title="Peiraikos">Peiraikos</a>, "whose artistry is surpassed by only a very few ... He painted barbershops and shoemakers’ stalls, donkeys, vegetables, and such, and for that reason came to be called the 'painter of vulgar subjects'; yet these works are altogether delightful, and they were sold at higher prices than the greatest [paintings] of many other artists.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The adjective "vulgar" is used here in its original meaning, which means "common".</p>
<p>The Greek antecedents of Roman art were legendary. In the mid-5th century BC, the most famous Greek artists were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polygnotos" title="Polygnotos">Polygnotos</a>, noted for his wall murals, and <a href="/wiki/Apollodorus_(painter)" title="Apollodorus (painter)">Apollodoros</a>, the originator of <a href="/wiki/Chiaroscuro" title="Chiaroscuro">chiaroscuro</a>. The development of realistic technique is credited to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zeuxis_and_Parrhasius" title="Zeuxis and Parrhasius">Zeuxis and Parrhasius</a>, who according to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greek</a> legend, are said to have once competed in a bravura display of their talents, history’s earliest descriptions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trompe_l%E2%80%99oeil" title="Trompe l’oeil">trompe l’oeil</a> painting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In sculpture, Skopas, <a href="/wiki/Praxiteles" title="Praxiteles">Praxiteles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phidias" title="Phidias">Phidias</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lysippos" title="Lysippos">Lysippos</a> were the foremost sculptors. It appears that Roman artists had much Ancient Greek art to copy from, as trade in art was brisk throughout the empire, and much of the Greek artistic heritage found its way into Roman art through books and teaching. Ancient Greek treatises on the arts are known to have existed in Roman times though are now lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piper.2C_p._252_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Piper.2C_p._252-4">[4]</a></sup> Many Roman artists came from Greek colonies and provinces...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janson.2C_p._158_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Janson.2C_p._158-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Roman_sacrifice_Louvre_Ma992.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Preparation of an <a href="/wiki/Animal_sacrifice" title="Animal sacrifice">animal sacrifice</a>; marble, fragment of an architectural <a href="/wiki/Relief" title="Relief">relief</a>, first quarter of the 2nd century CE; from Rome, Italy</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The high number of Roman copies of Greek art also speaks of the esteem Roman artists had for Greek art, and perhaps of its rarer and higher quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janson.2C_p._158_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Janson.2C_p._158-5">[5]</a></sup> Many of the art forms and methods used by the Romans – such as high and low relief, free-standing sculpture, bronze casting, vase art, <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">mosaic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cameo_(carving)" title="Cameo (carving)">cameo</a>, coin art, fine jewelry and metalwork, funerary sculpture, perspective drawing, <a href="/wiki/Caricature" title="Caricature">caricature</a>, genre and <a href="/wiki/Portrait_painting" title="Portrait painting">portrait painting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Landscape_painting" title="Landscape painting">landscape painting</a>, architectural sculpture, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trompe_l%E2%80%99oeil" title="Trompe l’oeil">trompe l’oeil</a> painting – all were developed or refined by Ancient Greek artists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> One exception is the Roman bust, which did not include the shoulders. The traditional head-and-shoulders bust may have been an Etruscan or early Roman form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piper.2C_p._255_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Piper.2C_p._255-7">[7]</a></sup> Virtually every artistic technique and method used by <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> artists 1,900 year later, had been demonstrated by Ancient Greek artists, with the notable exceptions of oil colors and mathematically accurate perspective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piper.2C_p._253_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Piper.2C_p._253-8">[8]</a></sup> Where Greek artists were highly revered in their society, most Roman artists were anonymous and considered tradesmen. There is no recording, as in Ancient Greece, of the great masters of Roman art, and practically no signed works. Where Greeks worshiped the aesthetic qualities of great art and wrote extensively on artistic theory, Roman art was more decorative and indicative of status and wealth, and apparently not the subject of scholars or philosophers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Owing in part to the fact that the Roman cities were far larger than the Greek city-states in power and population, and generally less provincial, art in Ancient Rome took on a wider, and sometimes more utilitarian, purpose. Roman culture assimilated many cultures and was for the most part tolerant of the ways of conquered peoples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janson.2C_p._158_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Janson.2C_p._158-5">[5]</a></sup> Roman art was commissioned, displayed, and owned in far greater quantities, and adapted to more uses than in Greek times. Wealthy Romans were more materialistic; they decorated their walls with art, their home with decorative objects, and themselves with fine jewelry.</p>
<p>In the Christian era of the late Empire, from 350 to 500 CE, wall painting, <a href="/wiki/Roman_mosaic" title="Roman mosaic">mosaic ceiling and floor work</a>, and funerary sculpture thrived, while full-sized sculpture in the round and panel painting died out, most likely for religious reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piper.2C_p._261_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Piper.2C_p._261-10">[10]</a></sup> When Constantine moved the capital of the empire to Byzantium (renamed Constantinople), Roman art incorporated Eastern influences to produce the Byzantine style of the late empire. When Rome was sacked in the 5th century, artisans moved to and found work in the Eastern capital. The Church of <a href="/wiki/Hagia_Sophia" title="Hagia Sophia">Hagia Sophia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> employed nearly 10,000 workmen and artisans, in a final burst of Roman art under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Justinian" title="Emperor Justinian">Emperor Justinian</a> (527–565 CE), who also ordered the creation of the famous mosaics of <a href="/wiki/Ravenna" title="Ravenna">Ravenna</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Painting">Painting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Painting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Pompeian_Styles" title="Pompeian Styles">Pompeian Styles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Wall_Painting_(200_BC-79_AD)" title="Roman Wall Painting (200 BC-79 AD)">Roman Wall Painting (200 BC-79 AD)</a></div>

<p>Of the vast body of Roman painting we now have only a very few pockets of survivals, with many documented types not surviving at all, or doing so only from the very end of the period. The best known and most important pocket is the wall paintings from <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herculaneum" title="Herculaneum">Herculaneum</a> and other sites nearby, which show how residents of a wealthy seaside resort decorated their walls in the century or so before the fatal eruption of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Vesuvius" title="Mount Vesuvius">Mount Vesuvius</a> in 79 CE. A <a href="/wiki/Pompeian_Styles" title="Pompeian Styles">succession of dated styles</a> have been defined and analysed by modern art historians beginning with <a href="/wiki/August_Mau" title="August Mau">August Mau</a>, showing increasing elaboration and sophistication.</p>
<p>Starting in the 3rd century CE and finishing by about 400 we have a large body of paintings from the <a href="/wiki/Catacombs_of_Rome" title="Catacombs of Rome">Catacombs of Rome</a>, by no means all Christian, showing the later continuation of the domestic decorative tradition in a version adapted - probably not greatly adapted - for use in burial chambers, in what was probably a rather humbler social milieu than the largest houses in Pompeii. Much of <a href="/wiki/Nero" title="Nero">Nero</a>'s palace in Rome, the <a href="/wiki/Domus_Aurea" title="Domus Aurea">Domus Aurea</a>, survived as grottos and gives us examples which we can be sure represent the very finest quality of wall-painting in its style, and which may well have represented significant innovation in style. There are a number of other parts of painted rooms surviving from Rome and elsewhere, which somewhat help to fill in the gaps of our knowledge of wall-painting. From <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Egypt" title="Roman Egypt">Roman Egypt</a> there are a large number of what are known as <a href="/wiki/Fayum_mummy_portraits" title="Fayum mummy portraits">Fayum mummy portraits</a>, bust portraits on wood added to the outside of <a href="/wiki/Mummy" title="Mummy">mummies</a> by a Romanized middle-class; despite their very distinct local character they are probably broadly representative of Roman style in painted portraits, which are otherwise entirely lost.</p>
<p>Nothing remains of the Greek paintings imported to Rome during the 4th and 5th centuries, or of the painting on wood done in Italy during that period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piper.2C_p._252_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Piper.2C_p._252-4">[4]</a></sup> In sum, the range of samples is confined to only about 200 years out of the about 900 years of Roman history,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janson.2C_p._190_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Janson.2C_p._190-12">[12]</a></sup> and of provincial and decorative painting. Most of this wall painting was done using the secco (“dry”) method, but some <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">fresco</a> paintings also existed in Roman times. There is <a href="/wiki/Roman_mosaic" title="Roman mosaic">evidence from mosaics</a> and a few inscriptions that some Roman paintings were adaptations or copies of earlier Greek works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janson.2C_p._190_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Janson.2C_p._190-12">[12]</a></sup> However, adding to the confusion is the fact that inscriptions may be recording the names of immigrant Greek artists from Roman times, not from Ancient Greek originals that were copied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piper.2C_p._253_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Piper.2C_p._253-8">[8]</a></sup> The Romans entirely lacked a tradition of figurative vase-painting comparable to that of the Ancient Greeks, which the Etruscans had emulated.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Variety_of_subjects">Variety of subjects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variety of subjects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zeffiro-e-clori---pompeii.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1097" data-file-width="1500" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Zeffiro-e-clori---pompeii.jpg/250px-Zeffiro-e-clori---pompeii.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Zeffiro-e-clori---pompeii.jpg/375px-Zeffiro-e-clori---pompeii.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Zeffiro-e-clori---pompeii.jpg/500px-Zeffiro-e-clori---pompeii.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zeffiro-e-clori---pompeii.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>The Wedding of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zephyrus" title="Zephyrus">Zephyrus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chloris" title="Chloris">Chloris</a></i> (54–68 AD, <a href="/wiki/Pompeian_Styles" title="Pompeian Styles">Pompeian Fourth Style</a>) within painted architectural panels from the Casa del Naviglio</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Roman painting provides a wide variety of themes: animals, still life, scenes from everyday life, portraits, and some mythological subjects. During the Hellenistic period, it evoked the pleasures of the countryside and represented scenes of shepherds, herds, rustic temples, rural mountainous landscapes and country houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piper.2C_p._253_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Piper.2C_p._253-8">[8]</a></sup> Erotic scenes are also relatively common. In the late empire, after 200AD, early Christian themes mixed with pagan imagery survive on catacomb walls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piper.2C_p._260_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Piper.2C_p._260-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Landscape_and_vistas">Landscape and vistas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Landscape and vistas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pompejanischer_Maler_um_10_20_001.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2377" data-file-width="2024" height="258" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Pompejanischer_Maler_um_10_20_001.jpg/220px-Pompejanischer_Maler_um_10_20_001.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Pompejanischer_Maler_um_10_20_001.jpg/330px-Pompejanischer_Maler_um_10_20_001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Pompejanischer_Maler_um_10_20_001.jpg/440px-Pompejanischer_Maler_um_10_20_001.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pompejanischer_Maler_um_10_20_001.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Boscotrecase" title="Boscotrecase">Boscotrecase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>. Third style</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The main innovation of Roman painting compared to Greek art was the development of landscapes, in particular incorporating techniques of perspective, though true mathematical perspective developed 1,500 years later. Surface textures, shading, and coloration are well applied but scale and spatial depth was still not rendered accurately. Some landscapes were pure scenes of nature, particularly gardens with flowers and trees, while others were architectural vistas depicting urban buildings. Other landscapes show episodes from mythology, the most famous demonstrating scenes from the <i><a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the traditional view, the art of the ancient East would have known landscape painting only as the backdrop to civil or military narrative scenes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This theory, defended by <a href="/wiki/Franz_Wickhoff" title="Franz Wickhoff">Franz Wickhoff</a>, is debatable. It is possible to see evidence of Greek knowledge of landscape portrayal in Plato's <i><a href="/wiki/Critias_(dialogue)" title="Critias (dialogue)">Critias</a></i> (107b–108b):</p>
<blockquote>
<p>... and if we look at the portraiture of divine and of human bodies as executed by painters, in respect of the ease or difficulty with which they succeed in imitating their subjects in the opinion of onlookers, we shall notice in the first place that as regards the earth and mountains and rivers and woods and the whole of heaven, with the things that exist and move therein, we are content if a man is able to represent them with even a small degree of likeness ...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Still_life">Still life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Still life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Roman <a href="/wiki/Still_life" title="Still life">still life</a> subjects are often placed in illusionist niches or shelves and depict a variety of everyday objects including fruit, live and dead animals, seafood, and shells. Examples of the theme of the glass jar filled with water were skillfully painted and later served as models for the same subject often painted during the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portraits">Portraits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Portraits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny</a> complained of the declining state of Roman portrait art, "The painting of portraits which used to transmit through the ages the accurate likenesses of people, has entirely gone out ... Indolence has destroyed the arts."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Greece and Rome, wall painting was not considered as high art. The most prestigious form of art besides sculpture was <a href="/wiki/Panel_painting" title="Panel painting">panel painting</a>, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Tempera" title="Tempera">tempera</a> or <a href="/wiki/Encaustic_painting" title="Encaustic painting">encaustic painting</a> on wooden panels. Unfortunately, since wood is a perishable material, only a very few examples of such paintings have survived, namely the <a href="/wiki/Severan_Tondo" title="Severan Tondo">Severan Tondo</a> from c. 200 AD, a very routine official portrait from some provincial government office, and the well-known <a href="/wiki/Fayum_mummy_portraits" title="Fayum mummy portraits">Fayum mummy portraits</a>, all from Roman Egypt, and almost certainly not of the highest contemporary quality. The portraits were attached to burial mummies at the face, from which almost all have now been detached. They usually depict a single person, showing the head, or head and upper chest, viewed frontally. The background is always monochrome, sometimes with decorative elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In terms of artistic tradition, the images clearly derive more from Greco-Roman traditions than Egyptian ones. They are remarkably realistic, though variable in artistic quality, and may indicate that similar art which was widespread elsewhere but did not survive. A few portraits painted on glass and medals from the later empire have survived, as have coin portraits, some of which are considered very realistic as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>Gold glass, or gold sandwich glass, was a technique for fixing a layer of <a href="/wiki/Gold_leaf" title="Gold leaf">gold leaf</a> with a design between two fused layers of glass, developed in <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_glass" title="Hellenistic glass">Hellenistic glass</a> and revived in the 3rd century AD. There are a very few large designs, including a very fine group of portraits from the 3rd century with added paint, but the great majority of the around 500 survivals are roundels that are the cut-off bottoms of wine cups or glasses used to mark and decorate graves in the <a href="/wiki/Catacombs_of_Rome" title="Catacombs of Rome">Catacombs of Rome</a> by pressing them into the mortar. They predominantly date from the 4th and 5th centuries. Most are Christian, though there are many pagan and a few Jewish examples. It is likely that they were originally given as gifts on marriage, or festive occasions such as New Year. Their <a href="/wiki/Iconography" title="Iconography">iconography</a> has been much studied, although artistically they are relatively unsophisticated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Their subjects are similar to the catacomb paintings, but with a difference balance including more portraiture. As time went on there was an increase in the depiction of saints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The same technique began to be used for gold <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tesserae" title="Tesserae">tesserae</a> for mosaics in the mid-1st century in Rome, and by the 5th century these had become the standard background for religious mosaics.</p>
<p>The earlier group are "among the most vivid portraits to survive from Early Christian times. They stare out at us with an extraordinary stern and melancholy intensity",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and represent the best surviving indications of what high quality Roman portraiture could achieve in paint. The Gennadios medallion in the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a> in New York, is a fine example of an Alexandrian portrait on blue glass, using a rather more complex technique and naturalistic style than most Late Roman examples, including painting onto the gold to create shading, and with the Greek inscription showing local <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialect</a> features. He had perhaps been given or commissioned the piece to celebrate victory in a musical competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> One of the most famous Alexandrian-style portrait medallions, with an inscription in Egyptian Greek, was later mounted in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Medieval" title="Early Medieval">Early Medieval</a> <a href="/wiki/Crux_gemmata" title="Crux gemmata">crux gemmata</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brescia" title="Brescia">Brescia</a>, in the mistaken belief that it showed the pious empress and <a href="/wiki/Ostrogoths" title="Ostrogoths">Gothic</a> queen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galla_Placida" title="Galla Placida">Galla Placida</a> and her children;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> in fact the knot in the central figure's dress may mark a devotee of <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> This is one of a group of 14 pieces dating to the 3rd century AD, all individualized secular portraits of high quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The inscription on the medallion is written in the <a href="/wiki/Greeks_in_Egypt" title="Greeks in Egypt">Alexanrian dialect of Greek</a> and hence most likely depicts a family from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Egypt" title="Roman Egypt">Roman Egypt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The medallion has also been compared to other works of contemporaneous Roman-Egyptian artwork, such as the <a href="/wiki/Fayum_mummy_portraits" title="Fayum mummy portraits">Fayum mummy portraits</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-howells_2015_p7_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-howells_2015_p7-22">[22]</a></sup> It is thought that the tiny detail of pieces such as these can only have been achieved using <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lenses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The later glasses from the catacombs have a level of portraiture that is rudimentary, with features, hairstyles and clothes all following stereotypical styles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genre_scenes">Genre scenes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Genre scenes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Roman genre scenes generally depict Romans at leisure and include gambling, music and sexual encounters.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some scenes depict gods and goddesses at leisure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piper.2C_p._253_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Piper.2C_p._253-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janson.2C_p._190_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Janson.2C_p._190-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>From the 3rd century BC, a specific genre known as <i>Triumphal Paintings</i> appeared, as indicated by Pliny (XXXV, 22).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pliny_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pliny-33">[33]</a></sup> These were paintings which showed triumphal entries after military victories, represented episodes from the war, and conquered regions and cities. Summary maps were drawn to highlight key points of the campaign. <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">Josephus</a> describes the painting executed on the occasion of <a href="/wiki/Vespasian" title="Vespasian">Vespasian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Titus" title="Titus">Titus</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Destruction_of_Jerusalem" title="Destruction of Jerusalem">sack of Jerusalem</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>There was also wrought gold and ivory fastened about them all; and many resemblances of the war, and those in several ways, and variety of contrivances, affording a most lively portraiture of itself. For there was to be seen a happy country laid waste, and entire squadrons of enemies slain; while some of them ran away, and some were carried into captivity; with walls of great altitude and magnitude overthrown and ruined by machines; with the strongest fortifications taken, and the walls of most populous cities upon the tops of hills seized on, and an army pouring itself within the walls; as also every place full of slaughter, and supplications of the enemies, when they were no longer able to lift up their hands in way of opposition. Fire also sent upon temples was here represented, and houses overthrown, and falling upon their owners: rivers also, after they came out of a large and melancholy desert, ran down, not into a land cultivated, nor as drink for men, or for cattle, but through a land still on fire upon every side; for the Jews related that such a thing they had undergone during this war. Now the workmanship of these representations was so magnificent and lively in the construction of the things, that it exhibited what had been done to such as did not see it, as if they had been there really present. On the top of every one of these pageants was placed the commander of the city that was taken, and the manner wherein he was taken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>These paintings have disappeared, but they likely influenced the composition of the historical reliefs carved on military <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagus" title="Sarcophagus">sarcophagi</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arch_of_Titus" title="Arch of Titus">Arch of Titus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trajan%27s_Column" title="Trajan's Column">Trajan's Column</a>. This evidence underscores the significance of landscape painting, which sometimes tended towards being perspective plans.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ranuccio_Bianchi_Bandinelli" title="Ranuccio Bianchi Bandinelli">Ranuccio</a> also describes the oldest painting to be found in Rome, in a tomb on the <a href="/wiki/Esquiline_Hill" title="Esquiline Hill">Esquiline Hill</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It describes a historical scene, on a clear background, painted in four superimposed sections. Several people are identified, such Marcus Fannius and Marcus Fabius. These are larger than the other figures ... In the second zone, to the left, is a city encircled with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crenellation" title="Crenellation">crenellated</a> walls, in front of which is a large warrior equipped with an oval buckler and a feathered helmet; near him is a man in a short tunic, armed with a spear...Around these two are smaller soldiers in short tunics, armed with spears...In the lower zone a battle is taking place, where a warrior with oval buckler and a feathered helmet is shown larger than the others, whose weapons allow to assume that these are probably Samnites.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This episode is difficult to pinpoint. One of Ranuccio's hypotheses is that it refers to a victory of the consul <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fabius_Maximus_Rullianus" title="Fabius Maximus Rullianus">Fabius Maximus Rullianus</a> during the second war against <a href="/wiki/Samnites" title="Samnites">Samnites</a> in 326 BC. The presentation of the figures with sizes proportional to their importance is typically Roman, and finds itself in plebeian reliefs. This painting is in the infancy of triumphal painting, and would have been accomplished by the beginning of the 3rd century BC to decorate the tomb.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sculpture">Sculpture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sculpture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Roman_sculpture" title="Roman sculpture">Roman sculpture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_portraiture" title="Roman portraiture">Roman portraiture</a></div>

<p>Early Roman art was influenced by the art of Greece and that of the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_art" title="Etruscan art">Etruscans</a>, themselves greatly influenced by their Greek trading partners. An Etruscan speciality was near life size tomb effigies in <a href="/wiki/Terracotta" title="Terracotta">terracotta</a>, usually lying on top of a <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagus" title="Sarcophagus">sarcophagus</a> lid propped up on one elbow in the pose of a diner in that period. As the expanding <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> began to conquer Greek territory, at first in Southern Italy and then the entire Hellenistic world except for the <a href="/wiki/Parthian_Empire" title="Parthian Empire">Parthian</a> far east, official and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_patrician" title="Roman patrician">patrician</a> sculpture became largely an extension of the Hellenistic style, from which specifically Roman elements are hard to disentangle, especially as so much Greek sculpture survives only in copies of the Roman period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> By the 2nd century BC, "most of the sculptors working in Rome" were Greek,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> often enslaved in conquests such as that of <a href="/wiki/Corinth" title="Corinth">Corinth</a> (146 BC), and sculptors continued to be mostly Greeks, often slaves, whose names are very rarely recorded. Vast numbers of Greek statues were imported to Rome, whether as booty or the result of extortion or commerce, and temples were often decorated with re-used Greek works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>A native Italian style can be seen in the tomb monuments of prosperous middle-class Romans, which very often featured portrait busts, and <a href="/wiki/Roman_portraiture" title="Roman portraiture">portraiture</a> is arguably the main strength of Roman sculpture. There are no survivals from the tradition of masks of ancestors that were worn in processions at the funerals of the great families and otherwise displayed in the home, but many of the busts that survive must represent ancestral figures, perhaps from the large family tombs like the <a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_the_Scipios" title="Tomb of the Scipios">Tomb of the Scipios</a> or the later mausolea outside the city. The famous bronze head supposedly of <a href="/wiki/Lucius_Junius_Brutus" title="Lucius Junius Brutus">Lucius Junius Brutus</a> is very variously dated, but taken as a very rare survival of <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italic</a> style under the Republic, in the preferred medium of bronze.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Similarly stern and forceful heads are seen in the coins of the consuls, and in the Imperial period coins as well as busts sent around the Empire to be placed in the <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilicas</a> of provincial cities were the main visual form of imperial propaganda; even <a href="/wiki/Londinium" title="Londinium">Londinium</a> had a near-colossal statue of <a href="/wiki/Nero" title="Nero">Nero</a>, though far smaller than the 30 metre high <a href="/wiki/Colossus_of_Nero" title="Colossus of Nero">Colossus of Nero</a> in Rome, now lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_Eurysaces_the_Baker" title="Tomb of Eurysaces the Baker">Tomb of Eurysaces the Baker</a>, a successful <a href="/wiki/Freedman" title="Freedman">freedman</a> (c. 50-20 BC) has a <a href="/wiki/Frieze" title="Frieze">frieze</a> that is an unusually large example of the "plebeian" style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Imperial portraiture was initially Hellenized and highly idealized, as in the <a href="/wiki/Blacas_Cameo" title="Blacas Cameo">Blacas Cameo</a> and other portraits of <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>.</p>

<p>The Romans did not generally attempt to compete with free-standing Greek works of heroic exploits from history or mythology, but from early on produced historical works in <a href="/wiki/Relief" title="Relief">relief</a>, culminating in the great <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_triumphal_column" title="Roman triumphal column">Roman triumphal columns</a> with continuous narrative reliefs winding around them, of which those commemorating <a href="/wiki/Trajan%27s_Column" title="Trajan's Column">Trajan</a> (113 CE) and <a href="/wiki/Column_of_Marcus_Aurelius" title="Column of Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a> (by 193) survive in Rome, where the <a href="/wiki/Ara_Pacis" title="Ara Pacis">Ara Pacis</a> ("Altar of Peace", 13 BC) represents the official Greco-Roman style at its most classical and refined, and the <a href="/wiki/Sperlonga_sculptures" title="Sperlonga sculptures">Sperlonga sculptures</a> it at its most baroque. Some late Roman public sculptures developed a massive, simplified style that sometimes anticipates Soviet <a href="/wiki/Socialist_realism" title="Socialist realism">socialist realism</a>. Among other major examples are the earlier re-used reliefs on the <a href="/wiki/Arch_of_Constantine" title="Arch of Constantine">Arch of Constantine</a> and the base of the <a href="/wiki/Column_of_Antoninus_Pius" title="Column of Antoninus Pius">Column of Antoninus Pius</a> (161),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Campana_relief" title="Campana relief">Campana reliefs</a> were cheaper pottery versions of marble reliefs and the taste for relief was from the imperial period expanded to the sarcophagus.</p>
<p>All forms of luxury small sculpture continued to be patronized, and quality could be extremely high, as in the silver <a href="/wiki/Warren_Cup" title="Warren Cup">Warren Cup</a>, glass <a href="/wiki/Lycurgus_Cup" title="Lycurgus Cup">Lycurgus Cup</a>, and large cameos like the <a href="/wiki/Gemma_Augustea" title="Gemma Augustea">Gemma Augustea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gonzaga_Cameo" title="Gonzaga Cameo">Gonzaga Cameo</a> and the "<a href="/wiki/Great_Cameo_of_France" title="Great Cameo of France">Great Cameo of France</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> For a much wider section of the population, moulded relief decoration of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman_pottery" title="Ancient Roman pottery">pottery vessels</a> and small figurines were produced in great quantity and often considerable quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>After moving through a late 2nd century "baroque" phase,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> in the 3rd century, Roman art largely abandoned, or simply became unable to produce, sculpture in the classical tradition, a change whose causes remain much discussed. Even the most important imperial monuments now showed stumpy, large-eyed figures in a harsh frontal style, in simple compositions emphasizing power at the expense of grace. The contrast is famously illustrated in the <a href="/wiki/Arch_of_Constantine" title="Arch of Constantine">Arch of Constantine</a> of 315 in Rome, which combines sections in the new style with <a href="/wiki/Roundel" title="Roundel">roundels</a> in the earlier full Greco-Roman style taken from elsewhere, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Portrait_of_the_Four_Tetrarchs" title="Portrait of the Four Tetrarchs">Four Tetrarchs</a></i> (c. 305) from the new capital of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, now in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Kitzinger" title="Ernst Kitzinger">Ernst Kitzinger</a> found in both monuments the same "stubby proportions, angular movements, an ordering of parts through symmetry and repetition and a rendering of features and drapery folds through incisions rather than modelling... The hallmark of the style wherever it appears consists of an emphatic hardness, heaviness and angularity – in short, an almost complete rejection of the classical tradition".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>This revolution in style shortly preceded the period in which <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> was adopted by the Roman state and the great majority of the people, leading to the end of large religious sculpture, with large statues now only used for emperors, as in the famous fragments of a colossal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acrolithic" title="Acrolithic">acrolithic</a> <a href="/wiki/Colossus_of_Constantine" title="Colossus of Constantine">statue of Constantine</a>, and the 4th or 5th century <a href="/wiki/Colossus_of_Barletta" title="Colossus of Barletta">Colossus of Barletta</a>. However rich Christians continued to commission reliefs for sarcophagi, as in the <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagus_of_Junius_Bassus" title="Sarcophagus of Junius Bassus">Sarcophagus of Junius Bassus</a>, and very small sculpture, especially in ivory, was continued by Christians, building on the style of the <a href="/wiki/Consular_diptych" title="Consular diptych">consular diptych</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>

<p>Traditional Roman sculpture is divided into five categories: portraiture, historical relief, funerary reliefs, sarcophagi, and copies of ancient Greek works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Contrary to the belief of early archaeologists, many of these sculptures were large <a href="/wiki/Polychrome" title="Polychrome">polychrome</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terra-cotta" title="Terra-cotta">terra-cotta</a> images, such as the Apollo of Veii (Villa Givlia, Rome), but the painted surface of many of them has worn away with time.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Narrative_reliefs">Narrative reliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Narrative reliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While Greek sculptors traditionally illustrated military exploits through the use of mythological allegory, the Romans used a more documentary style. Roman reliefs of battle scenes, like those on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Column_of_Trajan" title="Column of Trajan">Column of Trajan</a>, were created for the glorification of Roman might, but also provide first-hand representation of military costumes and military equipment. Trajan's column records the various <a href="/wiki/Trajan%27s_Dacian_Wars" title="Trajan's Dacian Wars">Dacian wars</a> conducted by <a href="/wiki/Trajan" title="Trajan">Trajan</a> in what is modern day <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>. It is the foremost example of Roman historical relief and one of the great artistic treasures of the ancient world. This unprecedented achievement, over 650 foot of spiraling length, presents not just realistically rendered individuals (over 2,500 of them), but landscapes, animals, ships, and other elements in a continuous visual history – in effect an ancient precursor of a documentary movie. It survived destruction when it was adapted as a base for Christian sculpture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piper.2C_p._256_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Piper.2C_p._256-50">[50]</a></sup> During the Christian era after 300 AD, the decoration of door panels and sarcophagi continued but full-sized sculpture died out and did not appear to be an important element in early churches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piper.2C_p._261_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Piper.2C_p._261-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Minor_arts">Minor arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Minor arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cameo_August_BM_Gem3577.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3200" data-file-width="2400" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Cameo_August_BM_Gem3577.jpg/220px-Cameo_August_BM_Gem3577.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Cameo_August_BM_Gem3577.jpg/330px-Cameo_August_BM_Gem3577.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Cameo_August_BM_Gem3577.jpg/440px-Cameo_August_BM_Gem3577.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cameo_August_BM_Gem3577.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>
<p>The Romans inherited a tradition of art in a wide range of the so-called "minor arts" or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decorative_art" title="Decorative art">decorative art</a>. Most of these flourished most impressively at the luxury level, but large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Terracotta" title="Terracotta">terracotta</a> figurines, both religious and secular, continued to be produced cheaply, as well as some larger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Campana_relief" title="Campana relief">Campana reliefs</a> in terracotta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Roman art did not use vase-painting in the way of the ancient Greeks, but vessels in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman_pottery" title="Ancient Roman pottery">Ancient Roman pottery</a> were often stylishly decorated in moulded relief.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Producers of the millions of small <a href="/wiki/Oil_lamp" title="Oil lamp">oil lamps</a> sold seem to have relied on attractive decoration to beat competitors and every subject of Roman art except landscape and portraiture is found on them in miniature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Luxury arts included fancy <a href="/wiki/Roman_glass" title="Roman glass">Roman glass</a> in a great range of techniques, many smaller types of which were probably affordable to a good proportion of the Roman public. This was certainly not the case for the most extravagant types of glass, such as the <a href="/wiki/Cage_cup" title="Cage cup">cage cups</a> or <i>diatreta</i>, of which the <a href="/wiki/Lycurgus_Cup" title="Lycurgus Cup">Lycurgus Cup</a> in the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> is a near-unique figurative example in glass that changes colour when seen with light passing through it. The Augustan <a href="/wiki/Portland_Vase" title="Portland Vase">Portland Vase</a> is the masterpiece of Roman <a href="/wiki/Cameo_glass" title="Cameo glass">cameo glass</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> and imitated the style of the large <a href="/wiki/Engraved_gem" title="Engraved gem">engraved gems</a> (<a href="/wiki/Blacas_Cameo" title="Blacas Cameo">Blacas Cameo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gemma_Augustea" title="Gemma Augustea">Gemma Augustea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Cameo_of_France" title="Great Cameo of France">Great Cameo of France</a>) and other <a href="/wiki/Hardstone_carving" title="Hardstone carving">hardstone carvings</a> that were also most popular around this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">Mosaic</a> was a minor art, though often on a very large scale, until the very end of the period, when late-4th-century Christians began to use it for large religious images on walls in their new large churches; in earlier Roman art mosaic was mainly used for floors, curved ceilings, and inside and outside walls that were going to get wet. The famous copy of a Hellenistic painting in the <i><a href="/wiki/Alexander_Mosaic" title="Alexander Mosaic">Alexander Mosaic</a></i> in Naples was originally placed in a floor in <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>; this is much higher quality work than most Roman mosaic, though very fine panels, often of <a href="/wiki/Still_life" title="Still life">still life</a> subjects in small or <a href="/wiki/Micromosaic" title="Micromosaic">micromosaic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tesserae" title="Tesserae">tesserae</a> have also survived. The Romans distinguished between normal <i><a href="/wiki/Opus_tessellatum" title="Opus tessellatum">opus tessellatum</a></i> with <i>tesserae</i> mostly over 4 mm across, which was laid down on site, and finer <i><a href="/wiki/Opus_vermiculatum" title="Opus vermiculatum">opus vermiculatum</a></i> for small panels, which is thought to have been produced offsite in a workshop, and brought to the site as a finished panel. The latter was a Hellenistic genre which is found in Italy between about 100 BC and 100 AD. Most signed mosaics have Greek names, suggesting the artists remained mostly Greek, though probably often slaves trained up in workshops. The late 2nd century BC <a href="/wiki/Nile_mosaic_of_Palestrina" title="Nile mosaic of Palestrina">Nile mosaic of Palestrina</a> is a very large example of the popular genre of <a href="/wiki/Nilotic_landscape" title="Nilotic landscape">Nilotic landscape</a>, while the 4th century <a href="/wiki/Gladiator_Mosaic" title="Gladiator Mosaic">Gladiator Mosaic</a> in Rome shows several large figures in combat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Metalwork was highly developed, and clearly an essential part of the homes of the rich, who dined off silver, while often drinking from glass, and had elaborate cast fittings on their furniture, jewellery, and small figurines. A number of important <a href="/wiki/Hoard" title="Hoard">hoards</a> found in the last 200 years, mostly from the more violent edges of the late empire, have given us a much clearer idea of Roman silver plate. The <a href="/wiki/Mildenhall_Treasure" title="Mildenhall Treasure">Mildenhall Treasure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hoxne_Hoard" title="Hoxne Hoard">Hoxne Hoard</a> are both from <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a> in England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> There are few survivals of upmarket <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman_furniture" title="Ancient Roman furniture">ancient Roman furniture</a>, but these show refined and elegant design and execution.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coins_and_medals">Coins and medals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Coins and medals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Few <a href="/wiki/Roman_currency" title="Roman currency">Roman coins</a> reach the artistic peaks of the best Greek coins, but they survive in vast numbers and their iconography and inscriptions form a crucial source for the study of Roman history, and the development of imperial iconography, as well as containing many fine examples of portraiture. They penetrated to the rural population of the whole Empire and beyond, with barbarians on the fringes of the Empire making their own copies. In the Empire medallions in precious metals began to be produced in small editions as imperial gifts, which are similar to coins, though larger and usually finer in execution. Images in coins initially followed Greek styles, with gods and symbols, but in the death throes of the Republic first <a href="/wiki/Pompey" title="Pompey">Pompey</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> appeared on coins, and portraits of the emperor or members of his family became standard on imperial coinage. The inscriptions were used for propaganda, and in the later Empire the army joined the emperor as the beneficiary.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_architecture" title="Roman architecture">Roman architecture</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aqueduct_of_Segovia_08.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3456" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Aqueduct_of_Segovia_08.jpg/220px-Aqueduct_of_Segovia_08.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Aqueduct_of_Segovia_08.jpg/330px-Aqueduct_of_Segovia_08.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Aqueduct_of_Segovia_08.jpg/440px-Aqueduct_of_Segovia_08.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Teatro_romano_de_M%C3%A9rida_(2).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1829" data-file-width="3863" height="104" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Teatro_romano_de_M%C3%A9rida_%282%29.jpg/220px-Teatro_romano_de_M%C3%A9rida_%282%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Teatro_romano_de_M%C3%A9rida_%282%29.jpg/330px-Teatro_romano_de_M%C3%A9rida_%282%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Teatro_romano_de_M%C3%A9rida_%282%29.jpg/440px-Teatro_romano_de_M%C3%A9rida_%282%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Roman theatre in <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9rida,_Spain" title="Mérida, Spain">Mérida</a></div>

</div>
<p>It was in the area of architecture that Roman art produced its greatest innovations. Because the Roman Empire extended over so great of an area and included so many urbanized areas, Roman engineers developed methods for city building on a grand scale, including the use of <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>. Massive buildings like the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Pantheon</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Colosseum" title="Colosseum">Colosseum</a> could never have been constructed with previous materials and methods. Though concrete had been invented a thousand years earlier in the Near East, the Romans extended its use from fortifications to their most impressive buildings and monuments, capitalizing on the material’s strength and low cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janson.2C_p._160_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Janson.2C_p._160-58">[58]</a></sup> The concrete core was covered with a plaster, brick, stone, or marble veneer, and decorative polychrome and gold-gilded sculpture was often added to produce a dazzling effect of power and wealth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janson.2C_p._160_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-Janson.2C_p._160-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of these methods, Roman architecture is legendary for the durability of its construction; with many buildings still standing, and some still in use, mostly buildings converted to churches during the Christian era. Many ruins, however, have been stripped of their marble veneer and are left with their concrete core exposed, thus appearing somewhat reduced in size and grandeur from their original appearance, such as with the Basilica of Constantine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janson.2C_p._165_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Janson.2C_p._165-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the Republican era, Roman architecture combined Greek and Etruscan elements, and produced innovations such as the round temple and the curved arch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> As Roman power grew in the early empire, the first emperors inaugurated wholesale leveling of slums to build grand palaces on the Palatine Hill and nearby areas, which required advances in engineering methods and large scale design. Roman buildings were then built in the commercial, political, and social grouping known as a forum, that of Julius Caesar being the first and several added later, with the Forum Romanum being the most famous. The greatest arena in the Roman world, the <a href="/wiki/Colosseum" title="Colosseum">Colosseum</a>, was completed around 80 AD at the far end of that forum. It held over 50,000 spectators, had retractable fabric coverings for shade, and could stage massive spectacles including huge gladiatorial contests and mock naval battles. This masterpiece of Roman architecture epitomizes Roman engineering efficiency and incorporates all three architectural orders – Doric, Ionic, and Corinthian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janson.2C_p._162_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Janson.2C_p._162-61">[61]</a></sup> Less celebrated but just as important if not more so for most Roman citizens, was the five-story <i>insula</i> or city block, the Roman equivalent of an apartment building, which housed tens of thousands of Romans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was during the reign of Trajan (98–117 AD.) and Hadrian (117–138 AD) that the Roman Empire reached its greatest extent and that Rome itself was at the peak of its artistic glory – achieved through massive building programs of monuments, meeting houses, gardens, aqueducts, baths, palaces, pavilions, sarcophagi, and temples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piper.2C_p._256_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-Piper.2C_p._256-50">[50]</a></sup> The Roman use of the <a href="/wiki/Arch" title="Arch">arch</a>, the use of <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> building methods, the use of the <a href="/wiki/Dome" title="Dome">dome</a> all permitted construction of vaulted ceilings and enabled the building of these public spaces and complexes, including the palaces, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_baths" title="Public baths">public baths</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilicas" title="Basilicas">basilicas</a> of the "Golden Age" of the empire. Outstanding examples of dome construction include the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Pantheon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Baths_of_Diocletian" title="Baths of Diocletian">Baths of Diocletian</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Baths_of_Caracalla" title="Baths of Caracalla">Baths of Caracalla</a>. The Pantheon (dedicated to all the planetary gods) is the best preserved temple of ancient times with an intact ceiling featuring an open "eye" in the center. The height of the ceiling exactly equals the interior diameter of the building, creating an enclosure that could contain giant sphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janson.2C_p._165_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-Janson.2C_p._165-59">[59]</a></sup> These grand buildings later served as inspirational models for architects of the Italian Renaissance, such as <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Brunelleschi</a>. By the age of Constantine (306-337 AD), the last great building programs in Rome took place, including the erection of the <a href="/wiki/Arch_of_Constantine" title="Arch of Constantine">Arch of Constantine</a> built near the <a href="/wiki/Colosseum" title="Colosseum">Colosseum</a>, which recycled some stone work from the forum nearby, to produce an eclectic mix of styles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piper.2C_p._260_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Piper.2C_p._260-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_aqueducts" title="Roman aqueducts">Roman aqueducts</a>, also based on the arch, were commonplace in the empire and essential transporters of water to large urban areas. Their standing masonry remains are especially impressive, such as the <a href="/wiki/Pont_du_Gard" title="Pont du Gard">Pont du Gard</a> (featuring three tiers of arches) and the <a href="/wiki/Aqueduct_of_Segovia" title="Aqueduct of Segovia">aqueduct of Segovia</a>, serving as mute testimony to their quality of their design and construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janson.2C_p._162_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-Janson.2C_p._162-61">[61]</a></sup></p>

<li id="cite_note-howells_2015_p7-22"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-howells_2015_p7_22-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-howells_2015_p7_22-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">Daniel Thomas Howells (2015). "<a class="external text" href="http://www.britishmuseum.org/pdf/Late_Antique_Gold_Glass_online.pdf" rel="nofollow">A Catalogue of the Late Antique Gold Glass in the British Museum (PDF).</a>" London: the British Museum (Arts and Humanities Research Council). Accessed 2 October 2016, p. 7: "<i>Other important contributions to scholarship included the publication of an extensive summary of gold glass scholarship under the entry ‘Fonds de coupes’ in Fernand Cabrol and Henri Leclercq’s comprehensive Dictionnaire d’archéologie chrétienne et de liturgie in 1923. Leclercq updated Vopel’s catalogue, recording 512 gold glasses considered to be genuine, and developed a typological series consisting of eleven iconographic subjects: biblical subjects; Christ and the saints; various legends; inscriptions; pagan deities; secular subjects; male portraits; female portraits; portraits of couples and families; animals; and Jewish symbols. In a 1926 article devoted to the brushed technique gold glass known as the Brescia medallion (Pl. 1), Fernand de Mély challenged the deeply ingrained opinion of Garrucci and Vopel that all examples of brushed technique gold glass were in fact forgeries. The following year, de Mély’s hypothesis was supported and further elaborated upon in two articles by different scholars. A case for the Brescia medallion’s authenticity was argued for, not on the basis of its iconographic and orthographic similarity with pieces from Rome (a key reason for Garrucci’s dismissal), but instead for its close similarity to the Fayoum mummy portraits from Egypt. Indeed, this comparison was given further credence by Walter Crum’s assertion that the Greek inscription on the medallion was written in the Alexandrian dialect of Egypt. De Mély noted that the medallion and its inscription had been reported as early as 1725, far too early for the idiosyncrasies of Graeco-Egyptian word endings to have been understood by forgers.</i>" "<i>Comparing the iconography of the Brescia medallion with other more closely dated objects from Egypt, Hayford Peirce then proposed that brushed technique medallions were produced in the early 3rd century, whilst de Mély himself advocated a more general 3rd-century date. With the authenticity of the medallion more firmly established, Joseph Breck was prepared to propose a late 3rd to early 4th century date for all of the brushed technique cobalt blue-backed portrait medallions, some of which also had Greek inscriptions in the Alexandrian dialect. Although considered genuine by the majority of scholars by this point, the unequivocal authenticity of these glasses was not fully established until 1941 when Gerhart Ladner discovered and published a photograph of one such medallion still in situ, where it remains to this day, impressed into the plaster sealing in an individual loculus in the Catacomb of Panfilo in Rome (Pl. 2). Shortly after in 1942, Morey used the phrase ‘brushed technique’ to categorize this gold glass type, the iconography being produced through a series of small incisions undertaken with a gem cutter’s precision and lending themselves to a chiaroscuro-like effect similar to that of a fine steel engraving simulating brush strokes.</i>"</span></li>
