<p><b>Ancient Roman temples</b> were among the most important buildings in <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_ancient_Rome" title="Culture of ancient Rome">Roman culture</a>, and some of the richest buildings in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Architecture_of_ancient_Rome" title="Architecture of ancient Rome">Roman architecture</a>, though only a few survive in any sort of complete state. Today they remain "the most obvious symbol of Roman architecture".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Summerson_1980.2C_25_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Summerson_1980.2C_25-1">[1]</a></sup> Their construction and maintenance was a major part of <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_ancient_Rome" title="Religion in ancient Rome">ancient Roman religion</a>, and all towns of any importance had at least one main temple, as well as smaller shrines. The main room <i>(<a href="/wiki/Cella" title="Cella">cella</a>)</i> housed the <a href="/wiki/Cult_(religious_practice)" title="Cult (religious practice)">cult</a> image of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Roman_deities" title="List of Roman deities">deity</a> to whom the temple was <a href="/wiki/Votum" title="Votum">dedicated</a>, and often a small altar for incense or <a href="/wiki/Libation" title="Libation">libations</a>. Behind the cella was a room or rooms used by temple attendants for storage of equipment and offerings. The ordinary worshipper rarely entered the cella, and most public ceremonies were performed outside, on the portico, with a crowd gathered in the temple precinct.</p>
<p>The most common architectural plan had a rectangular temple raised on a high podium, with a clear front with a portico at the top of steps, and a triangular <a href="/wiki/Pediment" title="Pediment">pediment</a> above columns. The sides and rear of the building had much less architectural emphasis, and typically no entrances. There were also circular plans, generally with columns all round, and outside Italy there were many compromises with traditional local styles. The Roman form of temple developed initially from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etruscan_temple" title="Etruscan temple">Etruscan temples</a>, themselves influenced by the Greeks, with subsequent heavy direct influence from Greece.</p>
<p>Public religious ceremonies of the official Roman religion took place outdoors, and not within the temple building. Some ceremonies were processions that started at, visited, or ended with a temple or shrine, where a ritual object might be stored and brought out for use, or where an offering would be deposited. <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_ancient_Rome#Sacrifice" title="Religion in ancient Rome">Sacrifices</a>, chiefly <a href="/wiki/Animal_sacrifice" title="Animal sacrifice">of animals</a>, would take place at an open-air altar within the <i>templum</i>. Especially under the Empire, exotic foreign cults gained followers in Rome, and were the local religions in large parts of the expanded Empire. These often had very different practices, some preferring underground places of worship, while others, like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Christians" title="Early Christians">Early Christians</a>, worshipped in houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some remains of many Roman temples survive, above all in Rome itself, but the relatively few near-complete examples were nearly all converted to Christian churches (and sometimes subsequently to <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">mosques</a>), usually a considerable time after the initial <a href="/wiki/Constantine_the_Great_and_Christianity" title="Constantine the Great and Christianity">triumph of Christianity under Constantine</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Decline_of_Greco-Roman_polytheism" title="Decline of Greco-Roman polytheism">decline of Roman religion</a> was relatively slow, and the temples themselves were not appropriated by the government until a decree of the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Honorius_(emperor)" title="Honorius (emperor)">Honorius</a> in 415. <a href="/wiki/Santi_Cosma_e_Damiano" title="Santi Cosma e Damiano">Santi Cosma e Damiano</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Forum" title="Roman Forum">Roman Forum</a>, originally the Temple of <a href="/wiki/Romulus" title="Romulus">Romulus</a>, was not dedicated as a church until 527. The best known is the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Pantheon, Rome</a>, which is however highly untypical, being a very large circular temple with a magnificent concrete roof, behind a conventional portico front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terms">Terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_temple&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The English word "temple" derives from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_ancient_Roman_religion#templum" title="Glossary of ancient Roman religion">templum</a></i>, which was originally not the building itself, but a sacred space surveyed and plotted ritually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The Roman architect <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a> always uses the word <i>templum</i> to refer to the sacred precinct, and not to the building. The more common Latin words for a temple or shrine were <i><a href="/wiki/Sacellum" title="Sacellum">sacellum</a></i> (a small shrine or chapel), <i><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_ancient_Roman_religion#aedes" title="Glossary of ancient Roman religion">aedes</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_ancient_Roman_religion#delubrum" title="Glossary of ancient Roman religion">delubrum</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_ancient_Roman_religion#fanum" title="Glossary of ancient Roman religion">fanum</a></i> (in this article, the English word "temple" refers to any of these buildings, and the Latin <i>templum</i> to the sacred precinct).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_temple&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The form of the Roman temple was mainly derived from the <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_architecture" title="Etruscan architecture">Etruscan</a> model, but in the late Republic there was a switch to using Greek classical and Hellenistic styles, without much change in the key features of the form. The Etruscans were a people of northern Italy, whose civilization was at its peak in the seventh century BC. The Etruscans were already influenced by early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_architecture" title="Greek architecture">Greek architecture</a>, so Roman temples were distinctive but with both Etruscan and Greek features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbell_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Surviving temples (both Greek and Roman) lack the extensive painted statuary that decorated the rooflines, and the elaborate revetments and <a href="/wiki/Antefix" title="Antefix">antefixes</a>, in colourful terracotta in earlier examples, that enlivened the <a href="/wiki/Entablature" title="Entablature">entablature</a>.</p>
<p>Etruscan and Roman temples emphasised the front of the building, which followed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_temple" title="Greek temple">Greek temple</a> models and typically consisted of wide steps leading to a <a href="/wiki/Portico" title="Portico">portico</a> with columns, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pronaos" title="Pronaos">pronaos</a>, and usually a triangular <a href="/wiki/Pediment" title="Pediment">pediment</a> above, which was filled with statuary in the most grand examples; this was as often in <a href="/wiki/Terracotta" title="Terracotta">terracotta</a> as stone, and no examples have survived except as fragments. Especially in the earlier periods, further statuary might be placed on the roof, and the <a href="/wiki/Entablature" title="Entablature">entablature</a> decorated with <a href="/wiki/Antefix" title="Antefix">antefixes</a> and other elements, all of this being brightly painted. However, unlike the Greek models, which generally gave equal treatment to all sides of the temple, which could be viewed and approached from all directions, the side and rear walls of Roman temples might be largely undecorated (as in the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Pantheon, Rome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_temple_of_Vic" title="Roman temple of Vic">Vic</a>), inaccessible by steps (as in the <a href="/wiki/Maison_Carr%C3%A9e" title="Maison Carrée">Maison Carrée</a> and Vic), and even back on to other buildings. As in the Maison Carrée, columns at the side might be half-columns, emerging from ("engaged with" in architectural terminology) the wall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The platform on which the temple sat was typically raised higher in Etruscan and Roman examples than Greek, with up to ten, twelve or more steps rather than the three typical in Greek temples; the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Claudius" title="Temple of Claudius">Temple of Claudius</a> was raised twenty steps. These steps were normally only at the front, and typically not the whole width of that. It might or might not be possible to walk around the temple exterior inside (<a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Hadrian" title="Temple of Hadrian">Temple of Hadrian</a>) or outside the <a href="/wiki/Colonnade" title="Colonnade">colonnade</a>, or at least down the sides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The description of the Greek models used here is a generalization of classical Greek ideals, and later Hellenistic buildings often do not reflect them. For example, the "Temple of Dionysus" on the terrace by the theatre at <a href="/wiki/Pergamon" title="Pergamon">Pergamon</a> (Ionic, 2nd century BC, on a hillside), had many steps in front, and no columns beyond the portico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Parthenon" title="Parthenon">Parthenon</a>, also approached up a hill, probably had many wide steps at the approach to the main front, followed by a flat area before the final few steps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the eclipse of the Etruscan models, the Greek <a href="/wiki/Classical_order" title="Classical order">classical orders</a> in all their details were closely followed in the façades of Roman temples, as in other prestigious buildings, with the direct adoption of Greek models apparently beginning around 200 BC, under the late Republic. But the distinctive differences in the general arrangement of temples between the Etruscan-Roman style and the Greek, as outlined above, were retained. However the idealized proportions between the different elements in the orders set out by the only significant Roman writer on architecture to survive, <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a>, and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a> writers, do not reflect actual Roman practice, which could be very variable, though always aiming at balance and harmony. Following a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic" title="Hellenistic">Hellenistic</a> trend, the <a href="/wiki/Corinthian_order" title="Corinthian order">Corinthian order</a> and its variant the <a href="/wiki/Composite_order" title="Composite order">Composite order</a> were most common in surviving Roman temples, but for small temples like <a href="/wiki/Roman_temple_of_Alc%C3%A1ntara" title="Roman temple of Alcántara">that at Alcántara</a>, a simple <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_order" title="Tuscan order">Tuscan order</a> could be used. Vitruvius does not recognise the Composite or Tuscan orders in his writings, which Renaissance writers formalized from observing surviving buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>The front of the temple typically carried an inscription saying who had built it, cut into the stone with a "V" section. This was filled with brighly coloured paint, usually scarlet or <a href="/wiki/Vermilion" title="Vermilion">vermilion</a>. In major imperial monuments the letters were cast in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_(metal)" title="Lead (metal)">lead</a> and held in by pegs, then also painted or <a href="/wiki/Gilding" title="Gilding">gilded</a>. These have usually long vanished, but archaeologists can generally reconstruct them from the peg-holes, and some have been re-created and set in place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>There was considerable local variation in style, as Roman architects often tried to incorporate elements the population expected in its <a href="/wiki/Sacred_architecture" title="Sacred architecture">sacred architecture</a>. This was especially the case in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a>, where different traditions of large stone temples were already millennia old. The <a href="/wiki/Romano-Celtic_temple" title="Romano-Celtic temple">Romano-Celtic temple</a> was a simple style, usually with little use of stone, for small temples found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Empire" title="Western Empire">Western Empire</a>, and by far the most common type in <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a>, where they were usually square, with an <a href="/wiki/Ambulatory" title="Ambulatory">ambulatory</a>. It often lacked any of the distinctive classical features, and may have had considerable continuity with pre-Roman temples of the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_polytheism" title="Celtic polytheism">Celtic religion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Circular_plans">Circular plans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_temple&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Circular plans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_round_temple_(Temple_of_Venus),_Baalbek,_Holy_Land,_(i.e.,_Ba%27labakk,_Lebanon)-LCCN2002724962.tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2770" data-file-width="3728" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/The_round_temple_%28Temple_of_Venus%29%2C_Baalbek%2C_Holy_Land%2C_%28i.e.%2C_Ba%27labakk%2C_Lebanon%29-LCCN2002724962.tif/lossy-page1-220px-The_round_temple_%28Temple_of_Venus%29%2C_Baalbek%2C_Holy_Land%2C_%28i.e.%2C_Ba%27labakk%2C_Lebanon%29-LCCN2002724962.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/The_round_temple_%28Temple_of_Venus%29%2C_Baalbek%2C_Holy_Land%2C_%28i.e.%2C_Ba%27labakk%2C_Lebanon%29-LCCN2002724962.tif/lossy-page1-330px-The_round_temple_%28Temple_of_Venus%29%2C_Baalbek%2C_Holy_Land%2C_%28i.e.%2C_Ba%27labakk%2C_Lebanon%29-LCCN2002724962.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/The_round_temple_%28Temple_of_Venus%29%2C_Baalbek%2C_Holy_Land%2C_%28i.e.%2C_Ba%27labakk%2C_Lebanon%29-LCCN2002724962.tif/lossy-page1-440px-The_round_temple_%28Temple_of_Venus%29%2C_Baalbek%2C_Holy_Land%2C_%28i.e.%2C_Ba%27labakk%2C_Lebanon%29-LCCN2002724962.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_round_temple_(Temple_of_Venus),_Baalbek,_Holy_Land,_(i.e.,_Ba%27labakk,_Lebanon)-LCCN2002724962.tif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Romano-Celtic temples were often circular, and circular temples of various kinds were built by the Romans. Greek models were available in <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tholos_(building)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tholos (building) (page does not exist)">tholos</a></i> shrines and some <a href="/wiki/Tholos_(Ancient_Rome)" title="Tholos (Ancient Rome)">other buildings</a>, as assembly halls and various other functions. Temples of the goddess <a href="/wiki/Vesta_(mythology)" title="Vesta (mythology)">Vesta</a>, which were usually small, typically had this shape, as in those at Rome and Tivoli (see list), which survive in part. Like the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Hercules_Victor" title="Temple of Hercules Victor">Temple of Hercules Victor</a> in Rome, which was perhaps by a Greek architect, these survivors had an unbroken colonnade encircling the building, and a low, Greek-style podium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Different formulae were followed in the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Pantheon, Rome</a> and a small temple at <a href="/wiki/Baalbek" title="Baalbek">Baalbek</a> (usually called the "Temple of Venus"), where the door is behind a full portico, though very different ways of doing this are used. In the Pantheon only the portico has columns, and the "thoroughly uncomfortable" exterior meeting of the portico and circular cella are often criticised. At Baalbek a wide portico with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broken_pediment" title="Broken pediment">broken pediment</a> is matched by four other columns round the building, with the <a href="/wiki/Architrave" title="Architrave">architrave</a> in scooped curving sections, each ending in a projection supported by a column.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>At <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Praeneste" title="Praeneste">Praeneste</a> (modern Palestrina) near Rome, a huge pilgrimage complex of the 1st century BC led visitors up several levels with large buildings on a steep hillside, before they eventually reached the sanctuary itself, a much smaller circular building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Caesareum">Caesareum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_temple&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Caesareum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vienne_(avril_2009)_036.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Vienne_%28avril_2009%29_036.jpg/220px-Vienne_%28avril_2009%29_036.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Vienne_%28avril_2009%29_036.jpg/330px-Vienne_%28avril_2009%29_036.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Vienne_%28avril_2009%29_036.jpg/440px-Vienne_%28avril_2009%29_036.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vienne_(avril_2009)_036.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Temple of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_cult_(ancient_Rome)" title="Imperial cult (ancient Rome)">Imperial cult</a> at <a href="/wiki/Vienne,_Is%C3%A8re" title="Vienne, Isère">Vienne</a>, from behind</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A <i>caesareum</i> was a temple devoted to the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_cult_(ancient_Rome)" title="Imperial cult (ancient Rome)">Imperial cult</a>. Caesarea were located throughout the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, and often funded by the imperial government, tending to replace state spending on new temples to other gods, and becoming the main or only large temple in new Roman towns in the provinces. This was the case at <a href="/wiki/Roman_Temple_of_%C3%89vora" title="Roman Temple of Évora">Évora</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vienne,_Is%C3%A8re" title="Vienne, Isère">Vienne</a> and <a href="/wiki/N%C3%AEmes" title="Nîmes">Nîmes</a>, which were all expanded by the Romans as <a href="/wiki/Colonia_(Roman)" title="Colonia (Roman)"><i>coloniae</i></a> from Celtic <a href="/wiki/Oppidum" title="Oppidum">oppida</a> soon after their conquest. Imperial temples paid for by the government usually used conventional Roman styles all over the empire, regardless of the local styles seen in smaller temples. In newly planned Roman cities the temple was normally centrally placed at one end of the forum, often facing the basilica at the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the city of Rome, a caesareum was located within the religious precinct of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arval_Brothers" title="Arval Brothers">Arval Brothers</a>. In 1570, it was documented as still containing nine statues of <a href="/wiki/Roman_emperor" title="Roman emperor">Roman emperors</a> in architectural niches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Most of the earlier emperors had their own very large temples in Rome,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> but a faltering economy meant that the building of new imperial temples mostly ceased after the reign of <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a> (d. 180), though the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temple_of_Romulus" title="Temple of Romulus">Temple of Romulus</a> on the Roman Forum was built and dedicated by the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Maxentius" title="Maxentius">Maxentius</a> to his son <a href="/wiki/Valerius_Romulus" title="Valerius Romulus">Valerius Romulus</a>, who died in childhood in 309 and was deified.</p>
<p>One of the earliest and most prominent of the caesarea was the <a href="/wiki/Caesareum_of_Alexandria" title="Caesareum of Alexandria">Caesareum of Alexandria</a>, located on the harbor. It was begun by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleopatra_VII" title="Cleopatra VII">Cleopatra VII</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_dynasty" title="Ptolemaic dynasty">Ptolemaic dynasty</a>, the last <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">pharaoh</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>, to honour her dead lover <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>, then converted by Augustus to his own cult. During the 4th century, after the Empire had come under Christian rule, it was converted to a church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_temple&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:St_Martin-in-the-Fields,_July_2011.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1590" data-file-width="1290" height="271" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/St_Martin-in-the-Fields%2C_July_2011.jpg/220px-St_Martin-in-the-Fields%2C_July_2011.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/St_Martin-in-the-Fields%2C_July_2011.jpg/330px-St_Martin-in-the-Fields%2C_July_2011.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/St_Martin-in-the-Fields%2C_July_2011.jpg/440px-St_Martin-in-the-Fields%2C_July_2011.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:St_Martin-in-the-Fields,_July_2011.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/St_Martin-in-the-Fields" title="St Martin-in-the-Fields">St Martin-in-the-Fields</a>, London (1720), <a href="/wiki/James_Gibbs" title="James Gibbs">James Gibbs</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Etruscan-Roman adaptation of the Greek temple model to place the main emphasis on the front façade and let the other sides of the building harmonize with it only as much as circumstances and budget allow has generally been adopted in <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">Neoclassical architecture</a>, and other classically derived styles. In these temple fronts with columns and a pediment are very common for the main entrance of grand buildings, but often flanked by large wings or set in courtyards. This flexibility has allowed the Roman temple front to be used in buildings made for a wide variety of purposes. The colonnade may no longer be pushed forward with a pronaus porch, and it may not be raised above the ground, but the essential shape remains the same. Among thousands of examples are the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buckingham_Palace" title="Buckingham Palace">Buckingham Palace</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Peters,_Rome" title="St Peters, Rome">St Peters, Rome</a>; in recent years the temple front has become fashionable in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Renaissance and later architects worked out ways of harmoniously adding high raised domes, towers and spires above a colonnaded temple portico front, something the Romans would have found odd. The Roman temple front remains a familiar feature of subsequent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Modern" title="Early Modern">Early Modern</a> architecture in the Western tradition, but although very commonly used for churches, it has lost the specific association with religion that it had for the Romans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Generally, later adaptions lack the colour of the original, and though there may be sculpture filling the pediment in grand examples, the full Roman complement of sculpture above the roofline is rarely emulated.</p>
<p>Variations on the theme, mostly Italian in origin, include: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Andrea,_Mantua" title="San Andrea, Mantua">San Andrea, Mantua</a>, 1462 by <a href="/wiki/Leon_Battista_Alberti" title="Leon Battista Alberti">Leon Battista Alberti</a>, which took a four-columned Roman <a href="/wiki/Triumphal_arch" title="Triumphal arch">triumphal arch</a> and added a pediment above; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_San_Giorgio_Maggiore" title="Church of San Giorgio Maggiore">San Giorgio Maggiore, Venice</a>, 1610 by <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Palladio" title="Andrea Palladio">Andrea Palladio</a>, which has two superimposed temple fronts, one low and wide, the other tall and narrow; the <a href="/wiki/Villa_Capra_%22La_Rotonda%22" title='Villa Capra "La Rotonda"'>Villa Rotunda</a>, 1567 on, also by Palladio, with four isolated temple fronts on each side of a rectangle, with a large central dome. In <a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">Baroque architecture</a> two temple fronts, often of different orders, superimposed one above the other, became extremely common for Catholic churches, often with the uppermost one supported by huge <a href="/wiki/Volute" title="Volute">volutes</a> to each side. This can be seen developing in the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Ges%C3%B9" title="Church of the Gesù">Gesù, Rome</a> (1584), <a href="/wiki/Santa_Susanna" title="Santa Susanna">Santa Susanna</a>, Rome (1597), <a href="/wiki/Santi_Vincenzo_e_Anastasio_a_Trevi" title="Santi Vincenzo e Anastasio a Trevi">Santi Vincenzo e Anastasio a Trevi</a> (1646) and <a href="/wiki/Val-de-Gr%C3%A2ce_(church)" title="Val-de-Grâce (church)">Val-de-Grâce</a>, Paris (1645 on).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>An archetypical pattern for churches in <a href="/wiki/Georgian_architecture" title="Georgian architecture">Georgian architecture</a> was set by <a href="/wiki/St_Martin-in-the-Fields" title="St Martin-in-the-Fields">St Martin-in-the-Fields</a> in London (1720), by <a href="/wiki/James_Gibbs" title="James Gibbs">James Gibbs</a>, who boldly added to the classical temple façade at the west end a large steeple on top of a tower, set back slightly from the main frontage. This formula shocked purists and foreigners, but became accepted and was very widely copied, at home and in the colonies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> for example at <a href="/wiki/St_Andrew%27s_Church,_Chennai" title="St Andrew's Church, Chennai">St Andrew's Church, Chennai</a> in India and <a href="/wiki/St._Paul%27s_Chapel" title="St. Paul's Chapel">St. Paul's Chapel</a> in New York City (1766).</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Madeleine_Paris.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3278" data-file-width="4598" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Madeleine_Paris.jpg/220px-Madeleine_Paris.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Madeleine_Paris.jpg/330px-Madeleine_Paris.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Madeleine_Paris.jpg/440px-Madeleine_Paris.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Madeleine_Paris.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/La_Madeleine,_Paris" title="La Madeleine, Paris">La Madeleine, Paris</a> (1807), now a church</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Examples of modern buildings that stick more faithfully to the ancient rectangular temple form are only found from the 18th century onwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Summerson_1980.2C_25_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Summerson_1980.2C_25-1">[1]</a></sup> Versions of the Roman temple as a discrete block include <a href="/wiki/La_Madeleine,_Paris" title="La Madeleine, Paris">La Madeleine, Paris</a> (1807), now a church but built by Napoleon as a <i>Temple de la Gloire de la Grande Armée</i> ("Temple to the Glory of the Great Army"), the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_State_Capitol" title="Virginia State Capitol">Virginia State Capitol</a> as originally built in 1785–88, and <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Town_Hall" title="Birmingham Town Hall">Birmingham Town Hall</a> (1832–34).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Small Roman circular temples with colonnades have often been used as models, either for single buildings, large or small, or elements such as domes raised on drums, in buildings on another plan such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Peters,_Rome" title="St Peters, Rome">St Peters, Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_Cathedral" title="St Paul's Cathedral">St Paul's Cathedral</a> in London and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">United States Capitol</a>. The great progenitor of these is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tempietto" title="Tempietto">Tempietto</a> of <a href="/wiki/Donato_Bramante" title="Donato Bramante">Donato Bramante</a> in the courtyard of <a href="/wiki/San_Pietro_in_Montorio" title="San Pietro in Montorio">San Pietro in Montorio</a> in Rome, c. 1502, which has been widely admired ever since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though the Pantheon's large circular domed cella, with a conventional portico front, is "unique" in Roman architecture, it has been copied many times by modern architects. Versions include the church of Santa Maria Assunta in <a href="/wiki/Ariccia" title="Ariccia">Ariccia</a> by <a href="/wiki/Gian_Lorenzo_Bernini" title="Gian Lorenzo Bernini">Gian Lorenzo Bernini</a> (1664), which followed his work restoring the Roman original,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Belle_Isle_(Windermere)" title="Belle Isle (Windermere)">Belle Isle House</a> (1774) in England, and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a>'s library at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia" title="University of Virginia">University of Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Rotunda_(University_of_Virginia)" title="The Rotunda (University of Virginia)">The Rotunda</a> (1817–26).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The Pantheon was much the largest and most accessible complete classical temple front known to the Italian Renaissance, and was the standard exemplar when these were revived.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temple_of_Jupiter_Optimus_Maximus">Temple of Jupiter Optimus Maximus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_temple&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Temple of Jupiter Optimus Maximus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bas_relief_from_Arch_of_Marcus_Aurelius_showing_sacrifice.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2898" data-file-width="2181" height="292" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Bas_relief_from_Arch_of_Marcus_Aurelius_showing_sacrifice.jpg/220px-Bas_relief_from_Arch_of_Marcus_Aurelius_showing_sacrifice.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Bas_relief_from_Arch_of_Marcus_Aurelius_showing_sacrifice.jpg/330px-Bas_relief_from_Arch_of_Marcus_Aurelius_showing_sacrifice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Bas_relief_from_Arch_of_Marcus_Aurelius_showing_sacrifice.jpg/440px-Bas_relief_from_Arch_of_Marcus_Aurelius_showing_sacrifice.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bas_relief_from_Arch_of_Marcus_Aurelius_showing_sacrifice.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Relief" title="Relief">Relief</a> of <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a> sacrificing at the 4th temple (left)</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Jupiter_Optimus_Maximus" title="Temple of Jupiter Optimus Maximus">Temple of Jupiter Optimus Maximus</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Capitoline_Hill" title="Capitoline Hill">Capitoline Hill</a> was the oldest large temple in Rome, dedicated to the <a href="/wiki/Capitoline_Triad" title="Capitoline Triad">Capitoline Triad</a> consisting of Jupiter and his companion deities, <a href="/wiki/Juno_(mythology)" title="Juno (mythology)">Juno</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minerva" title="Minerva">Minerva</a>, and had a cathedral-like position in the official religion of Rome. It was destroyed by fire three times, and rapidly rebuilt in contemporary styles. The first building, traditionally dedicated in 509 BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> has been claimed to have been almost 60 m × 60 m (200 ft × 200 ft), much larger than other Roman temples for centuries after, although its size is heavily disputed by specialists. Whatever its size, its influence on other early Roman temples was significant and long-lasting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The same may have been true for the later rebuildings, though here the influence is harder to trace.</p>
<p>For the first temple Etruscan specialists were brought in for various aspects of the building, including making and painting the extensive <a href="/wiki/Terracotta" title="Terracotta">terracotta</a> elements of the <a href="/wiki/Entablature" title="Entablature">entablature</a> or upper parts, such as <a href="/wiki/Antefix" title="Antefix">antefixes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> But for the second building they were summoned from Greece. Rebuildings after destruction by fire were completed in 69 BC, 75 AD, and in the 80s AD, under <a href="/wiki/Domitian" title="Domitian">Domitian</a> – the third building only lasted five years before burning down again. After a major sacking by <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">Vandals</a> in 455, and comprehensive removal of stone in the Renaissance, only foundations can now be seen, in the basement of the <a href="/wiki/Capitoline_Museums" title="Capitoline Museums">Capitoline Museums</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The sculptor <a href="/wiki/Flaminio_Vacca" title="Flaminio Vacca">Flaminio Vacca</a> (d 1605) claimed that the life-size <a href="/wiki/Medici_lions" title="Medici lions">Medici lion</a> he carved to match a Roman survival, now in <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, was made from a single capital from the temple.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>Most of the best survivals had been converted to churches (and sometimes later mosques), which some remain. Often the porticos were walled in between the columns, and the original cella front and side walls largely removed to create a large single space in the interior. Rural areas in the Islamic world have some good remains, which had been left largely undisturbed. In Spain some remarkable discoveries (Vic, Cordoba, Barcelona) were made in the 19th century when old buildings being reconstructed or demolished were found to contain major remains encased in later buildings. In Rome, Pula, and elsewhere some walls incorporated in later buildings have always been evident. The squared-off blocks of temple walls have always been attractive for later builders to reuse, while the large pieces of massive columns were less easy to remove and make use of; hence the podium, minus facing, and some columns are often all that remain. In most cases loose pieces of stone have been removed from the site, and some such as capitals may be found in local museums, along with non-architectural items excavated, such as terracotta <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Votive" title="Votive">votive</a> statuettes or amulets, which are often found in large numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Very little indeed survives from the significant quantities of large sculpture that originally decorated temples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Antoninus_and_Faustina" title="Temple of Antoninus and Faustina">Temple of Antoninus and Faustina</a> – the core of the building survives as a church, including parts of the frieze, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Forum" title="Roman Forum">Roman Forum</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Hadrian" title="Temple of Hadrian">Temple of Hadrian</a> – <a href="/wiki/Campus_Martius" title="Campus Martius">Campus Martius</a> – a huge wall with 11 columns, now incorporated in a later building</li>
