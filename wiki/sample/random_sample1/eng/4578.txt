<p>The <b>March on Rome</b> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><i>Marcia su Roma</i></span>) was a march by which Italian dictator <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a>'s <a href="/wiki/National_Fascist_Party" title="National Fascist Party">National Fascist Party</a> (<i>Partito Nazionale Fascista</i>, or PNF) came to power in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy" title="Kingdom of Italy">Kingdom of Italy</a> (<i>Regno d'Italia</i>). The march took place from 22 to 29 October 1922.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Context">Context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=March_on_Rome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In March 1919, Benito Mussolini founded the first "Italian Combat Leagues" (<i><a href="/wiki/Fasci_Italiani_di_Combattimento" title="Fasci Italiani di Combattimento">Fasci Italiani di Combattimento</a></i>) at the beginning of the "two red years" (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biennio_rosso" title="Biennio rosso">biennio rosso</a></i>). He suffered a defeat in <a href="/wiki/Italian_general_election,_1919" title="Italian general election, 1919">the election of November 1919</a> mainly due to Mussolini’s attempt to “out-socialist the socialists” at the ballot box.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> But, by the <a href="/wiki/Italian_general_election,_1921" title="Italian general election, 1921">election of 1921</a>, Mussolini entered the Parliament.</p>
<p>Out of his "Fascist" party the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milizia_Volontaria_per_la_Sicurezza_Nazionale" title="Milizia Volontaria per la Sicurezza Nazionale">Milizia Volontaria per la Sicurezza Nazionale</a> ("Blackshirts" or <i>Squadristi</i>) were formed. In August 1920, the Blackshirts were used to break the <a href="/wiki/General_strike" title="General strike">general strike</a> which had started at the <a href="/wiki/Alfa_Romeo" title="Alfa Romeo">Alfa Romeo</a> factory in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>. In November 1920, after the assassination of Giordana (a right-wing municipal counsellor in <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a>), the Blackshirts were used as a repression tool by the state<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to crush the socialist movement (which included a strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anarcho-syndicalist" title="Anarcho-syndicalist">anarcho-syndicalist</a> component), especially in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Po_River" title="Po River">Po Valley</a>.</p>
<p>Trade unions were dissolved while left-wing mayors resigned. The fascists, included on <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Giolitti" title="Giovanni Giolitti">Giovanni Giolitti</a>'s "National Union" lists at the May 1921 elections, then won 36 seats. Mussolini then withdrew his support to Giolitti and attempted to work out a temporary truce with the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Socialist_Party" title="Italian Socialist Party">socialists</a> by signing a "Pacification Pack" in summer 1921. This provoked a conflict with the most fanatical part of the movement, the <i><a href="/wiki/Blackshirts" title="Blackshirts">Squadristi</a></i> and their leaders the <i><a href="/wiki/Gerarca" title="Gerarca">Ras</a></i> ("Dukes", from an Ethiopian term). In July 1921, Giolitti attempted without success to dissolve the <i>squadristi</i>. The contract with the socialists was then broken at its turn in November 1921, Mussolini adopted a <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalist</a> program and founded the <a href="/wiki/National_Fascist_Party" title="National Fascist Party">National Fascist Party</a>, which boasted 700,000 members in July 1922.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In August, an <a href="/wiki/Anti-fascism" title="Anti-fascism">anti-fascist</a> general strike was triggered, but failed to rally the Italian People's Party (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_People%27s_Party_(1919%E2%80%931926)" title="Italian People's Party (1919–1926)">Partito Popolare Italiano</a></i>) and was repressed by the fascists. A few days before the march, Mussolini consulted with the U.S. Ambassador <a href="/wiki/Richard_Washburn_Child" title="Richard Washburn Child">Richard Washburn Child</a> about whether the U.S. government would object to Fascist participation in a future Italian government. Child encouraged him to go ahead. When Mussolini learned that Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Facta" title="Luigi Facta">Luigi Facta</a> had given <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gabriele_d%27Annunzio" title="Gabriele d'Annunzio">Gabriele d'Annunzio</a> the mission to organize a large demonstration on 4 November 1922 to celebrate the national victory during the war, he decided on the March to accelerate the process and sidestep any possible competition.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="March">March</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=March_on_Rome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: March">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:March_on_rome_1.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="460" data-file-width="672" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/March_on_rome_1.png/300px-March_on_rome_1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/March_on_rome_1.png/450px-March_on_rome_1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/March_on_rome_1.png/600px-March_on_rome_1.png 2x" width="300"/></a>

<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Quadrumvirs" title="Quadrumvirs">quadrumvirs</a></i> leading the Fascist Party, General <a href="/wiki/Emilio_De_Bono" title="Emilio De Bono">Emilio De Bono</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italo_Balbo" title="Italo Balbo">Italo Balbo</a> (one of the most famous <i>ras</i>), <a href="/wiki/Michele_Bianchi" title="Michele Bianchi">Michele Bianchi</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cesare_Maria_de_Vecchi" title="Cesare Maria de Vecchi">Cesare Maria de Vecchi</a>, organized the March while the <i><a href="/wiki/Duce" title="Duce">Duce</a></i> stayed behind for most of the march, though he allowed pictures to be taken of him marching along with the Fascist marchers. Generals Gustavo Fara and Sante Ceccherini assisted to the preparations of the March of 18 October. Other organizers of the march included the Marquis <a href="/wiki/Dino_Perrone_Compagni" title="Dino Perrone Compagni">Dino Perrone Compagni</a> and Ulisse Igliori.</p>
<p>On 24 October 1922, Mussolini declared before 60,000 people at the Fascist Congress in <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>: "Our program is simple: we want to rule Italy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Meanwhile, the Blackshirts, who had occupied the Po plain, took all strategic points of the country. On 26 October, former prime minister <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Salandra" title="Antonio Salandra">Antonio Salandra</a> warned current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Minister" title="Prime Minister">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Facta" title="Luigi Facta">Luigi Facta</a> that Mussolini was demanding his resignation and that he was preparing to march on Rome. However, Facta did not believe Salandra and thought that Mussolini would govern quietly at his side. To meet the threat posed by the bands of fascist troops now gathering outside Rome, Luigi Facta (who had resigned but continued to hold power) ordered a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_of_siege" title="State of siege">state of siege</a> for Rome. Having had previous conversations with the king about the repression of fascist violence, he was sure the king would agree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Victor_Emmanuel_III_of_Italy" title="Victor Emmanuel III of Italy">King Victor Emmanuel III</a> refused to sign the military order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> On 28 October, the King handed power to Mussolini, who was supported by the military, the business class, and the right-wing.</p>
<p>The march itself was composed of fewer than 30,000 men, but the king in part feared a civil war since the <i>squadristi</i> had already taken control of the Po plain and most of the country, while Fascism was no longer seen as a threat to the establishment. Mussolini was asked to form his cabinet on 29 October 1922, while some 25,000 Blackshirts were parading in Rome. Mussolini thus legally reached power, in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Statuto_Albertino" title="Statuto Albertino">Statuto Albertino</a>, the Italian Constitution. The March on Rome was not the seizure of power which <a href="/wiki/Italian_Fascism" title="Italian Fascism">Fascism</a> later celebrated but rather the precipitating force behind a transfer of power within the framework of the constitution. This transition was made possible by the surrender of public authorities in the face of fascist intimidation. Many business and financial leaders believed it would be possible to manipulate Mussolini, whose early speeches and policies emphasized <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">free market</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laissez_faire" title="Laissez faire">laissez faire</a> economics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This proved overly optimistic, as Mussolini's corporatist view stressed total state power over businesses as much as over individuals, via governing industry bodies ("corporations") controlled by the Fascist party, a model in which businesses retained the responsibilities of property, but few if any of the freedoms. By 1934 Mussolini claimed to have nationalized “three-fourths of the Italian economy, industrial and agricultural,” more than any other nation except the Soviet Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mussolini pretended to be willing to take a subalternate ministry in a Giolitti or <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Salandra" title="Antonio Salandra">Salandra</a> cabinet, but then demanded the presidency of the Council. Fearing a conflict with the fascists, the ruling class thus handed power to Mussolini, who went on to install the dictatorship after the 10 June 1924 assassination of <a href="/wiki/Giacomo_Matteotti" title="Giacomo Matteotti">Giacomo Matteotti</a> - who had finished writing <i>The Fascisti Exposed: A Year of Fascist Domination</i> - executed by <a href="/wiki/Amerigo_Dumini" title="Amerigo Dumini">Amerigo Dumini</a>, leader of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceka" title="Ceka">Ceka</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Secret_police" title="Secret police">secret police</a> <a href="/wiki/Government_agency" title="Government agency">agency</a> precursor to the <a href="/wiki/OVRA" title="OVRA">OVRA</a>.</p>
