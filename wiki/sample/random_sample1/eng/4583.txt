<p><i><b>The Holiday</b></i> is a 2006 American <a href="/wiki/Romance_film" title="Romance film">romantic</a> <a href="/wiki/Comedy_film" title="Comedy film">comedy film</a> written, produced and directed by <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Meyers" title="Nancy Meyers">Nancy Meyers</a>. Co-produced by <a href="/wiki/Bruce_A._Block" title="Bruce A. Block">Bruce A. Block</a>, it was filmed in both California and England, and stars <a href="/wiki/Cameron_Diaz" title="Cameron Diaz">Cameron Diaz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kate_Winslet" title="Kate Winslet">Kate Winslet</a> as Amanda and Iris, two lovelorn women from opposite sides of the Atlantic Ocean, who temporarily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_swapping" title="House swapping">exchange homes</a> to escape heartbreak during the <a href="/wiki/Christmas_and_holiday_season" title="Christmas and holiday season">holiday season</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jude_Law" title="Jude Law">Jude Law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Black" title="Jack Black">Jack Black</a> were cast as the film's <a href="/wiki/Leading_man" title="Leading man">leading men</a> Graham and Miles, with <a href="/wiki/Eli_Wallach" title="Eli Wallach">Eli Wallach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shannyn_Sossamon" title="Shannyn Sossamon">Shannyn Sossamon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Burns" title="Edward Burns">Edward Burns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rufus_Sewell" title="Rufus Sewell">Rufus Sewell</a> playing key supporting roles.</p>
<p>Distributed by <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Pictures" title="Universal Pictures">Universal Pictures</a>, <i>The Holiday</i> was first released on December 6, 2006, in Spain and on December 8, 2006, in North America and the United Kingdom. It grossed over $205 million worldwide. Critics praised the film's visual aesthetic design and the cast's performances, though criticized its plot as predictable.</p>

<p>Iris Simpkins (<a href="/wiki/Kate_Winslet" title="Kate Winslet">Kate Winslet</a>), a society columnist for <a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a> in London, has been in love with Jasper Bloom (<a href="/wiki/Rufus_Sewell" title="Rufus Sewell">Rufus Sewell</a>) for over three years, despite his infidelities and the fact they have broken up some time previously. When she learns at a company Christmas party that he is engaged to be married, Iris despairs over the state of affairs in her life. Meanwhile, Amanda Woods (<a href="/wiki/Cameron_Diaz" title="Cameron Diaz">Cameron Diaz</a>), a workaholic in Los Angeles who owns a company that produces <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Movie_trailers" title="Movie trailers">movie trailers</a>, discovers that her boyfriend, Ethan Ebbers (<a href="/wiki/Edward_Burns" title="Edward Burns">Edward Burns</a>), has been cheating on her with his 24-year-old secretary. On the spur of the moment she decides to get away for the Christmas holidays and visits a home-exchange website on which Iris has listed her "quaint cottage in <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>." Amanda contacts Iris, and the two women quickly agree to swap homes for two weeks.</p>
<p>While Iris revels in the luxury of Amanda's Los Angeles home, Amanda is disappointed by the slow, quiet pace of life in Iris's Surrey cottage. Amanda grows bored after just a few hours and books a flight home for the next day. Later that night, Iris's brother, Graham (<a href="/wiki/Jude_Law" title="Jude Law">Jude Law</a>), knocks at the door thinking Iris is home. Graham asks Amanda to let him spend the night despite the fact that he is a stranger, as he has been drinking at the pub and doesn't want to drive. Because of the obvious chemistry between them, they end up sleeping together.</p>
<p>In the morning, Graham receives phone calls from Sophie and Olivia, which arouse Amanda's suspicions that Graham has girlfriends. Graham, knowing that Amanda is planning to return home imminently, says to Amanda that "if the flight gets cancelled, [he is] having dinner at the pub" with friends. At the airport Amanda decides to stay and goes to the pub. Graham enters the pub and looks for her but cannot see her until he meets his friends and then sees Amanda. Amanda drinks far too much that night, and Graham agrees to stay the night with her. The next day Graham suggests they go to lunch to get to know one another better. During lunch, Amanda shares with Graham that her parents divorced when she was fifteen and since then she has been unable to cry. Graham tells her that he cries all the time: movies, books, and birthday cards. He also reveals himself to be a book editor. While obviously enjoying each other's company, Amanda is nonetheless worried that the relationship will become "complicated."</p>
<p>Meanwhile, Iris is enjoying her stay at Amanda's house. She meets Ethan's friend Miles (<a href="/wiki/Jack_Black" title="Jack Black">Jack Black</a>) when he comes with his girlfriend Maggie (<a href="/wiki/Shannyn_Sossamon" title="Shannyn Sossamon">Shannyn Sossamon</a>) to Amanda's house. Later, she meets an elderly neighbour named Arthur Abbott (<a href="/wiki/Eli_Wallach" title="Eli Wallach">Eli Wallach</a>), standing lost at the corner, and she escorts him home. She learns that he was a <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">screenwriter</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Age_of_Hollywood" title="Golden Age of Hollywood">Golden Age of Hollywood</a>. The two become fast friends. Arthur notes that Iris's relationship with Jasper is not serving her well, and he suggests movies for her to watch that include strong female characters, in hopes that she can acquire what he calls "gumption."</p>
<p>Amanda chooses not to see Graham again but, alone again in the cottage, she has a change of heart and surprises Graham at his house. He looks uncomfortable at the door, Amanda asks Graham whether he is alone, and he says he isn't. Before she can turn away, Olivia and Sophie come to the door, revealing that they are Graham's young daughters. Inside the house, Amanda asks Graham in a whisper if he is married. He says no, revealing that his wife died two years earlier. Amanda asks him why he did not reveal the existence of his daughters to her during their lunch, and Graham explains that he doesn't usually tell women about them because he doesn't know how to date women while also being a dad. Amanda gets along beautifully with Sophie and Olivia, making Graham consider how Amanda might fit into his family life.</p>
<p>Iris discovers that the Screenwriters' Guild wants to throw a grand celebration in Arthur's honor, but he is reluctant to attend for several reasons, chief among them being that he cannot walk without the assistance of his walker, and does not want to embarrass himself on stage. Iris encourages him to go because it's a a tremendous honor for him, and promises to help him prepare to attend the award function with her. She undertakes an exercise program with him, determined to help him walk without his walker. During this time Jasper contacts her, asking her to assist with the book he is writing. She agrees to look over his pages, but ends up having so much fun that she doesn't have the time.</p>
<p>Iris and Miles have slowly become friends, and one night go to the video store to rent some other films that Arthur had recommended. From the video store, Miles sees his girlfriend Maggie in the arms of another guy. He is shocked, as she told him that she was in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe,_New_Mexico" title="Santa Fe, New Mexico">Santa Fe</a> on an extended film shoot and would not be in L.A. on Christmas Eve. He runs out of the store to confront Maggie and realizes that she has been cheating on him. Iris and Miles then return to Amanda's house, where they discuss their tendencies to fall for people that they know are wrong for them, and they begin to bond with each other. Shortly afterwards Miles and Iris are eating lunch together, and Maggie calls Miles wanting to see him. Miles leaves to see her, but promises to come to Arthur's award ceremony that night. Iris returns to Amanda's house, and is later surprised to find Jasper at her doorstep. Iris is touched at first, but then asks if he is still engaged. When he responds that he is, she finally realizes he is no good for her and breaks up with him for good. Meanwhile, Maggie tries in vain to convince Miles to forgive her. He realizes that Maggie was never meant for him, ends his relationship with her for good and rushes to attend Arthur's award ceremony.</p>
<p>Iris and Arthur arrive at the ceremony and are pleasantly surprised to find that, despite Arthur's fears that the ceremony would be sparsely attended, the hall is filled with attendees standing and applauding his achievements. That, and the music that Miles wrote especially for Arthur for this moment, give him the confidence to walk onto the stage unassisted. Miles arrives and asks Iris for a date on New Year's Eve. Iris responds that she will be back in England by then; Miles replies "I've never been to England." Iris responds that she would love to spend the evening with Miles. Meanwhile, Graham confesses his love for Amanda on the night before she is scheduled to depart, but Amanda is still doubtful that a long-distance relationship will work. On her way to the airport, she cries for the first time since she was fifteen years old, and returns to the house to find Graham crying, too. Amanda tells him that she has decided to stay to spend New Year's Eve with him, and they embrace. Iris and Miles celebrate the new year with Amanda and Graham and his daughters, enjoying the evening, and laughing and dancing together.</p>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cameron_Diaz" title="Cameron Diaz">Cameron Diaz</a> as Amanda Woods:</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Amanda is the owner of a prospering business that produces <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trailer_(film)" title="Trailer (film)">movie trailers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-movonl_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-movonl-4">[4]</a></sup> A fan of Meyers' work, Diaz signed on after reading parts of the script.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-il-cd_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-il-cd-5">[5]</a></sup> Commenting on her decision to play Amanda, Diaz said that her character "was totally relatable to because we've all had these relationships that fail. But I loved the bravery that she displays. She [...] learns about who she is and opens herself up to possibilities she's never allowed herself to have before. I felt that was such a wonderful message to put out there."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indielondon_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-indielondon-6">[6]</a></sup> Meyers, who envisioned casting her still during the writing process, compared Diaz' performance in the film to <a href="/wiki/Goldie_Hawn" title="Goldie Hawn">Goldie Hawn</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-il-cd_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-il-cd-5">[5]</a></sup> complimenting her adeptness at physical comedy: "It's really hard I think to be that cute and sexy and that funny and that sort of girl-friendly [..] She seemed absolutely the right choice for a California girl," she commented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ros_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ros-7">[7]</a></sup> In developing her character, Diaz also <a href="/wiki/Improvisation" title="Improvisation">improvised</a> on set: "There were a few scenes that were written on the page but then Nancy and I fooled around with them a bit. We didn’t want to take it [the comedy] too broad. We wanted it to be believable, so we included realistic moments," she said.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indielondon_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-indielondon-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kate_Winslet" title="Kate Winslet">Kate Winslet</a> as Iris Simpkins:</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Iris is a society columnist, writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-movonl_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-movonl-4">[4]</a></sup> Winslet was hand-picked by Meyers, who wrote all of Iris' lines with her in mind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ew1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ew1-8">[8]</a></sup> A fan of Meyers' previous work on <i><a href="/wiki/Something%27s_Gotta_Give_(film)" title="Something's Gotta Give (film)">Something's Gotta Give</a></i> (2003), Winslet, then primarily known for her portrayals in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Period_film" title="Period film">period films</a>, "loved the idea" of playing a contemporary English woman in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romantic_comedy" title="Romantic comedy">romantic comedy</a>, a genre she had not done before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ew1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ew1-8">[8]</a></sup> Winslet said she had initially felt "nervous and [...] scared about trying to be funny" at times, stating that "<a href="/wiki/Jude_Law" title="Jude Law">Jude</a> [Law] and I would speak on the telephone a lot before we started shooting, 'Oh my god, they're going to fire us, they're going to recast, what if we don't make them laugh?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In preparing for her role, Winslet watched <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Screwball_comedy" title="Screwball comedy">screwball comedies</a> from the 1940s, such as <i><a href="/wiki/His_Girl_Friday" title="His Girl Friday">His Girl Friday</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Philadelphia_Story_(film)" title="The Philadelphia Story (film)">The Philadelphia Story</a></i>, to study the dialogues and performances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ew1_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-ew1-8">[8]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jude_Law" title="Jude Law">Jude Law</a> as Graham Simpkins:</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Graham is Iris' brother, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_editor" title="Book editor">book editor</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_father" title="Single father">single father</a> forced to raise his two daughters by himself after his wife's death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-movonl_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-movonl-4">[4]</a></sup> Law accepted the role as he was interested in playing a type of character that he had never played on film before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yt-1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-yt-1-10">[10]</a></sup> After his appearances in a string of period dramas and <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_film" title="Science fiction film">science fiction films</a> in the early to mid-2000s, Law found it tricky to approach the contemporary role of Graham. Like Winslet, the actor stated, he felt more vulnerable about playing a character who fitted his own look and did not require an accent, a costume or a relocation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yt-1_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-yt-1-10">[10]</a></sup> Meyers, who was not immediately sure if Law was going to fit into the genre and whose character evolved more during the writing than the others, decided to cast him after meeting in which they we went through the script together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yt-1_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-yt-1-10">[10]</a></sup> In preparing for his role, Meyers sent him a collection of <a href="/wiki/Clark_Gable" title="Clark Gable">Clark Gable</a> movies to prepare the performance that she wanted in <i>The Holiday</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ros_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ros-7">[7]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jack_Black" title="Jack Black">Jack Black</a> as Miles Dumont:</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Miles is a Hollywood <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Music_composer" title="Music composer">music composer</a> working with Amanda and an affiliate of her boyfriend Ethan. As with Diaz and Winslet, Meyers specially created the character for Black after watching his performance in the musical <a href="/wiki/Comedy_film" title="Comedy film">comedy film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/School_of_Rock" title="School of Rock">School of Rock</a></i> (2003).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-il-nm_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-il-nm-11">[11]</a></sup> On his cast, Meyers commented that "when I was thinking of this movie I thought he was someone I would like to write a part for and I'm aware he's not Clark Gable, he's not tall dark and handsome, but he's adorable, he's lovable. It's my way of saying this is the right kind of guy, this is what most guys look like if they're lucky, he's so adorable, and why not?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-il-jb_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-il-jb-12">[12]</a></sup> Cast against type, Black felt "flattered [and] a little bit nervous" about Meyers' approach to star in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romantic_comedy" title="Romantic comedy">rom-com</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-il-jb_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-il-jb-12">[12]</a></sup> though he eventually agreed to sign on upon learning that he would play opposite Winslet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-il-jb_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-il-jb-12">[12]</a></sup> While he felt, it was difficult to find the adorable side in his role, Black appreciated Miles' relationship with music: I could relate to that Miles was a film composer and I just got done composing my music for my score. So I knew about that world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eli_Wallach" title="Eli Wallach">Eli Wallach</a> as Arthur Abbott:</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Arthur is Amanda's neighbor, a famous screenwriter from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Age_of_Hollywood" title="Golden Age of Hollywood">Golden Age of Hollywood</a> whom Iris befriends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-movonl_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-movonl-4">[4]</a></sup> Wallach was 90 years old when <i>The Holiday</i> was filmed. Meyers found him so animated and energetic on the set, that she had to remind him several times during filming to slow down, move more slowly, and act more like an older man.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Shannyn_Sossamon" title="Shannyn Sossamon">Shannyn Sossamon</a> appears as Maggie, Miles' girlfriend and aspiring actress, while <a href="/wiki/Edward_Burns" title="Edward Burns">Edward Burns</a> plays as Ethan Ebbers, Amanda's boyfriend; <a href="/wiki/Rufus_Sewell" title="Rufus Sewell">Rufus Sewell</a> portrays Jasper Bloom, Iris' on-and-off affair. The film also reunited Sewell and Sossamon as they both starred in <a href="/wiki/A_Knight%27s_Tale" title="A Knight's Tale">A Knight's Tale</a> together, although they do not share a scene. Extended Simpkins family include Miffy Englefield and Emma Pritchard as Sophie and Olivia, Graham's daughters, respectively. The film also cast <a href="/wiki/Bill_Macy" title="Bill Macy">Bill Macy</a> as Ernie and <a href="/wiki/Shelley_Berman" title="Shelley Berman">Shelley Berman</a> as Norman, friends of Arthur, as well as <a href="/wiki/Kathryn_Hahn" title="Kathryn Hahn">Kathryn Hahn</a> as Bristol and <a href="/wiki/John_Krasinski" title="John Krasinski">John Krasinski</a> as Ben, Amanda's employees. <a href="/wiki/Jon_Prescott" title="Jon Prescott">Jon Prescott</a> appears as Maggie's short-time affair.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dustin_Hoffman" title="Dustin Hoffman">Dustin Hoffman</a> appears in the video rental store in an uncredited <a href="/wiki/Cameo_appearance" title="Cameo appearance">cameo</a> as Jack Black talks about the score from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Graduate" title="The Graduate">The Graduate</a></i>. According to Hoffman, this was unscripted and unexpected. He was going to Blockbuster for a movie, saw all the light and came over to see what was going on. He knew director Nancy Meyers, who scripted a short scene with him in it. <a href="/wiki/Lindsay_Lohan" title="Lindsay Lohan">Lindsay Lohan</a>, who had made her motion picture debut in Meyers' remake of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Parent_Trap_(1998_film)" title="The Parent Trap (1998 film)">The Parent Trap</a></i> (1998), and <a href="/wiki/James_Franco" title="James Franco">James Franco</a>, a friend of Meyers, make uncredited appearances in the trailer of the fictional movie <i>Deception</i>, which Amanda and her team finish at the beginning of <i>The Holiday</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ros_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-ros-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Holiday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Holiday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TheHoliday.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="160" data-file-width="240" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f9/TheHoliday.jpg/220px-TheHoliday.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/f/f9/TheHoliday.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TheHoliday.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Filming on location in Godalming.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Production on <i>The Holiday</i> began in Los Angeles, then moved to England for a month before completing filming back in California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sp1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-sp1-15">[15]</a></sup> Principal photography began in the <a href="/wiki/Brentwood,_Los_Angeles" title="Brentwood, Los Angeles">Brentwood</a> area on the <a href="/wiki/Westside_(Los_Angeles_County)" title="Westside (Los Angeles County)">Westside of Los Angeles</a>, where real <a href="/wiki/Santa_Ana_winds" title="Santa Ana winds">Santa Ana winds</a> reportedly gave Meyers and her team a winter day as warm as scripted in the screenplay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sp1_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-sp1-15">[15]</a></sup> Although Amanda's home is set in Brentwood, the exterior scenes at the gated property were actually filmed in front of Southern California architect <a href="/wiki/Wallace_Neff" title="Wallace Neff">Wallace Neff</a>'s Mission Revival house in <a href="/wiki/San_Marino,_California" title="San Marino, California">San Marino</a>, a suburb adjacent to <a href="/wiki/Pasadena,_California" title="Pasadena, California">Pasadena</a>. Neff had built the house for his family in 1928. The interiors of Amanda's house were filmed at <a href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Studios" title="Sony Pictures Studios">Sony Pictures Studios</a> in <a href="/wiki/Culver_City,_California" title="Culver City, California">Culver City</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sp1_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-sp1-15">[15]</a></sup> Other Los Angeles locations included Arthur's house in Brentwood and Miles' house, designed by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Neutra" title="Richard Neutra">Richard Neutra</a>, which is situated on Neutra Place in L.A.'s <a href="/wiki/Silver_Lake,_Los_Angeles" title="Silver Lake, Los Angeles">Silver Lake area</a>, near downtown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sp1_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-sp1-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The UK part of the film was partially shot in <a href="/wiki/Godalming" title="Godalming">Godalming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shere" title="Shere">Shere</a>, a town and village in the county of <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a> in South East England that dates back to the 11th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sp1_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-sp1-15">[15]</a></sup> Filming began January 2006</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Holiday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Holiday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film opened at number three on the United States box office, raking in $12,778,913 in the weekend of December 8, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-the-numbers_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-the-numbers-3">[3]</a></sup> Altogether, <i>The Holiday</i> made $63 million at the North American domestic box office, and $142 million at the international box office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mojo_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-mojo-1">[1]</a></sup> The film grossed at total $205,135,175, worldwide, against a production budget of $85 million, and an estimated advertising spend of $34 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-the-numbers_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-the-numbers-3">[3]</a></sup> <i>The Holiday</i> became the twelfth highest-grossing film of the 2000s to be helmed by a female director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Holiday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Holiday</i> received mixed reviews from critics. The review aggregator website <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> reported that 47% of critics gave the film a positive rating, based on 135 reviews, with an average score of 5.5/10. Its consensus states "<i>The Holiday</i>, while sweet and somewhat touching, lacks any surprises and eventually overstays its welcome."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which uses a normalized rating system, the film holds a 52/100 rating, based on 31 reviews, indicating "mixed or average reviews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In her review for <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i>, Claudia Puig found that <i>The Holiday</i> "is a rare <a href="/wiki/Chick_flick" title="Chick flick">chick flick</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romantic_comedy" title="Romantic comedy">romantic comedy</a> that, despite its overt sentimentality and fairy-tale premise, doesn't feel cloyingly sweet." She felt that "much of the credit goes to inspired casting and the actors' chemistry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Carina Chocano, writing for the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> noted that "like a magic trick in reverse, <i>The Holiday</i> reveals the mechanics of the formula while trying to keep up the illusion. She complimented Winslet and Law's performances, but was critical toward Diaz, who she felt "strikes the off-note, but then you tend to think it's not her fault."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rex_Reed" title="Rex Reed">Rex Reed</a> from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_New_York_Observer" title="The New York Observer">The New York Observer</a></i> noted that "at least 90 percent of <i>The Holiday</i> is a stocking-stuffer from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiffany%27s" title="Tiffany's">Tiffany's</a> [...] so loaded with charm that it makes you glow all over and puts a smile in your heart." While he felt that the final 15 minutes of film "diminish a lot of the film's good intentions," he added that Meyers "created some hearth-cozy situations, written some movie-parody zingers, and provided Eli Wallach with his best role in years."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Somewhat less enthusiastic, <a href="/wiki/Owen_Gleiberman" title="Owen Gleiberman">Owen Gleiberman</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> graded the film with a 'B–' rating, summing it as a "cookie-cutter chick flick." He concluded that "it's a self-consciously old-fashioned premise, with too much sub-<a href="/wiki/Bridget_Jones" title="Bridget Jones">Bridget Jones</a> dithering, but Nancy Meyers' dialogue has a perky synthetic sheen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Justin Chang from <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> wrote that while "Meyers' characters tend to be more thoughtful and self-aware (or at least more self-conscious) than most [...] this overlong film isn't nearly as smart as it would like to appear, and it willingly succumbs to the very rom-com cliches it pretends to subvert." He added, that "in a spirited cast [...] the Brits easily outshine their Yank counterparts. Winslet weeps and moans without sacrificing her radiance or aud's sympathy, while the marginally less teary-eyed Law effortlessly piles on the charm in a role that will have some amusing resonances for tabloid readers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Ruthe Stein of the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> remarked that the film was "the most love-centric movie since <i><a href="/wiki/Love_Actually" title="Love Actually">Love Actually</a></i>." She felt that <i>The Holiday</i> "has charming moments and a hopeful message for despondent singles, but it lacks the emotional resonance of Meyers' <i><a href="/wiki/Something%27s_Gotta_Give_(film)" title="Something's Gotta Give (film)">Something's Gotta Give</a></i> and the zaniness of <i><a href="/wiki/What_Women_Want" title="What Women Want">What Women Want</a></i>. Clocking in at two hours and 16 minutes, <i>Holiday</i> is ridiculously long for a romantic comedy and would benefit from losing at least a half-hour."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since its release, however, <i>The Holiday</i> has been labeled as a modern Christmas classic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
