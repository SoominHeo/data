<p><a href="/wiki/Major-general_(United_Kingdom)" title="Major-general (United Kingdom)">Major-General</a> <b>Robert Ross</b> (1766 – 12 September 1814) was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish" title="Anglo-Irish">Anglo-Irish</a> officer in the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> who served in the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> and the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>.</p>
<p>Ross enlisted in the British Army in 1789. He served as an officer in several battles during the Napoleonic Wars. In 1809, he was sent to serve in the <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">Peninsular War</a>. He was wounded in the neck at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Orthez" title="Battle of Orthez">Battle of Orthez</a> in France on February 27, 1814.</p>
<p>Upon returning to duty later that year, Ross was made a major general and sent to North America, where he took command of a British force designated to attack the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">United States</a> capital of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_D.C." title="Washington D.C.">Washington D.C.</a> during the War of 1812. Ross's professional soldiers quickly defeated a poorly organized American militia at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bladensburg" title="Battle of Bladensburg">Battle of Bladensburg</a> on August 24, and that evening proceeded to enter Washington. The British Army <a href="/wiki/Burning_of_Washington" title="Burning of Washington">burned</a> many important U.S. Government buildings, including the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">Capitol</a>, demoralizing and greatly damaging the American war effort. Ross then led a British invasion north up the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> towards the city of <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a> which culminated in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Baltimore" title="Battle of Baltimore">Battle of Baltimore</a> that September. On September 12, he was shot while commanding troops at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_North_Point" title="Battle of North Point">North Point</a>, and died while being moved to the rear.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Ross_(British_Army_officer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ross was born in <a href="/wiki/Rostrevor" title="Rostrevor">Rostrevor</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Down" title="County Down">County Down</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Ireland</a>, to Major David Ross, an officer in the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a> and his mother, half-sister to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earl_of_Charlemont" title="Earl of Charlemont">Earl of Charlemont</a>. He was educated at <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Dublin" title="Trinity College, Dublin">Trinity College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, Ireland, where he was a treasurer of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/College_Historical_Society_(Trinity_College,_Dublin)" title="College Historical Society (Trinity College, Dublin)">College Historical Society</a> and joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/25th_Regiment_of_Foot" title="25th Regiment of Foot">25th Regiment of Foot</a> as an ensign in 1789.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Napoleonic_Wars">Napoleonic Wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Ross_(British_Army_officer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Napoleonic Wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Philip_James_de_Loutherbourg_-_The_Battle_of_Alexandria,_21_March_1801_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2805" data-file-width="4001" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Philip_James_de_Loutherbourg_-_The_Battle_of_Alexandria%2C_21_March_1801_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/220px-Philip_James_de_Loutherbourg_-_The_Battle_of_Alexandria%2C_21_March_1801_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Philip_James_de_Loutherbourg_-_The_Battle_of_Alexandria%2C_21_March_1801_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/330px-Philip_James_de_Loutherbourg_-_The_Battle_of_Alexandria%2C_21_March_1801_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Philip_James_de_Loutherbourg_-_The_Battle_of_Alexandria%2C_21_March_1801_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/440px-Philip_James_de_Loutherbourg_-_The_Battle_of_Alexandria%2C_21_March_1801_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Ross was present at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Alexandria" title="Battle of Alexandria">Battle of Alexandria</a>, which expelled <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>'s army from Egypt.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Ross fought as a junior officer at the battles of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Krabbendam_(1799)" title="Battle of Krabbendam (1799)">Krabbendam</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> in 1799 and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Alexandria" title="Battle of Alexandria">Battle of Alexandria</a> in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> in 1801. In 1803, he was promoted to major and given command of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/20th_Regiment_of_Foot" title="20th Regiment of Foot">20th Regiment of Foot</a>. He next fought at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Maida" title="Battle of Maida">Maida</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">Kingdom of Naples</a> in 1806.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peninsular_War">Peninsular War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Ross_(British_Army_officer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Peninsular War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He was promoted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant-Colonel_(United_Kingdom)" title="Lieutenant-Colonel (United Kingdom)">Lieutenant–Colonel</a> at the end of 1808 and fought in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Corunna" title="Battle of Corunna">Battle of Corunna</a> in Spain in early 1809 during the <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">Peninsular War</a>. In 1810, Ross was made a full <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_Kingdom)" title="Colonel (United Kingdom)">Colonel</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Aide-de-camp" title="Aide-de-camp">aide-de-camp</a> to the King.</p>
<p>In 1813, Ross was sent to serve under <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Wellesley,_1st_Duke_of_Wellington" title="Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington">Arthur Wellesley</a> and commanded his regiment at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Vittoria" title="Battle of Vittoria">battles of Vittoria</a>, Roncesvalles, and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sorauren" title="Battle of Sorauren">Sorauren</a> that year. He was seriously wounded in the left side of his neck at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Orthez" title="Battle of Orthez">Battle of Orthez</a>, on 27 February 1814, and had just returned to service when he was given command of an expeditionary force to attack the United States.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="War_of_1812">War of 1812</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Ross_(British_Army_officer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: War of 1812">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ross sailed to North America as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_General" title="Major General">Major General</a> to take charge of all British troops off the east coast of the United States. He personally led the British troops ashore in <a href="/wiki/Benedict,_Maryland" title="Benedict, Maryland">Benedict</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, and marched through <a href="/wiki/Upper_Marlboro,_Maryland" title="Upper Marlboro, Maryland">Upper Marlboro</a>, Maryland, to the attack on the Americans at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bladensburg" title="Battle of Bladensburg">Battle of Bladensburg</a> on 24 August 1814, causing the hastily organised militia of the American army to collapse into a rout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Markham1910_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Markham1910-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Moving on from Bladensburg, Ross moved on to nearby <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, and was fired upon; his horse was shot from under him. The public buildings, facilities and Navy Yards of the city, including the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">United States Capitol</a> and the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> were burned as retaliation for destructive American raids into Canada, most notably the Americans' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burning_of_York" title="Burning of York">Burning of York</a> (modern Toronto) earlier in 1813, which were themselves in retaliation to British raids into the United States. Controversy surrounds Ross's decision to destroy public property but spare private property during the burning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>Ross then was persuaded to attack <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a>, Maryland. His troops landed at the southern tip of the "Patapsco Neck" peninsula (between the <a href="/wiki/Patapsco_River" title="Patapsco River">Patapsco River</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helen_Delich_Bentley_Port_of_Baltimore" title="Helen Delich Bentley Port of Baltimore">Baltimore Harbor</a> on the south and <a href="/wiki/Back_River_(Maryland)" title="Back River (Maryland)">Back River</a> on the north) of southeastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore_County" title="Baltimore County">Baltimore County</a> at North Point, twelve miles southeast from the city, on the morning of 12 September 1814. En route to what would be the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_North_Point" title="Battle of North Point">Battle of North Point</a>, a part of the larger <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Baltimore" title="Battle of Baltimore">Battle of Baltimore</a>, the British advance encountered American skirmishers. General Ross rode forward to personally direct his troops. An American sharpshooter shot him through the right arm into the chest. According to Baltimore tradition, two American riflemen, Daniel Wells, 18, and Henry McComas, 19, fired at him and one of them had fired the fatal shot. Ross died while he was being transported back to the fleet.</p>
<p>Ross's body was preserved in a barrel of 129 gallons (586 l) of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaican</a> rum aboard <a href="/wiki/HMS_Tonnant_(1798)" title="HMS Tonnant (1798)">HMS <i>Tonnant</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> When the Tonnant was diverted to New Orleans for the forthcoming <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_New_Orleans" title="Battle of New Orleans">battle</a> in January 1815, his body was shipped on the British ship <a href="/wiki/HMS_Royal_Oak_(1809)" title="HMS Royal Oak (1809)">HMS <i>Royal Oak</i></a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_of_Halifax" title="City of Halifax">Halifax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, where his body was interred on 29 September 1814 in the <a href="/wiki/Old_Burying_Ground_(Halifax,_Nova_Scotia)" title="Old Burying Ground (Halifax, Nova Scotia)">Old Burying Ground</a>.</p>

<p>He is commemorated by a 99-foot granite obelisk near the shoreline of <a href="/wiki/Carlingford_Lough" title="Carlingford Lough">Carlingford Lough</a> in the Ross home village of <a href="/wiki/Rostrevor" title="Rostrevor">Rostrevor</a>, County Down in Northern Ireland, as well as by a monument in <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_Cathedral" title="St Paul's Cathedral">St Paul's Cathedral</a> in London, England. The inscription on the monument reads:</p>
<center>
<p><small>DEDICATED AT THE PUBLIC EXPENSE TO THE MEMORY<br/>
OF MAJOR GENERAL ROBERT ROSS<br/>
WHO HAVING UNDERTOOK AND EXECUTED AN ENTERPRISE AGAINST THE<br/>
CITY OF WASHINGTON, THE CAPITAL OF THE UNITED STATES OF AMERICA<br/>
WHICH WAS CROWNED WITH COMPLETE SUCCESS WAS KILLED SHORTLY<br/>
AFTERWARDS WHILE DIRECTING A SUCCESSFUL ATTACK UPON A SUPERIOR FORCE NEAR THE<br/>
CITY OF BALTIMORE ON THE 12TH DAY OF SEPTEMPTER 1814<br/></small></p>
</center>
<p>Neither General Ross nor his immediate descendants were <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knighted</a> or received a title of nobility. However, his descendants were given an <a href="/wiki/Augmentation_of_honour" title="Augmentation of honour">augmentation of honour</a> to the Ross <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armorial_bearings" title="Armorial bearings">armorial bearings</a> (namely, a second <a href="/wiki/Crest_(heraldry)" title="Crest (heraldry)">crest</a> in which an arm is seen grasping the <a href="/wiki/Flag_Acts_(United_States)#Flag_Act_of_1794" title="Flag Acts (United States)">15 stars and 15 stripes</a> on a broken staff) and the family name was changed to the <a href="/wiki/Victory_title" title="Victory title">victory title</a> <b>"Ross-of-Bladensburg"</b>, which was granted to his widow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In honour of the history of Washington, D.C., there is also a portrait of Ross in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">U. S. Capitol's rotunda</a> and several illustrations in various <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a> historical sites in the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a> area, including a Monument near the site off Old North Point Road where he supposedly was shot. Additional details and exhibits have been preserved in various Baltimore historical institutions, such as the <a href="/wiki/Flag_House_%26_Star-Spangled_Banner_Museum" title="Flag House &amp; Star-Spangled Banner Museum">Star Spangled Banner Flag House</a> (also known recently as the Flag House and Star-Spangled Banner Museum) and the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> site of <a href="/wiki/Fort_McHenry" title="Fort McHenry">Fort McHenry</a>'s visitor center exhibits and in the local Dundalk-Patapsco Neck Historical Society museum in <a href="/wiki/Dundalk,_Maryland" title="Dundalk, Maryland">Dundalk</a>.</p>
<p>Traditionally the two snipers/riflemen "Wells and McComas" (of the unit of Aisquith's Sharpshooters") were buried in a local churchyard mourned by their fellow soldiers and citizens of the Town, but later in the 1850s were exhumed and reburied after elaborate processions and funerals in a monumental tomb in Ashland Square, off of Orleans Street and North Gay Street in the <a href="/wiki/Jonestown,_Baltimore" title="Jonestown, Baltimore">Jonestown/Old Town</a> neighborhood of East Baltimore. City streets were also named for them in South Baltimore.</p>
