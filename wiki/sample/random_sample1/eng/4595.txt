<p><b>Robert Edward "Bob" Rubin</b> (born August 29, 1938) is an American lawyer, former cabinet member, and retired banking executive. He served as the 70th <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Secretary of the Treasury">United States Secretary of the Treasury</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Bill_Clinton" title="Presidency of Bill Clinton">Clinton administration</a>. Before his government service, he spent 26 years at <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a>, eventually serving as a member of the board and co-chairman from 1990 to 1992; Rubin oversaw the loosening of financial industry underwriting guidelines which had been intact since the 1930s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TaibbiRollingStone_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TaibbiRollingStone-1">[1]</a></sup> His most prominent post-government role was as director and senior counselor of <a href="/wiki/Citigroup" title="Citigroup">Citigroup</a>, where he performed ongoing advisory and representational roles for the firm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> From November to December 2007, he served temporarily as <a href="/wiki/Chairman" title="Chairman">chairman</a> of Citigroup<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-citigroup_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-citigroup-4">[4]</a></sup> and resigned from the company on January 9, 2009. He received more than $126 million in cash and stock during his tenure at Citigroup,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dash_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dash-5">[5]</a></sup> up through and including Citigroup's bailout by the U.S. Treasury.</p>
<p>He is currently engaged actively as a founder of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Hamilton_Project" title="The Hamilton Project">The Hamilton Project</a>, an economic policy think tank that produces research and proposals on how to create a growing economy that benefits more <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">Americans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-citigroup_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-citigroup-4">[4]</a></sup> He is co-chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Council_on_Foreign_Relations" title="Council on Foreign Relations">Council on Foreign Relations</a>, and is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Africa_Progress_Panel" title="Africa Progress Panel">Africa Progress Panel (APP)</a>, a group of ten distinguished individuals who advocate at the highest levels for equitable and sustainable development in Africa. Rubin also serves as chairman of the board of the <a href="/wiki/Local_Initiatives_Support_Corporation" title="Local Initiatives Support Corporation">Local Initiatives Support Corporation</a>, the nation's leading community development support organization, and serves on the board of trustees of Mount Sinai-NYU Health. Additionally, Rubin serves as counselor at <a href="/wiki/Centerview_Partners" title="Centerview Partners">Centerview Partners</a>, an investment banking advisory firm based in New York City.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_background">Education and background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Rubin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education and background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rubin was born in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, the son of Sylvia (née Seiderman) and Alexander Rubin, a wealthy Jewish family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He moved to <a href="/wiki/Miami_Beach,_Florida" title="Miami Beach, Florida">Miami Beach, Florida</a>, at an early age and graduated from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami_Beach_High_School" title="Miami Beach High School">Miami Beach High School</a>. He was a member of <a href="/wiki/Boy_Scouts_of_America" title="Boy Scouts of America">Boy Scout</a> Troop 35, sponsored by the <a href="/wiki/American_Legion" title="American Legion">American Legion</a>, and received the rank of <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Scout_(Boy_Scouts_of_America)" title="Eagle Scout (Boy Scouts of America)">Eagle Scout</a>. In 1960, Rubin graduated with an <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">A.B.</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Summa_cum_laude" title="Summa cum laude">summa cum laude</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard College</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-citigroup_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-citigroup-4">[4]</a></sup> He then attended <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_School" title="Harvard Law School">Harvard Law School</a> for three days before leaving to see the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_1999_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_1999-8">[8]</a></sup> He later attended the <a href="/wiki/London_School_of_Economics" title="London School of Economics">London School of Economics</a> and received an <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Laws" title="Bachelor of Laws">LL.B.</a> from <a href="/wiki/Yale_Law_School" title="Yale Law School">Yale Law School</a> in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-citigroup_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-citigroup-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rubin began his career as an attorney at the firm of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleary,_Gottlieb,_Steen_%26_Hamilton" title="Cleary, Gottlieb, Steen &amp; Hamilton">Cleary, Gottlieb, Steen &amp; Hamilton</a> in New York City. He joined <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a> in 1966 as an associate in the <a href="/wiki/Risk_arbitrage" title="Risk arbitrage">risk arbitrage</a> department,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_1999_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_1999-8">[8]</a></sup> becoming a general partner in 1971. He joined the management committee in 1980 along with <a href="/wiki/Jon_Corzine" title="Jon Corzine">Jon Corzine</a>. Rubin was Vice Chairman and Co-Chief Operating Officer from 1987 to 1990. From the end of 1990 to 1992, Rubin served as Co-Chairman and Co-Senior Partner along with <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Friedman_(PFIAB)" title="Stephen Friedman (PFIAB)">Stephen Friedman</a>.</p>
<p>Rubin has been awarded honorary degrees from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Miami" title="University of Miami">University of Miami</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinton_administration">Clinton administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Rubin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Clinton administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From January 25, 1993, to January 10, 1995, Rubin served in the White House as Assistant to the President for Economic Policy. In that capacity, he directed the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Economic_Council" title="United States National Economic Council">National Economic Council</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> created after winning the presidency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The National Economic Council, or NEC, enabled the White House to coordinate closely the workings of the Cabinet departments and agencies on policies ranging from budget and tax to international trade and alleviating poverty. The NEC coordinated policy recommendations going into the President's office, and monitored implementation of the decisions that came out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-citigroup_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-citigroup-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_S._Strauss" title="Robert S. Strauss">Robert S. Strauss</a> credited Rubin with making the system work. "He's surely the only man or woman in America that I know who could make the NEC succeed," Strauss said in 1994. "Anyone else would have been a disruptive force, and the council wouldn't have worked."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s_global_financial_crisis">1990s global financial crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Rubin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1990s global financial crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 1995, one year after the signing of the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Free_Trade_Agreement" title="North American Free Trade Agreement">North American Free Trade Agreement</a> (NAFTA) and immediately after Rubin was sworn in as Secretary of Treasury, Mexico was suffering through a <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis" title="Financial crisis">financial crisis</a> that threatened to result in it <a href="/wiki/Default_(finance)" title="Default (finance)">defaulting</a> on its foreign obligations. President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a>, with the advice of Secretary Rubin and Federal Reserve Board Chairman <a href="/wiki/Alan_Greenspan" title="Alan Greenspan">Alan Greenspan</a>, provided $20 billion in US <a href="/wiki/Loan_guarantee" title="Loan guarantee">loan guarantees</a> to the Mexican government through the <a href="/wiki/Exchange_Stabilization_Fund" title="Exchange Stabilization Fund">Exchange Stabilization Fund</a> (ESF).</p>
<p>In 1997 and 1998, Treasury Secretary Rubin, Deputy Secretary <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Summers" title="Lawrence Summers">Lawrence Summers</a>, and Federal Reserve Board Chairman <a href="/wiki/Alan_Greenspan" title="Alan Greenspan">Alan Greenspan</a> worked with the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a> and others to promote U.S. policy in response to financial crises in Russian, Asian, and Latin American financial markets. In its February 15, 1999, edition, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_Magazine" title="Time Magazine">Time Magazine</a></i> dubbed the three policymakers "The Committee to Save the World."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1998, Rubin received the U.S. Senator John Heinz Award for Greatest Public Service by an Elected or Appointed Official, an award given out annually by <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_Awards_for_Public_Service" title="Jefferson Awards for Public Service">Jefferson Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rubin was succeeded on July 1, 1999, as Treasury Secretary by his deputy, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lawrence_H._Summers" title="Lawrence H. Summers">Lawrence H. Summers</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_record_and_the_2008_global_financial_crisis">Economic record and the 2008 global financial crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Rubin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economic record and the 2008 global financial crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Subprime_mortgage_crisis_solutions_debate#Derivatives_regulation" title="Subprime mortgage crisis solutions debate">Subprime mortgage crisis solutions debate § Derivatives regulation</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%9308" title="Financial crisis of 2007–08">Financial crisis of 2007–08</a></div>
<p>Upon Rubin's retirement, Clinton called him the "greatest secretary of the Treasury since <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Hamilton" title="Alexander Hamilton">Alexander Hamilton</a>". On April 18, 2010, in an interview on ABC’s <i><a href="/wiki/This_Week_(ABC_TV_series)" title="This Week (ABC TV series)">This Week</a></i> program, Clinton said Rubin was wrong in the advice he gave him not to regulate derivatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Following the interview, Clinton's assistant <a href="/wiki/Doug_Band" title="Doug Band">Doug Band</a> reaffirmed those statements saying Clinton still wished he had pursued legislation to regulate derivatives while confirming that he still believed he had received excellent advice on the economy and the financial system from Rubin and others during his presidency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>"During his tenure as Treasury Secretary", Senator <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Hagel" title="Chuck Hagel">Chuck Hagel</a> (R-NE) said, "Bob was an ideal public servant who put policy before politics."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1997, Rubin and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve" title="Federal Reserve">Federal Reserve</a> chairman <a href="/wiki/Alan_Greenspan" title="Alan Greenspan">Alan Greenspan</a> strongly opposed giving the <a href="/wiki/Commodity_Futures_Trading_Commission" title="Commodity Futures Trading Commission">Commodity Futures Trading Commission</a> oversight of over-the-counter <a href="/wiki/Derivative_(finance)" title="Derivative (finance)">credit derivatives</a> when this was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Brooksley_Born" title="Brooksley Born">Brooksley Born</a>, the head of the CFTC. Rubin's role was highlighted in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Broadcasting_Service" title="Public Broadcasting Service">Public Broadcasting Service</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Frontline_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Frontline (U.S. TV series)">Frontline</a></i> report, "The Warning".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Over-the-counter credit derivatives were eventually excluded from regulation by the CFTC by the <a href="/wiki/Commodity_Futures_Modernization_Act_of_2000" title="Commodity Futures Modernization Act of 2000">Commodity Futures Modernization Act of 2000</a>. According to the <i>Frontline</i> documentary, they played a key role in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2008_financial_crisis" title="2008 financial crisis">2008 financial crisis</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_Levitt_Jr." title="Arthur Levitt Jr.">Arthur Levitt Jr.</a>, a former chairman of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a>, has said in explaining Rubin's strong opposition to the regulations proposed by Born that Greenspan and Rubin were "...joined at the hip on this. They were certainly very fiercely opposed to this and persuaded me that this would cause chaos."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-reckoning_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-reckoning-17">[17]</a></sup> However, in Rubin’s autobiography, he notes that he believed derivatives could pose significant problems and that many people who used derivatives did not fully understand the risks they were taking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rubin and his deputy Lawrence Summers also steered through the 1999 repeal of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass%E2%80%93Steagall_Act" title="Glass–Steagall Act">Glass–Steagall Act</a> (1933), which had separated investment banking from the retail side. It allowed the banks to develop and sell the mortgage-backed instruments that became a principal factor in the financial collapse. In September 2011, the UK Independent Commission on Banking released a report in which it recommended a separation of investment and retail banking to prevent a repeat of the 2008 crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a December 2009 <i>Newsweek</i> article, Rubin described the extraordinary combination of circumstances that led to the global financial crisis, including market and credit excesses, low interest rates, massive increase in the use of complex derivatives, misguided AAA ratings, stagnant median real wages, abusive mortgage practices, and the over-leveraging of financial institutions, among many other factors. In the article, Rubin advocates for the reform of the financial system in order to better protect against systemic risk and devastating crises in the future. Rubin says "the market-based model must be combined with strong and effective government, nationally and transnationally, to deal with critical challenges that markets won't adequately address."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 9, 2009, Citigroup announced that Rubin had resigned as a senior adviser and would not seek re-election as a director of the corporation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Press reports noted that Rubin had drawn criticism for his role in the bank's recent problems that drove it to seek U.S. Government assistance after he received significant personal compensation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-political_career">Post-political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Rubin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post-political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Upon leaving the Clinton administration, Rubin joined the board of the <a href="/wiki/Local_Initiatives_Support_Corporation" title="Local Initiatives Support Corporation">Local Initiatives Support Corporation</a> (LISC), the nation's leading community development support organization, as chairman.</p>
<p>Reflecting on his decision to join an institution devoted to bringing economic activity to neglected areas of the country, the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> said the following in an editorial: "Even before he became Bill Clinton's treasury secretary, during his days as a high-powered Wall Street executive, Rubin was passionate about fostering business investment as the way to fight poverty in depressed city and rural areas. That made him somewhat unusual among Democrats, who generally emphasized government anti-poverty programs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1999, affirming his career-long interest in markets, Rubin joined Citigroup as a board member and as a participant "in strategic managerial and operational matters of the Company, but [...] no line responsibilities".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ20081203_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ20081203-24">[24]</a></sup> The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" title="Wall Street Journal">Wall Street Journal</a></i> called this mix of oversight and management responsibilities "murky".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ20081203_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ20081203-24">[24]</a></sup> In an interview with the Journal, Rubin said: "I think I've been a very constructive part of the Citigroup environment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ20081203_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ20081203-24">[24]</a></sup> Separately, the <i>Journal</i> noted that Citigroup shareholders have suffered losses of more than 70 percent since Rubin joined the firm and that he encouraged changes that led the firm to the brink of collapse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ20081203_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ20081203-24">[24]</a></sup> In December 2008, investors filed a lawsuit contending that Citigroup executives, including Rubin, sold shares at inflated prices while concealing the firm's risks. A Citigroup spokesman said the lawsuit was without merit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Earlier, Rubin had also come in for some criticism for his actions on Citigroup's behalf during the 2001 collapse of energy giant <a href="/wiki/Enron" title="Enron">Enron Corp.</a>, a major client of the bank. With the troubled Enron facing a credit ratings downgrade - a potentially disastrous development for the energy company's investors, the company itself and ultimately, its lenders - Rubin called a ranking Treasury Department official, unsuccessfully seeking the Bush Administration's help in forestalling the downgrade. Rubin later maintained that he had acted both as a Citigroup executive protecting his company's position and as a former Treasury official concerned about the impact that Enron's failure might have on the larger economy, brushing off the notion of any possible conflict of interest, and said that if faced with the same choice, he would do it again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 8, 2001, he was presented with the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Citizens_Medal" title="Presidential Citizens Medal">Presidential Citizens Medal</a> by President Clinton.</p>
<p>On July 1, 2002, Rubin became a member of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard_Corporation" title="Harvard Corporation">Harvard Corporation</a>, the executive governing board of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>. This happened one year after he had received an honorary doctoral degree from the same university.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> He served as a member of the Harvard Corporation until June 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rubin has written a memoir, <i>In an Uncertain World: Tough Choices from Wall Street to Washington</i> (<a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9780375505850">ISBN 978-0-375-50585-0</a>), co-written by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Weisberg" title="Jacob Weisberg">Jacob Weisberg</a>. It was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times_Best_Seller_list" title="New York Times Best Seller list"><i>New York Times</i> bestseller</a> as well as one of <i>Business Week</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s ten best business books of 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-citigroup_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-citigroup-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rubin had been suggested as a possible appointee to a cabinet post for President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>. Rubin, alongside <a href="/wiki/Austan_Goolsbee" title="Austan Goolsbee">Austan Goolsbee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Volcker" title="Paul Volcker">Paul Volcker</a>, was one of Obama's economic advisers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloomberg20080916_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg20080916-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, Rubin became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Africa_Progress_Panel" title="Africa Progress Panel">Africa Progress Panel</a> (APP), a group of ten distinguished individuals who advocate at the highest levels for equitable and sustainable development in Africa. Every year, the Panel releases a report that outlines an issue of immediate importance to the continent and suggests a set of associated policies.</p>
<p>In January 2014, Secretary Rubin joined former Senator Olympia Snowe, former Education Secretary Donna Shalala, former Secretary of State George Shultz, former Housing and Urban Affairs Secretary Henry Cisneros, Gregory Page the Chair of Cargill, and Al Sommer, the Dean Emeritus of the Bloomberg School of Public Health as members of the U.S. Climate Risk Committee. They oversaw the development of an analysis of the economic risks of climate change in the United States that was published on June 24, 2014<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an address at the Climate Leadership Conference<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> on March 4, 2015, Mr. Rubin spoke about the economic effects of climate change and the costs of inaction. Calling climate change “the existential threat of our age,” he called for the adoption of three proposals – revising estimates of the Gross Domestic Product to reflect climate change externalities, disclosure to investors by companies of the costs of carbon they emit that they might be required to absorb, and including in the U.S. government’s fiscal projections the future costs of dealing with climate change – to help catalyze a more active response to climate change risks. He first outlined the proposals in a Washington Post op-ed column titled “How Ignoring Climate Change Could Sink the U.S. Economy.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2016, he was one of eight former Treasury secretaries who called on the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> to remain a member of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> ahead of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_European_Union_membership_referendum,_2016" title="United Kingdom European Union membership referendum, 2016">June 2016 Referendum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Rubin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rubin is married to Judith Leah (Oxenberg) Rubin, who served as the New York City Commissioner of <a href="/wiki/Protocol_(politics)" title="Protocol (politics)">Protocol</a> for four years under Mayor <a href="/wiki/David_Dinkins" title="David Dinkins">David Dinkins</a>. The Rubins have two grown sons, James ("Jamie") Rubin (not to be confused with the former diplomat and journalist <a href="/wiki/James_Rubin" title="James Rubin">James Rubin</a> who served under President Clinton), and Philip Rubin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Jamie is married to writer <a href="/wiki/Gretchen_Rubin" title="Gretchen Rubin">Gretchen Rubin</a>. The Rubins were long time members of Temple Beth Shalom on Miami Beach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Positions_held">Positions held</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Rubin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Positions held">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During his time in the <a href="/wiki/Private_sector" title="Private sector">private sector</a>, Rubin has served on the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a> of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a>, Citigroup, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard_Corporation" title="Harvard Corporation">Harvard Corporation</a>, the New York Futures Exchange, the New York City Partnership and the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_National_Policy" title="Center for National Policy">Center for National Policy</a>. He has also served on the board of trustees of the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Corporation_of_New_York" title="Carnegie Corporation of New York">Carnegie Corporation of New York</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Sinai_Hospital,_New_York" title="Mount Sinai Hospital, New York">Mt. Sinai Hospital and Medical School</a>, the President's Advisory Committee for Trade Negotiations, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a> Market Oversight and Financial Services Advisory Committee, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mayor_of_New_York" title="Mayor of New York">Mayor of New York</a>'s Council of Economic Advisors and the Governor's Council on Fiscal and Economic Priorities for the State of New York. He has been co-chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Council_on_Foreign_Relations" title="Council on Foreign Relations">Council on Foreign Relations</a> since June 2007. On November 4, 2007, he became the Chairman of Citigroup and on January 9, 2009 he resigned from the position of Senior Counselor at Citigroup and announced he would not stand for re-election to the board.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compensation">Compensation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Rubin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Compensation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rubin received over $17 million in compensation from Citigroup and a further $33 million in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executive_stock_option" title="Executive stock option">stock options</a> as of 2008<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and total compensation of $126 million from Citigroup between 1999 and 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloomberg.com_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg.com-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition">Recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Rubin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is an exhibit about Robert Rubin's public service in FIU's <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Museum_of_Florida" title="Jewish Museum of Florida">Jewish Museum of Florida</a> in Miami Beach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Rubin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As Clinton's two-term Secretary of the Treasury, Rubin sharply opposed any regulation of <a href="/wiki/Collateralized_debt_obligation" title="Collateralized debt obligation">collateralized debt obligations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Credit_default_swap" title="Credit default swap">credit default swaps</a> and other so-called <a href="/wiki/Derivative_(finance)" title="Derivative (finance)">"derivative" financial instruments</a> which—despite having already created havoc for companies such as <a href="/wiki/Procter_%26_Gamble" title="Procter &amp; Gamble">Procter &amp; Gamble</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gibson_Greetings" title="Gibson Greetings">Gibson Greetings</a>, and disastrous consequences in 1994 for <a href="/wiki/Orange_County,_California" title="Orange County, California">Orange County, California</a> with its $1.5 billion default and subsequent bankruptcy—were nevertheless becoming the chief engine of profitability for Rubin's former employer <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a> and other Wall Street firms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ProctorDoctor_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-ProctorDoctor-39">[39]</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/Brooksley_Born" title="Brooksley Born">Brooksley Born</a>, head of the <a href="/wiki/Commodity_Futures_Trading_Commission" title="Commodity Futures Trading Commission">Commodity Futures Trading Commission</a>, circulated a letter urging increased regulation of derivatives in line with a 1994 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Accounting_Office" title="General Accounting Office">General Accounting Office</a> report, Rubin took the unusual step (for a Secretary of the Treasury) of going public in June 1998 to denounce Born and her proposal, eventually urging that the CFTC be stripped of its regulatory authority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ProctorDoctor_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-ProctorDoctor-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rubin sparked controversy in 2001 when he contacted an acquaintance at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Treasury_Department" title="U.S. Treasury Department">U.S. Treasury Department</a> and asked if the department could convince bond-rating agencies not to downgrade the corporate debt of <a href="/wiki/Enron" title="Enron">Enron</a>, a debtor of Citigroup. The Treasury official refused. A subsequent congressional staff investigation cleared Rubin of having done anything illegal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Journalist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Scheer" title="Robert Scheer">Robert Scheer</a>, in his book <i>The Great American Stickup</i>, claims the repeal of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass%E2%80%93Steagall_Act" title="Glass–Steagall Act">Glass–Steagall Act</a> was a key factor in the 2008 financial crisis.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (December 2011)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Enacted just after the 1930s <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, the Glass–Steagall Act separated commercial and investment banking. The law was repealed by Congress in 1999 during the Clinton presidency, while Rubin was Treasury Secretary. President Obama's current banking system proposals<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Precise_language" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The text near this tag is dated.">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> appear to be a return to these former principles although there is no talk of reinstating Glass–Steagall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <i>Bloomberg Businessweek</i>, Rubin pursued a romantic interest with <a href="/wiki/Iris_Mack" title="Iris Mack">Iris Mack</a> and did not tell her that he was married.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloomberg.com_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg.com-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
