<p><b>Robert Brown</b> <span style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society_of_Edinburgh" title="Fellow of the Royal Society of Edinburgh">FRSE</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society_of_London" title="Fellow of the Royal Society of London">FRS</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Linnean_Society_of_London" title="Fellow of the Linnean Society of London">FLS</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_the_Wernerian_Society" title="Member of the Wernerian Society">MWS</a></span> (21 December 1773 – 10 June 1858) was a Scottish botanist and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palaeobotanist" title="Palaeobotanist">palaeobotanist</a> who made important contributions to botany largely through his pioneering use of the microscope. His contributions include one of the earliest detailed descriptions of the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">cell nucleus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasmic_streaming" title="Cytoplasmic streaming">cytoplasmic streaming</a>; the observation of <a href="/wiki/Brownian_motion" title="Brownian motion">Brownian motion</a>; early work on plant <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fertilisation" title="Fertilisation">fertilisation</a>, including being the first to recognise the fundamental difference between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gymnosperms" title="Gymnosperms">gymnosperms</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiosperms" title="Angiosperms">angiosperms</a>; and some of the earliest studies in <a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">palynology</a>. He also made numerous contributions to plant taxonomy, including the erection of a number of plant families that are still accepted today; and numerous <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> plant genera and species, the fruit of his exploration of that continent with <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Flinders" title="Matthew Flinders">Matthew Flinders</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Brown_(Scottish_botanist_from_Montrose)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brown was born in <a href="/wiki/Montrose,_Angus" title="Montrose, Angus">Montrose</a> on 21 December 1773. He was the son of <a href="/wiki/James_Brown_(Scottish_clergyman)" title="James Brown (Scottish clergyman)">James Brown</a>, a minister in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Episcopal_Church" title="Scottish Episcopal Church">Scottish Episcopal Church</a> with <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobite</a> convictions so strong that in 1788 he defied his church's decision to give allegiance to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_III" title="George III">George III</a>. His mother was Helen <i>née</i> Taylor, the daughter of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterian" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterian</a> minister. As a child Brown attended the local Grammar School (now called <a href="/wiki/Montrose_Academy" title="Montrose Academy">Montrose Academy</a>), then <a href="/wiki/Marischal_College" title="Marischal College">Marischal College</a> at <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>, but withdrew in his fourth year when the family moved to <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> in 1790. His father died late the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mabberley_1985_p15.E2.80.9318_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mabberley_1985_p15.E2.80.9318-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brown enrolled to study <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a>, but developed an interest in <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a>, and ended up spending more of his time on the latter than the former. He attended the lectures of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Walker_(naturalist)" title="John Walker (naturalist)">John Walker</a>; made botanical expeditions into the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Scottish Highlands</a>, alone or with nurserymen such as <a href="/wiki/George_Don" title="George Don">George Don</a>; and wrote out meticulous botanical descriptions of the plants he collected. He also began corresponding with and collecting for <a href="/wiki/William_Withering" title="William Withering">William Withering</a>, one of the foremost British botanists of his day. Highlights for Brown during this period include his discovery of a new species of grass, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alopecurus_alpinus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alopecurus alpinus (page does not exist)">Alopecurus alpinus</a></i>; and his first botanical paper, "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_botanical_history_of_Angus" title="wikisource:The botanical history of Angus">The botanical history of Angus</a>", read to the Edinburgh Natural History Society in January 1792, but not published in print in Brown's lifetime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mabberley_1985_p18.E2.80.9328_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mabberley_1985_p18.E2.80.9328-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Brown dropped out of his medical course in 1793. Late in 1794, he enlisted in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fifeshire_Fencibles&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fifeshire Fencibles (page does not exist)">Fifeshire Fencibles</a>, and his regiment was posted to Ireland shortly after. In June 1795 he was appointed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surgeon%27s_Mate" title="Surgeon's Mate">Surgeon's Mate</a>. His regiment saw very little action, however, he had a good deal of leisure time, almost all of which he spent on botany. He was frustrated by his itinerant lifestyle, which prevented him from building his personal library and specimen collection as he would have liked, and cut him off from the most important herbaria and libraries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mabberley_1985_p28.E2.80.9360_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mabberley_1985_p28.E2.80.9360-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>During this period Brown was especially interested in <a href="/wiki/Cryptogam" title="Cryptogam">cryptogams</a>, and these would be the subject of Brown's first, albeit unattributed, publication. Brown began a correspondence with <a href="/wiki/James_Dickson_(botanist)" title="James Dickson (botanist)">James Dickson</a>, and by 1796 was sending him specimens and descriptions of mosses. Dickson incorporated Brown's descriptions into his <i>Fasciculi plantarum cryptogamicarum britanniae</i>, with Brown's permission but without any attribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mabberley_1985_p28.E2.80.9360_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mabberley_1985_p28.E2.80.9360-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1800, Brown was firmly established amongst Irish botanists, and was corresponding with a number of British and foreign botanists, including Withering, Dickson, <a href="/wiki/James_Edward_Smith" title="James Edward Smith">James Edward Smith</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Correia_da_Serra" title="José Correia da Serra">José Correia da Serra</a>. He had been nominated to the <a href="/wiki/Linnean_Society_of_London" title="Linnean Society of London">Linnean Society of London</a>; had contributed to Dickson's <i>Fasciculi</i>; was acknowledged in a number of other works; and had had a species of <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, <i>Conferva brownii</i> (now <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aegagropila_linnaei" title="Aegagropila linnaei">Aegagropila linnaei</a></i>) named after him by <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Weston_Dillwyn" title="Lewis Weston Dillwyn">Lewis Weston Dillwyn</a>. He had also begun experimenting with <a href="/wiki/Microscopy" title="Microscopy">microscopy</a>. However, as an army surgeon stationed in Ireland there seemed little prospect of him attracting the notice of those who could offer him a career in botany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mabberley_1985_p28.E2.80.9360_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mabberley_1985_p28.E2.80.9360-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="To_Australia_on_the_Investigator">To Australia on the <i>Investigator</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Brown_(Scottish_botanist_from_Montrose)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: To Australia on the Investigator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1798, Brown heard that <a href="/wiki/Mungo_Park_(explorer)" title="Mungo Park (explorer)">Mungo Park</a> had withdrawn from a proposed expedition into the interior of <a href="/wiki/New_Holland_(Australia)" title="New Holland (Australia)">New Holland</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>), leaving a vacancy for a <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">naturalist</a>. At Brown's request, Correia wrote to <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Banks" title="Joseph Banks">Sir Joseph Banks</a>, suggesting Brown as a suitable replacement:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"Science is the gainer in this change of man; Mr Brown being a professed naturalist. He is a Scotchman, fit to pursue an object with constance and cold mind."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>He was not selected, and the expedition did not end up going ahead as originally proposed, though <a href="/wiki/George_Caley" title="George Caley">George Caley</a> was sent to <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> as a botanical collector for Banks. In 1800, however, <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Flinders" title="Matthew Flinders">Matthew Flinders</a> put to Banks a proposal for an expedition that would answer the question whether New Holland was one island or several. Banks approved Flinders' proposal, and in December 1800 wrote to Brown offering him the position of naturalist to the expedition. Brown accepted immediately.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mabberley_1985_p59.E2.80.9363_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mabberley_1985_p59.E2.80.9363-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Brown_(Scottish_botanist_from_Montrose)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Brown was told to expect to sail at the end of 1800, only a few weeks after being offered the position. A succession of delays meant the voyage did not get under way until July 1801. Brown spent much of the meantime preparing for the voyage by studying Banks' Australian plant specimens and copying out notes and descriptions for use on the voyage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mabberley_1985_p66-72_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mabberley_1985_p66-72-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though Brown's brief was collect scientific specimens of all sorts, he was told to give priority to plants, insects, and birds, and to treat other fields, such as <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, as secondary pursuits. In addition to Brown, the scientific staff comprised the renowned <a href="/wiki/Botanical_illustrator" title="Botanical illustrator">botanical illustrator</a> <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Bauer" title="Ferdinand Bauer">Ferdinand Bauer</a>; the gardener <a href="/wiki/Peter_Good" title="Peter Good">Peter Good</a>, whose task was to collect live plants and viable seed for the use of <a href="/wiki/Kew_Gardens" title="Kew Gardens">Kew Gardens</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Miner" title="Miner">miner</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Allen_(miner)" title="John Allen (miner)">John Allen</a>, appointed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mineralogist" title="Mineralogist">mineralogist</a>; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landscape_artist" title="Landscape artist">landscape artist</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Westall" title="William Westall">William Westall</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Crosley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Crosley (page does not exist)">John Crosley</a>, who would fall ill on the voyage out and leave the ship at the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a>, being belatedly replaced at Sydney by <a href="/wiki/James_Inman" title="James Inman">James Inman</a>. Brown was given authority over Bauer and Good, both of whom were instructed to give any specimens they might collect to Brown, rather than forming separate collections. Both men would provide enthusiastic and hard-working companions for Brown, and thus Brown's specimen collections contain material collected by all three men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mabberley_1985_p66-72_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mabberley_1985_p66-72-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Desertas.2C_Madeira_and_the_Cape_of_Good_Hope">Desertas, Madeira and the Cape of Good Hope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Brown_(Scottish_botanist_from_Montrose)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Desertas, Madeira and the Cape of Good Hope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Investigator</i> sailed from London on 18 July. They made brief landfalls at <a href="/wiki/Bugio_Island" title="Bugio Island">Bugio Island</a> (<a href="/wiki/Desertas_Islands" title="Desertas Islands">Desertas Islands</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>, but Brown was disappointed to collect almost nothing of note from either site. They arrived at the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a> on 16 October, staying a little over two weeks, during which time Brown made extensive botanical expeditions, and climbed <a href="/wiki/Table_Mountain" title="Table Mountain">Table Mountain</a> at least twice. Many years later he would write to <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Harvey" title="William Henry Harvey">William Henry Harvey</a>, who was considering emigrating there, that "some of the pleasantest botanizing he ever had was on <a href="/wiki/Devil%27s_Peak_(Cape_Town)" title="Devil's Peak (Cape Town)">Devil's Mountain</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>, and he thought I could not pitch on a more delightful field of study."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvey_1869_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harvey_1869-7">[7]</a></sup> Amongst the plants collected at the Cape were two new species of <i><a href="/wiki/Serruria" title="Serruria">Serruria</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Proteaceae" title="Proteaceae">Proteaceae</a>), <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Serruria_foeniculacea&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Serruria foeniculacea (page does not exist)">S. foeniculacea</a></i> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Serruria_flagellaris&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Serruria flagellaris (page does not exist)">S. flagellaris</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mabberley_1985_p73.E2.80.9379_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mabberley_1985_p73.E2.80.9379-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Brown_(Scottish_botanist_from_Montrose)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/HMS_Investigator_(1801)" title="HMS Investigator (1801)"><i>Investigator</i></a> arrived in <a href="/wiki/King_George_Sound" title="King George Sound">King George Sound</a> in what is now <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a> in December 1801. For three and a half years Brown did intensive botanic research in Australia, collecting about 3400 species, of which about 2000 were previously unknown. A large part of this collection was lost when <a href="/wiki/HMS_Porpoise_(1799)" title="HMS Porpoise (1799)"><i>Porpoise</i></a> was wrecked <i>en route</i> to England.</p>
<p>Brown remained in Australia until May 1805. He then returned to Britain where he spent the next five years working on the material he had gathered. He published numerous species descriptions; in Western Australia alone he is the author of nearly 1200 species. The list of major Australian genera that he named includes: <a href="/wiki/Livistona" title="Livistona">Livistona</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triodia_(plant)" title="Triodia (plant)">Triodia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eriachne" title="Eriachne">Eriachne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caladenia" title="Caladenia">Caladenia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isolepis" title="Isolepis">Isolepis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prasophyllum" title="Prasophyllum">Prasophyllum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pterostylis" title="Pterostylis">Pterostylis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patersonia" title="Patersonia">Patersonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conostylis" title="Conostylis">Conostylis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thysanotus" title="Thysanotus">Thysanotus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pityrodia" title="Pityrodia">Pityrodia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemigenia" title="Hemigenia">Hemigenia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lechenaultia" title="Lechenaultia">Lechenaultia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eremophila_(plant)" title="Eremophila (plant)">Eremophila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logania" title="Logania">Logania</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dryandra" title="Dryandra">Dryandra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isopogon" title="Isopogon">Isopogon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grevillea" title="Grevillea">Grevillea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petrophile" title="Petrophile">Petrophile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waratah" title="Waratah">Telopea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leptomeria" title="Leptomeria">Leptomeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacksonia_(plant)" title="Jacksonia (plant)">Jacksonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leucopogon" title="Leucopogon">Leucopogon</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stenopetalum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stenopetalum (page does not exist)">Stenopetalum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ptilotus" title="Ptilotus">Ptilotus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sclerolaena" title="Sclerolaena">Sclerolaena</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhagodia" title="Rhagodia">Rhagodia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>In early 1809 he read his paper called <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_natural_order_of_plants_called_Proteaceae" title="On the natural order of plants called Proteaceae">On the natural order of plants called Proteaceae</a></i> to the <a href="/wiki/Linnean_Society_of_London" title="Linnean Society of London">Linnean Society of London</a>. This was subsequently published in March 1810 as <i>On the Proteaceae of Jussieu</i>. It is significant for its contribution to the <a href="/wiki/Systematics" title="Systematics">systematics</a> of Proteaceae, and to the <a href="/wiki/Floristics" title="Floristics">floristics</a> of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and also for its application of <a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">palynology</a> to systematics. This work was extensively plagiarised by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Anthony_Salisbury" title="Richard Anthony Salisbury">Richard Anthony Salisbury</a>, who had memorised much of the Linnean reading and then inserted it in <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Knight_(horticulturist)" title="Joseph Knight (horticulturist)">Joseph Knight's</a> 1809 publication <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_cultivation_of_the_plants_belonging_to_the_natural_order_of_Proteeae" title="On the cultivation of the plants belonging to the natural order of Proteeae">On the cultivation of the plants belonging to the natural order of Proteeae</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1810, he published the results of his collecting in his famous <i><a href="/wiki/Prodromus_Florae_Novae_Hollandiae_et_Insulae_Van_Diemen" title="Prodromus Florae Novae Hollandiae et Insulae Van Diemen">Prodromus Florae Novae Hollandiae et Insulae Van Diemen</a></i>, the first systematic account of the Australian flora. That year, he succeeded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jonas_C._Dryander" title="Jonas C. Dryander">Jonas C. Dryander</a> as Sir <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Banks" title="Joseph Banks">Joseph Banks</a>' librarian, and on Banks' death in 1820 Brown inherited his <a href="/wiki/Library" title="Library">library</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herbarium" title="Herbarium">herbarium</a>. This was transferred to the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> in 1827, and Brown was appointed <a href="/wiki/Keeper_of_Botany,_Natural_History_Museum" title="Keeper of Botany, Natural History Museum">Keeper of the Banksian Botanical Collection</a>.</p>
<p>In 1818 he published <i><a href="/wiki/Observations,_systematical_and_geographical,_on_the_herbarium_collected_by_Professor_Christian_Smith,_in_the_vicinity_of_the_Congo" title="Observations, systematical and geographical, on the herbarium collected by Professor Christian Smith, in the vicinity of the Congo">Observations, systematical and geographical, on the herbarium collected by Professor Christian Smith, in the vicinity of the Congo</a></i>. In 1822, he was elected a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Linnean_Society" title="Fellow of the Linnean Society">Fellow of the Linnean Society</a> and a foreign member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Swedish_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences">Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences</a>. In 1827 he became correspondent of the Royal Institute of the Netherlands, three years later he became associated member. When the institute became the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences">Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> in 1851 Brown joined as foreign member.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He was elected a Foreign Honorary Member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> in 1849.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAAS_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a paper read to the Linnean society in 1831 and published in 1833, Brown named the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">cell nucleus</a>. The nucleus had been observed before, perhaps as early as 1682 by the Dutch microscopist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leeuwenhoek" title="Leeuwenhoek">Leeuwenhoek</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Bauer" title="Franz Bauer">Franz Bauer</a> had noted and drawn it as a regular feature of plant cells in 1802, but it was Brown who gave it the name it bears to this day (while giving credit to Bauer's drawings). Neither Bauer nor Brown thought the nucleus to be universal, and Brown thought it to be primarily confined to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monocotyledons" title="Monocotyledons">Monocotyledons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the division of the Natural History Department of the British Museum into three sections in 1837, Robert Brown became the first <a href="/wiki/Keeper_of_Botany,_Natural_History_Museum" title="Keeper of Botany, Natural History Museum">Keeper of the Botanical Department</a>, remaining so until his death. He was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/John_Joseph_Bennett" title="John Joseph Bennett">John Joseph Bennett</a>.</p>
<p>He served as President of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linnean_Society" title="Linnean Society">Linnean Society</a> from 1849 to 1853.</p>
<p>Brown died at 17 Dean Street, <a href="/wiki/Soho_Square" title="Soho Square">Soho Square</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, on 10 June 1858.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spectator_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-spectator-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnnalsNatHist1858_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-AnnalsNatHist1858-16">[16]</a></sup> He was buried in <a href="/wiki/Kensal_Green_Cemetery" title="Kensal Green Cemetery">Kensal Green Cemetery</a> in London.</p>
<p>Brown's name is commemorated in the Australia <a href="/wiki/Herb" title="Herb">herb</a> <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Brunonia" title="Brunonia">Brunonia</a></i> as well as numerous Australian species such as <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_brownii" title="Eucalyptus brownii">Eucalyptus brownii</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Banksia_brownii" title="Banksia brownii">Banksia brownii</a></i> and the moss <a href="/wiki/Tetrodontium_brownianum" title="Tetrodontium brownianum">Brown's Tetrodontium Moss</a> (<i>Tetrodontium brownianum</i>), a species which he discovered growing at <a href="/wiki/Roslin,_Midlothian" title="Roslin, Midlothian">Roslin</a> near <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> whilst still a student. The plant can still be found at the site of its discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RBGE_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-RBGE-17">[17]</a></sup> Passing through the suburb of Kingston, south of Hobart, Tasmania, formerly Van Diemen's Land, is <a href="/wiki/Browns_River_(Tasmania)" title="Browns River (Tasmania)">Brown's River</a>, named in his honor, upon the banks of which, he collected botanical samples. In South Australia, Mount Brown and Point Brown (near <a href="/wiki/Smoky_Bay,_South_Australia" title="Smoky Bay, South Australia">Smoky Bay</a>) were named for him by Flinders during the <i>Investigator</i> expedition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mount_Brown_(British_Columbia)" title="Mount Brown (British Columbia)">Mount Brown</a> in <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, Canada is named for him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1938 the <a href="/wiki/London_County_Council" title="London County Council">London County Council</a> commemorated Brown, as well as botanists <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Banks" title="Joseph Banks">Joseph Banks</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Don" title="David Don">David Don</a>, and meetings of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linnean_Society" title="Linnean Society">Linnean Society</a>, with a rectangular stone plaque at 32 <a href="/wiki/Soho_Square" title="Soho Square">Soho Square</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EngHet_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-EngHet-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The standard <a href="/wiki/List_of_botanists_by_author_abbreviation" title="List of botanists by author abbreviation">author abbreviation</a> <b><span class="vcard"><span class="fn n"><span class="nickname">R.Br.</span></span></span></b> is used to indicate this individual as the author when <a href="/wiki/Author_citation_(botany)" title="Author citation (botany)">citing</a> a <a href="/wiki/Botanical_name" title="Botanical name">botanical name</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brownian_motion">Brownian motion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Brown_(Scottish_botanist_from_Montrose)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Brownian motion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1827, while examining grains of pollen of the plant <i><a href="/wiki/Clarkia_pulchella" title="Clarkia pulchella">Clarkia pulchella</a></i> suspended in water under a <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">microscope</a>, Brown observed minute particles, now known to be amyloplasts (starch organelles) and spherosomes (lipid organelles), ejected from the pollen grains, executing a continuous jittery motion. He then observed the same motion in particles of inorganic matter, enabling him to rule out the hypothesis that the effect was life-related. Although Brown did not provide a theory to explain the motion, and <a href="/wiki/Jan_Ingenhousz" title="Jan Ingenhousz">Jan Ingenhousz</a> already had reported a similar effect using charcoal particles, in German and French publications of 1784 and 1785,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> the phenomenon is now known as <a href="/wiki/Brownian_motion" title="Brownian motion">Brownian motion</a>.</p>
<p>In recent years controversy arose over whether Brown's microscopes were insufficient to reveal phenomena of this order. Brown's re-discoveries were denied in a brief paper in 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Shortly thereafter, in an illustrated presentation, British microscopist <a href="/wiki/Brian_J._Ford" title="Brian J. Ford">Brian J. Ford</a> presented to Inter Micro 1991 in Chicago a reprise of the demonstration using Brown's original microscope. His video sequences substantiated Brown's observations, suggesting Brown's microscope was sufficient to allow him to see motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Physicist Phil Pearle and colleagues presented a detailed discussion of Brown's original observations of particles from pollen of <i><a href="/wiki/Clarkia_pulchella" title="Clarkia pulchella">Clarkia pulchella</a></i> undergoing Brownian motion, including the relevant history, botany, microscopy, and physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
