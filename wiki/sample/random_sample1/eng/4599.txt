<p><b>Robert Sengstacke Abbott</b> (November 24, 1870 – February 29, 1940) was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American" title="African-American">African-American</a> lawyer and newspaper publisher and editor. Abbott founded the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chicago_Defender" title="The Chicago Defender">The Chicago Defender</a></i> newspaper, which grew to have the highest circulation of any black-owned newspaper in the country. An early adherent of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD" title="Bahá'í">Bahá'í</a> religion in the United States, he founded the <a href="/wiki/Bud_Billiken_Parade_and_Picnic" title="Bud Billiken Parade and Picnic">Bud Billiken Parade and Picnic</a> in 1929, which has developed as a celebration in Chicago of African-American life.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Sengstacke_Abbott&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Sengstacke_Abbott&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Abbot was born on November 24, 1870 in <a href="/wiki/St._Simons,_Georgia" title="St. Simons, Georgia">St. Simons Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> (although some sources state <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWICA-1933_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-WWICA-1933-1">[1]</a></sup>) to <a href="/wiki/Freedman" title="Freedman">freedman</a> parents, who had been enslaved before the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>. The Sea Islands were a place of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gullah_people" title="Gullah people">Gullah people</a>, an African-descended ethnic group who continued stronger aspects of African cultures than among African Americans in other areas of the South. His father Thomas Abbott died when Robert was a baby.</p>
<p>His widowed mother Flora Abbott (<i>née</i> Butler) met and married John Sengstacke, an American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mixed-race" title="Mixed-race">mixed-race</a> man of unusual background who had recently come to the US from Germany. His parents were Tama, a freed slave woman of African descent, and her husband Herman Sengstacke, a German sea captain who had a regular route from Hamburg to Savannah. In the Georgia port city in 1847, Herman saw a slave sale. He was so distressed he bought the freedom of Tama, a young woman from West Africa. They married in Charleston, South Carolina, before returning to Georgia, where their interracial marriage was prohibited. Their mixed-race son John was born the next year and a daughter in 1848. Tama died soon after their daughter was born, and Herman took the children back to Germany to be raised by family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-familypapers_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-familypapers-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>John met the young black widow Flora, who had a year-old son Robert. He cared for Robert as if he were his own. Together the couple had seven children together; their family crossed rigid racial boundaries. Robert was given the middle name Sengstacke to mark his belonging in the family. John Sengstacke had become a Congregationalist missionary as an adult; he wrote, "There is but one church, and all who are born of God are members of it. God made a church, man made denominations. God gave us a Holy Bible, disputing men made different kinds of disciples."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-door_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-door-3">[3]</a></sup> Sengstacke became a teacher, determined to improve the education of black children. He also became a publisher, founding the <i>Woodville Times,</i> based in what was then a town named Woodville; it was later annexed by the city of <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a>.</p>
<p>Given the industrialization under way in the country, from 1892 to 1896, Abbott studied the printing trade at <a href="/wiki/Hampton_University" title="Hampton University">Hampton Institute</a> (now Hampton University), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historically_black_college" title="Historically black college">historically black college</a> in Virginia. At Hampton, he sang with the "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hampton_Choir_and_Quartet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hampton Choir and Quartet (page does not exist)">Hampton Choir and Quartet</a>," which toured nationally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWICA-1933_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-WWICA-1933-1">[1]</a></sup> He earned a <a href="/wiki/Law_degree" title="Law degree">law degree</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kent_College_of_Law" title="Kent College of Law">Kent College of Law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, in 1898.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Sengstacke_Abbott&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Abbott tried to set up a law practice, working for a few years in <a href="/wiki/Gary,_Indiana" title="Gary, Indiana">Gary, Indiana</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Topeka,_Kansas" title="Topeka, Kansas">Topeka, Kansas</a>. He returned home to Georgia for a period, then went back to Chicago, where he could see changes arriving with thousands of new migrants from the rural South.</p>
<p>After settling in Chicago, in 1905 Abbott founded <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chicago_Defender" title="The Chicago Defender">The Chicago Defender</a></i> newspaper with an initial investment of ¢25 (around $600 in 2010 terms).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-personsunknown_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-personsunknown-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He started printing in a room at his boardinghouse; his landlady encouraged him, and he later bought her an 8-room house.</p>
<p>He wanted to push for job opportunities and social justice, and was eager to persuade blacks to leave the segregated, Jim Crow South for Chicago. A key part of his distribution network was made up of African-American railroad porters, who were highly respected among blacks. (By 1925 they organized a union as the <a href="/wiki/Brotherhood_of_Sleeping_Car_Porters" title="Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters">Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters</a>). They often sold or distributed the paper on trains. <i>Defender</i> circulation reached 50,000 by 1916; 125,000 by 1918; and more than 200,000 by the early 1920s. Credited with contributing to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Migration_(African_American)" title="Great Migration (African American)">Great Migration</a> of rural southern blacks to Chicago, the <i>Defender</i> became the most widely circulated black newspaper in the country. It was known as "America's Black Newspaper." Its success resulted in Abbott becoming one of the first self-made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millionaires" title="Millionaires">millionaires</a> of African-American descent; his business expanded as African Americans moved to the cities and became an urbanized, northern population. From the early 20th century through 1940, 1.5 million blacks moved to major cities in the North and Midwest.</p>
<p>They were eager to know about conditions, to find housing, and to learn more about their new lives in cities. Most were from rural areas of the South. From 1890 to 1908 all the southern states had passed constitutions or laws that raised barriers to voter registration and effectively <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disfranchisement_after_Reconstruction_era" title="Disfranchisement after Reconstruction era">disenfranchised most blacks and many poor whites</a>. They were utterly closed out of the political systems. Schools and other public facilities reserved for blacks were typically underfunded and ill-maintained. Legislatures imposed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jim_Crow" title="Jim Crow">Jim Crow</a> conditions, producing facilities for blacks that were "separate" but never "equal" (referring to the <i><a href="/wiki/Plessy_v._Ferguson" title="Plessy v. Ferguson">Plessy v. Ferguson</a></i> (1896) case, in which the US Supreme Court ruled that segregated facilities, such as railroad cars providing "separate but equal" conditions, were constitutional).</p>
<p>The northern and midwestern industrial centers, where blacks could vote and send children to school, were recruiting workers based on expansion of manufacturing and infrastructure to supply the US's expanding population as well as the war in Europe, which started in 1914. The <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a> and others were expanding at a rapid rate across the North, needing workers for construction and later to serve the train passengers.</p>
<p>The <i>Defender</i> told stories of earlier migrants to the North, giving hope to disenfranchised and oppressed people in the South of other ways to live. Abbott, through his writings in the <i>Chicago Defender</i>, expressed those stories and encouraged people to leave the South for the North. He even set a date of May 15, 1917, for what he called 'The Great Northern Drive' to occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In his weekly, he showed pictures of Chicago and had numerous classifieds for housing. In addition, Abbott wrote about how awful a place the South was to live in comparison to the idealistic North. Abbott's words described the North as a place of prosperity and justice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lochard125_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lochard125-7">[7]</a></sup> This persuasive writing, “thereby made this journal probably the greatest stimulus that the migration had,”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lochard125_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lochard125-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sengstacke was a fighter, a defender of rights. He listed nine goals as the <i>Defender</i>′s 'Bible:'</p>
<ol>
<li>American race prejudice must be destroyed;</li>
<li>Opening up all trade unions to blacks as well as whites;</li>
<li>Representation in the President's Cabinet'</li>
<li>Hiring black engineers, firemen, and conductors on all American railroads, and to all jobs in government;</li>
<li>Gaining representation in all departments of the police forces over the entire United States;</li>
<li>Government schools giving preference to American citizens before foreigners;</li>
<li>Hiring black motormen and conductors on surface, elevated, and motor bus lines throughout America;</li>
<li>Federal legislation to abolish lynching; and</li>
<li>Full enfranchisement of all American citizens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>The <i>Chicago Defender</i> not only encouraged people to migrate north for a better life, but to fight for their rights once they got there. The slogan of the paper and the first goal was “American race prejudice must be destroyed.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sengstacke openly discussed African-American history in his articles, including its difficult issues. He wrote, "<a href="/wiki/Miscegenation" title="Miscegenation">Miscegenation</a> began as soon as the African slaves were introduced into the colonial population and continues unabated to this day.... What's more, the opposition to intermarriage has heightened the interest and solidified the feelings of those who resent the injunction of racial distinction in their private and personal affairs.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He believed that laws restricting personal choice in a mate violated the constitution and that the “decision of two intelligent people to mutual love and self-sacrifice should not be a matter of public concern.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Abbott also published a short-lived periodical called <i>Abbott's Monthly</i>.</p>
<p>The <i>Defender</i> actively promoted the northward migration of Black Southerners, particularly to Chicago; its columns not only reported on, but encouraged the Great Migration.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bah.C3.A1.27.C3.AD_Faith">Bahá'í Faith</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Sengstacke_Abbott&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bahá'í Faith">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1912, Abbott met <a href="/wiki/%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1" title="`Abdu'l-Bahá">`Abdu'l-Bahá</a>, head of the <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bahá'í Faith">Bahá'í Faith</a>, through covering a talk of his during his stay in Chicago during his <a href="/wiki/%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1%27s_journeys_to_the_West" title="`Abdu'l-Bahá's journeys to the West">journeys in the West</a>. By 1924 Abbott and his wife were listed as attending Bahá'í events in Chicago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-door_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-door-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>After inventing the fictional character "Bud Billiken" with David Kellum for articles in the <i>Defender</i>, Abbott established the <a href="/wiki/Bud_Billiken_Club" title="Bud Billiken Club">Bud Billiken Club</a>. In 1929 Abbott and Kellum founded the <a href="/wiki/Bud_Billiken_Parade_and_Picnic" title="Bud Billiken Parade and Picnic">Bud Billiken Parade and Picnic</a>. It became an occasion for African Americans to celebrate their pride and connections<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Abbott was seeking an atmosphere free of race prejudice. Even in religious communities, he sometimes found that mixed-race African Americans who were light-skinned sometimes also demonstrated prejudice against those who were darker. Abbott officially joined the Bahá'í Faith in 1934. He had found that its convention to elect its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Spiritual_Assembly" title="National Spiritual Assembly">National Spiritual Assembly</a> seemed free of prejudice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-door_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-door-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years">Final years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Sengstacke_Abbott&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Final years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1919, Illinois Governor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frank_Lowden" title="Frank Lowden">Frank Lowden</a> appointed Abbott to the state Race Relations Commission. The commission conducted studies about the changes resulting from the Great Migration; in one period, 5,000 African Americans were arriving in the city every week. The Commission collected data to assess the population and published the book, <i>The Negro in Chicago</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWICA-1933_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-WWICA-1933-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though some of his stepfather Sengstacke's relatives in Germany became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazis" title="Nazis">Nazis</a> in the 1930s and later, Abbott continued correspondence and economic aid to those who had accepted him and his father's family. He also assisted descendants of Captain Charles Stevens, the former owner of his enslaved birth father before emancipation. With his wealth, Abbott aided the Stevens descendants in Georgia during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Depression</a>, and paid for the education of their children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-door_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-door-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Abbott died of <a href="/wiki/Bright%27s_disease" title="Bright's disease">Bright's disease</a> in 1940 in Chicago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> He was buried in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lincoln_Cemetery_(Blue_Island)" title="Lincoln Cemetery (Blue Island)">Lincoln Cemetery</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_Island" title="Blue Island">Blue Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>. His will left the newspaper in the control of his nephew, <a href="/wiki/John_H._Sengstacke" title="John H. Sengstacke">John Henry Sengstacke</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Sengstacke_Abbott&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Robert_S._Abbott_House" title="Robert S. Abbott House">Robert S. Abbott House</a> in Chicago, where he lived from 1926 to his death, was designated a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark</a> in 1976.</li>
<li>His childhood home in the Woodville neighborhood now in <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a>, was designated as a city historic landmark.<a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark</a>.</li>
<li>An early biography of him was published in 1955 by Roi Ottley, <i>The Lonely Warrior: The Life and Times of Robert S. Abbott</i> (Chicago: H. Regnery Co., 1955).</li>
</ul>
