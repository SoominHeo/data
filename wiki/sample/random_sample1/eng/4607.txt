<p><b>Robert Lee Frost</b> (March<span class="nowrap"> </span>26, 1874 – January<span class="nowrap"> </span>29, 1963) was an American poet. His work was initially published in England before it was published in America. He is highly regarded for his realistic depictions of rural life and his command of American colloquial speech.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-5">[5]</a></sup> His work frequently employed settings from rural life in <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> in the early twentieth century, using them to examine complex social and philosophical themes. One of the most popular and critically respected American poets of the twentieth century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stine_1983_110_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stine_1983_110-6">[6]</a></sup> Frost was honored frequently during his lifetime, receiving four <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_Poetry" title="Pulitzer Prize for Poetry">Pulitzer Prizes for Poetry</a>. He became one of America's rare "public literary figures, almost an artistic institution."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stine_1983_110_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stine_1983_110-6">[6]</a></sup> He was awarded the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Congressional_Gold_Medal_recipients" title="List of Congressional Gold Medal recipients">Congressional Gold Medal</a> in 1960 for his poetic works. On July 22, 1961, Frost was named <a href="/wiki/Poet_laureate" title="Poet laureate">poet laureate</a> of <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jb_modern_frost_2_e.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="580" data-file-width="410" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Jb_modern_frost_2_e.jpg/180px-Jb_modern_frost_2_e.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Jb_modern_frost_2_e.jpg/270px-Jb_modern_frost_2_e.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Jb_modern_frost_2_e.jpg/360px-Jb_modern_frost_2_e.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jb_modern_frost_2_e.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Robert Frost, circa 1910</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Robert Frost was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco,_California" title="San Francisco, California">San Francisco, California</a>, to journalist William Prescott Frost, Jr., and Isabelle Moodie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-5">[5]</a></sup> His mother was a Scottish immigrant, and his father descended from Nicholas Frost of <a href="/wiki/Tiverton,_Devon" title="Tiverton, Devon">Tiverton, Devon</a>, England, who had sailed to <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a> in 1634 on the <i>Wolfrana</i>.</p>
<p>Frost's father was a teacher and later an editor of the <i>San Francisco Evening Bulletin</i> (which later merged with <i><a href="/wiki/The_San_Francisco_Examiner" title="The San Francisco Examiner">The San Francisco Examiner</a></i>), and an unsuccessful candidate for city tax collector. After his death on May 5, 1885, the family moved across the country to <a href="/wiki/Lawrence,_Massachusetts" title="Lawrence, Massachusetts">Lawrence, Massachusetts</a>, under the patronage of (Robert's grandfather) William Frost, Sr., who was an overseer at a New England mill. Frost graduated from Lawrence High School in 1892.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Frost's mother joined the <a href="/wiki/The_New_Church" title="The New Church">Swedenborgian</a> church and had him baptized in it, but he left it as an adult.</p>
<p>Although known for his later association with rural life, Frost grew up in the city, and he published his first poem in his high school's magazine. He attended <a href="/wiki/Dartmouth_College" title="Dartmouth College">Dartmouth College</a> for two months, long enough to be accepted into the <a href="/wiki/Theta_Delta_Chi" title="Theta Delta Chi">Theta Delta Chi</a> fraternity. Frost returned home to teach and to work at various jobs, including helping his mother teach her class of unruly boys, delivering newspapers, and working in a factory maintaining <a href="/wiki/Arc_lamp#Carbon_arc_lamp" title="Arc lamp">carbon arc lamps</a>. He did not enjoy these jobs, feeling his true calling was poetry.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adult_years">Adult years</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Robert_Frost_NYWTS_5.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="2313" height="233" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Robert_Frost_NYWTS_5.jpg/180px-Robert_Frost_NYWTS_5.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Robert_Frost_NYWTS_5.jpg/270px-Robert_Frost_NYWTS_5.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Robert_Frost_NYWTS_5.jpg/360px-Robert_Frost_NYWTS_5.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Robert_Frost_NYWTS_5.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Frost, 1959</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1894 he sold his first poem, "My Butterfly. An Elegy" (published in the November 8, 1894, edition of the <i>New York Independent</i>) for $15 ($415 today). Proud of his accomplishment, he proposed marriage to Elinor Miriam White, but she demurred, wanting to finish college (at <a href="/wiki/St._Lawrence_University" title="St. Lawrence University">St. Lawrence University</a>) before they married. Frost then went on an excursion to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Dismal_Swamp" title="Great Dismal Swamp">Great Dismal Swamp</a> in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> and asked Elinor again upon his return. Having graduated, she agreed, and they were married at Lawrence, Massachusetts on December 19, 1895.</p>
<p>Frost attended <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> from 1897 to 1899, but he left voluntarily due to illness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TutenZubizarreta2001_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-TutenZubizarreta2001-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parini2000_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parini2000-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meyers1996_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meyers1996-10">[10]</a></sup> Shortly before his death, Frost's grandfather purchased <a href="/wiki/Robert_Frost_Farm_(Derry,_New_Hampshire)" title="Robert Frost Farm (Derry, New Hampshire)">a farm</a> for Robert and Elinor in <a href="/wiki/Derry,_New_Hampshire" title="Derry, New Hampshire">Derry, New Hampshire</a>; Frost worked the farm for nine years while writing early in the mornings and producing many of the poems that would later become famous. Ultimately his farming proved unsuccessful and he returned to the field of education as an English teacher at New Hampshire's <a href="/wiki/Pinkerton_Academy" title="Pinkerton Academy">Pinkerton Academy</a> from 1906 to 1911, then at the New Hampshire Normal School (now <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_State_University" title="Plymouth State University">Plymouth State University</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Plymouth,_New_Hampshire" title="Plymouth, New Hampshire">Plymouth, New Hampshire</a>.</p>
<p>In 1912 Frost sailed with his family to <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, settling first in <a href="/wiki/Beaconsfield" title="Beaconsfield">Beaconsfield</a>, a small town outside London. His first book of poetry, <i>A Boy's Will</i>, was published the next year. In England he made some important acquaintances, including <a href="/wiki/Edward_Thomas_(poet)" title="Edward Thomas (poet)">Edward Thomas</a> (a member of the group known as the <a href="/wiki/Dymock_poets" title="Dymock poets">Dymock poets</a> and Frost's inspiration for "<a href="/wiki/The_Road_Not_Taken" title="The Road Not Taken">The Road Not Taken</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>), <a href="/wiki/T._E._Hulme" title="T. E. Hulme">T. E. Hulme</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Pound" title="Ezra Pound">Ezra Pound</a>. Although Pound would become the first American to write a favorable review of Frost's work, Frost later resented Pound's attempts to manipulate his American prosody. Frost met or befriended many contemporary poets in England, especially after his first two poetry volumes were published in London in 1913 (<i>A Boy's Will</i>) and 1914 (<i>North of Boston</i>).</p>

</div>

<p>In 1915, during World War I, Frost returned to America, where <a href="/wiki/Henry_Holt_and_Company" title="Henry Holt and Company">Holt's</a> American edition of <i>A Boy's Will</i> had recently been published, and bought a farm in <a href="/wiki/Franconia,_New_Hampshire" title="Franconia, New Hampshire">Franconia, New Hampshire</a>, where he launched a career of writing, teaching, and lecturing. This family homestead served as the Frosts' summer home until 1938. It is maintained today as <a href="/wiki/The_Frost_Place" title="The Frost Place">The Frost Place</a>, a museum and poetry conference site. During the years 1916–20, 1923–24, and 1927–1938, Frost taught English at <a href="/wiki/Amherst_College" title="Amherst College">Amherst College</a> in Massachusetts, notably encouraging his students to account for the myriad sounds and intonations of the spoken English language in their writing. He called his colloquial approach to language "the sound of sense."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www.learner.org_series57_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-www.learner.org_series57-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1924, he won the first of four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prizes" title="Pulitzer Prizes">Pulitzer Prizes</a> for the book <i>New Hampshire: A Poem with Notes and Grace Notes</i>. He would win additional Pulitzers for <i>Collected Poems</i> in 1931, <i>A Further Range</i> in 1937, and <i>A Witness Tree</i> in 1943.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>For forty-two years — from 1921 to 1963 — Frost spent almost every summer and fall teaching at the <a href="/wiki/Bread_Loaf_School_of_English" title="Bread Loaf School of English">Bread Loaf School of English</a> of <a href="/wiki/Middlebury_College" title="Middlebury College">Middlebury College</a>, at its mountain campus at <a href="/wiki/Ripton,_Vermont" title="Ripton, Vermont">Ripton, Vermont</a>. He is credited as a major influence upon the development of the school and its writing programs. The college now owns and maintains his former Ripton farmstead as a national historic site near the Bread Loaf campus. In 1921 Frost accepted a fellowship teaching post at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ann_Arbor" title="Ann Arbor">Ann Arbor</a>, where he resided until 1927 when he returned to teach at Amherst. While teaching at the University of Michigan, he was awarded a lifetime appointment at the University as a Fellow in Letters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frost_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frost-13">[13]</a></sup> The Robert Frost Ann Arbor home was purchased by <a href="/wiki/The_Henry_Ford" title="The Henry Ford">The Henry Ford</a> Museum in Dearborn, Michigan and relocated to the museum's Greenfield Village site for public tours.</p>
<p>In 1940 he bought a 5-acre (2.0 ha) plot in South Miami, Florida, naming it <i>Pencil Pines</i>; he spent his winters there for the rest of his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> His properties also included a <a href="/wiki/Robert_Frost_House" title="Robert Frost House">house</a> on Brewster Street in Cambridge, Massachusetts, that today belongs to the National Historic Register.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard</a>'s 1965 alumni directory indicates Frost received an honorary degree there. Although he never graduated from college, Frost received over 40 honorary degrees, including ones from <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_University" title="Oxford University">Oxford</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" title="Cambridge University">Cambridge</a> universities, and was the only person to receive two honorary degrees from <a href="/wiki/Dartmouth_College" title="Dartmouth College">Dartmouth College</a>. During his lifetime, the Robert Frost Middle School in <a href="/wiki/Fairfax,_Virginia" title="Fairfax, Virginia">Fairfax, Virginia</a>, the Robert L. Frost School in <a href="/wiki/Lawrence,_Massachusetts" title="Lawrence, Massachusetts">Lawrence, Massachusetts</a>, and the main library of <a href="/wiki/Amherst_College" title="Amherst College">Amherst College</a> were named after him.</p>

<p>In 1960, Frost was awarded a United States <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Gold_Medal" title="Congressional Gold Medal">Congressional Gold Medal</a>, <i>"In recognition of his poetry, which has enriched the culture of the United States and the philosophy of the world,"</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> which was finally bestowed by President Kennedy in March 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parini1999_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parini1999-16">[16]</a></sup> Also in 1962, he was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Edward_MacDowell_Medal" title="Edward MacDowell Medal">Edward MacDowell Medal</a> for outstanding contribution to the arts by the <a href="/wiki/MacDowell_Colony" title="MacDowell Colony">MacDowell Colony</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Frost was 86 when he read his well-known poem "<a href="/wiki/The_Gift_Outright" title="The Gift Outright">The Gift Outright</a>" at the inauguration of <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">President John F. Kennedy</a> on January 20, 1961. He died in Boston two years later, on January 29, 1963, of complications from prostate surgery. He was buried at the Old Bennington Cemetery in Bennington, Vermont. His <a href="/wiki/Epitaph" title="Epitaph">epitaph</a> quotes the last line from his poem, "The Lesson for Today (1942): "I had a lover's quarrel with the world."</p>
<p>One of the original collections of Frost materials, to which he himself contributed, is found in the Special Collections department of the <a href="/wiki/Jones_Library" title="Jones Library">Jones Library</a> in <a href="/wiki/Amherst,_Massachusetts" title="Amherst, Massachusetts">Amherst, Massachusetts</a>. The collection consists of approximately twelve thousand items, including original manuscript poems and letters, correspondence and photographs, as well as audio and visual recordings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Archives and Special Collections at Amherst College holds a small collection of his papers. The University of Michigan Library holds the <a class="external text" href="http://quod.lib.umich.edu/cgi/f/findaid/findaid-idx?c=sclead&amp;idno=umich-scl-frostfamily" rel="nofollow">Robert Frost Family Collection</a> of manuscripts, photographs, printed items, and artwork. The most significant collection of Frost's working manuscripts is held by Dartmouth.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Robert_Frost%27s_Grave.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="1944" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Robert_Frost%27s_Grave.JPG/220px-Robert_Frost%27s_Grave.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Robert_Frost%27s_Grave.JPG/330px-Robert_Frost%27s_Grave.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Robert_Frost%27s_Grave.JPG/440px-Robert_Frost%27s_Grave.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Robert_Frost%27s_Grave.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Frost family grave in Bennington Old Cemetery</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Robert Frost's personal life was plagued with grief and loss. In 1885 when Frost was 11, his father died of <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>, leaving the family with just eight dollars. Frost's mother died of <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> in 1900. In 1920, Frost had to commit his younger sister Jeanie to a mental hospital, where she died nine years later. Mental illness apparently ran in Frost's family, as both he and his mother suffered from <a href="/wiki/Major_depressive_disorder" title="Major depressive disorder">depression</a>, and his daughter Irma was committed to a mental hospital in 1947. Frost's wife, Elinor, also experienced bouts of depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frost_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frost-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Elinor and Robert Frost had six children: son Elliot (1896–1904, died of <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>); daughter Lesley Frost Ballantine (1899–1983); son Carol (1902–1940, committed suicide); daughter Irma (1903–1967); daughter Marjorie (1905–1934, died as a result of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puerperal_fever" title="Puerperal fever">puerperal fever</a> after childbirth); and daughter Elinor Bettina (died just three days after her birth in 1907). Only Lesley and Irma outlived their father. Frost's wife, who had heart problems throughout her life, developed <a href="/wiki/Breast_cancer" title="Breast cancer">breast cancer</a> in 1937, and died of <a href="/wiki/Heart_failure" title="Heart failure">heart failure</a> in 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frost_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Frost-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Style_and_critical_response">Style and critical response</span></h3>
<p>The poet/critic <a href="/wiki/Randall_Jarrell" title="Randall Jarrell">Randall Jarrell</a> often praised Frost's poetry and wrote, "Robert Frost, along with <a href="/wiki/Wallace_Stevens" title="Wallace Stevens">Stevens</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T.S._Eliot" title="T.S. Eliot">Eliot</a>, seems to me the greatest of the American poets of this century. Frost's virtues are extraordinary. No other living poet has written so well about the actions of ordinary men; his wonderful dramatic monologues or dramatic scenes come out of a knowledge of people that few poets have had, and they are written in a verse that uses, sometimes with absolute mastery, the rhythms of actual speech." He also praised "Frost's seriousness and honesty," stating that Frost was particularly skilled at representing a wide range of human experience in his poems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jarrell's notable and influential essays on Frost include the essays "Robert Frost's 'Home Burial'" (1962), which consisted of an extended close reading of that particular poem, and "To The Laodiceans" (1952) in which Jarrell defended Frost against critics who had accused Frost of being too "traditional" and out of touch with Modern or <a href="/wiki/Modernist_poetry" title="Modernist poetry">Modernist poetry</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RobertFrost.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="599" data-file-width="385" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/RobertFrost.jpg/160px-RobertFrost.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/RobertFrost.jpg/240px-RobertFrost.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/RobertFrost.jpg/320px-RobertFrost.jpg 2x" width="160"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RobertFrost.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
U.S stamp, 1974</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In Frost's defense, Jarrell wrote "the regular ways of looking at Frost's poetry are grotesque simplifications, distortions, falsifications—coming to know his poetry well ought to be enough, in itself, to dispel any of them, and to make plain the necessity of finding some other way of talking about his work." And Jarrell's close readings of poems like "Neither Out Too Far Nor In Too Deep" led readers and critics to perceive more of the complexities in Frost's poetry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an introduction to Jarrell's book of essays, <a href="/wiki/Brad_Leithauser" title="Brad Leithauser">Brad Leithauser</a> notes that, "the 'other' Frost that Jarrell discerned behind the genial, homespun New England rustic—the 'dark' Frost who was desperate, frightened, and brave—has become the Frost we've all learned to recognize, and the little-known poems Jarrell singled out as central to the Frost canon are now to be found in most anthologies." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jarrell lists a selection of the Frost poems he considers the most masterful, including "The Witch of Coös," "Home Burial," "A Servant to Servants," "Directive," "Neither Out Too Far Nor In Too Deep," "Provide, Provide," "<a href="/wiki/Acquainted_with_the_Night" title="Acquainted with the Night">Acquainted with the Night</a>," "After Apple Picking," "Mending Wall," "The Most of It," "An Old Man's Winter Night," "To Earthward," "<a href="/wiki/Stopping_by_Woods_on_a_Snowy_Evening" title="Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening">Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening</a>," "Spring Pools," "The Lovely Shall Be Choosers," "Design," [and] "Desert Places."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="background-color: #F9F9F9; color: black; text-align: center; font-size: larger; font-weight: bold;">From "Birches"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></div>
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<div class="poem">
<p>I'd like to get away from earth awhile<br/>
And then come back to it and begin over.<br/>
May no fate willfully misunderstand me<br/>
And half grant what I wish and snatch me away<br/>
Not to return. Earth's the right place for love:<br/>
I don't know where it's likely to go better.<br/>
I'd like to go by climbing a birch tree,<br/>
And climb black branches up a snow-white trunk<br/>
Toward heaven, till the tree could bear no more,<br/>
But dipped its top and set me down again.<br/>
That would be good both going and coming back.<br/>
One could do worse than be a swinger of birches.</p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;">Robert Frost</cite></div>
<p>In 2003, the critic Charles McGrath noted that critical views on Frost's poetry have changed over the years (as has his public image). In an article called "The Vicissitudes of Literary Reputation," McGrath wrote, "Robert Frost ... at the time of his death in 1963 was generally considered to be a New England folkie ... In 1977, the third volume of Lawrance Thompson's biography suggested that Frost was a much nastier piece of work than anyone had imagined; a few years later, thanks to the reappraisal of critics like William H. Pritchard and <a href="/wiki/Harold_Bloom" title="Harold Bloom">Harold Bloom</a> and of younger poets like <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Brodsky" title="Joseph Brodsky">Joseph Brodsky</a>, he bounced back again, this time as a bleak and unforgiving modernist."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i>The Norton Anthology of Modern Poetry</i>, editors <a href="/wiki/Richard_Ellmann" title="Richard Ellmann">Richard Ellmann</a> and Robert O'Clair compared and contrasted Frost's unique style to the work of the poet <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Arlington_Robinson" title="Edwin Arlington Robinson">Edwin Arlington Robinson</a> since they both frequently used New England settings for their poems. However, they state that Frost's poetry was "less [consciously] literary" and that this was possibly due to the influence of English and Irish writers like <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardy" title="Thomas Hardy">Thomas Hardy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W.B._Yeats" title="W.B. Yeats">W.B. Yeats</a>. They note that Frost's poems "show a successful striving for utter colloquialism" and always try to remain down to earth, while at the same time using traditional forms despite the trend of American poetry towards free verse which Frost famously said was "'like playing tennis without a net.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellman_1988._2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ellman_1988.-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In providing an overview of Frost's style, the <a href="/wiki/Poetry_Foundation" title="Poetry Foundation">Poetry Foundation</a> makes the same point, placing Frost's work "at the crossroads of nineteenth-century American poetry [with regard to his use of traditional forms] and modernism [with his use of idiomatic language and ordinary, every day subject matter]." They also note that Frost believed that "the self-imposed restrictions of meter in form" was more helpful than harmful because he could focus on the content of his poems instead of concerning himself with creating "innovative" new verse forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>An earlier 1963 study by the poet <a href="/wiki/James_Radcliffe_Squires" title="James Radcliffe Squires">James Radcliffe Squires</a> spoke to the distinction of Frost as a poet whose verse soars more for the difficulty and skill by which he attains his final visions, than for the philosophical purity of the visions themselves. "'He has written at a time when the choice for the poet seemed to lie among the forms of despair: Science, solipsism, or the religion of the past century…Frost has refused all of these and in the refusal has long seemed less dramatically committed than others…But no, he must be seen as dramatically uncommitted to the single solution…Insofar as Frost allows to both fact and intuition a bright kingdom, he speaks for many of us. Insofar as he speaks through an amalgam of senses and sure experience so that his poetry seems a nostalgic memory with overtones touching some conceivable future, he speaks better than most of us. That is to say, as a poet must."'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span></h3>
<p>In <i>Contemporary Literary Criticism</i>, the editors state that "Frost's best work explores fundamental questions of existence, depicting with chilling starkness the loneliness of the individual in an indifferent universe."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stine_1983_110_129_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stine_1983_110_129-29">[29]</a></sup> The critic T. K. Whipple focused in on this bleakness in Frost's work, stating that "in much of his work, particularly in <i>North of Boston</i>, his harshest book, he emphasizes the dark background of life in rural New England, with its degeneration often sinking into total madness." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stine_1983_110_129_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stine_1983_110_129-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In sharp contrast, the founding publisher and editor of <i><a href="/wiki/Poetry_(magazine)" title="Poetry (magazine)">Poetry</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Harriet_Monroe" title="Harriet Monroe">Harriet Monroe</a>, emphasized the folksy New England persona and characters in Frost's work, writing that "perhaps no other poet in our history has put the best of the Yankee spirit into a book so completely." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stine_1983_110_129_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stine_1983_110_129-29">[29]</a></sup> She notes his frequent use of rural settings and farm life, and she likes that in these poems, Frost is most interested in "showing the human reaction to nature's processes." She also notes that while Frost's narrative, character-based poems are often satirical, Frost always has a "sympathetic humor" towards his subjects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stine_1983_110_129_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-Stine_1983_110_129-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poet_laureate_of_Vermont">Poet laureate of Vermont</span></h2>
<p>In June 1922 the Vermont State League of Women's Clubs elected Frost as Poet laureate of Vermont. When a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> editorial strongly criticised the decision of the Women's Clubs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarah_Cleghorn" title="Sarah Cleghorn">Sarah Cleghorn</a> and other women wrote to the newspaper defending Frost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frost2007_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frost2007-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 22, 1961 Frost was named <a href="/wiki/Poet_laureate" title="Poet laureate">Poet laureate</a> of Vermont by the state legislature through Joint Resolution R-59 of the Acts of 1961, which also created the position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TutenZubizarreta2001p249_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-TutenZubizarreta2001p249-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fagan2009_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fagan2009-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-State1985_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-State1985-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span></h2>
