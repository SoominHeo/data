<p><b>Robert William "Willie" Pickton</b> (born October 24, 1949)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Port_Coquitlam" title="Port Coquitlam">Port Coquitlam</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, is a former multi-millionaire <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swine_production" title="Swine production">pig farmer</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Serial_killer" title="Serial killer">serial killer</a> convicted in 2007 of the second-degree <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murders</a> of six women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He was also charged in the deaths of an additional twenty women,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> many of them from <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Eastside" title="Downtown Eastside">Downtown Eastside</a>; however, these charges were stayed by the Crown in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chad_Skelton_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chad_Skelton-7">[7]</a></sup> In December 2007, he was sentenced to <a href="/wiki/Life_imprisonment" title="Life imprisonment">life in prison</a>, with no possibility of <a href="/wiki/Parole" title="Parole">parole</a> for 25 years – the longest sentence then available under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_law_for_murder" title="Canadian law for murder">Canadian law for murder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the trial's first day of jury evidence, January 22, 2007, <a href="/wiki/Crown_attorney" title="Crown attorney">the Crown</a> stated he confessed to 49 murders to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undercover" title="Undercover">undercover</a> <a href="/wiki/Police_officer" title="Police officer">police officer</a> posing as a cellmate. The Crown reported that Pickton told the officer that he wanted to kill another woman to make it an even 50, and that he was caught because he was "sloppy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By 1992, Robert William Pickton and his brother David owned a <a href="/wiki/Port_Coquitlam" title="Port Coquitlam">Port Coquitlam</a> farm. Worker Bill Hiscox called it a "creepy-looking place", noting that it was patrolled by a 600-lb. (270 kg) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boar" title="Boar">boar</a>, one of the few actual pigs on the farm. "I never saw a pig like that, who would chase you and bite at you," he said. "It was running out with the dogs around the property." He later described Pickton as a "pretty quiet guy, hard to strike up a conversation with," whose occasionally bizarre behavior, despite no evidence of <a href="/wiki/Substance_abuse" title="Substance abuse">substance abuse</a>, would draw attention. Pickton's only vehicle was a converted bus, with deeply tinted windows, to which he was emotionally attached.</p>
<p>The Pickton brothers gradually neglected the site's farming operations. They registered a non-profit charity, the Piggy Palace Good Times Society, with the Canadian government in 1996 as aiming to "organize, co-ordinate, manage and operate special events, functions, dances, shows and exhibitions on behalf of service organizations, sports organizations and other worthy groups." Its events included <a href="/wiki/Rave" title="Rave">raves</a> and wild parties featuring Vancouver prostitutes and gatherings in a converted slaughterhouse. These events attracted as many as 2,000 people. Hell's Angel members were known to often frequent the farm.</p>
<p>On March 23, 1997, Pickton was charged with the attempted murder of prostitute Wendy Lynn Eistetter, whom he stabbed several times during an altercation at the farm. The victim informed police that Pickton handcuffed her, but that she escaped after suffering several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacerations" title="Lacerations">lacerations</a>, disarming him, and stabbing him with his weapon. Pickton sought treatment at Eagle Ridge Hospital, while Eistetter healed at the nearest emergency room. He was released on <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">C$</a>2,000 bond, but the charge was dismissed in January 1998. Months later, the Picktons were sued by <a href="/wiki/Port_Coquitlam" title="Port Coquitlam">Port Coquitlam</a> officials for violating <a href="/wiki/Zoning" title="Zoning">zoning</a> ordinances – neglecting the agriculture for which it had been zoned, and having "altered a large farm building on the land for the purpose of holding dances, concerts and other recreations." The Picktons ignored the pressure from the officials and held a 1998 New Years party, after which they were faced with an injunction banning future parties; the police were "authorized to arrest and remove any person" attending future Piggy Palace Good Times Society events at the farm. The society's non-profit status was removed the following year, for inability to procure financial statements, and it subsequently disbanded.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Murders">Murders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Murders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Over the course of the next three years, Hiscox noticed that women who visited the farm eventually went missing. On February 6, 2002, police executed a <a href="/wiki/Search_warrant" title="Search warrant">search warrant</a> for illegal firearms at the property owned by the brothers. After they were taken into custody, police obtained a second court order to search the farm as part of the <a href="/wiki/BC_Missing_Women_Investigation" title="BC Missing Women Investigation">BC Missing Women Investigation</a>. Personal items (including a prescription asthma inhaler) belonging to one of the missing women were found at the farm, which was sealed off by members of the joint <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Mounted_Police" title="Royal Canadian Mounted Police">RCMP</a>–<a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Police_Department" title="Vancouver Police Department">Vancouver Police Department</a> task force. The following day Pickton was charged with storing a firearm contrary to regulations, possession of a firearm while not being holder of a licence, and possession of a loaded restricted firearm without a licence. He was later released and was kept under police surveillance.</p>
<p>On February 22, Pickton was arrested and charged with two counts of first-degree murder in the deaths of Sereena Abotsway and Mona Wilson. On April 2, three more charges were added for the murders of Jacqueline McDonell, Diane Rock and Heather Bottomley. A sixth charge for the murder of Andrea Joesbury was laid on April 9, followed shortly by a seventh for Brenda Wolfe. On September 20, four more charges were added for the slayings of Georgina Papin, Patricia Johnson, Helen Hallmark and Jennifer Furminger. Four more charges for the murders of Heather Chinnock, Tanya Holyk, Sherry Irving and Inga Hall were laid on October 3, bringing the total to fifteen, making the investigation the largest of any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serial_killer_in_Canadian_history" title="Serial killer in Canadian history">serial killer in Canadian history</a>. On May 26, 2005, 12 more charges were laid against him for the killings of Cara Ellis, Andrea Borhaven, Debra Lynne Jones, Marnie Frey, Tiffany Drew, Kerry Koski, Sarah de Vries, Cynthia Feliks, Angela Jardine, Wendy Crawford, Diana Melnick, and Jane Doe (unidentified woman) bringing the total number of first-degree murder charges to 27.</p>
<p>Excavations continued through November 2003; the cost of the investigation is estimated to have been $70 million by the end of 2003, according to the provincial government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the property is fenced off, under <a href="/wiki/Lien" title="Lien">lien</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_in_the_Canadian_provinces#Constitutional_role" title="Monarchy in the Canadian provinces">Crown in Right of British Columbia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In the meantime, all the buildings on the property had been demolished. Forensic analysis proved difficult because the bodies may have been left to decompose or be eaten by insects and pigs on the farm. During the early days of the excavations, forensic anthropologists brought in heavy equipment, including two 50-foot (15-meter) flat conveyor belts and soil sifters to find traces of remains. On March 10, 2004, it was revealed that Pickton may have ground up human flesh and mixed it with pork that he sold to the public; the province's health authority later issued a warning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.27Human_meat.27_alert_at_pig_farm_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-.27Human_meat.27_alert_at_pig_farm-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alert_issued_about_meat_from_Pickton.27s_pig_farm_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alert_issued_about_meat_from_Pickton.27s_pig_farm-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Human_remains_from_Pickton_farm_may_have_reached_food_supply_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Human_remains_from_Pickton_farm_may_have_reached_food_supply-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ottawa_rates_health_risk_from_human_remains_in_farm_meat.3B_Pickton_meat_warning_lifts_number_to_2.2C500_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ottawa_rates_health_risk_from_human_remains_in_farm_meat.3B_Pickton_meat_warning_lifts_number_to_2.2C500-17">[17]</a></sup> Another claim was made that he fed the bodies directly to his pigs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bodies_fed_to_pigs_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-bodies_fed_to_pigs-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preliminary_inquiry">Preliminary inquiry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Preliminary inquiry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A preliminary inquiry was held in 2003, the testimony from which was covered by a publication ban until 2010. At the inquiry, the fact that Pickton had been charged with attempted murder in connection with the stabbing of prostitute Wendy Lynn Eistetter in 1997 was revealed. Eistetter testified at the inquiry that after he had driven her to the Port Coquitlam farm and had sex with her, Pickton slapped a handcuff on her left hand and stabbed her in the abdomen. She stabbed Pickton in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self_defense" title="Self defense">self defense</a>. Later, both she and Pickton were treated at the same hospital, where staff used a key they found in Pickton's pocket to remove the handcuffs from the woman's wrist. The attempted-murder charge against Pickton was <a href="/wiki/Stay_of_proceedings" title="Stay of proceedings">stayed</a> on January 27, 1998, because the woman had drug addiction issues and prosecutors believed her too unstable for her testimony to help secure a conviction. The clothes and rubber boots Pickton had been wearing that evening were seized by police and left in an RCMP storage locker for more than seven years. Not until 2004 did lab testing show that the DNA of two missing women were on the items seized from Pickton in 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1998, according to Vancouver police detective constable Lori Shenher, Shenher learned of a call made to a police tip phoneline stating that Pickton should be investigated in the case of the disappearances. According to Shenher's account, described at length in her 2015 book about the case, she struggled to attract sufficient police resources and attention to the case until a 2002 search of Pickton's farm by the RCMP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1999, Canadian police had received a tip that Pickton had a freezer filled with human flesh on his farm. Although they interviewed Pickton and obtained his consent to a search of his farm, the police never conducted one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trial">Trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pickton's trial began on January 30, 2006 in <a href="/wiki/New_Westminster" title="New Westminster">New Westminster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Pickton pleaded not guilty to 27 charges of first-degree murder in the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_British_Columbia" title="Supreme Court of British Columbia">Supreme Court of British Columbia</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Voir_dire" title="Voir dire">voir dire</a> phase of the trial took most of the year to determine what evidence might be admitted before the jury. Reporters were not allowed to disclose any of the material presented in the arguments. On March 2, one of the 27 counts was rejected by Justice James Williams for lack of evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 9, Justice Williams severed the charges, splitting them into one group of six counts and another group of twenty counts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The trial proceeded on the group of six counts. The remaining 20 counts could have been heard in a separate trial, but ultimately were stayed on August 4, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chad_Skelton_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chad_Skelton-7">[7]</a></sup> Because of the publication ban, full details of the decision are not publicly available; but the judge has explained that trying all 26 charges at once would put an unreasonable burden on the jury, as the trial could last up to two years, and have an increased chance for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mistrial_(law)" title="Mistrial (law)">mistrial</a>. The judge also added that the six counts he chose had "materially different" evidence from the other 20.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The date for the jury trial of the first six counts was initially set to start January 8, 2007, but later delayed to January 22.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> On that date, Pickton faced first-degree murder charges in the deaths of Frey, Abotsway, Papin, Joesbury, Wolfe and Wilson. The media ban was lifted and for the first time Canadians heard the details of what was found during the long investigation: skulls cut in half with hands and feet stuffed inside; the remains of one victim found stuffed in a garbage bag, and her blood-stained clothing found in Pickton's trailer; part of another victim's jawbone and teeth found beside Pickton's <a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse" title="Slaughterhouse">slaughterhouse</a>; and a .22 calibre<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> revolver with an attached <a href="/wiki/Dildo" title="Dildo">dildo</a> containing both his and a victim's DNA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In a videotaped recording played for the jury, Pickton claimed to have attached the dildo to his weapon as a makeshift silencer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bodies_fed_to_pigs_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-bodies_fed_to_pigs-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of February 20, 2007, the following information has been presented to the court:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>During Pickton's trial, lab staff testified that about 80 unidentified DNA profiles, roughly half male and half female, have shown up on evidence.</li>
<li>The items police found inside Pickton's trailer: A loaded .22 revolver with a <a href="/wiki/Dildo" title="Dildo">dildo</a> over the barrel and one round fired, boxes of <a href="/wiki/.357_Magnum" title=".357 Magnum">.357 Magnum</a> handgun ammunition, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Night-vision_goggles" title="Night-vision goggles">night-vision goggles</a>, two pairs of faux fur-lined handcuffs, a <a href="/wiki/Syringe" title="Syringe">syringe</a> with three millilitres of blue liquid inside, and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_fly" title="Spanish fly">Spanish fly</a>" <a href="/wiki/Aphrodisiac" title="Aphrodisiac">aphrodisiac</a>.</li>
<li>A videotape of Pickton's friend Scott Chubb saying Pickton had told him a good way to kill a female heroin addict was to inject her with <a href="/wiki/Windshield_washer_fluid" title="Windshield washer fluid">windshield washer fluid</a>. A second tape was played for Pickton, in which an associate named Andrew Bellwood said Pickton mentioned killing prostitutes by handcuffing and strangling them, then bleeding and gutting them before feeding them to pigs.</li>
<li>Photos of the contents of a garbage can found in Pickton's slaughterhouse, which held some remains of Mona Wilson.</li>
</ul>
<p>In October 2007, a juror was accused of having made up her mind already that Pickton was innocent. The trial judge questioned the juror, saying "It's reported to me you said from what you had seen you were certain Mr. Pickton was innocent, there was no way he could have done this. That the court system had arrested the wrong guy." The juror denied this completely. Justice Williams ruled that she could remain on the jury since it had not been proven she made the statements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Justice James Williams suspended jury deliberations on December 6, 2007 after he discovered an error in his charge to the jury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Earlier in the day, the jury had submitted a written question to Justice James requesting clarification of his charge, asking "Are we able to say 'yes' [i.e., find Pickton guilty] if we infer the accused acted indirectly?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 9, 2007, the jury returned a verdict that Pickton is not guilty on 6 counts of first-degree murder, but is guilty on 6 counts of second-degree murder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> A second-degree murder conviction carries a punishment of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_imprisonment_(Canada)" title="Life imprisonment (Canada)">life sentence</a>, with no possibility of <a href="/wiki/Parole" title="Parole">parole</a> for a period between 10 and 25 years, to be set by the trial judge. On December 11, 2007, after reading 18 <a href="/wiki/Victim_impact_statement" title="Victim impact statement">victim impact statements</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Columbia_Supreme_Court" title="British Columbia Supreme Court">British Columbia Supreme Court</a> Judge Justice James Williams sentenced Pickton to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_sentence" title="Life sentence">life</a> with no possibility of parole for 25 years – the maximum punishment for second-degree murder – and equal to the sentence which would have been imposed for a first-degree murder conviction. "Mr. Pickton's conduct was murderous and repeatedly so. I cannot know the details but I know this: What happened to them was senseless and despicable," said Justice Williams in passing the sentence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Columbia_Court_of_Appeal">British Columbia Court of Appeal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: British Columbia Court of Appeal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The B.C. Court of Appeal rendered judgment in June 2009 on two appeals, one brought by the Crown (prosecution) and the other brought by the defence.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crown_appeal">Crown appeal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Crown appeal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On January 7, 2008, the Attorney General filed an appeal in the <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia_Court_of_Appeal" title="British Columbia Court of Appeal">British Columbia Court of Appeal</a>, against Pickton's acquittals on the first-degree murder charges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The grounds of appeal relate to a number of evidentiary rulings made by the trial judge, certain aspects of the trial judge’s <a href="/wiki/Jury_instructions" title="Jury instructions">jury instructions</a>, and the ruling to sever the six charges Pickton was tried on from the remaining twenty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some relatives of the victims in the case were taken aback by the announcement of a Crown appeal, especially because Attorney-General <a href="/wiki/Wally_Oppal" title="Wally Oppal">Wally Oppal</a> had said a few days earlier that the prosecution would likely <i>not</i> appeal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Although Pickton had been acquitted on the first-degree murder charges, he was convicted of second-degree murder and received the same sentence as he would have on first-degree murder convictions. The relatives of the victims expressed concern that the convictions would be jeopardized if the Crown argued that the trial judge had made errors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Opposition critic <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Krog" title="Leonard Krog">Leonard Krog</a> criticized the Attorney-General for not having briefed the victims’ families in advance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vancouver_Sun_2008-01-07_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vancouver_Sun_2008-01-07-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Oppal apologized to the victims’ families for not informing them of the appeal before it was announced to the general public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vancouver_Sun_2008-01-07_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vancouver_Sun_2008-01-07-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Oppal also said that the appeal was filed largely for “strategic” reasons, in anticipation of an appeal by the defence. The prosecution’s rationale was that <i>if</i> Pickton appeals his convictions, and <i>if</i> that appeal is allowed, resulting in a new trial, the prosecution will want to hold that new trial on the original 26 charges of first-degree murder. But the Crown would be precluded from doing so unless it had successfully appealed the original acquittals on the first-degree murder charges, and the severance of the 26 counts into one group of six and one group of twenty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under the applicable rules of court,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> the time period for the Crown to appeal expired 30 days after December 9, when the verdicts were rendered, while the time period for the defence to appeal expired 30 days after December 11, when Pickton was sentenced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vancouver_Sun_2008-01-07_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-Vancouver_Sun_2008-01-07-41">[41]</a></sup> That is why the Crown announced its appeal first, even though the Crown appeal is intended to be conditional on an appeal by the defence. If the defence had not filed an appeal, then the Crown could have withdrawn its appeal.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defense_appeal">Defense appeal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Defense appeal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On January 9, 2008, lawyers for Pickton filed a notice of appeal in the British Columbia Court of Appeal, seeking a new trial on six counts of second-degree murder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vancouver_Sun_2008-01-09_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vancouver_Sun_2008-01-09-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The lawyer representing Pickton on the appeal was Gil McKinnon, who had been a Crown prosecutor in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>The notice of appeal enumerated various areas in which the defense alleged that the trial judge erred: the main charge to the jury, the response to the jurors’ question, amending the jury charge, similar fact evidence, and Pickton’s statements to the police.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decisions_of_the_Court_of_Appeal">Decisions of the Court of Appeal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Decisions of the Court of Appeal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The British Columbia Court of Appeal issued its decisions on June 25, 2009, but some parts of the decisions were not publicly released because of publication bans still in place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Court of Appeal dismissed the defence appeal by a 2:1 majority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Because there was a dissent on a point of law, Pickton was entitled to appeal to the Supreme Court of Canada, without first seeking leave to appeal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> His notice of appeal was filed in the Supreme Court of Canada on August 24, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Court of Appeal allowed the crown appeal, finding that the trial judge erred in excluding some evidence and in severing the 26 counts into one group of 20 counts and one group of 6. The order resulting from this finding was stayed, so that the conviction on the six counts of second degree murder would not be set aside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Supreme_Court_of_Canada">Supreme Court of Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Supreme Court of Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On June 26, 2009, Pickton's lawyers confirmed that they would exercise his right to appeal to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Canada" title="Supreme Court of Canada">Supreme Court of Canada</a>. The appeal was based on the dissent in the British Columbia Court of Appeal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Pickton had an automatic right to appeal to the Supreme Court of Canada based on the legal issues on which Justice Donald had dissented, Pickton's lawyers applied to the Supreme Court of Canada for leave to appeal on other issues as well. On November 26, 2009, the Supreme Court of Canada granted this application for leave to appeal. The effect of this was to broaden the scope of Pickton's appeal, allowing him to raise arguments that had been rejected unanimously in the B.C. Court of Appeal (not just arguments that had been rejected by the 2-1 majority).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 30, 2010, the Supreme Court of Canada rendered its decision dismissing Pickton's appeal and affirming his convictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The argument that Pickton should be granted a new trial was unanimously rejected by the Justices of the Supreme Court of Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although unanimous in its result, the Supreme Court split six to three in its legal analysis of the case. The issue was whether the trial judge made a legal error in his instructions to the jury, and in particular in his "re-instruction" responding to the jury's question about Pickton's liability if he was not the only person involved. Writing for the majority, <a href="/wiki/Louise_Charron" title="Louise Charron">Madam Justice Charron</a> found that "the trial judge's response to the question posed by the jury did not adversely impact on the fairness of the trial". She further found that the trial judge's overall instructions with respect to other suspects "compendiously captured the alternative routes to liability that were realistically in issue in this trial. The jury was also correctly instructed that it could convict Mr. Pickton if the Crown proved this level of participation coupled with the requisite intent."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R._v._Pickton_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-R._v._Pickton-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Louis_LeBel" title="Louis LeBel">Mr. Justice LeBel</a>, writing for the minority, found that the jury was not properly informed "of the legal principles which would have allowed them as triers of fact to consider evidence of Mr. Pickton’s aid and encouragement to an unknown shooter, as an alternative means of imposing liability for the murders." However, LeBel J. would have applied the so-called curative proviso<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> so as not to overturn Pickton's convictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R._v._Pickton_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-R._v._Pickton-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_and_aftermath_to_the_court_proceedings">Reaction and aftermath to the court proceedings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reaction and aftermath to the court proceedings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discontinuance_of_prosecution_of_other_counts_against_Pickton">Discontinuance of prosecution of other counts against Pickton</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Discontinuance of prosecution of other counts against Pickton">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>B.C. Crown spokesman Neil MacKenzie announced that the prosecution of Pickton on the 20 other murder charges would likely be discontinued. "In reaching this position," he said, "the branch has taken into account the fact that any additional convictions could not result in any increase to the sentence that Mr. Pickton has already received."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBC_News_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBC_News-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Families of the victims had varied reactions to this announcement. Some were disappointed that Pickton would never be convicted of the 20 other murders, while others were relieved that the gruesome details of the murders would not be aired in court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charges_stayed_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-charges_stayed-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vancouver_Police_Department_management_review_of_investigation">Vancouver Police Department management review of investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Vancouver Police Department management review of investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Police_Department" title="Vancouver Police Department">Vancouver Police Department</a> issued a statement that an "exhaustive management review of the Missing Women Investigation" had been conducted, and the VPD would make the Review available to the public once the criminal matters are concluded and the publication bans are removed. In addition, the VPD disclosed that for several years it has "communicated privately to the Provincial Government that it believed a Public Inquiry would be necessary for an impartial examination of why it took so long for Robert Pickton to be arrested."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> In August of that year, the VPD released the <i>Missing Women: Investigation Review</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="VPD_apology">VPD apology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: VPD apology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At a press conference, Deputy Chief Constable Doug LePard of the VPD apologized to the victims' families, saying "I wish from the bottom of my heart that we would have caught him sooner. I wish that, the several agencies involved, that we could have done better in so many ways. I wish that all the mistakes that were made, we could undo. And I wish that more lives would have been saved. So on my behalf and behalf of the Vancouver Police Department and all the men and women that worked on this investigation, I would say to the families how sorry we all are for your losses and because we did not catch this monster sooner."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Missing_Women_Commission_of_Inquiry">The Missing Women Commission of Inquiry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: The Missing Women Commission of Inquiry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After Robert Pickton lost his final appeal at the Supreme Court of Canada, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missing_Women_Commission_of_Inquiry" title="Missing Women Commission of Inquiry">Missing Women Commission of Inquiry</a> chaired by <a href="/wiki/Wally_Oppal" title="Wally Oppal">Wally Oppal</a> was called to examine the role of the Vancouver police and the RCMP in the disappearances and murders of women in the Downtown Eastside. Families of the missing and murdered women have been calling for public hearings since before Pickton was arrested and eventually convicted of six murders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The Commission's final report submission to the Attorney General was dated November 19, 2012 and was released to the public on December 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> During the inquiry, lawyers for some of the victims' families sought to have an unpublished 289-page manuscript authored by former police investigator Lori Shenher entered as evidence and made entirely public. Several passages were read into the inquiry's record but Commissioner Oppal declined to publicize the entire manuscript.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transfer_to_penitentiary">Transfer to penitentiary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Transfer to penitentiary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During a court hearing on August 4, 2010, Judge Williams stated that Pickton should be committed to a federal penitentiary; up to that point he had been held at a provincial pretrial institution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charges_stayed_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-charges_stayed-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stay_of_final_20_murder_charges">Stay of final 20 murder charges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Stay of final 20 murder charges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pickton had faced a further 20 first degree murder charges involving other female victims from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vancouver%27s_Downtown_Eastside" title="Vancouver's Downtown Eastside">Vancouver's Downtown Eastside</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> On February 26, 2008, a family member of one of the 20 women named as alleged victims told the media that the Crown had told her a trial on the further 20 counts might not proceed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 4, 2010, Crown prosecutors stayed the balance of the pending murder charges against Pickton, ending the prospect of any further trials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 20 charges were formally stayed by crown counsel Melissa Gillespie shortly after 4 p.m. during a British Columbia Supreme Court hearing at <a href="/wiki/New_Westminster" title="New Westminster">New Westminster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chad_Skelton_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chad_Skelton-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most (but not all) of the publication bans in the case were lifted by the trial judge, James Williams of the British Columbia Supreme Court, after lawyers spent hours in court going through the various complicated bans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vancouversun.com_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-vancouversun.com-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 6, 2010, various media outlets released a transcript of conversations between an RCMP undercover operator and Pickton in his holding cell. While the RCMP censored the undercover officer's name throughout most of the document, his name was left uncensored in several portions of the document that the RCMP released to the public. This uncensored version was available to the public, through <a href="/wiki/Global_News" title="Global News">Global News</a>, <a href="/wiki/CTV_News" title="CTV News">CTV News</a>, and the Vancouver Sun, for about an hour before being pulled and re-edited. It is not known the extent of the damage this mistake caused the undercover officer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vancouversun.com_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-vancouversun.com-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Victims">Victims</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Victims">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On December 9, 2007, Pickton was convicted of second-degree murder in the deaths of six women:</p>
<ul>
<li>Count 1, Sereena Abotsway<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> (born August 20, 1971), age 29 when she disappeared in August 2001; her foster mother reported her missing on Aug. 22, 2001.</li>
<li>Count 2, Mona Lee Wilson<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> (born January 13, 1975), age 26 when she went to her doctor on Nov. 30, 2001, and was reported missing that night.</li>
<li>Count 6, Andrea Joesbury, age 22 when last seen in June 2001 and was reported missing June 8, 2001.</li>
<li>Count 7, Brenda Ann Wolfe,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> age 32 when last seen in February 1999 and was reported missing on April 25, 2000.</li>
<li>Count 16, Marnie Lee Frey,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> last seen August 1997 and reported missing on Dec. 29, 1997.<sup>Vancouver Police Missing Persons Case #98-209922.</sup></li>
<li>Count 11, Georgina Faith Papin, last seen in January 1999 and reported missing in March 2001.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="More_victims">More victims</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: More victims">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pickton also stood accused of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First-degree_murder" title="First-degree murder">first-degree murder</a> in the deaths of twenty other women until these charges were stayed on August 4, 2010.</p>
<ul>
<li>Count 3, Jacqueline Michelle McDonell,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> 23 when she was last seen in January 1999. <sup>Vancouver Police Missing Persons Case # 99-039699.</sup></li>
<li>Count 4, Dianne Rosemary Rock<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> (born September 2, 1967), 34 when last seen on October 19, 2001. Reported missing December 13, 2001.</li>
<li>Count 5, Heather Kathleen Bottomley<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> (born August 17, 1976), 25 when she was last seen (and reported missing) on April 17, 2001.</li>
<li>Count 8, Jennifer Lynn Furminger, last seen in 1999.</li>
<li>Count 9, Helen Mae Hallmark,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> last seen August 1997. <sup>Vancouver Police Missing Persons Case #98-226384.</sup></li>
<li>Count 10, Patricia Rose Johnson,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> last seen in March 2001.</li>
<li>Count 12, Heather Chinnook, 30 when last seen in April 2001.</li>
<li>Count 13, Tanya Holyk, 23 when last seen in October 1996.</li>
<li>Count 14, Sherry Irving,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> 24 when last seen in 1997.</li>
<li>Count 15, Inga Monique Hall,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> 46 when last seen in February 1998. <sup>Vancouver Police Missing Persons Case # 98-047919.</sup></li>
<li>Count 17, Tiffany Drew, last seen December 1999.</li>
<li>Count 18, Sarah de Vries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> last seen April 1998.</li>
<li>Count 19, Cynthia Feliks,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> last seen in December 1997.</li>
<li>Count 20, Angela Rebecca Jardine,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> last seen November 20, 1998 between 3:30- 4p.m. at Oppenheimer Park at a rally in the downtown Eastside of Vancouver, British Columbia, Canada. <sup>Vancouver Police Missing Persons Case # 98.286097.</sup></li>
<li>Count 21, Diana Melnick,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> last seen in December 1995.</li>
<li>Count 22, <a href="/wiki/John_Doe" title="John Doe">Jane Doe</a> – charge lifted; see below.</li>

<li>Count 26, Andrea Fay Borhaven,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> last seen in March 1997. <sup>Vancouver Police Missing Persons Case # 99.105703.</sup></li>
<li>Count 27, Cara Louise Ellis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> aka Nicky Trimble (born April 13, 1971), 25 when last seen in 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> Reported missing October 2002.</li>
</ul>
<p>As of March 2, 2006, the murder charge involving the unidentified victim has been lifted. Pickton refused to enter a plea on the charge involving this victim, known in the proceedings as Jane Doe, so the court registered a not-guilty plea on his behalf. "The count as drawn fails to meet the minimal requirement set out in Section 581 of the Criminal Code. Accordingly, it must be quashed," wrote Justice James Williams. The detailed reasons for the judge's ruling cannot be reported in Canada because of the publication ban covering this stage of the trial.</p>
<p>Pickton is implicated in the murders of the following women, but charges have not yet been laid (incomplete list):</p>
<ul>
<li>Mary Ann Clark<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> aka Nancy Greek, 25, disappeared in August 1991 from downtown Victoria.</li>
<li>Yvonne Marie Boen (sometimes used the surname England)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> (born November 30, 1967), 34 when last seen on March 16, 2001 and reported missing on March 21, 2001.</li>
<li>Dawn Teresa Crey,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> reported missing in December 2000. Crey is the main subject of a 2006 documentary film about murdered and missing Aboriginal women in Canada, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Finding_Dawn" title="Finding Dawn">Finding Dawn</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CM_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-CM-99">[99]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NFB.ca_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-NFB.ca-100">[100]</a></sup></li>
<li>Two unidentified women.</li>
</ul>
<p>After Pickton was arrested many people started coming forward and talking to police about what was going on at the farm. One of these witnesses that came forward was Lynn Ellingsen. Ellingsen claimed to have seen Pickton skinning a woman hanging from a meat hook years earlier; she did not tell anyone about this out of fear for her life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-King.2C_Gary_2009_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-King.2C_Gary_2009-101">[101]</a></sup> Additionally, Ellingsen admitted that she blackmailed Pickton<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> about the incident on more than one occasion.</p>
<p>The victims' children filed a civil lawsuit in May 2013 against the Vancouver Police Department, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Mounted_Police" title="Royal Canadian Mounted Police">Royal Canadian Mounted Police</a> and the Crown for failing to protect the victims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> They reached a settlement in March 2014, where each of the children was to be compensated $50,000, without an admission of liability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="August_2006_.27Pickton_Letters.27">August 2006 'Pickton Letters'</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: August 2006 'Pickton Letters'">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In August 2006, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Loudamy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thomas Loudamy (page does not exist)">Thomas Loudamy</a>, a 27-year-old <a href="/wiki/Fremont,_California" title="Fremont, California">Fremont, California</a> resident, claimed that he had received three letters from Robert Pickton in response to letters Loudamy sent under an assumed identity.</p>
<p>In the letters, Pickton allegedly speaks with concern about the expense of the investigation, asserts his innocence, quotes and refers to the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> praises the trial judge, and responds in detail to (fictional) information in Loudamy's letters, which were written in the guise of Mya Barnett, a 'down on her luck' woman.</p>
<p>The news of the letters' existence was broken by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Vancouver_Sun" title="The Vancouver Sun">The Vancouver Sun</a></i>, in an exclusive published on Saturday, September 2, 2006, and as of that date, neither law enforcement nor any representative of Pickton has verified the authenticity of the letters. The <i>Sun</i>, however, has undertaken several actions to confirm the documents' authenticity, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Confirming that the outgoing stamps are consistent with those of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=North_Fraser_Pretrial_Centre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="North Fraser Pretrial Centre (page does not exist)">North Fraser Pretrial Centre</a> (NFPC), where Pickton is being held;</li>
<li>Confirming through a representative of <a href="/wiki/Canada_Post" title="Canada Post">Canada Post</a> that the outgoing stamps are not forgeries; and</li>
<li>Confirming that the machine (identifiable with a serial number included in the stamp) used to stamp the envelopes is the machine used by the NFPC.</li>
</ul>
<p>Loudamy claims not to have kept copies of his outgoing letters to Pickton, and as of September 4, 2006, no information on their existence has been forthcoming from Pickton or his representatives.</p>
<p>Loudamy has a history of writing to accused and convicted criminals, in some instances under his own identity (as with his correspondence with <a href="/wiki/Clifford_Olson" title="Clifford Olson">Clifford Olson</a>), and in others in the guise of a character he believes will be more readily accepted by the targets of the letters. Loudamy, an aspiring journalist, claims that his motivation in releasing the letters is to help the public gain insights into Pickton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2015_filming">2015 filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: 2015 filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2015, a film with the working title of <i>Full Flood</i> began production in Vancouver by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CBC-TV" title="CBC-TV">CBC-TV</a>. Based on <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Cameron" title="Stevie Cameron">Stevie Cameron</a>'s book <i>On The Farm</i> it was to use the life experiences of Pickton's victims for a fictional story about women in the Downtown Eastside who became victims of a serial killer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2016_autobiography">2016 autobiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Pickton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: 2016 autobiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2016, a book claimed to have been written by Pickton and titled <i>Pickton: In his Own Words</i> went up for sale and initiated controversy, critical petitions and actions by government to prevent Pickton from profiting from the work.</p>
<p>Pickton was described as getting his manuscript out of prison by passing it to a former cellmate who then sent it to a retired construction worker from California named Michael Chilldres — who typed it up and is credited as the author of the 144-page book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ctvbook_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-ctvbook-109">[109]</a></sup> Provincial Solicitor General <a href="/wiki/Mike_Morris_(politician)" title="Mike Morris (politician)">Mike Morris</a> and an online petition on <a href="/wiki/Change.org" title="Change.org">Change.org</a> each sought to remove the book from sale on <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ctvbook_109-1"><a href="#cite_note-ctvbook-109">[109]</a></sup> Premier <a href="/wiki/Christy_Clark" title="Christy Clark">Christy Clark</a> expressed interest in introducing new legislation similar to laws in Alberta, Saskatchewan, Nova Scotia and Ontario to prevent criminal profits from such books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GM-book_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-GM-book-110">[110]</a></sup> Colorado publisher <a href="/wiki/Outskirts_Press" title="Outskirts Press">Outskirts Press</a> ceased publication of the book and asked Amazon to remove it from their site after finding out that although Chilldres had his name on the book cover, the author was actually an incarcerated criminal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GM-book_110-1"><a href="#cite_note-GM-book-110">[110]</a></sup></p>
