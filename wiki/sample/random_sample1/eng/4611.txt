<p><b>Robert II</b> (27 March 972 – 20 July 1031), called <b>the Pious</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>le Pieux</i></span>) or <b>the Wise</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>le Sage</i></span>), was <a href="/wiki/List_of_French_monarchs" title="List of French monarchs">King of the Franks</a> from 996 until his death. The second reigning member of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Capet" title="House of Capet">House of Capet</a>, he was born in <a href="/wiki/Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Orléans">Orléans</a> to <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Capet" title="Hugh Capet">Hugh Capet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_of_Aquitaine" title="Adelaide of Aquitaine">Adelaide of Aquitaine</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Co-rule_with_father">Co-rule with father</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_II_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Co-rule with father">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Denier_de_Robert_II_le_Pieux.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="165" data-file-width="331" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Denier_de_Robert_II_le_Pieux.jpg/220px-Denier_de_Robert_II_le_Pieux.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Denier_de_Robert_II_le_Pieux.jpg/330px-Denier_de_Robert_II_le_Pieux.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/Denier_de_Robert_II_le_Pieux.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Denier_de_Robert_II_le_Pieux.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Denier of Robert II the Pious, struck at Soissons</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Immediately after his own coronation, Robert's father Hugh began to push for the coronation of Robert. "The essential means by which the early Capetians were seen to have kept the throne in their family was through the association of the eldest surviving son in the royalty during the father's lifetime," Andrew W. Lewis has observed, in tracing the phenomenon in this line of kings who lacked dynastic legitimacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Hugh's claimed reason was that he was planning an expedition against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moorish" title="Moorish">Moorish</a> armies harassing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borrel_II_of_Barcelona" title="Borrel II of Barcelona">Borrel II of Barcelona</a>, an invasion which never occurred, and that the stability of the country necessitated a co-king, should he die while on expedition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ralph_Glaber" title="Ralph Glaber">Ralph Glaber</a>, however, attributes Hugh's request to his old age and inability to control the nobility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Modern scholarship has largely imputed to Hugh the motive of establishing a dynasty against the claims of electoral power on the part of the aristocracy, but this is not the typical view of contemporaries and even some modern scholars have been less sceptical of Hugh's "plan" to campaign in Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Robert was eventually crowned on 25 December 987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A measure of Hugh's success is that when Hugh died in 996, Robert continued to reign without any succession dispute, but during his long reign actual royal power dissipated into the hands of the great territorial magnates.</p>
<p>Robert had begun to take on active royal duties with his father in the early 990s. In 991, he helped his father prevent the French bishops from trekking to <a href="/wiki/Mousson" title="Mousson">Mousson</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Germany" title="Kingdom of Germany">Kingdom of Germany</a> for a synod called by <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XV" title="Pope John XV">Pope John XV</a>, with whom Hugh was then in disagreement.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marital_problems">Marital problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_II_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marital problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As early as 989, having been rebuffed in his search for a Byzantine princess,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Hugh Capet arranged for Robert to marry <a href="/wiki/Rozala_of_Italy" title="Rozala of Italy">Rozala</a>, the recently widowed daughter of <a href="/wiki/Berengar_II_of_Italy" title="Berengar II of Italy">Berengar II of Italy</a>, many years his senior, who took the name of Susanna upon becoming Queen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> She was the widow of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arnulf_II_of_Flanders" title="Arnulf II of Flanders">Arnulf II of Flanders</a>, with whom she had two children. Robert divorced her within a year of his father's death in 996. He tried instead to marry <a href="/wiki/Bertha_of_Burgundy" title="Bertha of Burgundy">Bertha</a>, daughter of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conrad_of_Burgundy" title="Conrad of Burgundy">Conrad of Burgundy</a>, around the time of his father's death. She was a widow of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Odo_I_of_Blois" title="Odo I of Blois">Odo I of Blois</a>, but was also Robert's cousin. For reasons of <a href="/wiki/Consanguinity" title="Consanguinity">consanguinity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_V" title="Pope Gregory V">Pope Gregory V</a> refused to sanction the marriage, and Robert was <a href="/wiki/Excommunication" title="Excommunication">excommunicated</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> After long negotiations with Gregory's successor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pope_Silvester_II" title="Pope Silvester II">Sylvester II</a>, the marriage was annulled.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:200px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seal_of_Robert_II.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="246" data-file-width="198" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/52/Seal_of_Robert_II.jpg" width="198"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seal_of_Robert_II.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Seal of Robert II</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Finally, in 1001, Robert entered into his final and longest-lasting marriage to <a href="/wiki/Constance_of_Arles" title="Constance of Arles">Constance of Arles</a>, the daughter of <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_Provence" title="William I of Provence">William I of Provence</a>. Her southern customs and entourage were regarded with suspicion at court. After his companion Hugh of Beauvais urged the king to repudiate her as well, knights of her kinsman <a href="/wiki/Fulk_III,_Count_of_Anjou" title="Fulk III, Count of Anjou">Fulk III, Count of Anjou</a> had Beauvais murdered. The king and Bertha then went to Rome to ask <a href="/wiki/Pope_Sergius_IV" title="Pope Sergius IV">Pope Sergius IV</a> for an annulment so they could remarry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> After this was refused, he went back to Constance and fathered several children by her. Her ambition alienated the chroniclers of her day, who blamed her for several of the king's decisions. Constance and Robert remained married until his death in 1031.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Piety">Piety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_II_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Piety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Robert was a devout Catholic, hence his sobriquet "the Pious." He was musically inclined, being a composer, chorister, and poet, and made his palace a place of religious seclusion where he conducted the matins and vespers in his royal robes. Robert's reputation for piety also resulted from his lack of toleration for heretics, whom he harshly punished. He is credited with advocating forced conversions of local Jewry. He supported riots against the Jews of Orléans who were accused of conspiring to destroy the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Holy_Sepulchre" title="Church of the Holy Sepulchre">Church of the Holy Sepulchre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Furthermore, Robert reinstated the Roman imperial custom of burning heretics at the stake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_career">Military career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_II_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Military career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The kingdom Robert inherited was not large, and in an effort to increase his power, he vigorously pursued his claim to any feudal lands that became vacant, usually resulting in war with a counter-claimant. In 1003, his invasion of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Burgundy" title="Duchy of Burgundy">Duchy of Burgundy</a> was thwarted, and it would not be until 1016 that he was finally able to get the support of the Church to be recognized as <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Burgundy" title="Duke of Burgundy">Duke of Burgundy</a>.</p>
<p>The pious Robert made few friends and many enemies, including his own sons: <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Magnus" title="Hugh Magnus">Hugh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_I_of_France" title="Henry I of France">Henry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Robert_I,_Duke_of_Burgundy" title="Robert I, Duke of Burgundy">Robert</a>. They turned against their father in a civil war over power and property. Hugh died in revolt in 1025. In a conflict with Henry and the younger Robert, King Robert's army was defeated, and he retreated to <a href="/wiki/Beaugency" title="Beaugency">Beaugency</a> outside Paris, his capital. He died in the middle of the war with his sons on 20 July 1031 at <a href="/wiki/Melun" title="Melun">Melun</a>. He was interred with Constance in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Denis_Basilica" title="Saint Denis Basilica">Saint Denis Basilica</a> and succeeded by his son Henry, in both France and Burgundy.</p>

<p>Robert had no children from his short-lived marriage to Susanna. His illegal marriage to Bertha gave him one stillborn son in 999, but only Constance gave him surviving children:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hedwig_of_France,_Countess_of_Nevers" title="Hedwig of France, Countess of Nevers">Hedwig (or Advisa)</a>, Countess of Auxerre (c. 1003 – after 1063), married <a href="/wiki/Renauld_I,_Count_of_Nevers" title="Renauld I, Count of Nevers">Renauld I, Count of Nevers</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> on 25 January 1016 and had issue.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hugh_Magnus" title="Hugh Magnus">Hugh Magnus</a>, co-king (1007 – 17 September 1025)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Henry_I_of_France" title="Henry I of France">Henry I</a>, successor (4 May 1008 – 4 August 1060)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adela_of_France,_Countess_of_Flanders" title="Adela of France, Countess of Flanders">Adela, Countess of Contenance</a> (1009 – 5 June 1063), married (1) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_III_of_Normandy" title="Richard III of Normandy">Richard III of Normandy</a> and (2) Count <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baldwin_V_of_Flanders" title="Baldwin V of Flanders">Baldwin V of Flanders</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Robert_I,_Duke_of_Burgundy" title="Robert I, Duke of Burgundy">Robert</a> (1011 – 21 March 1076)</li>
<li>Odo or Eudes (1013–c.1056), who may have been <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_disability" title="Intellectual disability">intellectually disabled</a> and died after his brother's failed invasion of Normandy</li>
<li>Constance (1014–1052), married Count <a href="/wiki/Manasses,_Count_of_Dammartin" title="Manasses, Count of Dammartin">Manasses de Dammartin</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Robert also left an illegitimate son: Rudolph, Bishop of Bourges.</p>
