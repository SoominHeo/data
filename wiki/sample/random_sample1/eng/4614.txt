<p><b>Robert Musil</b> (<small>German:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ˈmuːzɪl]</a></span> or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈmuːsɪl]</span>; 6 November 1880 – 15 April 1942) was an Austrian philosophical writer. His unfinished novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_Without_Qualities" title="The Man Without Qualities">The Man Without Qualities</a></i> (German: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Der Mann ohne Eigenschaften</i></span>) is generally considered to be one of the most important and influential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modernist_novel" title="Modernist novel">modernist novels</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Musil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Musil was born in Klagenfurt, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carinthia_(state)" title="Carinthia (state)">Kärnten</a>, the son of engineer Alfred <i>Edler von</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-since_October_22.2C_1917_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-since_October_22.2C_1917-1">[1]</a></sup> Musil (1846, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timi%C5%9Foara" title="Timişoara">Timișoara</a> – 1924) and his wife Hermine Bergauer (1853, <a href="/wiki/Linz" title="Linz">Linz</a> – 1924). The orientalist <a href="/wiki/Alois_Musil" title="Alois Musil">Alois Musil</a> ("The Czech <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lawrence_Of_Arabia" title="Lawrence Of Arabia">Lawrence</a>") was his second cousin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Soon after Robert's birth, the family moved to <a href="/wiki/Chomutov" title="Chomutov">Chomutov</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bohemia" title="Kingdom of Bohemia">Bohemia</a>, and in 1891 Musil's father was appointed to the chair of Mechanical Engineering at the <a href="/wiki/German_Technical_University_in_Brno" title="German Technical University in Brno">German Technical University in Brno</a>, and awarded a hereditary nobility in the <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austro-Hungarian Empire</a>. He was baptized <i>Robert Mathias Musil</i> and his name was officially <i>Robert Mathias Edler von Musil</i> from 22 October 1917, when his father received a hereditary title of nobility <a href="/wiki/Edler" title="Edler">Edler</a>, until 3 April 1919, when the use of noble titles was <a href="/wiki/Austrian_nobility#Abolition_of_nobility_in_1919" title="Austrian nobility">forbidden in Austria</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Musil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Robert_Musil,_pam%C4%9Btn%C3%AD_deska,_Brno.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Robert_Musil%2C_pam%C4%9Btn%C3%AD_deska%2C_Brno.JPG/220px-Robert_Musil%2C_pam%C4%9Btn%C3%AD_deska%2C_Brno.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Robert_Musil%2C_pam%C4%9Btn%C3%AD_deska%2C_Brno.JPG/330px-Robert_Musil%2C_pam%C4%9Btn%C3%AD_deska%2C_Brno.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Robert_Musil%2C_pam%C4%9Btn%C3%AD_deska%2C_Brno.JPG/440px-Robert_Musil%2C_pam%C4%9Btn%C3%AD_deska%2C_Brno.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Musil was short in stature but strong and skilled at wrestling, and by his early teens, he already more than his parents could handle. They sent him to military boarding school at <a href="/wiki/Eisenstadt" title="Eisenstadt">Eisenstadt</a> (1892–1894) and then <a href="/wiki/Hranice_(P%C5%99erov_District)" title="Hranice (Přerov District)">Hranice</a> (1894–1897). The school experiences are reflected in his first novel, <i>Die Verwirrungen des Zöglings Törless</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Confusions_of_Young_T%C3%B6rless" title="The Confusions of Young Törless">The Confusions of Young Törless</a></i>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Studies">Studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Musil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After graduating as a cadet, Musil studied briefly at a military academy in Vienna during the fall of 1897 but then switched to mechanical engineering, joining his father's department at Technical University in Brno. During his university studies, he studied engineering by day, but at night read literature and philosophy, and went to the theatre and art exhibits. <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">Friedrich Nietzsche</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fyodor_Dostoyevsky" title="Fyodor Dostoyevsky">Fyodor Dostoyevsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Ralph Waldo Emerson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Mach" title="Ernst Mach">Ernst Mach</a> were particular interests of his university years. Musil finished his studies in three years and, in 1902–1903, served as an unpaid assistant to Professor <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Julius_Carl_von_Bach&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Julius Carl von Bach (page does not exist)">Julius Carl von Bach</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_von_Bach" title="de:Carl von Bach">de</a>)</span>, in <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>. Meanwhile, he began work on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Confusions_of_Young_T%C3%B6rless" title="The Confusions of Young Törless">Young Törless</a></i> and invented "Musil'scher Farbkreisel", the Musil color top, a simple device for continuous producing mixed colours by additive colour -mixing with two differently colored rotating disks.</p>
<p>Musil grew tired of engineering and what he perceived as the limited world-view of engineers. He launched a new round of doctoral studies (1903–1908) in psychology and philosophy at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Berlin" title="University of Berlin">University of Berlin</a> under the renowned Professor <a href="/wiki/Carl_Stumpf" title="Carl Stumpf">Carl Stumpf</a>. In 1905, Musil met his future wife, Martha Marcovaldi (née Heinemann, 21 January 1874 – 6 November 1949). She had already been widowed and remarried, with two children, and was seven years older than Musil. His first novel, <i>Young Törless</i>, was published in 1906.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Author">Author</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Musil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Author">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1909, Musil completed his doctorate and Professor <a href="/wiki/Alexius_Meinong" title="Alexius Meinong">Alexius Meinong</a> offered him a position at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Graz" title="University of Graz">University of Graz</a>, which he turned down to concentrate on writing of novels. Over the next two years, he wrote and published two stories ("The Temptation of Quiet Veronica" and "The Perfecting of a Love") collected in <i>Vereinigungen</i> (<i>Unions</i>) published in 1911. During the same year, Martha's divorce was completed, and Musil married her. As she was Jewish, they both converted to <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a> as a sign of their union.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://escholarship.org/uc/item/5012d14x#page-10" rel="nofollow">[1]</a> <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.deutsche-biographie.de/sfz67509.html" rel="nofollow">[2]</a> <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.hls-dhs-dss.ch/textes/d/D12180.php" rel="nofollow">[3]</a> Until then, Musil had been supported by his family, but he now found employment first as a librarian in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technical_University_of_Vienna" title="Technical University of Vienna">Technical University of Vienna</a> and then in an editorial role with the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Berlin_Literary_Journal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Berlin Literary Journal (page does not exist)">Berlin Literary Journal</a></i>, He also worked on a play entitled <i>Die Schwärmer</i> (<i>The Enthusiasts</i>), which was published in 1921.</p>

<p>When <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> began, Musil joined the Army and was stationed first in <a href="/wiki/Tyrol_(state)" title="Tyrol (state)">Tirol</a> and then at Austria's Supreme Army Command in <a href="/wiki/Bolzano" title="Bolzano">Bolzano</a>. In 1916, Musil visited <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> and met <a href="/wiki/Franz_Kafka" title="Franz Kafka">Franz Kafka</a>, whose work he held in high esteem. After the war's end, and the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austro-Hungarian Empire</a>, Musil returned to his literary career in Vienna. He published a collection of short stories, <i>Drei Frauen</i> (<i>Three Women</i>), in 1924. He also admired the Bohemian poet <a href="/wiki/Rainer_Maria_Rilke" title="Rainer Maria Rilke">Rainer Maria Rilke</a> at whose memorial service in 1926 in Berlin, Musil called "undervalued" for most of his life. He said that by the time of his death, Rilke had turned into "a delicate, well-matured liqueur suitable for grown-up ladies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, his work is "too demanding" to be "considered relaxing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1930 and 1933,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bookseller_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bookseller-5">[5]</a></sup> in Berlin – 1,074-page<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiki_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-wiki-6">[6]</a></sup> he published Volume 1 (Part I: A Sort of Introduction, and Part II: The Like of It Now Happens) and 605-page unfinished Volume 2 (Part III: Into the Millennium (The Criminals)) of his masterpiece, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_Without_Qualities" title="The Man Without Qualities">The Man Without Qualities</a></i> (<i>Der Mann ohne Eigenschaften</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freed_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Freed-7">[7]</a></sup> Part III did not include 20 chapters withdrawn from Volume 2 of 1933 in printer's galley proofs. The novel deals with the moral and intellectual decline of the Austro-Hungarian Empire through the eyes of the book's protagonist, Ulrich, an ex-mathematician who has failed to engage with the world around him in a manner that would allow him to possess <i>qualities</i>. It is set in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> on the eve of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.</p>
<p><i>The Man Without Qualities</i> brought Musil only mediocre commercial success. Though he was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize in Literature</a>, he felt that he did not receive the recognition he deserved. He sometimes expressed annoyance at the success of more famous colleagues like <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Mann" title="Thomas Mann">Thomas Mann</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Broch" title="Hermann Broch">Hermann Broch</a>, who admired his work deeply and tried to shield him from economic difficulties and encouraged his writing even though Musil was initially critical of Mann.</p>
<p>In the early 1920s, Musil lived mostly in Berlin. In Vienna, Musil was a frequent visitor to <a href="/wiki/Eugenie_Schwarzwald" title="Eugenie Schwarzwald">Eugenie Schwarzwald</a>'s salon (the model for <a href="/wiki/Diotima_of_Mantinea" title="Diotima of Mantinea">Diotima</a> in <i>The Man Without Qualities</i>). In 1932, the Robert Musil Society was founded in Berlin on the initiative of Mann. In the same year, Mann was asked to name outstanding contemporary novels, and he cited only one, <i>The Man Without Qualities</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1936, Musil suffered his first stroke.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Musil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The last years of Musil's life were dominated by <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazism</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>: the Nazis banned his books. He saw early Nazism first-hand while he was living in Berlin from 1931 to 1933. In 1938, when Austria became a part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Reich" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a>, Musil and his Jewish wife, Martha, left for exile in Switzerland, where he died at the age of 61. Martha wrote to Franz Theodor Csokor that he had suffered a stroke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Only eight people attended his <a href="/wiki/Cremation" title="Cremation">cremation</a>. Martha cast his ashes into the woods of Mont <a href="/wiki/Sal%C3%A8ve" title="Salève">Salève</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> From 1933 to his death, Musil was working on Part III of <i>The Man Without Qualities</i>. In 1943 in Lausanne, Martha published a 462-page collection of material from his literary remains, including the 20 galley chapters withdrawn from Part III before Volume 2 appeared in 1933,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bookseller_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bookseller-5">[5]</a></sup> as well as drafts of the final incomplete chapters and notes on the development and direction of the novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freed_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Freed-7">[7]</a></sup> She died in Rome in 1949.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Musil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After his death Musil's work was almost forgotten. His writings began to reappear during the early 1950s. The first translation of <i>The Man Without Qualities</i> in English was published by Ernst Kaiser and Eithne Wilkins in 1953, 1954 and 1960. An updated translation by Sophie Wilkins and <a href="/wiki/Burton_Pike" title="Burton Pike">Burton Pike</a>, containing extensive selections from unpublished drafts, appeared in 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Musil's work has received more attention since that time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> including the philosophical aspects of his novels. One of the most important philosophy journals, <a href="/wiki/The_Monist" title="The Monist">The Monist</a> published a special issue on The Philosophy of Robert Musil in 2014, edited by <a href="/wiki/Bence_Nanay" title="Bence Nanay">Bence Nanay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li>1880 November 6, Robert Musil born in Klagenfurt. Father Engineer Alfred Musil, mother Hermine.</li>
<li>1881–1882 The Musils move to Chomutov in Bohemia.</li>
<li>1882–1891 The Musils move to <a href="/wiki/Steyr" title="Steyr">Steyr</a> (Austria). Robert attends the Elementary School and the first grade of the <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)">gymnasium</a>.</li>
<li>1891–1892 Moves to Brno. Attends the <i><a href="/wiki/Realschule" title="Realschule">Realschule</a></i>.</li>
<li>1892–1894 Attends the military boarding school in Eisenstadt.</li>
<li>1894–1897 Attends the military <i>Militär-Oberrealschule</i> in <a href="/wiki/Hranice_(P%C5%99erov_District)" title="Hranice (Přerov District)">Hranice</a> (present-day in the Czech Republic) During his working with artillery Musil discovers his interest in technique.</li>
<li>1897 Attends the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Technische_Milit%C3%A4rakademie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Technische Militärakademie (page does not exist)">Technische Militärakademie</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/k.u.k._Technische_Milit%C3%A4rakademie" title="de:k.u.k. Technische Militärakademie">de</a>)</span></i> in Vienna.</li>
<li>1898–1901 Quits officer training and starts studies at the Technical University in Brno. His father was a professor there since 1890. First literary attempt and first diary notations.</li>
<li>1901 doctoral examinations.</li>
<li>1901–1902 Musil enlists in the infantry regiment of Freiherr von Hess Nr. 49 in Brno.</li>
<li>1902–1903 Moves to Stuttgart to work at the University. Works on his first novel <i>Die Verwirrungen des Zöglings Törless</i></li>
<li>1903–1908 Takes up studies in philosophy; his majors are "logic and experimental psychology".</li>
<li>1905 In his diaries he makes the first notes that develop into <i>Der Mann ohne Eigenschaften</i>.</li>
<li>1906 <i>Die Verwirrungen des Zöglings Torless</i> is published. Developed an apparatus to research colour experience in people.</li>
<li>1908 <i>Beiträge zur Beurteilung der Lehren Machs</i> is the title of his doctoral thesis. Declines an offer to upgrade his last military rank to an equal civilian rank in favour of writing.</li>
<li>1908–1910 Works in Berlin as an editor for the magazine <i>Pan</i> and on his <i>Vereinigungen</i> and <i>Die Schwärmer</i>.</li>
<li>1911–1914 Librarian at the Technical University of Vienna.</li>
<li>1911 on 15 April Musil marries Martha Marcovaldi. <i>Vereinigungen</i> is published.</li>
<li>1912–1914 Editor for several literary magazines, including <i><a href="/wiki/Neue_Rundschau" title="Neue Rundschau">Neue Rundschau</a></i>.</li>
<li>1914–1918 During World War I, Musil is officer at the Italian front. Decorated several times.</li>
<li>1916–1917 July–April: publishes the "Soldaten-Zeitung".</li>
<li>1917 On 22 October, Alfred Musil was hereditary ennobled as <i>Alfred <a href="/wiki/Edler" title="Edler">Edler</a> von Musil</i>, making Robert Musil also a member of the nobility until it was abolished less than two years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-since_October_22.2C_1917_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-since_October_22.2C_1917-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>1918 Takes up writing again.</li>
<li>1919–1920 Works for the Information Service of the Austrian foreign department in Vienna.</li>
<li>1920 April–June: lives in Berlin. Meets Ernest Rowohlt who will become his publisher in 1923.</li>
<li>1920–1922 Adviser for army matters in Vienna.</li>
<li>1921–1931 Works as theatre critic, essayist and writer in Vienna. Works on <i>Der Mann ohne Eigenschaften</i>.</li>
<li>1921 The play <i>Die Schwärmer</i> is published.</li>
<li>1923–1929 Is vice-president of <i>Schutzverbandes deutscher Schriftsteller in Östereich</i>. Meets <a href="/wiki/Hugo_von_Hofmannsthal" title="Hugo von Hofmannsthal">Hugo von Hofmannsthal</a>, who is president of the foundation.</li>
<li>1923 Awarded the <a href="/wiki/Kleist_Prize" title="Kleist Prize">Kleist Prize</a> for <i>Die Schwärmer</i>. On 4 December <i>Vinzenz und die Freundin bedeutender Männer</i> is premièred in Berlin.</li>
<li>1924 on 24 January his mother and on 1 October his father die. Awarded the art prize of the city of Vienna. <i>Drei Frauen</i> is published.</li>
<li>1927 Delivers a speech following the death the previous year of <a href="/wiki/Rainer_Maria_Rilke" title="Rainer Maria Rilke">Rainer Maria Rilke</a> in Berlin.</li>
<li>1929 4 April première of <i>Die Schwärmer</i>. Over Musil's objections, the play is shortened and, according to him, incomprehensible. In the autumn awarded the <a href="/wiki/Gerhart_Hauptmann" title="Gerhart Hauptmann">Gerhart Hauptmann</a> award.</li>
<li>1930 The first two parts of <i>Der Mann ohne Eigenschaften</i> are published. In spite of critical support, Musil's financial situation is precarious.</li>
<li>1931–1933 Lives and works in Berlin.</li>
<li>1932 Foundation of a <i>Musil-Gesellschaft</i> by Kurt Glaser in Berlin. The foundation aims to provide Musil with the means necessary to continue working on his novel. At the end of the year the third part of <i>Der Mann ohne Eigenschaften</i> is published.</li>
<li>1933 in May Musil leaves Berlin with his wife Martha. Via <a href="/wiki/Karlovy_Vary" title="Karlovy Vary">Karlovy Vary</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potstejn" title="Potstejn">Potstejn</a> in <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a> they reach Vienna.</li>
<li>1934–1938 After the dismantling of the Berlin <i>Musil-Gesellschaft</i>, a new one is founded in Vienna.</li>
<li>1935 Lecture for the Internationalen Schriftstellerkongress für die Verteidigung der Kultur" in Paris.</li>
<li>1936 Publishes his collection of thoughts, observations and stories <i>Nachlass zu Lebzeiten</i>. Suffers a stroke.</li>
<li>1938 Via northern Italy Musil and his wife flee to <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a>. Two days after their arrival, on 4 September, they have tea at Thomas Mann's home in Küsnacht.</li>
<li>1939 In July moves to Geneva. Musil continues to work on his novel and grows lonelier with exile. Thanks to the Zürich vicar Robert Lejeune, Musil receives some financial support, including from the American couple Henry Hall and Barbara Church. In Germany and Austria <i>Der Mann ohne Eigenschaften</i> and <i>Nachlaß zu Lebzeiten</i> are banned. All his works are banned in 1941.</li>
<li>1942 April 15, Musil dies in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>.</li>
<li>1943 Martha Musil publishes the unfinished remains of <i>Der Mann ohne Eigenschaften</i>.</li>
<li>1952–1957 Adolf Frisé publishes the complete works of Robert Musil at Rowohlt.</li>
</ul>
