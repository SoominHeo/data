<p><b>Robert Heinrich Hermann Koch</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɔː/ 'au' in 'fraud'">ɔː</span><span title="/x/ 'ch' in 'loch'">x</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <small>German:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ˈkɔχ]</a></span>; 11 December 1843 – 27 May 1910) was a celebrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_people" title="German people">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a> and pioneering <a href="/wiki/Microbiologist" title="Microbiologist">microbiologist</a>. As the founder of modern <a href="/wiki/Bacteriology" title="Bacteriology">bacteriology</a>, he is known for his role in identifying the specific causative agents of <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anthrax" title="Anthrax">anthrax</a> and for giving experimental support for the concept of infectious disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (March 2016)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In addition to his innovative studies on these diseases, Koch created and improved laboratory technologies and techniques in the field of microbiology, and made key discoveries in public health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_A_Life_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch_A_Life-5">[5]</a></sup> His research led to the creation of <a href="/wiki/Koch%27s_postulates" title="Koch's postulates">Koch’s postulates</a>, a series of four generalized principles linking specific microorganisms to specific diseases that remain today the "gold standard" in medical microbiology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_A_Life_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch_A_Life-5">[5]</a></sup> As a result of his groundbreaking research on tuberculosis, Koch received the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> in 1905.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_A_Life_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch_A_Life-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Koch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Robert Koch was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Klausthal" title="Klausthal">Clausthal, Hanover, Germany</a>, on 11 December 1843, to Hermann Koch and Mathilde Julie Henriette Biewand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Founders_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Founders-6">[6]</a></sup> Koch excelled in academics from an early age. Before entering school in 1848, he had taught himself how to read and write.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup> He graduated from high school in 1862, having excelled in science and maths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup> At the age of 19, Koch entered the <a href="/wiki/University_of_G%C3%B6ttingen" title="University of Göttingen">University of Göttingen</a>, studying natural science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heinrich_Hermann_Robert_Koch_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heinrich_Hermann_Robert_Koch-7">[7]</a></sup> However, after three semesters, Koch decided to change his area of study to medicine, as he aspired to be a physician.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup> During his fifth semester of medical school, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacob_Henle" title="Jacob Henle">Jacob Henle</a>, an anatomist who had published a theory of contagion in 1840, asked him to participate in his research project on uterine nerve structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup> In his sixth semester, Koch began to conduct research at the Physiological Institute, where he studied <a href="/wiki/Succinic_acid" title="Succinic acid">succinic acid</a> secretion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup> This would eventually form the basis of his dissertation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_A_Life_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch_A_Life-5">[5]</a></sup> In January 1866, Koch graduated from medical school, earning honors of the highest distinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup> In July 1867, Koch married Emma Adolfine Josephine Fraatz, and the two had a daughter, Gertrude, in 1868.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_A_Life_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch_A_Life-5">[5]</a></sup> Several years after his graduation in 1866, he worked as a surgeon in the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a>, and following his service, worked as a physician in <a href="/wiki/Wolsztyn" title="Wolsztyn">Wollstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Posen" title="Province of Posen">Posen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heinrich_Hermann_Robert_Koch_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heinrich_Hermann_Robert_Koch-7">[7]</a></sup> From 1885 to 1890, he served as an administrator and professor at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berlin_University" title="Berlin University">Berlin University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup> Koch’s marriage to Emma Fraatz ended in 1893, and later that same year, he married actress Hedwig Freiberg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_A_Life_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch_A_Life-5">[5]</a></sup> Koch suffered a heart attack on 9 April 1910, and never made a complete recovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup> On 27 May, only three days after giving a lecture on his tuberculosis research at the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Prussian Academy of Sciences">Prussian Academy of Sciences</a>, Robert Koch died in <a href="/wiki/Baden-Baden" title="Baden-Baden">Baden-Baden</a> at the age of 66.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heinrich_Hermann_Robert_Koch_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Heinrich_Hermann_Robert_Koch-7">[7]</a></sup> Following his death, the Institute named its establishment after him in his honour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup> He was irreligious.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_contributions">Research contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Koch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthrax">Anthrax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Koch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Anthrax">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Robert Koch is widely known for his work with <a href="/wiki/Anthrax" title="Anthrax">anthrax</a>, discovering the causative agent of the fatal disease to be <i><a href="/wiki/Bacillus_anthracis" title="Bacillus anthracis">Bacillus anthracis</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Germ_theory_of_disease_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Germ_theory_of_disease-9">[9]</a></sup> Koch discovered the formation of <a href="/wiki/Bacterial_spore" title="Bacterial spore">spores</a> in anthrax bacteria that could remain dormant under specific conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heinrich_Hermann_Robert_Koch_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Heinrich_Hermann_Robert_Koch-7">[7]</a></sup> However, under optimal conditions, the spores were activated and caused disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heinrich_Hermann_Robert_Koch_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Heinrich_Hermann_Robert_Koch-7">[7]</a></sup> To determine this causative agent, he dry-fixed bacterial cultures onto glass slides, used dyes to stain the cultures, and observed them through a microscope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup> Koch’s work with anthrax is notable in that he was the first to link a specific microorganism with a specific disease, rejecting the idea of <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_generation" title="Spontaneous generation">spontaneous generation</a> and supporting the <a href="/wiki/Germ_theory_of_disease" title="Germ theory of disease">germ theory of disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Germ_theory_of_disease_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Germ_theory_of_disease-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Koch.27s_four_postulates">Koch's four postulates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Koch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Koch's four postulates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Koch%27s_postulates" title="Koch's postulates">Koch's postulates</a></div>
<p>Koch accepted a position as government advisor with the Imperial Department of Health in 1880.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tuberculosis.2C_overview_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tuberculosis.2C_overview-10">[10]</a></sup> During his time as government advisor, he published a report in which he stated the importance of pure cultures in isolating disease-causing organisms and explained the necessary steps to obtain these cultures, methods which are summarized in <a href="/wiki/Koch%27s_postulates" title="Koch's postulates">Koch’s four postulates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bacteriology.2C_historical_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bacteriology.2C_historical-11">[11]</a></sup> Koch’s discovery of the causative agent of anthrax led to the formation of a generic set of postulates which can be used in the determination of the cause of most infectious diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Germ_theory_of_disease_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Germ_theory_of_disease-9">[9]</a></sup> These postulates, which not only outlined a method for linking cause and effect of an infectious disease but also established the significance of laboratory culture of infectious agents, are listed here:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Germ_theory_of_disease_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Germ_theory_of_disease-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>The organism must always be present, in every case of the disease.</li>
<li>The organism must be isolated from a host containing the disease and grown in pure culture.</li>
<li>Samples of the organism taken from pure culture must cause the same disease when inoculated into a healthy, susceptible animal in the laboratory.</li>
<li>The organism must be isolated from the inoculated animal and must be identified as the same original organism first isolated from the originally diseased host.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isolating_pure_culture_on_solid_media">Isolating pure culture on solid media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Koch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Isolating pure culture on solid media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Koch began conducting research on microorganisms in a laboratory connected to his patient examination room.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heinrich_Hermann_Robert_Koch_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Heinrich_Hermann_Robert_Koch-7">[7]</a></sup> Koch’s early research in this laboratory proved to yield one of his major contributions to the field of microbiology, as it was there that he developed the technique of growing bacteria. Koch's second postulate calls for the isolation and growth of a selected pathogen in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pure_culture" title="Pure culture">pure laboratory culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brock_Biology_of_Microorganisms_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brock_Biology_of_Microorganisms-12">[12]</a></sup> In an attempt to grow bacteria, Koch began to use solid nutrients such as potato slices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brock_Biology_of_Microorganisms_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brock_Biology_of_Microorganisms-12">[12]</a></sup> Through these initial experiments, Koch observed individual colonies of identical, pure cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brock_Biology_of_Microorganisms_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brock_Biology_of_Microorganisms-12">[12]</a></sup> Coming to the conclusion that potato slices were not suitable media for all organisms, Koch later began to use nutrient solutions with gelatin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brock_Biology_of_Microorganisms_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Brock_Biology_of_Microorganisms-12">[12]</a></sup> However, he soon realized that gelatin, like potato slices, was not the optimal medium for bacterial growth, as it did not remain solid at 37 °C, the ideal temperature for growth of most human pathogens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brock_Biology_of_Microorganisms_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Brock_Biology_of_Microorganisms-12">[12]</a></sup> As suggested to him by <a href="/wiki/Walther_Hesse" title="Walther Hesse">Walther</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fanny_Hesse" title="Fanny Hesse">Fanny Hesse</a>, Koch began to utilize <a href="/wiki/Agar" title="Agar">agar</a> to grow and isolate pure cultures, as this <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharide</a> remains solid at 37 °C, is not degraded by most bacteria, and results in a transparent medium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brock_Biology_of_Microorganisms_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Brock_Biology_of_Microorganisms-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cholera">Cholera</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Koch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cholera">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Koch next turned his attention to <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>, and began to conduct research in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> in the hopes of isolating the causative agent of the disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heinrich_Hermann_Robert_Koch_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-Heinrich_Hermann_Robert_Koch-7">[7]</a></sup> However, he was not able to complete the task before the epidemic in Egypt ended, and subsequently traveled to <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> to continue with the study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup> In India, Koch was indeed able to determine the causative agent of cholera, isolating <i><a href="/wiki/Vibrio_cholerae" title="Vibrio cholerae">Vibrio cholerae</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The bacterium had originally been isolated in 1854 by Italian anatomist <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Pacini" title="Filippo Pacini">Filippo Pacini</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> but its exact nature and his results were not widely known.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tuberculosis">Tuberculosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Koch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tuberculosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>During his time as the government advisor with the Imperial Department of Health in Berlin in the 1880s, Robert Koch became interested in <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-13"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup> At the time, it was widely believed that tuberculosis was an inherited disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-14"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup> However, Koch was convinced that the disease was caused by a bacterium and was infectious, and tested his four postulates using guinea pigs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-15"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup> Through these experiments, he found that his experiments with tuberculosis satisfied all four of his postulates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-16"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1882, he published his findings on tuberculosis, in which he reported the causative agent of the disease to be the slow-growing <i><a href="/wiki/Mycobacterium_tuberculosis" title="Mycobacterium tuberculosis">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brock_Biology_of_Microorganisms_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-Brock_Biology_of_Microorganisms-12">[12]</a></sup> His work with this disease won Koch the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> in Physiology and Medicine in 1905.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-17"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup> Additionally, Koch's research on tuberculosis, along with his studies on tropical diseases, won him the Prussian Order Pour le Merite in 1906 and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Koch_Medal" title="Robert Koch Medal">Robert Koch medal</a>, established to honour the greatest living physicians, in 1908.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robert_Koch_4-18"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Koch-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honors">Awards and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Koch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Awards and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to being awarded a Nobel Prize, Koch was elected a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Fellows_of_the_Royal_Society_elected_in_1897" title="List of Fellows of the Royal Society elected in 1897">Foreign Member of the Royal Society (ForMemRS) in 1897</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-frs-2">[2]</a></sup> His microbial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postulate" title="Postulate">postulates</a> are named in his honour, <a href="/wiki/Koch%27s_postulates" title="Koch's postulates">Koch's postulates</a>.</p>
<p>Koch's name is one of twenty-three, from the fields of hygiene and tropical medicine, featured on the frieze of the <a href="/wiki/London_School_of_Hygiene_%26_Tropical_Medicine" title="London School of Hygiene &amp; Tropical Medicine">London School of Hygiene &amp; Tropical Medicine</a> building in Keppel Street, <a href="/wiki/Bloomsbury" title="Bloomsbury">Bloomsbury</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>A large marble statue of Koch stands in a small park known as Robert Koch Platz, just north of the <a href="/wiki/Charit%C3%A9" title="Charité">Charity Hospital</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Mitte" title="Mitte">Mitte</a> section of <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>.</p>

(March 20, 1884) <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=yY41AQAAMAAJ&amp;pg=PA191#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">"Sechster Bericht der Leiters der deutschen wissenschaftlichen Commission zur Erforschung der Cholera"</a> (Sixth report of the head of the German scientific commission for research on cholera), <i>Deutsche medizinische Wochenscrift</i> (German Medical Weekly), <b>10</b> (12) : 191-192. On page 191, he mentions the characteristic comma shape of <i>Vibrio cholerae</i>: <i>"Im letzten Berichte konnte ich bereits gehorsamst mittheilen, dass an den Bacillen des Choleradarms besondere Eigenschaften aufgefunden wurden, durch welche sie mit aller Sicherheit von anderen Bakterien zu unterscheiden sind. Von diesen Merkmalen sind folgende die am meisten charakteristischen: Die Bacillen sind nicht ganz geradlinig, wie die übrigen Bacillen, sondern ein wenig gekrümmt, einem Komma ähnlich."</i> (In the last report, I could already respectfully report that unusual characteristics were discovered in the bacteria of enteric cholera, by which they are to be distinguished with complete certainty from other bacteria. Of these features, the following are the most characteristic: the bacteria are not quite straight, like the rest of the bacilli, but a little bent, similar to a comma.)</span></li>
