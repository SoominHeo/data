<p><b>Robison Field</b> is the best-known of several names given to a former <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> <a href="/wiki/Stadium" title="Stadium">park</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a>. It was the home of the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Cardinals" title="St. Louis Cardinals">St. Louis Cardinals</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> from April 27, <a href="/wiki/1893_in_baseball" title="1893 in baseball">1893</a> until June 6, <a href="/wiki/1920_in_baseball" title="1920 in baseball">1920</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robison_Field&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Today's Cardinals of the National League began in 1882, as the St. Louis Browns of the then-major <a href="/wiki/American_Association_(19th_century)" title="American Association (19th century)">American Association</a>. They won four championships during the Association's ten-year existence of 1882 through 1891. During that decade, the team was playing their home games at <a href="/wiki/Sportsman%27s_Park" title="Sportsman's Park">Sportsman's Park</a>, at the corner of Grand and Dodier. In 1892, four of the Association clubs were absorbed into the National League, and the Association folded. Sportsman's Park remained the home of the Browns during their first NL season.</p>
<p>Although the Browns had been the most successful of the Association clubs, they fell on hard times for some years after the merger. For 1893, owner <a href="/wiki/Chris_von_der_Ahe" title="Chris von der Ahe">Chris von der Ahe</a> moved his team a few blocks to the northwest and opened a <b>"New" Sportsman's Park</b>, on the southeast corner of Natural Bridge and Vandeventer. The move to this particular site was part of a "deal", as the property had been owned by a trolley company, who then ran a trolley line out near the ballpark. The diamond was in the northwest corner of the block. Prairie Avenue was the east (left field) border. Right field, the shorter of the outfields, was bordered by Lexington Avenue.</p>
<p>The ballpark was generations ahead of its time in some ways. Along with the basic stands, Von der Ahe had built an adjoining <a href="/wiki/Amusement_park" title="Amusement park">amusement park</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Beer_garden" title="Beer garden">beer garden</a>, a race track in the outfield, a "shoot-the-shoots" water flume ride, and an artificial lake (used for <a href="/wiki/Ice_skating" title="Ice skating">ice skating</a> in winter). The side show notwithstanding, the club performed poorly on the field for most of the 1890s, consistently finishing at or near last place in the 12-team league as Von der Ahe sold off his best players in order to keep the club solvent.</p>
<p>On April 16, 1898, a fire ignited by a dropped and still-burning cigar consumed the grandstands and other structures. A heroic overnight effort was made to construct some temporary seating, and the game scheduled for the 17th was played. Von der Ahe was able to secure financing to rebuild the permanent stands, albeit in a much more modest design than before.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.thisgameofgames.com/home/the-sportsmans-park-fire-of-1898" rel="nofollow">[1]</a> But this was the last step in the decline of his once-proud franchise.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/1899_in_baseball" title="1899 in baseball">1899</a>, Von der Ahe sold the Browns to <a href="/wiki/Frank_Robison" title="Frank Robison">Frank</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Robison" title="Stanley Robison">Stanley Robison</a>. By then the ballpark was no longer the "New" Sportsman's Park. During the Robison's tenure, the venue was simply called <b>League Park</b>. It would bear that name through the <a href="/wiki/1910_in_baseball" title="1910 in baseball">1910</a> season.</p>
<p>The Robison brothers sought to change the image and reverse the fortunes of their team. They dropped the old "Browns" identity, abandoning that team color and switching to Cardinal red. This would yield a new and lasting nickname. However, for 1899 they are known to historians as the "Perfectos".</p>
<p>The Robisons had previously owned the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Spiders" title="Cleveland Spiders">Cleveland Spiders</a>, and in fact still owned them as the 1899 season began (a situation that would not be allowed today). They stripped Cleveland of its best players, including <a href="/wiki/Cy_Young" title="Cy Young">Cy Young</a>, and sent them to St. Louis. If this made the St. Louis club the "Perfectos", it also unfortunately made the Cleveland club the "Wanderers", as they became known when they were forced to play most of that season (their last) on the road. Despite loading up their roster, the previously cellar-dwelling St. Louis club was only able to improve to fifth place.</p>
<p>Cleveland and three other clubs were contracted after the 1899 season. The Cardinals began the <a href="/wiki/1900_in_baseball" title="1900 in baseball">1900</a> season with less competition, but sank back toward the cellar again. The only thing that kept the baseball situation in balance was that the new Browns of the American League generally fared equally poorly in the pennant races.</p>
<p>The wooden ballpark continued to be plagued by fire. On May 4, 1901, a fire began under the main part of the stands, and as with the 1898 fire, the structure was consumed within half an hour. During the rebuilding process, the Robison brothers arranged with the management of Athletic Park (as the "old" Sportsman's Park was being called by then) to play there until yet another wooden stand could be built. However, after playing on the 5th, they went on a month-long road trip, and returned to a newly-rebuilt League Park on June 3.</p>
<p>In its final form, the park was typical for its era: a wooden structure, with a covered grandstand behind home plate and extending to first and third base; open bleachers extending to the left field corner; bleachers in parts of the outfield; and a covered pavilion from first base to the right field corner.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Helene_Hathaway_Britton" title="Helene Hathaway Britton">Helene Britton</a>, daughter of Frank Robison, inherited the Cardinals team and the ballpark from her uncle Stanley upon his death in <a href="/wiki/1911_in_baseball" title="1911 in baseball">1911</a>. Helene renamed the ballpark <b>Robison Field</b> as a memorial to her father (who had died in 1908) and her uncle. She held the club for next few seasons, until selling her interest in the team in 1917 to a group that included <a href="/wiki/Sam_Breadon" title="Sam Breadon">Sam Breadon</a>.</p>
<p>With the Robison family no longer associated with the team, the park was often called simply <b>Cardinal Field</b> in its final years. Breadon attained majority ownership in 1920 and decided that the nearly three-decades-old wooden ballpark was no longer adequate.</p>
<p>In mid-season 1920 the Cardinals abandoned this ballpark and moved back to <a href="/wiki/Sportsman%27s_Park" title="Sportsman's Park">Sportsman's Park</a>, making their debut there on June 6. The park had been rebuilt, owned and occupied by the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> version of the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Browns</a> since <a href="/wiki/1902_in_baseball" title="1902 in baseball">1902</a>. The "Old" Sportsman's Park would outlive the "New" by some four decades.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robison_Field&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Robison Field was the last remaining major league ballpark that was primarily wooden. The park site continued to be owned by the Cardinals for a few more years. They finally sold the property to the developers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beaumont_High_School_(St._Louis,_Missouri)" title="Beaumont High School (St. Louis, Missouri)">Beaumont High School</a>, which was built on the site and was opened in <a href="/wiki/1926_in_baseball" title="1926 in baseball">1926</a>. After their constant on-field struggles at the old park, the club had begun to improve after their move, and with some fresh cash, they were ready to taste success. 1926 would also be the year of the Cardinals' first modern league and <a href="/wiki/1926_World_Series" title="1926 World Series">World Series</a> championship.</p>
