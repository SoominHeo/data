<p><b>Robyn Rowan Hitchcock</b> (born 3 March 1953) is an English <a href="/wiki/Singer-songwriter" title="Singer-songwriter">singer-songwriter</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trager1997_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trager1997-1">[1]</a></sup> and guitarist. While primarily a vocalist and guitarist, he also plays harmonica, piano, and bass guitar.</p>
<p>After reaching prominence in the late 1970s with <a href="/wiki/The_Soft_Boys" title="The Soft Boys">The Soft Boys</a>, Hitchcock launched a prolific solo career. His musical and lyrical styles have been influenced by the likes of <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Syd_Barrett" title="Syd Barrett">Syd Barrett</a>. Hitchcock's lyrics tend to include <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">surrealism</a>, comedic elements, characterisations of English eccentrics, and melancholy depictions of everyday life.</p>
<p>He was signed to two major American labels (<a href="/wiki/A%26M_Records" title="A&amp;M Records">A&amp;M Records</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a>) over the course of the 1980s and 1990s, and was the subject of a live performance/documentary film (<a href="/wiki/Storefront_Hitchcock" title="Storefront Hitchcock">Storefront Hitchcock</a>) by major motion picture director <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Demme" title="Jonathan Demme">Jonathan Demme</a> in 1998, but despite this, mainstream success has been limited. He has earned strong critical reviews over a steady stream of album releases and live performances, and a "cult following"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trager1997_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Trager1997-1">[1]</a></sup> for his songs.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Soft_Boys_to_the_Egyptians_.281972.E2.80.931993.29">The Soft Boys to the Egyptians (1972â1993)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robyn_Hitchcock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The Soft Boys to the Egyptians (1972â1993)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hitchcock was educated at <a href="/wiki/Winchester_College" title="Winchester College">Winchester College</a>, where he was a "groovy and alternative" friend of <a href="/wiki/Julia_Darling" title="Julia Darling">Julia Darling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> While at art school in London around 1972, Hitchcock was a member of the college band the Beetles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1974 he moved to <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, where he did some busking, and joined a series of local bands: B.B. Blackberry and the Swelterettes, the Worst Fears, and Maureen and the Meatpackers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oldies_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oldies-5">[5]</a></sup> His next group, Dennis and the Experts, became the <a href="/wiki/Neo-psychedelia" title="Neo-psychedelia">neo-psychedelia</a> band <a href="/wiki/The_Soft_Boys" title="The Soft Boys">The Soft Boys</a> in Cambridge in 1976, recording their first EP, "Give It to the Soft Boys", at Spaceward studios, Cambridge, in 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> After recording <i><a href="/wiki/A_Can_of_Bees" title="A Can of Bees">A Can of Bees</a></i> (1979) and <i><a href="/wiki/Underwater_Moonlight" title="Underwater Moonlight">Underwater Moonlight</a></i> (1980) the group broke up in 1981.</p>
<p>Hitchcock released his solo debut, <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Snake_Diamond_R%C3%B6le" title="Black Snake Diamond RÃ¶le">Black Snake Diamond RÃ¶le</a></i> in 1981, which included instrumental backing by several former Soft Boys. He followed it in 1982 with the generally critically maligned <i><a href="/wiki/Groovy_Decay" title="Groovy Decay">Groovy Decay</a></i>, a record which he would ultimately disown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Following his solo acoustic album <i><a href="/wiki/I_Often_Dream_of_Trains" title="I Often Dream of Trains">I Often Dream of Trains</a></i> in 1984, he formed a new band, <a href="/wiki/Robyn_Hitchcock_and_the_Egyptians" title="Robyn Hitchcock and the Egyptians">The Egyptians</a>, comprising former members of the Soft Boys (<a href="/wiki/Andy_Metcalfe" title="Andy Metcalfe">Andy Metcalfe</a> and Morris Windsor, supplemented at first by early keyboardist Roger Jackson), resulting in their 1985 debut <i><a href="/wiki/Fegmania!" title="Fegmania!">Fegmania!</a></i>, which featured typically surrealist Hitchcock songs such as "My Wife and My Dead Wife" and "The Man with the Lightbulb Head". (A live album, <i><a href="/wiki/Gotta_Let_This_Hen_Out!" title="Gotta Let This Hen Out!">Gotta Let This Hen Out!</a></i>, was released at the end of that year.) Their popularity grew with the 1986 album <i><a href="/wiki/Element_of_Light" title="Element of Light">Element of Light</a></i> and they were subsequently signed to <a href="/wiki/A%26M_Records" title="A&amp;M Records">A&amp;M Records</a> in the U.S. The album <i><a href="/wiki/Globe_of_Frogs" title="Globe of Frogs">Globe of Frogs</a></i>, released in 1988, further expanded their reach, as the single "Balloon Man" became a <a href="/wiki/Campus_radio" title="Campus radio">college radio</a> and MTV hit, followed in 1989 by "Madonna of the Wasps" from their <i><a href="/wiki/Queen_Elvis" title="Queen Elvis">Queen Elvis</a></i> album. In 1989 they also teamed up with <a href="/wiki/Peter_Buck" title="Peter Buck">Peter Buck</a> of <a href="/wiki/R.E.M." title="R.E.M.">R.E.M.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Holsapple" title="Peter Holsapple">Peter Holsapple</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_dB%27s" title="The dB's">The dB's</a>, playing two gigs as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nigel_and_the_Crosses&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nigel and the Crosses (page does not exist)">Nigel and the Crosses</a>, mostly covers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The Crosses also had their cover of "<a href="/wiki/Wild_Mountain_Thyme" title="Wild Mountain Thyme">Wild Mountain Thyme</a>" included on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byrds" title="Byrds">Byrds</a> tribute album, though Hitchcock always alluded to the <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Ferry" title="Bryan Ferry">Bryan Ferry</a> version when performing it live with the Egyptians.</p>
<p>At the beginning of 1990, Hitchcock took a break from the Egyptians and A&amp;M Records to release another solo acoustic album, <i><a href="/wiki/Eye_(album)" title="Eye (album)">Eye</a></i>, then resumed with the band's <i><a href="/wiki/Perspex_Island" title="Perspex Island">Perspex Island</a></i> release in 1991. 1993's <i><a href="/wiki/Respect_(Robyn_Hitchcock_album)" title="Respect (Robyn Hitchcock album)">Respect</a></i>, influenced a great deal by his father's death,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> marked the last Egyptians release and the end of his association with <a href="/wiki/A%26M_Records" title="A&amp;M Records">A&amp;M Records</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Soft_Boys_reunions_.281994.E2.80.932006.29">The Soft Boys reunions (1994â2006)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robyn_Hitchcock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Soft Boys reunions (1994â2006)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early in 1994, after disbanding the Egyptians, Hitchcock embarked on a short reunion tour with the Soft Boys. His work received a slight boost in 1995 when his back catalogue (including both solo releases and Egyptians albums) were re-packaged and re-issued in the United States by the respected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhino_Records" title="Rhino Records">Rhino Records</a> label. For the rest of the decade he continued recording and performing as a solo artist, releasing several albums on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warner_Brothers" title="Warner Brothers">Warner Brothers</a> Records, such as 1996's <i><a href="/wiki/Moss_Elixir" title="Moss Elixir">Moss Elixir</a></i> (which featured the contributions of violinist <a href="/wiki/Deni_Bonet" title="Deni Bonet">Deni Bonet</a> and guitarist <a href="/wiki/Tim_Keegan" title="Tim Keegan">Tim Keegan</a>), and the soundtrack from the <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Demme" title="Jonathan Demme">Jonathan Demme</a>-directed concert film <i><a href="/wiki/Storefront_Hitchcock" title="Storefront Hitchcock">Storefront Hitchcock</a></i> in 1998. The 1999 release <i><a href="/wiki/Jewels_for_Sophia" title="Jewels for Sophia">Jewels for Sophia</a></i>, also on Warner, featured cameos from Southern California-based musicians <a href="/wiki/Jon_Brion" title="Jon Brion">Jon Brion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grant-Lee_Phillips" title="Grant-Lee Phillips">Grant-Lee Phillips</a>, both of whom often shared the stage with Hitchcock when he played Los Angeles nightclub <a href="/wiki/Largo_(nightclub)" title="Largo (nightclub)">Largo</a>. An album of outtakes from the Sophia sessions called <i><a href="/wiki/A_Star_for_Bram" title="A Star for Bram">A Star for Bram</a></i>, released on Hitchcock's own label, followed, and his subsequent albums appeared on a variety of independent labels.</p>
<p>In 1999 he authorised a book about him written by Italian underground writer Luca Ferrari, edited in December 2000 in Italian-English with the title <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A_Middle-Class_Hero&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A Middle-Class Hero (page does not exist)">A Middle-Class Hero</a></i> (Stampa Alternativa, Rome 2000): a long 'in the raw' interview about life, death, religion, music and passions that portraits the musician as very human and real. Included also some of his paintings and for the first time his father Raymond's old ones.</p>
<p>In 2001 Hitchcock reunited and toured with <a href="/wiki/Kimberley_Rew" title="Kimberley Rew">Kimberley Rew</a>, bassist Matthew Seligman, and Morris Windsor for the Soft Boys' re-release of their best-known album, 1980's <i>Underwater Moonlight</i>. The following year they recorded and released a new album, <i>Nextdoorland</i>, which was accompanied by a short album of outtakes, <i>Side Three</i>. The reunion proved to be short-lived.</p>
<p>The 2002 double album <i>Robyn Sings</i> comprised <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">cover versions</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a> songs, including a live re-creation (performed in 1996) of Dylan's so-called <i>Live at the Royal Albert Hall</i> 1966 concert. Hitchcock celebrated his 50th birthday in 2003 with a concert at the Queen Elizabeth Hall in London at which his then-new solo acoustic album <i><a href="/wiki/Luxor_(album)" title="Luxor (album)">Luxor</a></i> was given away as a gift to all those attending, and an original poem of his was read by actor <a href="/wiki/Alan_Rickman" title="Alan Rickman">Alan Rickman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He continued collaborating with a series of different musicians, as on the album <i><a href="/wiki/Spooked_(album)" title="Spooked (album)">Spooked</a></i>, which was recorded with country/folk duo (and longtime Hitchcock fans) <a href="/wiki/Gillian_Welch" title="Gillian Welch">Gillian Welch</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Rawlings" title="David Rawlings">David Rawlings</a>. The Soft Boys re-formed again in 2006 to perform a live concert of Syd Barrett-era Pink Floyd covers in London, benefiting <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9decins_Sans_Fronti%C3%A8res" title="MÃ©decins Sans FrontiÃ¨res">MÃ©decins Sans FrontiÃ¨res</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Venus_3_to_present_.282002.E2.80.93.29">The Venus 3 to present (2002â)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robyn_Hitchcock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Venus 3 to present (2002â)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In 2006 <i><a href="/wiki/Ol%C3%A9!_Tarantula" title="OlÃ©! Tarantula">OlÃ©! Tarantula</a></i> was released with the Venus 3, a band which consisted of longtime friends and collaborators <a href="/wiki/R.E.M." title="R.E.M.">R.E.M.</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Peter_Buck" title="Peter Buck">Peter Buck</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Young_Fresh_Fellows" title="Young Fresh Fellows">Young Fresh Fellows</a>' frontman <a href="/wiki/Scott_McCaughey" title="Scott McCaughey">Scott McCaughey</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Ministry_(band)" title="Ministry (band)">Ministry</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Bill_Rieflin" title="Bill Rieflin">Bill Rieflin</a> (by then also R.E.M.'s full-time drummer). The song "'Cause It's Love (Saint Parallelogram)" was written with <a href="/wiki/Andy_Partridge" title="Andy Partridge">Andy Partridge</a> of <a href="/wiki/XTC" title="XTC">XTC</a>.</p>
<p>In 2007, he was the subject of a documentary <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Robyn_Hitchcock:_Sex,_Food,_Death..._and_Insects&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Robyn Hitchcock: Sex, Food, Death... and Insects (page does not exist)">Robyn Hitchcock: Sex, Food, Death... and Insects</a></i> directed by John Edginton,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> shown on the U.S. Sundance Channel and in the UK on BBC Four (and later released on DVD). "Food, sex and death are all corridors to life if you like. You need sex to get you here, you need food to keep you here and you need death to get you out and theyâre the entry and exit signs."</p>
<p>The filmmaker eavesdrops on Hitchcock at work on his latest collection of songs with contributors including <a href="/wiki/Nick_Lowe" title="Nick Lowe">Nick Lowe</a>, former <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">Led Zeppelin</a> bassist <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_Jones_(musician)" title="John Paul Jones (musician)">John Paul Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Buck" title="Peter Buck">Peter Buck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gillian_Welch" title="Gillian Welch">Gillian Welch</a>. The film culminates with Hitchcock and the band taking the songs on the road in America. A live EP with The Venus 3, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sex,_Food,_Death..._and_Tarantulas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sex, Food, Death... and Tarantulas (page does not exist)">Sex, Food, Death... and Tarantulas</a></i>, was released in conjunction with the documentary. The film also includes candid interviews with Hitchcock, who reveals much about the source of his work: "At heart I'm a frightened angry person. That's probably why my stuff isnât totally insubstantial. I'm constantly, deep down inside, in a kind of rage."</p>
<p>Late in 2007, Hitchcock's music was again re-packaged and re-released in the U.S., as <a href="/wiki/Yep_Roc_Records" title="Yep Roc Records">Yep Roc Records</a> began an extensive reissue campaign with three early solo releases and a double-CD compilation of rarities, which would be available separately or as part of a new boxed set release, <i><a href="/wiki/I_Wanna_Go_Backwards" title="I Wanna Go Backwards">I Wanna Go Backwards</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, that boxed set was followed up with <i><a href="/wiki/Luminous_Groove" title="Luminous Groove">Luminous Groove</a></i>, a boxed set of three early Egyptians releases and two further discs of rarities. In 2009, the electro-pop artist and remixer <a href="/wiki/Pocket_(musician)" title="Pocket (musician)">Pocket</a> released an EP featuring Hitchcock called "Surround Him With Love", while Hitchcock released an entirely separate new album, <i><a href="/wiki/Goodnight_Oslo" title="Goodnight Oslo">Goodnight Oslo</a></i>, with the Venus 3. At the end of the year, a live album called <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=I_Often_Dream_of_Trains_in_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="I Often Dream of Trains in New York (page does not exist)">I Often Dream of Trains in New York</a></i> documented the late-2008 onstage re-creation of his acclaimed 1984 acoustic album (a limited-edition deluxe version also included the materials to construct a kind of moving-image generator called a <a href="/wiki/Phenakistoscope" title="Phenakistoscope">phenakistoscope</a>).</p>
<p>In 2009, Hitchcock contributed to <a href="/wiki/The_Decemberists" title="The Decemberists">The Decemberists</a>' <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hazards_of_Love" title="The Hazards of Love">The Hazards of Love</a></i>, performing the short instrumental solo "An Interlude."</p>
<p>Concurrent with the redesign of his official website in early 2010, Hitchcock began to offer a series of "Phantom 45s" as downloads, each "45" being two newly recorded songs that would initially be offered as a free download. He also released the <i><a href="/wiki/Propellor_Time" title="Propellor Time">Propellor Time</a></i> album, containing new material partially based on the "Sex, Food, Death" sessions shown in the 2007 documentary, but mainly featuring the Venus 3. In 2011, he released <i><a href="/wiki/Troms%C3%B8,_Kaptein" title="TromsÃ¸, Kaptein">TromsÃ¸, Kaptein</a></i>, an album of songs written in Norway, and released physically only in that country. Hitchcock was chosen by <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Mangum" title="Jeff Mangum">Jeff Mangum</a> of <a href="/wiki/Neutral_Milk_Hotel" title="Neutral Milk Hotel">Neutral Milk Hotel</a> to perform "I Often Dream of Trains" at the <a href="/wiki/All_Tomorrow%27s_Parties_(music_festival)" title="All Tomorrow's Parties (music festival)">All Tomorrow's Parties</a> festival, to be curated by Mangum in March 2012 in Minehead, England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Love_From_London" title="Love From London">Love From London</a></i> (working title: File Under Pop) was released on <a href="/wiki/Yep_Roc_Records" title="Yep Roc Records">Yep Roc Records</a> on 5 March 2013. The label also released his subsequent record, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_Upstairs_(Robyn_Hitchcock_album)" title="The Man Upstairs (Robyn Hitchcock album)">The Man Upstairs</a></i>, on 26 August 2014.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robyn_Hitchcock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hitchcock was born in Paddington, London, England, son of novelist <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Hitchcock_(author)" title="Raymond Hitchcock (author)">Raymond Hitchcock</a> (writer of <i><a href="/wiki/Percy_(film)" title="Percy (film)">Percy</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> He was educated at <a href="/wiki/Winchester_College" title="Winchester College">Winchester College</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and Trinity College, Cambridge from where he was sent down, failing to graduate . He writes short stories, paints (often in a whimsical, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surrealist" title="Surrealist">surrealist</a> style) and draws in the cartoon-strip mode. Hitchcock's album covers often make use of his paintings or drawings, and the liner notes sometimes include a short story. His live concerts usually include story-telling, in the form of self-consciously imaginative and surreal ad-libbed monologues in his lyrical style. Hitchcock collaborated with director <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Demme" title="Jonathan Demme">Jonathan Demme</a> in 1998 for a live concert and film <i><a href="/wiki/Storefront_Hitchcock" title="Storefront Hitchcock">Storefront Hitchcock</a></i>, and later appeared in Demme's 2004 remake of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Manchurian_Candidate_(2004_movie)" title="The Manchurian Candidate (2004 movie)">The Manchurian Candidate</a></i>, in which he played double agent Laurent Tokar. He also appeared in Demme's <i><a href="/wiki/Rachel_Getting_Married" title="Rachel Getting Married">Rachel Getting Married</a></i> in 2008, singing and playing guitar in the wedding-party band. In September 2008 Hitchcock joined the Disko Bay Cape Farewell expedition to the West Coast of Greenland. <a href="/wiki/Cape_Farewell,_UK" title="Cape Farewell, UK">Cape Farewell</a> is a UK-based arts organisation that brings artists, scientists and communicators together to instigate a cultural response to climate change. Other voyagers on the trip included musicians <a href="/wiki/Jarvis_Cocker" title="Jarvis Cocker">Jarvis Cocker</a>, <a href="/wiki/KT_Tunstall" title="KT Tunstall">KT Tunstall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martha_Wainwright" title="Martha Wainwright">Martha Wainwright</a>.</p>
