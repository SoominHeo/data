<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Company_history">Company history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robinson_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Company history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planets:_TEOS"><i>Planets: TEOS</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robinson_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Planets: TEOS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The second video game released by Robinson is another text-based game, <i>Planets: The Exploration of Space</i>. Commonly referred to as <i>Planets: TEOS</i>, it was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_trading_and_combat_simulator" title="Space trading and combat simulator">space trading</a> game, which had players flying to various kinds of planets, buying, selling, and trading items in order to make money. The game even allowed fights that were very similar in style to <i>LORD</i>, allowing players to attack others when they were offline. Players could also attack planets in order to take control of them.</p>
<p>The game featured two sides, the "Alliance" and the "Maraken". Players could join and fight for either side. Players could even own planets for whichever side they belonged to. However, they could also play independently and even start their own "guilds" called cartels. The game had a notable <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek" title="Star Trek">Star Trek</a></i> influence, including references to the <a href="/wiki/Borg_(Star_Trek)" title="Borg (Star Trek)">Borg</a> and characters from the different <i>Star Trek</i> shows.</p>
<p>Seth Robinson sold the rights to <i>Planets: TEOS</i> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metropolis_Gameport" title="Metropolis Gameport">Metropolis Gameport</a> in 1998.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_World"><i>New World</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robinson_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: New World">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In various releases of <i>Planets: TEOS</i>, a game that Robinson had been working on was advertised. This game, <i>New World</i>, would never see release, due to events that were never made public. Instead, another of Robinson's games inherited the name, <i><a href="/wiki/Legend_of_the_Red_Dragon#Legend_of_the_Red_Dragon_II" title="Legend of the Red Dragon">Legend of the Red Dragon II: New World</a></i>. Fans wanted a new game from Robinson and wanted <i>New World</i> as well, so he released the official sequel to his original hit, <i>Legend of the Red Dragon</i>.</p>
<p>This game is completely different than the original <i>Red Dragon</i>, with many differences. Instead of a text based menu concept, the game was changed to an ANSI-based graphical map concept. Players controlled a smiley face-like character that could roam around, much like modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MMORPG" title="MMORPG">MMORPGs</a>. The game was never as successful as the original <i>Red Dragon</i>, but it did gather a cult following.</p>
<p>Seth Robinson sold the rights to <i>LORD II</i> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metropolis_Gameport" title="Metropolis Gameport">Metropolis Gameport</a> in 1998.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dink_Smallwood" title="Dink Smallwood">Dink Smallwood</a></div>
<p>In 1997, Robinson Technologies released an adventure/<a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">RPG</a> title by the name of <i>Dink Smallwood</i>. This was an effort made by Seth Robinson to move away from BBS door games and into something more profitable. The title featured an isometric view and had a comedic focus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KotakuDink_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-KotakuDink-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an attempt to keep the game's player-base active longer, Robinson took the idea of IGMs from <i>Legend of the Red Dragon</i> and created "D-Mods," an add-on feature where players could create their own adventure for others to explore. Anyone could create D-Mods and distribute them as they wished.</p>
<p>On 17 October, 1999, Robinson Technologies released the game to the public for free, and now it can be downloaded without charge from their website. On 16 December, 2011, a new version of <i>Dink Smallwood</i> called <i>Dink Smallwood HD</i> was released for <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a>, <a href="/wiki/IPad" title="IPad">iPad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows XP/Vista/7</a>, <a href="/wiki/WebOS" title="WebOS">webOS</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mac_OS_X" title="Mac OS X">Mac OS X</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dink_Smallwood_HD_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dink_Smallwood_HD-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KotakuDink_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-KotakuDink-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sascha_Pennartz"><i>Sascha Pennartz</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robinson_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sascha Pennartz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 3D action/simulation/interactive story game, where players mowed lawns to make money to support their family. It was an <a href="/wiki/Independent_Games_Festival" title="Independent Games Festival">Independent Games Festival</a> finalist at <a href="/wiki/Game_Developers_Conference" title="Game Developers Conference">GDC</a> 2003.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Niklas_Kohlen"><i>Niklas Kohlen</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robinson_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Niklas Kohlen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dungeon_Scroll" title="Dungeon Scroll">Dungeon Scroll</a></div>
<p>A spelling game with a <a href="/wiki/Dungeon_crawl" title="Dungeon crawl">dungeon</a> <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">RPG</a> theme. Players spelled words from letters they were given to deal damage to enemies they run into within the dungeon. It was an <a href="/wiki/Independent_Games_Festival" title="Independent Games Festival">Independent Games Festival</a> finalist at <a href="/wiki/Game_Developers_Conference" title="Game Developers Conference">GDC</a> 2004.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growtopia"><i>Growtopia</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robinson_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Growtopia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>An independent server-run <a href="/wiki/Open_world" title="Open world">sandbox</a> <a href="/wiki/Massively_multiplayer_online_game" title="Massively multiplayer online game">MMO</a> game, where players can chat, farm, add friends, game-in-the-game, trade, building worlds and pvp. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is currently one of the most successful games by Robinson Technologies and Hamumu Software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pocketgamer_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-pocketgamer-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Co-developed with Mike Hommel of Hamumu Software, and other developers developing and designing some of the items, the game has more than 15 million accounts (as of April 2016) and more than 300 million worlds (as of April 2016). The game also had a number of 64,000 online players at the same time (as of March 2016). The iOS version was released shortly after the Android version in December 2012. A PC Windows and Mac beta version was also released in July 2013.</p>
