<p>The <b>Roa–Hønefoss Line</b> (<a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>: <span lang="no" xml:lang="no"><i>Roa–Hønefossbanen</i></span>, formerly <span lang="no" xml:lang="no"><i>Roa–Hønefosslinjen</i></span>) is a 32-kilometer (20 mi) long, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_track_(rail)" title="Single track (rail)">single track</a> <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">railway line</a> between <a href="/wiki/Roa,_Norway" title="Roa, Norway">Roa</a> and <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B8nefoss" title="Hønefoss">Hønefoss</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>. At <a href="/wiki/Roa_Station" title="Roa Station">Roa Station</a>, the line connects to the <a href="/wiki/Gj%C3%B8vik_Line" title="Gjøvik Line">Gjøvik Line</a>, while at <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B8nefoss_Station" title="Hønefoss Station">Hønefoss Station</a>, it connects to the <a href="/wiki/Randsfjorden_Line" title="Randsfjorden Line">Randsfjorden Line</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bergen_Line" title="Bergen Line">Bergen Line</a>. The line runs through <a href="/wiki/Buskerud" title="Buskerud">Buskerud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oppland" title="Oppland">Oppland</a> and allowed the Bergen Line access to <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oslo_East_Station" title="Oslo East Station">Oslo East Station</a>.</p>
<p>The Line was built by the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_State_Railways_(1883%E2%80%931996)" title="Norwegian State Railways (1883–1996)">Norwegian State Railways</a> (NSB) and opened on 1 December 1909, along with the last part of the Bergen Line. The line was electrified in 1961. Until 1989, nearly all Oslo–<a href="/wiki/Bergen" title="Bergen">Bergen</a> trains used the line to terminate at Oslo East Station. It was also possible to reach Oslo from Hønefoss via the Randsfjord Line, albeit terminating at <a href="/wiki/Oslo_West_Station" title="Oslo West Station">Oslo West Station</a>. NSB also ran a local service between Hønefoss and Oslo along the Roa–Hønefoss Line. Oslo–Bergen trains ran via the Randsfjord Line after 1989 and local train services have been terminated. However, the line remains in use for freight trains along the Oslo–Bergen route.</p>

<p>The line starts at Roa Station, which is also located on the Gjøvik Line and is 57.54 kilometers (35.75 mi) from <a href="/wiki/Oslo_Central_Station" title="Oslo Central Station">Oslo Central Station</a> (Oslo S).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a70_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-a70-1">[1]</a></sup> Located at 313.2 meters (1,028 ft) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" title="Above mean sea level">above mean sea level</a>, Roa has the highest elevation on the whole line. The line runs first west, then south to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jevnaker_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jevnaker Station (page does not exist)">Jevnaker Station</a>, passing Kalvsjø Station, Grindvoll Station, Gunstad Station, Bjellum Tunnel and Kvellsrud Station, before reaching Jevnaker, which is 19 kilometers (12 mi) from Roa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b123_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-b123-2">[2]</a></sup> For a long time, Jevnaker had two separate stations located at each end of the town, with the other station serving as the terminus of the Randsfjord Line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a74_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-a74-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The line then runs south-west, past Bergerfoss and Kistefoss, before reaching Viul. There, there is a branch to a lumber mill. After Viul, the line runs through two tunnels, 95 and 185 meters (312 and 607 ft) long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b123_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-b123-2">[2]</a></sup> At Hval, located 27 kilometers (17 mi) from Roa, there was formerly a spur to a military camp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a74_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-a74-3">[3]</a></sup> The line then runs over a 55-meter (180 ft) long bridge over <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Randselva&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Randselva (page does not exist)">Randselva</a> before passing Hønen and a spur at Hønen and to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gomanbakeren_Holding_AS" title="Gomanbakeren Holding AS">Goman</a> bakery. Just before reaching Hønefoss Station, the line runs on a 216 meters (709 ft) long bridge over <a href="/wiki/Begna_(river)" title="Begna (river)">Begna</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b123_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-b123-2">[2]</a></sup> At Hønefoss, the Roa–Hønefoss Line and the Randsfjord Line come in from the east while the Randsfjord Line and the Bergen Line continue to the west. Hønefoss is 89.57 kilometers (55.66 mi) from Oslo via Roa and 124.21 kilometers (77.18 mi) via Drammen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a74_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-a74-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Hønefoss Station opened on 13 October 1868 as an intermediate station on the Randsfjord Line which connects <a href="/wiki/Randsfjorden" title="Randsfjorden">Randsfjorden</a> to <a href="/wiki/Drammen" title="Drammen">Drammen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a74_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-a74-3">[3]</a></sup> The initial proposals for the Bergen Line route went from Hønefoss via <a href="/wiki/Sandvika" title="Sandvika">Sandvika</a> to Oslo West Station (Oslo V). However, this was met with a combination of political opposition and technical restrictions. During the 1880s and 1890s, all lines that did not connect to Sweden were to be built with <span class="nowrap">1,067 mm</span> (<span class="nowrap">3 ft 6 in</span>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narrow_gauge" title="Narrow gauge">narrow gauge</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Drammen_Line" title="Drammen Line">Drammen Line</a>, which connected Sandvika to Oslo, was narrow gauge, as were the rest of the lines west of Oslo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bergh_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bergh-4">[4]</a></sup> During the planning of the Gjøvik Line (at the time known as the North Line), military considerations eventually led to the decision to build it with <a href="/wiki/Standard_gauge" title="Standard gauge">standard gauge</a>. Eventually it was decided that the Bergen Line would also be built with standard gauge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a70_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-a70-1">[1]</a></sup> However, to reach Oslo from Hønefoss, a longer route to connect to the Gjøvik Line would have to be built, allowing the line to connect to the more important Oslo East Station. The construction of the Roa–Hønefoss Line and the Bergen Line from Hønefoss to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Taugevatn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Taugevatn (page does not exist)">Taugevatn</a> was passed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Norway" title="Parliament of Norway">Parliament of Norway</a> on 2 June 1898.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aspenberg_1999:_19_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aspenberg_1999:_19-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The line was built by NSB as part of the Bergen Line project, for which construction across the mountain had commenced in 1895.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Construction of the Roa–Hønefoss Line started in 1903.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This section is relatively flat and was the easiest part of the project to build.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aspenberg_1999:_19_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aspenberg_1999:_19-5">[5]</a></sup> The line was officially opened along with the section of the Bergen Line from <a href="/wiki/Gulsvik" title="Gulsvik">Gulsvik</a> to Hønefoss, by <a href="/wiki/Haakon_VII_of_Norway" title="Haakon VII of Norway">King Haakon VII</a> on 27 November 1909. It was taken into ordinary use on 1 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> At the time the line opened, there were stations at Roa, Grindvoll, Jevnaker, Hval and Hønefoss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b123_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-b123-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>To begin with, the line had a single through train from Oslo to Bergen, with an additional summer express—with a limited number of stops. In addition, there were two additional trains from Hønefoss to Oslo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> From 1 May 1912, a <a href="/wiki/NSB_Night_Train" title="NSB Night Train">night train</a> was also introduced and the same year mixed passenger and freight trains were introduced from <a href="/wiki/%C3%85l" title="Ål">Ål</a> on the Bergen Line to Oslo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A station was opened at Viul on 16 August 1915, and another at Kistefoss on 1 October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b123_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-b123-2">[2]</a></sup> Because of economic impact of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the night train services was reduced to only twice a week from 20 May 1917 until 7 June 1919.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1930, new stations were established at Kalvsjø, Gunstad, Bergerfoss and Hønen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b123_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-b123-2">[2]</a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> from 1940 to 1945, the frequency was reduced to no more than one train per day. During parts of 1940, the Oslo–Bergen line was instead run via the Drammen and Randsfjord Lines. However, two local trains were kept on the route from Hønefoss to Roa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 18 November 1945, the daily Oslo–Bergen trains again ran via Roa, while the local trains from Oslo to Hønefoss increased to three daily round trips. Three weekly night trains were introduced from 1946. From 2 January to 1 May 1947, there was four weekly express services from Oslo to <a href="/wiki/Geilo" title="Geilo">Geilo</a> on the Bergen Line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> By 1950, the situation had normalized and there were daily day and night trains and three weekly express trains—the latter with no stops between Hønefoss and Oslo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Kvellsrud Station opened on 18 August 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b123_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-b123-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1930s, it was decided that the Bergen Line was to be electrified. After the first section, from Bergen to Voss, was completed in 1954, the Gjøvik Line and the Roa–Hønefoss Line were the next to be electrified. The sections Oslo–<a href="/wiki/Jaren_Station" title="Jaren Station">Jaren</a> and Roa–Hønefoss opened with <a href="/wiki/15_kV_AC_railway_electrification" title="15 kV AC railway electrification">15 kV <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap">16<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>2</sup>⁄<sub>3</sub></span> Hz AC</a> electric traction on 1 February 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Automatic signaling was put into service on 10 December 1973 and <a href="/wiki/Automatic_train_stop" title="Automatic train stop">automatic train stop</a> was put into operation on 27 October 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 3 June 1984, the day train was moved to run via Drammen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Until 1989, the Roa–Hønefoss Line had four daily round trips with a local train from Hønefoss via Roa to Oslo, normally a <a href="/wiki/NSB_Class_65" title="NSB Class 65">Class 65</a> or <a href="/wiki/NSB_Class_67" title="NSB Class 67">Class 67</a> unit. From 1989, this was reduced to a single round trip, with a morning train from Hønefoss to Oslo and a return in the afternoon. Eventually this was taken over by a <a href="/wiki/NSB_Class_69" title="NSB Class 69">Class 69</a> unit and by the late 1990s taken out of service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a70_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-a70-1">[1]</a></sup> The Roa–Hønefoss Line remains a pure freight track for long-haul freight trains on the Bergen Line. After the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Oslo_Tunnel" title="Oslo Tunnel">Oslo Tunnel</a> and Oslo Central Station in 1989, passenger trains run via Drammen; however, the Roa–Hønefoss Line is used as a reserve line for passenger trains when the Drammen Line or Randsfjord Line is out of service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
