<p><b>Royal Copenhagen</b>, officially the <b>Royal Porcelain Factory</b> (<a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>: <span lang="da" xml:lang="da"><i>Den Kongelige Porcelænsfabrik</i></span>), is a manufacturer of <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a> products and was founded in <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a> <a href="/wiki/1775_in_Denmark" title="1775 in Denmark">1 May 1775</a> under the protection of <a href="/wiki/Juliana_Maria_of_Brunswick-Wolfenb%C3%BCttel" title="Juliana Maria of Brunswick-Wolfenbüttel">Queen Juliane Marie</a>. It is recognized by its factory mark, the three wavy lines above each other, symbolizing Denmark’s three straits: <a href="/wiki/%C3%98resund" title="Øresund">Øresund</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Belt" title="Great Belt">Great Belt</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Little_Belt" title="Little Belt">Little Belt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Copenhagen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Den_kongelige_Porcel%C3%A6nsfabrik.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="886" height="298" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Den_kongelige_Porcel%C3%A6nsfabrik.jpg/220px-Den_kongelige_Porcel%C3%A6nsfabrik.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Den_kongelige_Porcel%C3%A6nsfabrik.jpg/330px-Den_kongelige_Porcel%C3%A6nsfabrik.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Den_kongelige_Porcel%C3%A6nsfabrik.jpg/440px-Den_kongelige_Porcel%C3%A6nsfabrik.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Royal Porcelain manufactory on <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B8bmagergade" title="Købmagergade">Købmagergade</a> in Copenhagen (19th century)</div>

<p>Starting in the 17th century, Europeans were fascinated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_and_white_porcelain" title="Blue and white porcelain">blue and white</a> and white porcelain exported from <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" title="Qing Dynasty">Qing</a> dynasties,.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Royal Copenhagen manufactory operations began in a converted post office in 1775. It was founded by a chemist, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Frantz_Heinrich_M%C3%BCller&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Frantz Heinrich Müller (page does not exist)">Frantz Heinrich Müller</a>, who was given a 50-year monopoly to create porcelain. The first pieces manufactured were dining services for the royal family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> When, in 1779, <a href="/wiki/Christian_VII_of_Denmark" title="Christian VII of Denmark">King Christian VII</a> assumed financial responsibility, the manufactory was styled the Royal Porcelain Factory. In 1790, Royal Copenhagen brought out its now famous <a href="/wiki/Flora_Danica" title="Flora Danica">Flora Danica</a> ‘Blue Fluted’ dinner service, with gilded edge and Danish flora motifs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and Royal Copenhagen held a monopoly on the "Blue Fluted" name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1851, Royal Copenhagen qualified for the <a href="/wiki/The_Great_Exhibition" title="The Great Exhibition">World Expo</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>. In 1868, as a result of royal companies' privatization, the Royal Porcelain Factory came into private hands. It was purchased by the <a href="/wiki/Faience" title="Faience">faience</a> factory <a href="/wiki/Aluminia" title="Aluminia">Aluminia</a> in 1882. Shortly after Aluminia's acquisition, Royal Copenhagen production was moved to a modern factory building at Aluminia’s site in <a href="/wiki/Frederiksberg" title="Frederiksberg">Frederiksberg</a>, on the outskirts of Copenhagen. By 1889, Royal Copenhagen qualified for the <a href="/wiki/Exposition_Universelle_(1889)" title="Exposition Universelle (1889)">World Expo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, winning the Grand Prix, giving it international exposure.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_company">Current company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Copenhagen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current company">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In recent years, Royal Copenhagen acquired <a href="/wiki/Georg_Jensen" title="Georg Jensen">Georg Jensen</a> in 1972, incorporated with <a href="/wiki/Holmegaard_Glass_Factory" title="Holmegaard Glass Factory">Holmegaard Glass Factory</a> in 1985, and finally <a href="/wiki/Bing_%26_Gr%C3%B8ndahl" title="Bing &amp; Grøndahl">Bing &amp; Grøndahl</a> in 1987. Royal Copenhagen was a part of a group of <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> companies, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Scandinavia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Royal Scandinavia (page does not exist)">Royal Scandinavia</a></i>, together with Georg Jensen, and was owned by the Danish private equity fund, <a href="/wiki/Axcel" title="Axcel">Axcel</a>. Following Axcel's acquisition of Royal Scandinavia, <a href="/wiki/Holmegaard" title="Holmegaard">Holmegaard</a> Glasværk was sold in a <a href="/wiki/Management_buyout" title="Management buyout">MBO</a> and a controlling interest in the Swedish glass works <a href="/wiki/Orrefors" title="Orrefors">Orrefors</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosta_Boda" title="Kosta Boda">Kosta Boda</a> was sold to New Wave Group.</p>
<p>In December 2012 Axcel sold Royal Copenhagen to the Finnish listed company <a href="/wiki/Fiskars" title="Fiskars">Fiskars</a>, which was founded in 1649.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company now produces its products in Thailand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collectables">Collectables</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Copenhagen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Collectables">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Royal_Copenhagen_2010_plaquettes" title="Royal Copenhagen 2010 plaquettes">Royal Copenhagen 2010 plaquettes</a>: Numbered and named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_and_white_porcelain" title="Blue and white porcelain">cobalt blue decorated</a> plates depicted a variety of scenes, holidays, years, and occasions. Most commonly round, they measured about 8 cm (3-1/4") in diameter, with a blue scene on white background.</li>
<li>Larger, approximately 18.5 cm, blue on white <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_and_white_porcelain" title="Blue and white porcelain">cobalt blue decorated</a> plates, created annually for Mother's Day, the Olympics and other commemorations. Some weren't annual issues, instead depicted scenes. In 1895, Royal Copenhagen began producing annual Christmas plates, and continue to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>Porcelein pipes, beginning in 1969 and manufactured for about 15 years.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Christmas_plates">Christmas plates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Copenhagen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Christmas plates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The tradition of Christmas plates started hundreds of years ago in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, when wealthy people presented their servants with cakes and sweets, served on decorative plates of wood or metal at <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> time. The servants referred to these gifts as their Christmas Plate. In 1895 <a href="/wiki/Bing_%26_Gr%C3%B8ndahl" title="Bing &amp; Grøndahl">Bing &amp; Grøndahl</a> produced the first Christmas plate made from porcelain, with the date inscribed, and has made one each year since. In 1908 the Royal Copenhagen factory followed suit. Each year these plates are made in limited quantities and have been collectable for over 100 years. Each plate is made in the year of issue only, after which the mould is destroyed, and the design is never made again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
