<p><b>RMIT University</b> (officially the <b>Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology</b>, informally <b>RMIT</b>) is an Australian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_research_university" title="Public research university">public research university</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>.</p>
<p>Founded in 1887 as the <a href="/wiki/Working_Men%27s_College_of_Melbourne" title="Working Men's College of Melbourne">Working Men's College of Melbourne</a> by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Ormond" title="Francis Ormond">Francis Ormond</a>, it initially opened as a private night school offering instruction in art, science and technology in response to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> in Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup> It was made a <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public university</a> by act of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Victoria" title="Parliament of Victoria">Parliament of Victoria</a> in 1992 after merging with the <a href="/wiki/Phillip_Institute_of_Technology" title="Phillip Institute of Technology">Phillip Institute of Technology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RMITact1992_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-RMITact1992-5">[5]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Dual_sector_education" title="Dual sector education">dual sector institute</a>, its enrolment of 320 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_(occupation)" title="Trade (occupation)">trades</a> students in 1887 has since grown to around 83,000 <a href="/wiki/Vocational_education" title="Vocational education">vocational</a> and <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">higher education</a> students in 2015;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-annualpp13-14_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-annualpp13-14-1">[1]</a></sup> which makes it the largest <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_education_in_Australia" title="Tertiary education in Australia">tertiary education provider</a> in Australia. With an annual revenue of <a href="/wiki/Australian_dollar" title="Australian dollar">A$</a>1.163 billion in 2015,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-annualpp13-14_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-annualpp13-14-1">[1]</a></sup> it is also one of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Australian_universities_by_annual_revenue" title="List of Australian universities by annual revenue">wealthiest universities in Australia</a>.</p>
<p>The foundation campus of RMIT forms part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melbourne_city_centre" title="Melbourne city centre">Melbourne city centre</a> and is located on the northern edge of its <a href="/wiki/Hoddle_Grid" title="Hoddle Grid">Hoddle Grid</a>. It began as the Working Men's College building, on the corner of Bowen Street and La Trobe Street, and has since grown to 87 buildings that are contiguous with the surrounding city. In addition to its <a href="/wiki/City_campus_of_the_Royal_Melbourne_Institute_of_Technology" title="City campus of the Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology">City campus</a>, it has two radial campuses in the Melbourne metropolitan area which are located in the northern suburbs of <a href="/wiki/Brunswick,_Victoria" title="Brunswick, Victoria">Brunswick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bundoora,_Victoria" title="Bundoora, Victoria">Bundoora</a>. It also maintains a training site in the western suburb of <a href="/wiki/Point_Cook,_Victoria" title="Point Cook, Victoria">Point Cook</a> located on the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Air_Force" title="Royal Australian Air Force">Royal Australian Air Force</a> base <a href="/wiki/RAAF_Williams" title="RAAF Williams">Williams</a>, and a research site in the <a href="/wiki/Grampians_National_Park" title="Grampians National Park">Southern Grampians region</a> located in the rural city of <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_Victoria" title="Hamilton, Victoria">Hamilton</a>. Outside of Australia, it has a presence in Asia and Europe. In Asia, it has two <a href="/wiki/International_branch_campus" title="International branch campus">branch campuses</a> in Vietnam which are located in <a href="/wiki/Hanoi" title="Hanoi">Hanoi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh_City" title="Ho Chi Minh City">Ho Chi Minh City</a>, and maintains teaching partnerships in China, Hong Kong, Indonesia, Singapore, and Sri Lanka. In Europe, it has a coordinating centre in Spain located in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>.</p>
<p>RMIT is rated a five star university by <a href="/wiki/Quacquarelli_Symonds" title="Quacquarelli Symonds">Quacquarelli Symonds</a> (QS). It is ranked as 16th in the world for art and <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a> in the 2016 <a href="/wiki/QS_World_University_Rankings" title="QS World University Rankings">QS World University Rankings</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QS_Rankings.2C_Art_and_Design.2C_2016_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-QS_Rankings.2C_Art_and_Design.2C_2016-6">[6]</a></sup> which makes it the top art and design school in Australia and the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>. It is also ranked as 16th in the top 50 universities under 50 years old by QS,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QSTop50_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-QSTop50-7">[7]</a></sup> and as 98th in the top 150 universities under 50 years old by <a href="/wiki/Times_Higher_Education" title="Times Higher Education">Times Higher Education</a> in 2016 (due to it gaining the status of university in 1992).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-THES150RMIT_University_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-THES150RMIT_University-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Royal_Melbourne_Institute_of_Technology" title="History of the Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology">History of the Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ConstructionWorkingMensCollegeMelbourne.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2598" data-file-width="3064" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/ConstructionWorkingMensCollegeMelbourne.jpg/220px-ConstructionWorkingMensCollegeMelbourne.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/ConstructionWorkingMensCollegeMelbourne.jpg/330px-ConstructionWorkingMensCollegeMelbourne.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/ConstructionWorkingMensCollegeMelbourne.jpg/440px-ConstructionWorkingMensCollegeMelbourne.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ConstructionWorkingMensCollegeMelbourne.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Construction of the Working Men's College (1880s)</div>
</div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history_.28before_1887.29">Early history (before 1887)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history (before 1887)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The antecedent of RMIT, the Working Men's College of Melbourne, was founded by the Scottish-born <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grazier" title="Grazier">grazier</a> and politician <a href="/wiki/The_Honourable" title="The Honourable">The Hon.</a> Francis Ormond in the 1880s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup> Planning began in 1881, with Ormond basing his model for the college on the <a href="/wiki/Birkbeck,_University_of_London" title="Birkbeck, University of London">Birkbeck Literary and Scientific Institution</a> (now a constituent college of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a>), Brighton College of Art (now the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Brighton" title="University of Brighton">University of Brighton</a>), <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Art" title="Royal College of Art">Royal College of Art</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Working_Men%27s_College" title="Working Men's College">Working Men's College of London</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ormond donated the sum of £5000 toward the foundation of the college.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup> He was supported in the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Victoria" title="Parliament of Victoria">Victorian Parliament</a> by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Henry_Pearson" title="Charles Henry Pearson">Charles Pearson</a> and in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melbourne_Trades_Hall" title="Melbourne Trades Hall">Melbourne Trades Hall</a> by William Murphy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">workers' unions</a> of Melbourne rallied their members to match Ormond's donation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup> The site for the college, on the corners of Bowen Street and La Trobe Street, opposite the <a href="/wiki/State_Library_of_Victoria" title="State Library of Victoria">Melbourne Public Library</a>, was donated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_Victoria_(Australia)" title="Government of Victoria (Australia)">Victorian Government</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Working_Men.27s_College_.281887.E2.80.931960.29">Working Men's College (1887–1960)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Working Men's College (1887–1960)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WorkingMensCollegeMelbourneCrest.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="288" data-file-width="255" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/43/WorkingMensCollegeMelbourneCrest.png/220px-WorkingMensCollegeMelbourneCrest.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/4/43/WorkingMensCollegeMelbourneCrest.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WorkingMensCollegeMelbourneCrest.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Early crest (1900s)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Working Men's College of Melbourne opened on June 4, 1887, with a gala ceremony at the <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_Town_Hall" title="Melbourne Town Hall">Melbourne Town Hall</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup> becoming the fifth tertiary education provider in Victoria (the <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_Athenaeum" title="Melbourne Athenaeum">Melbourne Athenaeum</a> was founded in 1839, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Melbourne" title="University of Melbourne">University of Melbourne</a> in 1853, the <a href="/wiki/Federation_University_Australia#School_of_Mines" title="Federation University Australia">Ballarat School of Mines</a> in 1870 and the <a href="/wiki/Bendigo_School_of_Mines_and_Industries" title="Bendigo School of Mines and Industries">Bendigo School of Mines</a> in 1873). It took 320 enrollments on its opening night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>It opened as a night school for instruction in "art, science and <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a>"—in the words of its founder—"especially to working men".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:Ross_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:Ross-9">[9]</a></sup> Ormond was a firm believer in the transformative power of education and believed the college would be of "great importance and value" to the <a href="/wiki/Industrialisation" title="Industrialisation">industrialisation</a> of Melbourne during the late-19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:Ross_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:Ross-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1904, it was incorporated under the <i>Companies Act</i> as a private college.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between the turn of the 20th century and the 1930s, it expanded over the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Old_Melbourne_Gaol" title="Old Melbourne Gaol">Old Melbourne Gaol</a> and constructed buildings for new art, engineering and radio schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup> It also made its first contribution to Australia's war effort through training of returned <a href="/wiki/Military_personnel" title="Military personnel">military personnel</a> from World War I.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup> Following a petition by students, it officially changed its name to the Melbourne Technical College in 1934.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-13"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The expanded college made a greater contribution to Australia's effort during World War II by training a sixth of the country's military personnel—including the majority of its Royal Australian Air Force communication officers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-14"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup> It also trained 2000 civilians in <a href="/wiki/Ammunition" title="Ammunition">munitions</a> manufacturing and was commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Australia" title="Government of Australia">Australian Government</a> to manufacture military aircraft parts—including the majority of parts for the <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Beaufort" title="Bristol Beaufort">Beaufort Bomber</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-15"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation_of_RMIT_.281960.E2.80.932000.29">Creation of RMIT (1960–2000)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Creation of RMIT (1960–2000)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EmilyMcPhersonCollegeFacade.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="257" data-file-width="381" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/EmilyMcPhersonCollegeFacade.jpg/220px-EmilyMcPhersonCollegeFacade.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/EmilyMcPhersonCollegeFacade.jpg/330px-EmilyMcPhersonCollegeFacade.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/87/EmilyMcPhersonCollegeFacade.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EmilyMcPhersonCollegeFacade.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Emily McPherson College (1930s)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Following World War II, in 1954 it became the first Australian tertiary education provider to be awarded royal patronage (by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-16"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup> for its service to the Commonwealth in the area of education and for its contribution to the war effort; and was officially renamed the "Royal Melbourne Technical College".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-17"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup> It became (and remains to this day) the only higher education institution in Australia with the right of the prefix "Royal" along with the use of the monarchy of England's regalia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-18"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Its name was officially changed to the Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology in 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-19"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup> During the mid-20th century, it was restructured as a provider of <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">general higher</a> and vocational education, and pioneered <a href="/wiki/Dual_sector_education" title="Dual sector education">dual sector education</a> in Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-20"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup> It also began an engagement with <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> during this time (under the Australian Government's <a href="/wiki/Colombo_Plan" title="Colombo Plan">Colombo Plan</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-21"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1979, the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Emily_McPherson_College_of_Domestic_Economy" title="Emily McPherson College of Domestic Economy">Emily McPherson College of Domestic Economy</a> joined with RMIT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-22"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>After merging with the Phillip Institute of Technology in 1992,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_Line_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Time_Line-10">[10]</a></sup> it became a public university by act of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_Victoria_(Australia)" title="Government of Victoria (Australia)">Victorian Government</a> under the <i>Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology Act 1992</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RMITact1992_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-RMITact1992-5">[5]</a></sup> During the 1990s, the university underwent a rapid expansion and amalgamated with a number of nearby colleges and institutes. The Melbourne College of Decoration and Design joined RMIT in 1993, to create a new dedicated vocational design school,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_Line_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Time_Line-10">[10]</a></sup> followed by the Melbourne College of Printing and Graphic Arts in 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_Line_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Time_Line-10">[10]</a></sup> That same year, it opened its first radial campus in <a href="/wiki/Bundoora,_Victoria" title="Bundoora, Victoria">Bundoora</a> in the northern Melbourne metropolitan area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_Line_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Time_Line-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1999, it acquired the Melbourne Institute of Textiles campus in Brunswick in the inner-northern Melbourne metropolitan area for its vocational design schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_Line_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Time_Line-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history_.282000-present.29">Recent history (2000-present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recent history (2000-present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the turn of the 21st century, it was invited by the <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Vietnam" title="Politics of Vietnam">Vietnamese Government</a> to become the country's first foreign-owned university.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vietnamhistory_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vietnamhistory-11">[11]</a></sup> Its first <a href="/wiki/International_branch_campus" title="International branch campus">international branch campus</a> opened in <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh_City" title="Ho Chi Minh City">Ho Chi Minh City</a> in 2001 with a second in <a href="/wiki/Hanoi" title="Hanoi">Hanoi</a> in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vietnamhistory_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vietnamhistory-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2013, it established a presence in Europe by opening a centre in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RMITEurope_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-RMITEurope-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campuses">Campuses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Campuses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Located in the Melbourne city centre, the historic City campus of RMIT is the foundation campus of the university as well as its largest and most recognisable. It is known for its striking <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_architecture" title="Contemporary architecture">contemporary architecture</a> as well as its well-preserved <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interwar_period" title="Interwar period">interwar period</a> buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edquist_2008_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Edquist_2008-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historicbuildings_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-historicbuildings-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Founded in 1887, the City campus began as the Working Men's College of Melbourne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-23"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup> Its original building is situated on the corner of Bowen Street and La Trobe Street,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edquist_2008_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Edquist_2008-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historicbuildings_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-historicbuildings-14">[14]</a></sup> and the campus has since grown to 87 buildings in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RMICitymap_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-RMICitymap-15">[15]</a></sup> The campus has no perimeter walls. As such, its buildings are contiguous with the surrounding city. Most of its buildings are spread across six city blocks covering approximately 720,000 square metres (7,800,000 sq ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RMICitymap_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-RMICitymap-15">[15]</a></sup> It is roughly bound by La Trobe Street to the south, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Street,_Melbourne" title="Elizabeth Street, Melbourne">Elizabeth Street</a> to the south-east and Swanston Street to the north-east (connected by Franklin Street), Queensberry Street to the north, <a href="/wiki/Lygon_Street,_Melbourne" title="Lygon Street, Melbourne">Lygon Street</a> to the north-west and <a href="/wiki/Russell_Street,_Melbourne" title="Russell Street, Melbourne">Russell Street</a> to the south-west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RMICitymap_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-RMICitymap-15">[15]</a></sup> The campus area is situated between the two oldest sections of the city; the northern edge of the Hoddle Grid to its south and the <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria_Market" title="Queen Victoria Market">Queen Victoria Market</a> to its south-west. The area is sometimes referred to as the "RMIT quarter" of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AgeSAB_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-AgeSAB-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GreenDesignHub_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-GreenDesignHub-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the intersection of La Trobe Street and Swanston Street, the campus also benefits from its proximity to the <a href="/wiki/State_Library_of_Victoria" title="State Library of Victoria">State Library of Victoria</a> as well as the adjacent <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_Central_Shopping_Centre" title="Melbourne Central Shopping Centre">Melbourne Central Shopping Centre</a> and its <a href="/wiki/City_Loop" title="City Loop">City Loop</a> <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_Central_railway_station" title="Melbourne Central railway station">underground railway station</a>. It is also well-serviced by the <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Melbourne" title="Trams in Melbourne">city tram network</a> along La Trobe Street and Swanston Street and has its own tram stop (Stop 7 RMIT University/Swanston Street) in the densest section of the campus.</p>
<p>The city block bound by Bowen Street, Franklin Street, La Trobe Street, and Russell Street, served as the justice precinct of the city for over 100 years. While it is mostly occupied by campus buildings today, which were constructed over the site of the demolished Old Melbourne Gaol, some original buildings from the precinct remain and are used by the university. From the Old Melbourne Gaol, they include its east wing cell block (1854) which is now operated as a museum by the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_of_Australia" title="National Trust of Australia">National Trust of Australia</a>, its <a href="/wiki/RMIT_Spiritual_Centre" title="RMIT Spiritual Centre">former chapel and gatehouse</a> (1860) which are now used as a multi-faith place of worship for the campus, and the site of its former hospital which is now used as a landscaped space known as Alumni Courtyard. Other buildings from the precinct that remain are the former Melbourne City Watchhouse (1904) which is also operated as a museum by the National Trust, and the <a href="/wiki/Former_Melbourne_Magistrates%27_Court" title="Former Melbourne Magistrates' Court">former Melbourne Magistrates' Court</a> (1914) which is now used to house university administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edquist_2008_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Edquist_2008-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historicbuildings_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-historicbuildings-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other notable buildings on the City campus include <a href="/wiki/Storey_Hall" title="Storey Hall">Storey Hall</a> original section (1887), Forresters' Hall (1888), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capitol_Theatre_(Melbourne)" title="Capitol Theatre (Melbourne)">Capitol Theatre</a> (1924), <a href="/wiki/Emily_McPherson_College_of_Domestic_Economy" title="Emily McPherson College of Domestic Economy">Emily McPherson College</a> (1927), <a href="/wiki/RMIT_Building_8" title="RMIT Building 8">Building 8</a> (1993), Storey Hall annex (1995), Singer Building "green brain" (2010), <a href="/wiki/RMIT_Design_Hub" title="RMIT Design Hub">Design Hub</a> (2011), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RMIT_Swanston_Academic_Building_(SAB)" title="RMIT Swanston Academic Building (SAB)">Swanston Academic Building</a> (2012).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bundoora">Bundoora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Bundoora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Bundoora campus was established in 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_Line_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Time_Line-10">[10]</a></sup> It is located 18 km from the City campus in the outer northern suburb of <a href="/wiki/Bundoora,_Victoria" title="Bundoora, Victoria">Bundoora</a>. The campus is divided into 'East' and 'West' by Plenty Road. In a contrast to the urban City campus, the Bundoora West campus is set amongst almost 400,000 square metres (4,300,000 sq ft) of parkland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bundooracampus_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bundooracampus-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Programs in <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineering" title="Aerospace engineering">aerospace engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineering" title="Mechanical engineering">mechanical engineering</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_science" title="Medical science">medical sciences</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social sciences</a> are offered at the Bundoora campus.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Brunswick">Brunswick</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Brunswick">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Brunswick campus became a part of RMIT in 1999 as a dedicated site for its vocational design schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_Line_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-Time_Line-10">[10]</a></sup> It is located 6 km from the City campus in the inner northern suburb of Brunswick. Prior to its annexation by RMIT, it was the campus of the former Melbourne Institute of Textiles for nearly 50 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Programs in fashion design, graphic design, printing, publishing and textiles are offered at the Brunswick campus.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sites">Other sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>RMIT's flight training programs are conducted from its site at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Air_Force" title="Royal Australian Air Force">Royal Australian Air Force</a>'s historic Williams base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It is located 20 km from the City campus in the outer south-western suburb of <a href="/wiki/Point_Cook,_Victoria" title="Point Cook, Victoria">Point Cook</a>. RAAF Williams is the world's oldest operating air force base and the birthplace of the Royal Australian Air Force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The university also has a regional research site in the rural town of <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_Victoria" title="Hamilton, Victoria">Hamilton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> It is located 300 km north-west of the City campus in regional Victoria—just south of the <a href="/wiki/Grampians_National_Park" title="Grampians National Park">Grampians National Park</a>. The Potter Rural Community Research Centre at the site focuses on rural and regional issues in a global context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ho_Chi_Minh_City">Ho Chi Minh City</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ho Chi Minh City">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1998, RMIT was invited by the <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Vietnam" title="Politics of Vietnam">Vietnamese Government</a> to establish the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RMIT_International_University" title="RMIT International University">country's first foreign-owned university</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vietnamhistory_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Vietnamhistory-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2001, it purchased and restored a 19th-century <a href="/wiki/French_Colonial" title="French Colonial">French Colonial</a> building and grounds in <a href="/wiki/District_3,_Ho_Chi_Minh_City" title="District 3, Ho Chi Minh City">District 3</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh_City" title="Ho Chi Minh City">Ho Chi Minh City</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vietnamhistory_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Vietnamhistory-11">[11]</a></sup> The building, located on Pham Ngoc Thach Street, is informally referred to as "the Castle" by students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phamngocthach_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-phamngocthach-24">[24]</a></sup> Today, the Pham Ngoc Thach site remains a radial site of the present Ho Chi Minh City campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phamngocthach_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-phamngocthach-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The present Ho Chi Minh City campus is located in the Phu My Hung area of the Saigon South development in <a href="/wiki/District_7,_Ho_Chi_Minh_City" title="District 7, Ho Chi Minh City">District 7</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The first academic buildings on the large purpose-built campus opened in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vietnamhistory_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Vietnamhistory-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2011, its recreation complex and residential centres opened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vietnamhistory_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-Vietnamhistory-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hanoi">Hanoi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Hanoi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Hanoi campus was established in 2004. It was initially located in the Van Phuc Diplomatic Compound in the government precinct of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ba_Dinh_District" title="Ba Dinh District">Ba Dinh District</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hanoi" title="Hanoi">Hanoi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vietnamhistory_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-Vietnamhistory-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2007, it also acquired a building in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dong_Da_District" title="Dong Da District">Dong Da District</a> to accommodate rising student numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vietnamhistory_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-Vietnamhistory-11">[11]</a></sup> It consolidated its two buildings in a newly built tower overlooking Ngoc Khanh Lake in the Ba Dinh District in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Barcelona">Barcelona</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Barcelona">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisation_and_governance">Organisation and governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Organisation and governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology is a public university created under the <i>Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology Act 1992</i> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_Victoria_(Australia)" title="Government of Victoria (Australia)">Government of Victoria</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RMITact1992_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-RMITact1992-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-act2010sc4-1_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-act2010sc4-1-29">[29]</a></sup> and continues in accordance with the <i>Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology Act 2010</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-act2010sc1_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-act2010sc1-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The university trades under the name "RMIT University" which is a registered business name and trademark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> It is composed of the academic colleges and schools, research centres and institutes of the Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orgchart_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-orgchart-33">[33]</a></sup> and is governed by the RMIT Council and is managed by the RMIT Chancellery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-act2010sc8-2_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-act2010sc8-2-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-councilcharter_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-councilcharter-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>"RMIT Group" is the business unit of the university and consists of the entities controlled by the Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orgchart_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-orgchart-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-councilcharter_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-councilcharter-35">[35]</a></sup> including wholly owned subsidiaries such as: the RMIT Foundation and RMIT Training (and its sub-entities RMIT English Worldwide and RMIT Publishing); its international holdings companies: RMIT Spain (trading as RMIT Europe) and <a href="/wiki/RMIT_University_Vietnam" title="RMIT University Vietnam">RMIT Vietnam</a>; as well other commercial interests and sub-entities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-annualp30_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-annualp30-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>RMIT University is separated into two divisions: the Higher Education Division and the Vocational Education and Training (VET) Division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The divisions are responsible for the 17 academic schools of RMIT—which are grouped into three academic portfolios referred to as colleges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The higher education schools offer undergraduate and postgraduate degrees, while the VET schools offer vocational certificates and diplomas.</p>

<p>RMIT is governed by a council consisting of 21 members,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-act2010sc8-2_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-act2010sc8-2-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> which is responsible for the "general direction and superintendence of the University".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-act2010sc8-2_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-act2010sc8-2-34">[34]</a></sup> The RMIT Council is led by the RMIT <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_(education)" title="Chancellor (education)">Chancellor</a> who is an <a href="/wiki/Ex_officio_member" title="Ex officio member">ex officio member</a> and serves as its Governor-in-Council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The RMIT <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice-Chancellor" title="Vice-Chancellor">Vice-Chancellor and President</a>, as well as the Chair of the RMIT Academic Board, are also ex officio members of the council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Five members of the RMIT Council are elected by direct ballot of the staff and students of the university.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> They consist of three staff members elected to represent the higher education, vocational education and general staff of the university,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> and two students elected to represent higher education and vocational education students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The remaining members are appointed directly by the RMIT Chancellor and Governor, or by a vote of the sitting council members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Members appointed directly to the council are required to possess a substantial expertise in academic or financial management, vocational education or training experience, and be drawn from beyond the university community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>The RMIT Chancellor and Governor of the RMIT Council, since January 1, 2011, is <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunications</a> businessman and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">nuclear physicist</a> <a href="/wiki/Ziggy_Switkowski" title="Ziggy Switkowski">Ziggy Switkowski</a> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Australia" title="Order of Australia">AO</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vice-Chancellor">Vice-Chancellor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Vice-Chancellor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The RMIT Council grants power over all academic and administrative affairs of the university to the RMIT Vice-Chancellor and President—who is the <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a> of the university.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The Vice-Chancellor and President is "responsible for the conduct of the University's affairs in all matters".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Management of RMIT's colleges and portfolios is then delegated by the Vice-Chancellor and President to a team of Deputy and Pro Vice-Chancellors as well as senior executives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>The RMIT Vice-Chancellor and President, since 1 February 2015, is <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">information technology</a> businessman and former Vice-Chancellor of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Open_University" title="Open University">Open University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_G._Bean" title="Martin G. Bean">Martin G. Bean</a> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">CBE</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_Board">Academic Board</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Academic Board">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The requirements for the conferring of an academic degree of the university is determined and approved by the RMIT Academic Board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> The board consists of the RMIT Chancellery as ex officio members,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> and up to a further 46 members—34 of which must be elected by staff and students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Those conferred an academic degree of the university may use the <a href="/wiki/Post-nominal_letters" title="Post-nominal letters">post-nominal letters</a> "RMIT" with the abbreviation of their degree title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colleges_and_schools">Colleges and schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Colleges and schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The three academic colleges housing the schools of RMIT are the College of Business (BUS), College of Design and Social Context (DSC) and College of Science, Engineering and Health (SEH). Additionally, the university's Portfolio of Research and Innovation (R&amp;I) also contains a specialist research school in order to foster excellence in research methodology and <a href="/wiki/Pedagogy" title="Pedagogy">pedagogy</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academics">Academics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Academics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_research_centres_and_institutes_of_the_Royal_Melbourne_Institute_of_Technology" title="List of research centres and institutes of the Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology">List of research centres and institutes of the Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology</a></div>
<p>RMIT focuses on applied research as well as outcome-related research and consultancy services, and has extensive partnerships with government and industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-research_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-research-62">[62]</a></sup> It mainly focuses its research in the areas of design, technology, health, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globalisation" title="Globalisation">globalisation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>.</p>
<p>Its Portfolio of Research and Innovation operates on a similar scale to its colleges, and also contains a specialist research school in order to foster excellence in research methodology and pedagogy. In addition to the Portfolio of Research and Innovation, over 50 <a href="/wiki/Research_center" title="Research center">research centres</a> operate independently within RMIT's colleges and schools as well as a large number of smaller research groups.</p>

<p>RMIT University Library is the central libraries network of the university. It has five locations across RMIT's three Australian campuses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-universitylibrary_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-universitylibrary-74">[74]</a></sup> Swanston Library is the largest in the network, and is located in <a href="/wiki/RMIT_Building_8" title="RMIT Building 8">Building 8</a> at the City campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Swanston Library is also reported to be amongst the top five libraries in all of Melbourne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Other libraries in the network are the Brunswick Library, Bundoora East Library, Bundoora West Library and Carlton Library (the latter of which is also at the City campus).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-universitylibrary_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-universitylibrary-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>The City campus also benefits from its proximity to the State Library of Victoria—the central public reference library and the largest library in Melbourne.</p>
<p>In addition to its libraries network, RMIT schools also maintain their own specialised collections. Notable examples of school-maintained collections are the <a href="/wiki/RMIT_School_of_Media_and_Communication#AFI_Research_Collection" title="RMIT School of Media and Communication">AFI Research Collection</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFI_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-AFI-77">[77]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/RMIT_School_of_Architecture_and_Design#RMIT_Design_Archives" title="RMIT School of Architecture and Design">RMIT Design Archives</a> and <a href="/wiki/RMIT_School_of_Aerospace,_Mechanical_and_Manufacturing_Engineering#National_Aerospace_Resource_Centre" title="RMIT School of Aerospace, Mechanical and Manufacturing Engineering">National Aerospace Resource</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RMITdesign_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-RMITdesign-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-narc_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-narc-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two libraries are located at RMIT's Vietnam campuses; Beanland Library and Hanoi Library.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> The Beanland Library is the largest of the two libraries, and is located at the Ho Chi Minh City campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Online_databases">Online databases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Online databases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Selected research of RMIT academics and postgraduate students can be accessed through the RMIT Research Repository—an open access database of peer-reviewed published articles, conference papers, books and chapters, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RMIT_Research_Repository_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-RMIT_Research_Repository-82">[82]</a></sup> Documents held by the RMIT Research Repository are also indexed by <a href="/wiki/Google_Scholar" title="Google Scholar">Google Scholar</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_Australia" title="National Library of Australia">National Library of Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/WorldCat" title="WorldCat">WorldCat</a>. As of March 2013, there are more than 19,000 records in the Repository.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rmit_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-rmit-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>The university's subsidiary RMIT Publishing also owns and operates the <i>Informit</i> online library database, which is the largest database of research from across Australia and the <a href="/wiki/Asia-Pacific" title="Asia-Pacific">Asia-Pacific</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RMITPublishing_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-RMITPublishing-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Galleries">Galleries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Galleries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/RMIT_Gallery" title="RMIT Gallery">RMIT Gallery</a></div>
<p>The major public art gallery of the university is <a href="/wiki/RMIT_Gallery" title="RMIT Gallery">RMIT Gallery</a>, located at the City campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> The gallery runs a highly regarded program of Australian and international exhibitions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> and focuses on <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_art" title="Contemporary art">contemporary art</a>, design and <a href="/wiki/Visual_culture" title="Visual culture">visual culture</a>. It is located in the historic original section of Storey Hall on Swanston Street and is considered to be one of Melbourne's most vibrant art galleries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> The galery also publishes widely on art and design research in partnership with RMIT Publishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RMITPublishing_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-RMITPublishing-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>RMIT First Site Gallery at the City campus is the main gallery of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RMIT_Union" title="RMIT Union">RMIT Link</a> campus union,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-firstsite_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-firstsite-90">[90]</a></sup> and focuses on emerging artists and is located beneath RMIT Gallery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> The campus union also manages the Artland program at the Brunswick campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artland_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-artland-92">[92]</a></sup> Artland consists of 16 sites around the campus and Brunswick streets showcasing work of design students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artland_92-1"><a href="#cite_note-artland-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the Story Hall galleries, many of RMIT's schools also manage their own discipline-relevant galleries. Notable examples are the <a href="/wiki/RMIT_School_of_Art" title="RMIT School of Art">School of Art</a>'s main gallery and Project Space Gallery,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/RMIT_School_of_Media_and_Communication" title="RMIT School of Media and Communication">School of Media and Communication</a>'s Field36 Gallery,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/RMIT_School_of_Architecture_and_Design" title="RMIT School of Architecture and Design">School of Architecture and Design</a>'s Virtual Reality Centre and Design Hub Gallery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GreenDesignHub_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-GreenDesignHub-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> The acclaimed <a href="/wiki/Public_art" title="Public art">public art</a> program of the School of Art also produces art in public spaces around RMIT's campuses as well as the greater Melbourne city centre and metropolitan area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="University_Art_Collection">University Art Collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: University Art Collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>RMIT Gallery is also charged as the caretaker of RMIT's permanent art collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> It includes the substantial Linsday Edwards Collection of fine art and invaluable W. E. Macmillan Collection of gold and silver as well as a number of other sub-collections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SHCM_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-SHCM-98">[98]</a></sup> The Linsday Edwards Collection has a strong focus on <a href="/wiki/Australian_art" title="Australian art">Australian art</a>, and holds work by leading Australian artists (including RMIT alumni or former faculty) such as <a href="/wiki/Howard_Arkley" title="Howard Arkley">Howard Arkley</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Brack" title="John Brack">John Brack</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leonard_French" title="Leonard French">Leonard French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Kemp" title="Roger Kemp">Roger Kemp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inge_King" title="Inge King">Inge King</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Meldrum" title="Max Meldrum">Max Meldrum</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Olsen_(artist)" title="John Olsen (artist)">John Olsen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lenton_Parr" title="Lenton Parr">Lenton Parr</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fred_Williams_(artist)" title="Fred Williams (artist)">Fred Williams</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdquistGrierson2008105-127_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdquistGrierson2008105-127-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<p>A history of the art collection is documented in the publication <i>A Skilled Hand and Cultivated Mind: A Guide to the Architecture and Art of RMIT</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdquistGrierson2008_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdquistGrierson2008-100">[100]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_life">Student life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Student life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Link_.28campus_union.29">Link (campus union)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Link (campus union)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/RMIT_Link" title="RMIT Link">RMIT Link</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/RMIT_Redbacks" title="RMIT Redbacks">RMIT Redbacks</a></div>
<p>RMIT Link is the university's campus union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aboutlink_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-aboutlink-101">[101]</a></sup> It exists to sponsor and promote social, cultural, educational, sporting and recreational programs and activities among the RMIT community, and to provide such facilities and services at RMIT's Australian campuses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> Link is separated into two divisions: Arts &amp; Culture and Sports &amp; Recreation. It is a controlled entity under the authority of RMIT's Council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aboutlink_101-1"><a href="#cite_note-aboutlink-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup></p>
<p>Arts &amp; Culture manages a number of <a href="/wiki/Extracurricular_activity" title="Extracurricular activity">extra-curricular</a> arts collectives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> It also offers workshop and seminars as well as funding for arts initiatives,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> and runs a free cinema program at the City and Bundoora campuses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sports &amp; Recreation manages the university's <a href="/wiki/Semi-professional" title="Semi-professional">semi-professional</a> sports teams, which are collectively known as the <a href="/wiki/RMIT_Redbacks" title="RMIT Redbacks">Redbacks</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> and has an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sportsperson" title="Sportsperson">elite athlete</a> funding program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> It offers funding for community and social <a href="/wiki/Sports_club" title="Sports club">sports clubs</a> on RMIT's Australian campuses,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> and also runs community and charity sporting events and tournaments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> It also operates the City campus gym,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> and co-owns a ski lodge on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Buller,_Victoria" title="Mount Buller, Victoria">Mount Buller</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="RUSU_.28student_union.29">RUSU (student union)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: RUSU (student union)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/RMIT_University_Student_Union" title="RMIT University Student Union">RMIT University Student Union</a></div>
<p>RMIT's University Student Union (RUSU) is the independent body representing students enrolled at RMIT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> It was founded in 1944 by John Storey Jr., after whom Storey Hall at the City campus is named.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray-Smith_1987_4-24"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-Smith_1987-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> The objective of RUSU is to safeguard the interests and rights of students,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> and to advance education, welfare, social life and cultural activities of students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> RUSU has a number of departments advocating various elements of student life, and it also supports academic, cultural, political, spiritual and special interest clubs and societies run by students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accommodation">Accommodation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Accommodation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>RMIT operates several student accommodation facilities including: RMIT Village, Cambridge Court and College Square on the City campus and Walert House on the Bundoora campus—all of which operate as self-catered apartment complexes. Twelve other student hostels are also operated by other providers.</p>
<p>Some of the traditional residential colleges of the nearby University of Melbourne also reserve places for RMIT students. The college fees include all catering, utilities, academic and pastoral support. The colleges affiliated with RMIT include: <a href="/wiki/International_House_(University_of_Melbourne)" title="International House (University of Melbourne)">International House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Janet_Clarke_Hall" title="Janet Clarke Hall">Janet Clarke Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newman_College_(University_of_Melbourne)" title="Newman College (University of Melbourne)">Newman College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_College_(University_of_Melbourne)" title="Queen's College (University of Melbourne)">Queen's College</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s_College_(University_of_Melbourne)" title="St Mary's College (University of Melbourne)">St Mary's College</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_College_(University_of_Melbourne)" title="University College (University of Melbourne)">University College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whitley_College" title="Whitley College">Whitley College</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_Demographics">Student Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Student Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2014, RMIT's program enrollments by gender were 54% male and 46% female.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup> RMIT's Higher Education student body was 52% male and 48% female which its Vocational Educational student body was 53% male and 47% female. According to a study of over 100 RMIT STEM graduates, male RMIT University STEM graduates outnumber females by 7 to 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spiritual_Centre">Spiritual Centre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Spiritual Centre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/RMIT_Spiritual_Centre" title="RMIT Spiritual Centre">RMIT Spiritual Centre</a></div>
<p>RMIT's Spiritual Centre is a multi-faith place of worship located on the City campus. It is housed in the historic Old Melbourne Gaol chapel, built in 1860.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> The centre provides a contemplative space to all staff and students of RMIT, regardless of their faith and without showing favour to any one faith, and houses the RMIT Chaplaincy services. RMIT has <a href="/wiki/Chaplain" title="Chaplain">chaplains</a> that represent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> faiths of various branches as well as for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integral_(spirituality)" title="Integral (spirituality)">Integral</a> spirituality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="People">People</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: People">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_alumni_and_faculty_of_the_Royal_Melbourne_Institute_of_Technology" title="List of alumni and faculty of the Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology">List of alumni and faculty of the Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology</a></div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Graduation_traditions">Graduation traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RMIT_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Graduation traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Academic_dress_of_the_Royal_Melbourne_Institute_of_Technology" title="Academic dress of the Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology">Academic dress of the Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology</a></div>
<p>A notable graduation tradition of RMIT is its graduation parade. The parade is <a href="/wiki/Town_and_gown" title="Town and gown">town and gown</a>-style <a href="/wiki/Academic_procession" title="Academic procession">academic procession</a> which proceeds from the City campus down the major city thoroughfare of Swanston Street to <a href="/wiki/Federation_Square" title="Federation Square">Federation Square</a> (until 2002 the parade culminated outside the Melbourne Town Hall).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gradparade_126-0"><a href="#cite_note-gradparade-126">[126]</a></sup> Graduands and faculty march in full <a href="/wiki/Academic_dress" title="Academic dress">academic regalia</a> and receive a military escort from the central marching band of the Royal Australian Air Force. The parade is welcomed at Federation Square by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Mayor_of_Melbourne" title="Lord Mayor of Melbourne">Lord Mayor of Melbourne</a>—on behalf of the city and its citizens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gradparade_126-1"><a href="#cite_note-gradparade-126">[126]</a></sup> The mayor grants RMIT's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice-chancellor_(education)" title="Vice-chancellor (education)">vice-chancellor</a> a "writ of passage" to proceed with the graduation ceremony, which takes place at the <a href="/wiki/Docklands_Stadium" title="Docklands Stadium">Docklands Stadium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup></p>
