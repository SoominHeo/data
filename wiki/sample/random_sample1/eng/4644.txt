<p><b>Roy Patrick Kerr</b> <span style="font-size:85%;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Companion_of_The_New_Zealand_Order_of_Merit" title="Companion of The New Zealand Order of Merit">CNZM</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fellow_of_The_Royal_Society_of_New_Zealand" title="Fellow of The Royal Society of New Zealand">FRSNZ</a></span> (born 16 May 1934) is a New Zealand mathematician who discovered the <a href="/wiki/Kerr_metric" title="Kerr metric">Kerr geometry</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Exact_solutions_in_general_relativity" title="Exact solutions in general relativity">exact solution</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Einstein_field_equation" title="Einstein field equation">Einstein field equation</a> of <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>. His solution models the gravitational field outside an uncharged rotating massive object, including a <a href="/wiki/Rotating_black_hole" title="Rotating black hole">rotating black hole</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_life">Private life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Kerr&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Private life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kerr was born in 1934 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kurow,_New_Zealand" title="Kurow, New Zealand">Kurow, New Zealand</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Born into a dysfunctional family, his mother was forced to leave when he was three. When his father went to war, he was sent to a farm. After his father's return from war, they moved to <a href="/wiki/Christchurch" title="Christchurch">Christchurch</a>. He managed to get into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Andrew%27s_College,_Christchurch" title="St Andrew's College, Christchurch">St Andrew's College</a>, a private school, as his father had served under a former headmaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bright_sparks_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bright_sparks-3">[3]</a></sup> His solution to Einstein's equations predicted spinning black holes before they were discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kerr is married to Margaret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bright_sparks_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bright_sparks-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2013 they moved from Christchurch to <a href="/wiki/Tauranga" title="Tauranga">Tauranga</a>.</p>
<p>Kerr was a notable bridge player representing New Zealand internationally in the mid 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He was co-author of the Symmetric Relay System, a <a href="/wiki/Bidding_system" title="Bidding system">bidding system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_history">Professional history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Kerr&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Professional history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kerr's mathematical talent was first recognised while he was still a high school student at St Andrew's College. Although there was no maths teacher there at the time he was able in 1951 to go straight into third year Mathematics at the Canterbury University College of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_New_Zealand" title="University of New Zealand">University of New Zealand</a>, the precursor to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Canterbury" title="University of Canterbury">University of Canterbury</a>. Their regulations did not permit him to graduate until 1954 and so it was not until September 1955 that he moved to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a>, where he earned his PhD in 1959. His dissertation concerned the difficult problem of the equations of motion in general relativity.</p>
<p>After a stint as a post-doctoral researcher at <a href="/wiki/Syracuse_University" title="Syracuse University">Syracuse University</a>, where Einstein's collaborator <a href="/wiki/Peter_Bergmann" title="Peter Bergmann">Peter Bergmann</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> was professor, he spent some time working for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> at <a href="/wiki/Wright-Patterson_Air_Force_Base" title="Wright-Patterson Air Force Base">Wright-Patterson Air Force Base</a>. Kerr speculated that the "main reason why the US Air Force had created a General Relativity section was probably to show the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">Navy</a> that they could also do pure research."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1962 Kerr moved to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">University of Texas at Austin</a>, where in 1963, he discovered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kerr_vacuum" title="Kerr vacuum">Kerr vacuum</a> solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1965, with <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Schild" title="Alfred Schild">Alfred Schild</a>, he introduced the concept of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kerr-Schild_spacetime" title="Kerr-Schild spacetime">Kerr-Schild spacetimes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> During his time in Texas, Kerr supervised four PhD students. Kerr was interviewed about his work on the solution for the book <i>Cracking the Einstein Code: Relativity and the Birth of Black Hole Physics</i>, for which he also wrote an afterword.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1971, Kerr returned to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Canterbury" title="University of Canterbury">University of Canterbury</a> in New Zealand. Kerr retired from his position as Professor of Mathematics at the University of Canterbury in 1993 after having been there for twenty-two years, including ten years as the head of the Mathematics department.</p>
<p>In 2008 Kerr was appointed to the Yevgeny Lifshitz <a href="/wiki/ICRANet" title="ICRANet">ICRANet</a> Chair in <a href="/wiki/Pescara" title="Pescara">Pescara</a>, Italy.</p>
<p>His life was the subject of the semi-biographical book <a class="external text" href="http://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/C/bo6817175.html" rel="nofollow"><i>Cracking the Einstein Code</i></a> by <a href="/wiki/Fulvio_Melia" title="Fulvio Melia">Fulvio Melia</a>, published in 2009.</p>
<p>In 2012 it was announced that Kerr would be honoured by the <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein_Society" title="Albert Einstein Society">Albert Einstein Society</a> in Switzerland with the 2013 <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein_Medal" title="Albert Einstein Medal">Albert Einstein Medal</a>. He is the first New Zealander to receive the prestigious award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2015, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Canterbury" title="University of Canterbury">University of Canterbury</a> awarded Kerr an honorary <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Science" title="Doctor of Science">Doctor of Science</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2016 Kerr was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Crafoord_Prize" title="Crafoord Prize">Crafoord Prize</a> in Astronomy by The Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Kerr&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hector_Memorial_Medal" title="Hector Memorial Medal">Hector Medal</a> (1982) "for his work in theoretical physics. ... an exact solution of Einstein's equations of general relativity, ..."</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hughes_Medal" title="Hughes Medal">Hughes Medal</a> (1984) "for his distinguished work on relativity, especially for his discovery of the so-called Kerr Black Hole, which has been very influential."</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rutherford_Medal_(Royal_Society_of_New_Zealand)" title="Rutherford Medal (Royal Society of New Zealand)">Rutherford Medal</a> (1993) "For his outstanding discoveries in the extra-terrestrial world of black holes."</li>
<li><a class="external text" href="http://www.icra.it/MG/mg11/mg11awards-booklet.pdf" rel="nofollow">Marcel Grossmann Award</a> (2006) "for his fundamental contribution to Einstein's theory of general relativity: ..."</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Companion_of_The_New_Zealand_Order_of_Merit" title="Companion of The New Zealand Order of Merit">Companion of The New Zealand Order of Merit</a> (2011) <a class="external text" href="http://www.dpmc.govt.nz/node/157" rel="nofollow">"For services to astrophysics."</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein_Medal" title="Albert Einstein Medal">Albert Einstein Medal</a> (2013) "for his 1963 discovery of a solution to Einstein's gravitational field equations."</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Crafoord_Prize" title="Crafoord Prize">Crafoord Prize</a> (2016) <a class="external text" href="http://www.crafoordprize.se/press/arkivpressreleases/thecrafoordprizesinmathematicsandastronomy2016.5.76308e0c152098549fa15a0.html" rel="nofollow">"for fundamental work on rotating black holes and their astrophysical consequences."</a></li>
