<p><b>Roy Harper</b> (born 12 June 1941)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is an English folk rock singer, songwriter and guitarist who has been a professional musician since 1964. Harper has released 32 albums (including 10 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_album" title="Live album">live albums</a>) across his 50-year career. As a musician, Harper is known for his distinctive <a href="/wiki/Fingerstyle_guitar" title="Fingerstyle guitar">fingerstyle</a> playing and lengthy, lyrical, complex compositions, a result of his love of jazz and <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">Keats</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>His influence upon other musicians has been acknowledged by <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Page" title="Jimmy Page">Jimmy Page</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Plant" title="Robert Plant">Robert Plant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pete_Townshend" title="Pete Townshend">Pete Townshend</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kate_Bush" title="Kate Bush">Kate Bush</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ian_Anderson_(musician)" title="Ian Anderson (musician)">Ian Anderson</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jethro_Tull_(band)" title="Jethro Tull (band)">Jethro Tull</a>, who said Harper was his "...primary influence as an acoustic guitarist and songwriter."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Neil McCormick of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> described him as "one of Britain's most complex and eloquent lyricists and genuinely original songwriters... much admired by his peers"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Across the Atlantic his influence has been acknowledged by <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>-based acoustic band <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Foxes" title="Fleet Foxes">Fleet Foxes</a>, American musician and producer <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Wilson_(musician)" title="Jonathan Wilson (musician)">Jonathan Wilson</a> and <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">Californian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harpist" title="Harpist">harpist</a> <a href="/wiki/Joanna_Newsom" title="Joanna Newsom">Joanna Newsom</a> with whom he has also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concert_tour" title="Concert tour">toured</a>.</p>
<p>In 2005, Harper was awarded the <a href="/wiki/MOJO_Awards" title="MOJO Awards">MOJO</a> <a href="/wiki/2005_MOJO_Awards" title="2005 MOJO Awards">Hero Award</a>, and in 2013 a Lifetime Achievement Award at the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2_Folk_Awards" title="BBC Radio 2 Folk Awards">BBC Radio 2 Folk Awards</a>. His most recent album, <i><a href="/wiki/Man_and_Myth" title="Man and Myth">Man and Myth</a></i>, was released in 2013. In 2016, Harper celebrated his 75th birthday by performing concerts in Clonakilty, Birmingham, Manchester, London and Edinburgh.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Harper was born in 1941 in <a href="/wiki/Rusholme" title="Rusholme">Rusholme</a>, a suburb of <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>. His mother, Muriel, died three weeks after he was born. From the age of 6, he lived in <a href="/wiki/Lytham_St_Annes" title="Lytham St Annes">St Annes on Sea</a>, a place he described as being "like a cemetery with bus stops".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He was brought up by his father and stepmother, with whom he became disillusioned because of her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witness" title="Jehovah's Witness">Jehovah's Witness</a> beliefs, although they reconciled in 1980, just before her death. His <a href="/wiki/Antireligion" title="Antireligion">anti-religious</a> views would later become a familiar theme within his music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popmatters_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-popmatters-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Harper began writing poems when he was 12. At the age of 13 he began playing <a href="/wiki/Skiffle" title="Skiffle">skiffle</a> music with his younger brother David ("Davey" on the album <i><a href="/wiki/Flat_Baroque_and_Berserk" title="Flat Baroque and Berserk">Flat Baroque and Berserk</a></i>), as well as becoming influenced by <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> music. At 14 he formed his first group (<i>De Boys</i>) with his brothers David and Harry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Harper was educated at <a href="/wiki/King_Edward_VII_and_Queen_Mary_School" title="King Edward VII and Queen Mary School">King Edward VII School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lytham_St_Annes" title="Lytham St Annes">Lytham St Annes</a>, then a Grammar school and left at the age of 15 (1956) to join the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> in order to follow an ambition to be a pilot. After two years Harper rejected the rigid discipline, feigned madness in order to obtain a <a href="/wiki/Military_discharge" title="Military discharge">military discharge</a> and received an <a href="/wiki/Electroconvulsive_therapy" title="Electroconvulsive therapy">electroconvulsive therapy</a> treatment at <a href="/wiki/RAF_Halton" title="RAF Halton">Princess Mary's RAF Hospital</a>, Wendover. After being discharged from there, he spent one day inside the former 'Lancaster Moor Mental Institute' before escaping. These experiences would be recalled in "Committed", a song on Harper's debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Sophisticated_Beggar" title="Sophisticated Beggar">Sophisticated Beggar</a></i>. Upon his eventual exit from a troubled youth (around 1961) he <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Busking" title="Busking">busked</a> around North Africa, Europe and London for a few years.</p>
<p>Musically, Harper's earliest influences were American <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> musician <a href="/wiki/Lead_Belly" title="Lead Belly">Lead Belly</a> and <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk singer</a> <a href="/wiki/Woody_Guthrie" title="Woody Guthrie">Woody Guthrie</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dl94_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-dl94-8">[8]</a></sup> and, in his teens, jazz musician <a href="/wiki/Miles_Davis" title="Miles Davis">Miles Davis</a>. Of the blues musicians Lead Belly, <a href="/wiki/Big_Bill_Broonzy" title="Big Bill Broonzy">Big Bill Broonzy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Josh_White" title="Josh White">Josh White</a> Harper said they made music which "...seemed to be from a different planet ...We'd never heard anything like it. It changed our world overnight, a sledge hammer of a cultural change ...an equivalent would be to suddenly hear music from outer space".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Harper was also exposed to classical music in his childhood and has pointed to the influence of <a href="/wiki/Jean_Sibelius" title="Jean Sibelius">Jean Sibelius</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Karelia_Suite" title="Karelia Suite">Karelia Suite</a></i>. Lyrical influences include the 19th century <a href="/wiki/Romantic_poetry" title="Romantic poetry">Romantics</a>, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Percy_Bysshe_Shelly" title="Percy Bysshe Shelly">Shelley</a>, and Keats's poem "<a href="/wiki/Endymion_(poem)" title="Endymion (poem)">Endymion</a>". Harper has also cited the <a href="/wiki/Beat_Generation" title="Beat Generation">Beat poets</a> as being highly influential, particularly <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kerouac" title="Jack Kerouac">Jack Kerouac</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Returning to the UK in 1964/65, Harper recorded some demo recordings and played his first paid performance at a poetry reading in <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle</a>. Eventually he obtained a <a href="/wiki/Residency_show" title="Residency show">residency</a> at London's famous <a href="/wiki/Soho" title="Soho">Soho</a> <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a> club, <a href="/wiki/Les_Cousins_(music_club)" title="Les Cousins (music club)">Les Cousins</a> in 1965, having been introduced to it by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Bellamy" title="Peter Bellamy">Peter Bellamy</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Young_Tradition" title="The Young Tradition">the Young Tradition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-12">[12]</a></sup> Harpers first advertised performance was 5 October 1965. Within his first week Harper saw <a href="/wiki/John_Renbourn" title="John Renbourn">John Renbourn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexis_Korner" title="Alexis Korner">Alexis Korner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Simon" title="Paul Simon">Paul Simon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alex_Campbell_(singer)" title="Alex Campbell (singer)">Alex Campbell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bert_Jansch" title="Bert Jansch">Bert Jansch</a> play<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-12">[12]</a></sup> and he would play, associate, and 'rub shoulders' with other artists arriving later, including <a href="/wiki/John_Martyn" title="John Martyn">John Martyn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joni_Mitchell" title="Joni Mitchell">Joni Mitchell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nick_Drake" title="Nick Drake">Nick Drake</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_career">Musical career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Musical career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1966.E2.80.9369:_The_first_record_deals">1966–69: The first record deals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1966–69: The first record deals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Harper's first album, <i><a href="/wiki/Sophisticated_Beggar" title="Sophisticated Beggar">Sophisticated Beggar</a></i>, was recorded in 1966 after he was spotted at Les Cousins and signed to Peter Richards' Strike Records. The album consisted of Harper's songs and poetry backed by <a href="/wiki/Steel-string_acoustic_guitar" title="Steel-string acoustic guitar">acoustic guitar</a>, recorded with a <a href="/wiki/Revox" title="Revox">Revox</a> <a href="/wiki/Reel-to-reel_audio_tape_recording" title="Reel-to-reel audio tape recording">tape machine</a> by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Tubbs" title="Pierre Tubbs">Pierre Tubbs</a> and with contributions from English guitarist <a href="/wiki/Paul_Brett" title="Paul Brett">Paul Brett</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a> recognised Harper's potential and hired American producer <a href="/wiki/Shel_Talmy" title="Shel Talmy">Shel Talmy</a> to produce Harper's second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Come_Out_Fighting_Ghengis_Smith" title="Come Out Fighting Ghengis Smith">Come Out Fighting Ghengis Smith</a></i>, which was released in 1968. The 11 minute track "Circle", "a soundscape of Harper's difficult youth",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2011_Roy_Harper_Interview_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-2011_Roy_Harper_Interview-13">[13]</a></sup> was notable for marking a widening of his musical style away from the more traditional side of contemporary <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a> heard at the time. Harper had an interest in <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">traditional folk</a> but did not consider himself a bona fide member of the folk scene. He later explained:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I was too much of a modernist, really. Just too modern for what was going on in the folk clubs. I wanted to modernise music, but more than that to completely modernise people's attitudes towards life in general. I was involved in trying to bring (more) meat to the (contemporary) folk music...(of the time).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2008_Roy_Harper_interview_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-2008_Roy_Harper_interview-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Harper's record company had different expectations. "They wanted me to write commercial pop songs and when they heard the album I made for them, they didn’t have a clue. They wanted hits. And I gave them "Circle"".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2011_Roy_Harper_Interview_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-2011_Roy_Harper_Interview-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bert_Jansch" title="Bert Jansch">Bert Jansch</a> contributed <a href="/wiki/Liner_notes" title="Liner notes">sleeve notes</a> for the album and Harper paid tribute to Jansch with the song "Pretty Baby"; the B-Side non album track of his first single (released in March 1966). During this period, Harper was managed by American music entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/Jo_Lustig" title="Jo Lustig">Jo Lustig</a>, manager of <a href="/wiki/Pentangle_(band)" title="Pentangle (band)">The Pentangle</a> and former agent to <a href="/wiki/Julie_Felix" title="Julie Felix">Julie Felix</a>.</p>
<p>In June 1968, Harper performed at the first free concert ever held at <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_London" title="Hyde Park, London">Hyde Park</a>, acting as compere and sharing the bill with <a href="/wiki/Jethro_Tull_(band)" title="Jethro Tull (band)">Jethro Tull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a> and <a href="/wiki/T._Rex_(band)" title="T. Rex (band)">Tyrannosaurus Rex</a>. At the time, he spoke of co-writing a rock opera with Pink Floyd. No opera resulted, but it was the beginning of a musical relationship. Harper began to attract a following of fans from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Underground_(British_subculture)" title="Underground (British subculture)">underground</a> music scene<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and tour the UK, performing at numerous venues such as the <a href="/wiki/Lyceum_Theatre,_London" title="Lyceum Theatre, London">Lyceum Ballroom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Klooks_Kleek" title="Klooks Kleek">Klooks Kleek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mothers_(music_venue)" title="Mothers (music venue)">Mothers</a>; venues that would gradually gain recognition for the variety and quality of their musical acts. Mothers in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> was one such venue, and one to which Harper would frequently return.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mabbett_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mabbett-16">[16]</a></sup> Harper later told <a href="/wiki/Brum_Beat" title="Brum Beat">Brum Beat</a> magazine:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>That was the first club outside London that meant anything at all and that's why there's been this long association with Birmingham. I played there about six times between 1968 and 1970. I have always enjoyed playing here.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mabbett_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mabbett-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>A track from <i>Come Out Fighting Ghengis Smith</i>, "Nobody's Got Any Money In The Summer" also appeared on the first bargain-priced <a href="/wiki/Sampler_album" title="Sampler album">sampler album</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rock_Machine_Turns_You_On" title="The Rock Machine Turns You On">The Rock Machine Turns You On</a></i>. The album was released in the UK, the Netherlands, Germany and a number of other European countries as part of an international marketing campaign by <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a> (known in Europe as CBS).</p>
<p>In 1969 Harper undertook a short 6-venue tour with <a href="/wiki/Ron_Geesin" title="Ron Geesin">Ron Geesin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ralph_McTell" title="Ralph McTell">Ralph McTell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The tour programme contained the introductory paragraph:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Roy Harper isn't an example of any category, the epitome of any movement or a rung on anybody's ladder; he built himself alone, piece by piece and his defiant character stands proud as if chiselled from belligerent granite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>That same year, Harper released his third album <i><a href="/wiki/Folkjokeopus" title="Folkjokeopus">Folkjokeopus</a></i> again produced by Shel Talmy, and released by <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Records" title="Liberty Records">Liberty Records</a>. Side two included an extended 17-minute track, titled "McGoohan's Blues", which Harper referred to as the "main statement" within the album. Of his non-conformance to radio-friendly, standard, three-minute songs, Harper claimed it to be a revolt, and that he regarded the three-minute pop song as an anathema, a <a href="/wiki/Jingle" title="Jingle">jingle</a> to sell a band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2008_Roy_Harper_interview_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-2008_Roy_Harper_interview-14">[14]</a></sup> (The title for "McGoohan's Blues" was a reference to actor <a href="/wiki/Patrick_McGoohan" title="Patrick McGoohan">Patrick McGoohan</a>, who had starred in the UK TV series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prisoner" title="The Prisoner">The Prisoner</a></i> two years earlier). The track "Sergeant Sunshine" would also appear on <i><a href="/wiki/Son_of_Gutbucket" title="Son of Gutbucket">Son of Gutbucket</a></i> a 1969 sampler album released to promote artists on the <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Records" title="Liberty Records">Liberty Records</a> label.</p>
<p>During this period Harper also visited the <a href="/wiki/Dolphin_Club" title="Dolphin Club">Dolphin Club</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>, Norway, where he became acquainted with folk singer <a href="/wiki/Lillebj%C3%B8rn_Nilsen" title="Lillebjørn Nilsen">Lillebjørn Nilsen</a>. Nilsen learned one of Harper's songs, "On the First Day of April" which he translated to "Ravn Ferd" and recorded for his debut solo album <i>Tilbake</i> in 1971. Harper and Nilsen along with <a href="/wiki/Finn_Kalvik" title="Finn Kalvik">Finn Kalvik</a> performed together on 23 January 1970 at a concert held in the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oslo" title="University of Oslo">University of Oslo</a>. Harper's visit coincided with the emergence of the Norwegian 'folk music wave' (<a href="/wiki/Swedish_ballad_tradition" title="Swedish ballad tradition">Visebølgen</a>) and the Norwegian Broadcasting Corporation (<a href="/wiki/NRK" title="NRK">NRK</a>) recorded Harper on the occasion of the concert. Kalvik would later go on to record Norwegian versions of two Harper songs; "I Hate the White Man" ("Den hvite mann") and "Don't You Grieve" ("Kjære ikke gråt").</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970.E2.80.9380:_The_Harvest_years">1970–80: The Harvest years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1970–80: The Harvest years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With Harper's reputation growing, <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a>'s manager <a href="/wiki/Peter_Jenner" title="Peter Jenner">Peter Jenner</a> signed him to a long-term (and at times confrontational) deal with <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a>'s 'underground' subsidiary, <a href="/wiki/Harvest_Records" title="Harvest Records">Harvest Records</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Over a ten-year period, Harper recorded eight albums at the <a href="/wiki/Abbey_Road_Studios" title="Abbey Road Studios">Abbey Road Studios</a> for the Harvest label<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and for much of this period was <a href="/wiki/Talent_manager" title="Talent manager">managed</a> and produced by Jenner, initially acting for <a href="/wiki/Blackhill_Enterprises" title="Blackhill Enterprises">Blackhill Enterprises</a>. According to Jenner, <i>"Harper is a terrific songwriter, but a bit crazy"</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Harper's first tour of the United States followed the release of his fourth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Flat_Baroque_and_Berserk" title="Flat Baroque and Berserk">Flat Baroque and Berserk</a></i>. The album included the track "<a href="/wiki/Another_Day_(Roy_Harper_song)" title="Another Day (Roy Harper song)">Another Day</a>", a song destined to be performed live by Harper for many years to come, and covered by several other artists including <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Fraser" title="Elizabeth Fraser">Elizabeth Fraser</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kate_Bush" title="Kate Bush">Kate Bush</a>. The album also featured <a href="/wiki/The_Nice" title="The Nice">the Nice</a> on the track "Hell's Angels"; its ethereal sound achieved by a <a href="/wiki/Wah-wah_pedal" title="Wah-wah pedal">wah-wah pedal</a> attached to Harper's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steel-string_guitar" title="Steel-string guitar">acoustic guitar</a>.</p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a> Festival of 1970, <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">Led Zeppelin</a> paid tribute to Harper with their version of the traditional song "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shake_%27Em_on_Down" title="Shake 'Em on Down">Shake 'Em on Down</a>". Retitled "<a href="/wiki/Hats_Off_to_(Roy)_Harper" title="Hats Off to (Roy) Harper">Hats Off to (Roy) Harper</a>", it appeared on the album <i><a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin_III" title="Led Zeppelin III">Led Zeppelin III</a></i>. According to <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Page" title="Jimmy Page">Jimmy Page</a>, the band admired the way Harper stood by his principles and did not sell out to commercial pressures. In mutual appreciation of their work, Harper would often attend live performances by Led Zeppelin over the subsequent decade, contributed sleeve photography to the album <i><a href="/wiki/Physical_Graffiti" title="Physical Graffiti">Physical Graffiti</a></i> and also appeared, albeit uncredited, in the 1976 Led Zeppelin documentary film, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Song_Remains_the_Same_(film)" title="The Song Remains the Same (film)">The Song Remains the Same</a></i>.</p>
<p>Harper's critically acclaimed 1971 album was a four-song epic, <i><a href="/wiki/Stormcock_(album)" title="Stormcock (album)">Stormcock</a></i>. The album featured <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Page" title="Jimmy Page">Jimmy Page</a> on guitar (credited as "S. Flavius Mercurius" for contractual reasons) and <a href="/wiki/David_Bedford" title="David Bedford">David Bedford</a>'s orchestral arrangements (Bedford would also collaborate on some of Harper's future releases). Harper felt the album to be not particularly well promoted by his record label at the time and later stated:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>They hated Stormcock. No singles. No way of promoting it on the radio. They said there wasn't any money to market it. Stormcock dribbled out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Nevertheless, <i>Stormcock</i> would remain a favourite album of Harper's fans and influence musicians for decades to come. Thirty-five years later (in 2006) fellow <a href="/wiki/List_of_people_from_Manchester" title="List of people from Manchester">Mancunian</a> <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Marr" title="Johnny Marr">Johnny Marr</a> of English <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/The_Smiths" title="The Smiths">the Smiths</a> said:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>If ever there was a secret weapon of a record it would be Stormcock... It's intense and beautiful and clever: <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">Bowie's</a> <a href="/wiki/Hunky_Dory" title="Hunky Dory">Hunky Dory</a>'s big, badder brother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joanna_Newsom" title="Joanna Newsom">Joanna Newsom</a> cited <i>Stormcock</i> as an influence upon her 2006 release <a href="/wiki/Ys_(Joanna_Newsom_album)" title="Ys (Joanna Newsom album)">Ys</a> and in 2011, <a href="/wiki/Robin_Pecknold" title="Robin Pecknold">Robin Pecknold</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seattle,_Washington" title="Seattle, Washington">Seattle, Washington</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk</a> band <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Foxes" title="Fleet Foxes">Fleet Foxes</a> stated that he took inspiration from <i>Stormcock</i> when recording Fleet Foxes second album <i><a href="/wiki/Helplessness_Blues" title="Helplessness Blues">Helplessness Blues</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1972, Harper made his acting debut playing Mike Preston alongside <a href="/wiki/Carol_White" title="Carol White">Carol White</a> in the <a href="/wiki/John_Mackenzie_(film_director)" title="John Mackenzie (film director)">John Mackenzie</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Made_(1972_film)" title="Made (1972 film)">Made</a></i>. The film was chosen (along with <i><a href="/wiki/A_Clockwork_Orange_(film)" title="A Clockwork Orange (film)">A Clockwork Orange</a></i>) to represent Britain at the <a href="/wiki/Venice_Film_Festival" title="Venice Film Festival">Venice Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hats_off_to_Harper_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hats_off_to_Harper-24">[24]</a></sup> Harper also recorded the soundtrack for the film, released the following year as <i><a href="/wiki/Lifemask" title="Lifemask">Lifemask</a></i>. At the time, <i>Lifemask</i> was created as Harper's <a href="/wiki/Curtain_call" title="Curtain call">final bow</a>, as he had been diagnosed with the (then) little-known genetic condition <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hereditary_haemorrhagic_telangiectasia" title="Hereditary haemorrhagic telangiectasia">HHT</a>, which caused <a href="/wiki/Polycythemia" title="Polycythemia">polycythemia</a>, incapacitating him. The cover art shows Harper's life mask, as opposed to the '<a href="/wiki/Death_mask" title="Death mask">death mask</a>' it might have been.</p>
<p>After recovering (treatment involved frequent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venesection" title="Venesection">venesection</a>), his next album (<i><a href="/wiki/Valentine_(album)" title="Valentine (album)">Valentine</a></i>) was released on <a href="/wiki/Valentine%27s_Day" title="Valentine's Day">Valentine's Day</a>, 14 February 1974, and featured contributions from Jimmy Page. A concert to mark its release was held on the same day at London's <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_Theatre" title="Rainbow Theatre">Rainbow Theatre</a>, with Page, Bedford, <a href="/wiki/Max_Middleton" title="Max Middleton">Max Middleton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Lane" title="Ronnie Lane">Ronnie Lane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Keith_Moon" title="Keith Moon">Keith Moon</a> performing alongside Harper. His first live album <i><a href="/wiki/Flashes_from_the_Archives_of_Oblivion" title="Flashes from the Archives of Oblivion">Flashes from the Archives of Oblivion</a></i>, featuring two tracks recorded at that concert, soon followed.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a>'s 1975 release <i><a href="/wiki/Wish_You_Were_Here_(Pink_Floyd_album)" title="Wish You Were Here (Pink Floyd album)">Wish You Were Here</a></i> saw Harper sing lead vocals on the song "<a href="/wiki/Have_a_Cigar" title="Have a Cigar">Have a Cigar</a>". <a href="/wiki/Roger_Waters" title="Roger Waters">Roger Waters</a> intended to record the part himself, but had strained his voice while recording "<a href="/wiki/Shine_On_You_Crazy_Diamond" title="Shine On You Crazy Diamond">Shine On You Crazy Diamond</a>" and <a href="/wiki/David_Gilmour" title="David Gilmour">David Gilmour</a> declined to sing. Harper was recording his album <i><a href="/wiki/HQ_(album)" title="HQ (album)">HQ</a></i> in Studio 2 of Abbey Road at the same time as Pink Floyd were working in Studio 3; learning of the band's dilemma, Harper offered to sing the lead. The song is one of only two songs by Pink Floyd not sung by one of their permanent members. David Gilmour returned the favour by appearing on <i>HQ</i>, along with Harper's occasional backing band, 'Trigger' (<a href="/wiki/Chris_Spedding" title="Chris Spedding">Chris Spedding</a>, Dave Cochran, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Bruford" title="Bill Bruford">Bill Bruford</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_Jones_(musician)" title="John Paul Jones (musician)">John Paul Jones</a>). The single "<a href="/wiki/When_an_Old_Cricketer_Leaves_the_Crease" title="When an Old Cricketer Leaves the Crease">When an Old Cricketer Leaves the Crease</a>", taken from <i>HQ</i>, is one of Harper's best known songs. Harper also co-wrote the song "Short and Sweet" with Gilmour for Gilmour's first solo record, <i><a href="/wiki/David_Gilmour_(album)" title="David Gilmour (album)">David Gilmour</a></i>, released in 1978.</p>
<p>Controversy followed the release of 1977's <i><a href="/wiki/Bullinamingvase" title="Bullinamingvase">Bullinamingvase</a></i>. The owners of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watford_Gap_service_station" title="Watford Gap service station">Watford Gap service station</a> objected to criticism of their food – "<i>Watford Gap, Watford Gap/A plate of grease and a load of crap...</i>" – in the lyrics of the song "Watford Gap", as did an <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a> board member who was also a <a href="/wiki/Non-executive_director" title="Non-executive director">non-executive director</a> of Blue Boar (the owners of the service station). Harper was forced to drop it from future UK copies of the album, though it remained on the US LP and reappeared on a later CD reissues. The album also featured the song "One of Those Days in England", with backing vocals by <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linda_McCartney" title="Linda McCartney">Linda</a>; the single from the album went to number 42 in the UK charts. During this period, Harper's band were renamed 'Chips' and included <a href="/wiki/Andy_Roberts_(musician)" title="Andy Roberts (musician)">Andy Roberts</a>, Dave Lawson, Henry McCullough, John Halsey and Dave Cochran. In April 1978, Harper began writing lyrics for the next <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">Led Zeppelin</a> album with <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Page" title="Jimmy Page">Jimmy Page</a>, but the project was shelved when lead singer <a href="/wiki/Robert_Plant" title="Robert Plant">Robert Plant</a> returned from a <a href="/wiki/Sabbatical" title="Sabbatical">sabbatical</a> after the death of his son, Karac Pendragon.</p>
<p>Following the success of <i>Bullinamingvase</i>, Harper was asked "to write another record quickly". <a href="/wiki/Demo_(music)" title="Demo (music)">Demo</a> recordings with Harper's newly formed backing band 'Black Sheep' (Andy Roberts, Dave Lawson, Henry McCullough, John Halsey and Dave Cochran, a.k.a. Dave C. Drill)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> were made, but Harper felt them to be rushed. The record company who <i>"...were in the first stages of a collapse in sales..."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royharper.co.uk_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-royharper.co.uk-27">[27]</a></sup> were not interested in the recordings, nor were they prepared to provide <a href="/wiki/Recording_studio" title="Recording studio">studio</a> time when requested, telling Harper to come back in six months. As a result, Harper withheld the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Publishing_rights" title="Publishing rights">publishing rights</a> to that which had been recorded; an album provisionally entitled <i>Commercial Breaks (doesn't it?)</i> and was (in his own words) "outlawed"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royharper.co.uk_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-royharper.co.uk-27">[27]</a></sup> by the record company.</p>
<p>From 1975 to 1980 Harper worked with English musician and 'Black Sheep' member <a href="/wiki/Andy_Roberts_(musician)" title="Andy Roberts (musician)">Andy Roberts</a> sometimes performing as a duo. During this period, Harper spent considerable time in the United States and signed with the US division of <a href="/wiki/Chrysalis_Records" title="Chrysalis Records">Chrysalis Records</a>, who released <i>HQ</i> under a different title – <i>When an Old Cricketer Leaves the Crease</i> – and with alternative artwork. Chrysalis considered the original <a href="/wiki/Hipgnosis" title="Hipgnosis">Hipgnosis</a>-designed album cover of Harper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus%27_walk_on_water" title="Jesus' walk on water">walking on water</a> to be too offensive for an American release. Harper disagreed, but was given no choice by the label. Chrysalis also changed the title of Harper's next album, <i><a href="/wiki/Bullinamingvase" title="Bullinamingvase">Bullinamingvase</a></i>, to <i>One of Those Days in England</i>. In 1978, US Chrysalis reissued Harper's first five <a href="/wiki/Harvest_Records" title="Harvest Records">Harvest</a> albums, only one of which (<i>Flat, Baroque and Berserk</i>) had been previously released in America.</p>
<p>On 28 December 1979, BBC TV aired the <i>Kate Bush Christmas Special</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> As well as playing songs from her first two albums, Bush and her guest, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gabriel" title="Peter Gabriel">Peter Gabriel</a>, performed Harper's "<a href="/wiki/Another_Day_(Roy_Harper_song)" title="Another Day (Roy Harper song)">Another Day</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_Christmas_Special_1979_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_Christmas_Special_1979-29">[29]</a></sup> Their duet was discussed for release as a single, but never appeared.</p>
<p>Harper returned to the studio a few years after his dispute with EMI to record and prepare his next album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Unknown_Soldier_(album)" title="The Unknown Soldier (album)">The Unknown Soldier</a></i> (1980). At the time, Harper knew it would be his last release on the label and it was these demos that <i>"...were destined to gather dust on a shelf labelled 'Commercial Breaks'..."</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royharper.co.uk_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-royharper.co.uk-27">[27]</a></sup> (It was not until Harper's 1988 release <i><a href="/wiki/Loony_on_the_Bus" title="Loony on the Bus">Loony on the Bus</a></i> that some of these songs became officially available, and another six years until the album was finally released as <i><a href="/wiki/Commercial_Breaks_(album)" title="Commercial Breaks (album)">Commercial Breaks</a> (1994)).</i></p>
<p>In 1980 Harper released <i>The Unknown Soldier</i>, which was indeed his final <a href="/wiki/Harvest_Records" title="Harvest Records">Harvest</a> release. The album contains a <a href="/wiki/Duet" title="Duet">duet</a> with <a href="/wiki/Kate_Bush" title="Kate Bush">Kate Bush</a> on the track "You". Harper later reciprocated by singing backing vocals on "<a href="/wiki/Breathing_(Kate_Bush_song)" title="Breathing (Kate Bush song)">Breathing</a>" on Bush's album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Never_For_Ever" title="Never For Ever">Never For Ever</a></i>; Bush's first no. 1 album, the first ever album by a British female solo artist to top the UK album chart, and the first album by any female solo artist to enter the chart at no. 1. Bush thanked Harper on the album's cover for "holding onto the poet in his music".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hats_off_to_Harper_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hats_off_to_Harper-24">[24]</a></sup> During a BBC Radio interview by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gambaccini" title="Paul Gambaccini">Paul Gambaccini</a>, Bush praised Harper, stating:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Roy is one of the greatest English songwriters we've had, and people just don't realise it. And I really think that when they do we're going to have another top songwriter up there. He's brilliant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Of Bush, Harper later said,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Kate is a fantastic musician and very professional as well. Working with Kate is a very smooth operation because she always knows what she wants to do, surprising you too, which is what good musicians always do"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>A decade later, Harper and Bush would again collaborate on his 1990 release <i><a href="/wiki/Once_(Roy_Harper_album)" title="Once (Roy Harper album)">Once</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1981.E2.80.9389:_Recession_and_repossession">1981–89: Recession and repossession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1981–89: Recession and repossession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Harper's 1982 album <i><a href="/wiki/Work_of_Heart" title="Work of Heart">Work of Heart</a></i> was released on Public Records, a newly formed record label Harper created with Mark Thompson (son of English historian, socialist and peace campaigner <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E.P._Thompson" title="E.P. Thompson">E.P. Thompson</a> and brother of <a href="/wiki/Kate_Thompson_(author)" title="Kate Thompson (author)">Kate Thompson</a>). During this period Harper toured with a band consisting of <a href="/wiki/Tony_Franklin_(musician)" title="Tony Franklin (musician)">Tony Franklin</a> on <a href="/wiki/Bass_guitar" title="Bass guitar">Bass</a>, Bob Wilson of <a href="/wiki/Steve_Gibbons_(musician)" title="Steve Gibbons (musician)">the Steve Gibbons Band</a>, George Jackson on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drumkit" title="Drumkit">drums</a> and Dave Morris on <a href="/wiki/Keyboard_instrument" title="Keyboard instrument">keyboards</a>. The album was chosen by <a href="/wiki/Derek_Jewell" title="Derek Jewell">Derek Jewell</a> of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times_(UK)" title="The Sunday Times (UK)">The Sunday Times</a></i> as "Album of the Year" in 1982, but it did not sell well and the short-lived label went under.</p>
<p>During this period Harper lost his home, a farm in the village of <a href="/wiki/Marden,_Herefordshire" title="Marden, Herefordshire">Marden, Herefordshire</a>, to the bank. His manager at the time mortgaged Harper's house (by virtue of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_of_Attorney" title="Power of Attorney">Power of Attorney</a> Harper had granted him), pocketed the proceeds and disappeared to America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Of this period Harper stated:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>...I can proudly say that I was one of the first casualties of the eighties <a href="/wiki/Early_1980s_recession" title="Early 1980s recession">recession</a>!... It was a chaotic period and one that I don't care to remember that often... There is no doubt in my own mind that the early eighties were the <a href="/wiki/Nadir" title="Nadir">nadir</a> of my life in music"<i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></i></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The original demo version of <i>Work of Heart</i> was later released (in 1984) on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limited_edition" title="Limited edition">limited edition</a> (830 copies) <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" title="Gramophone record">vinyl</a> release entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Born_in_Captivity" title="Born in Captivity">Born in Captivity</a></i>.</p>
<p>Throughout 1984, Harper toured the United Kingdom with <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Page" title="Jimmy Page">Jimmy Page</a> performing a predominantly acoustic set at folk festivals under various guises such as the MacGregors, and Themselves. In 1985, <i><a href="/wiki/Whatever_Happened_to_Jugula%3F" title="Whatever Happened to Jugula?">Whatever Happened to Jugula?</a></i> was released. The album caused a resurgence of interest in Harper and his music. (<a href="/wiki/Tony_Franklin_(musician)" title="Tony Franklin (musician)">Tony Franklin</a>, bass player in Harper's group at this time, later joined <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Page" title="Jimmy Page">Page</a> in <a href="/wiki/The_Firm_(rock_band)" title="The Firm (rock band)">the Firm</a>). In April 1984, Harper and Gilmour performed "Short and Sweet" (a song they co-wrote) during Gilmour's three-night run at <a href="/wiki/Hammersmith_Apollo" title="Hammersmith Apollo">The Hammersmith Odeon</a>. This version later appeared on the <i><a href="/wiki/David_Gilmour_Live_1984" title="David Gilmour Live 1984">David Gilmour Live 1984</a></i> concert film. Harper also provided backing vocals on Gilmour's newly released album, <i><a href="/wiki/About_Face_(album)" title="About Face (album)">About Face</a></i>.</p>
<p>On 20 June 1984 Harper performed at the last <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge_Free_Festival" title="Stonehenge Free Festival">Stonehenge Free Festival</a> along with <a href="/wiki/Hawkwind" title="Hawkwind">Hawkwind</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Enid" title="The Enid">the Enid</a>. The concert was videoed and released as <a href="/wiki/Hawkwind_videography#Stonehenge_84" title="Hawkwind videography">Stonehenge 84</a>.</p>
<p>As a result of his continual touring and the popularity of <i>Whatever Happened to Jugula?</i>, Harper re-signed to <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a> and in 1986 released a live album, <i><a href="/wiki/In_Between_Every_Line" title="In Between Every Line">In Between Every Line</a></i> (containing recordings from his performances at the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Folk_Festival" title="Cambridge Folk Festival">Cambridge Folk Festival</a>), and in 1988 the studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Descendants_of_Smith" title="Descendants of Smith">Descendants of Smith</a></i>. The renewed relationship between Harper and EMI did not last and from 1985 more of his earlier albums were becoming available on the newly formed <a href="/wiki/Awareness_Records" title="Awareness Records">Awareness Records</a> label.</p>
<p>1988 also saw the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Loony_on_the_Bus" title="Loony on the Bus">Loony on the Bus</a></i>, a collection of tracks recorded a decade earlier and intended for release in 1977 as <a href="/wiki/Commercial_Breaks_(album)" title="Commercial Breaks (album)"><i>Commercial Breaks</i></a> (with the sub-title, 'doesn't it?'). The original release having been held back because of disputes over funding and content between Harper and EMI. Sales of <i>Loony on the Bus</i> would fund Harper's 1990 release; <i><a href="/wiki/Once_(Roy_Harper_album)" title="Once (Roy Harper album)">Once</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990.E2.80.9399:_Science_Friction">1990–99: Science Friction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1990–99: Science Friction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1993<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Harper established his own record label <a href="/wiki/Science_Friction" title="Science Friction">Science Friction</a> and obtained the rights to all his previously released albums. As a result, from 1994 much of Harper's back catalogue became available on CD once more.</p>
<p>Harper was very productive during the decade, releasing five studio albums: <i>Once</i> (1990), <i><a href="/wiki/Death_or_Glory%3F_(album)" title="Death or Glory? (album)">Death or Glory?</a></i> (1992), <i><a href="/wiki/Commercial_Breaks_(album)" title="Commercial Breaks (album)">Commercial Breaks</a></i> (1994), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dream_Society" title="The Dream Society">The Dream Society</a></i> (1998), a collection of poetry and <a href="/wiki/Spoken_word" title="Spoken word">spoken word</a> tracks <i><a href="/wiki/Poems,_Speeches,_Thoughts_and_Doodles" title="Poems, Speeches, Thoughts and Doodles">Poems, Speeches, Thoughts and Doodles</a></i> (1997); two live albums: <i><a href="/wiki/Unhinged_(album)" title="Unhinged (album)">Unhinged</a></i> (1993) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_At_Les_Cousins" title="Live At Les Cousins">Live At Les Cousins</a></i> (1996; recorded in 1969) and six individual CDs of live concerts and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recording_session" title="Recording session">sessions</a> recorded by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> (1997). Two official <a href="/wiki/Compact_Cassette" title="Compact Cassette">C90 cassette tapes</a> of concerts at the Red Lion in Birmingham (1984 &amp; 1985) were made available from Harper's agency (Acorn Entertainments).</p>
<p>In addition, Harper released a live <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_cassette" title="Video cassette">video</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Once_(Roy_Harper_album)#Video" title="Once (Roy Harper album)">Once</a></i> (1990), an <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Burn_the_World" title="Burn the World">Burn the World</a></i> (1990), a 4-track CD single <i>Death or Glory?</i> (1992), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limited_edition" title="Limited edition">limited edition</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_album" title="Live album">live</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_cassette" title="Compact cassette">cassette</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Born_in_Captivity_II" title="Born in Captivity II">Born in Captivity II</a></i> (1992) (featuring cricketer <a href="/wiki/Graeme_Fowler" title="Graeme Fowler">Graeme Fowler</a> and a cricket poem written by Harper: "<a href="/wiki/Three_Hundred_Words" title="Three Hundred Words">Three Hundred Words</a>"), a <a href="/wiki/Compilation_album" title="Compilation album">compilation album</a> <i><a href="/wiki/An_Introduction_to_....." title="An Introduction to .....">An Introduction to .....</a></i> (1994), and a <a href="/wiki/Reissue" title="Reissue">reissue</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Descendants_of_Smith" title="Descendants of Smith">Descendants of Smith</a></i> (his 1988 release) renamed <i><a href="/wiki/Garden_of_Uranium" title="Garden of Uranium">Garden of Uranium</a></i> (1994).</p>
<p>Once again Harper collaborated with <a href="/wiki/David_Gilmour" title="David Gilmour">David Gilmour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kate_Bush" title="Kate Bush">Kate Bush</a> on his 1990 release, <i>Once</i>. The album also featured contributions from <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Mazlyn_Jones" title="Nigel Mazlyn Jones">Nigel Mazlyn Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Feltham_(musician)" title="Mark Feltham (musician)">Mark Feltham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tony_Franklin_(musician)" title="Tony Franklin (musician)">Tony Franklin</a>. One of the album tracks, "The Black Cloud of Islam" a song written about Colonel Gaddafi, <a href="/wiki/Pan_Am_Flight_103" title="Pan Am Flight 103">the Lockerbie bombing</a>, and a despairing castigation of <a href="/wiki/Islamic_fundamentalism" title="Islamic fundamentalism">radical Islam</a>, provoked criticism from some of Harper's fans at the time. Whilst religion, Harper's "first and only enemy"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cloudy_Days_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cloudy_Days-36">[36]</a></sup> has always been a recurring theme in his music,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> he was '<a href="/wiki/Penalty_card" title="Penalty card">red-carded</a> by a lot of his 1990 following... who left in substantial numbers'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cloudy_Days_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cloudy_Days-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992, his second marriage ended and Harper released <i>Death or Glory?</i> an album that (upon its original release) contained a number of songs and <a href="/wiki/Spoken_word" title="Spoken word">spoken word</a> pieces referencing his loss and pain. "She ran off with someone else" said Harper, "a violin player (<a href="/wiki/Nigel_Kennedy" title="Nigel Kennedy">Nigel Kennedy</a>) I’d been working on an adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Brahms)" title="Violin Concerto (Brahms)">Brahms's Violin Concerto</a> with. I was really traumatised by that. Anybody who's been suddenly left like that will know it's very, very traumatic. I managed to come out of it, but it took about five years. It was like a death, a loss, like being told your child's been killed in a war. There's no other way to describe it. When you go through that, it changes your life forever, there's no point in not admitting it. I withdrew, retreated, became an exile".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout the decade, Harper's musical influence began to be recognised by a younger generation of musicians, some of whom <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">covered</a> his songs or invited him to make guest appearances on their albums. In 1995 Harper contributed <a href="/wiki/Spoken_word" title="Spoken word">spoken words</a> on <a href="/wiki/The_Tea_Party" title="The Tea Party">the Tea Party</a>'s 1995 album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Edges_of_Twilight" title="The Edges of Twilight">The Edges of Twilight</a></i>, and appeared on stage for their New Year concert in Montreal. In 1996 Roy recited "Bad Speech" from his album <i><a href="/wiki/Whatever_Happened_to_Jugula%3F" title="Whatever Happened to Jugula?">Whatever Happened to Jugula?</a></i> on <a href="/wiki/Anathema_(band)" title="Anathema (band)">Anathemas</a> album <i><a href="/wiki/Eternity_(Anathema_album)" title="Eternity (Anathema album)">Eternity</a></i> (the album also contains a <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">cover version</a> of "Hope" from the same album). The track "Time" from The Tea Party's 1996 <a href="/wiki/Multimedia" title="Multimedia">multimedia CD</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alhambra_(album)" title="Alhambra (album)">Alhambra</a></i>, was sung and co-written by Harper.</p>
<p>Harper contributed his version of <a href="/wiki/Jethro_Tull_(band)" title="Jethro Tull (band)">Jethro Tull's</a> song, "Up the 'Pool" (from <i><a href="/wiki/Living_in_the_Past_(album)" title="Living in the Past (album)">Living in the Past</a></i>) for the 1996 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tribute_album" title="Tribute album">tribute album</a>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=To_Cry_You_A_Song_%E2%80%93_A_Collection_Of_Tull_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="To Cry You A Song – A Collection Of Tull Tales (page does not exist)">To Cry You A Song – A Collection Of Tull Tales</a></i>, a version Anderson liked so much, he began to perform the <i>"forgotten piece"</i> again in concert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1998, Jethro Tull singer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ian_Anderson_(musician)" title="Ian Anderson (musician)">Ian Anderson</a> contributed <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flute</a> to the song, "These Fifty Years" on Harper's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dream_Society" title="The Dream Society">The Dream Society</a></i>, an album based on emotional, philosophical and actual events in Harper's life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Views of procreation, his mother's continued presence in him and something of his psychological impulses are punctuated by a couple of moments of satire,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> a love song and a lament, followed by the lengthy "These Fifty years", of which he has said, <i>"In some ways its (anti-organised religion) theme is similar to 'The Same Old Rock', but in many others I think it's stronger"</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HarperLtd2003_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-HarperLtd2003-42">[42]</a></sup> Reportedly, Anderson said that the only reason he originally left Blackpool was because Harper did.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biog_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-biog-43">[43]</a></sup> Other artists who <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">covered</a> Harper's songs (or songs on his albums) throughout the decade include Dean Carter, Ava Cherry &amp; The Astronettes, Green Crown, The Kitchen Cynics, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Levellers_(band)" title="The Levellers (band)">the Levellers</a>, Roydan Styles and <a href="/wiki/Pete_Townshend" title="Pete Townshend">Pete Townshend</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Harper also undertook a short tour of the US, where some performances were supported by <a href="/wiki/Daevid_Allen" title="Daevid Allen">Daevid Allen</a>, former <a href="/wiki/Soft_Machine" title="Soft Machine">Soft Machine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gong_(band)" title="Gong (band)">Gong</a> band member.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000.E2.80.9310:_Into_the_new_millennium">2000–10: Into the new millennium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2000–10: Into the new millennium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, Harper released an almost entirely acoustic album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Green_Man_(album)" title="The Green Man (album)">The Green Man</a></i>, accompanied by <a href="/wiki/The_Tea_Party" title="The Tea Party">The Tea Party</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Martin_(Canadian_musician)" title="Jeff Martin (Canadian musician)">Jeff Martin</a> on guitar, <a href="/wiki/Hurdy-gurdy" title="Hurdy-gurdy">hurdy-gurdy</a> and numerous other instruments. The following year (2001) Harper celebrated his 60th birthday with a concert performance at London's <a href="/wiki/Royal_Festival_Hall" title="Royal Festival Hall">Royal Festival Hall</a> and was joined by numerous guest artists including; <a href="/wiki/David_Bedford" title="David Bedford">David Bedford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nick_Harper" title="Nick Harper">Nick Harper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Martin_(Canadian_musician)" title="Jeff Martin (Canadian musician)">Jeff Martin</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Renbourn" title="John Renbourn">John Renbourn</a>. The concert was recorded and released shortly after as a double CD, <i><a href="/wiki/Royal_Festival_Hall_Live_%E2%80%93_June_10th_2001" title="Royal Festival Hall Live – June 10th 2001">Royal Festival Hall Live – June 10th 2001</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 2003, Harper published <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Passions_of_Great_Fortune&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Passions of Great Fortune (page does not exist)">The Passions of Great Fortune</a></i>, a large format book containing all the lyrics to his albums (and singles) to date, it also contained a wealth of photographs and commentary on his songs.</p>
<p>Harper released his second CD single in April 2005; "<a href="/wiki/The_Death_of_God_(song)" title="The Death of God (song)">The Death of God</a>". The 13 minute song, a critique of the <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">war in Iraq</a>, featured guest guitarist Matt Churchill (who also performed live with Harper during this period). A video of the track, intermixing animation with a live performance, is <a class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=x6j0IJvD8bQ" rel="nofollow"><span class="plainlinks">available in four parts</span></a> on <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a>. 2003 also saw the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Counter_Culture_(album)" title="Counter Culture (album)">Counter Culture</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Double_album" title="Double album">double</a> <a href="/wiki/Compilation_album" title="Compilation album">compilation album</a> featuring songs from Harper's 35 year songwriting period. <i>Counter Culture</i> received a five-star review from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNCUT_(magazine)" title="UNCUT (magazine)">UNCUT</a></i> magazine. Harper also contributed a recital of "<a href="/wiki/Jabberwocky" title="Jabberwocky">Jabberwocky</a>" for <i>The Wildlife Album</i>, an 18-track compilation CD to benefit the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_For_Nature" title="World Wide Fund For Nature">World Wide Fund For Nature</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a> <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">Wildlife</a> Trust.</p>

<p>2005 saw Harper release his first DVD, <i><a href="/wiki/Beyond_the_Door_(DVD)" title="Beyond the Door (DVD)">Beyond the Door</a></i>. Composed of live footage recorded in 2004 at Irish <a href="/wiki/Folk_club" title="Folk club">folk club</a> "De Barra's" in <a href="/wiki/Clonakilty" title="Clonakilty">Clonakilty</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cork_(Ireland)" title="Cork (Ireland)">Cork</a> and "<a href="/wiki/The_Death_of_God_(song)" title="The Death of God (song)">The Death of God</a>" video. The package also includes an additional 10-track audio CD and received a 4-star review from <i><a href="/wiki/Mojo_(magazine)" title="Mojo (magazine)">Mojo</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNCUT_(magazine)" title="UNCUT (magazine)">UNCUT</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Classic_Rock_(magazine)" title="Classic Rock (magazine)">Classic Rock</a></i> magazine, who made it their "DVD of the month".</p>
<p>In September 2007, Harper supported <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">Californian</a> harpist <a href="/wiki/Joanna_Newsom" title="Joanna Newsom">Joanna Newsom</a> at her <a href="/wiki/Royal_Albert_Hall" title="Royal Albert Hall">Royal Albert Hall</a> performance. Newsom, impressed by Harper's 1971 album <i>Stormcock</i> found it served as an inspiration for her similarly expansive second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Ys_(Joanna_Newsom_album)" title="Ys (Joanna Newsom album)">Ys</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popmatters_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-popmatters-6">[6]</a></sup> During his Royal Albert Hall appearance with Newsom, Harper played <i>Stormcock</i> in its entirety. At the time, Harper made an announcement on his website that he was <i>"...taking a break from the live scene... retired from gigging..."</i> and just wanted <i>"...the time and space to write..."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>During this period, Harper dedicated his time to collecting and compiling his life's work in various formats. One of the intended projects was to be the making of a documentary DVD to round off this process. However, as of 2016 this remains unreleased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biog_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-biog-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, plans were announced for a Roy Harper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tribute_album" title="Tribute album">tribute album</a>. The album, <i>What You Need Is What You Have, The Songs of Roy Harper</i> was being compiled by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laurel_Canyon,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="Laurel Canyon, Los Angeles, California">Laurel Canyon</a> <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk</a> singer, musician and producer <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Wilson_(musician)" title="Jonathan Wilson (musician)">Jonathan Wilson</a>, and was to feature <a href="/wiki/Chris_Robinson_(singer)" title="Chris Robinson (singer)">Chris Robinson</a> (<a href="/wiki/The_Black_Crowes" title="The Black Crowes">the Black Crowes</a>), <a href="/wiki/Gary_Louris" title="Gary Louris">Gary Louris</a> (<a href="/wiki/The_Jayhawks" title="The Jayhawks">the Jayhawks</a>), <a href="/wiki/Johnathan_Rice" title="Johnathan Rice">Johnathan Rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Johnson" title="Eric Johnson">Eric Johnson</a> (<a href="/wiki/Fruit_Bats_(band)" title="Fruit Bats (band)">Fruit Bats</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Shins" title="The Shins">the Shins</a>), <a href="/wiki/Benji_Hughes" title="Benji Hughes">Benji Hughes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Will_Oldham" title="Will Oldham">Will Oldham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andy_Cabic" title="Andy Cabic">Andy Cabic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dawes_(band)" title="Dawes (band)">Dawes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jenny_O." title="Jenny O.">Jenny O.</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Josh_Tillman" title="Josh Tillman">Josh Tillman</a> and others. At present, the collection remains unfinished, having been delayed beyond its planned 2009 release date. Six of the tracks can be heard on the project's Myspace page.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, Newsom once again invited Harper to guest for her on several of her European Tour Dates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Plans for Harper to star as Rodriguez El Toro in the film <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rebel_City_Rumble&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rebel City Rumble (page does not exist)">Rebel City Rumble</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imdb.com_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-imdb.com-50">[50]</a></sup> were also announced. As of 2016 the project is still categorised as 'in development'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011.E2.80.93present:_Man_and_Myth">2011–present: <i>Man and Myth</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2011–present: Man and Myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 2 April 2011, Roy Harper played a concert for a small audience at <a href="/wiki/Metropolis_Group#Studios" title="Metropolis Group">Metropolis Studios</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">ITV</a> Legends series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The concert was recorded on video and released on DVD as <i>Classic Rock Legends: Roy Harper - Live In Concert At Metropolis Studios</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The package also contains an audio CD of the concert. Through the summer of 2011, Harper made a number of appearances on broadcast media. On 24 July 2011, Harper appeared as the lunchtime guest on the British cricketing radio programme, <a href="/wiki/Test_Match_Special" title="Test Match Special">Test Match Special</a>. During the show Harper was interviewed and also performed "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/When_An_Old_Cricketer_Leaves_The_Crease" title="When An Old Cricketer Leaves The Crease">When An Old Cricketer Leaves The Crease</a>". A televised interview followed on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Breakfast" title="BBC Breakfast">BBC Breakfast</a> on the 19 September 2011, and Harper was also interviewed by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Elms" title="Robert Elms">Robert Elms</a> on his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC_London_94.9" title="BBC London 94.9">BBC London 94.9</a> show on the 20 September 2011. During the show, Harper performed "<a href="/wiki/Another_Day_(Roy_Harper_song)" title="Another Day (Roy Harper song)">Another Day</a>" (a song from his 1970 album <i>Flat Baroque and Berserk</i>) live in the studio. A further interview took place on <a href="/wiki/Mike_Harding" title="Mike Harding">Mike Hardings</a> <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2" title="BBC Radio 2">BBC Radio 2</a> show on the 21 September 2011.</p>
<p>On 23 September Harper was interviewed on <a href="/wiki/Later..._with_Jools_Holland" title="Later... with Jools Holland">Later... with Jools Holland</a>. A segment of Harper performing "Commune" (from his 1974 album <i>Valentine</i>) on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Old_Grey_Whistle_Test" title="The Old Grey Whistle Test">The Old Grey Whistle Test</a></i> in 1974 was shown. Harper performed "Another Day", an abbreviated version of "I Hate The White Man" (from his 1970 album <i>Flat Baroque and Berserk</i>) and "The Green Man" (as part of a web exclusive performance).</p>
<p>The media appearances were to promote the release of a new compilation album, <i><a href="/wiki/Songs_of_Love_and_Loss" title="Songs of Love and Loss">Songs of Love and Loss</a></i>, a compilation of Harper's love songs released as an introduction to the digital release of 19 of Harper's albums for the first time. The digital catalogue was to be released in batches of four over the forthcoming months. The album (and most of Harper's back catalogue) remain available to download on Harper's website in <a href="/wiki/FLAC" title="FLAC">FLAC</a> and <a href="/wiki/MP3" title="MP3">MP3</a> formats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 5 November 2011 Harper returned to London's <a href="/wiki/Royal_Festival_Hall" title="Royal Festival Hall">Royal Festival Hall</a> to celebrate his 70th birthday and perform once again with special guests Jonathan Wilson,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nick_Harper" title="Nick Harper">Nick Harper</a>, Joanna Newsom and Jimmy Page. The performance was described in the media as "...an evening of devastating musical brilliance..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> and an "...historic concert".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, the Press Photographers Association of Ireland awarded third place in the Portraits section of their annual competition to a <a href="/wiki/Portrait_photography" title="Portrait photography">photographic portrait</a> of Harper by photographer Alan Place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2012, plans to release a new album, Harper's first studio release of newly recorded material for 13 years, were confirmed. The album, <i><a href="/wiki/Man_and_Myth" title="Man and Myth">Man and Myth</a></i>, featured contributions from <a href="/wiki/Pete_Townshend" title="Pete Townshend">Pete Townshend</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Wilson_(musician)" title="Jonathan Wilson (musician)">Jonathan Wilson</a>, and was released 23 September 2013.</p>
<p>Harper performed live through August 2013, playing at Debarras Folk Club in <a href="/wiki/Clonakilty" title="Clonakilty">Clonakilty</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Cork" title="West Cork">West Cork</a>, Ireland (11 August), at the <a href="/wiki/Green_Man_Festival" title="Green Man Festival">Green Man Festival</a> in Glanusk, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> (17 August) and at <a href="/wiki/Beautiful_Days_(festival)" title="Beautiful Days (festival)">Beautiful Days</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Escot_Park" title="Escot Park">Escot Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> (18 August).</p>
<p>During this period Harper was interviewed by Laura Rawlings on her <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_Bristol" title="BBC Radio Bristol">BBC Radio Bristol</a> show on 15 August 2013, and also by Rob Hughes of the <a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">Telegraph Online</a> who declared "Roy Harper has spent the past five decades crafting some of the most vivid, ravishingly beautiful music of our times...".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Harper also performed at an in-store performance at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rough_Trade_Shops" title="Rough Trade Shops">Rough Trade East</a>, London. A limited number of tickets were available to those who purchased the album in store that day, and the event was also streamed live to a limited number of fans who had pre-ordered the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 25 September <a href="/wiki/Sky_Arts" title="Sky Arts">Sky Arts</a> broadcast "Roy Harper: Man &amp; Myth – The Documentary". An exclusive documentary film, shot mainly at Harper's home in Ireland, it traced Harper's career and examined his output. Included were interviews with fellow musicians Jimmy Page, Robert Plant and Johnny Marr.</p>
<p>In support of <i>Man and Myth's</i> release, Harper undertook a short, three-date, UK tour, performing at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Festival_Hall" title="Royal Festival Hall">Royal Festival Hall</a>, London (22 October), the <a href="/wiki/Bridgewater_Hall" title="Bridgewater Hall">Bridgewater Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> (25 October) and the <a href="/wiki/Colston_Hall" title="Colston Hall">Colston Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a> (27 October). At each performance he was accompanied by Jonathan Wilson and supported by a <a href="/wiki/String_instrument" title="String instrument">string</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brass_instrument" title="Brass instrument">brass</a> ensemble.</p>
<p>In November 2013, <a href="/wiki/Uncut_(magazine)" title="Uncut (magazine)">Uncut</a> placed <i>Man &amp; Myth</i> at 6 in their top 50 Albums of 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mojo_(magazine)" title="Mojo (magazine)">Mojo</a> also placed <i>Man and Myth</i> at 39 in their list of the top 50 Albums of 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2016, to celebrate his 75th Birthday, Harper announced four concerts in September with string and brass ensemble. The shows are planned for Birmingham, Manchester, London and Edinburgh.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/HQ_(album)" title="HQ (album)">HQ</a></i> was awarded Record of the Year in Portugal in 1975. That year Harper also received a similar award in Finland for the same record.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Work_of_Heart" title="Work of Heart">Work of Heart</a></i> was named <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times_(UK)" title="The Sunday Times (UK)">The Sunday Times</a></i> Album of the Year in 1982.</p>
<p>Harper was given the <a href="/wiki/MOJO_Awards" title="MOJO Awards">MOJO</a> <a href="/wiki/2005_MOJO_Awards" title="2005 MOJO Awards">Hero Award</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> by the staff of <i><a href="/wiki/Mojo_(magazine)" title="Mojo (magazine)">Mojo</a></i> magazine on 16 June 2005 at the Porchester Hall, <a href="/wiki/Central_London" title="Central London">Central London</a>. The award itself was presented by longtime collaborator and friend, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Page" title="Jimmy Page">Jimmy Page</a> and now hangs upon the wall at De Barras Folk Club in <a href="/wiki/Clonakilty" title="Clonakilty">Clonakilty</a>, Ireland.</p>
<p>On 30 January 2013, Harper was awarded a Lifetime Achievement Award at the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2_Folk_Awards" title="BBC Radio 2 Folk Awards">BBC Radio 2 Folk Awards</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Royal_Concert_Hall" title="Glasgow Royal Concert Hall">Glasgow Royal Concert Hall</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nick_Harper">Nick Harper</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Nick Harper">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of Harper's sons, <a href="/wiki/Nick_Harper" title="Nick Harper">Nick Harper</a>, is a <a href="/wiki/Singer-songwriter" title="Singer-songwriter">singer-songwriter</a>. He has occasionally toured and recorded with his father and appeared as a guitarist on a number of his albums since 1985.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Court_case">Court case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Court case">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 15 November 2013, Harper was charged with alleged historic sexual abuse. In response, Harper posted on his <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> page and <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> feed that he "...vigorously denies the allegations that have been made against him and looks forward to clearing his name."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> The trial started on 20 January 2015 and on 5 February 2015 Harper was unanimously acquitted by a jury of indecent assault.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The following day, Harper was cleared of a second charge of indecent assault, and the jury at Worcester Crown Court was discharged after failing to reach verdicts on the remaining charges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> According to the BBC, the judge Robert Juckes QC then gave the prosecution two weeks to consider whether they wished to seek a re-trial on the undecided charges,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> but it wasn't until 9 November 2015 that the prosecution announced that they were dropping all allegations of sexual abuse against Harper and the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Prosecution_Service" title="Crown Prosecution Service">Crown Prosecution Service</a> would offer no evidence against him following a review by the <a href="/wiki/Director_of_Public_Prosecutions_(England_and_Wales)" title="Director of Public Prosecutions (England and Wales)">Director of Public Prosecutions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alison_Saunders" title="Alison Saunders">Alison Saunders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>Outside court, in a statement issued by his legal team,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Harper said he had always maintained his innocence and that:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>the case should never have gone as far as this, or taken so long to resolve. The psychological and personal cost to my wife and myself has been enormous and the financial cost hugely unfair. I lost my livelihood and I spent my savings... and more, on my defence... I thank my lawyers for standing by me and working so hard to show the truth. Despite coming out of this without a blemish on my name, I cannot recoup my costs and that's left me incredibly angry. I’m now going to restart my working life where I left off nearly three years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
