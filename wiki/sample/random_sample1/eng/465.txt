<p>On 26 February 2013, at 07:00 <a href="/wiki/Egypt_Standard_Time" title="Egypt Standard Time">Egypt Standard Time</a> (05:00 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a>), a <a href="/wiki/Hot_air_balloon" title="Hot air balloon">hot air balloon</a> crashed near <a href="/wiki/Luxor" title="Luxor">Luxor</a>, Egypt. The crash resulted in 19 deaths out of 21 passengers – 18 on-site and one in hospital hours later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-RT-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-3">[3]</a></sup> It is the deadliest ballooning accident in history and the deadliest <a href="/wiki/Aerostat" title="Aerostat">aerostat</a> disaster since the <a href="/wiki/Hindenburg_disaster" title="Hindenburg disaster">Hindenburg disaster</a> in 1937 which killed 36 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_News_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-US_News-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2013_Luxor_hot_air_balloon_crash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tourism_in_Egypt" title="Tourism in Egypt">Tourism in Egypt</a></div>
<p>Hot air balloons are commonly used in Egypt to provide tourists with an aerial view of the country's landscape and famous landmarks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-6">[6]</a></sup> In Luxor, such rides offer views of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_River" title="Nile River">Nile River</a>, the temple of <a href="/wiki/Karnak" title="Karnak">Karnak</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Valley_of_the_Kings" title="Valley of the Kings">Valley of the Kings</a>, among other attractions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-7">[7]</a></sup> Concerns over passenger safety have been raised from time to time, with multiple crashes reported in 2007, 2008 and 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2009, 16 people were hurt when a balloon crashed during a tour of Luxor. After the crash, flights were grounded for six months while safety measures were improved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-3">[3]</a></sup> Pilot training was increased and balloons were given a designated landing zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-7">[7]</a></sup> Following the toppling of <a href="/wiki/Hosni_Mubarak" title="Hosni Mubarak">Hosni Mubarak</a> in 2011, the rule of law has largely been ignored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sky Cruise, the operator of the balloon, had suffered a previous accident in October 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The company has stated that it is properly insured and prepared to compensate victims' families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-angered_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-angered-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crash">Crash</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2013_Luxor_hot_air_balloon_crash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Crash">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early on 26 February, an <a href="/wiki/Ultramagic" title="Ultramagic">Ultramagic</a> N-425 balloon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-N425_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-N425-11">[11]</a></sup> registration SU-283,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ahram_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ahram-12">[12]</a></sup> operated by Sky Cruise departed on a sight-seeing flight carrying twenty passengers and a pilot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WaPost_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-WaPost-13">[13]</a></sup> According to a nearby balloon pilot, Mohamed Youssef, a fire started in the Sky Cruise balloon a few meters off the ground as it was attempting to land, as a result of a leaking fuel line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-What-REALLY-happened_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-What-REALLY-happened-14">[14]</a></sup> As the fire engulfed the basket, the pilot and one passenger leaped to safety as the craft rose rapidly aided by a wind gust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WaPost_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-WaPost-13">[13]</a></sup> As the balloon rose, approximately seven passengers jumped to their deaths to escape the fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-RT-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-3">[3]</a></sup> At an altitude of approximately 300 meters (980 ft), there was an explosion which could be heard several kilometers away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-3">[3]</a></sup> The balloon and remaining passengers plunged to the ground, killing everyone remaining on board. One eyewitness remarked that he heard "a huge bang. It was a frightening bang, even though it was several kilometers away" from his location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-RT-2">[2]</a></sup> Youssef said it appeared that a gas leak in one of the balloon's tanks caused the fire and resulting explosion, consistent with information reported in state-run media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-6">[6]</a></sup> Earlier reports had indicated that the balloon may have contacted a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_line" title="Power line">power line</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two minutes later, the burning craft crashed into a sugar cane field west of Luxor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP-15">[15]</a></sup> A second explosion was reported 15 seconds later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-3">[3]</a></sup> Ambulances arrived on the scene after 15 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Bodies were scattered across the field when rescue workers arrived on the scene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-7">[7]</a></sup> The balloon's final moments were caught on amateur video.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Casualties">Casualties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2013_Luxor_hot_air_balloon_crash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Casualties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the time of its ascent, the balloon carried 20 passengers and Momin Murad, the balloon's Egyptian pilot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-angered_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-angered-10">[10]</a></sup> Nineteen of the passengers were tourists: nine from Hong Kong, four from Japan, three from Britain, two from France, and one from Hungary; the 20th passenger was an Egyptian tour guide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Egypt_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Egypt-17">[17]</a></sup> Of the Hong Kongers, five were women and four were men. They were members of three families on a tour group organized by <a href="/wiki/Kuoni_Travel" title="Kuoni Travel">Kuoni Travel</a>. The Japanese victims were two couples from Tokyo in their 60s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HongKongeffects_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-HongKongeffects-18">[18]</a></sup> They were on a ten-day tour of Egypt organized by <a href="/wiki/JTB_Corporation" title="JTB Corporation">JTB Corporation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The three Britons and the Hungarian-born passenger, a resident of the UK, were on a tour organized by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cook_Group" title="Thomas Cook Group">Thomas Cook Group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeachHorror_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-BeachHorror-20">[20]</a></sup> The French victims were a 48-year-old woman and her 14-year-old daughter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The accident killed 18 of the passengers on site; the pilot and two passengers survived the initial crash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-22">[22]</a></sup> The two surviving passengers, both British men, were rushed to hospital in critical condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup> One of them died after five hours of surgery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-8">[8]</a></sup> Dr. Mohammad Abdullah, the head of the emergency ward of the Luxor hospital, said that the Briton who died in the hospital had probably suffered a 50-meter (160 ft) fall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeachHorror_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-BeachHorror-20">[20]</a></sup> The surviving Briton was described as being in critical but stable condition, while the pilot was said to be conscious and talking, but with burns covering 70% of his body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-RT-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-3">[3]</a></sup> Doctors at the Luxor International Hospital said that many of the dead suffered severe internal injuries and severe burns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeachHorror_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-BeachHorror-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2013_Luxor_hot_air_balloon_crash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After news of the tragedy broke, <a href="/wiki/Luxor_Governorate" title="Luxor Governorate">Governor of Luxor</a> <a href="/wiki/Ezzat_Saad" title="Ezzat Saad">Ezzat Saad</a> banned hot air balloon flights in his jurisdiction until further notice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-6">[6]</a></sup> Egypt's <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Civil_Aviation_(Egypt)" title="Ministry of Civil Aviation (Egypt)">civil aviation minister</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wael_el-Maadawi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wael el-Maadawi (page does not exist)">Wael el-Maadawi</a>, followed by suspending balloon flights nationwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-3">[3]</a></sup> In a statement, <a href="/wiki/President_of_Egypt" title="President of Egypt">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Mohamed_Morsi" title="Mohamed Morsi">Mohamed Morsi</a> expressed his "deepest condolences and sympathy for the families of those who lost their lives in this tragic incident."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WaPost_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-WaPost-13">[13]</a></sup> National government spokesman Alaa Hadidi said a committee would be formed to investigate the accident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bodies of the victims were transported to four hospitals in <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>. Republic of China within the Arab Republic of Egypt consular officials and Hong Kong Immigration Department officers were scheduled to travel with the family members of the Hong Kong victims to Cairo and help family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Kuoni Travel, the Hong Kong travel agency that organized the tour attended by the Hong Kong passengers, made plans for the six tour members who did not take the balloon ride to leave Egypt. The tour agency stated that, in addition to the US$7000 per person stipulated by contract, additional compensation would be given.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HongKongeffects_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-HongKongeffects-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mohammed Osman, head of the Luxor's Tourism Chamber, accused civil aviation authorities of lowering standards prior to the accident. "I don't want to blame the revolution for everything, but the laxness started with the revolution," he said. "These people are not doing their job, they are not checking the balloons and they just issue the licenses without inspection."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-7">[7]</a></sup> National authorities were quick to deny the allegations, noting that the balloon had recently been inspected. They also said the pilot should have shut off gas valves and attempted to put out the fire instead of bailing, and thus may have contributed to the tragedy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-7">[7]</a></sup> An anonymous civil aviation ministry official acknowledged to the press that standards had been weakened by the current regime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-7">[7]</a></sup> The pilot's license had been renewed one month prior to the accident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Local and foreign media analysts speculated that the crash would hurt Egypt's already weakened <a href="/wiki/Tourism_in_Egypt" title="Tourism in Egypt">tourism industry</a>, which was down 22% from 2010 levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_3-11"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Egypt_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Egypt-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Precise_language" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="What effect did it have?">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> Wael Ibrahim, who oversees the tourism syndicate in Luxor, did not expect the accident to worsen the situation since tourism was already down so much. "This (type of) accident could happen anywhere in the world", he remarked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-22">[22]</a></sup> A local balloon operator, angered by the industry shutdown, remarked: "Why the mass punishment? Do you stop all flights when you have a plane crash? ... You will cut the livelihoods for nearly 3,000 human beings who live on this kind of tourism."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-7">[7]</a></sup> Angered by the industry-wide shutdown, tourism workers threatened to organize protests on 2 March.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-angered_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-angered-10">[10]</a></sup> The same day, Saad admitted that pressure to resume balloon flights was mounting and promised that downtime would be less than a month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-angered_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-angered-10">[10]</a></sup> Hot air balloon rides at Luxor were set to resume in April, according to a statement on the Egyptian Civil Aviation Regulatory Commission's (CARC) website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Results_of_investigation">Results of investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2013_Luxor_hot_air_balloon_crash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Results of investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Preliminary results of the government investigation ruled out criminal conduct as the cause of the crash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-angered_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-angered-10">[10]</a></sup> On 2 March, Luxor-area balloon pilots held a press conference to defend Egypt's safety protocol and their colleague's actions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-angered_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-angered-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Egyptian Civil Aviation Authority released its final report of the crash on 7 January 2014. The 219-page report blamed a leak in a fuel line connected to the balloon’s burner. The aging line had been in use since 2005 and sprung a leak, which ignited as the balloon came in to land. The fire severely injured the pilot, who jumped or fell from the basket. Some of the ground crew released the ground line in order to attend to the pilot, so that the remaining crew could not keep the balloon near the ground. The flaming balloon rose rapidly and uncontrolled, then exploded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-What-REALLY-happened_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-What-REALLY-happened-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The balloon’s pilot and a maintenance engineer were arrested for negligence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-What-REALLY-happened_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-What-REALLY-happened-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
