<p><b>Roger William Corman</b> (born April 5, 1926)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ows_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ows-2">[2]</a></sup> is an American independent film producer, director, and actor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMDb_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMDb-3">[3]</a></sup> He has been called "The Pope of Pop Cinema" and is known as a trailblazer in the world of independent film. Much of Corman's work has an established critical reputation, such as his cycle of low budget cult films adapted from the tales of <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olsen_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-olsen-4">[4]</a></sup> Admired by members of the <a href="/wiki/French_New_Wave" title="French New Wave">French New Wave</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Cahiers_du_cin%C3%A9ma" title="Cahiers du cinéma">Cahiers du cinéma</a></i>, in 1964 Corman was the youngest filmmaker to have a retrospective at the <a href="/wiki/Cin%C3%A9math%C3%A8que_Fran%C3%A7aise" title="Cinémathèque Française">Cinémathèque Française</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> as well as the British Film Institute and the Museum of Modern Art. In 2009, he was awarded an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honorary_Academy_Award" title="Honorary Academy Award">Honorary Academy Award</a>.</p>
<p>Corman mentored and gave a start to many young film directors such as <a href="/wiki/Francis_Ford_Coppola" title="Francis Ford Coppola">Francis Ford Coppola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ron_Howard" title="Ron Howard">Ron Howard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">Martin Scorsese</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">James Cameron</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_Review_-_Roger_Corman:_Hollywood.27s_Wild_Angel_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Review_-_Roger_Corman:_Hollywood.27s_Wild_Angel-6">[6]</a></sup> He also helped to launch the careers of actors <a href="/wiki/Peter_Fonda" title="Peter Fonda">Peter Fonda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Nicholson" title="Jack Nicholson">Jack Nicholson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_Review_-_Roger_Corman:_Hollywood.27s_Wild_Angel_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Review_-_Roger_Corman:_Hollywood.27s_Wild_Angel-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Corman has occasionally taken minor acting roles in the films of directors who started with him, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Silence_of_the_Lambs_(film)" title="The Silence of the Lambs (film)">The Silence of the Lambs</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Godfather_Part_II" title="The Godfather Part II">The Godfather Part II</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Apollo_13_(film)" title="Apollo 13 (film)">Apollo 13</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Manchurian_Candidate_(2004_film)" title="The Manchurian Candidate (2004 film)">The Manchurian Candidate</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_(film)" title="Philadelphia (film)">Philadelphia</a></i>.</p>
<p>A documentary about Corman's life and career entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Corman%27s_World:_Exploits_of_a_Hollywood_Rebel" title="Corman's World: Exploits of a Hollywood Rebel">Corman's World: Exploits of a Hollywood Rebel</a></i>, directed by Alex Stapleton, premiered at the Sundance and Cannes Film Festivals in 2011. The film's TV rights were picked up by A&amp;E IndieFilms after a well-received screening at Sundance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcn_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcn-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Corman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Corman was born in <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>, the son of Anne (née High) and William Corman, an engineer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-owe_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-owe-9">[9]</a></sup> His younger brother, <a href="/wiki/Gene_Corman" title="Gene Corman">Eugene Harold "Gene" Corman</a>, has also produced numerous films, sometimes in collaboration with Roger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-owe_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-owe-9">[9]</a></sup> Corman and his brother were baptized in their mother's Catholic faith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Corman went to <a href="/wiki/Beverly_Hills_High_School" title="Beverly Hills High School">Beverly Hills High School</a> and then to <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> to study Industrial Engineering. While at Stanford, Corman enlisted in the <a href="/wiki/V-12_Navy_College_Training_Program" title="V-12 Navy College Training Program">V-12 Navy College Training Program</a>. After the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Corman returned to Stanford and received a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> degree in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_Engineering" title="Industrial Engineering">Industrial Engineering</a> in 1947.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STANFORDALUMNIMAGAZINE_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-STANFORDALUMNIMAGAZINE-1">[1]</a></sup> While at Stanford University, Roger Corman was initiated in the fraternity <a href="/wiki/Sigma_Alpha_Epsilon" title="Sigma Alpha Epsilon">Sigma Alpha Epsilon</a>. In 1948, he worked briefly at U.S. Electrical Motors on <a href="/wiki/Slauson_Avenue" title="Slauson Avenue">Slauson Avenue</a> in Los Angeles, but his career in engineering lasted only four days; he began work on Monday and quit on Thursday, telling his boss "I've made a terrible mistake."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>More interested in film, Corman found work at <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> initially in the mail room. He worked his way up to a story reader. The one property that he liked the most and provided ideas for was filmed as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Gunfighter_(film)" title="The Gunfighter (film)">The Gunfighter</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Peck" title="Gregory Peck">Gregory Peck</a>. When Corman received no credit at all he left Fox and decided he would work in film by himself. Under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GI_Bill" title="GI Bill">GI Bill</a>, Corman studied English Literature at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_University" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a>. He then returned to Los Angeles, beginning his film career in 1953 as a producer and screenwriter, then started directing films in 1955.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Corman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Roger_Corman_crop.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1442" data-file-width="1180" height="269" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Roger_Corman_crop.jpg/220px-Roger_Corman_crop.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Roger_Corman_crop.jpg/330px-Roger_Corman_crop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Roger_Corman_crop.jpg/440px-Roger_Corman_crop.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Roger_Corman_crop.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Corman in 2006</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Corman began to direct films in the mid-1950s, including <i><a href="/wiki/Swamp_Women" title="Swamp Women">Swamp Women</a></i> (1955). In his early period, he produced up to nine movies a year. His fastest film was perhaps <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Shop_of_Horrors" title="The Little Shop of Horrors">The Little Shop of Horrors</a></i> (1960), which was reputedly shot in two days and one night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Supposedly, he had made a bet that he could shoot an entire feature film in less than three days. Another version of the story claims that he had a set rented for a month, and finished using it with three days to spare, thus pushing him to use the set to make a new film. (This is a variation of the story behind 1963's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Terror_(1963_film)" title="The Terror (1963 film)">The Terror</a></i>, much of which was filmed in two leftover days with <a href="/wiki/Boris_Karloff" title="Boris Karloff">Boris Karloff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Nicholson" title="Jack Nicholson">Jack Nicholson</a>, after <i><a href="/wiki/The_Raven" title="The Raven">The Raven</a></i>, which featured them both, wrapped with two days to spare.)</p>
<p>In addition to producing and directing films for <a href="/wiki/American_International_Pictures" title="American International Pictures">American International Pictures</a> (AIP), Corman also partially funded other low-budget films released by other film companies. In 1959, Corman founded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filmgroup" title="Filmgroup">Filmgroup</a> with his brother <a href="/wiki/Gene_Corman" title="Gene Corman">Gene</a>, a company producing or releasing low-budget black-and-white films as <a href="/wiki/Double_feature" title="Double feature">double features</a> for drive-ins and action houses. Finding that black-and-white double features were not as successful as colour films, Corman returned to AIP,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and Filmgroup ceased operation in 1962.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Edgar_Allan_Poe_adaptations">The Edgar Allan Poe adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Corman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Edgar Allan Poe adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Corman's greatest acclaim as a director came with his <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a> cycle released between 1959 and 1964. Based on the works of Poe, made through <a href="/wiki/American_International_Pictures" title="American International Pictures">American International Pictures</a> and mostly in collaboration with writer/scenarist <a href="/wiki/Richard_Matheson" title="Richard Matheson">Richard Matheson</a>, the series of eight films comprises <i><a href="/wiki/House_of_Usher_(film)" title="House of Usher (film)">House of Usher</a></i> (1960), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pit_and_the_Pendulum_(1961_film)" title="The Pit and the Pendulum (1961 film)">The Pit and the Pendulum</a></i> (1961), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Premature_Burial_(film)" title="The Premature Burial (film)">The Premature Burial</a></i> (1962), <i><a href="/wiki/Tales_of_Terror" title="Tales of Terror">Tales of Terror</a></i> (1962), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Raven_(1963_film)" title="The Raven (1963 film)">The Raven</a></i> (1963), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Haunted_Palace" title="The Haunted Palace">The Haunted Palace</a></i> (1963), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Masque_of_the_Red_Death_(1964_film)" title="The Masque of the Red Death (1964 film)">The Masque of the Red Death</a></i> (1964) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tomb_of_Ligeia" title="The Tomb of Ligeia">The Tomb of Ligeia</a></i> (1964). All but <i><a href="/wiki/The_Premature_Burial" title="The Premature Burial">The Premature Burial</a></i> starred <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Price" title="Vincent Price">Vincent Price</a>. Other Poe films were made at AIP in the late 1960s and early 1970s directed by other filmmakers with Price in starring roles.</p>
<p>Corman also worked with set designer <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Haller" title="Daniel Haller">Daniel Haller</a> and cinematographer <a href="/wiki/Floyd_Crosby" title="Floyd Crosby">Floyd Crosby</a> on the series. Others who joined him include cinematographer <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Roeg" title="Nicolas Roeg">Nicolas Roeg</a> (on <i>Masque</i>), writers <a href="/wiki/Robert_Towne" title="Robert Towne">Robert Towne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Beaumont" title="Charles Beaumont">Charles Beaumont</a>, and actors <a href="/wiki/Ray_Milland" title="Ray Milland">Ray Milland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basil_Rathbone" title="Basil Rathbone">Basil Rathbone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hazel_Court" title="Hazel Court">Hazel Court</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Steele" title="Barbara Steele">Barbara Steele</a>, <a href="/wiki/Debra_Paget" title="Debra Paget">Debra Paget</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Lorre" title="Peter Lorre">Peter Lorre</a>. After <i>The Raven</i> was completed, Corman reportedly realized that he still had some shooting days left before the sets were torn down and so made another film, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Terror_(1963_film)" title="The Terror (1963 film)">The Terror</a></i> (1963), on the spot with the remaining cast, crew and sets.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_work">Other work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Corman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He also directed one of <a href="/wiki/William_Shatner" title="William Shatner">William Shatner</a>'s earliest appearances in a lead role with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Intruder_(1962_film)" title="The Intruder (1962 film)">The Intruder</a></i> (a.k.a. <i>The Stranger</i>, 1962). Based on a novel by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Beaumont" title="Charles Beaumont">Charles Beaumont</a>, the film, made for approximately US$80,000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> is known for its treatment of <a href="/wiki/Racial_segregation" title="Racial segregation">segregation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civil_and_political_rights" title="Civil and political rights">civil rights</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The late 1960s saw Corman and his films give a voice to the counter culture of the era. In 1966, Corman made the first biker movie with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wild_Angels" title="The Wild Angels">The Wild Angels</a></i>. It starred <a href="/wiki/Peter_Fonda" title="Peter Fonda">Peter Fonda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Sinatra" title="Nancy Sinatra">Nancy Sinatra</a> and opened the 1966 Venice Film Festival. In 1967, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Trip_(1967_film)" title="The Trip (1967 film)">The Trip</a></i>, written by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Nicholson" title="Jack Nicholson">Jack Nicholson</a> and starring <a href="/wiki/Peter_Fonda" title="Peter Fonda">Peter Fonda</a>, began the psychedelic film craze of the late 1960s, and was the American entry at Cannes that year. <a href="/wiki/Joan_Didion" title="Joan Didion">Joan Didion</a> said she went to see <i>The Wild Angels</i> because "there on the screen was some news I was not getting from the <i>New York Times</i>. I began to think I was seeing ideograms of the future."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Didion_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Didion-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_St._Valentine%27s_Day_Massacre_(film)" title="The St. Valentine's Day Massacre (film)">The St. Valentine's Day Massacre</a></i> (1967) was one of the few films that Roger Corman directed from a major Hollywood studio (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twentieth_Century_Fox" title="Twentieth Century Fox">Twentieth Century Fox</a>) with a generous budget and an open-ended schedule. While most directors would love such an assignment, Corman was disgusted with the incredible waste of time and money involved with "typical" movie production techniques. He was given a $2.5 million budget and made it for $400,000 less.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AIP_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-AIP-17">[17]</a></sup> Corman, an independent director, was most comfortable in his own style: shoestring budgets, and shooting schedules measured in days, rather than weeks. Nonetheless, it is generally considered one of his best films as a director.</p>
<p>In 1970, Corman founded <a href="/wiki/New_World_Pictures" title="New World Pictures">New World Pictures</a> which became a small independently-owned production/distribution studio,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> making many cult films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Women_in_Cages" title="Women in Cages">Women in Cages</a></i> (1971), <i><a href="/wiki/Death_Race_2000" title="Death Race 2000">Death Race 2000</a></i> (1975), <i><a href="/wiki/Rock_%27n%27_Roll_High_School" title="Rock 'n' Roll High School">Rock 'n' Roll High School</a></i> (1979), <i><a href="/wiki/Galaxy_of_Terror" title="Galaxy of Terror">Galaxy of Terror</a></i> (1981), <i><a href="/wiki/Children_of_the_Corn" title="Children of the Corn">Children of the Corn</a></i> (1984), and the <a href="/wiki/Joe_Dante" title="Joe Dante">Joe Dante</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Piranha_(1978_film)" title="Piranha (1978 film)">Piranha</a></i> (1978).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Corman's distribution side of New World brought many foreign films to mass audiences in the US for the first time, including the works of <a href="/wiki/Ingmar_Bergman" title="Ingmar Bergman">Ingmar Bergman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Truffaut" title="François Truffaut">François Truffaut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Federico_Fellini" title="Federico Fellini">Federico Fellini</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Akira_Kurosawa" title="Akira Kurosawa">Akira Kurosawa</a>. In a ten-year period, New World Pictures won more Academy Awards for Best Foreign Film than all other studios combined. Corman eventually sold New World to an investment group in 1983 and later formed Concorde Pictures and New Horizons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Corman's penultimate film as director was <i><a href="/wiki/Von_Richthofen_and_Brown" title="Von Richthofen and Brown">Von Richthofen and Brown</a></i> (1971). Corman had always wanted to make an aviation movie, having piloted model planes as a lad. He returned to directing once more with <i><a href="/wiki/Frankenstein_Unbound" title="Frankenstein Unbound">Frankenstein Unbound</a></i> (1990). In total, Roger Corman has produced over 300 movies and directed 55.</p>
<p>In 2009, Corman produced and directed alongside director <a href="/wiki/Joe_Dante" title="Joe Dante">Joe Dante</a> the <a href="/wiki/Web_series" title="Web series">web series</a> "Splatter" for Netflix.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The protagonist of the film is portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Corey_Feldman" title="Corey Feldman">Corey Feldman</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and the story talks of the haunting tale of rock-and-roll legend Johnny Splatter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> He also started contributing trailer commentaries to Dante's <a href="/wiki/Web_series" title="Web series">web series</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Trailers_from_Hell" title="Trailers from Hell">Trailers from Hell</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Corman produced the 2010 films <i><a href="/wiki/Dinoshark" title="Dinoshark">Dinoshark</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Dinocroc_vs._Supergator" title="Dinocroc vs. Supergator">Dinocroc vs. Supergator</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/Syfy" title="Syfy">Syfy</a> cable television channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <i>Dinoshark</i> premiered on March 13, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Sharktopus" title="Sharktopus">Sharktopus</a></i>, a Syfy production, premiered in September 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Corman produced the 2017 film <i><a href="/wiki/Death_Race_2050" title="Death Race 2050">Death Race 2050</a></i>, a sequel to the 1975 film <i><a href="/wiki/Death_Race_2000" title="Death Race 2000">Death Race 2000</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Corman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Corman married <a href="/wiki/Julie_Corman" title="Julie Corman">Julie Halloran</a> on December 26, 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> They have four children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Remembrances_and_awards">Remembrances and awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Corman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Remembrances and awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His autobiography, titled <i>How I Made a Hundred Movies in Hollywood and Never Lost a Dime</i> (<a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0306808749">ISBN 0-306-80874-9</a>), documents his experiences in the film industry.</p>
<p>In 1964, Corman was the youngest producer/director to be given a retrospective at the <a href="/wiki/Cin%C3%A9math%C3%A8que_Fran%C3%A7aise" title="Cinémathèque Française">Cinémathèque Française</a>, as well as retrospectives at the <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Institute" title="British Film Institute">British Film Institute</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a>.</p>
<p>Corman won the Lifetime Achievement Award at <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_International_Film_Festival" title="Stockholm International Film Festival">Stockholm International Film Festival</a> in 1990.</p>
<p>Corman was the subject of the 1978 documentary <i>Roger Corman: Hollywood's Wild Angel</i>, produced and directed by <a href="/wiki/Christian_Blackwood" title="Christian Blackwood">Christian Blackwood</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Portions of the film reappeared in 2011's <i>Corman's World</i>.</p>
<p>In 1998, he won the first Producer's Award ever given by the <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a>.</p>
<p>In 2006, Corman received the David O. Selznick Award from the <a href="/wiki/Producers_Guild_of_America" title="Producers Guild of America">Producers Guild of America</a>. Also in 2006, his film <i><a href="/wiki/House_of_Usher_(film)" title="House of Usher (film)">Fall of the House of Usher</a></i> was among the twenty-five movies selected for the <a href="/wiki/National_Film_Registry" title="National Film Registry">National Film Registry</a>, a compilation of significant films being preserved by the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a>.</p>
<p>In 2010, The <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Motion_Picture_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences">Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences</a> awarded Corman with an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Honorary_Award" title="Academy Honorary Award">Academy Honorary Award</a> at the inaugural <a href="/wiki/Governors_Awards" title="Governors Awards">Governors Awards</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> on November 14, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, writer and actor <a href="/wiki/Mark_Gatiss" title="Mark Gatiss">Mark Gatiss</a> interviewed Corman for his BBC documentary series <i><a href="/wiki/A_History_of_Horror" title="A History of Horror">A History of Horror</a></i>, of which the second half of the second episode focuses on Corman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-horrorqa_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-horrorqa-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, Corman was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Beverly_Hills_High_School" title="Beverly Hills High School">Beverly Hills High School</a> Hall of Fame.</p>
<p>In 2012, Corman was honored with the Filmmaker on the Edge Award at the <a href="/wiki/Provincetown_International_Film_Festival" title="Provincetown International Film Festival">Provincetown International Film Festival</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id=".22The_Corman_Film_School.22">"The Corman Film School"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Corman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title='Edit section: "The Corman Film School"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of noted film directors and producers worked with Corman, usually early in their careers, including <a href="/wiki/Francis_Ford_Coppola" title="Francis Ford Coppola">Francis Ford Coppola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">Martin Scorsese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ron_Howard" title="Ron Howard">Ron Howard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Bogdanovich" title="Peter Bogdanovich">Peter Bogdanovich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armondo_Linus_Acosta" title="Armondo Linus Acosta">Armondo Linus Acosta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Bartel" title="Paul Bartel">Paul Bartel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Demme" title="Jonathan Demme">Jonathan Demme</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donald_G._Jackson" title="Donald G. Jackson">Donald G. Jackson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gale_Anne_Hurd" title="Gale Anne Hurd">Gale Anne Hurd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Colpaert" title="Carl Colpaert">Carl Colpaert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Dante" title="Joe Dante">Joe Dante</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">James Cameron</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Sayles" title="John Sayles">John Sayles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monte_Hellman" title="Monte Hellman">Monte Hellman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Franklin" title="Carl Franklin">Carl Franklin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Armitage" title="George Armitage">George Armitage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Kaplan" title="Jonathan Kaplan">Jonathan Kaplan</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Hickenlooper" title="George Hickenlooper">George Hickenlooper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Curtis_Hanson" title="Curtis Hanson">Curtis Hanson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Hill" title="Jack Hill">Jack Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Towne" title="Robert Towne">Robert Towne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Menahem_Golan" title="Menahem Golan">Menahem Golan</a>, Michael Venzor and <a href="/wiki/Timur_Bekmambetov" title="Timur Bekmambetov">Timur Bekmambetov</a>. Many have said that Corman's influence taught them some of the ins and outs of filmmaking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EW_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-EW-35">[35]</a></sup> In the extras for the DVD of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Terminator" title="The Terminator">The Terminator</a></i>, director James Cameron asserts, "I trained at the Roger Corman Film School." The British director <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Roeg" title="Nicolas Roeg">Nicolas Roeg</a> served as the <a href="/wiki/Cinematographer" title="Cinematographer">cinematographer</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Masque_of_the_Red_Death_(1964_film)" title="The Masque of the Red Death (1964 film)">The Masque of the Red Death</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Cameron, Coppola, Demme, Hanson, Howard and Scorsese have all gone on to win <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Awards</a>. Howard was reportedly told by Corman, "If you do a good job on this film, you'll never have to work for me again."</p>
<p>Actors who obtained their career breaks working for Corman include <a href="/wiki/Jack_Nicholson" title="Jack Nicholson">Jack Nicholson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Fonda" title="Peter Fonda">Peter Fonda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Dern" title="Bruce Dern">Bruce Dern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bronson" title="Charles Bronson">Charles Bronson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Todd_Field" title="Todd Field">Todd Field</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michael_McDonald_(actor)" title="Michael McDonald (actor)">Michael McDonald</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Hopper" title="Dennis Hopper">Dennis Hopper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Talia_Shire" title="Talia Shire">Talia Shire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandra_Bullock" title="Sandra Bullock">Sandra Bullock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_De_Niro" title="Robert De Niro">Robert De Niro</a>, and <a href="/wiki/David_Carradine" title="David Carradine">David Carradine</a>, who received one of his first starring film roles in the Corman-produced <i><a href="/wiki/Boxcar_Bertha" title="Boxcar Bertha">Boxcar Bertha</a></i> (1972) and went on to star in <i><a href="/wiki/Death_Race_2000" title="Death Race 2000">Death Race 2000</a></i> (along with <a href="/wiki/Sylvester_Stallone" title="Sylvester Stallone">Sylvester Stallone</a>).</p>
<p>Many of Corman's protegés have paid their mentor homage by awarding him cameos in films, such as in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Godfather_Part_II" title="The Godfather Part II">The Godfather Part II</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imagesjournal_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-imagesjournal-38">[38]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Silence_of_the_Lambs_(film)" title="The Silence of the Lambs (film)">The Silence of the Lambs</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Apollo_13_(film)" title="Apollo 13 (film)">Apollo 13</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EW_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-EW-35">[35]</a></sup> and as recently as Demme's 2008 film <i><a href="/wiki/Rachel_Getting_Married" title="Rachel Getting Married">Rachel Getting Married</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Filmography">Filmography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Corman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Filmography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Roger_Corman_filmography" title="Roger Corman filmography">Roger Corman filmography</a></div>
<p>The IMDB credits Corman with 55 directed films and some 385 produced films from 1954 through 2008, many as un-credited producer or executive producer (consistent with his role as head of his own New World Pictures from 1970 through 1983). Corman also has significant credits as writer and actor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cult_Classics">Cult Classics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Corman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cult Classics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Roger_Corman%27s_Cult_Classics" title="Roger Corman's Cult Classics">Roger Corman's Cult Classics</a></div>
<p>In 2010, Roger Corman teamed up with <a href="/wiki/Shout!_Factory" title="Shout! Factory">Shout! Factory</a> to release new DVD and Blu-ray editions of Corman productions under the name <a href="/wiki/Roger_Corman%27s_Cult_Classics" title="Roger Corman's Cult Classics">Roger Corman's Cult Classics</a>. The releases have concentrated on 1970–1980s films he produced through New World rather than directed. These titles include <i><a href="/wiki/Rock_%27n%27_Roll_High_School" title="Rock 'n' Roll High School">Rock 'n' Roll High School</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Death_Race_2000" title="Death Race 2000">Death Race 2000</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Galaxy_of_Terror" title="Galaxy of Terror">Galaxy of Terror</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Forbidden_World" title="Forbidden World">Forbidden World</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Piranha_(1978_film)" title="Piranha (1978 film)">Piranha</a></i>, with additional titles continuing to be released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
