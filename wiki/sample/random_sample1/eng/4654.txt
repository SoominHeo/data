<p><b>Roger David Kornberg</b> (born April 24, 1947) is an American <a href="/wiki/Biochemist" title="Biochemist">biochemist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">professor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Structural_biology" title="Structural biology">structural biology</a> at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> School of Medicine. Kornberg was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> in 2006 for his studies of the process by which genetic information from <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> is copied to <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>, "the molecular basis of <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcription_(genetics)" title="Transcription (genetics)">transcription</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-decadelong_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-decadelong-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_early_life">Education and early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_D._Kornberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kornberg was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a>, the eldest of three sons of biochemist <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Kornberg" title="Arthur Kornberg">Arthur Kornberg</a>, who won the Nobel Prize, and Sylvy Ruth (Levy) who was also a biochemist. He earned his <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">bachelor's degree</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in 1967 and his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ph.D." title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_physics" title="Chemical physics">chemical physics</a> from Stanford in 1972 supervised by <a href="/wiki/Harden_M._McConnell" title="Harden M. McConnell">Harden M. McConnell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phd_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-phd-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_D._Kornberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kornberg became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postdoctoral_research" title="Postdoctoral research">postdoctoral research</a> fellow at the <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_of_Molecular_Biology" title="Laboratory of Molecular Biology">Laboratory of Molecular Biology</a> in Cambridge, England and then an Assistant Professor of Biological Chemistry at Harvard Medical School in 1976, before moving to his present position as Professor of Structural Biology at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University_School_of_Medicine" title="Stanford University School of Medicine">Stanford Medical School</a> in 1978. Kornberg is married to biochemist Yahli Lorch. They have three children, Guy, Maya, and Gil.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_D._Kornberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>All organisms are controlled by their genes, which are coded by DNA, which is copied to RNA, which creates <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, which are sequences of <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>. DNA resides in the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nucleus</a>. When a cell expresses a gene, it copies (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcription_(genetics)" title="Transcription (genetics)">transcribes</a>) that gene's DNA sequence onto a <a href="/wiki/Messenger_RNA" title="Messenger RNA">messenger RNA</a> (mRNA) sequence. mRNA is transported out of the nucleus to <a href="/wiki/Ribosome" title="Ribosome">ribosomes</a>. The ribosomes read the mRNA and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Translation_(genetics)" title="Translation (genetics)">translate</a> the code into the right amino acid sequence to make that gene's protein.</p>
<p>The DNA is transcribed to mRNA by an enzyme, <a href="/wiki/RNA_polymerase_II" title="RNA polymerase II">RNA polymerase II</a>, with the help of many other proteins. Using <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a>, Kornberg identified the role of RNA polymerase II and other proteins in transcribing DNA, and he created three-dimensional images of the protein cluster using <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a>. Polymerase II is used by all organisms with nuclei, including humans, to transcribe DNA.</p>
<p>Kornberg and his research group have made several fundamental discoveries concerning the mechanisms and regulation of eukaryotic transcription. While a graduate student working with Harden McConnell at Stanford in the late 1960s, he discovered the "flip-flop" and lateral diffusion of phospholipids in bilayer membranes. While a postdoctoral fellow working with <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Klug" title="Aaron Klug">Aaron Klug</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Crick" title="Francis Crick">Francis Crick</a> at the MRC in the 1970s, Kornberg discovered the <a href="/wiki/Nucleosome" title="Nucleosome">nucleosome</a> as the basic protein complex packaging chromosomal DNA in the nucleus of eukaryotic cells (chromosomal DNA is often termed "<a href="/wiki/Chromatin" title="Chromatin">Chromatin</a>" when it is bound to proteins in this manner, reflecting <a href="/wiki/Walther_Flemming" title="Walther Flemming">Walther Flemming</a>'s discovery that certain structures within the cell nucleus would absorb dyes and become visible under a microscope).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Within the nucleosome, Kornberg found that roughly 200 bp of DNA are wrapped around an octamer of histone proteins.</p>
<p>Kornberg's research group at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanford" title="Stanford">Stanford</a> later succeeded in the development of a faithful transcription system from <a href="/wiki/Baker%27s_yeast" title="Baker's yeast">baker's yeast</a>, a simple unicellular eukaryote, which they then used to isolate in a purified form all of the several dozen proteins required for the transcription process. Through the work of Kornberg and others, it has become clear that these protein components are remarkably conserved across the full spectrum of eukaryotes, from yeast to human cells.</p>
<p>Using this system, Kornberg made the major discovery that transmission of gene regulatory signals to the RNA polymerase machinery is accomplished by an additional protein complex that they dubbed <a href="/wiki/Mediator_(coactivator)" title="Mediator (coactivator)"><i>Mediator</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> As noted by the Nobel Prize committee, "the great complexity of eukaryotic organisms is actually enabled by the fine interplay between tissue-specific substances, enhancers in the DNA and Mediator. The discovery of Mediator is therefore a true milestone in the understanding of the transcription process."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the same time as Kornberg was pursuing these biochemical studies of the transcription process, he devoted two decades to the development of methods to visualize the atomic structure of RNA polymerase and its associated protein components. Initially, Kornberg took advantage of expertise with lipid membranes gained from his graduate studies to devise a technique for the formation of two-dimensional protein crystals on lipid bilayers. These 2D crystals could then be analyzed using electron microscopy to derive low-resolution images of the protein's structure. Eventually, Kornberg was able to use <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a> to solve the <a href="/wiki/Protein_structure" title="Protein structure">3-dimensional structure</a> of RNA polymerase at atomic resolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He has recently extended these studies to obtain structural images of RNA polymerase associated with accessory proteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Through these studies, Kornberg has created an actual picture of how transcription works at a molecular level. According to the Nobel Prize committee, "the truly revolutionary aspect of the picture Kornberg has created is that it captures the process of transcription in full flow. What we see is an RNA-strand being constructed, and hence the exact positions of the DNA, polymerase and RNA during this process."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
