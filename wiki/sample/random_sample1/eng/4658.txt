<p><b>Roger Vitrac</b> (<small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[vitʁak]</a></span>; 17 November 1899 – 22 January 1952) was a French <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">surrealist</a> playwright and poet.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Vitrac&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Pinsac" title="Pinsac">Pinsac</a>, Roger Vitrac moved to Paris in 1910. As a young man, he was influenced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symbolist_poetry" title="Symbolist poetry">symbolism</a> and the writings of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lautr%C3%A9amont" title="Lautréamont">Lautréamont</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Jarry" title="Alfred Jarry">Alfred Jarry</a>, and he developed a passion for theatre and poetry. A year after his <i>baccalauréat</i>, he published a collection of poems, <i>Le Faune noir</i>.</p>
<p>During his three-year-long military service, he met <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Arland" title="Marcel Arland">Marcel Arland</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fran%C3%A7ois_Baron&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="François Baron (page does not exist)">François Baron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georges_Limbour" title="Georges Limbour">Georges Limbour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Crevel" title="René Crevel">René Crevel</a>, who together founded the literary revue, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aventure&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aventure (page does not exist)">Aventure</a></i>. Through the group, Vitrac met <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Breton" title="André Breton">André Breton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louis_Aragon" title="Louis Aragon">Louis Aragon</a> at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Caf%C3%A9_Certa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Café Certa (page does not exist)">Café Certa</a> in 1921, where many <a href="/wiki/Dada" title="Dada">Dada</a> activities took place until it became one of the <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">surrealist</a> group's headquarters. In the early twenties, Vitrac joined the surrealist movement and contributed to the first few editions of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_R%C3%A9volution_Surr%C3%A9aliste" title="La Révolution Surréaliste">La Révolution Surréaliste</a></i>. Slowly segregated from the group along with <a href="/wiki/Antonin_Artaud" title="Antonin Artaud">Antonin Artaud</a>, together they created the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Th%C3%A9atre_Alfred-Jarry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Théatre Alfred-Jarry (page does not exist)">Théatre Alfred-Jarry</a> in 1926, where they produced some of their most important plays such as <i>The Mysteries of Love</i> (<i>Les Mystères de l'amour</i>, 1927) and <i>Victor, or Power to the Children</i> (<i>Victor ou les enfants au pouvoir</i>, 1928), setting the tone for the <a href="/wiki/Theatre_of_the_Absurd" title="Theatre of the Absurd">Theatre of the Absurd</a>, twenty years before <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionesco" title="Ionesco">Ionesco</a>. <i>The Cambridge Guide to Theatre</i> describes <i>The Mysteries of Love</i> as a masterpiece of surrealist theatre. It is now considered a key play in 20th-century French theatre.</p>
<p>Vitrac joined <a href="/wiki/Georges_Bataille" title="Georges Bataille">Georges Bataille</a> as one of the signatories of <i><a href="/wiki/Un_Cadavre" title="Un Cadavre">Un Cadavre</a></i> against <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Breton" title="André Breton">Breton</a> and contributed to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Documents" title="Documents">Documents</a></i> with articles on "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gaston-Louis_Roux&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gaston-Louis Roux (page does not exist)">Gaston-Louis Roux</a>" (1929, issue 7), "The Abduction of the Sabines" (1930, issue 6) and a poem, "Humorage to Picasso" (1930, issue 3), dedicated to the artist. From 1931, he worked as a journalist while further exploring burlesque style playwriting, which often operated between <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Boulevard_comedy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Boulevard comedy (page does not exist)">boulevard comedy</a> and intimate tragedy. His multi-thematic <i>Coup de Trafalgar</i> (1934) and <i>Les Demoiselles du large</i> (1938) gained as little recognition as his more slapstick plays such as <i>Le Loup-Garou</i> (1939) and <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Sabre_de_mon_p%C3%A8re" title="Le Sabre de mon père">Le Sabre de mon père</a></i> (1951).</p>
<p>Vitrac died in Paris in 1952.</p>
<p>Only after his death did Vitrac reach popular stardom status with <a href="/wiki/Jean_Anouilh" title="Jean Anouilh">Jean Anouilh</a>'s 1962 production of <i>Victor, or Power to the Children</i>.</p>
