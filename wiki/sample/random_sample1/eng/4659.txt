<p><b>Roger Caillois</b> (<small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[ʁɔʒe kajwa]</a></span>; 3 March 1913 – 21 December 1978) was a French <a href="/wiki/Intellectual" title="Intellectual">intellectual</a> whose idiosyncratic work brought together <a href="/wiki/Literary_criticism" title="Literary criticism">literary criticism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> by focusing on diverse subjects such as <a href="/wiki/Game" title="Game">games</a>, <a href="/wiki/Play_(activity)" title="Play (activity)">play</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Sacred" title="Sacred">sacred</a>. He was also instrumental in introducing Latin American authors such as <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Luis_Borges" title="Jorge Luis Borges">Jorge Luis Borges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Neruda" title="Pablo Neruda">Pablo Neruda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miguel_%C3%81ngel_Asturias" title="Miguel Ángel Asturias">Miguel Ángel Asturias</a> to the French public. After his death, the French Literary award <a href="/wiki/Prix_Roger_Caillois" title="Prix Roger Caillois">Prix Roger Caillois</a> was named after him in 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Caillois&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Caillois was born in <a href="/wiki/Reims" title="Reims">Reims</a> but moved to <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> as a child. There he studied at the <a href="/wiki/Lyc%C3%A9e_Louis-le-Grand" title="Lycée Louis-le-Grand">Lycée Louis-le-Grand</a>, an elite school where students took courses after graduating from secondary school in order to prepare for entry examinations for France's most prestigious university, the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Normale_Sup%C3%A9rieure" title="École Normale Supérieure">École Normale Supérieure</a>. Caillois's efforts paid off and he graduated as a normalien in 1933. After this he studied at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Pratique_des_Hautes_%C3%89tudes" title="École Pratique des Hautes Études">École Pratique des Hautes Études</a> where he came into contact with thinkers such as <a href="/wiki/Georges_Dum%C3%A9zil" title="Georges Dumézil">Georges Dumézil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Koj%C3%A8ve" title="Alexandre Kojève">Alexandre Kojève</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Mauss" title="Marcel Mauss">Marcel Mauss</a>.</p>
<p>The years before the war were marked by Caillois's increasingly leftist political commitment, particularly in his fight against <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascism</a>. He was also engaged in Paris's avant-garde intellectual life. With <a href="/wiki/Georges_Bataille" title="Georges Bataille">Georges Bataille</a> he founded the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Sociology" title="College of Sociology">College of Sociology</a>, a group of intellectuals who lectured regularly to one another. Formed partly as a reaction to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surrealist" title="Surrealist">Surrealist</a> movement that was dominant in the 1920s, the College sought to move away from surrealism's focus on the fantasy life of an individual's unconscious and focus instead more on the power of ritual and other aspects of communal life. Caillois's background in <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, and particularly his interest in the sacred, exemplified this approach. He participated in Bataille's review, <i><a href="/wiki/Ac%C3%A9phale" title="Acéphale">Acéphale</a></i> (1936–39).</p>
<p>Caillois left France in 1939 for Argentina, where he stayed until the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">WWII</a>. During the war he was active in fighting the spread of Nazism in Latin America as an editor and author of anti-Nazi periodicals. From 1940 to 1945, he lived in South America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1948, after the War, he worked with <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> and traveled widely. In 1971 he was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_fran%C3%A7aise" title="Académie française">Académie française</a>. In 1977, he started to write a book with the painter <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bernard_Mandeville_(painter)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bernard Mandeville (painter) (page does not exist)">Bernard Mandeville</a>. In 1978, Caillois wrote <i>Le fleuve Alphée</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> an award-winning autobiographical essay (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marcel_Proust_Prize" title="Marcel Proust Prize">Marcel Proust Awards</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_Union_Prize_for_Literature" title="European Union Prize for Literature">European Union Prize for Literature</a>), followed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cases_d%E2%80%99un_%C3%A9chiquier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cases d’un échiquier (page does not exist)">Cases d’un échiquier</a>. He died, aged 65, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kremlin-Bic%C3%AAtre" title="Kremlin-Bicêtre">Kremlin-Bicêtre</a>.</p>
<p>Today Caillois is remembered for founding and editing <i><a href="/wiki/Diogenes_(journal)" title="Diogenes (journal)">Diogenes</a></i>, an interdisciplinary journal funded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Educational,_Scientific_and_Cultural_Organization" title="United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization">UNESCO</a>, and <i>La Croix du Sud</i> (<i>Southern Cross</i>), a collection of books translated from contemporary Latin American authors published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallimard" title="Gallimard">Gallimard</a> that is responsible for introducing authors such as <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Luis_Borges" title="Jorge Luis Borges">Jorge Luis Borges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alejo_Carpentier" title="Alejo Carpentier">Alejo Carpentier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Ocampo" title="Victoria Ocampo">Victoria Ocampo</a> to the French-speaking public. He is also widely cited in the nascent field of <a href="/wiki/Game_studies" title="Game studies">ludology</a>, primarily from passages in his book <i>Les Jeux et les Hommes(1958).</i> The book has been translated to English by <a class="external text" href="http://www.goodreads.com/author/show/3975386.Meyer_Barash" rel="nofollow">Meyer Barash</a> in 1961 as <a href="/wiki/Man,_Play_and_Games" title="Man, Play and Games">Man, Play and Games</a><i>.</i></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Caillois.27_key_ideas_on_play">Caillois' key ideas on play</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Caillois&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Caillois' key ideas on play">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Caillois built critically on an earlier theory of play developed by the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> cultural historian <a href="/wiki/Johan_Huizinga" title="Johan Huizinga">Johan Huizinga</a> in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_Ludens" title="Homo Ludens">Homo Ludens</a></i> (1938). Huzinga had discussed the importance of play as an element of culture and society. He used the term "Play Theory" to define the conceptual space in which play occurs, and argued that play is a necessary (though not sufficient) condition for the generation of culture.</p>
<p>Caillois began his own book <i><a href="/wiki/Man,_Play_and_Games" title="Man, Play and Games">Man, Play and Games</a></i> (1961)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> with Huizinga's definition of play:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>Summing up the formal characteristics of play we might call it a free activity standing quite consciously outside "ordinary" life as being "not serious," but at the same time absorbing the player intensely and utterly. It is an activity connected with no material interest, and no profit can be gained by it. It proceeds within its own proper boundaries of time and space according to fixed rules and in an orderly manner. It promotes the formation of social groupings which tend to surround themselves with secrecy and to stress their difference from the common world by disguise or other means.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Caillois disputed Huizinga's emphasis on competition in play. He also noted the considerable difficulty in arriving at a comprehensive definition of play, concluding that play is best described by six core characteristics:</p>
<ul>
<li>1. it is free, or not obligatory</li>
<li>2. it is separate from the routine of life, occupying its own time and space</li>
<li>3. it is uncertain, so that the results of play cannot be pre-determined and the player's initiative is involved</li>
<li>4. it is unproductive in that it creates no wealth, and ends as it begins economically speaking</li>
<li>5. it is governed by rules that suspend ordinary laws and behaviours and that must be followed by players</li>
<li>6. it involves imagined realities that may be set against 'real life'.</li>
</ul>
<p>Caillois' definition has itself been criticized by subsequent thinkers;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and ultimately, in spite Caillois' attempt at a definitive treatment, definitions of play remain open to negotiation.</p>
<p>Caillois distinguished four categories of games:</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Agon" title="Agon">Agon</a></b>, or competition. It's the form of play in which a specific set of skills is put to the test among players (strength, intelligence, memory). The winner is who proves to have mastery of said skill through the game, for example a quiz game is a competition of intelligence, the winner proves that it's more intelligent than the other players. E.g. <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a></li>
<li><b><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/alea" title="wikt:alea">Alea</a></b>, or chance, the opposite of Agon, Caillois describes Alea as "the resignation of will, An abandonment to destiny". if Agon used the skills pf players to determinate a victor Alea leaves that to luck, an external agent decides who the victor is. E.g. playing a <a href="/wiki/Slot_machine" title="Slot machine">slot machine</a></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Mimicry" title="Mimicry">Mimicry</a></b>, or mimesis, or role playing Caillois defines it as "When the individual plays to believe, to make himself or others believe that he is different from himself". E.g. playing an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MMORPG" title="MMORPG">online role-playing game</a></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ilinx" title="Ilinx">Ilinx</a></b> (Greek for "whirlpool"), or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertigo_(medical)" title="Vertigo (medical)">vertigo</a>, in the sense of altering perception by experiencing a strong emotion (pánic,fear, ecstasy) the stronger the emotion is, the stronger the sense of excitement and fun becomes. E.g. taking <a href="/wiki/Hallucinogen" title="Hallucinogen">hallucinogens</a>, riding <a href="/wiki/Roller_coaster" title="Roller coaster">roller coasters</a>, children spinning until they fall down.</li>
</ul>
<p>It's worth noting that these categories can be combined to create a more diverse experience and enhance the players interaction, for example poker is a form of Agon-Alea, Alea is present in the form of the cards and their combinations, but it's not the only winning factor, since Agon is present in the form of bluffing, making your opponent think you have better cards by rising the bet therefore putting pressure on the other players and thus making it possible to win by having a card combination but winning by implementing the bluff skill</p>
<p>Caillois also described a dualistic polarity within which the four categories of games can be variously located:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Paidia</b> or uncontrolled fantasy, it's spontaneous play due improvisation, the rules are created during playing time. E.g. <a href="/wiki/Concert" title="Concert">concerts</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Festivals" title="Festivals">festivals</a></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ludus" title="Ludus">Ludus</a></b> which requires effort, patience, skill, or ingenuity, the rules are set from the beginning and the game was designed before playing time. E.g. the Chinese game of <a href="/wiki/Go_(game)" title="Go (game)">Go</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Caillois disagreed particularly with Huizinga's treatment of gambling. Huizinga had argued in <i>Homo Ludens</i> that the risk of death or of losing money corrupts the freedom of "pure play". Thus to Huizinga card-games are not play but "deadly earnest business". Moreover, Huizinga considered gambling to be a "futile activity" which inflicts damage on society. Thus Huizinga argued that gambling is a corruption of a more original form of play.</p>
<p>Against this, Caillois argued that gambling is a true game, a mode of play that falls somewhere between games of skill or competition and games of chance (i.e. between the <i>Agon</i> and <i>Alea</i> categories). Whether or not a game involves money or a risk of death, it can be considered a form of <i>Agon</i> or <i>Alea</i> as long as it provides social activity and triumph for the winner. Gambling is "like a combat in which equality of chances is artificially created, in order that adversaries should confront each other under ideal conditions, susceptible of giving precise and incontestable value to the winner’s triumph."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Caillois.27_interest_in_mimicry">Caillois' interest in mimicry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Caillois&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Caillois' interest in mimicry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When Caillois worked with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Bataille" title="George Bataille">Bataille</a> at the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Sociology" title="College of Sociology">College of Sociology</a>, they worked on two essays on insects in the 1930s: <i>‘La mante religieuse. De la biologie à la psychanalyse’</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>(1934) and <i>‘Mimétisme et la psychasthénie légendaire’</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> (1935) Caillois identifies "the praying mantis and mimicking animals as nature’s automatons and masquerades." He formulates "in his peculiarly naturalist fashion what it would mean to act and create without the intervention of the sovereign ego, that magnificent artifact of the modern West that <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">surrealism</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> have taken such drastic measures to counteract." These articles "might read like two obscurantist entomological studies that, in a way some would describe as bizarre, try to contradict all evolutionary explications for animal cannibalism and mimicry. Their publication in the context of [the surrealist journal] <a href="/wiki/Minotaure" title="Minotaure">Minotaure</a> makes it possible to see them as the search for figures that evidence the possibility of intelligence without thought, creativity without art, and agency in the absence of the (human) agent."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roger_Caillois_French_Literary_Prize">Roger Caillois French Literary Prize</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Caillois&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roger Caillois French Literary Prize">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Prix_Roger_Caillois" title="Prix Roger Caillois">Roger Caillois French Literary Prize</a> for Latin American Literature was created in 1991 and has also been awarded to figures such as <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Fuentes" title="Carlos Fuentes">Carlos Fuentes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Donoso" title="José Donoso">José Donoso</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Adolfo_Bioy_Casares" title="Adolfo Bioy Casares">Adolfo Bioy Casares</a>.</p>
