<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Rhodes Scholarship</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The <b>Rhodes Scholarship</b>, named for the British mining magnate and South African politician <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Rhodes" title="Cecil Rhodes">Cecil John Rhodes</a>, is an international <a href="/wiki/Postgraduate_education" title="Postgraduate education">postgraduate</a> award for students to study at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is widely considered to be one of the world's most prestigious <a href="/wiki/Scholarship" title="Scholarship">scholarships</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Established in 1902, it was the first large-scale programme of international scholarships,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">[3]</a></sup> inspiring the creation of a great many other awards in other countries.</p>
<p>As elaborated on in his will, Cecil Rhodes' goals in creating the Rhodes Scholarships were to promote civic-minded leadership among "young colonists"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-will_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-will-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:24</sup> with "moral force of character and instincts to lead", for "the furtherance of the British Empire, for the bringing of the whole uncivilised world under British rule, for the recovery of the United States, for the making the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxon</a> race but one Empire."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-will_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-will-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:59</sup> With the scholarships, he "aimed at making Oxford University the educational centre of the English-speaking race."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-will_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-will-4">[4]</a></sup> Since its creation, <a href="/wiki/Rhodes_Scholarship#Controversies" title="Rhodes Scholarship">controversy</a> has surrounded both its former exclusion of women (thus leading to the establishment of the co-educational <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Scholarship" title="Marshall Scholarship">Marshall Scholarship</a>), and Rhodes' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_supremacist" title="White supremacist">white supremacist</a> beliefs and legacy of <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonialism</a>.</p>
<p>As of 2016, there have been 7,776 scholars since the programme's inception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> More than 4,700 are still living.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BriefHistory_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BriefHistory-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodes_Scholarship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_and_motivation">Founding and motivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodes_Scholarship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding and motivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Rhodes Scholarships are administered and awarded by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhodes_Trust" title="Rhodes Trust">Rhodes Trust</a>, which was established in 1902 under the terms and conditions of the will of <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Rhodes" title="Cecil Rhodes">Cecil John Rhodes</a>, and funded by his legacy. The Trust has been modified by three Acts of Parliament: The Rhodes Estate Act 1916, the Rhodes Trust Act 1929, The Rhodes Trust Act 1946; and most recently by The Rhodes Trust (Modification) Order 1976, a statutory instrument in accordance with Section 78 (4) of the Sex Discrimination Act 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Rhodes' motivation in establishing the scholarship is reflected in his will. Writing in 1902, W. T. Stead noted that it "[revealed] him to the world as the first distinguished British statesman whose Imperialism was that of Race and not that of Empire." With the scholarships, he "aimed at making Oxford University the educational centre of the English-speaking race."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-will_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-will-4">[4]</a></sup> Rhodes, who attended <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oriel_College" title="Oriel College">Oriel College</a>, Oxford, chose his <i>alma mater</i> as the site of his great experiment because he believed its residential colleges would be a "great advantage" to "young Colonists" for "giving breadth to their views for instruction in life and manners and for instilling into their minds the advantage to the Colonies as well as to the United Kingdom of the retention of the unity of the Empire."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-will_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-will-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:23</sup> With this motivation in mind, the legacy originally provided for scholarships for the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British colonies</a>, the United States and Germany. These three were chosen because it was thought that "a good understanding between England, Germany and the United States of America will secure the peace of the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-will_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-will-4">[4]</a></sup> As he developed the idea for the scholarship, Rhodes wrote that his dream was "the furtherance of the British Empire, for the bringing of the whole uncivilised world under British rule, for the recovery of the United States, for the making the Anglo-Saxon race but one Empire."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-will_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-will-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:59</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_Rhodes.27_death">After Rhodes' death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodes_Scholarship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: After Rhodes' death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1925, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Fund_Fellowship" title="Commonwealth Fund Fellowship">Commonwealth Fund Fellowships</a> (later renamed the <a href="/wiki/Harkness_Fellowship" title="Harkness Fellowship">Harkness Fellowships</a>) were established to reciprocate the Rhodes Scholarships by enabling British graduates to study in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harkness_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harkness-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Scholarship" title="Kennedy Scholarship">Kennedy Scholarship</a> programme, created in 1966 as a memorial to <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>, adopts a comparable selection process to the Rhodes Scholarships to allow ten British post-graduate students per year to study at either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard" title="Harvard">Harvard</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> (MIT). It also cooperates with universities in China, BLCC for example. BLCC offers high-level scholarships for international students who aim to study Chinese in Beijing. In 1953, the Parliament of the United Kingdom created the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Scholarship" title="Marshall Scholarship">Marshall Scholarship</a> as a coeducational alternative to the Rhodes Scholarship that would serve as a "living gift" to the United States.</p>
<p>For at least its first 75 years, Rhodes Scholars usually studied for a second <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">Bachelor of Arts</a> degree. While that remains an option, more recent scholars usually study for an <a href="/wiki/Degrees_of_the_University_of_Oxford" title="Degrees of the University of Oxford">advanced degree</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In recognition of the centenary of the foundation of the Rhodes Trust in 2003, four former Rhodes Scholars were awarded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honorary_degrees" title="Honorary degrees">honorary degrees</a> by the University of Oxford. These were <a href="/wiki/John_Brademas" title="John Brademas">John Brademas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Hawke" title="Bob Hawke">Bob Hawke</a> (Western Australia and <a href="/wiki/University_College,_Oxford" title="University College, Oxford">University</a> 1953), <a href="/wiki/Rex_Nettleford" title="Rex Nettleford">Rex Nettleford</a> and David R. Woods. During the centenary celebrations, the foundation of the Mandela Rhodes Foundation was also marked.</p>
<p>In 2013, during the 110th Rhodes Anniversary celebrations, <a href="/wiki/John_MacBain" title="John MacBain">John McCall MacBain</a>, Marcy McCall MacBain and the McCall MacBain Foundation donated £75 million towards the fundraising efforts of the Rhodes Trust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, Rhodes Scholar <a href="/wiki/R._W._Johnson" title="R. W. Johnson">R. W. Johnson</a> published a critical account of the decline of the Rhodes Trust under its warden, <a href="/wiki/John_Rowett" title="John Rowett">John Rowett</a>, and commended the recovery under wardens <a href="/wiki/Donald_Markwell" title="Donald Markwell">Donald Markwell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_R._Conn" title="Charles R. Conn">Charles R. Conn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cecil Rhodes wished current scholars and Rhodes alumni (in the words of his will) to have "opportunities of meeting and discussing their experiences and prospects". This has been reflected, for example, in the initiation by the first warden (Sir Francis Wylie), of an annual warden's Christmas letter (now supplemented by Rhodes e-news and other communications); the creation of alumni associations in several countries, most prominently the Association of American Rhodes Scholars (which publishes <i>The American Oxonian</i>, founded in 1914, and oversees the Eastman Professorship); and the holding of reunions for Rhodes Scholars of all countries.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Selection_and_selectivity">Selection and selectivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodes_Scholarship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Selection and selectivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Selection_criteria">Selection criteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodes_Scholarship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Selection criteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rhodes's legacy specified four standards by which applicants were to be judged:</p>
<ul>
<li>Literary and scholastic attainments;</li>
<li>Energy to use one's talents to the fullest, as exemplified by fondness for and success in sports (today this is expressed as energy used in many ways, not purely through sports);</li>
<li>Truth, courage, devotion to duty, sympathy for and protection of the weak, kindliness, unselfishness and fellowship;</li>
<li>Moral force of character and instincts to lead, and to take an interest in one's fellow beings.</li>
</ul>
<p>Each country's scholarship varies in its selectivity. In the United States, in 2014, there were 857 university-endorsed applicants for the Americans Rhodes scholarship, of whom 3.7% were ultimately elected. As such, the Rhodes Scholarship is more selective than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Truman_Scholarship" title="Truman Scholarship">Truman Scholarship</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fulbright_Program" title="Fulbright Program">Fulbright Scholarship</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gates_Scholarship" title="Gates Scholarship">Gates Scholarship</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitchell_Scholarship" title="Mitchell Scholarship">Mitchell Scholarship</a>, but marginally less selective than the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Scholarship" title="Marshall Scholarship">Marshall Scholarship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In Canada between 1997-2002, there were an average of 234 university-endorsed applicants annually for 11 scholarships, for an acceptance rate of 4.7%. In addition, Canadian provinces differ widely in the number of applications received, with Ontario receiving 58 applications on average for 2 spots (3.4%) and Newfoundland and Labrador receiving 18 applications for 1 spot (5.7%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>An early change was the elimination of the scholarships for Germany during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wars" title="World Wars">World Wars</a>. No German scholars were chosen from 1914 to 1929, nor from 1940 to 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-numbers_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-numbers-13">[13]</a></sup> Rhodes's bequest was whittled down considerably in the first decades after his death, as various scholarship trustees were forced to pay taxes upon their own deaths.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A change occurred in 1929, when an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a> established a fund separate from the original proceeds of Rhodes's will and made it possible to expand the number of scholarships. Between 1993 and 1995, scholarships were extended to other countries in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Community" title="European Community">European Community</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scholarship_terms">Scholarship terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodes_Scholarship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Scholarship terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rhodes Scholars may study any full-time postgraduate course offered by the university,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> whether a taught master's programme, a research degree, or a second undergraduate degree (senior status). In the first instance, the scholarship is awarded for two years. However, it may also be held for one year or three years. Applications for a third year are considered during the course of the second year. University and college fees are paid by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhodes_Trust" title="Rhodes Trust">Rhodes Trust</a>. In addition, scholars receive a monthly maintenance <a href="/wiki/Stipend" title="Stipend">stipend</a> to cover accommodation and living expenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Although all scholars become affiliated with a <a href="/wiki/Residential_college" title="Residential college">residential college</a> while at Oxford, they also enjoy access to <a href="/wiki/Rhodes_House" title="Rhodes House">Rhodes House</a>, an early 20th-century mansion with numerous public rooms, gardens, a library, study areas, and other facilities.</p>

<p>There were originally 57 scholarships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-will_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-will-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-numbers_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-numbers-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Four <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South African</a> boys' schools were mentioned in Rhodes' will, each to receive an annual scholarship: the <a href="/wiki/Paul_Roos_Gymnasium" title="Paul Roos Gymnasium">Boys High School</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Stellenbosch" title="Stellenbosch">Stellenbosch</a> (today known as Paul Roos Gymnasium); the <a href="/wiki/Diocesan_College" title="Diocesan College">Diocesan College</a> (Bishops) in <a href="/wiki/Rondebosch" title="Rondebosch">Rondebosch</a>; the <a href="/wiki/South_African_College_Schools" title="South African College Schools">South African College Schools</a> (SACS) in <a href="/wiki/Newlands,_Cape_Town" title="Newlands, Cape Town">Newlands</a>; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Andrew%27s_College_(South_Africa)" title="St. Andrew's College (South Africa)">St Andrew's College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Grahamstown" title="Grahamstown">Grahamstown</a>. These have subsequently been opened also to former students of their partner schools (girls' or co-educational schools).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the ensuing 100 years, the trustees have added about another 40 scholarships at one time or another, though not all have continued. Some of these extended the scheme to Commonwealth countries not mentioned in the will.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BriefHistory_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-BriefHistory-6">[6]</a></sup> A more detailed allocation by region by year can be found at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhodes_Scholarship_Allocations" title="Rhodes Scholarship Allocations">Rhodes Scholarship Allocations</a>. Very brief summaries of some of the terms and conditions can be found on the trust's website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Complete details can be obtained from the nominating countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Currently, scholars are selected from citizens of 14 specified geographic constituencies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> namely: Australia; Bermuda; Canada; Germany; Hong Kong; India; Jamaica &amp; Commonwealth Caribbean; Kenya; New Zealand; Pakistan; Southern Africa (South Africa and neighbours Botswana, Lesotho, Malawi, Namibia and Swaziland); United States; Zambia; and Zimbabwe. The year 2015 saw the expansion of the Rhodes Scholarship into new territories, first with the announcement of a yet-to-be-determined number of scholarships for <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> later with the announcement of one to two scholarships per year for the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The organization administering the scholarships is preparing to begin naming scholars from China. The move into China is the biggest expansion since women became eligible in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodes_Scholarship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exclusion_of_women_before_1977.2C_and_before_2012_in_South_Africa">Exclusion of women before 1977, and before 2012 in South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodes_Scholarship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Exclusion of women before 1977, and before 2012 in South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Rhodes Scholarship was open only to men until 1977, when an Act of Parliament changed Rhodes' will to extend the selection criteria to include women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Before that amendment, some universities protested against the exclusion of women by nominating female candidates, who were later disqualified at the state level of the American competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In 1977, the first year women were eligible, 24 women (out of 72 total scholars) were selected worldwide, with 13 women and 19 men selected from the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WomenScholars_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-WomenScholars-44">[44]</a></sup> Since then, the average female share of the scholarship in the United States had been around 35 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WomenScholars_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-WomenScholars-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In South Africa, the will of Cecil Rhodes expressly allocated scholarships to four all-male private schools. In 1992, one of the four schools partnered with an all-girls school in order to allow female applicants. In 2012, the three remaining schools followed suit to allow women to apply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhodeshouse.ox.ac.uk_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-rhodeshouse.ox.ac.uk-45">[45]</a></sup> Today, 4 of the 9 scholarships allocated to South Africa are open only to students and alumni of these schools and partner schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhodeshouse.ox.ac.uk_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-rhodeshouse.ox.ac.uk-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exclusion_of_Black_Africans_before_1991">Exclusion of Black Africans before 1991</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodes_Scholarship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Exclusion of Black Africans before 1991">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beginning in 1970, scholars began protesting against the fact that all Rhodes Scholars from southern Africa were white, with 120 Oxford dons and 80 of the 145 Rhodes Scholars in residence at the time signing a petition calling for non-white scholars to be elected in 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schaeper_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schaeper-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:238</sup> The case of South Africa was especially difficult to resolve, because in his will establishing the scholarships, unlike for other constituencies, Rhodes specifically allocated four scholarships to alumni of four white-only private secondary schools. According to Schaeper and Schaeper, the issue became "explosive" in the 1970s and 1980s as scholars argued that the scholarship be changed while the trustees argued they were powerless to change the will.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schaeper_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schaeper-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:236–237</sup> Despite such protests, only in 1991 with the rise of the African National Congress did black South Africans begin to win the scholarships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schaeper_46-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schaeper-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:240</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_as_colonialist">Criticism as colonialist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodes_Scholarship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Criticism as colonialist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Rhodes_Must_Fall" title="Rhodes Must Fall">Rhodes Must Fall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Rhodes#Political_Views" title="Cecil Rhodes">Cecil Rhodes § Political Views</a></div>
<p>Public criticism of the scholarship has also focused on Cecil Rhodes's alleged white supremacist views. For example, in 1966, regional committees in interviews asked a white American candidate to assure them he would not publicly belittle the scholarship after he referred to its founding on "blood money".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schaeper_46-3"><a href="#cite_note-Schaeper-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:238</sup> In 2015, a South African Rhodes Scholar, Ntokozo Qwabe, began a campaign to address Rhodes' <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Rhodes#Political_Views" title="Cecil Rhodes">controversial historical and political legacy</a>, with a focus on "dismantling the open glorification of colonial genocide in educational &amp; other public spaces – which makes it easy for British people to believe that these genocides were 'not that bad' – and props up the continuing structural legacies of British colonialism, neocolonialism, and ongoing imperialism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cherwell.org_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-cherwell.org-47">[47]</a></sup> Among other things, the campaign called for the removal of a statue of Rhodes from Oriel College and changes to Oxford's curriculum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> While the college agreed to review the placement of the statue, the Chancellor of the university, Lord Patten, warned against "pandering to contemporary views".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>A group of Rhodes Scholars also created the group Redress Rhodes whose mission was to "attain a more critical, honest, and inclusive reflection of the legacy of Cecil John Rhodes" and to "make reparative justice a more central theme for Rhodes Scholars." Their demands include, among other things, shifting the Rhodes Scholarships awarded exclusively to previously all-white South African Schools (rather than the at-large national pool), dedicating a "space at Rhodes House for the critical engagement with Cecil Rhodes' legacy, as well as imperial history", and ending a ceremonial toast Rhodes Scholars make to the founder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> While the group does not have a position on the removal of the statue, its co-founder has called for the scholarship to be renamed as it is "the ultimate form of veneration and colonial apologism; it’s a large part of why many continue to understand Rhodes as a benevolent founder and benefactor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theguardian.com_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian.com-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Public criticism has also focused on the alleged hypocrisy of applying for and accepting the Rhodes Scholarship while criticizing it, with University of Cambridge academic <a href="/wiki/Mary_Beard_(classicist)" title="Mary Beard (classicist)">Mary Beard</a>, writing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times_Literary_Supplement" title="The Times Literary Supplement">The Times Literary Supplement</a></i>, arguing that Scholars "[could not] have your cake and eat it here: I mean you can't whitewash Rhodes out of history, but go on using his cash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cherwell.org_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-cherwell.org-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Reacting to this criticism, Qwabe replied that "all that [Rhodes] looted must absolutely be returned immediately. I’m no beneficiary of Rhodes. I’m a beneficiary of the resources and labour of my people which Rhodes pillaged and slaved."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theguardian.com_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian.com-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> A group of 198 Rhodes Scholars of various years later signed a statement supporting Qwabe and arguing that there was "no hypocrisy in being a recipient of a Rhodes scholarship and being publicly critical of Cecil Rhodes and his legacy – a legacy that continues to alienate, silence, exclude and dehumanise in unacceptable ways. There is no clause that binds us to find 'the good' in Rhodes' character, nor to sanitise the imperialist, colonial agenda he propagated."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theguardian.com_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian.com-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_over_recipients_declining_to_pursue_careers_in_public_service">Criticism over recipients declining to pursue careers in public service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodes_Scholarship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Criticism over recipients declining to pursue careers in public service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The tendency of a growing number of Rhodes Scholars to enter business or private law as opposed to public service, for which the scholarship was intended, has been a source of frequent criticism and "occasional embarrassment".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Writing in 2009, the Secretary of the Rhodes Trust criticised the trend of Rhodes Scholars to pursue careers on Wall Street, noting that "more than twice as many [now] went into business in just one year than did in the entire 1970s", attributing it to "grotesque" remuneration offered by such occupations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> At least a "half dozen" 1990s Rhodes Scholars were partners at <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a> and, since the 1980s, <a href="/wiki/McKinsey" title="McKinsey">McKinsey</a> has had numerous Rhodes Scholars as partners. Similarly, of Rhodes Scholars who became attorneys, about one-third serve as staff attorneys for private corporations, while another third remain in private practice or academic posts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Schaeper and Schaper, "From 1904 to the present, the programme's critics have had two main themes: first, that too many scholars were content with comfortable, safe jobs in academe, in law, and in business; second, that too few had careers in government or other fields where public service was the number-one goal."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Sullivan" title="Andrew Sullivan">Andrew Sullivan</a> wrote in 1988 that "of the 1,900 or so living American scholars ... about 250 fill middle-rank administrative and professorial positions in middle-rank state colleges and universities ... [while] another 260...have ended up as lawyers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quality_of_Post-Graduate_Education_at_Oxford">Quality of Post-Graduate Education at Oxford</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodes_Scholarship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Quality of Post-Graduate Education at Oxford">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2007, an op-ed by two American Rhodes Scholars caused an "international row over Oxford's status as a top university"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> when they criticized the university's post-graduate education as "outdated" and "frustrating" in comparison to their education in the United States, specifically pointing to the perceived low quality of instruction and an insufficient scholarship stipend for living expenses. They also criticized the Rhodes application process itself, arguing that potential applicants should not apply unless they are "ready to study and live in Oxford."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>The original op-ed spurred responses on both sides of the Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Other students critiqued the authors for their tone of "ingratitude and entitlement," while <a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times" title="The Sunday Times">The Sunday Times</a> noted that it fueled the "long rivalry between Harvard, Cambridge and Oxford" and existing concerns about the quality of British graduate education. In response, the Rhodes Trust released two statements, one to the Sunday Times arguing that their view was "unrepresentative of the vast majority of Americans" studying at Oxford,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> and another as a reply to the original op-ed arguing that "false expectations," particularly for those uncertain about their degree choice, and going to Oxford for the "wrong reasons," could contribute to dissatisfaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_scholars_and_career_trajectories">Notable scholars and career trajectories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodes_Scholarship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Notable scholars and career trajectories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Rhodes_Scholars" title="List of Rhodes Scholars">List of Rhodes Scholars</a></div>
<p>Surveying the history of the Rhodes Scholarship, Schaeper and Schaeper conclude that "while "few of them have 'changed the world' ... most of them have been a credit to their professions ... and communities", finding that "the great majority of Rhodes Scholars have had solid, respectable careers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Eight former Rhodes scholars subsequently became heads of government or heads of <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">state</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Wasim_Sajjad" title="Wasim Sajjad">Wasim Sajjad</a> (Pakistan), <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> (United States), <a href="/wiki/Dom_Mintoff" title="Dom Mintoff">Dom Mintoff</a> (Malta), <a href="/wiki/John_Turner" title="John Turner">John Turner</a> (Canada), <a href="/wiki/Norman_Manley" title="Norman Manley">Norman Manley</a> (Jamaica) and three Australian prime ministers: <a href="/wiki/Bob_Hawke" title="Bob Hawke">Bob Hawke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Abbott" title="Tony Abbott">Tony Abbott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Turnbull" title="Malcolm Turnbull">Malcolm Turnbull</a>.</p>
<p>Between 1951–1997, 32% of American Rhodes Scholars pursued careers in education and academia, 20% in law, 15% in business and 10% in medicine and science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Although Cecil Rhodes imagined that scholars would "pursue a full-time career in government ... the number of scholars in local, state and federal government has remained at a steady 7 percent" over the past century. Of the 200 or so scholars who have spent their careers in government, "most of them have had solid, but undistinguished careers," while "perhaps forty or can be said to have had a significant, national impact in their particular areas."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>At least two scholars have served prison terms since the 1980s (one of them being <a href="/wiki/Mel_Reynolds" title="Mel Reynolds">Mel Reynolds</a>, for statutory rape) and around three dozen have committed suicide in the history of the programme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_to_other_post-graduate_scholarships">Comparison to other post-graduate scholarships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodes_Scholarship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Comparison to other post-graduate scholarships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Scholarship#Comparison_to_other_post-graduate_scholarships" title="Marshall Scholarship">Comparison of the Marshall Scholarship to other Scholarships</a></div>
<p>As the first large-scale programme of international scholarships,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">[3]</a></sup> the Rhodes Scholarship inspired the creation of other awards, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>The international <a href="/wiki/Fulbright_Program" title="Fulbright Program">Fulbright Program</a> to teach English or study in over 150 countries (1946)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Scholarship" title="Marshall Scholarship">Marshall Scholarship</a> for Americans to study at any university in the United Kingdom (1953)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Scholarship" title="Kennedy Scholarship">Kennedy Scholarship</a> for British nationals to study at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> (1964)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Harkness_Fellowship" title="Harkness Fellowship">Harkness Fellowship</a> for citizens of nine countries to conduct research related to health policy in the United States (1997)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Mitchell_Scholarship" title="Mitchell Scholarship">Mitchell Scholarship</a> for Americans to study at any university in Ireland (2000)</li>
<li>The international <a href="/wiki/Gates_Cambridge_Scholarship" title="Gates Cambridge Scholarship">Gates Cambridge Scholarship</a> to study at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> (2000)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/John_Monash_Scholars" title="John Monash Scholars">John Monash Scholarship</a> for Australians to study at any university worldwide (2004)</li>
<li>The international <a href="/wiki/Yenching_Scholars" title="Yenching Scholars">Yenching Scholarship</a> for study at <a href="/wiki/Peking_University" title="Peking University">Peking University</a> (2015)</li>
<li>The international <a href="/wiki/Schwarzman_Scholars" title="Schwarzman Scholars">Schwarzman Scholarship</a> for study at <a href="/wiki/Tsinghua_University" title="Tsinghua University">Tsinghua University</a> (2016)</li>
<li>The international <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knight-Hennessy_Scholars" title="Knight-Hennessy Scholars">Knight-Hennessy Scholars</a> to study at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> (2018)</li>
</ul>
<p>In structure and selection criteria, the scholarship is most similar to the <a href="/wiki/Gates_Cambridge_Scholarship" title="Gates Cambridge Scholarship">Gates Cambridge Scholarship</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Scholarship" title="Marshall Scholarship">Marshall Scholarship</a>. Like the Rhodes, the Marshall is a two-stage geographic scholarship organised through districts in selecting countries. Like the Gates Cambridge, the Rhodes is tenable at only one university. In structure, the Marshall Scholarship is more flexible than the Rhodes Scholarship, in that Marshall Scholars can study at any British university and can also attend a different university each year during a scholar's tenure. In addition, a limited number of one-year Marshall scholarships are available. The Marshall Scholarship also places a greater emphasis on academic achievement and potential, requiring a minimum grade point average of 3.7. For example, winners of the Marshall Scholarship from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> have had average GPAs of 3.92, while winners of the Rhodes Scholarship from Harvard have had an average GPA of 3.8.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
