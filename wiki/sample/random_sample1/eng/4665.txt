<p><b>Lhotse Shar</b> is a subsidiary mountain of <a href="/wiki/Lhotse" title="Lhotse">Lhotse</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains" title="List of highest mountains">11th-highest mountain</a> on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, at 8,383 m (27,503 ft) high. It has the highest fatality rate of all the <a href="/wiki/Eight-thousander" title="Eight-thousander">eight-thousanders</a> – for every 2 people who summit the mountain, one person dies attempting to. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fatality_rate_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-fatality_rate-1">[1]</a></sup> However, this is primarily because most climbers tend to try to ascend to the primary peak of Lhotse, rather than the lowest summit of the mountain. It was first climbed by <a href="/wiki/Sepp_Mayerl" title="Sepp Mayerl">Sepp Mayerl</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rolf_Walter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rolf Walter (page does not exist)">Rolf Walter</a> on May 12, 1970.</p>
<p>On April 27, 1980, <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Jaeger" title="Nicolas Jaeger">Nicolas Jaeger</a> was seen for the last time at 8,200 metres (26,900 ft) altitude during an attempted ascent of Lhotse Shar in Nepal, and is presumed dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monde_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-monde-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
