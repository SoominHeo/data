<p><b>Saint Roch</b> or <b>Rocco</b> (lived c. 1348 – 15/16 August 1376/79 (traditionally c. 1295 – 16 August 1327<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>)) was a <a href="/wiki/Saint#Catholicism" title="Saint">Catholic saint</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Confessor" title="Confessor">confessor</a> whose death is commemorated on 16 August; he is specially invoked against the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">plague</a>. He may also be called <b>Rock</b> in English, and has the designation of St Rollox in <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, said to be a corruption of St Roch's <a href="/wiki/Loch" title="Loch">Loch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He is a patron saint of dogs, falsely accused people, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bachelors" title="Bachelors">bachelors</a>, and several other things.</p>

<p>According to his <i>Acta</i> and his <i>vita</i> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Golden_Legend" title="Golden Legend">Golden Legend</a></i>, he was born at <a href="/wiki/Montpellier" title="Montpellier">Montpellier</a>, at that time "upon the border of France", as the <i>Golden Legend</i> has it,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the son of the noble governor of that city. Even his birth was accounted a miracle, for his noble mother had been barren until she prayed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" title="Virgin Mary">Virgin Mary</a>. Miraculously marked from birth with a red cross on his breast that grew as he did, he early began to manifest strict <a href="/wiki/Asceticism" title="Asceticism">asceticism</a> and great devoutness; on days when his "devout mother fasted twice in the week, and the blessed child Rocke abstained him twice also, when his mother fasted in the week, and would suck his mother but once that day".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the death of his parents in his twentieth year he distributed all his worldly goods among the poor like <a href="/wiki/Francis_of_Assisi" title="Francis of Assisi">Francis of Assisi</a>—though his father on his deathbed had ordained him governor of Montpellier—and set out as a mendicant pilgrim for Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Coming into Italy during an epidemic of <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">plague</a>, he was very diligent in tending the sick in the public hospitals at <a href="/wiki/Acquapendente" title="Acquapendente">Acquapendente</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cesena" title="Cesena">Cesena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rimini" title="Rimini">Rimini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Novara" title="Novara">Novara</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and Rome, and is said to have effected many miraculous cures by prayer and the <a href="/wiki/Sign_of_the_cross" title="Sign of the cross">sign of the cross</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Laying_on_of_hands" title="Laying on of hands">touch of his hand</a>. At Rome, according to the <i>Golden Legend</i> he preserved the "cardinal of Angleria in Lombardy"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> by making the mark of the cross on his forehead, which miraculously remained. Ministering at <a href="/wiki/Piacenza" title="Piacenza">Piacenza</a> he himself finally fell ill. He was expelled from the town; and withdrew into the forest, where he made himself a hut of boughs and leaves, which was miraculously supplied with water by a spring that arose in the place; he would have perished had not a dog belonging to a nobleman named Gothard Palastrelli supplied him with bread and licked his wounds, healing them. Count Gothard, following his hunting dog that carried the bread, discovered Saint Roch and became his acolyte.</p>
<p>On his return incognito to Montpellier he was arrested as a spy (by orders of his own uncle) and thrown into prison, where he languished five years and died on 16 August 1327, without revealing his name, to avoid worldly glory. (Evidence suggests, as mentioned earlier, that the previous events occurred, instead at Voghera in the 1370s.) After his death, according to the <i>Golden Legend</i>,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>anon an angel brought from heaven a table divinely written with letters of gold into the prison, which he laid under the head of S. Rocke. And in that table was written that God had granted to him his prayer, that is to wit, that who that calleth meekly to S. Rocke he shall not be hurt with any hurt of pestilence</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The townspeople recognized him as well by his <a href="/wiki/Birthmark" title="Birthmark">birthmark</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> he was soon canonized in the popular mind,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and a great church erected in veneration.</p>
<p>The date (1327) asserted by Francesco Diedo for Saint Roch's death would precede the traumatic advent of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> in Europe (1347–49) after long centuries of absence, for which a rich iconography of the plague, its victims and its protective saints was soon developed, in which the iconography of Roche finds its historical place: previously the <i>topos</i> did not exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoeckl200135_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoeckl200135-11">[11]</a></sup> In contrast, however, St. Roch of Montpellier cannot be dismissed based on dates of a specific plague event. In medieval times, the term "plague" was used to indicate a whole array of illnesses and epidemics.</p>
<p>The first literary account is an undated <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/acta" title="wikt:acta">Acta</a></i> that is labeled, by comparison with the longer, elaborated accounts that were to follow, <i>Acta Breviora</i>, which relies almost entirely on standardized <a href="/wiki/Hagiography" title="Hagiography">hagiographic <i>topoi</i></a> to celebrate and promote the cult of Roch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The story that when the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Constance" title="Council of Constance">Council of Constance</a> was threatened with plague in 1414, public processions and prayers for the intercession of Roch were ordered, and the outbreak ceased, is provided by Francesco Diedo, the Venetian governor of <a href="/wiki/Brescia" title="Brescia">Brescia</a>, in his <i>Vita Sancti Rochi</i>, 1478. The cult of Roch gained momentum during the <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">bubonic plague</a> that passed through northern Italy in 1477–79.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_figure">Historical figure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Roch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historical figure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the doctoral thesis of history student Pierre Bolle in 2001,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Saint Roch is a hagiographical <a href="/wiki/Doublet_(linguistics)" title="Doublet (linguistics)">doublet</a> of a more ancient saint, <a href="/wiki/Racho_of_Autun" title="Racho of Autun">Racho of Autun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burgundy" title="Burgundy">Burgundy</a> who died about 660. Racho was invoked for protection against storms and Bolle believes that his name was the basis of the name of this saint and of his patronage of plague-sufferers via a process of <a href="/wiki/Apheresis_(linguistics)" title="Apheresis (linguistics)">aphaeresis</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> word for a storm, <i>tempeste</i>, to -<i>peste</i> "plague". This also accords with the <a href="/wiki/Humorism" title="Humorism">equilibrium of humours theory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Medieval_medicine_of_Western_Europe" title="Medieval medicine of Western Europe">medieval medicine of Western Europe</a> that held that illness could be caused by corruption of the air.</p>
<p>Gian Paolo Vico, of the Associazione San Rocco Italia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> states that a prisoner of French origin who had been held there for five years died in <a href="/wiki/Voghera" title="Voghera">Voghera</a>, Italy during the night of 15–16 August between 1376 and 1379. This prisoner had, according to some sources, attained a certain fame for sanctity in <a href="/wiki/Piacenza" title="Piacenza">Piacenza</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarmato" title="Sarmato">Sarmato</a>. According to Vico, the 1391 calendar of Voghera records a <a href="/wiki/Midsummer" title="Midsummer">midsummer</a> festival in honor of <i>Sancti Rochi</i> (Saint Roch of <a href="/wiki/Montpellier" title="Montpellier">Montpellier</a>, on 16 August) and not <i>Sancti Rochonis</i> (Racho of Autun, on 25 January), indicating the existence of two different saints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ascogni_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ascogni-16">[16]</a></sup> This information strongly suggests that a local cult and feast of Roch of Montpellier existed at least as early as 1391, starting in Voghera before Montpellier. We also have documentation that the body of Roch of Montpellier was in Voghera in 1469 and that it has been venerated since at least then. There is also indications of a feast in his honor being celebrated in 1483 in the presence of his remains. This information has led to the now common belief that Roch probably died in Voghera instead of Montpellier.</p>

<p>His popularity, originally in central and northern Italy and at Montpellier, spread through Spain, France, <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a>, Brazil and Germany, where he was often interpolated into the roster of the <a href="/wiki/Fourteen_Holy_Helpers" title="Fourteen Holy Helpers">Fourteen Holy Helpers</a>, whose veneration spread in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a>. The magnificent 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Scuola_Grande_di_San_Rocco" title="Scuola Grande di San Rocco">Scuola Grande di San Rocco</a> and the adjacent <a href="/wiki/San_Rocco,_Venice" title="San Rocco, Venice">church of San Rocco</a> were dedicated to him by a confraternity at <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, where his body was said to have been surreptitiously translated and was triumphantly inaugurated in 1485;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarshall1994505_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarshall1994505-17">[17]</a></sup> the <i><a href="/wiki/Scuola_Grande_di_San_Rocco" title="Scuola Grande di San Rocco">Scuola Grande</a></i> is famous for its sequence of paintings by <a href="/wiki/Tintoretto" title="Tintoretto">Tintoretto</a>, who painted St Roch in glory in a ceiling canvas (1564).</p>

<p>We know for certain that the body of St. Roch was carried from Voghera, instead of Montpellier as previously thought, to Venice in 1485. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_VI" title="Pope Alexander VI">Pope Alexander VI</a> (1492–1503) built a church and a hospital in his honor. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_III" title="Pope Paul III">Pope Paul III</a> (1534–1549) instituted a <a href="/wiki/Confraternity" title="Confraternity">confraternity</a> of St. Roch. This was raised to an arch-confraternity in 1556 by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_IV" title="Pope Paul IV">Pope Paul IV</a>; it still thrives today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Saint Roch had not been officially recognized as yet, however. In 1590 the Venetian ambassador at Rome reported back to the Serenissima that he had been repeatedly urged to present the witnesses and documentation of the life and miracles of San Rocco, already deeply entrenched in the Venetian life, because <a href="/wiki/Pope_Sixtus_V" title="Pope Sixtus V">Pope Sixtus V</a> "is strong in his opinion either to canonize him or else to remove him from the ranks of the saints"; the ambassador had warned a cardinal of the general scandal that would result if the widely venerated San Rocco were impugned as an impostor. Sixtus did not pursue the matter but left it to later popes to proceed with the canonization process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> His successor, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIV" title="Pope Gregory XIV">Pope Gregory XIV</a> (1590–1591), added Roch of Montpellier, who had already been memorialized in the Holy Sacrifice of the Mass for two centuries, to the Roman Catholic Church <a href="/wiki/Martyrology" title="Martyrology">Martyrology</a>, thereby fixing August 16 as his universal feast day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ascogni_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ascogni-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Numerous brotherhoods have been instituted in his honor. He is usually represented in the garb of a pilgrim, often lifting his tunic to demonstrate the plague sore in his thigh, and accompanied by a dog carrying a loaf in its mouth. The <a href="/wiki/Third_Order_of_Saint_Francis" title="Third Order of Saint Francis">Third Order of Saint Francis</a>, by tradition, claims him as a member and includes his feast on its own <a href="/wiki/Calendar_of_saints" title="Calendar of saints">calendar of saints</a>, observing it on August 17.</p>
<p>Saint Roch joined <a href="/wiki/Gerard_of_Lunel" title="Gerard of Lunel">Gerard of Lunel</a> (San Gerardo) as a patron saint of the city of <a href="/wiki/Potenza" title="Potenza">Potenza</a>, Italy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Saint_Roch_in_art">Saint Roch in art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Roch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Saint Roch in art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the Black Death, especially the Italian plague epidemic of 1477-79, new images of Christian martyrs and saints appeared and Saint Roch gained new fame and popularity. The religious art of the time emphasized the importance of the saint to plague-ridden Christians.</p>
<p>The new plague-related images of Saint Roch were drawn from a variety of sources. Plague texts dating from ancient and classical times, as well as Christian, scientific and folk beliefs, all contributed to this emerging visual tradition. Some of the most popular symbols of plague were swords, darts, and most especially arrows. There was also a prevalence of memento mori themes, dark clouds, and astrological signs (signa magna) such as comets, which were often referenced by physicians and writers of plague tracts as causes of plague. The physical symptoms of plague – a raised arm, a tilted head, or a collapsed body – began to symbolize plague in post-Black Death painting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Plague saints offered hope and healing before, during, and after times of plague. A specific style of painting, the plague votive, was considered a talisman for warding off plague. It portrayed a particular saint as an intercessor between God and the person or persons who commissioned the painting – usually a town, government, lay confraternity, or religious order to atone for the "collective guilt" of the community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>These plague votives worked as a psychological defense against disease in which people attempted to manipulate their situation through requesting the intercession of a saint against the arrows of plague. Rather than a society depressed and resigned to repeated epidemics, these votives represent people taking positive steps to regain control over their environment. Paintings of St. Roch represent the confidence in which renaissance worshipers sought to access supernatural aid in overcoming the ravages of plague.</p>
<p>The very abundance of means by which people invoked the aid of the celestial court is essential in understanding Renaissance responses to the disease. Rather than depression or resignation, people "possessed a confidence that put even an apocalyptic disaster of the magnitude of the Black Death into perspective of God's secure and benevolent plan for humankind."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The plague votives functioned both to request intercessory aid from plague saints and to provide catharsis for a population that had just witnessed the profound bodily destruction of the plague. By showing plague saints such as St. Roch and St. Sebastian, votives influenced the distribution of God's mercy by invoking the memory of the human suffering experienced by Christ during the Passion. In the art of St. Roch after 1477 the saint displayed the wounds of his martyrdom without evidence of pain or suffering. Roch actively lifted his clothing to display the plague bubo in his thigh. This display of his plague bubo showed that "he welcomed his disease as a divinely sent opportunity to imitate the sufferings of Christ… [his] patient endurance [of the physical suffering of plague was] a form of martyrdom."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marshall_1994_505_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marshall_1994_505-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Roch's status as a pilgrim who suffered plague is paramount in his iconography. "The sight of Roch scarred by the plague yet alive and healthy must have been an emotionally charged image of a promised cure. Here was literal proof that one could survive the plague, a saint who had triumphed over the disease in his own flesh."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marshall_1994_505_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Marshall_1994_505-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Roch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Palmi" title="Palmi">Palmi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, the feast of San Rocco is held on August 16. There are numerous traditions. During the <a href="/wiki/Procession" title="Procession">procession</a> of the statue through the streets, some faithful participate with votive offerings, stripped to the waist, wearing a cloak of thorns of wild broom (called "spalas"). The procession lasts four and a half hours and covers a seven-mile route, with the participation of about 30,000 devotees. Another form of votive offering is an anatomical human made of wax, as a sign of gratitude for a miraculous healing. During the days of the festival the streets echo to the rhythm of drums, two <a href="/wiki/Gigantes_y_cabezudos" title="Gigantes y cabezudos">giants</a> of cardboard called "Mata" and "Griffon".</li>
<li>A popular Spanish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tongue_twister" title="Tongue twister">tongue twister</a> is <i>El perro de san Roque no tiene rabo porque Ramón Ramírez se lo ha robado</i> ("Saint Roch's dog has no tail because Ramón Ramírez stole it").</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, Saint Roch's day, though not as celebrated as it once was, is considered the "birthday of all dogs", in which the dogs around town can be seen with colorful ribbons tied to them.</li>
<li>The main train station of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montpellier,_France" title="Montpellier, France">Montpellier, France</a> is named after St. Roch, as well as a church, a hospital and many squares and streets.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Bingen_am_Rhein" title="Bingen am Rhein">Bingen</a>, Germany there is a St. Rochus pilgrimage church on top of a hill. Every year in August a one-week pilgrimage—the "St. Rochusfest"—is held in memory of a 17th-century vow of the city council.</li>
<li>Some churches that are named after the saint distribute, as a pietistic practice, the "bread of Saint Rocco" to parishioners on August 16, his feast day.</li>
<li>Saint Rocco's procession is featured in the movie <i><a href="/wiki/The_Godfather_Part_II" title="The Godfather Part II">The Godfather Part II</a></i>. In the procession, the St. Rocco Society of Potenza, Inc., which still exists after its commencement in 1889, carries the Italian-made (original) statue in a similar manner that a replica statue is carried today. The original statue, also from 1889, can be viewed in St. Joseph's Church in New York City.</li>
<li>According to <a href="/wiki/Montague_Summers" title="Montague Summers">Montague Summers</a>' <i>The Vampire in Europe</i>, St. Roch was prayed to in Poland to ward off vampire attacks.</li>
<li>The initials VSR (Viva San Rocco or Long Live St. Rocco) can still be found above doorways in Europe: This was engraved as a plea to ward off the plague.</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Batman:_Arkham_City" title="Batman: Arkham City">Batman: Arkham City</a></i>, if the player visits <a href="/wiki/Calendar_Man" title="Calendar Man">Calendar Man</a> on the Feast Day of St. Roch (August 16), he will talk about the year where he set rabid dogs upon <a href="/wiki/Gotham_City" title="Gotham City">Gotham City</a> on that day. He notes that it is an obscure holiday but remarks "I couldn't imagine a better way to celebrate the dog days of summer."</li>
<li>In 2012, <a href="/wiki/GMA_Network" title="GMA Network">GMA 7</a> aired a drama named after him entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Aso_ni_San_Roque" title="Aso ni San Roque">Aso ni San Roque</a></i>.</li>
<li>St Roch's Football Club are also a semi professional football from the Royston area of Glasgow, affectionately known as "the Candy" or "the Candy Rock" because the green and yellow stripes of their shirts resemble a rock confectionery. On their badge there is a picture of St. Roch and his dog.</li>
<li>A statue of St. Rocco, with a dog at his feet, is featured in the 2014 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Drop_(film)" title="The Drop (film)">The Drop</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hardy" title="Tom Hardy">Tom Hardy</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Gandolfini" title="James Gandolfini">James Gandolfini</a> (his last feature film). The protagonist Bob, played by Hardy, names his adopted dog Rocco.</li>
</ul>
