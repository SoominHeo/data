<p><i><b>Lokta Paper</b></i> is a wildcrafted, handmade artisan paper indigenous to <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview_of_lokta_paper">Overview of lokta paper</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lokta_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview of lokta paper">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nepalese handmade lokta paper is made from the fibrous inner bark of high elevation <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrubs" title="Shrubs">shrubs</a> primarily from two species of <a href="/wiki/Daphne_(plant)" title="Daphne (plant)">Daphne (plant)</a> (Greek: meaning "Laurel"): <a href="/wiki/Daphne_bholua" title="Daphne bholua">Daphne bholua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daphne_papyracea" title="Daphne papyracea">Daphne papyracea</a>, known collectively and vernacularly as lokta bushes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-himalayantimes2_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-himalayantimes2-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lokta bushes proliferate in open clusters or colonies on the southern slopes of Nepal’s <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayan</a> forests between 1,600 and 4,000 m (c.5,250–13,000 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-positivedeviance_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-positivedeviance-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historically the handcrafting of lokta paper occurred in the rural areas of Nepal, most notably in the <a href="/wiki/Baglung_District" title="Baglung District">Baglung District</a>. Today raw lokta paper is produced in more than 22 districts in Nepal, but finished lokta paper products are produced only in Kathmandu Valley and Janakpur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HimalayanTimes_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-HimalayanTimes-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lokta paper's durability and resistance to tearing, humidity, insects and mildew have traditionally made lokta paper the preferred choice for the recording official government records (see photo on right) and sacred religious texts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-positivedeviance_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-positivedeviance-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_lokta_paper_making">History of lokta paper making</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lokta_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of lokta paper making">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest surviving lokta paper document appears in Nepal's National Archives in Kathmandu in the form of the sacred Buddhist text, the Karanya Buha Sutra. The Karanya Buha Sutra was written in Lichchhavi script and block printed on lokta paper and is estimated to be between 1,000 and 1,900 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forestrynepal_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-forestrynepal-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the introduction of paper craft imports from Tibet in the 1930s, the production of handmade lokta paper began to decline. By the 1960s competition from commercially mass-produced paper from India placed the Nepalese handmade paper industry in a state of terminal decline with only a few families in Baglung and neighboring Parbat District retaining the traditional knowledge of handmade lokta paper production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-positivedeviance_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-positivedeviance-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1970s interest in rejuvenating lokta craft paper making occurred as the tourism industry in Nepal began to grow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geoturistica_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-geoturistica-5">[5]</a></sup> Moreover, an effective conservation program was started in 1970 for the development of national parks and wildlife reserves in Nepal to provide raw materials for the development of forest based industries such as the production of lokta paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biodiversity_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-biodiversity-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1980s the United Nations Children's Fund (UNICEF) and the Agricultural Development Bank of Nepal/Small Farmer Development Program (ADBN/SFDP) launched the CDHP (Community Development and Health Project) project to revive Nepal's indigenous paper making processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-positivedeviance_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-positivedeviance-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 1980s and early 1990s, with the growth of the popularity of lokta paper on the rise, Nepalese social and environmental entrepreneurs sought out and developed international trading partners and the export market for handmade lokta paper was established. Today the handmade paper industry in Nepal is growing at a rate of 15% per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-positivedeviance_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-positivedeviance-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paper_making_process">Paper making process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lokta_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Paper making process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fabrication_papier_lokta.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="3072" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Fabrication_papier_lokta.jpg/250px-Fabrication_papier_lokta.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Fabrication_papier_lokta.jpg/375px-Fabrication_papier_lokta.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Fabrication_papier_lokta.jpg/500px-Fabrication_papier_lokta.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

Manufacturing of lokta paper, <a href="/wiki/Kathmandu" title="Kathmandu">Kathmandu</a></div>

<p>Lokta paper production is a forest-based industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-positivedeviance_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-positivedeviance-2">[2]</a></sup> It relies as much on a ready supply of Daphne bark as it does on the skills of traditional paper makers and block printers, and on markets for end products. There are four main steps in manufacturing and marketing of lokta paper and lokta paper craft products: 1) harvesting the lokta bark 2) processing the paper pulp 3) producing craft products from the finished paper 4) marketing the final products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-positivedeviance_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-positivedeviance-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_responsibility_and_environmental_sustainability_of_the_lokta_paper_industry_in_Nepal">Social responsibility and environmental sustainability of the lokta paper industry in Nepal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lokta_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Social responsibility and environmental sustainability of the lokta paper industry in Nepal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Daphne shrub, a subspecies of laurel, grows wildly and covers more than million hectares of forest land in 55 districts of Nepal, of which 25 districts have an abundant supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ovop_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ovop-7">[7]</a></sup> Like the laurel plants (<a href="/wiki/Laurus_nobilis" title="Laurus nobilis">laurus nobilis</a>) of Turkey and Syria, the Daphne shrub is one of the world's few underutilized species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cropsforthefuture_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-cropsforthefuture-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forestprotection_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-forestprotection-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lokta is a non-wood forest product (NWFP) harvested from protected areas (national parks, reserves, conservation areas) and is an important reservoir of biological resources maintained under in situ condition in the unique and diverse Himalayan ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biodiversity_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-biodiversity-6">[6]</a></sup> When harvested, the lokta bush automatically regenerates to a fully grown 4-5 meter plant with in 5–7 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biodiversity_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-biodiversity-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><b>Women’s involvement</b></i>: The poor rural women of Nepal have traditionally been the principle forest users. Current economic conditions have reinforced the local employment of women, as many men are leaving the rural villages in search of employment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biodiversity_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-biodiversity-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cropsforthefuture_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-cropsforthefuture-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Cottage Industry Department of Nepal reports 377 registered handmade paper production industries, out of approximately 600 units operating in the country. Of these, 175 manufacture about 30,000 metric tons of paper products each year. Yet, despite this major increase in handmade paper production in recent years, large scale lokta resources remain untapped. Handmade lokta-based craft paper products continue to offer considerable economic sustainability for poor rural Nepalese women due to their high-quality niche market potential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cropsforthefuture_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-cropsforthefuture-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_uses_of_lokta_paper_crafts">Modern uses of lokta paper crafts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lokta_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modern uses of lokta paper crafts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the traditional uses of lokta paper were largely restricted to government documents or religious texts, today lokta paper use is wide spread. Lokta paper is used for prayer flags,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forestrynepal_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-forestrynepal-4">[4]</a></sup> book bindings,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forestrynepal_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-forestrynepal-4">[4]</a></sup> restaurant menus (see photo on right), wallpaper<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-10">[10]</a></sup> wrapping paper to retain the potency of incense, spices and medicine,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forestrynepal_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-forestrynepal-4">[4]</a></sup> packaging,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loktapaperpackaging_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-loktapaperpackaging-11">[11]</a></sup> origami,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Nathan Miller Chocolate bean to bar chocolate packaging,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and even dresses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thehimalayantimes_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-thehimalayantimes-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
