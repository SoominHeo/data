<p><b>Loughton</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/aʊ/ 'ou' in 'pout'">aʊ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ən/ 'on' in 'button'">ən</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a town and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epping_Forest_(district)" title="Epping Forest (district)">Epping Forest</a> district of <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>. It is located between 11 and 13 miles (21 km) north east of <a href="/wiki/Charing_Cross" title="Charing Cross">Charing Cross</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, south of the <a href="/wiki/M25_motorway" title="M25 motorway">M25</a> and west of the <a href="/wiki/M11_motorway" title="M11 motorway">M11 motorway</a> and has boundaries with <a href="/wiki/Chingford" title="Chingford">Chingford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waltham_Abbey_(town)" title="Waltham Abbey (town)">Waltham Abbey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theydon_Bois" title="Theydon Bois">Theydon Bois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chigwell" title="Chigwell">Chigwell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buckhurst_Hill" title="Buckhurst Hill">Buckhurst Hill</a>. Loughton includes three conservation areas and there are 56 <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed buildings</a> in the town, together with a further 50 locally listed.</p>
<p>The parish of Loughton covers an area of about 3,724 acres (15 km<sup>2</sup>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loughton_CP_ward_areas_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Loughton_CP_ward_areas-1">[1]</a></sup> of which over 1,300 acres (5 km<sup>2</sup>) are part of <a href="/wiki/Epping_Forest" title="Epping Forest">Epping Forest</a>. The ancient parish contained over 3,900 acres (16 km<sup>2</sup>), but in 1996 some parts of the south of the old parish were transferred to <a href="/wiki/Buckhurst_Hill" title="Buckhurst Hill">Buckhurst Hill</a> parish, and other small portions to <a href="/wiki/Chigwell" title="Chigwell">Chigwell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theydon_Bois" title="Theydon Bois">Theydon Bois</a>. At the time of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2001" title="United Kingdom Census 2001">2001 census</a> Loughton had a population of 30,340,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loughton_CP_headcount_2001_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Loughton_CP_headcount_2001-2">[2]</a></sup> and at the 2011 Census, 31,106.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loughton_CP_headcount_2001_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Loughton_CP_headcount_2001-2">[2]</a></sup> It is the most populous civil parish in the Epping Forest district, and within Essex it is the second most populous civil parish (after <a href="/wiki/Canvey_Island" title="Canvey Island">Canvey Island</a>) and the second largest in area.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Loughton" title="History of Loughton">History of Loughton</a></div>
<p>The earliest structure in Loughton is <a href="/wiki/Loughton_Camp" title="Loughton Camp">Loughton Camp</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> earth fort in Epping Forest dating from around 500 BC. Hidden by dense undergrowth for centuries it was rediscovered in 1872.</p>
<p>The first references to the site of modern-day Loughton date from the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> period when it was known as <i>Lukintune</i> ("the farm of Luhha"). The earliest written evidence of this settlement is in the charter of <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a> in 1062 which granted various estates, including <i>Tippedene</i> (Debden) and <i>Alwartune</i> (Alderton Hall, in Loughton), to <a href="/wiki/Harold_Godwinson" title="Harold Godwinson">Harold Godwinson</a> (later King Harold II) following his re-founding of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waltham_Abbey_(abbey)" title="Waltham Abbey (abbey)">Waltham Abbey</a>. Following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_conquest" title="Norman conquest">Norman conquest</a>, the town is also mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> of 1086, with the name <i>Lochintuna</i>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Loughton_Hall-geograph.org.uk-1226307.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="427" data-file-width="640" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Loughton_Hall-geograph.org.uk-1226307.jpg/220px-Loughton_Hall-geograph.org.uk-1226307.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Loughton_Hall-geograph.org.uk-1226307.jpg/330px-Loughton_Hall-geograph.org.uk-1226307.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Loughton_Hall-geograph.org.uk-1226307.jpg/440px-Loughton_Hall-geograph.org.uk-1226307.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>The settlement remained a small village until the early 17th century when the high road was extended north through the forest. The road quickly became the main route from London to <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a>, and Loughton grew into an important stop with <a href="/wiki/Coaching_inn" title="Coaching inn">coaching inns</a>. The most significant of the great houses of this period, built as country retreats for wealthy City merchants and courtiers, was Loughton Hall, owned by <a href="/wiki/Mary_I_of_England" title="Mary I of England">Mary Tudor</a> two months before she became Queen Mary of England in 1553, and later by the Wroth family from 1578 to 1738. Sir Robert Wroth (c. 1576 – 1614) and his wife <a href="/wiki/Lady_Mary_Wroth" title="Lady Mary Wroth">Lady Mary Wroth</a> (1587 – c. 1652) entertained many of the great literary figures of the time, including <a href="/wiki/Ben_Jonson" title="Ben Jonson">Ben Jonson</a>, at the house. It was rebuilt in 1878 by Revd. J. W. Maitland, whose family held the manor for much of the 19th century. It is now a Veecare Homes care home and is a grade II listed building.</p>
<p>Loughton's growth since Domesday has largely been at the expense of the forest. Expansion towards the <a href="/wiki/River_Roding" title="River Roding">River Roding</a> was arrested owing to the often flooding marshy meadows, encroachments into the forest to the north and west of the village were nevertheless possible. Loughton landlords and villagers both exploited the forest waste (open spaces and scrub of the forest), but the trickle of forest destruction threatened to turn into a flood in the 19th century after royalty had lost interest in protecting the woodland as a hunting reserve. As the forest disappeared and landowners began enclosing more of it for private use, many began to express concern at the loss of such a significant natural resource and common land. Some Loughton villagers defied landowners to practice their ancient right to lop wood—a series of court cases, including one brought by the Loughton labourer <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Willingale" title="Thomas Willingale">Thomas Willingale</a>, was needed before the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London_Corporation" title="City of London Corporation">City of London Corporation</a> took legal action against the landowners' enclosures, resulting in the Epping Forest Act of 1878 which preserved the forest for use by the public.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The arrival of the railway spurred on the town's development. The railway first came to Loughton in 1856 when the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Counties_Railway" title="Eastern Counties Railway">Eastern Counties Railway</a>, (later the <a href="/wiki/Great_Eastern_Railway" title="Great Eastern Railway">Great Eastern Railway</a>), opened a branch line via <a href="/wiki/Woodford,_London" title="Woodford, London">Woodford</a>. In 1948 the line was electrified and transferred to <a href="/wiki/London_Transport_Executive" title="London Transport Executive">London Transport</a> to become part of the <a href="/wiki/Central_line_(London_Underground)" title="Central line (London Underground)">Central line</a> on the <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a>. The arrival of the railways also provided visitors from London with a convenient means of reaching Epping Forest and thus transforming it into the "East Enders' Playground".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ragged_School" title="Ragged School">Ragged School</a> Union began organising visits to the forest for parties of poor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_End" title="East End">East End</a> children in 1891 paid for by the Pearsons Fresh Air Fund. Loughton artist Octavius Dixie Deacon depicted many scenes of the town including some of its residents during the late Victorian period.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As the <a href="/wiki/Great_Eastern_Railway" title="Great Eastern Railway">Great Eastern Railway</a> Company did not offer workmen's fares, the town's development was of a middle-class character. Much of the housing in Loughton was built in the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edwardian_period" title="Edwardian period">Edwardian eras</a>, with significant expansion in the 1930s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Loughton was a fashionable place for artistic and scientific residents in Victorian and Edwardian times, and a number of prominent residents were renowned socialists, nonconformists, and social reformers. In the north-east is a post-war development being one of the London County Council's country estates. Built with the express purpose of co-locating industrial, retail and residential properties to facilitate supported re-location of London families affected by war damage within the Capital. Located within Debden's industrial estate is the former printing works of the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_England" title="Bank of England">Bank of England</a>; in 1993 the printing works were taken over by <a href="/wiki/De_La_Rue" title="De La Rue">De La Rue</a> on their winning the contract to print the banknotes. The headquarters of greeting card company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clinton_Cards" title="Clinton Cards">Clinton Cards</a> and construction firm Higgins Group are also located within the Debden Industrial Estate. In 2008, electronics firm <a href="/wiki/Amshold" title="Amshold">Amshold</a> announced their intention to move the group's headquarters to Loughton from <a href="/wiki/Brentwood,_Essex" title="Brentwood, Essex">Brentwood</a>. They moved to a site in Langston Road; in 2012, their property company <a href="/wiki/Amsprop" title="Amsprop">Amsprop</a> converted a headquarters building next to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Town_Council" title="Town Council">Town Council</a> offices in Rectory Lane.</p>
<p>In 2002 Loughton featured in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ITV1" title="ITV1">ITV1</a> programme <i><a href="/wiki/Essex_Wives" title="Essex Wives">Essex Wives</a></i>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Documentary_series" title="Documentary series">documentary series</a> about the lives of some of the <a href="/wiki/Nouveau_riche" title="Nouveau riche">nouveau riche</a> who have resided in the Essex <a href="/wiki/Satellite_town" title="Satellite town">satellite towns</a> of London since the 1980s. The series propelled <a href="/wiki/Jodie_Marsh" title="Jodie Marsh">Jodie Marsh</a>, one of its featured characters to fame. Journalists' use of the term "golden triangle" to describe the towns of Loughton, Buckhurst Hill and Chigwell for their propensity to attract wealthy footballers, soap-opera actors and TV celebrities as residents derives from this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The town has been used as a backdrop in other television series, notably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Only_Way_is_Essex" title="The Only Way is Essex">The Only Way is Essex</a>, and two shops in the High Rd are associated with members of its cast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Loughton is bounded by Epping Forest to the west and the Roding river valley to the east. After the Epping Forest Act of 1878 prohibited any further expansion of the town into the forest, the forest and the river have formed two natural barriers constraining any expansion westwards or eastwards, and consequently most of the growth in the last 100 years has been through <a href="/wiki/Infill#Urban_infill" title="Infill">infilling</a> and construction of new housing estates to the north and south of the old town centre, plus the purpose-built suburb of Debden to the north-east. The Roding valley is somewhat marshy and the river is prone to flooding, so construction close to the river is very limited and the majority of the land around it has been designated as a nature reserve or left as open space parkland. The M11 motorway that follows the course of the Roding along this section of its length is built on raised banks or <a href="/wiki/Overpass" title="Overpass">flyovers</a>, to avoid potential problems with flooding.</p>

<p>The highest parts of the town are the roads that border the forest's edge; from the green outside the Gardeners Arms <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_house" title="Public house">pub</a> near the junction of York Hill, Pump Hill and Baldwins Hill there are views of London, south-west Essex, Kent and Surrey. From here, on a clear day, there is a panoramic view of London landmarks and the North Downs beyond. There are numerous other fine views from different parts of the town, including one roughly at the junction of Traps Hill, Borders Lane, Alderton Hill and Spareleaze Hill, and another on Spring Grove and Hillcrest Road. In the valley between these two hills flows Loughton Brook, which rises in Epping Forest near Waltham Abbey and flows through the forest and Baldwins and Staples Ponds before traversing the town and emptying into the Roding.</p>
<p>There are several distinctive neighbourhoods in Loughton mostly identifiable by the building types incorporated during their development:</p>
<ul>
<li>Old Loughton refers to the original settlement which grew up around Loughton High Road.</li>
<li>Debden occupies about 650 acres/225 hectares to the north east of Loughton; <a href="/wiki/London_County_Council" title="London County Council">London County Council</a> built the woodland development between 1947 and 1952 out of county to rehouse people from London whose homes had been destroyed or damaged during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>.</li>
<li>Debden Green is a hamlet set around an ancient green in the north-east corner of the parish. <a href="/wiki/Debden_House" title="Debden House">Debden House</a> in Debden Green is an adult learning and conference centre run by the <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Newham" title="London Borough of Newham">London Borough of Newham</a>; the grounds include a campsite.</li>
<li>Goldings Manor is a modern estate of mostly large detached houses built in the grounds of 'Goldings Manor', a large mansion demolished after being hit during the Blitz. It comprises four residential streets; Broadstrood, Campions, Garden Way and Stanmore Way.</li>
<li>Great Woodcote Park is a modern housing estate at the southern end of Loughton, built on the site of the former North Farm.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Little_Cornwall" title="Little Cornwall">Little Cornwall</a> is a hilly area of north-west Loughton closest to Epping Forest characterised by steep hills, weatherboarded houses, narrow lanes and high holly hedges.</li>
<li>Roding Estate<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> or South Loughton is the area south-east of the <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a> Central line and was mostly built up between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a> and Second World War.</li>
</ul>
<p>Between 1839 and 31 March 2000 policing and crime prevention was provided by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Police" title="Metropolitan Police">Metropolitan Police</a>. From 1 April 2000 responsibilities were transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Essex_Police" title="Essex Police">Essex Police</a>. Telephone numbers in the town have the London (<a href="/wiki/020" title="020">020</a>) area code. This anomaly is shared with <a href="/wiki/Chigwell" title="Chigwell">Chigwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thames_Ditton" title="Thames Ditton">Thames Ditton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molesey" title="Molesey">Molesey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ewell" title="Ewell">Ewell</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Loughton" title="Politics of Loughton">Politics of Loughton</a></div>
<p>Loughton was an Urban District Council from 1900 to 1933, when it became part of <a href="/wiki/Chigwell_Urban_District" title="Chigwell Urban District">Chigwell Urban District</a> until 1974, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epping_Forest_(district)" title="Epping Forest (district)">Epping Forest District Council</a> was created. Loughton Town Council was established in 1996. The Town Council consists of 22 councillors representing 7 wards, elected for a four-year term.</p>
<p>At district council level, Loughton is represented by two councillors from each of the 7 wards, elected for a four-year term. At county council level, Loughton is split between three divisions, Buckhurst Hill &amp; Loughton South, Chigwell &amp; Loughton Broadway, and Loughton Central, each returning one councillor elected for a four-year term.</p>
<p>Loughton has been part of the <a href="/wiki/Epping_Forest_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Epping Forest (UK Parliament constituency)">Epping Forest parliamentary constituency</a> since its creation in 1974.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_arts">The arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drama">Drama</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Drama">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Loughton is home to the <a href="/wiki/East_15_Acting_School" title="East 15 Acting School">East 15 Acting School</a>. East 15 grew from the work of <a href="/wiki/Joan_Littlewood" title="Joan Littlewood">Joan Littlewood</a>'s famed <a href="/wiki/Theatre_Workshop" title="Theatre Workshop">Theatre Workshop</a>. Joan Littlewood's Theatre Workshop was based in <a href="/wiki/Stratford,_London" title="Stratford, London">Stratford</a>, London, whose postal district is E15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The School, which became part of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Essex" title="University of Essex">University of Essex</a> in 2000, includes the Corbett Theatre in its campus. Regular productions are staged at the theatre, which was named after <a href="/wiki/Harry_H._Corbett" title="Harry H. Corbett">Harry H. Corbett</a> (1925–1982), himself a Theatre Workshop member and benefactor of East 15. The theatre building is actually a converted medieval flint barn from <a href="/wiki/Ditchling" title="Ditchling">Ditchling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sussex" title="Sussex">Sussex</a> which was dismantled and rebuilt in Loughton.</p>
<p>The character actor <a href="/wiki/Jack_Watling" title="Jack Watling">Jack Watling</a> (1923–2001) lived for many years in Alderton Hall, Loughton. His son, <a href="/wiki/Giles_Watling" title="Giles Watling">Giles</a> (born 1953), also an actor, was born there. Actor and playwright <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ken_Campbell_(actor)" title="Ken Campbell (actor)">Ken Campbell</a> (1941–2008), nicknamed 'The Elf of Epping Forest', lived in Baldwins Hill, Loughton, where a <a href="/wiki/Blue_plaque" title="Blue plaque">blue plaque</a> to him was erected in 2013. Comedy-drama actor <a href="/wiki/Alan_Davies" title="Alan Davies">Alan Davies</a> (born 1966) grew up in Loughton, and attended Staples Road school. Actress <a href="/wiki/Jane_Carr" title="Jane Carr">Jane Carr</a> (born 1950), best known for her role as "Louise Mercer" in the American version of the <a href="/wiki/Sitcom" title="Sitcom">sitcom</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dear_John_(US_TV_series)" title="Dear John (US TV series)">Dear John</a></i> from 1988 to 1992, was born in Loughton.</p>

<p>Amateur drama is performed mainly at Lopping Hall. Performances are from Loughton Amateur Dramatic Society, founded in 1924, which until 2006 alternated with those from the now-defunct West Essex Repertory Company, founded in 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Lopping Hall opened in 1884 and was paid for by the Corporation of London to compensate villagers for the loss of traditional rights to lop wood in Epping Forest, rights which were bought out when the management of the forest was taken over by the Corporation in 1878. Lopping Hall served as Loughton's town hall and was the venue for most of the parish's social – and especially musical - activities during the early 20th century. There are ambitious plans by the Trustees for the building's restoration. There is also a full-scale theatre, the College Theatre, on the campus of Epping Forest College.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Loughton's classical music scene dates back to the late 19th century, when there were regular concerts by the Loughton Choral Society in Lopping Hall under the redoubtable conductorship of Henry Riding. Today, performances are mainly at two venues: Loughton Methodist Church hosts the annual Loughton Youth Music Festival, which showcases talented pupils from local schools and colleges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> St. John's Church festival choir undertakes extensive overseas tours, and in turn hosts well-known soloists, chamber and operatic groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The music hall artiste <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Collins" title="José Collins">José Collins</a> (1887–1958) lived at 107 High Road for many years. The hymn writer <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Fuller_Flower_Adams" title="Sarah Fuller Flower Adams">Sarah Flower Adams</a> (1805–1848) lived with her husband <a href="/wiki/William_Bridges_Adams" title="William Bridges Adams">William Bridges Adams</a> (1797–1872) at a house called 'Sunnybank', demolished in 1888 and replaced by No. 9 Woodbury Hill. Sarah's most famous hymn was "<a href="/wiki/Nearer,_My_God,_to_Thee" title="Nearer, My God, to Thee">Nearer, My God, to Thee</a>", apparently written at Loughton in 1840, while William, a locomotive engineer, was the inventor of the <a href="/wiki/Fishplate" title="Fishplate">fishplate</a> used to connect rail tracks.</p>
<p>Loughton is also home to the National Jazz Archive (see below), which hosts occasional jazz performances. Gladys Mills (1918–1978), a well-known music-hall pianist who performed as "<a href="/wiki/Mrs_Mills" title="Mrs Mills">Mrs Mills</a>", lived in Roding Road from 1934, and upon her marriage in 1947, in Barncroft Close until 1964. Loughton boasts a few rock and pop music connections; <a href="/wiki/Mark_Knopfler" title="Mark Knopfler">Mark Knopfler</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dire_Straits" title="Dire Straits">Dire Straits</a> was a lecturer at Loughton College (now <a href="/wiki/Epping_Forest_College" title="Epping Forest College">Epping Forest College</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Genesis_(band)" title="Genesis (band)">Genesis</a> song "The Battle of Epping Forest" is based on an actual event when rival East End gangs fought a turf war in the forest. The <i>Wake Arms</i> public house (now demolished), which was about 50 yards (46 m) north of the Loughton boundary in <a href="/wiki/Waltham_Abbey_(town)" title="Waltham Abbey (town)">Waltham Abbey</a> on a roundabout, was a notable rock music venue from 1968–1973, hosting bands such as <a href="/wiki/Black_Sabbath" title="Black Sabbath">Black Sabbath</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deep_Purple" title="Deep Purple">Deep Purple</a>, Genesis, <a href="/wiki/Pretty_Things" title="Pretty Things">Pretty Things</a>, <a href="/wiki/Status_Quo_(band)" title="Status Quo (band)">Status Quo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uriah_Heep_(band)" title="Uriah Heep (band)">Uriah Heep</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Graaf_Generator" title="Van der Graaf Generator">Van der Graaf Generator</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ray_Dorset" title="Ray Dorset">Ray Dorset</a>, the lead singer of <a href="/wiki/Mungo_Jerry" title="Mungo Jerry">Mungo Jerry</a>, had his first taste of fame when his band 'The Tramps' won the Loughton Beat Contest in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Roding Players is an amateur orchestra which rehearses at Roding Valley High School and gives three concerts a year in the Epping Forest area; composer Miles Harwood is Musical Director. Loughton Ladies Choir gives regular afternoon concerts in the Epping Forest area. Epping Forest Brass Band, founded in 1935, also has regular concerts in the Epping Forest area, and competes in national competitions and exhibitions. Loughton Cinema had a resident ladies' band during the 1930s. Music at the LMC is a series of concerts given by visiting artists in the winter months.</p>
<p>Loughton also has its own music academy the 'Loughton Music Academy' founded in 2001 to cope with the growing demand for music in the area. Performances are with full ochestral participation. The 'Community Music Initiative'or CMI is a charitable project led by the LMA which provides music lessons for schools in the area who do not benefit from musical facilities.</p>
<p>Loughton Folk Club was founded on 28 October 2010 and held its first Loughton Folk Day on 9 April 2011. The Club meets weekly at 8pm at Loughton Club, Station Road, Loughton<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opera_and_dance">Opera and dance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Opera and dance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1930,s Loughton was home to the Pollards Operas, outdoor operatic performances in the garden of a large house. These were directed by Iris Lemare (1902–1997) and produced by Geoffrey Dunn (1903–1981), a prominent impresario, actor and cinematographer, and included several first British performances of operas. Loughton Operatic Society, founded in 1894, is one of the oldest arts organisations in Essex, and still stages regular musicals and operas at Lopping Hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Epping Forest District Council's Arts Unit, Epping Forest Arts, stages occasional dance-based performance works in Loughton, with community and schools participation. Loughton School of Dancing, which meets at Lopping Hall, encourages the town's younger talent. Harlow Ballet, which stages full-scale amateur ballet productions at Harlow Playhouse, also recruits in the area.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_arts">Visual arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Visual arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The proximity of <a href="/wiki/Epping_Forest" title="Epping Forest">Epping Forest</a> has made Loughton a magnet for artists for many years. The sculptor and painter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Jacob_Epstein" title="Sir Jacob Epstein">Sir Jacob Epstein</a> (1880–1959) lived at 'Deerhurst' between 1933–1950, after having rented no.49 Baldwins Hill; he produced some of his best known works there. Artist <a href="/wiki/John_Strevens" title="John Strevens">John Strevens</a> (1902–1990) lived at 8 Lower Park Road from 1963 until his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Walter Spradbury (1889–1969), best known for his iconic interwar London Transport posters, lived nearby in <a href="/wiki/Buckhurst_Hill" title="Buckhurst Hill">Buckhurst Hill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EppingForestSpradbury_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-EppingForestSpradbury-16">[16]</a></sup> Octavius Deacon was a 19th-century naïve artist from Loughton who painted many amusing scenes of village life. <a href="/wiki/William_Lakin_Turner" title="William Lakin Turner">William Lakin Turner</a> lived and painted at Clovelly, York Hill, Loughton, in the 1890s. From 1908 to 1936, <a href="/wiki/William_Brown_Macdougall" title="William Brown Macdougall">William Brown Macdougall</a>, artist, and his wife, the author and translator, Margaret Armour, lived in Loughton. Juggler Mark Robertson (1963–1992) lived at 'The Avenue' and had a highly successful career appearing at the London Palladium and on many TV shows.</p>
<p>There is a thriving Loughton Arts Club, and there are frequent exhibitions by contemporary local artists and photographers at Loughton Library. Loughton Camera Club, a member of the East Anglian Federation of Photographic Societies, meets at Lopping Hall in Loughton, and holds regular exhibitions of members' work in Loughton Library and elsewhere.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cinema">Cinema</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cinema">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Early cinematic shows took place in the Lopping Hall. A purpose-built Loughton Cinema was opened by actress <a href="/wiki/Evelyn_Laye" title="Evelyn Laye">Evelyn Laye</a> on 9 October 1928; designed by local architect Theodore Legg, it could seat 847. This was later reduced to 700. The cinema was renamed the Century in 1953, and closed on 25 May 1963, and has since been demolished and replaced by shops. In July 2010 Loughton Town Council organised a screening of <i><a href="/wiki/An_Education" title="An Education">An Education</a></i>, the first film screening in Loughton since the closure of the cinema, and its success prompted the formation of the Loughton Film Society in September 2010 to redress the lack of a local cinema.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/George_Pearson_(filmmaker)" title="George Pearson (filmmaker)">George Pearson</a> (1875–1973), a pioneering director and film-writer in the early years of British cinematography, was headmaster of Staples Road Junior School, Loughton 1908–1913. Charles Ashton (1884 – c. 1968), film actor from the silent movie era, lived at 20 Carroll Hill, Loughton, from 1917–34. He starred in more than 20 films between 1918–29, including the first film version of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Monkey%27s_Paw" title="The Monkey's Paw">The Monkey's Paw</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Kitty_(1929_film)" title="Kitty (1929 film)">Kitty</a></i>, based on <a href="/wiki/Warwick_Deeping" title="Warwick Deeping">Warwick Deeping</a>'s novel of the same name.</p>
<p>Several films have been set in the Loughton area, including the 2001 TV movie <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_Money" title="Hot Money">Hot Money</a></i>, based on real events at Loughton's <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_England" title="Bank of England">Bank of England</a> printing works.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As with the visual arts, Epping Forest has long attracted and inspired writers. Shakespeare's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midsummer_Night%27s_Dream" title="Midsummer Night's Dream">Midsummer Night's Dream</a></i> was written for the marriage of Sir Thomas Heneage Vice-Chamberlain of the Royal Household to the Countess of Southampton, who lived near Loughton at <a href="/wiki/Copped_Hall" title="Copped Hall">Copped Hall</a>, where it was first performed in the long gallery in 1594.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lady_Mary_Wroth" title="Lady Mary Wroth">Lady Mary Wroth</a> (1586–1652), niece of poet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Philip_Sidney" title="Sir Philip Sidney">Sir Philip Sidney</a>, lived at Loughton Hall with her husband Sir Robert Wroth, and they turned the mansion into a centre of <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_English" title="Early Modern English">Jacobean</a> literary life. <a href="/wiki/Ben_Jonson" title="Ben Jonson">Ben Jonson</a> was a frequent visitor, and dedicated his play <i><a href="/wiki/The_Alchemist_(play)" title="The Alchemist (play)">The Alchemist</a></i> to Mary and poetry collection <i>The Forest</i> to Sir Robert. Lady Mary was an author of considerable repute in her own right, and her book <i>Urania</i> is generally regarded as the first full-length English novel by a woman.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Anthony_Trollope" title="Anthony Trollope">Anthony Trollope</a> (1815–1882) who lived for some time at nearby <a href="/wiki/Waltham_Cross" title="Waltham Cross">Waltham Cross</a>, set part of his novel <i><a href="/wiki/Phineas_Finn" title="Phineas Finn">Phineas Finn</a></i> (1869), which parodies corrupt electoral procedures, in a fictitious Loughton. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Hunter_(Encyclop%C3%A6dist)" title="Robert Hunter (Encyclopædist)">Robert Hunter</a>, lexicographer and encyclopaedist (1823-1897) built a house in Loughton, and there compiled his massive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Encyclopaedic_dictionary" title="Encyclopaedic dictionary">Encyclopaedic dictionary</a>.<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Wymark_Jacobs" title="William Wymark Jacobs">William Wymark Jacobs</a> (1863–1943) lived at The Outlook, Upper Park Road before moving to Feltham House, Goldings Road. Best known as the author of the short story <i><a href="/wiki/The_Monkey%27s_Paw" title="The Monkey's Paw">The Monkey's Paw</a></i>. Jacobs also wrote numerous sardonic short stories based in 'Claybury', which is a thinly-fictionalised Loughton. <a href="/wiki/Rudyard_Kipling" title="Rudyard Kipling">Rudyard Kipling</a> (1865–1936) stayed as a child at Goldings Hill Farm. <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Morrison" title="Arthur Morrison">Arthur Morrison</a> (1863–1945), best known for his grim novels about London's East End, lived in Salcombe House, Loughton High Road. <a href="/wiki/Hesba_Stretton" title="Hesba Stretton">Hesba Stretton</a> (1832–1911) was a children's author who lived in Loughton. Hesba Stretton was the pen name of Sarah Smith; her novels about the street children of Victorian London raised awareness of their plight. <a href="/wiki/Horace_Newte" title="Horace Newte">Horace Newte</a> lived at Alderton Hall and the Chestnuts: he was a prolific novelist. Another children's writer, Winifred Darch (1884–1960), taught at Loughton County High School for Girls 1906–1935 (now Roding Valley High School), as did the hymnodist and poet, Emily Chisholm (1910–1991), who lived in Loughton at 3 Lower Park Rd.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ruth_Rendell" title="Ruth Rendell">Ruth Rendell</a>, Baroness Rendell of Babergh (1930-2015), who lived in Shelley Grove, Loughton, was educated at Loughton County High School for Girls and subsequently worked as a journalist in Loughton at the West Essex Gazette. Some of her fiction is set in Epping Forest, and 'Little Cornwall', the hilly area of north-west Loughton close to Epping Forest, takes its name from her description in the novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Face_of_Trespass" title="The Face of Trespass">The Face of Trespass</a></i>. Much of her 2014 novel <i>The Girl Next Door</i> is set in the Loughton of 1944 and 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Poets associated with Loughton include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarah_Flower_Adams" title="Sarah Flower Adams">Sarah Flower Adams</a> (1805–1848), and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Byam_Martin#Sarah_Catherine_Martin" title="Thomas Byam Martin">Sarah Catherine Martin</a> (c. 1766 – 1826), author of the nursery rhyme "Old Mother Hubbard", who is buried in the churchyard of St. Nicholas Church, Loughton. <a href="/wiki/William_Sotheby" title="William Sotheby">William Sotheby</a> (1757–1833), poet and classicist, lived at Fairmead, Loughton. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfred_Tennyson,_1st_Baron_Tennyson" title="Alfred Tennyson, 1st Baron Tennyson">Alfred, Lord Tennyson</a> (1809–1892) lived at Beech Hill House, <a href="/wiki/High_Beach" title="High Beach">High Beach</a> 1837–1840 where he wrote parts of his magnum opus "<a href="/wiki/In_Memoriam_A.H.H." title="In Memoriam A.H.H.">In Memoriam</a>". <a href="/wiki/John_Clare" title="John Clare">John Clare</a> (1793–1864) lived at a private asylum at High Beach 1837–1841. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a> poet <a href="/wiki/Edward_Thomas_(poet)" title="Edward Thomas (poet)">Edward Thomas</a> (1878–1917) also lived at High Beach 1915–1917. The poet <a href="/wiki/George_Barker_(poet)" title="George Barker (poet)">George Barker</a> (1913–1991) was born at 116 Forest Road, Loughton. Geoffrey Ainger (1925–2013), who wrote the <a href="/wiki/Christmas_carol" title="Christmas carol">Christmas carols</a> "Born in the Night", "Mary's Child", "Do Shepherds Stand" and several other hymns, was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodist" title="Methodist">Methodist</a> minister of Loughton 1958–63. <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Russell" title="Ralph Russell">Ralph Russell</a>, foremost Western scholar of <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a> language and literature, lived in Queens Road as a child and attended Staples Road School.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/T._E._Lawrence" title="T. E. Lawrence">T. E. Lawrence</a> bought land at <a href="/wiki/Pole_Hill" title="Pole Hill">Pole Hill</a> in Chingford after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a> and constructed a hut and swimming pool there. After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chingford_Urban_District" title="Chingford Urban District">Chingford Urban District</a> council bought the land in 1930 and demolished his structures, he re-erected the hut in the grounds of The Warren in Loughton in 1931. The hut remains there, but in a state of disrepair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Museum_and_archives">Museum and archives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Museum and archives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Loughton is home to two important national archives:</p>
<ul>
<li>The British Postal Museum Store, in Lenthall Road, houses objects ranging from the desk of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rowland_Hill_(postal_reformer)" title="Rowland Hill (postal reformer)">Rowland Hill</a> (founder of the <a href="/wiki/Uniform" title="Uniform">Penny Post</a>), to <a href="/wiki/Mobile_post_office" title="Mobile post office">mobile post office</a> vehicles and an astounding range of <a href="/wiki/Post_box" title="Post box">post boxes</a>. The museum has public open days once a month.</li>
<li>The National Jazz Archive is housed in Loughton Library; it is the national repository and research centre for printed material, photographs and memorabilia relating to jazz, with an emphasis on <a href="/wiki/British_jazz" title="British jazz">British jazz</a>. Founded by jazz trumpeter <a href="/wiki/Digby_Fairweather" title="Digby Fairweather">Digby Fairweather</a> in 1988, it contains an unrivalled collection of British jazz recordings, photographs, posters and memorabilia. The archive holds regular celebrity and live jazz events.</li>
</ul>
<p>In addition to the two museums listed above, guided tours are occasionally offered of the disused <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signal_box" title="Signal box">signal box</a> near <a href="/wiki/Loughton_tube_station" title="Loughton tube station">Loughton tube station</a> which is owned by the <a href="/wiki/London_Transport_Museum" title="London Transport Museum">London Transport Museum</a>. Funding was pledged in 2006 to help establish a Street Museum in Loughton. There is also an Epping Forest District Museum store in the town, but this is not open to the public.</p>
<p>A number of Loughton buildings, including the Masonic Hall, Lopping Hall, Mortuary Chapel and several churches, were opened for <a href="/wiki/Heritage_Open_Days" title="Heritage Open Days">Heritage Open Days</a> in September 2007, the first time this had been done. On one of the days, a vintage bus rally was held in the town, attracting a large number of visitors.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport_and_leisure">Sport and leisure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sport and leisure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although Loughton is surrounded by open countryside it contains in addition many parks and open spaces. Sports play an important part in the town's life as is reflected in the variety and range of facilities found in the town.</p>
<p>Loughton Leisure Centre at Traps Hill, managed by a private operator on behalf of the Epping Forest District Council includes a swimming pool complex and fitness facilities. Other large commercial sports and leisure facilities are also to be found in the area.</p>
<ul>
<li>Athletics - Members of the <a class="external text" href="http://www.loughtonac.org.uk/" rel="nofollow">Loughton Athletic Club</a>, based at the Pavilion in Southview Road and affiliated to the Essex AAA, compete in a variety of regional track and field competitions, including the Women's Southern League and the Men's Southern League. The club was founded in 1906, making it Britain's oldest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athletics_(sport)" title="Athletics (sport)">athletics</a> club.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bowls" title="Bowls">Bowls</a> - Loughton Bowls Club has its ground at Eleven Acre Rise.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">Cricket</a> - Loughton Cricket Club was founded in 1879, and plays in the Shepherd Neame Essex League. Its cricket ground, complete with thatched <a href="/wiki/Pavilion" title="Pavilion">pavilion</a>, and facing the war memorial, is one of the town's most important open spaces, and originated as a field named Mott's Piece. One of the earliest presidents of the Loughton Cricket Club was Julius Rohrweger, a local German extraction who owned Uplands, a large house adjacent to the cricket ground. As he was politically a Liberal, the local Conservative party created and supported for some time a rival team, the Loughton Park Cricket Club, though this no longer exists.<br/>
The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=South_Loughton_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="South Loughton Cricket Club (page does not exist)">South Loughton Cricket Club</a> was founded in 1938, and plays at the Roding Road Cricket Ground. In 2007, its 1st XI became Ten-17 Herts &amp; Essex League champions, having won the title following three consecutive promotions. The club also runs four other teams playing league and friendly cricket, and has a thriving junior section offering coaching and matchplay for children aged six upwards. The club was one of the first in the UK to gain Sport England's prestigious 'Clubmark' accreditation. It is an ECB 'Focus Club'.</li>
<li>Fencing - Loughton Fencing Club meets at Debden Park High School.</li>
<li>Football - At Willingale Road Playing Fields and At the Roding Valley Recreation Ground a variety of local <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> teams play. Coppice Row play their home games at the latter, in the Harlow and District League. GFA Loughton FC, founded in 2014, have youth teams in the Echo Junior League as well as running Grassroots Football Academy, a successful Youth Football Academy at GGSK College, Roding Lane IG9.</li>
</ul>
<p>Loughton FC, founded in 1965, dropped out of the <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire_Senior_County_League" title="Hertfordshire Senior County League">Hertfordshire Senior County League</a> in 2007 and now plays in the Bishops Stortford, Stansted and District League and has youth teams in the Echo Junior League and the Barking Youth League. <a href="/wiki/Ron_Greenwood" title="Ron Greenwood">Ron Greenwood</a> (1921–2006), manager of the England football team 1977–82, lived in Loughton for some years at 18 Brooklyn Avenue. The Football Academy UK opened in July 2007 on the site of the Britannia Sports Club in Langston Road.</p>
<ul>
<li>Golf - Loughton <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">Golf</a> Club owns a 9-hole course in Clays Lane. There are many other golf courses close by, including Abridge Golf and Country Club, Chigwell Golf Club, Chingford Golf Club, Royal Epping Forest Golf Club, Theydon Bois Golf Club, West Essex Golf Club, Woodford Golf Club and Woolston Manor Golf Club.</li>
<li>Horse-riding - Horse-riding is very popular in Epping Forest; riders need to be registered with the Epping Forest conservators before they are allowed to ride in the forest. Pine Lodge Riding Centre at Springfield Farm, Loughton, is an ABRS-approved stables.</li>
<li>Mountain-biking - Epping Forest attracts large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Mountain_biking" title="Mountain biking">mountain bikers</a>. Mountain biking is generally permitted except around Loughton Camp and Ambresbury Banks (both <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> forts), Loughton Brook and other ecologically or geomorphologically sensitive areas. A number of clubs organise rides, particularly on Sunday mornings. Epping Forest was considered as a venue for the mountain-biking event of the 2012 Summer Olympics, though a later (but subsequently abandoned) choice was <a href="/wiki/Weald_Country_Park" title="Weald Country Park">Weald Country Park</a> near Brentwood, Essex.</li>
<li>Orienteering and Rambling - Several long-distance footpaths pass through Loughton, including the Forest Way and the <a href="/wiki/London_Outer_Orbital_Path" title="London Outer Orbital Path">London Outer Orbital Path</a>, and shorter walks are also popular, especially in Epping Forest. Chigwell &amp; Epping Forest <a href="/wiki/Orienteering" title="Orienteering">Orienteering</a> Club was founded in 1966, and active orienteering in Epping Forest takes place most weekends. West Essex Ramblers, founded in 1970, are the local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rambling" title="Rambling">rambling</a> club for Loughton; the club holds four walks a week in the Loughton area, with summer excursions to more distant locations. The most important event in the ramblers calendar in the area is the traditional Epping Forest Centenary Walk, an all-day event commemorating the saving of Epping Forest as a public space, which takes place annually on the fourth Sunday in September. West Essex Ramblers have over 1,000 members.</li>
<li>Speedway - High Beach near Loughton is acknowledged by most speedway historians as being the first venue for speedway racing in the UK. The first event was staged on 19 February 1928.</li>
<li>Swimming - Epping Forest District <a href="/wiki/Swimming_(sport)" title="Swimming (sport)">Swimming</a> Club, founded in 1977, meets at Loughton Leisure Centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>Tennis - The Avenue Lawn <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">Tennis</a> Club has four artificial grass courts at its ground between The Avenue and Lower Park Road. From November 2006 to March 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a> courts were resurfaced with a new layer of astroturf and sand. There is a children's half-court with a basketball net. The courts surround the club house which (among other things) contains a table tennis table and a pool table. The Town Council maintains tennis courts on the Roding Valley, but those which are part of the Loughton Bowls and Lawn Tennis Club are disused.</li>
<li>Taekwondo - Loughton Taekwondo meets at Debden Park High School. The club ranked top 5 at the BTCB National Taekwondo Championships in 2010 with 4 athletes becoming British Champion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The instructor Chan Sau won England's first ever Gold Medal at the 2008 Commonwealth Games in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Junction 5 (south) of the <a href="/wiki/M11_motorway" title="M11 motorway">M11 motorway</a> linking Cambridge to London is accessed at Loughton's eastern boundary. The junction does not permit entry to north-bound carriageway, nor exit southbound. The <a class="external text" href="http://www.cbrd.co.uk/motorway/m11" rel="nofollow">M11</a> was constructed in a number of phases beginning in the 1970s and finally opening in the 1980s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railway">Railway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Railway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Loughton_station.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="706" data-file-width="1024" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Loughton_station.jpg/220px-Loughton_station.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Loughton_station.jpg/330px-Loughton_station.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Loughton_station.jpg/440px-Loughton_station.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Loughton_station.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Loughton underground station</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Loughton is served by both <a href="/wiki/Loughton_tube_station" title="Loughton tube station">Loughton tube station</a> and, further north-east, <a href="/wiki/Debden_tube_station" title="Debden tube station">Debden tube station</a>, both served by the Central line of the <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a> since 1949. The current Loughton station was opened in 1940, but both the line and stations existed before that; the railway line dates back to 22 August 1856, when the branch from <a href="/wiki/Stratford_station" title="Stratford station">Stratford</a> was opened by the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Counties_Railway" title="Eastern Counties Railway">Eastern Counties Railway</a>. Debden station was named "Chigwell Lane" from 1856 until 1949 (though for a few months in 1856 it was "Chigwell Road"). The railway's 150th anniversary was celebrated by an exhibition and activity day at Loughton station on 19 August 2006.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The section of line between Loughton and <a href="/wiki/Leyton" title="Leyton">Leyton</a> is the oldest railway alignment in use of the London Underground system, pre-dating the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Railway" title="Metropolitan Railway">Metropolitan Railway</a> by some seven years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bus">Bus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Bus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many bus routes in Loughton, which are either <a href="/wiki/London_Buses" title="London Buses">London Buses</a> routes, commercial routes or Essex County Council contract routes. Services operating to destinations south of Loughton are mostly frequent and are <a href="/wiki/London_Buses" title="London Buses">London Buses</a> services. Services operating to destinations north of Loughton are not <a href="/wiki/London_Buses" title="London Buses">London Buses</a> routes (meaning <a href="/wiki/Oyster_card" title="Oyster card">Oyster cards</a> and other forms of TfL ticketing are not accepted), and are either commercial services or services under contract to Essex County Council.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2006, schools in Loughton had approximately 2330 places in post-16 education, approximately 1200 places in <a href="/wiki/Key_Stage_4" title="Key Stage 4">Key Stage 4</a>, approx. 1700 places in <a href="/wiki/Key_Stage_3" title="Key Stage 3">Key Stage 3</a>, approximately 1500 places in <a href="/wiki/Key_Stage_2" title="Key Stage 2">Key Stage 2</a> and approximately 600 places in <a href="/wiki/Key_Stage_1" title="Key Stage 1">Key Stage 1</a> - almost all of which were in <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_school" title="Comprehensive school">comprehensive schools</a>. Davenant Foundation has always had a sixth form; the other two secondary schools were to open sixth forms in September 2015.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_residents">Notable residents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loughton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Notable residents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Notable people associated with Loughton (apart from those listed above) include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dick_Turpin" title="Dick Turpin">Dick Turpin</a> (1705–1739), notorious highwayman, was familiar with Epping Forest (his butcher's shop was in <a href="/wiki/Buckhurst_Hill" title="Buckhurst Hill">Buckhurst Hill</a>) and carried out many documented robberies in the area during the 1730s, sometimes escaping and hiding in the forest in <a href="/wiki/Turpin%27s_Cave" title="Turpin's Cave">Turpin's Cave</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Willingale" title="Thomas Willingale">Thomas Willingale</a> (1799–1870), whose name is associated with the campaign that resulted in the preservation of <a href="/wiki/Epping_Forest" title="Epping Forest">Epping Forest</a>. A plaque commemorating him is to be found in the wall of St John's Church at Church Lane.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/James_Cubitt" title="James Cubitt">James Cubitt</a> (1836–1912), architect, best known for his design of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonconformism" title="Nonconformism">nonconformist</a> chapels such as the <a href="/wiki/Union_Chapel,_Islington" title="Union Chapel, Islington">Union Chapel, Islington</a> and the Welsh Church in <a href="/wiki/Charing_Cross_Road" title="Charing Cross Road">Charing Cross Road</a> in London, lived from c. 1880 onwards at Brook Villas and Cotsall Eaton Villas on the High Road, and spent the last years of his life at Monghyr Cottage in Traps Hill<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Think_Loughton_2005.2F6_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Think_Loughton_2005.2F6-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Everard_Calthrop" title="Everard Calthrop">Everard Calthrop</a> (1857–1927), railway engineer and parachute pioneer, lived at 'Goldings' from the early 1900s onwards<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li>Sir <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leonard_Erskine_Hill" title="Leonard Erskine Hill">Leonard Erskine Hill</a> (1866–1952), physiologist</li>
<li>Dr <a href="/wiki/Millais_Culpin" title="Millais Culpin">Millais Culpin</a> (1874–1953), surgeon and pioneer of psychiatry, lived at 'Slyder's Gate' and then 'The Meads', both in Church Hill, from 1913 onwards - a fictionalised version of the romance between him and his future wife Ethel, a nurse at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_London_Hospital" title="Royal London Hospital">Royal London Hospital</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whitechapel" title="Whitechapel">Whitechapel</a> where they both worked, was dramatised in the BBC TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Casualty_1900s#Casualty_1907" title="Casualty 1900s">Casualty 1907</a></i> in 2008 and <i><a href="/wiki/Casualty_1900s#Casualty_1909" title="Casualty 1900s">Casualty 1909</a></i> in 2009<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Think_Loughton_2005.2F6_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Think_Loughton_2005.2F6-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>Sir <a href="/wiki/Frank_Baines" title="Frank Baines">Frank Baines</a> (1877–1933), former Principal Architect of the government's <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Works_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Works (United Kingdom)">Office of Works</a> and chiefly known for designing <a href="/wiki/Thames_House" title="Thames House">Thames House</a> and <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Chemical_House" title="Imperial Chemical House">Imperial Chemical House</a> in London, lived at 'Hillside' and built other large houses in Loughton</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Major_Greenwood" title="Major Greenwood">Major Greenwood</a> (1880–1949), epidemiologist and statistician - Sir Leonard Hill gave him his first job after graduation as an assistant physiologist before he turned to his later career,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and he later became a neighbour of the Hill family in Loughton<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>Sir <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Cairns_(surgeon)" title="Hugh Cairns (surgeon)">Hugh Cairns</a> (1896–1952), neurosurgeon and advocate of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crash_helmet" title="Crash helmet">crash helmet</a> lived at Loughton whilst working at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Hospital" title="London Hospital">London Hospital</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>Sir <a href="/wiki/Austin_Bradford_Hill" title="Austin Bradford Hill">Austin "Tony" Bradford Hill</a> (1897–1991), epidemiologist and statistician, and son of Sir Leonard Erskine Hill, grew up in the family home at Osborne House - published several research collaborations with Major Greenwood, a family friend</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/William_Wilkinson_Addison" title="William Wilkinson Addison">Sir William Addison</a> (1905–1992), – historian and author, owned a bookshop in Loughton High Road for forty years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kneale_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kneale-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morris-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>Commander <a href="/wiki/Rupert_Brabner" title="Rupert Brabner">Rupert Brabner</a> (1911–1945), Conservative MP for <a href="/wiki/Hythe_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Hythe (UK Parliament constituency)">Hythe</a> 1939–1945 and WWII pilot with the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>, was born in Loughton</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mrs_Mills" title="Mrs Mills">Gladys Mills, née Gladys Jordan</a> (1918–1978), musician (Mrs Mills).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ralph_Russell" title="Ralph Russell">Ralph Russell</a> 1918-2008, Western scholar of <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ron_Greenwood" title="Ron Greenwood">Ron Greenwood</a> (1921–2006), footballer and manager of <a href="/wiki/West_Ham_United_F.C." title="West Ham United F.C.">West Ham United</a> and <a href="/wiki/England_national_football_team" title="England national football team">England</a>, lived in Brooklyn Avenue during his time as West Ham manager</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Len_Murray" title="Len Murray">Len Murray</a> (1922–2004), later Baron Murray of Epping Forest, leader of the <a href="/wiki/Trades_Union_Congress" title="Trades Union Congress">Trades Union Congress</a> 1973–1984, lived for over 50 years in The Crescent and played an active role in town life. He is commemorated by the Murray Hall, opened 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Matt_Johnson_(singer)" title="Matt Johnson (singer)">Matt Johnson</a> (born 1961), frontman of the band <a href="/wiki/The_The" title="The The">The The</a>, spent part of his childhood in "The Crown" pub, run by his parents Eddie and Shirley in the 1970s and 80s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Joanna_Forest" title="Joanna Forest">Joanna Forest</a> (born 1977), British Classical Crossover soprano</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Richard_Hounslow" title="Richard Hounslow">Richard Hounslow</a> (born 1981), Silver medallist at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_2012" title="London 2012">London 2012</a> in canoeing</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gary_Hooper" title="Gary Hooper">Gary Hooper</a> (born 1988), <a href="/wiki/Celtic_F.C." title="Celtic F.C.">Glasgow Celtic</a> footballer, was born in Loughton</li>
</ul>
