<p>The <b>Pons Aemilius</b> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><i>Ponte Emilio</i></span>), today called <b>Ponte Rotto</b>, is the oldest <a href="/wiki/Roman_bridge" title="Roman bridge">Roman stone bridge</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. Preceded by a wooden version, it was rebuilt in stone in the 2nd century BC. It once spanned the <a href="/wiki/Tiber" title="Tiber">Tiber</a>, connecting the <a href="/wiki/Forum_Boarium" title="Forum Boarium">Forum Boarium</a> with <a href="/wiki/Trastevere" title="Trastevere">Trastevere</a>; a single arch in mid-river is all that remains today, lending the bridge its name <i>Ponte Rotto</i> ("Broken bridge").</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pons_Aemilius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_antiquity">Classical antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pons_Aemilius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classical antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The oldest piers of the bridge were probably laid when the <a href="/wiki/Via_Aurelia" title="Via Aurelia">Via Aurelia</a> was constructed in the mid-2nd century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Livy" title="Livy">Titus Livius</a> recorded that a bridge in the same location existed in 192 BC. The first stone bridge was constructed by <a href="/wiki/Roman_censor" title="Roman censor">Censor</a> <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Fulvius_Nobilior_(consul_189_BC)" title="Marcus Fulvius Nobilior (consul 189 BC)">Marcus Fulvius Nobilior</a> in the year 179 BC, although it was not completed until 151 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-train_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-train-2">[2]</a></sup> The bridge's piers date from this early period, although its arches were constructed in 142 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The bridge kept its place for several hundred years, although it was repaired and rebuilt both by <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and later by Emperor <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius_Probus" title="Marcus Aurelius Probus">Probus</a> in 280 AD.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_ages">Middle ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pons_Aemilius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Middle ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tiber_with_the_Ponte_Rotto_and_the_Aventine_Hill_1690.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="955" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Tiber_with_the_Ponte_Rotto_and_the_Aventine_Hill_1690.jpg/300px-Tiber_with_the_Ponte_Rotto_and_the_Aventine_Hill_1690.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Tiber_with_the_Ponte_Rotto_and_the_Aventine_Hill_1690.jpg/450px-Tiber_with_the_Ponte_Rotto_and_the_Aventine_Hill_1690.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Tiber_with_the_Ponte_Rotto_and_the_Aventine_Hill_1690.jpg/600px-Tiber_with_the_Ponte_Rotto_and_the_Aventine_Hill_1690.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tiber_with_the_Ponte_Rotto_and_the_Aventine_Hill_1690.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ponte Rotto (1690) by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaspar_Van_Wittel" title="Gaspar Van Wittel">Van Wittel</a>, showing the damage wrought by severe floods.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After the collapse of the Roman Empire, the bridge was damaged several times by floods, with each flood taking a greater and greater toll on the overall structure. It was first severely damaged in 1230 AD, after which it was rebuilt by Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gregory_XI" title="Gregory XI">Gregory XI</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-train_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-train-2">[2]</a></sup> Later, the bridge was more seriously damaged by the flood of 1557, but again was later rebuilt by Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gregory_XIII" title="Gregory XIII">Gregory XIII</a>; the remnants of the bridge today still bear Latin inscriptions detailing Gregory XIII's renovation of the bridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Finally, floods in 1575 and 1598 carried the eastern half away, resulting in its abandonment as a functioning bridge for several centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-train_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-train-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-6">[6]</a></sup> For many years, it was used as a fishing pier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-website_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-website-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1853, Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pius_IX" title="Pius IX">Pius IX</a> had the remnants of the bridge connected to the mainland via an iron footbridge, but the heavy metal weakened the structural integrity of the stone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-train_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-train-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-website_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-website-7">[7]</a></sup> The remaining half was demolished in 1887 to make room for the <a href="/wiki/Ponte_Palatino" title="Ponte Palatino">Ponte Palatino</a>, leaving behind only one arch that remains to this day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
