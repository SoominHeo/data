<p>The Lockheed <b>HC-130</b> is an extended-range, <a href="/wiki/Search_and_rescue" title="Search and rescue">search and rescue</a> (SAR)/<a href="/wiki/Combat_search_and_rescue" title="Combat search and rescue">combat search and rescue</a> (CSAR) version of the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_C-130_Hercules" title="Lockheed C-130 Hercules">C-130</a> <b>Hercules</b> military transport aircraft, with two different versions operated by two separate services in the U.S. armed forces.</p>
<p>The HC-130H Hercules and HC-130J Hercules versions are operated by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">United States Coast Guard</a> in a SAR and maritime reconnaissance role.</p>
<p>The HC-130P Combat King and HC-130J Combat King II models are operated by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> for long-range SAR and CSAR. The USAF variants also execute on scene CSAR command and control, airdrop pararescue forces and equipment, and are also capable of providing <a href="/wiki/Aerial_refueling" title="Aerial refueling">aerial refueling</a> to appropriately equipped USAF, US Army, USN, USMC and NATO/Allied helicopters in flight. In this latter role, they are primarily used to extend the range and endurance of combat search and rescue <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopters</a>.</p>
<p>In July 2015, it was announced that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Forest_Service" title="U.S. Forest Service">U.S. Forest Service</a> will be receiving some of the U.S. Coast Guard's HC-130H aircraft to use as aerial fire retardant drop tankers as the Coast Guard replaces the HC-130H with additional HC-130J and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-27_Spartan" title="C-27 Spartan">HC-27J Spartan</a> aircraft, the latter being received from the <a href="/wiki/Air_National_Guard" title="Air National Guard">Air National Guard</a> as part of a USAF-directed divestment of the C-27.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_HC-130&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The United States Coast Guard was the first recipient of the HC-130 variant. In keeping with the USN/USMC/USCG designation system of the time, the designation for the first order in 1958 was R8V-1G, but with the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/1962_United_States_Tri-Service_aircraft_designation_system" title="1962 United States Tri-Service aircraft designation system">Tri-Service aircraft designation system</a> for commonality with the US Army and USAF in 1962, this was eventually changed to HC-130B.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Six USCG HC-130E aircraft were produced in 1964,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globalsecurity.org_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-globalsecurity.org-4">[4]</a></sup> but production soon switched to the new C-130H platform which was entering service. The first HC-130H flew on 8 December 1964 and<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> the USCG still operates this aircraft.</p>
<p>First flown in 1964, the USAF HC-130P Combat King aircraft has served many roles and missions. Based on the USAF C-130E airframe, it was modified to conduct search and rescue missions, provide a command and control platform, conduct in-flight refueling of helicopters, and carry supplemental fuel in additional internal cargo bay fuel tanks for extending range or air refueling. They were also originally modified to employ the <a href="/wiki/Fulton_surface-to-air_recovery_system" title="Fulton surface-to-air recovery system">Fulton surface-to-air recovery system</a>, although this system has since been discontinued and the specialized equipment removed. The HC-130N was a follow-up order without the Fulton recovery system and all USAF extant HC-130Ps have since had their Fulton recovery systems removed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role">Role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_HC-130&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="USAF_HC-130P.2FN_Combat_King">USAF HC-130P/N Combat King</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_HC-130&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: USAF HC-130P/N Combat King">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The USAF HC-130P/N, also known as the Combat King aircraft, can fly in the day against a reduced threat; however, crews normally fly night, low-level, air refueling and airdrop operations using night vision goggles (NVG). The aircraft can routinely fly low-level NVG tactical flight profiles to avoid detection. To enhance the probability of mission success and survivability near populated areas, USAF HC-130 crews employ tactics that include incorporating no external lighting or communications and avoiding radar and weapons detection.</p>
<p>Secondary mission capabilities include performing tactical <a href="/wiki/Airdrop" title="Airdrop">airdrops</a> of pararescue specialist teams, small bundles, <a href="/wiki/Inflatable_boat" title="Inflatable boat">zodiac watercraft</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Four-wheel_drive" title="Four-wheel drive">four-wheel drive</a> <a href="/wiki/All-terrain_vehicle" title="All-terrain vehicle">all-terrain vehicles</a>; and providing direct assistance to a survivor in advance of the arrival of a recovery vehicle. Other capabilities are extended visual and electronic searches over land or water, tactical airborne <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> approaches and unimproved <a href="/wiki/Aerodrome" title="Aerodrome">airfield</a> operations. A team of three <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force_Pararescue" title="United States Air Force Pararescue">Pararescuemen</a> (PJ's), trained in emergency <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_trauma" title="Physical trauma">trauma medicine</a>, harsh environment survival and assisted evasion techniques, is part of the basic mission crew complement.</p>
<p>HC-130P/N aircraft of the Combat Air Forces (CAF) are currently undergoing extensive modifications. These modifications include <a href="/wiki/Night_vision_device" title="Night vision device">night vision</a>-compatible interior and exterior lighting, a personnel locator system compatible with aircrew survival radios, improved digital low-power color radar and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forward-looking_infrared" title="Forward-looking infrared">forward-looking infrared</a> systems.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="USCG_HC-130H">USCG HC-130H</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_HC-130&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: USCG HC-130H">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>U.S. Coast Guard HC-130Hs were primarily acquired for long-range overwater search missions, support airlift, maritime patrol, North Atlantic <a href="/wiki/International_Ice_Patrol" title="International Ice Patrol">Ice Patrol</a> and command and control of search and rescue, replacing previously operated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HU-16_Albatross" title="HU-16 Albatross">HU-16 Albatross</a> amphibious and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-123_Provider" title="C-123 Provider">HC-123 Provider</a> land-based aircraft. Like their USAF counterparts, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USCG" title="USCG">USCG</a> HC-130s also have the capability of air dropping rescue equipment to survivors at sea or over open terrain.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="USAF_HC-130P_Combat_Shadow">USAF HC-130P Combat Shadow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_HC-130&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: USAF HC-130P Combat Shadow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MC-130P_Combat_Shadow" title="MC-130P Combat Shadow">MC-130P Combat Shadow</a></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MC-130P_Combat_Shadow" title="MC-130P Combat Shadow">MC-130P Combat Shadow</a> series of aircraft initially entered service in December 1965 during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> as the HC-130H CROWN airborne controller. The CROWN airborne controllers located downed aircrew and directed <a href="/wiki/Search_and_rescue" title="Search and rescue">Combat Search and Rescue</a> operations over North Vietnam. In mid-1966 flight testing began of rescue helicopters equipped with aerial refueling receivers, and 11 of the controller aircraft were modified as tankers and redesignated the HC-130P SAR Command and Control/vertical lift (helicopter) aerial refueling aircraft, entering service in Southeast Asia in November 1966. Originally assigned to the <a href="/wiki/Tactical_Air_Command" title="Tactical Air Command">Tactical Air Command</a> (TAC) and then the <a href="/wiki/Military_Airlift_Command" title="Military Airlift Command">Military Airlift Command</a> (MAC), Combat Shadows have been part of the <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Special_Operations_Command" title="Air Force Special Operations Command">Air Force Special Operations Command</a> (AFSOC) since that command's establishment in 1993. In February 1996, AFSOC's 28-aircraft tanker fleet was redesignated the <b>MC-130P Combat Shadow</b>, aligning the variant with AFSOC's other M-series special operations mission aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fact3_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-fact3-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> At the same time as this redesignation, USAF continued to field HC-130P/N aircraft as dedicated CSAR platforms under the <a href="/wiki/Air_Combat_Command" title="Air Combat Command">Air Combat Command</a> (ACC).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="HC-130J">HC-130J</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_HC-130&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: HC-130J">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Coast_Guard">U.S. Coast Guard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_HC-130&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: U.S. Coast Guard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The new HC-130J aircraft are derived from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_KC-130J" title="Lockheed Martin KC-130J">Lockheed Martin KC-130J</a> tanker operated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Marine_Corps" title="U.S. Marine Corps">U.S. Marine Corps</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The USCG has six HC-130Js in service, but they are not capable of refueling helicopters in flight. The first delivery of this variant to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">United States Coast Guard</a> was in October 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> They initially operated in a logistic support role until they received significant modifications, including installations of a large window on each side of the fuselage to allow crew members to visually scan the sea surface, the addition of an inverse synthetic aperture sea search radar, flare tubes, a forward-looking infrared/electro-optical sensor, a gaseous oxygen system for the crew and an enhanced communications suite. The first of these modified Coast Guard HC-130Js was delivered in March 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Air_Force">U.S. Air Force</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_HC-130&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: U.S. Air Force">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The USAF HC-130J Combat King II combat rescue variant has modifications for in-flight refueling of helicopters and tilt-rotor aircraft, including refueling pods on underwing pylons and additional internal fuel tanks in the cargo bay. The HC-130J Combat King II is also capable of itself being refueled in flight by <a href="/wiki/Aerial_refueling" title="Aerial refueling">boom-equipped tankers</a>.</p>
<p>Lockheed Martin officials conducted the first flight of the USAF HC-130J version on 29 July 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The first HC-130J was delivered to the USAF in September 2010,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockheedmartin.com_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-lockheedmartin.com-12">[12]</a></sup> but will undergo further testing before achieving Initial Operational Capability (IOC) in 2012.</p>
<p>The HC-130J personnel recovery aircraft completed developmental testing on 14 March 2011. The final test point was air-to-air refueling, and was the first ever boom refueling of a C-130 where the aircraft’s refueling receiver was installed during aircraft production. This test procedure also applied to the MC-130J Combat Shadow II aircraft in production for Air Force Special Operations Command.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Given the advancing age of its current HC-130 airframes, all of which are based on the venerable C-130E airframe, the Air Force plans to eventually buy up to 78 HC-130J Combat King IIs to equip rescue squadrons in the active Air Force, the <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Reserve_Command" title="Air Force Reserve Command">Air Force Reserve Command</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Air_National_Guard" title="Air National Guard">Air National Guard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The first HC-130J was delivered to the <a href="/wiki/563d_Rescue_Group" title="563d Rescue Group">563d Rescue Group</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Davis-Monthan_Air_Force_Base" title="Davis-Monthan Air Force Base">Davis-Monthan Air Force Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> on 15 November 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">United States Coast Guard</a> operates 21 HC-130H aircraft from four bases around the United States:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Coast_Guard_Air_Station_Sacramento" title="Coast Guard Air Station Sacramento">CGAS Sacramento</a> (former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McClellan_AFB" title="McClellan AFB">McClellan AFB</a>), California</li>

<p>These aircraft are used for search and rescue, enforcement of laws and treaties, illegal drug interdiction, marine environmental protection, military readiness, International Ice Patrol missions, as well as cargo and personnel transport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uscg1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-uscg1-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Coast Guard also currently operates an additional 6 HC-130J aircraft from <a href="/wiki/Coast_Guard_Air_Station_Elizabeth_City" title="Coast Guard Air Station Elizabeth City">CGAS Elizabeth City</a>, North Carolina.</p>
<p>Neither the HC-130H nor the HC-130J in their U.S. Coast Guard variants are equipped for the aerial refueling of helicopters.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Air_Force_operations">U.S. Air Force operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_HC-130&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: U.S. Air Force operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The HC-130P (to include HC-130P/N) is primarily based on the C-130E airlift aircraft, with a smaller number based on the C-130H. The USAF HC-130J is a newly manufactured aircraft. As the dedicated <a href="/wiki/Fixed-wing_aircraft" title="Fixed-wing aircraft">fixed-wing</a> combat search and rescue platform in the USAF inventory, the HC-130 is operated by the following units:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Air_Combat_Command" title="Air Combat Command">Air Combat Command</a>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/347th_Rescue_Group" title="347th Rescue Group">347th Rescue Group</a> (347 RQG), <a href="/wiki/71st_Rescue_Squadron" title="71st Rescue Squadron">71st Rescue Squadron</a> (71 RQS), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moody_AFB" title="Moody AFB">Moody AFB</a>, Georgia - HC-130J</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/563d_Rescue_Group" title="563d Rescue Group">563d Rescue Group</a> (563 RQG), <a href="/wiki/79th_Rescue_Squadron" title="79th Rescue Squadron">79th Rescue Squadron</a> (79 RQS), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Davis-Monthan_AFB" title="Davis-Monthan AFB">Davis-Monthan AFB</a>, Arizona - HC-130J</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Air_Education_and_Training_Command" title="Air Education and Training Command">Air Education and Training Command</a>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/58th_Special_Operations_Wing" title="58th Special Operations Wing">58th Special Operations Wing</a> (58 SOW), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kirtland_AFB" title="Kirtland AFB">Kirtland AFB</a>, New Mexico

<li><a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Reserve_Command" title="Air Force Reserve Command">Air Force Reserve Command</a>
<ul>

<p>HC-130s were assigned to the <a href="/wiki/Air_Combat_Command" title="Air Combat Command">Air Combat Command</a> (ACC) from 1992 to 2003, to include those <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Reserve_Command" title="Air Force Reserve Command">Air Force Reserve Command</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_National_Guard" title="Air National Guard">Air National Guard</a> rescue units operationally-gained by ACC. Prior to 1992, they were assigned to the <a href="/wiki/Air_Rescue_Service" title="Air Rescue Service">Air Rescue Service</a> as part of <a href="/wiki/Military_Airlift_Command" title="Military Airlift Command">Military Airlift Command</a> (MAC). In October 2003, operational responsibility for the Continental United States (CONUS) and Alaskan air search and rescue (SAR) mission, as well as the worldwide combat search and rescue (CSAR) mission was transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Special_Operations_Command" title="Air Force Special Operations Command">Air Force Special Operations Command</a> (AFSOC) at <a href="/wiki/Hurlburt_Field" title="Hurlburt Field">Hurlburt Field</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.</p>
<p>In October 2006, all USAF CSAR forces were reassigned back to <a href="/wiki/Air_Combat_Command" title="Air Combat Command">Air Combat Command</a> with the exception of those <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Air_National_Guard" title="Alaska Air National Guard">Alaska Air National Guard</a> CSAR assets which were transferred to the operational claimancy of <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Air_Forces" title="Pacific Air Forces">Pacific Air Forces</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PACAF" title="PACAF">PACAF</a>). The CONUS and Alaska SAR missions were also transferred back to ACC and PACAF, respectively. However, the Air Force Rescue Coordination Center (AFRCC) that had been previously located at <a href="/wiki/McClellan_Air_Force_Base" title="McClellan Air Force Base">McClellan Air Force Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scott_Air_Force_Base" title="Scott Air Force Base">Scott Air Force Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> under MAC and at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Langley_Air_Force_Base" title="Langley Air Force Base">Langley Air Force Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> under ACC, was relocated to <a href="/wiki/Tyndall_Air_Force_Base" title="Tyndall Air Force Base">Tyndall Air Force Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> under the control of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1st_Air_Force" title="1st Air Force">1st Air Force</a> (1 AF), the USAF component command to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Northern_Command" title="U.S. Northern Command">U.S. Northern Command</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USNORTHCOM" title="USNORTHCOM">USNORTHCOM</a>) and ACC's numbered air force for the <a href="/wiki/Air_National_Guard" title="Air National Guard">Air National Guard</a>.</p>
<p>While under AFSOC and since returning to ACC and PACAF, USAF, AFRC and ANG HC-130s have been deployed to <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kyrgyzstan" title="Kyrgyzstan">Kyrgyzstan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Djibouti" title="Djibouti">Djibouti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> in support of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Southern_Watch" title="Operation Southern Watch">Operations Southern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Operation_Northern_Watch" title="Operation Northern Watch">Northern Watch</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1999_NATO_bombing_of_the_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="1999 NATO bombing of the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Operation Allied Force</a>, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Enduring_Freedom" title="Operation Enduring Freedom">Operation Enduring Freedom</a>, <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">Operation Iraqi Freedom</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Operation_Unified_Protector" title="Operation Unified Protector">Operation Unified Protector</a>. HC-130s also support continuous alert commitments in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, and provided rescue coverage for <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> operations in Florida until that program's termination in 2011.</p>
<p>The USAF's first HC-130Js gained initial operating capability (IOC) in April 2013,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> permitting retirement of the first group of HC-130P aircraft built in the mid and late 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The first HC-130J was delivered by Lockheed Martin to <a href="/wiki/Air_Combat_Command" title="Air Combat Command">Air Combat Command</a> on September 23, 2010 for testing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockheedmartin.com_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-lockheedmartin.com-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are 13 HC-130 aircraft operated by the <a href="/wiki/Air_National_Guard" title="Air National Guard">Air National Guard</a>, and 10 by the <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Reserve_Command" title="Air Force Reserve Command">Air Force Reserve Command</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World.27s_longest_turboprop_aircraft_distance_record">World's longest turboprop aircraft distance record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_HC-130&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: World's longest turboprop aircraft distance record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 20 February 1972, Lieutenant Colonel Edgar Allison, USAF, and his flight crew set a recognized turboprop aircraft class record of 8,732.09 miles (14,052.94 km) for a great circle distance without landing. The USAF Lockheed HC-130H was flown from <a href="/wiki/Ching_Chuan_Kang_Air_Base" title="Ching Chuan Kang Air Base">Ching Chuan Kang Air Base</a>, Republic of China (Taiwan), to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scott_AFB" title="Scott AFB">Scott AFB</a>, Illinois in the United States. As of 2015, this record still stands more than 40 years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_HC-130&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dt>HC-130B</dt>
<dd>Rescue version of the C-130B for <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">United States Coast Guard</a> (USCG) introduced in 1959, formerly R8V-1G and SC-130B.</dd>
<dt>HC-130E</dt>
<dd>Modified rescue version of the C-130E for USCG, six were produced in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globalsecurity.org_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-globalsecurity.org-4">[4]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>HC-130H</dt>
<dd>Combat rescue version of the C-130E and C-130H for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> (USAF) and enhanced SAR version for the USCG, with <a href="/wiki/Fulton_surface-to-air_recovery_system" title="Fulton surface-to-air recovery system">Fulton surface-to-air recovery system</a> installed in USAF versions; many USAF versions later updated to HC-130P standard.</dd>
<dt>HC-130P Combat King</dt>
<dd>Extended range version of the HC-130H, modified for in-flight refueling of helicopters, refueling pods on underwing pylons, and additional internal fuel tanks in the cargo bay.</dd>
<dt>HC-130P/N Combat King</dt>
<dd>Additional order of HC-130P but without Fulton surface-to-air recovery system or extant HC-130P with Fulton system removed.</dd>
<dt>HC-130J</dt>
<dd>Modified rescue version of the C-130J for USCG.</dd>
<dt>HC-130J Combat King II</dt>
<dd>USAF combat rescue variant of the C-130J with changes for in-flight refueling of helicopters, including refueling pods on underwing pylons and capabilities to receive fuel inflight from boom-equipped tankers. The USAF HC-130J eliminates the enlisted Flight Engineer position, but unlike the USAF C-130J airlift version, still retains a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combat_Systems_Officer" title="Combat Systems Officer">Combat Systems Officer</a>/Navigator position.</dd>
</dl>

<p style="font-size: 90%; margin:0; padding: .2em"><i>Data from</i> USCG Specs<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uscg1_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-uscg1-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>General characteristics</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Crew:</b> USAF: 10;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> USCG: 5 to 7, contingent on mission</li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 97 ft 9 in (29.8 m)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">Wingspan</a>:</b> 132 ft 7 in (40.4 m)</li>
<li><b>Height:</b> 38 ft 3 in (11.6 m)</li>
<li><b>Wing area:</b> 1,745 ft² (162.1 m²)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Manufacturer%27s_empty_weight" title="Manufacturer's empty weight">Empty weight</a>:</b> 76,780 lb (34,826 kg)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Maximum_takeoff_weight" title="Maximum takeoff weight">Max. takeoff weight</a>:</b> 175,000 lb (79,379 kg)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">Powerplant</a>:</b> 4 × <a href="/wiki/Allison_T56" title="Allison T56">Allison T56-A-15</a> <a href="/wiki/Turboprop" title="Turboprop">turboprops</a>, 4,300 shp (3,210 kW) each</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Performance</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/V_speeds#Regulatory_V-speeds" title="V speeds">Maximum speed</a>:</b> 330 knots (380 mph, 611 km/h)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/V_speeds#Vc" title="V speeds">Cruise speed</a>:</b> 290 knots (333 mph, 537 km/h)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Range_(aeronautics)" title="Range (aeronautics)">Range</a>:</b> 4,500 nmi (5,178 mi, 8,334 km)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ceiling_(aeronautics)" title="Ceiling (aeronautics)">Service ceiling</a>:</b> 33,000 ft (10,000 m)</li>
</ul>
