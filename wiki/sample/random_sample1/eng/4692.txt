<p><b>Ronald Dennis</b> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Most_Excellent_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Commander of the Most Excellent Order of the British Empire">CBE</a> (born 1 June 1947) is a <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">British</a> businessman and Official British Business Ambassador for The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. Dennis is the Global Consultant for Minsheng Investment Corporation and also owner of <a href="/wiki/Absolute_Taste" title="Absolute Taste">Absolute Taste</a>. He is best known for his former role as owner, CEO, chairman and founder of <a href="/wiki/McLaren_Technology_Group" title="McLaren Technology Group">McLaren Technology Group</a>. Dennis was removed from his McLaren management roles in 2016 but remains a director of the company and a 25% shareholder.</p>
<p>Since 1981, Dennis has been the team principal of the McLaren Formula One team, and was instrumental in transforming the outfit into a regular world championship contender. Constructors' and drivers' world championships were won with <a href="/wiki/Niki_Lauda" title="Niki Lauda">Niki Lauda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alain_Prost" title="Alain Prost">Alain Prost</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">Ayrton Senna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mika_H%C3%A4kkinen" title="Mika HÃ¤kkinen">Mika HÃ¤kkinen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Hamilton" title="Lewis Hamilton">Lewis Hamilton</a>.</p>
<p>After being handed a three page business plan by a chef for his Formula One team, he set up a catering company, <a href="/wiki/Absolute_Taste" title="Absolute Taste">Absolute Taste</a>, today retaining 55% of the company.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In January 2007, Dennis sold half of his 30 percent shareholding in the McLaren Group to the Bahrain Mumtalakat Holding Company,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> leaving him with a 15 percent share. In 2009, Dennis, together with co-owners <a href="/wiki/Mansour_Ojjeh" title="Mansour Ojjeh">Mansour Ojjeh</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TAG_Group" title="TAG Group">TAG Group</a> and the Bahraini Mumtalakat Holding Company, bought back Daimler AG's 40 per cent shareholding in a deal that was concluded in 2011. As of January 2014, Mumtalakat hold 50 percent of the Group's shares, with Dennis and Mansour Ojjeh holding 25 percent each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeebRon_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BeebRon-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ron Dennis was placed at number 648 in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunday_Times_Rich_List_2006" title="Sunday Times Rich List 2006">Sunday Times Rich List 2006</a> with a net worth of Â£90 million, however <i>The Times</i> put his wealth at Â£200Â million in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In the Sunday Times Rich List 2011, Dennis' net worth is estimated to be Â£177m. In April 2014, it was stated he had a net worth of approx. Â£300m ($513m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> His net worth grew in 2014 when he purchased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McLaren_Group" title="McLaren Group">McLaren Group</a>.</p>
<p>Aside from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McLaren_Group" title="McLaren Group">McLaren Group</a>, he is also one of six British business persons to be an official British Business Ambassador for advanced engineering and manufacturing. Dennis is also a main trustee for Tommy's, a miscarriage help charity. He is the Global Consultant for the state owned China Minsheng Investment Group and also the chairman for the UK Summit. With interests in foreign trade, Dennis also became Chairman of the British East Asian Council in 2014. Before the merger with <a href="/wiki/Louis_Vuitton" title="Louis Vuitton">Louis Vuitton</a>, Dennis was a major shareholder in the luxury watch maker <a href="/wiki/TAG_Heuer" title="TAG Heuer">TAG Heuer</a>, along with his business partner, <a href="/wiki/Mansour_Ojjeh" title="Mansour Ojjeh">Mansour Ojjeh</a>.</p>
<p>In December 2014, he attempted to purchase 50% of McLaren Technology Company from <a href="/wiki/Hamad_bin_Isa_Al_Khalifa" title="Hamad bin Isa Al Khalifa">Hamad bin Isa Al Khalifa</a> and Ojjeh, after shareholder relations worsened, but the investment deals Dennis had arranged failed to materialise and the shareholding structure remained as before. Dennis tried to purchase the company again in October 2016, with an estimated bid of 1.7bn, but Ojjeh and Mumtalakat wanted Dennis out, refusing his bid and consequently in November 2016, Ojjeh and Mumtalakat placed Dennis on 'gardening leave' for two months, and will not have his contract renewed in January 2017 when it expires. He remains 25% shareholder of <a href="/wiki/McLaren_Technology_Group" title="McLaren Technology Group">McLaren Technology Group</a> as well as retaining his 'company director' role, and 11% shareholder of <a href="/wiki/McLaren_Automotive" title="McLaren Automotive">McLaren Automotive</a>, as well as is role there as Chairman and 'company director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Dennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dennis was born and raised in <a href="/wiki/Woking" title="Woking">Woking</a>, England, and studied motor vehicle engineering at Guildford Technical College.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He began working for the <a href="/wiki/Cooper_Car_Company" title="Cooper Car Company">Cooper</a> <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> team in 1966 as a mechanic at the age of 18 where he worked alongside lead driver <a href="/wiki/Jochen_Rindt" title="Jochen Rindt">Jochen Rindt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mclarenbio_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-mclarenbio-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1968 Rindt moved to <a href="/wiki/Brabham" title="Brabham">Brabham</a> and took Dennis with him. For the 1969 season Rindt moved to <a href="/wiki/Team_Lotus" title="Team Lotus">Team Lotus</a>; however, Dennis stayed on, choosing instead to work for Sir <a href="/wiki/Jack_Brabham" title="Jack Brabham">Jack Brabham</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Brabham chose retirement in 1971, Dennis and his colleague Neil Trundle decided to start their own team. In 1971, <a href="/wiki/Rondel_Racing" title="Rondel Racing">Rondel Racing</a> was founded in Dennis's native <a href="/wiki/Woking" title="Woking">Woking</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mclarenbio_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-mclarenbio-8">[8]</a></sup> Money was at a premium though and Dennis was trying to find sponsorship. Through Ron's then girlfriend, who was the daughter of John Phelps, director of Phelps Antique Furniture in <a href="/wiki/Twickenham" title="Twickenham">Twickenham</a>, one of its regular customers <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tony_Vlassopulos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tony Vlassopulos (page does not exist)">Tony Vlassopulos</a>, a barrister son of a Greek shipowner, was asked to sponsor Rondel. Vlassopulos asked his friend <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ken_Grob&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ken Grob (page does not exist)">Ken Grob</a>, chairman of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Howden&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alexander Howden (page does not exist)">Alexander Howden</a>, insurance brokers in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> if he was interested in joining in. Grob said yes on the proviso that his young son <a href="/wiki/Ian_Grob" title="Ian Grob">Ian Grob</a> could be part of the team, which was agreed. From that moment forward, Vlassopulos became Dennis' first sponsor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ORC_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ORC-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the mid-1970s the team was enjoying considerable success in Formula Two. Rondel aspired to be more than a customer team, however, and Dennis soon managed to find an additional backer to Grob and Vlassopulos in Motul, to help fund a Rondel F1 car. For 1974, a Ray Jessop-designed F1 car was planned but the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">energy crisis</a> affected Motul's backing. However, ultimately, Dennis didn't have the overall fund anyway and so Trundle continued with the already designed car from Jessop and Vlassopulos and Grob took over the ownership, with the car becoming the Token.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ORC_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ORC-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dennis regrouped, forming a <a href="/wiki/Marlboro_(cigarette)" title="Marlboro (cigarette)">Marlboro</a>-backed F2 team for two talented and well-sponsored drivers from <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>. In 1975 Dennis founded the Project Three team, and his cars once again became race winners. In the late 1970s, Dennis founded <a href="/wiki/Project_Four_Racing" title="Project Four Racing">Project Four Racing</a>. This team went on to great success in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formula_2" title="Formula 2">Formula 2</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formula_3" title="Formula 3">Formula 3</a>, winning championships in 1979 and 1980 with <a href="/wiki/Philip_Morris_USA" title="Philip Morris USA">Philip Morris</a> (Marlboro) backing. Project Four also participated in the build programme for Procar <a href="/wiki/BMW_M1" title="BMW M1">BMW M1</a> racing cars. As his business interests became increasingly successful and lucrative, Dennis aspired to return to Formula One, hiring talented designer <a href="/wiki/John_Barnard" title="John Barnard">John Barnard</a> to spearhead the design and development of an innovative new F1 car.</p>
<p>Dennis's return to Formula One was well-timed. The recent poor performance of the former world championship-winning McLaren team had prompted Philip Morris executive John Hogan to initiate a takeover of the outfit by Dennis's Project Four operation. Effectively a <a href="/wiki/Reverse_takeover" title="Reverse takeover">reverse takeover</a>, it heralded the arrival of the rebranded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McLaren_International" title="McLaren International">McLaren International</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> operation and ultimately placed the thirty-four-year-old in full control of the outfit. In addition to hiring Barnard to begin work on the team's revolutionary new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fibre_composite" title="Carbon fibre composite">carbon fibre composite</a> chassis, the <a href="/wiki/McLaren_MP4/1" title="McLaren MP4/1">MP4/1</a>, Dennis also successfully recruited the <a href="/wiki/Porsche" title="Porsche">Porsche</a> automobile firm to build the cars' engines from 1984-87.</p>
<p>The 'MP4' designation originally stood for Marlboro Project Four (the MP4/1 design was complete before the merger). The numbering system has been retained to the present time (the 2016 car is known as the <a href="/wiki/McLaren_MP4-31" title="McLaren MP4-31">McLaren MP4-31</a>). After the change of title sponsor in 1997, the same abbreviation was retained, with the 'M' now standing for McLaren.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_McLaren">Building McLaren</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Dennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Building McLaren">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_1980s">The 1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Dennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Prior to Dennis's arrival at McLaren in September 1980, the team was going through a particularly uncompetitive stint. The team had last won a grand prix with then defending World Champion <a href="/wiki/James_Hunt" title="James Hunt">James Hunt</a> in <a href="/wiki/1977_Formula_One_season" title="1977 Formula One season">1977</a> and had finished a lowly seventh in the <a href="/wiki/1980_Formula_One_season" title="1980 Formula One season">1980</a> constructors' title with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Watson_(driver)" title="John Watson (driver)">John Watson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alain_Prost" title="Alain Prost">Alain Prost</a>. Even in those early days Dennis recognised the young <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frenchman" title="Frenchman">Frenchman</a>'s potential but was unable to prevent him moving to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renault_F1" title="Renault F1">Renault</a> team for <a href="/wiki/1981_Formula_One_season" title="1981 Formula One season">1981</a>, a season that saw McLaren once again winning races. The year also saw many other teams struggling to duplicate <a href="/wiki/John_Barnard" title="John Barnard">Barnard</a>'s revolutionary carbon-fibre chassis.</p>
<p>In 1981 Dennis and his business partners bought out the other McLaren shareholders, <a href="/wiki/Teddy_Mayer" title="Teddy Mayer">Teddy Mayer</a> and Tyler Alexander. In 1983 Dennis persuaded then-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Williams_F1" title="Williams F1">Williams</a> backer <a href="/wiki/Mansour_Ojjeh" title="Mansour Ojjeh">Mansour Ojjeh</a> to become a partner in McLaren International. Ojjeh invested in <a href="/wiki/Porsche" title="Porsche">Porsche</a>-built <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turbocharged" title="Turbocharged">turbocharged</a> engines which carried the name of his company, <a href="/wiki/Techniques_d%27Avant_Garde" title="Techniques d'Avant Garde">Techniques d'Avant Garde</a> (<a href="/wiki/Techniques_d%27Avant_Garde" title="Techniques d'Avant Garde">TAG</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dennis then persuaded the retired <a href="/wiki/Niki_Lauda" title="Niki Lauda">Niki Lauda</a> to return to Formula One for the <a href="/wiki/1982_Formula_One_season" title="1982 Formula One season">1982</a> season and at the season opening <a href="/wiki/1982_South_African_Grand_Prix" title="1982 South African Grand Prix">South African Grand Prix</a> the double World Champion lined up alongside Watson at the start of the season (only after an F1 drivers strike was averted). By the end of the year both drivers had secured two victories and <a href="/wiki/1983_Formula_One_season" title="1983 Formula One season">1983</a> began with more success with Watson's win in <a href="/wiki/1983_United_States_Grand_Prix_West" title="1983 United States Grand Prix West">Long Beach</a>. No more victories followed that year but Lauda debuted the Porsche-powered MP4-1E interim chassis at that season's <a href="/wiki/1983_Dutch_Grand_Prix" title="1983 Dutch Grand Prix">Dutch Grand Prix</a>. By the following race, the <a href="/wiki/1983_Italian_Grand_Prix" title="1983 Italian Grand Prix">Italian Grand Prix</a>, both cars were powered by turbocharged engines: McLaren-Ford had become McLaren-TAG. Convinced by his initial investment, Ojjeh became the major investor in McLaren, taking 60 per cent of the shares. By the end of the year, <a href="/wiki/Alain_Prost" title="Alain Prost">Alain Prost</a>, who had finished second in the 1983 Drivers' Championship with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renault_F1" title="Renault F1">Renault</a> but was fired after publicly criticising the team following the loss of both the Drivers' and Constructors Championships, had been signed to replace Watson. With the experienced Lauda at his side, everything was set for a title challenge in <a href="/wiki/1984_Formula_One_season" title="1984 Formula One season">1984</a>.</p>
<p>In just four years Dennis had turned McLaren from an also-ran team into a front-runner and in 1984, with Barnard's revolutionary <a href="/wiki/McLaren_MP4/2" title="McLaren MP4/2">MP4/2</a> car his work was rewarded with 12 wins from 16 races and both drivers' and constructors' titles. Lauda took the drivers' crown by a half point from his McLaren team-mate Prost, with both drivers scoring more than double the tally of third-placed <a href="/wiki/Elio_de_Angelis" title="Elio de Angelis">Elio de Angelis</a>. While neither Lauda nor Prost were the fastest combination of the year, that title going to reigning World Champion <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Piquet" title="Nelson Piquet">Nelson Piquet</a> in his <a href="/wiki/BMW_M12" title="BMW M12">BMW</a> powered <a href="/wiki/Brabham_BT53" title="Brabham BT53">Brabham BT53</a> who claimed 9 pole positions, the McLaren-TAG's reliability was unmatched. As said by <a href="/wiki/Clive_James" title="Clive James">Clive James</a> in commentary on the official video review of the Formula One season produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formula_One_Constructors_Association" title="Formula One Constructors Association">FOCA</a>: "Anything as fast as the McLarens fell apart, anything as reliable finished later".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The following year the situation was reversed and Prost won his first World Championship while Lauda suffered multiple failures and only won one race. At his home race in <a href="/wiki/1985_Austrian_Grand_Prix" title="1985 Austrian Grand Prix">Austria</a> Lauda announced his permanent retirement from Formula One at the end of the season. McLaren finished eight points ahead of second placed <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">Ferrari</a> that year but the pack was closing and in <a href="/wiki/1986_Formula_One_season" title="1986 Formula One season">1986</a>, McLaren lost out to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Williams_F1" title="Williams F1">Williams</a>, although the consistent Prost won the drivers' title. Lauda's replacement was the 1982 World Champion <a href="/wiki/Keke_Rosberg" title="Keke Rosberg">Keke Rosberg</a>, who also retired from Formula One at the end of the 1986 season.</p>
<p>Dennis signed <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Johansson" title="Stefan Johansson">Stefan Johansson</a> from Ferrari for the <a href="/wiki/1987_Formula_One_season" title="1987 Formula One season">1987</a> season to partner Prost, but it was clear that the TAG engine was no longer competitive in the face of increased manufacturer involvement, and so Dennis approached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honda_Racing_F1" title="Honda Racing F1">Honda</a>, who were at the time supplying rivals Williams and Lotus. Williams' unwillingness to accept a Japanese driver (<a href="/wiki/Satoru_Nakajima" title="Satoru Nakajima">Satoru Nakajima</a>) in place of <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Mansell" title="Nigel Mansell">Nigel Mansell</a>, as well as their displeasure with Williams team management for losing the 1986 Drivers' title, led Honda to transfer their engine supply to the McLaren team. Dennis made no secret that Johansson's seat was only temporary as his intention had been to sign the brilliant Brazilian <a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">Ayrton Senna</a> (who had developed a close relationship with Honda while at Lotus in 1987) to partner double champion Prost. Senna was under contract with Lotus for 1987 but Dennis got his man and midway through the year it was announced that Senna would be joining the team, along with Honda, for three years from <a href="/wiki/1988_Formula_One_season" title="1988 Formula One season">1988</a>.</p>
<p>In 1988 McLaren was supremely dominant, even when compared to the superiority it had experienced in 1984, taking 15 of the 16 races and taking 15 of the 16 pole positions, and both the drivers' and constructors titles with no real opposition. The team won the Constructors Championship with a then record 199 points, 134 in front of second placed Ferrari while Senna won his first World Championship by three points from Prost (Senna won 8 races to Prost's 7). It was behind the scenes that Dennis's political manoeuvering was most required. Dennis masterfully kept the drivers focused on racing, yet it could not last. Prost was highly skilled, "the professor": Senna was volatile but brilliant. Their pairing had always been a recipe for conflict, despite it being Prost who first suggested to Dennis that he sign the Brazilian, and the sign of things to come made its first public appearance on the second lap of the <a href="/wiki/1988_Portuguese_Grand_Prix" title="1988 Portuguese Grand Prix">Portuguese Grand Prix</a>. Senna almost forced Prost into the pit wall at high speed as the Frenchman passed him and took control of the race, Prost going on to win while Senna could only finish sixth. The two had words about the incident after the race and while it all seemed settled, the die had been cast.</p>
<p>By mid-<a href="/wiki/1989_Formula_One_season" title="1989 Formula One season">1989</a> it was becoming impossible to pacify the two warring drivers. Following a fall out in the aftermath of a broken promise between them at <a href="/wiki/1989_San_Marino_Grand_Prix" title="1989 San Marino Grand Prix">San Marino</a> where Prost and Senna agreed before the race that whoever got to the first corner in front wouldn't be challenged into that turn. After the restart following <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Berger" title="Gerhard Berger">Gerhard Berger</a>'s crash, Senna outbraked Prost going into the Tosa turn and broke the agreement, something which angered Prost. Senna later denied making the agreement but Prost was backed up by Marlboro's John Hogan who had been with the drivers when the agreement was made. The result was that the two barely talked for the rest of the season. Prost angered Dennis when not only did he announce that he would be joining Ferrari for <a href="/wiki/1990_Formula_One_season" title="1990 Formula One season">1990</a>, thus ending a highly successful five year stint with McLaren, but also with his public comments about the team and Honda allegedly favoring Senna at the <a href="/wiki/1989_Italian_Grand_Prix" title="1989 Italian Grand Prix">Italian Grand Prix</a>. Dennis then showed his anger in public (a rare event) after Prost, who won the race following an engine failure for Senna, dropped his winners trophy from the podium to the <i><a href="/wiki/Tifosi" title="Tifosi">Tifosi</a></i> showing how unhappy he was despite the win. Dennis flung the Constructors winners trophy at Prost's feet and walked off the podium (Prost was later made to make a public apology over his comments about his unfavorable treatment under the threat of being sacked before the season ended). The "cold war" between the drivers came to a head at the <a href="/wiki/1989_Japanese_Grand_Prix" title="1989 Japanese Grand Prix">Japanese Grand Prix</a> when, at Honda's home track, Prost and Senna had their infamous collision while fighting for the lead late in the race which took Prost out and led to Senna's disqualification and gave Prost his third World Championship.</p>
<p>It was Alain Prost's actions with the winners trophy in Italy in 1989 that prompted Dennis to rule from that point on McLaren had ownership of all trophies won by the team and its drivers, with the drivers free to have replicas made of their race winning trophies for their own collections if they so desired (Dennis reportedly made the decision in the McLaren team transporter within minutes of the episode on the podium at <a href="/wiki/Autodromo_Nazionale_Monza" title="Autodromo Nazionale Monza">Monza</a>). Even though Dennis is no longer the F1 team principal, this rule still exists at McLaren as of the <a href="/wiki/2012_Formula_One_season" title="2012 Formula One season">2012 Formula One season</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_1990s">The 1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Dennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>At the start of the 1990s, McLaren continued to dominate the sport with <a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">Ayrton Senna</a> taking back-to-back titles in 1990 and 1991. McLaren signed the promising newcomer <a href="/wiki/Mika_H%C3%A4kkinen" title="Mika HÃ¤kkinen">Mika HÃ¤kkinen</a> as a test driver at the end of 1992, but by 1992 Williams was once more in the ascendancy. McLaren was not to win another title for seven years. With the loss of Honda power in 1993, Dennis was left haggling with <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford</a> and Ford's works team <a href="/wiki/Benetton_Formula" title="Benetton Formula">Benetton</a> for a supply of competitive engines. A disappointing partnership with <a href="/wiki/Peugeot" title="Peugeot">Peugeot</a> in 1994 failed to yield the expected results and left Dennis searching to find a fourth engine partner in as many years. In October 1994 he agreed terms with <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz" title="Mercedes-Benz">Mercedes-Benz</a> to supply engines from 1995 onwards, an association that continued until 2014.</p>
<p>The first couple of seasons the McLaren-Mercedes relationship was difficult, teething troubles with the engine, chassis challenges, and the driver for 1995 was the ageing <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Mansell" title="Nigel Mansell">Nigel Mansell</a> who did not even fit into the car so <a href="/wiki/Mark_Blundell" title="Mark Blundell">Mark Blundell</a> deputised. When a revised chassis was produced Mansell's performances were not successful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mika_H%C3%A4kkinen" title="Mika HÃ¤kkinen">Mika HÃ¤kkinen</a> gradually assumed leadership of the team but suffered severe head injuries in a crash at the end of the 1995 season, from which he eventually made a complete recovery.</p>
<p>By the mid-1990s Dennis was once more guiding his team towards domination of the sport, and in 1996 he approached Williams's star designer <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Newey" title="Adrian Newey">Adrian Newey</a> to become technical director of McLaren. Newey agreed, and in 1998 McLaren took both the drivers' and the constructors' titles with <a href="/wiki/Mika_H%C3%A4kkinen" title="Mika HÃ¤kkinen">Mika HÃ¤kkinen</a>. A second drivers' title followed in 1999, but <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">Ferrari</a> took the constructors' title.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_2000s">The 2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Dennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The 2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2000 Dennis was made a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Most_Excellent_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Commander of the Most Excellent Order of the British Empire">Commander of the Most Excellent Order of the British Empire</a>.</p>
<p>In 2001 Dennis was faced with a crisis amongst his staff when <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_Racing" title="Jaguar Racing">Jaguar</a> boss <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Rahal" title="Bobby Rahal">Bobby Rahal</a> attempted to lure Newey from McLaren. Details of how Dennis convinced Newey to stay have remained extremely vague but rumours in the specialist motor racing press suggested a deal allowing the designer to work on racing <a href="/wiki/Yacht" title="Yacht">yachts</a>. In the same year, team leader <a href="/wiki/Mika_H%C3%A4kkinen" title="Mika HÃ¤kkinen">Mika HÃ¤kkinen</a> announced that he was to leave the sport. Faced with the loss of his double world champion star driver, Dennis signed another <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kimi_R%C3%A4ikk%C3%B6nen" title="Kimi RÃ¤ikkÃ¶nen">Kimi RÃ¤ikkÃ¶nen</a>.</p>
<p>In 2005 Dennis remained at the top of the sport following a poor year in 2004. Despite producing their strongest performance for several years and winning more grands prix than any of their rivals, McLaren were narrowly beaten in both championships by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renault_F1" title="Renault F1">Renault F1</a> team. A further blow was the announcement that Adrian Newey was to join <a href="/wiki/Red_Bull_Racing" title="Red Bull Racing">Red Bull Racing</a> from the start of 2006.</p>
<p>In December 2005, McLaren announced a title sponsorship deal with <a href="/wiki/Vodafone" title="Vodafone">Vodafone</a> estimated to be worth Â£500 million and the signing of World Champion <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Alonso" title="Fernando Alonso">Fernando Alonso</a>, both to begin in 2007. In the interim, McLaren had a difficult <a href="/wiki/2006_Formula_One_season" title="2006 Formula One season">2006 season</a>, failing to win a race for the first time since the <a href="/wiki/1996_Formula_One_season" title="1996 Formula One season">1996 season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Halfway through the 2006 season, McLaren gave up on perfecting their current car and the team focused on the 2007 MP4-22 car.</p>
<p>The start of the 2007 season saw McLaren in top condition, with only <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">Ferrari</a> as a challenger for the world championships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The McLarens became the cars to beat, with both drivers, <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Alonso" title="Fernando Alonso">Fernando Alonso</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Hamilton" title="Lewis Hamilton">Lewis Hamilton</a>, in the race for the drivers' championship.</p>
<p>However, throughout the 2007 season the team suffered from the in-fighting between Alonso and Hamilton. In the Alonso vs. Hamilton conflict, Dennis always advocated treating his two drivers equally. However, after the <a href="/wiki/2007_Chinese_Grand_Prix" title="2007 Chinese Grand Prix">2007 Chinese Grand Prix</a>, he said "We weren't racing Kimi, we were basically racing Fernando." <i>The Times</i> said his comments "made a nonsense of his claims to be treating his drivers equitably in the World Championship run-in"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> but the team's insistence on parity until the very final race in Brazil underlined Dennis's core philosophy even if it ultimately lost the team the world championship.</p>
<p>The season was also notable for the <a href="/wiki/2007_Formula_One_espionage_controversy" title="2007 Formula One espionage controversy">2007 Formula One espionage controversy</a>, where the team was judged to have been complicit in the theft and use of Ferrari intellectual property; fined a record $100 million USD by the FIA - and stripped of all constructor championship points for season.</p>
<p>Ron Dennis announced on 16 January 2009 that he would be stepping down as McLaren's team principal on 1 March 2009, to be replaced by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Whitmarsh" title="Martin Whitmarsh">Martin Whitmarsh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> On 16 April 2009 Dennis reportedly handed over complete control of the McLaren F1 team to Whitmarsh, citing a desire to pursue new challenges, especially focusing on the McLaren road car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 17 November 2011 Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> formally opened the <a href="/wiki/McLaren" title="McLaren">McLaren</a> Production Centre, a new building that sits alongside the existing <a href="/wiki/McLaren" title="McLaren">McLaren</a> Technology Centre. "I want this country to be respected for what we make, as well as the services we provide," said the Prime Minister. "<a href="/wiki/McLaren" title="McLaren">McLaren</a>, in breaking new ground, are a powerful rebuke to those who say the manufacturing base has gone. Visits to places like this make me optimistic that we can in time ride out this storm and come out stronger." In the period between 22 April 2005 and 16 June 2011 the Conservative Party accepted Â£126,200 in donations from Ron Dennis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GMBF1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-GMBF1-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_of_McLaren">Future of McLaren</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Dennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Future of McLaren">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 16 November 2009 McLaren parted ways with Mercedes as it bought debut season driver and constructor championship-winning Brawn GP. However Mercedes continued to supply engines to McLaren until 2015 when the team's current Honda engine deal began. Dennis said one reason they had parted ways was because of McLaren's plan to become a car manufacturer. The company's first car since the 1990s was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McLaren_MP4-12C" title="McLaren MP4-12C">McLaren MP4-12C</a>, launched in 2011, (which was replaced in 2013 by the <a href="/wiki/McLaren_650S" title="McLaren 650S">McLaren 650S</a>). The successor to the McLaren F1, the <a href="/wiki/McLaren_P1" title="McLaren P1">McLaren P1</a> was put into production in 2014. The company completed the buy-back of Mercedes's 40 per cent stake in 2011. On 16 January 2014 it was announced that Ron Dennis has returned to the role of Group CEO of McLaren, combining his current role as Chairman of McLaren Group. Following a two-year power struggle within the company, the majority shareholders of McLaren attempted to force Dennis into taking a gardening leave in November 2016, having him stay away from the business side of the team until his contract was due to expire in January 2017. After failing to receive a court injuction against this decision, Dennis announced on 15 November 2016 that he was stepping down as the boss of <a href="/wiki/McLaren" title="McLaren">McLaren</a> after 35 years. He will however still be involved in the company as he holds a 25% stake in the McLaren Group and has declared that he will use his influence "to protect the interests and value of McLaren and help shape its future."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Businesses">Other Businesses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Dennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other Businesses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dennis has held shares and founded numerous companies other than his major commitment, McLaren Technology Group. In 1997, <a href="/wiki/McLaren" title="McLaren">McLaren</a> catering chef handed Dennis a three page business plan to start their own catering company. In 1998 they formally founded <a href="/wiki/Absolute_Taste" title="Absolute Taste">Absolute Taste</a>, to which Dennis retains 55% ownership to this day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dennis is a Business Ambassador for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_Government" title="UK Government">UK Government</a>, working for The Department of International Trade. Dennis also holds the role as Global Adviser for Chinese investment group, Minsheng Investment.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mansour_Ojjeh" title="Mansour Ojjeh">Mansour Ojjeh</a>, owner of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TAG_Group" title="TAG Group">TAG Group</a> and Dennis' business partner at <a href="/wiki/McLaren_Technology_Group" title="McLaren Technology Group">MTG</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TAG_Group" title="TAG Group">TAG Group</a> purchased luxury watchmaker, <a href="/wiki/TAG_Heuer" title="TAG Heuer">Heuer</a>, forming the present <a href="/wiki/TAG_Heuer" title="TAG Heuer">TAG Heuer</a>. Ojjeh sold a significant stake in the business to Dennis, and both business partners sold the watchmaker in 1999 to <a href="/wiki/LVMH" title="LVMH">LVMH</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, Dennis founded a charity, <b>Dreamchasing</b>, which aims to "help young people achieve their aspirations and, through their successes, to become inspirational role models for others".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> His charity's first operation was a sponsorship programme for families in Ethiopia, where, supporting the Fida International charity, has assisted over 40 families out of poverty, additionally paying education costs to some.</p>
<p>Dennis oversees the company behind McLaren Technology Group's ownership of <a href="/wiki/Lydden_Hill_Race_Circuit" title="Lydden Hill Race Circuit">Lydden Hill Race Circuit</a>, which is currently leased out.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Ronspeak.22">"Ronspeak"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Dennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title='Edit section: "Ronspeak"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Ronspeak" is the term coined for the style of speech used by Dennis, and has become a well-used phrase in the F1 paddock to describe sentences of unneeded complexity. Dennis is renowned for his excessively verbose, evasive and cautious answers to tough questions from Formula One journalists. It started about 1980, when sponsorship started to play a more prominent role in the sport.</p>
<p>However while acknowledging that the term has been used to criticise Dennis, the former editor-in-chief of <i><a href="/wiki/F1_Racing" title="F1 Racing">F1 Racing</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Matt_Bishop" title="Matt Bishop">Matt Bishop</a>, who now works for <a href="/wiki/McLaren" title="McLaren">McLaren</a> as Group Head of Communications and Public Relations, argues that "Ronspeak" is not a vice; rather, it is informative and accurate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-f1longinterview_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-f1longinterview-27">[27]</a></sup> Dennis, in describing Fernando Alonso's contribution to the McLaren team's development, said his experience and ability "[prevented] an F1 team from going down [time wasting] technical cul-de-sacs â and as a result, car-developmental progress becomes more linear." Bishop described this as a prime example of Ronspeak, hailing it as "logical, informative and insightful. [but also] careful... in that what it doesn't do is compare Alonso's exceptional all-round ability with that of his predecessors."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-f1longinterview_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-f1longinterview-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Dennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 15 February 2008 Dennis announced his intention to separate from his wife of 22 years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Lisa, with whom he has three children. Lisa Dennis was the author of a series of illustrated children's books during the 1990s; they followed the Formula One adventures of main characters "Mac" and "Lauren".</p>
<p>Ron Dennis has subsequently divorced Lisa Dennis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and is dating Carol Weatherall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2011, Dennis was banned from driving for six months after <a href="/wiki/Point_system_(driving)" title="Point system (driving)">accumulation of points</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
