<p><b>Ronald Edward Santo</b> (February 25, 1940 – December 3, 2010) was an American <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> (MLB) <a href="/wiki/Third_baseman" title="Third baseman">third baseman</a> who played for the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cubs" title="Chicago Cubs">Chicago Cubs</a> from 1960 through 1973 and the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a> in 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_Baseball_Reference_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_Baseball_Reference-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1990, Santo became a member of the Cubs broadcasting team providing commentary for Cubs games on <a href="/wiki/WGN_(AM)" title="WGN (AM)">WGN radio</a> and remained at that position until his death in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Library_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Library-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1999, he was selected to the Cubs All-Century Team. He was inducted into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" title="National Baseball Hall of Fame">National Baseball Hall of Fame</a> in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Santo grew up in Southeast Seattle, attending Franklin High School, and played newly organized youth baseball in the Babe Ruth League. At age 14 he made the Seattle, Washington All Star Babe Ruth team which advanced to the 1954 Babe Ruth World Series. In a game at then Washington DC Stadium, Dave Tacher (coach) inserted Santo at first base to replace his 15 year old who broke his thumb. In that game Santo hit a grand slam home run over the 354 mark in left center field and Washington all stars defeated Tennessee.</p>
<p>Santo was an <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All-Star</a> for nine seasons during his 15-year career. He led the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> (NL) in <a href="/wiki/Triple_(baseball)" title="Triple (baseball)">triples</a> one time, in <a href="/wiki/Base_on_balls" title="Base on balls">walks</a> four times, and in <a href="/wiki/On-base_percentage" title="On-base percentage">on-base percentage</a> two times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_Baseball_Reference_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_Baseball_Reference-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame-6">[6]</a></sup> He <a href="/wiki/Batting_average" title="Batting average">batted</a> .300 or more and hit 30 or more <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home runs</a> four times each, and is the only third baseman in MLB history to post eight consecutive seasons with over 90 <a href="/wiki/Run_batted_in" title="Run batted in">runs batted in</a> (RBI) (1963–70).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_Baseball_Reference_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_Baseball_Reference-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame-6">[6]</a></sup> He and fellow Hall of Famer <a href="/wiki/Mike_Schmidt" title="Mike Schmidt">Mike Schmidt</a> are the only third basemen after <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Mathews" title="Eddie Mathews">Eddie Mathews</a> to hit 300 career home runs. Santo is second to Mathews in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slugging_average" title="Slugging average">slugging average</a> (.464), and is the third ranking third baseman in walks (1,108), in RBI (1,331), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_bases" title="Total bases">total bases</a> (3,779).</p>
<p>He also was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_Glove_Award" title="Gold Glove Award">Gold Glove Award</a> winner for five consecutive seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He led the NL in <a href="/wiki/Total_chances" title="Total chances">total chances</a> eight times, in games, <a href="/wiki/Putout" title="Putout">putouts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assist_(baseball)" title="Assist (baseball)">assists</a> seven times each, and in <a href="/wiki/Double_play" title="Double play">double plays</a> six times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame-6">[6]</a></sup> From <a href="/wiki/1966_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1966 Major League Baseball season">1966</a> to <a href="/wiki/1974_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1974 Major League Baseball season">1974</a>, he held the NL record for assists in a single season. He also set NL records for career assists (4,532), total chances (6,777) and double plays (389) at third base, all of which were eventually broken between <a href="/wiki/1986_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1986 Major League Baseball season">1986</a> and <a href="/wiki/1988_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1988 Major League Baseball season">1988</a> by <a href="/wiki/Mike_Schmidt" title="Mike Schmidt">Mike Schmidt</a>. His NL total of 2,102 games at third base is 52 short of Mathews' league record, and he ranks sixth in putouts (1,930) and ninth in <a href="/wiki/Fielding_percentage" title="Fielding percentage">fielding percentage</a> (.954).</p>
<p>Santo enjoyed his success despite battling <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus_type_1" title="Diabetes mellitus type 1">diabetes</a> since he was a teenager, a condition which was carefully and generally concealed publicly until 1971; it eventually necessitated the amputation of the lower half of both his legs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Library_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Library-4">[4]</a></sup> Since 1979, Santo endorsed the Juvenile Diabetes Research Foundation's annual Ron Santo Walk to Cure Diabetes in Chicago. He helped raise over $65 million for the foundation. In 2002, he was named the Juvenile Diabetes Research Foundation's "Person of the Year".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_Uniform_Number_to_be_Retired_at_Baseball_Almanac_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_Uniform_Number_to_be_Retired_at_Baseball_Almanac-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chicago_Cubs_.281960.E2.80.9373.29">Chicago Cubs (1960–73)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Santo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chicago Cubs (1960–73)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Santo was signed as a <a href="/wiki/Free_agent" title="Free agent">free agent</a> by the Chicago Cubs in <a href="/wiki/1959_Chicago_Cubs_season" title="1959 Chicago Cubs season">1959</a>, and made his debut on June 26, 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-br-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_Trades_and_Transactions_at_Baseball_Almanac_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_Trades_and_Transactions_at_Baseball_Almanac-11">[11]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/1961_Chicago_Cubs_season" title="1961 Chicago Cubs season">1961</a> he set a Cubs record with 41 <a href="/wiki/Double_play" title="Double play">double plays</a> at third base, breaking the previous mark of 33 set by <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Friberg" title="Bernie Friberg">Bernie Friberg</a> in 1923. In <a href="/wiki/1962_Chicago_Cubs_season" title="1962 Chicago Cubs season">1962</a> he led the National League in <a href="/wiki/Assist_(baseball)" title="Assist (baseball)">assists</a> for the first time with 332, setting the team record for assists at third base, breaking the mark of 323 set by <a href="/wiki/Randy_Jackson_(baseball)" title="Randy Jackson (baseball)">Randy Jackson</a> in 1951. Santo continued to lead the NL in assists every year through 1968, breaking <a href="/wiki/Ned_Williamson" title="Ned Williamson">Ned Williamson</a>'s major league record of leading the league six times; <a href="/wiki/Brooks_Robinson" title="Brooks Robinson">Brooks Robinson</a> went on to lead the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> eight times. <a href="/wiki/Mike_Schmidt" title="Mike Schmidt">Mike Schmidt</a> eventually tied Santo's NL mark of seven. In 1963 Santo broke the modern NL record with 374 assists at third base, passing <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Leach" title="Tommy Leach">Tommy Leach</a>'s 1904 mark of 371. In 1966, he set the all-time league record with 391, the previous record being <a href="/wiki/Billy_Shindle" title="Billy Shindle">Billy Shindle</a>'s 382 in 1892; his total was 99 higher than that of league runner-up <a href="/wiki/Ken_Boyer" title="Ken Boyer">Ken Boyer</a>. Santo broke his own record in 1967 with 393 assists,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Page_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Page-12">[12]</a></sup> which remained the NL record until Schmidt posted 404 in 1974. He also finished fourth in the <a href="/wiki/1967_Chicago_Cubs_season" title="1967 Chicago Cubs season">1967</a> NL <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_Most_Valuable_Player_Award" title="Major League Baseball Most Valuable Player Award">Most Valuable Player Award</a> voting results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Santo's assist totals from 1963 through 1968 were the six highest by an NL third baseman between 1905 and 1973. He also led the NL in <a href="/wiki/Putout" title="Putout">putouts</a> every year from 1962 through 1967 and again in 1969, tying the league record shared by <a href="/wiki/Pie_Traynor" title="Pie Traynor">Pie Traynor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Willie_Jones_(baseball)" title="Willie Jones (baseball)">Willie Jones</a> in leading the league seven times;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tim_Wallach" title="Tim Wallach">Tim Wallach</a> later tied the mark as well.</p>
<p>Santo was deeply saddened by the loss of teammate <a href="/wiki/Ken_Hubbs" title="Ken Hubbs">Ken Hubbs</a>, the Cubs second baseman, killed in a plane crash just prior to the 1964 season. Santo was interviewed by <a href="/wiki/Tom_Harmon" title="Tom Harmon">Tom Harmon</a>, narrator of the film <i>A Glimpse of Greatness–The Story of Ken Hubbs</i>, in which Santo paid the highest respects to the young Hubbs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1969, Santo and <a href="/wiki/1969_Chicago_Cubs_season" title="1969 Chicago Cubs season">the Cubs</a> were in first place in the <a href="/wiki/National_League_East" title="National League East">National League East</a> for 180 days, before going 8–17 in their final 25 games, while the <a href="/wiki/1969_New_York_Mets_season" title="1969 New York Mets season">New York "Miracle" Mets</a> went 37–11 in their final 48 games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> During that season, the Cubs sent their entire starting <a href="/wiki/Infielder" title="Infielder">infield</a>, including Santo, to the <a href="/wiki/1969_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="1969 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All-Star Game</a> in Washington, D.C.; he and Cubs shortstop <a href="/wiki/Don_Kessinger" title="Don Kessinger">Don Kessinger</a> started for the NL team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Santo finished the season with a .289 <a href="/wiki/Batting_average" title="Batting average">batting average</a>, 29 <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home runs</a> and a career-high 123 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Runs_batted_in" title="Runs batted in">runs batted in</a> (RBI), and finished fifth in the NL's MVP voting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-br-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Heel_click">Heel click</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Santo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Heel click">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the 1969 season, Santo became known for performing a heel click after a game on June 22, 1969 against the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Expos" title="Montreal Expos">Montreal Expos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Page_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Page-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-18">[18]</a></sup> Going into the bottom of the ninth inning, the Expos were leading 6–3. With one out, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Popovich" title="Paul Popovich">Paul Popovich</a> hit a <a href="/wiki/Single_(baseball)" title="Single (baseball)">single</a> and moved up to second base after another single by <a href="/wiki/Billy_Williams_(left_fielder)" title="Billy Williams (left fielder)">Billy Williams</a>. Although Santo <a href="/wiki/Baseball_rules" title="Baseball rules">grounded out</a> for the second out, Popovich and Williams each moved up a base. Then future Hall of Famer <a href="/wiki/Ernie_Banks" title="Ernie Banks">Ernie Banks</a> singled to bring home Williams and Popovich and bring the Cubs within a <a href="/wiki/Run_(baseball)" title="Run (baseball)">run</a>. <a href="/wiki/Rick_Bladt" title="Rick Bladt">Rick Bladt</a> substituted as a <a href="/wiki/Pinch_runner" title="Pinch runner">pinch runner</a> for Banks. That set it up for <a href="/wiki/Jim_Hickman_(1960s_outfielder)" title="Jim Hickman (1960s outfielder)">Jim Hickman</a>, who hit a two-run <a href="/wiki/Walk-off_home_run" title="Walk-off home run">walk-off home run</a> to win the game 7–6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> When Hickman reached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Home_plate" title="Home plate">home plate</a>, Santo was so excited that after congratulating him by bear hugging and pounding him on his head, Santo ran down the third base line and jumped three times, clicking his heels on each jump.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Library_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Library-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Page_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Page-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The next day, Santo walked into <a href="/wiki/Manager_(baseball)" title="Manager (baseball)">manager</a> <a href="/wiki/Leo_Durocher" title="Leo Durocher">Leo Durocher</a>'s office; Durocher asked him to keep clicking his heels whenever the Cubs won at <a href="/wiki/Wrigley_Field" title="Wrigley Field">Wrigley Field</a> to motivate the team. Santo continued this after every home win. The stunt antagonized opponents and served to make the team a target for payback in the final weeks of the season. When the Cubs began their September <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Losing_streak_(sport)" title="Losing streak (sport)">swoon</a>, which took place shortly after Santo called out rookie teammate <a href="/wiki/Donald_Young_(baseball)" title="Donald Young (baseball)">Don Young</a> in public after a loss against the Mets in New York, he discontinued the heel click routine suddenly. His final "click" was performed on September 2, the last Cub home victory while still in first place. During and after the epic collapse, Santo never again performed the heel click, as critics decried the routine for its arrogance and overconfidence, which many believe was at the root of the late fade.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> On the day Santo was enshrined in the Hall of Fame, the Cubs' starting lineup all did the "kick" at the start of the game in tribute.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade_veto">Trade veto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Santo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Trade veto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1973, Santo became the first player to invoke the ten-and-five rule under the <a href="/wiki/Collective_bargaining" title="Collective bargaining">collective bargaining</a> agreement signed after the <a href="/wiki/1972_Major_League_Baseball_strike" title="1972 Major League Baseball strike">1972 Major League Baseball strike</a>. The rule allowed players with ten years' service, the last five with the same team, to decline any trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Page_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Page-12">[12]</a></sup> The Cubs had agreed upon a deal to send Santo to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/California_Angels" title="California Angels">California Angels</a>; the ballclub would have received in return two young pitchers: <a href="/wiki/Andy_Hassler" title="Andy Hassler">Andy Hassler</a>, who went on to have a middling career as a <a href="/wiki/Relief_pitcher" title="Relief pitcher">reliever</a>/spot starter, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bruce_Heinbechner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bruce Heinbechner (page does not exist)">Bruce Heinbechner</a>, a very highly regarded left-handed pitching <a href="/wiki/Prospect_(sports)" title="Prospect (sports)">prospect</a>, who died before the beginning of the 1974 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Santo didn't want to play on the West Coast and vetoed the deal.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chicago_White_Sox_.281974.29">Chicago White Sox (1974)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Santo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Chicago White Sox (1974)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Cubs still wanted to trade Santo, and since his preference was to stay in Chicago, they worked out a deal with the White Sox in December 1973, acquiring <a href="/wiki/Catcher" title="Catcher">catcher</a> <a href="/wiki/Steve_Swisher" title="Steve Swisher">Steve Swisher</a>, and three young pitchers: <a href="/wiki/Jim_Kremmel" title="Jim Kremmel">Jim Kremmel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ken_Frailing" title="Ken Frailing">Ken Frailing</a>, and one of Santo's future co-broadcasters, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Stone_(baseball)" title="Steve Stone (baseball)">Steve Stone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_Trades_and_Transactions_at_Baseball_Almanac_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_Trades_and_Transactions_at_Baseball_Almanac-11">[11]</a></sup> The White Sox already had a third baseman, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Melton" title="Bill Melton">Bill Melton</a>, so Santo was relegated mostly to <a href="/wiki/Designated_hitter" title="Designated hitter">designated hitter</a> duty, which he hated. He wanted to play in the field, but White Sox manager <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Tanner" title="Chuck Tanner">Chuck Tanner</a> would not bench Melton and unsuccessfully tried Santo at second base. Finishing <a href="/wiki/1974_Chicago_White_Sox_season" title="1974 Chicago White Sox season">1974</a> with a .221 batting average and 5 home runs, Santo retired from baseball at the age of 34.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-br-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-retirement">Post-retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Santo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Post-retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Broadcast_career">Broadcast career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Santo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Broadcast career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the "single biggest Cubs fan of all time", Santo joined the Cubs' broadcast booth in 1990 as the <a href="/wiki/WGN_(AM)" title="WGN (AM)">WGN radio</a> <a href="/wiki/Color_commentator" title="Color commentator">color commentator</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Library_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Library-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cubsb_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cubsb-21">[21]</a></sup> He worked with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sports_commentary" title="Sports commentary">play-by-play announcer</a> <a href="/wiki/Pat_Hughes_(sportscaster)" title="Pat Hughes (sportscaster)">Pat Hughes</a>, and these radio broadcasts were also known as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pat_and_Ron_Show" title="Pat and Ron Show">Pat and Ron Show</a></i>. He also worked with <a href="/wiki/Harry_Caray" title="Harry Caray">Harry Caray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thom_Brennaman" title="Thom Brennaman">Thom Brennaman</a>, Steve Stone and <a href="/wiki/Bob_Brenly" title="Bob Brenly">Bob Brenly</a>. Santo also briefly worked with <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Bears" title="Chicago Bears">Chicago Bears</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_Bay_Packers" title="Green Bay Packers">Green Bay Packers</a> commentator <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Larrivee" title="Wayne Larrivee">Wayne Larrivee</a>. In addition to his broadcasting career, he did commercials for <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Seattle_Sutton%27s_Healthy_Eating&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Seattle Sutton's Healthy Eating (page does not exist)">Seattle Sutton's Healthy Eating</a>, which he endorsed, along with joining Hughes in ads for <a href="/wiki/Walgreens" title="Walgreens">Walgreens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet" title="Chevrolet">Chevrolet</a>. In Chicago, Santo was known for his unabashed broadcast enthusiasm, including groans and cheers during the game. As excitable as Santo was when a great play for the Cubs occurred, he was equally as vocal in his displeasure when events turned against the Cubs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Struggle_with_diabetes">Struggle with diabetes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Santo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Struggle with diabetes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early years of his playing career, he carefully concealed the fact that he had <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus_type_1" title="Diabetes mellitus type 1">type 1 diabetes</a>. He feared that if this information were to be known, he would be forced into retirement. Because the methods of regulating diabetes in the 1960s and 1970s were not as advanced as they are today,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SomewhereACub_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-SomewhereACub-22">[22]</a></sup> Santo gauged his <a href="/wiki/Blood_sugar" title="Blood sugar">blood sugar</a> levels based on his moods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thisoldcub_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-thisoldcub-23">[23]</a></sup> If he felt his blood sugar was low, he would snack on a candy bar in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locker_room" title="Locker room">clubhouse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thisoldcub_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-thisoldcub-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>As part of the publicity surrounding "Ron Santo Day" at Wrigley Field on August 28, 1971, he revealed his struggle with diabetes. He was diagnosed with this disease at the age of 18, and was given a life expectancy of 25 years. Santo had both his legs amputated below the knee as a result of his diabetes: the right in 2001 and the left in 2002.</p>
<p>In 2004 Santo and his battle against diabetes were the subject of a documentary, <i><a href="/wiki/This_Old_Cub" title="This Old Cub">This Old Cub</a></i>. The film was written, co-produced and directed by Santo's son Jeff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SomewhereACub_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-SomewhereACub-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Santo shared a bond in this respect with 2008 Cubs rookie <a href="/wiki/Sam_Fuld" title="Sam Fuld">Sam Fuld</a>, who also suffers from type 1 diabetes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Charities">Charities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Santo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Charities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Santo family has been involved with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juvenile_Diabetes_Research_Foundation" title="Juvenile Diabetes Research Foundation">Juvenile Diabetes Research Foundation</a> since 1979, with the annual Ron Santo Walk to Cure Diabetes in Chicago having raised over $65 million for the organization. In 2002, Santo was named the Juvenile Diabetes Research Foundation's "Person of the Year".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_Uniform_Number_to_be_Retired_at_Baseball_Almanac_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_Uniform_Number_to_be_Retired_at_Baseball_Almanac-9">[9]</a></sup> Santo also inspired <a href="/wiki/Bill_Holden_(schoolteacher)" title="Bill Holden (schoolteacher)">Bill Holden</a> to walk 2,100 miles from <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> to Chicago to raise $250,000 for diabetes research. During the <a href="/wiki/2016_World_Series" title="2016 World Series">2016 World Series</a>, the JDRF hosted watch parties for road games hosted by family members.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Santo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Santo died at 12:40 am on December 3, 2010<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rozner_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rozner-25">[25]</a></sup> in a hospital in <a href="/wiki/Scottsdale,_Arizona" title="Scottsdale, Arizona">Scottsdale, Arizona</a>, due to complications from <a href="/wiki/Bladder_cancer" title="Bladder cancer">bladder cancer</a> and diabetes. (Many media outlets reported the date as "the night of the 2nd" or "overnight".) Santo had lapsed into a coma on December 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Report:_Ron_Santo_dead_at_70_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Report:_Ron_Santo_dead_at_70-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> A funeral mass was celebrated at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Name_Cathedral,_Chicago" title="Holy Name Cathedral, Chicago">Holy Name Cathedral</a> on December 10, where Santo's casket was carried in by former teammates Ernie Banks, <a href="/wiki/Ferguson_Jenkins" title="Ferguson Jenkins">Ferguson Jenkins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Randy_Hundley" title="Randy Hundley">Randy Hundley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Beckert" title="Glenn Beckert">Glenn Beckert</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Billy_Williams_(left_fielder)" title="Billy Williams (left fielder)">Billy Williams</a>, draped with the No. 10 flag that flew over Wrigley the day his number was retired. He was eulogized by his longtime broadcast partner <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pat_Hughes_(radio_sports_announcer)" title="Pat Hughes (radio sports announcer)">Pat Hughes</a>, along with Cubs owner <a href="/wiki/Thomas_S._Ricketts" title="Thomas S. Ricketts">Tom Ricketts</a> and MLB Commissioner <a href="/wiki/Bud_Selig" title="Bud Selig">Bud Selig</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Following the service, the procession paused outside <a href="/wiki/Tribune_Tower" title="Tribune Tower">Tribune Tower</a>, home of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WGN_Radio" title="WGN Radio">WGN Radio</a>, before heading north to circle Wrigley Field, starting at third base. Santo was later cremated and his ashes scattered on the field at the Friendly Confines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 10, 2011, Santo was memorialized and "immortalized" at Wrigley Field with the presentation of a statue in his likeness. The statue is a portrayal of a young Santo playing defense at third base, leaning to his right while throwing a ball.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hall_of_Fame_candidacy">Hall of Fame candidacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Santo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Hall of Fame candidacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="BBWAA">BBWAA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Santo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: BBWAA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When Santo first became eligible for election to the <a href="/wiki/National_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_and_Museum" title="National Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum">Baseball Hall of Fame</a> in <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_balloting,_1980" title="Baseball Hall of Fame balloting, 1980">1980</a>, he was named on less than four percent of all ballots cast by the <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Writers%27_Association_of_America" title="Baseball Writers' Association of America">Baseball Writers' Association of America</a> (BBWAA), resulting in his removal from the ballot in subsequent years; he was one of several players re-added to the ballot in <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_balloting,_1985" title="Baseball Hall of Fame balloting, 1985">1985</a> following widespread complaints about overlooked candidates, with the remainder of their 15 years of eligibility restored even if this extended beyond the usual limit of 20 years after their last season. After receiving 13 percent of the vote in the 1985 election, his vote totals increased in 10 of the next 13 years until he received 43 percent of the vote in his final year on the <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_balloting,_1998" title="Baseball Hall of Fame balloting, 1998">1998</a> ballot, finishing third in the voting behind electee <a href="/wiki/Don_Sutton" title="Don Sutton">Don Sutton</a> and 2000 inductee <a href="/wiki/Tony_P%C3%A9rez" title="Tony Pérez">Tony Pérez</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Veterans_Committee">Veterans Committee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Santo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Veterans Committee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following revamped voting procedures for the <a href="/wiki/Veterans_Committee" title="Veterans Committee">Veterans Committee</a>, which had elected players retired for over 20 years to the Hall of Fame, Santo finished third in <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_balloting,_2003" title="Baseball Hall of Fame balloting, 2003">2003</a>, tied for first in <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_balloting,_2005" title="Baseball Hall of Fame balloting, 2005">2005</a>, and again finished first in voting for the <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_balloting,_2007" title="Baseball Hall of Fame balloting, 2007">2007</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_balloting,_2009" title="Baseball Hall of Fame balloting, 2009">2009</a> inductions, but fell short of the required number of votes each year.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Golden_Era_Committee">Golden Era Committee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Santo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Golden Era Committee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Santo's next opportunity for admission to the Hall of Fame following a further major change to the voting structure and process announced in 2010, came during the voting in 2011 by the new 16-member Golden Era Committee which considers every three years, ten candidates identified by the Historical Overview Committee from the 1947 to 1972 era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although Santo became a widely supported candidate for selection, his initial poor showing in balloting has been attributed to various factors, including a longtime tendency of BBWAA voters to overlook third basemen; at the time Santo retired, only three of the over 120 players elected were third basemen, and only <a href="/wiki/Pie_Traynor" title="Pie Traynor">Pie Traynor</a> had been elected by the BBWAA. Also, the fact that Santo's best years occurred in the 1960s, when offensive statistics were relatively lower than in many other eras (due to an enlarged <a href="/wiki/Strike_zone" title="Strike zone">strike zone</a> and raised <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitcher%27s_mound" title="Pitcher's mound">pitcher's mounds</a>, among other things), has been cited as a factor that led the voters to perhaps overlook him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Hall_of_Fame_Case_for_Ron_Santo_at_The_Cub_Reporter_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Hall_of_Fame_Case_for_Ron_Santo_at_The_Cub_Reporter-34">[34]</a></sup> Another possible reason that was suggested was that voters had not focused sufficiently on Santo's high walk totals and defense. These aspects of play are perhaps more valued by <a href="/wiki/Sabermetrics" title="Sabermetrics">sabermetrics</a> — newer methods of evaluating a baseball player's productivity — than they have been by BBWAA voters in the past. For example, Santo's career <a href="/wiki/On-base_plus_slugging#Adjusted_OPS" title="On-base plus slugging">adjusted on-base plus slugging</a> (OPS+; the sum of a player's on-base percentage and slugging percentage, adjusted for the park and league in which he played, and expressed as a percentage of the league average) would rank him exactly in the middle of the ten major league third basemen who were in the Hall of Fame in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Hall_of_Fame_Case_for_Ron_Santo_at_The_Cub_Reporter_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Hall_of_Fame_Case_for_Ron_Santo_at_The_Cub_Reporter-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>One argument that was raised against Santo's Hall of Fame candidacy is that his <a href="/wiki/Baseball_statistics" title="Baseball statistics">batting statistics</a>, over the course of his career, were significantly better at home than on the road. He hit 216 of his 342 home runs at home, and only 126 on the road.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_Home-Away_splits_at_Baseball_Reference_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_Home-Away_splits_at_Baseball_Reference-36">[36]</a></sup> His career batting average at home was .296, versus .257 on the road.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_Home-Away_splits_at_Baseball_Reference_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_Home-Away_splits_at_Baseball_Reference-36">[36]</a></sup> However, several players elected to the Hall of Fame by the BBWAA, such as <a href="/wiki/Carl_Yastrzemski" title="Carl Yastrzemski">Carl Yastrzemski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wade_Boggs" title="Wade Boggs">Wade Boggs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Rice" title="Jim Rice">Jim Rice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kirby_Puckett" title="Kirby Puckett">Kirby Puckett</a>, batted significantly better in their home parks than they did on the road.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Hall of Famers with a significant differential between their home numbers and road numbers in terms of home runs include <a href="/wiki/Mel_Ott" title="Mel Ott">Mel Ott</a> (323 homers at home and 188 on the road), <a href="/wiki/Frank_Robinson" title="Frank Robinson">Frank Robinson</a> (321 at home, 265 on the road), <a href="/wiki/Jimmie_Foxx" title="Jimmie Foxx">Jimmie Foxx</a> (299 at home, 235 on the road) and <a href="/wiki/Hank_Greenberg" title="Hank Greenberg">Hank Greenberg</a> (205 at home, 126 on the road).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Others have also commented that two Cub players who were in their prime during Santo's prime years have already been honored by the Hall of Fame (<a href="/wiki/Ferguson_Jenkins" title="Ferguson Jenkins">Ferguson Jenkins</a> and Billy Williams), and the Cubs also featured a third Hall of Famer, Ernie Banks, who was arguably past his prime, yet the team never won a pennant. However, the late 1960s Cubs were far from the only team in baseball history with multiple Hall of Famers that did not win a pennant or a <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a>.</p>
<p>Santo also fell short of such traditional standards of Hall election as <a href="/wiki/3,000_hit_club" title="3,000 hit club">3,000 hits</a> and <a href="/wiki/500_home_run_club" title="500 home run club">500 home runs</a>; however, by the time his career ended, only two third basemen (<a href="/wiki/Brooks_Robinson" title="Brooks Robinson">Brooks Robinson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lave_Cross" title="Lave Cross">Lave Cross</a>) had even collected 2,500 hits, and only one (<a href="/wiki/Eddie_Mathews" title="Eddie Mathews">Eddie Mathews</a>) had reached the 500-home run plateau.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bill_James" title="Bill James">Bill James</a>, a notable <a href="/wiki/Sabermetrics" title="Sabermetrics">statistical guru</a> who has ranked Santo among the 100 greatest players of all time (sixth among third basemen), believed his election to the Hall of Fame was long overdue.</p>
<p>Even though Santo was disappointed at being bypassed by the Hall of Fame, on the day his jersey number 10 was retired by the Cubs, the ever-optimistic and emotional "old Cub" told the cheering Wrigley Field crowd, "This is <i>my</i> Hall of Fame!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_Uniform_Number_to_be_Retired_at_Baseball_Almanac_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_Uniform_Number_to_be_Retired_at_Baseball_Almanac-9">[9]</a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/Ryne_Sandberg" title="Ryne Sandberg">Ryne Sandberg</a>'s Hall of Fame acceptance speech in 2005, Sandberg reiterated his support for Santo's selection, saying, "...for what it's worth, Ron Santo just gained one more vote from the Veterans Committee."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> On April 19, 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_House_of_Representatives" title="Illinois House of Representatives">Illinois House of Representatives</a> adopted HB 109 (<a href="/wiki/Tom_Cross_(politician)" title="Tom Cross (politician)">Cross</a>), urging the Veterans Committee of the Baseball Hall of Fame to elect Ron Santo to the Baseball Hall of Fame. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HR109_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-HR109-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Santo initially received little support for induction into the Hall of Fame, his standing among baseball enthusiasts and <a href="/wiki/Sabermetrics" title="Sabermetrics">sabermetricians</a> gradually increased over time, culminating with his induction to the Hall of Fame two years after his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_Elected_to_Hall_of_Fame_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_Elected_to_Hall_of_Fame-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SomewhereACub_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-SomewhereACub-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hall_of_Fame_election">Hall of Fame election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Santo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Hall of Fame election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On December 5, 2011, the 16-member Golden Era Committee that began voting on ten candidates selected by the BBWAA screening committee,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> was composed of <a href="/wiki/Hank_Aaron" title="Hank Aaron">Hank Aaron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pat_Gillick" title="Pat Gillick">Pat Gillick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Al_Kaline" title="Al Kaline">Al Kaline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Kiner" title="Ralph Kiner">Ralph Kiner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Lasorda" title="Tommy Lasorda">Tommy Lasorda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Marichal" title="Juan Marichal">Juan Marichal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brooks_Robinson" title="Brooks Robinson">Brooks Robinson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Billy_Williams_(left_fielder)" title="Billy Williams (left fielder)">Billy Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Beeston" title="Paul Beeston">Paul Beeston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_DeWitt" title="Bill DeWitt">Bill DeWitt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roland_Hemond" title="Roland Hemond">Roland Hemond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gene_Michael" title="Gene Michael">Gene Michael</a>, <a href="/wiki/Al_Rosen" title="Al Rosen">Al Rosen</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dick_Kaegel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dick Kaegel (page does not exist)">Dick Kaegel</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_O%27Connell_(journalist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jack O'Connell (journalist) (page does not exist)">Jack O'Connell</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Van_Dyck&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dave Van Dyck (page does not exist)">Dave Van Dyck</a>. They were charged with determining whether Santo would be elected to the National Baseball Hall of Fame's Class of 2012. Williams, Santo's long-time teammate and friend, had made a fresh case for Santo, emphasizing his personal struggle with diabetes during his career, and his post-retirement charitable work to try to find a cure. Santo received 15 of the 16 possible votes and was the only one of the ten Golden Era Ballot candidates to be elected to the Hall of Fame by the Committee's first vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_Elected_to_Hall_of_Fame_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_Elected_to_Hall_of_Fame-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SomewhereACub_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-SomewhereACub-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Santo's widow Vicki accepted the plaque on Induction Day, and spoke about his love of the Cubs and his devotion to sufferers of diabetes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Santo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Banks-Santo_retired_numbers.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="636" data-file-width="848" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Banks-Santo_retired_numbers.jpg/220px-Banks-Santo_retired_numbers.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Banks-Santo_retired_numbers.jpg/330px-Banks-Santo_retired_numbers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Banks-Santo_retired_numbers.jpg/440px-Banks-Santo_retired_numbers.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Santo led the league in double plays six times (1961, 1964, 1966–68, 1971), tying the major league record held by <a href="/wiki/Heinie_Groh" title="Heinie Groh">Heinie Groh</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame-6">[6]</a></sup> Mike Schmidt also later tied this record. He led the National League in total chances every season from 1961 through 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame-6">[6]</a></sup> He appeared at third base in every Cubs game from April 19, <a href="/wiki/1964_Chicago_Cubs_season" title="1964 Chicago Cubs season">1964</a> through May 31, <a href="/wiki/1966_Chicago_Cubs_season" title="1966 Chicago Cubs season">1966</a>, establishing a league record with 364 consecutive games at the position;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Page_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Page-12">[12]</a></sup> his 164 games at third base in <a href="/wiki/1965_Chicago_Cubs_season" title="1965 Chicago Cubs season">1965</a> remain the major league record.</p>
<p>He won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Player_of_the_Month" title="Player of the Month">NL Player of the Month award</a> three times: June 1963 (.384, 6 HR, 22 RBI); July 1964 (.395, 7 HR, 27 RBI); and June 1969 (.400, 6 HR, 34 RBI).</p>
<p>Santo broke Eddie Mathews' NL record of 369 career double plays at third base in 1972, and in 1973 he broke Mathews' league records of 4,284 assists and 6,606 total chances. Schmidt passed Santo's record for double plays in 1986, his record for assists in 1987, and his mark for total chances in 1988. During his 14-season run with the Cubs, Santo hit 337 home runs, then the eighth most by a NL <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right-handedness" title="Right-handedness">right-handed</a> hitter; his 1,071 career walks with the Cubs remain the team record for a right-handed hitter. He was the first third baseman to hit 300 home runs and win five Gold Gloves, a feat since matched only by Schmidt and <a href="/wiki/Scott_Rolen" title="Scott Rolen">Scott Rolen</a>.</p>
<p>Santo became the first player in major league history to wear a <a href="/wiki/Batting_helmet" title="Batting helmet">batting helmet</a> with protective ear flaps, when in 1966, in the midst of trying to break the Cubs' modern consecutive-game <a href="/wiki/Hitting_streak" title="Hitting streak">hitting streak</a> record of 27 games (set by <a href="/wiki/Hack_Wilson" title="Hack Wilson">Hack Wilson</a> in 1929), Santo was sidelined for nearly two weeks following a pitch thrown by the Mets' <a href="/wiki/Jack_Fisher" title="Jack Fisher">Jack Fisher</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Brushback_pitch" title="Brushback pitch">beaning</a> <a href="/wiki/Bone_fracture" title="Bone fracture">fractured</a> his cheekbone and ended his consecutive playing streak. When he returned (and broke the hitting record with a 28-game streak<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Page_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ron_Santo_at_the_Baseball_Page-12">[12]</a></sup>) he was wearing an improvised ear flap on his batting helmet in order to protect the injury; ear flaps have since become standard equipment on batting helmets.</p>
<p>In 1999, he was named to the Cubs All-Century Team.</p>

<p>On September 28, 2003, Santo's jersey No. 10 was retired by the Cubs organization, making him the third player so honored behind his teammates Ernie Banks (#14) and Billy Williams (#26).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Other prominent Cubs had worn No. 10 after Santo's retirement, notably <a href="/wiki/Dave_Kingman" title="Dave Kingman">Dave Kingman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leon_Durham" title="Leon Durham">Leon Durham</a>; the most recent wearer had been interim manager <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Kimm" title="Bruce Kimm">Bruce Kimm</a>, just the previous year. In April 2004, Santo was inducted into the inaugural class of the Washington Interscholastic Activities Association (Washington's high school athletics league) Hall of Fame as a graduate of Seattle's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franklin_High_School_(Seattle,_Washington)" title="Franklin High School (Seattle, Washington)">Franklin High School</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIAAHof_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-WIAAHof-55">[55]</a></sup> About a month after Santo's death, Cubs Chairman Tom Ricketts announced that Santo would be honored by the Cubs in the <a href="/wiki/2011_Chicago_Cubs_season" title="2011 Chicago Cubs season">2011 season</a>. From spring training through the end of the season, the Cubs wore a patch on the sleeve of their jersey with the number 10 on it.</p>
