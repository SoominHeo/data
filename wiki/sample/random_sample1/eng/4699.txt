<p><b>Roller derby</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Contact_sport" title="Contact sport">contact sport</a> played by two teams of five members <a href="/wiki/Roller_skating" title="Roller skating">roller skating</a> in the same direction (counter-clockwise) around a track. Game play consists of a series of short match ups (jams) in which both teams designate a jammer who scores points by lapping members of the opposing team. The teams attempt to hinder the opposing jammer while assisting their own jammerâin effect, playing both <a href="/wiki/Offense_(sports)" title="Offense (sports)">offense</a> and <a href="/wiki/Defense_(sports)" title="Defense (sports)">defense</a> simultaneously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Roller derby is played by approximately 1,250 <a href="/wiki/Amateur" title="Amateur">amateur</a> leagues worldwide, nearly half of them outside the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the sport has its origins in the banked-track roller skating marathons of the 1930s, <a href="/wiki/Leo_Seltzer" title="Leo Seltzer">Leo Seltzer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Damon_Runyon" title="Damon Runyon">Damon Runyon</a> are credited with the basic evolution of the sport to its initial competitive form. Professional roller derby quickly became popular; in 1940, more than five million spectators watched in about 50 American cities. In the ensuing decades, however, it predominantly became a form of <a href="/wiki/Sports_entertainment" title="Sports entertainment">sports entertainment</a> where the theatrical elements overshadowed the athleticism. This gratuitous showmanship largely ended with the sport's contemporary grassroots revival in the first decade of the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Although some sports entertainment qualities such as player pseudonyms and colorful uniforms were retained, scripted bouts with predetermined winners were abandoned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern roller derby is an international sport dominated by all-female amateur teams, in addition to a growing number of male, unisex, and <a href="/wiki/Junior_roller_derby" title="Junior roller derby">junior roller derby</a> teams, and was (as a <a href="/wiki/Roller_sport" title="Roller sport">roller sport</a>) under consideration for the <a href="/wiki/2020_Summer_Olympics" title="2020 Summer Olympics">2020 Summer Olympics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Most modern leagues (their back-office volunteers included) share a strong <a href="/wiki/DIY_ethic" title="DIY ethic">"do it yourself" ethic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> which combines athleticism and elements from <a href="/wiki/Camp_(style)" title="Camp (style)">camp</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> As of 2016, the <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Flat_Track_Derby_Association" title="Women's Flat Track Derby Association">Women's Flat Track Derby Association</a> (WFTDA), had 397 full member leagues and 48 Apprentice Leagues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDA_Member_Leagues_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDA_Member_Leagues-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>Contemporary roller derby has a basic set of rules, with variations reflecting the interests of a <a href="#Associations_and_governing_bodies">governing body</a>'s member leagues. The summary below is based on a comprehensive rule set developed by the Women's Flat Track Derby Association (WFTDA),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup> which is used by the vast majority of leagues. In March 2010, <a href="/wiki/Derby_News_Network" title="Derby News Network">Derby News Network</a> claimed that more than 98% of roller derby competitions were conducted under WFTDA rules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> For example, members of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Roller_Derby_Association" title="United Kingdom Roller Derby Association">United Kingdom Roller Derby Association</a> are required to play by WFTDA rules,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> while members of the Canadian Women's Roller Derby Association are encouraged to join the WFTDA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Thus, the rules described below are WFTDA rules.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basics_of_play">Basics of play</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roller_derby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Basics of play">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Roller derby is played by two teams of up to fourteen players, who both field up to five members for each two-minute jam, simultaneously skating counterclockwise on a circuit track. Each team designates a scoring player (the "jammer"); the other four members are "blockers." One blocker can be designated as a "pivot"âa special blocker who is allowed to become a jammer in the course of play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:7</sup> The jammer wears a helmet cover bearing two stars; the pivot wears a striped cover; the remaining members' helmets are uncovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:7</sup></p>
<p>The bout is played in two periods of 30 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:4</sup> Point scoring occurs during "jams": plays that last up to two minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:5</sup> During a jam, points are scored when a jammer on a scoring pass (every pass a jammer makes through the pack after the initial pass) laps members of the opposing team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:33</sup> Each team's blockers use body contact, changing positions, and other tactics to assist its jammer to score while hindering the opposing team's jammer. Certain types of blocks and other play are violations; referees call penalties and require violators to serve time in a penalty box.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:29</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jams">Jams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roller_derby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Jams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TwoNorthWestJammers.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="1200" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/TwoNorthWestJammers.jpg/220px-TwoNorthWestJammers.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/TwoNorthWestJammers.jpg/330px-TwoNorthWestJammers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/TwoNorthWestJammers.jpg/440px-TwoNorthWestJammers.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Two jammers (from the <a href="/wiki/Oly_Rollers" title="Oly Rollers">Oly Rollers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rainy_City_Roller_Dolls" title="Rainy City Roller Dolls">Rainy City</a>) race from the jammer line</div>

<p>Play begins by blockers lining up on the track anywhere between the rear "jammer line" and the front "pivot line". The jammers start behind the "jammer line," a second starting line 30 feet behind the pivot line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:12</sup> Jams begin on a single short whistle blast,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-static.wftda.com_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-static.wftda.com-16">[16]</a></sup> upon which both jammers and blockers may begin engaging immediately.</p>
<p>The pack is the largest single group of blockers containing members of both teams skating in proximity, arranged such that each are within 10 feet of the next.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:11</sup> While blockers are required to maintain the pack, they are permitted to skate freely within the area of the track beginning 20 feet behind the pack and ending 20 feet ahead of it, an area known as the "engagement zone."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-9"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:13</sup> The first jammer to exit the front of the pack, having legally passed all blockers at least once, earns the status of "lead jammer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-10"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:7</sup> Once earned, lead jammer status cannot be transferred to other skaters, but certain actions (notably, being sent to the penalty box) can cause it to be lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-11"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:8</sup> After the initial pass through the pack, each jammer scores a point every time they lap any member of the opposing team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-12"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:33</sup></p>
<p>The lead jammer can stop the jam at any time by repeatedly placing both hands on their hips.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-13"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:</sup> If the jam is not stopped early, it ends after 2 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-14"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:5</sup> If time remains in the period, teams then have 30 seconds to get on the track and line up for the next jam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-15"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:5</sup> Team members typically rotate between jams from the 14 players on the team's roster. Designations may change between jams: a pivot in one jam might be the jammer in a later jam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-16"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:7</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scoring">Scoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roller_derby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The jammer scores by passing opposition team members. Each jammer must first complete a non-scoring pass through the pack, which determines which jammer (if any) is the lead jammer. After this, the jammer scores a point each time they lap an opposition team member, including the other team's jammer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-19"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:33</sup> A five-point scoring pass is commonly called a "grand slam."</p>

<p>Roller derby athletes may attempt to knock their opponents out of bounds or impede their movements by blocking (actions which are not solely within the prerogative of the official blockers). Legal blocks follow certain rules. Contact by hands, elbows, head, and feet are prohibited, as is contact above the shoulders or below mid-thigh. Furthermore, contact may not be from the rear, only from a player's front or sides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-20"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:14</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Penalties">Penalties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roller_derby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Penalties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Referees determine penalties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-21"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:36</sup> A player receiving a penalty is removed from play to sit in a penalty box for 30 seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-22"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:29</sup> If a jam ends beforehand, the player remains in the penalty box during the subsequent jam until the penalty is completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-23"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:30</sup> Jammers are released from the penalty box earlier if the second jammer is sent to the box while the first is still seated. The second jammer's penalty is then only as long as the amount of time the first jammer spent in the box.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-24"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:31</sup> A player "fouls out" of the game upon the seventh time they are sent to the penalty box, and is required to return to the locker room.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-25"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:32</sup></p>

<p>Players skate on four-wheeled ("quad") roller skates,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-26"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:11</sup> and are required to wear protective equipment, including a helmet, wrist guards, elbow pads, knee pads, and mouth guards. All currently-played sets of roller derby rules explicitly forbid <a href="/wiki/Inline_skates" title="Inline skates">inline skates</a> for players (WFTDA and MRDA permit inline skates for referees while USARS requires quad skates for all skaters; in practice virtually all skaters, referee and player alike, wear quad skates) Additional gear that is acceptable though subject to individual team rules include padded knee length pants, similar to what aggressive skateboarders wear, and biologically specific gear such as a hard case sports bra for female players and protective cups for males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-27"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:39</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategy_and_tactics">Strategy and tactics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roller_derby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Strategy and tactics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Roller derby is a game where offense and defense are played simultaneously,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> a highly volatile aspect which considerably complicates strategy and tactics. Blockers, for example, may create a large hole for their jammer to pass through and score, but this same maneuver might also allow the opposing team's jammer to score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vodoo1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-vodoo1-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Ending the Jam</b>: The lead jammer can "call off" or end the jam at any time, thus they control the opposing team's ability to score points. Ideally, the lead jammer will attempt to score as many points as possible, and end the jam before the opposing team scores any.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Passing the Star</b>: The jammer for a team may "pass the star" to the pivotâthat is, they hand the star helmet cover to the pivot, at which point the pivot becomes the scoring jammer for the team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-28"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:9</sup> A jammer might pass the star because of fatigue, injury, penalty trouble, or because the pivot is in a better position to score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Passing the star is also colloquially referred to as "passing the panty," as helmet covers are sometimes known as "panties."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>The Whip</b>: A blocker or pivot grasps her jammer's hand and swings her forward, thus transferring some of her speed and momentum to the jammer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Roller_Derby_(22).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1274" data-file-width="2264" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Roller_Derby_%2822%29.jpg/220px-Roller_Derby_%2822%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Roller_Derby_%2822%29.jpg/330px-Roller_Derby_%2822%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Roller_Derby_%2822%29.jpg/440px-Roller_Derby_%2822%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Roller_Derby_(22).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Two <a href="/wiki/Richter_City_Roller_Derby" title="Richter City Roller Derby">Wellington</a> skaters (in purple) form a wall, limiting their opponents' movement, while their jammer (leftmost skater in purple) takes a hip whip to accelerate past the pack</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Walling Up</b>: A basic "wall" consists of two blockers working together to take up as much space on the track as possible to make it difficult for the opposing team to maneuver. "Walling up" refers to creating a wall formation at a strategic time, so the opposing team (especially the jammer) has no time to respond. A wall can inhibit, slow down, and ultimately trap the opposing jammer, and may last an entire jam if they can effectively hold the opposing team. Variations on the strategy include backwards bracing (one skater on the disengaged side of the wall skates backward to keep a pair of eyes on the jammer while the remaining skaters act as a wall), the box wall (associated with <a href="/wiki/Victorian_Roller_Derby_League" title="Victorian Roller Derby League">Victorian Roller Derby League</a>), and setting up blockers to rotate through the wall positions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Goating</b>: The pack is defined as <i>the largest group of in bounds Blockers, skating in proximity, containing members from both teams.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-29"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:11</sup> In the "goatherding" tactic, one team surrounds a blocker of the opposing team (usually the strongest or best blocker), and then slows so that that group becomes the pack. The opposing team, skating ahead, are thus put out of play and thus cannot legally block the goatherders' jammer.</li>
<li><b>Bridging</b>: blockers move behind the main pack by up to 10 feet to stretch out the pack. This also stretches out the engagement zone, allow their team members to continue hindering the opposition jammer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Running back</b> or <b>recycling</b>: a skater who bumps an opposition skater off the track then skates backwards (in clockwise direction), forcing the opposition to re-enter further behind the pack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-western-style_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-western-style-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Officials">Officials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roller_derby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Officials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Regulation WFTDA bouts are officiated by three to seven skating officials<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-30"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:35</sup> and a multitude of non-skating officials (NSOs). Due to the volunteer nature of the sport, many officiating positions are optional. Skating officials are usually referred to as referees and non-skating officials are called NSOs for short. Referees skate on the inside and outside of the track. They are in charge of calling penalties, awarding points, and most importantly ensuring safe game play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-31"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:36</sup> Referees must be on skates (inline skates are allowed) and typically wear white and black stripes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-32"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:38</sup> NSOs take up a range of positions inside and outside of the track, and are responsible for starting and timing the jams, recording and displaying scores and penalties communicated by referees, recording the number of each skater on track for a given jam, and timing and recording skaters in the penalty box.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roller_derby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_endurance_races">Professional endurance races</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roller_derby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Professional endurance races">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_roller_derby" title="History of roller derby">History of roller derby</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Roller_Derby_1950.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2134" data-file-width="2734" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Roller_Derby_1950.jpg/220px-Roller_Derby_1950.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Roller_Derby_1950.jpg/330px-Roller_Derby_1950.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Roller_Derby_1950.jpg/440px-Roller_Derby_1950.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Roller_Derby_1950.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Two women's league roller derby skaters leap over two who have fallen in a 1950 bout in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a></div>

</div>
<p>The growing popularity of <a href="/wiki/Roller_skating" title="Roller skating">roller skating</a> in the United States led to the formation of organized multi-day endurance races for cash prizes, as early as the mid-1880s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chicago-tribune_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-chicago-tribune-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-1885_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-1885-32">[32]</a></sup> Speed and endurance races continued to be held on both flat and banked tracks in the century's first three decades<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chicagi-Daily-1908_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chicagi-Daily-1908-33">[33]</a></sup> and spectators enjoyed the spills and falls of the skaters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-52-1914_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-52-1914-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The term <i>derby</i> was used to refer to such races by 1922.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDT-4-24_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDT-4-24-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[note 1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_to_contact_sport">Evolution to contact sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roller_derby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Evolution to contact sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The endurance races began to transform into the contemporary form of the sport in the mid-1930s, when promoter <a href="/wiki/Leo_Seltzer" title="Leo Seltzer">Leo Seltzer</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[note 2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[note 3]</a></sup> created the Transcontinental Roller Derby, a month-long simulation of a road race between two-person teams of professional skaters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rasmussen_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-rasmussen-42">[39]</a></sup> The spectacle became a popular touring exhibition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_feb1936_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-time_feb1936-43">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_variations_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-time_variations-44">[41]</a></sup> In the late 1930s, sportswriter <a href="/wiki/Damon_Runyon" title="Damon Runyon">Damon Runyon</a> persuaded Seltzer to change the Roller Derby rules to increase skater contact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rasmussen_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-rasmussen-42">[39]</a></sup> By 1939, after experimenting with different team and scoring arrangements, Seltzer's created a touring company of four pairs of teams (always billed as the local "home" team versus either New York or Chicago),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deford_84_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-deford_84-45">[42]</a></sup> with two five-person teams on the track at once, scoring points when its members lapped opponents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NMRS_2004_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-NMRS_2004-46">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roller_derby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1948, Roller Derby debuted on New York <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a>âbroadcasting well before television viewership was widespread.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dedford-89_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-dedford-89-47">[44]</a></sup> The broadcasts increased spectator turnout for live matches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rushin_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-rushin-48">[45]</a></sup> For the 1949â1950 season, Seltzer formed the <a href="/wiki/National_Roller_Derby_League" title="National Roller Derby League">National Roller Derby League</a> (NRDL).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dedford-95_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-dedford-95-49">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KTVU_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-KTVU-50">[47]</a></sup> The NRDL consisted of six teams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dedford-95_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-dedford-95-49">[46]</a></sup> NRDL season playoffs sold out Madison Square Garden for a week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KTVU_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-KTVU-50">[47]</a></sup> During the late 1950s and 1960s, the sport was broadcast on several networks, but attendance declined. <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Seltzer" title="Jerry Seltzer">Jerry Seltzer</a> (Leo's son), the <i>RollerDerby</i> "commissioner", hoped to use television to expand the live spectator base. He adapted the sport for television by developing scripted story lines, and rules designed to improve television appeal; derby's popularity declined in spite of this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dedford-no-page_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-dedford-no-page-51">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>1989 saw the debut of <i><a href="/wiki/RollerGames" title="RollerGames">RollerGames</a></i>, a show which presented an even more theatrical variant of roller derby for national audiences, which ran one season for some of its distributors for syndication went bankrupt. It used a figure-8 track and different rules adapted for this track. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Griffiths,_Sr.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bill Griffiths, Sr. (page does not exist)">Bill Griffiths, Sr.</a> served as commissioner while his son, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Griffiths,_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bill Griffiths, Jr. (page does not exist)">Bill Griffiths, Jr.</a> served as manager for the L.A. T-Birds, who (according to the storylines) were seeking revenge on the Violators (led by Skull) for cheating in the Commissioner's Cup and stealing it from them when the Skull interfered with gameplay. The other teams included the Maniancs (led by Guru Drew), Bad Attitude (led by Ms. Georgia Hase), the Rockers (led by DJ Terringo and consisting of skaters that were also professional rock and roll musicians), and Hot Flash (led by Juan Valdez Lopez).</p>
<p>In 1999, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spike_TV" title="Spike TV">Spike TV</a> (<a href="/wiki/The_Nashville_Network" title="The Nashville Network">TNN</a> at the time) debuted <a href="/wiki/RollerJam" title="RollerJam">RollerJam</a>, which used exactly the same rules on just a banked oval track as classic roller derby, but allowed for inline skates to be used (although some skaters went with traditional quad skates). Jerry Seltzer was commissioner for this version.</p>

<p>Roller derby began its modern revival in the early 2000s as an all-female, woman-organized amateur sport. The revival initially began in <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin, Texas</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ddan_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-ddan-52">[49]</a></sup> and by August 2006 there were over 135 similar leagues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt200811_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt200811-53">[50]</a></sup> Leagues outside the U.S. also began forming in 2006, and international competition soon followed. There are over 2,000 amateur leagues worldwide<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flattrackstats_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-flattrackstats-54">[51]</a></sup> in countries including, but not limited to, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[52]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[53]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[54]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[55]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[56]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[59]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[61]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[64]</a></sup> (where, like many other international leagues, all of the gear and equipment must be imported).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[65]</a></sup> Roller derby's contemporary resurgence has been regarded as an aspect of <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a> which demonstrates "the speed with which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop_culture" title="Pop culture">pop culture</a> is now transported by highly mobile <a href="/wiki/Expatriate" title="Expatriate">expatriates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a>, while also highlighting the changing role of women in many societies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A large number of contemporary roller derby leagues are amateur, self-organized and all-female <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[66]</a></sup> and were formed in a DIY spirit by relatively new roller derby enthusiasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[67]</a></sup> In many leagues (particularly but not exclusively in the U.S.), a <a href="/wiki/Punk_subculture" title="Punk subculture">punk</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[69]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetic</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Third-wave_feminism" title="Third-wave feminism">third-wave feminist</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[70]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethic" title="Ethic">ethic</a> is prominent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[71]</a></sup> Furthermore, roller derby teams are typically composed of members of various social strata such as stay-at-home mothers, lawyers, and nurses,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[72]</a></sup> and "Being gay/straight/bi/trans is simply no big deal, as long as you can skate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[73]</a></sup> Members of fledgling leagues often practice and strategize together, regardless of team affiliation, between bouts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[74]</a></sup> Most compete on flat tracks, though several leagues skate on banked tracks, with more in the planning stages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>Each league typically features local teams in public bouts which are popular with a diverse fan base;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[77]</a></sup> larger venues hosting audiences ranging from 4,000 to 7,000 are no longer unusual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[78]</a></sup> Many leagues took advantage of the release of the roller derby <a href="/wiki/Feature_film" title="Feature film">feature film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Whip_It_(film)" title="Whip It (film)">Whip It</a></i> (2009), to increase awareness of the sport<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[79]</a></sup> (which has also received <a href="/wiki/Cin%C3%A9ma_v%C3%A9rit%C3%A9" title="CinÃ©ma vÃ©ritÃ©">cinÃ©ma vÃ©ritÃ©</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[80]</a></sup> and high definition <a href="/wiki/Digital_movie_camera" title="Digital movie camera">digital movie camera</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[81]</a></sup> treatments).</p>
<p>As the sport has matured, successful local leagues have formed "travel teams" composed of the league's best players to compete with travel teams from other cities and regions. Furthermore, corporate advertising has used roller derby themes in television commercials for <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[82]</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Breakfast_cereal" title="Breakfast cereal">breakfast cereal</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[83]</a></sup> and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Over-the-counter" title="Over-the-counter">over-the-counter</a> <a href="/wiki/Analgesic" title="Analgesic">analgesic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[84]</a></sup> At the 123rd <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> session in South Africa in February 2012, it was announced that roller derby was one of the eight sports under consideration for inclusion in the 2020 Olympic Games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[86]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_concerns">Safety concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roller_derby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Safety concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Since roller derby is a contact sport, there is a risk of injury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[95]</a></sup> Injuries range from common bruises and sprains to broken bones and <a href="/wiki/Concussion" title="Concussion">concussions</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[97]</a></sup> and beyond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[99]</a></sup> As is the case with many sporting events and other large public gatherings, modern roller derby games are required to be played with appropriate medical professionals on site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDARules_12-45"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDARules-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:39</sup> Some leagues prominently display their injuries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[100]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[101]</a></sup> and safety and injuries are a perennial topic on skating blogs and other forums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[102]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[103]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[104]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion">Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roller_derby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although the early 2000s revival of roller derby was initially all-female, some leagues later introduced all-male teams and unisex games. Furthermore, as of May 2013 there were over 140 <a href="/wiki/Junior_roller_derby" title="Junior roller derby">junior roller derby</a> programs in the United States, and many more around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[105]</a></sup> As the sport of roller derby expands, so does the media devoted to the sport. Viewing roller derby bouts is now possible via live online streaming and archived footage of previous bouts and tournaments. The WFTDA offers live streaming video of its tournaments at wftda.tv,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[106]</a></sup> and WFTDA membership is a major goal of aspiring leagues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[107]</a></sup> The Derby News Network offers live streaming video and archived video of many more roller derby bouts, both WFTDA events and non-WFTDA events at www.derbynewsnetwork.com/live.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNN_video_111-0"><a href="#cite_note-DNN_video-111">[108]</a></sup></p>
<p>FiveOnFive magazine is devoted solely to covering the sport of roller derby, and covers diverse topics regarding the sport such as business, training, junior roller derby, and nutrition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FiveOnFive_Magazine_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-FiveOnFive_Magazine-112">[109]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance_and_organization">Governance and organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roller_derby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Governance and organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_roller_derby_associations" title="List of roller derby associations">List of roller derby associations</a></div>
<p>The largest governing body for the sport is the <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Flat_Track_Derby_Association" title="Women's Flat Track Derby Association">Women's Flat Track Derby Association</a>, with 397 Full Member Leagues, and 48 Apprentice Leagues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WFTDA_Member_Leagues_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-WFTDA_Member_Leagues-11">[11]</a></sup> Other associations support either coed or men-only derby; among them is the largest organization supporting the growing trend of male roller derby, <a href="/wiki/Men%27s_Roller_Derby_Association" title="Men's Roller Derby Association">MRDA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-article1_113-0"><a href="#cite_note-article1-113">[110]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-article2_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-article2-114">[111]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-article3_115-0"><a href="#cite_note-article3-115">[112]</a></sup> Within the United States, roller derby played by those under 18 is governed by the Junior Roller Derby Association, which uses the widely used rule set of the WFTDA, with modifications for players who are minors (such as no hitting, or accelerating into a block).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Junior_Roller_Derby_Association_116-0"><a href="#cite_note-Junior_Roller_Derby_Association-116">[113]</a></sup> Outside of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> many roller derby leagues enjoy support from their national skate federations such as the <a href="/wiki/Skate_Australia" title="Skate Australia">Skate Australia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skate-australia_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-skate-australia-117">[114]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/British_Roller_Sports_Federation" title="British Roller Sports Federation">British Roller Sports Federation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[115]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Roller_Sports_Canada" title="Roller Sports Canada">Roller Sports Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skate-Canada_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-skate-Canada-119">[116]</a></sup> In Europe, roller derby otherwise received recognition as a legitimate sport by the Federation Internationale de Roller Sports (FIRS) in Paris in 2010; this organization reports directly to the International Olympic Committee. Although affiliation with a <a href="/wiki/USA_Roller_Sports" title="USA Roller Sports">national organization</a> has been declined by American leagues who prefer governance on a grass roots level,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rejected_120-0"><a href="#cite_note-rejected-120">[117]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[118]</a></sup> the WFTDA and USARS maintain a reciprocity agreement for insurance purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reciprocity-agreement_122-0"><a href="#cite_note-reciprocity-agreement-122">[119]</a></sup> Canada's national roller derby league works with the American federation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rdaf-can_123-0"><a href="#cite_note-rdaf-can-123">[120]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tournaments">Tournaments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roller_derby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Tournaments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 2006, the WFTDA has sponsored an annual championship. In 2008 this first took the modern form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WFTDA_Championship" title="WFTDA Championship">the "Big 5"</a>: four regional playoffs, and a final championship tournament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[121]</a></sup> The association also officially recognizes eligible tournaments hosted by member leagues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[122]</a></sup> E.g., the annual SpudTown Knockdown, which is held in <a href="/wiki/Boise,_Idaho" title="Boise, Idaho">Boise, Idaho</a> beforehand, is "the Best of the Rest of the West." The first <a href="/wiki/Roller_Derby_World_Cup" title="Roller Derby World Cup">Roller Derby World Cup</a>, an international competition, took place in Toronto, Canada, in December 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[123]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/2014_Roller_Derby_World_Cup" title="2014 Roller Derby World Cup">second World Cup</a> took place in Dallas, Texas, in December 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[124]</a></sup></p>
