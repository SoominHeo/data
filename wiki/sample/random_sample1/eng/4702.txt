<p>The <b>roloway monkey</b> (<i>Cercopithecus roloway</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered</a> species of <a href="/wiki/Old_World_monkey" title="Old World monkey">Old World monkey</a> found in a small area of eastern <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a> and the forests of <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>, between the <a href="/wiki/Sassandra_River" title="Sassandra River">Sassandra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pra_River_(Ghana)" title="Pra River (Ghana)">Pra Rivers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msw3_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-msw3-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup> Traditionally, it has been considered a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Diana_monkey" title="Diana monkey">Diana monkey</a> (<i>C. diana</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msw3_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-msw3-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roloway_monkey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The roloway monkey is similar to other species of <a href="/wiki/Guenon" title="Guenon">guenons</a> but is distinguished by its lengthy beard and broader <a href="/wiki/Diadem" title="Diadem">diadem</a>-like browband.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB-3">[3]</a></sup> Like its closest relative the <a href="/wiki/Diana_monkey" title="Diana monkey">Diana monkey</a> (<i>C. diana</i>), its face and much of its fur are black. It has a white beard, chest, and throat; there are a white stripe along each thigh and a deep reddish or orange patch on its back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ADW-4">[4]</a></sup> On the inside of the thighs, the fur is whitish, yellowish, or reddish. The body length ranges from 40 to 55 centimetres, with a tail of 50 to 75 cm. Its weight is between 4 and 7 kilograms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ADW-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roloway_monkey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Roloway monkeys consume a diverse array of varying insects, fruit, seeds, and flowers. They can feed on the plant parts of roughly 130 species of trees, climbers, and <a href="/wiki/Epiphyte" title="Epiphyte">epiphytes</a>. Like many omnivores, roloway monkeys also consume mature fruit pulp, arthropods, oil-rich seeds- and young leaves. Their food sources usually consist of twigs and small supports in the terminal branches of trees in their infancy and within large woody climbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_habits">Social habits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roloway_monkey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Social habits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Roloway monkeys dwell in the canopies of jungles and rainforests where they reside and sleep in the branches of primeval trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-ADW-4">[4]</a></sup> They are typically diurnal and sleep throughout the West African nights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-ADW-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The species is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arboreal" title="Arboreal">arboreal</a>, and forms <a href="/wiki/Social_group" title="Social group">social groups</a> <a href="/wiki/Group_size_measures" title="Group size measures">of</a> 15 to 30 individuals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-ADW-4">[4]</a></sup> typically with 1 male,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-ADW-4">[4]</a></sup> around 10 females, and their children. It is commonplace for the males of groups of roloway monkeys to head off elsewhere on their own, whereas the females will stay with the same group they were born into.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-ADW-4">[4]</a></sup> This makes it harder for breeding to continue to be as fluent as it was once before, especially given the depreciation of forest areas in Ghana that suits this breed of monkey. They give birth typically to one monkey at a time, with a period of around 5 months required for each baby to be conceived and then born.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-ADW-4">[4]</a></sup> The life span of a roloway monkey is about 20 years in the wild,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-ADW-4">[4]</a></sup> and those in captivity can stay alive for more than 30 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-ADW-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Endangered_species">Endangered species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roloway_monkey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Endangered species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The roloway monkey is among the most threatened <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primates</a> on the African continent, although exact figures for the species are not available. Recent surveys could not find evidence of it in Ghana's <a href="/wiki/Bia_National_Park" title="Bia National Park">Bia National Park</a>, where it was probably eliminated between the mid-1970s and 1990. There are estimates are that there probably has been a population decline of at least 80% over the last three <a href="/wiki/Generation" title="Generation">generations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup> Roloway monkeys are hunted by a multitude of predators including crowned hawk-eagles, leopards, chimpanzees and, most notoriously, humans. Humans hunt roloway monkeys and other primate species like them for their <a href="/wiki/Bushmeat" title="Bushmeat">bushmeat</a> which is a delicacy in many West African cultures. The main threat facing roloway monkeys is hunting for the bushmeat trade. Over 800 tons of bushmeat is sold in Ghana's markets every year. The roloway monkeys’ conspicuous colours and loud calls make them very susceptible to hunting. Their habitat is also becoming increasingly fragmented due to a decline in forest habitats and deforestation as human settlements expand and farming increases. In the past 100 years, Ghana has lost 80% of its forested lands. The species is listed as one of "<a href="/wiki/The_World%27s_25_Most_Endangered_Primates" title="The World's 25 Most Endangered Primates">The World's 25 Most Endangered Primates</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2008-2010Top25_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-2008-2010Top25-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
