<p><b>Raleigh–Durham International Airport</b> (<a href="/wiki/International_Air_Transport_Association_airport_code" title="International Air Transport Association airport code">IATA</a>: <b>RDU</b>, <a href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization_airport_code" title="International Civil Aviation Organization airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>KRDU</b>, <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">FAA</a> <a href="/wiki/Location_identifier" title="Location identifier">LID</a>: <b>RDU</b>) is the main airport serving <a href="/wiki/Raleigh,_North_Carolina" title="Raleigh, North Carolina">Raleigh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Durham,_North_Carolina" title="Durham, North Carolina">Durham</a> and the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Research_Triangle" title="Research Triangle">Research Triangle</a> region of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>. It is located 4.5 miles (7 km) northeast of the town of <a href="/wiki/Morrisville,_North_Carolina" title="Morrisville, North Carolina">Morrisville</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wake_County,_North_Carolina" title="Wake County, North Carolina">Wake County</a>. The airport covers 5,000 acres (2,000 ha) and has three <a href="/wiki/Runway" title="Runway">runways</a> and has passenger service to 41 destinations with 126 average daily departures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2015, 10,000,000 passengers traveled through the airport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The RDU Airport Authority is in charge of the airport facilities and its operations. The Airport Authority is controlled by a board of representatives from the counties of Wake and <a href="/wiki/Durham_County,_North_Carolina" title="Durham County, North Carolina">Durham</a> and the cities of Raleigh and Durham.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raleigh%E2%80%93Durham_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding">Founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raleigh%E2%80%93Durham_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RDU_hist.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="153" data-file-width="385" height="87" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/35/RDU_hist.jpg/220px-RDU_hist.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/35/RDU_hist.jpg/330px-RDU_hist.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/3/35/RDU_hist.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RDU_hist.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Early photo of Raleigh–Durham Airport</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The region's first airport opened in 1929 as Raleigh's Municipal Airport, south of town at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Raleigh%E2%80%93Durham_International_Airport&amp;params=35.735_N_-78.656_E_"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">35°44′06″N</span> <span class="longitude">78°39′22″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">35.735°N 78.656°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">35.735; -78.656</span></span></span></a></span>. It was quickly outgrown, and in 1939 the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_General_Assembly" title="North Carolina General Assembly">North Carolina General Assembly</a> chartered the Raleigh–Durham Aeronautical Authority to build and operate a larger airport between Raleigh and Durham. This was promoted by <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Air_Lines" title="Eastern Air Lines">Eastern Air Lines</a>, led by then chairman <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Rickenbacker" title="Eddie Rickenbacker">Eddie Rickenbacker</a> who wanted to make RDU a stop on the airline's New York–Miami route.</p>
<p>The new Raleigh–Durham Airport opened on May 1, 1943 with flights by Eastern Airlines. The passenger terminal was built from materials remaining after the construction of four barracks for the Army Air Forces Air Technical Service Command airfield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The three runways the airport had in 1951 are still visible on the southeast side of the airport: 4500-ft runway 5, 4500-ft runway 18 and 4490-ft runway 14.</p>
<p>After World War II <a href="/wiki/Capital_Airlines" title="Capital Airlines">Capital Airlines</a> joined Eastern at RDU; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piedmont_Airlines_(1948-1989)" title="Piedmont Airlines (1948-1989)">Piedmont Airlines</a> arrived in 1948. The April 1957 Official Airline Guide shows 36 departures a day: twenty Eastern, eight Capital and eight Piedmont. Nonstop flights did not reach beyond Washington, Atlanta, or the Appalachians (but Eastern started a Super Constellation nonstop to Newark in 1958). The next airline (aside from United's takeover of Capital in 1961) was <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a> in 1970. In April 1969 nonstops didn't reach beyond New York or Atlanta, and Chicago was the only nonstop west of the Appalachians.</p>
<p>In the 1970s, the last decade before airline deregulation, Piedmont connected RDU to Charlotte, Greensboro, New Bern, Norfolk, Richmond, Rocky Mount, Washington, Wilmington and Winston-Salem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> United flew to Asheville, Charlotte, Huntsville and Newark,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> while Eastern flew to Atlanta, Charlotte, Miami, New York, Philadelphia, Richmond and Washington,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and Delta flew to Chicago and Greensboro.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>After deregulation <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Airlines" title="Allegheny Airlines">Allegheny Airlines</a> arrived in 1979 and by 1985 <a href="/wiki/Trans_World_Airlines" title="Trans World Airlines">Trans World Airlines</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a>, Ozark, People Express, New York Air and Pan Am had all put in appearances.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hub_years">Hub years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raleigh%E2%80%93Durham_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hub years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a> built a terminal at RDU between 1985 and 1987 to house a new hub operation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airportbusiness_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-airportbusiness-10">[10]</a></sup> and flew to 38 cities when the hub started in June 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The December 1987 timetable shows AA nonstops to 36 airports and Eagle prop nonstops to 18 more. The RDU hub operated at a loss even during its heyday in the early 1990s, like the north-south hubs AA then had at <a href="/wiki/Nashville_International_Airport" title="Nashville International Airport">Nashville</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Jose_International_Airport" title="San Jose International Airport">San Jose</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hub's traffic peaked in 1991. Although the Raleigh/Durham area had growing local traffic, the connecting hub faced competition from Delta and Eastern in Atlanta and from USAir in Charlotte, as well as the short-lived Continental Airlines hub in Greensboro that opened in 1993. American began to consider closing the hub in late 1993; operations were reduced until June 1995 when American closed the hub, retaining flights to three AA hubs and London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Midway_Airlines_(1993%E2%80%932003)" title="Midway Airlines (1993–2003)">Midway Airlines</a> replaced AA as the airport's hub carrier from 1995 until 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1995, Midway had flights to Boston, Hartford, Long Island, Newark, Newburgh, New York, Philadelphia and Washington in the Northeast, and to Fort Lauderdale, Jacksonville, Orlando, Tampa and West Palm Beach in Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> American subleased its gates at RDU to Midway in order to repay $113 million in American-guaranteed bonds which had been used to construct the hub facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fins_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-fins-16">[16]</a></sup> Midway suspended service for some time after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_attacks" title="September 11, 2001 attacks">September 11, 2001 attacks</a>, ceased operations in 2002 and filed for bankruptcy in 2003.</p>

<p>American Airlines retained the daily nonstop flight from RDU to London, originally launched to <a href="/wiki/Gatwick_Airport" title="Gatwick Airport">Gatwick Airport</a> in May 1994 with a Boeing 767-200ER. The route continues to operate, partly due to the needs of the region's corporate travelers, particularly those of <a href="/wiki/GlaxoSmithKline" title="GlaxoSmithKline">GlaxoSmithKline</a> which has major bases of operation in west London near <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">Heathrow Airport</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Research_Triangle_Park" title="Research Triangle Park">Research Triangle Park</a> near RDU. On March 29, 2008, American shifted the RDU-London flight to <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">Heathrow Airport</a>. The RDU-London flight used three-class <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_777-200" title="Boeing 777-200">Boeing 777-200s</a> for several years and now is operated with a two-class <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_767-300ER" title="Boeing 767-300ER">Boeing 767-300ER</a>. From June to September 2015, American operated the 777-200 back to RDU temporarily, as the 767s were being refurbished with new interiors. From March 5, 2017, the <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">London</a> flight will see the return of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_777-200" title="Boeing 777-200">Boeing 777-200ER</a> permanently as demand for international travel increases and as the Boeing 767-300ERs previously serving the flight are being retired from service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-hub_years">Post-hub years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raleigh%E2%80%93Durham_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post-hub years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>RDU's post-hub years have brought the addition of several new carriers (notably <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a> and new destinations). Because of the economic downturn and high fuel prices in 2008, <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American</a> ended the flights to <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville_International_Airport" title="Jacksonville International Airport">Jacksonville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_International_Airport" title="Kansas City International Airport">Kansas City</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberty_International_Airport" title="Liberty International Airport">Newark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louisville_International_Airport" title="Louisville International Airport">Louisville</a>. Mainline flights to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austin-Bergstrom_International_Airport" title="Austin-Bergstrom International Airport">Austin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Columbus_International_Airport" title="Port Columbus International Airport">Columbus (OH)</a>, <a href="/wiki/LaGuardia_Airport" title="LaGuardia Airport">New York–LaGuardia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lambert-St._Louis_International_Airport" title="Lambert-St. Louis International Airport">St. Louis</a> were also dropped and service to other cities was reduced or downgraded. Other airlines cut flights and destinations also, including United's service to <a href="/wiki/Denver_International_Airport" title="Denver International Airport">Denver</a>, US Airways' services to <a href="/wiki/McCarran_International_Airport" title="McCarran International Airport">Las Vegas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Sky_Harbor_International_Airport" title="Phoenix Sky Harbor International Airport">Phoenix–Sky Harbor</a>. <a href="/wiki/ExpressJet" title="ExpressJet">ExpressJet</a> ended its independent flights to <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_International_Airport" title="Kansas City International Airport">Kansas City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louis_Armstrong_New_Orleans_International_Airport" title="Louis Armstrong New Orleans International Airport">New Orleans</a> (<a href="/wiki/ExpressJet" title="ExpressJet">ExpressJet</a> now only operates as United Express and Delta Connection). In 2008 RDU lost over 30 flights from the March 2008 schedule.</p>
<p>In 2010 RDU's traffic began to recover. In the first few months of the year passenger numbers stabilized at RDU, ending the decrease in 2008/2009. In the first four months of 2010, 2.7 million passengers traveled through RDU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rdu.com_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-rdu.com-18">[18]</a></sup> Growth was flat, compared to the same period a year before, but these signs were positive, indicating that the decline was over. Also, after cutting service, carriers at RDU began to add (or re-add) new service to the schedule. In April 2010 <a href="/wiki/Midwest_Airlines" title="Midwest Airlines">Midwest Airlines</a> resumed service from RDU, adding service to <a href="/wiki/General_Mitchell_International_Airport" title="General Mitchell International Airport">Milwaukee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> This route ended when Midwest was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Frontier_Airlines" title="Frontier Airlines">Frontier Airlines</a>. <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a>, which began service to RDU in June 1999,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> had also aggressively expanded at RDU, adding service to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lambert-St._Louis_International_Airport" title="Lambert-St. Louis International Airport">St. Louis</a> in May, and replacing <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a> as the largest carrier at RDU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JetBlue_Airways" title="JetBlue Airways">JetBlue Airways</a> also increased service to <a href="/wiki/Logan_International_Airport" title="Logan International Airport">Boston</a> in May 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a> has increased operations since 2010, with resumed nonstop service to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_International_Airport" title="Los Angeles International Airport">Los Angeles</a> in June 2010,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> followed by new nonstop service to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Columbus_International_Airport" title="Port Columbus International Airport">Columbus (OH)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Florida_International_Airport" title="Southwest Florida International Airport">Fort Myers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bradley_International_Airport" title="Bradley International Airport">Hartford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miami_International_Airport" title="Miami International Airport">Miami</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orlando_International_Airport" title="Orlando International Airport">Orlando</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore_Washington_International_Airport" title="Baltimore Washington International Airport">Baltimore</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lambert-St._Louis_International_Airport" title="Lambert-St. Louis International Airport">St. Louis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tampa_International_Airport" title="Tampa International Airport">Tampa</a>, and additional frequencies to <a href="/wiki/Logan_International_Airport" title="Logan International Airport">Boston</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minneapolis-Saint_Paul_International_Airport" title="Minneapolis-Saint Paul International Airport">Minneapolis-St. Paul</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_International_Airport" title="John F. Kennedy International Airport">New York (JFK)</a>, in November 2010, and new services to <a href="/wiki/Albany_International_Airport" title="Albany International Airport">Albany (NY)</a> and <a href="/wiki/T._F._Green_Airport" title="T. F. Green Airport">Providence</a> in April 2011.</p>

<p>In February 2011 Continental Airlines commenced its first international flight from RDU, to <a href="/wiki/Canc%C3%BAn_International_Airport" title="Cancún International Airport">Cancún</a>. Delta added three daily flights to Baltimore in September 2011. In November <a href="/wiki/Vision_Airlines" title="Vision Airlines">Vision Airlines</a> began operations at RDU and added a twice weekly flight to <a href="/wiki/Grand_Bahama_International_Airport" title="Grand Bahama International Airport">Freeport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This service was taken over by <a href="/wiki/Bahamasair" title="Bahamasair">Bahamasair</a> in May 2012. <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines" title="United Airlines">United Airlines</a> started the airport's first non-stop flight to <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_International_Airport" title="San Francisco International Airport">San Francisco</a> in August 2012. In December 2012 <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a> announced the airport's first daily non-stop flight to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_International_Airport" title="Los Angeles International Airport">Los Angeles</a>, beginning in April 2013. April 2013 marked the return of <a href="/wiki/Frontier_Airlines" title="Frontier Airlines">Frontier Airlines</a> with nonstop service to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trenton-Mercer_Airport" title="Trenton-Mercer Airport">Trenton, New Jersey</a>. Since then, Frontier has added non-stop service to <a href="/wiki/O%27Hare_International_Airport" title="O'Hare International Airport">Chicago–O'Hare</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleveland-Hopkins_International_Airport" title="Cleveland-Hopkins International Airport">Cleveland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Denver_International_Airport" title="Denver International Airport">Denver</a>. <a href="/wiki/Allegiant_Air" title="Allegiant Air">Allegiant Air</a> began their first non-stop services out of RDU to <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Sanford_International_Airport" title="Orlando Sanford International Airport">Orlando/Sanford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Punta_Gorda_Airport_(Florida)" title="Punta Gorda Airport (Florida)">Punta Gorda/Fort Myers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Petersburg-Clearwater_International_Airport" title="St. Petersburg-Clearwater International Airport">St. Petersburg/Clearwater</a> in May 2015. In September 2015, United resumed service to Denver at twice a day using 76-seat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embraer_175" title="Embraer 175">Embraer 175</a> jets. In April 2015, <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Airlines" title="Alaska Airlines">Alaska Airlines</a> announced RDU's first daily non-stop flight to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seattle-Tacoma_International_Airport" title="Seattle-Tacoma International Airport">Seattle</a>, beginning in October 2015.</p>
<p>In September 2015, RDU announced its second transatlantic flight, to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris-Charles_de_Gaulle_Airport" title="Paris-Charles de Gaulle Airport">Paris</a>, which began in May 2016 and is operated by Delta using Boeing 757-200 aircraft. For Summer 2017, Delta will operate this flight using a Boeing 767-300. In a Triangle Business Journal Article, published on March 2, 2016, Delta explicitly referred to Raleigh-Durham as a focus city. Since then Delta has added several new flights out of Raleigh-Durham including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austin-Bergstrom_International_Airport" title="Austin-Bergstrom International Airport">Austin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newark_Liberty_International_Airport" title="Newark Liberty International Airport">Newark</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan_National_Airport" title="Ronald Reagan National Airport">Ronald Reagan National Airport</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_DC" title="Washington DC">Washington DC</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seattle-Tacoma_International_Airport" title="Seattle-Tacoma International Airport">Seattle</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future">Future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raleigh%E2%80%93Durham_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Raleigh-Durham has finished most of its construction with the completion of the Terminal 1 renovation, leading the airport to have the proper infrastructure until 2040. Nevertheless, the airport still owns nearly 2000 acres of unused land around the airport that it wants to develop. Some ideas for developing that land include an airport hotel or warehouse facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> For air service, the airport is looking at several domestic destinations including Kansas City and San Diego for new non-stop air service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> With the addition of the Paris flight, Raleigh is now looking into a trans-pacific flight, possibly to somewhere in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> or China where companies such as <a href="/wiki/Lenovo" title="Lenovo">Lenovo</a> have frequent business flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Airport_facilities">Airport facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raleigh%E2%80%93Durham_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Airport facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Raleigh–Durham International Airport has terminals <b>1</b> (Concourse A gates A1-A9 though only A5, A6, A7 and A8 are in use;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> there is no Concourse B) and <b>2</b> (Concourse C gates C1–C25, Concourse D gates D1–D20). Gates C23, C24, and C25 are international gates and can accommodate aircraft up to the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747</a>.The airport has implemented the most ambitious expansion in its history, begun in 2006 and completed in January 2011. Terminal 1 was re-opened after extensive renovation in 2014; and Terminal 2 was opened in 2008. The two terminals do not have an airside connection. Passengers moving between the terminals may ride a <a href="/wiki/Airport_bus" title="Airport bus">shuttle bus</a> or take the <a href="/wiki/Moving_walkway" title="Moving walkway">moving walkway</a> through the covered parking decks between the terminals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminal_1">Terminal 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raleigh%E2%80%93Durham_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Terminal 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RDU_Terminal_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/RDU_Terminal_1.jpg/220px-RDU_Terminal_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/RDU_Terminal_1.jpg/330px-RDU_Terminal_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/RDU_Terminal_1.jpg/440px-RDU_Terminal_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RDU_Terminal_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Terminal 1</div>

<p>The first terminal at RDU opened in 1955. Expanded in 1972 and again in 1976,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> the building was named Terminal B in 1982 when the then-new Terminal A opened. The two terminals were connected by a landside interior walkway. Terminal B was closed in 1989. In 1994, Terminal B lost its identity when it was renovated into an extension of Terminal A; an airside walkway was added to link all gates. In 2001 the south end of Terminal A was extended to include five temporary gates; these gates were closed in 2009, as the airlines using them moved to other gates.</p>
<p>Terminal A was renamed Terminal 1 on October 26, 2008 to bring RDU in line with terminal naming conventions and to end years of confusion.</p>
<p>After years of proposals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> the 1981 part of Terminal 1 was closed in 2012 for a complete renovation. The building reopened on April 13, 2014 with nine gates; Southwest and AirTran occupied the rebuilt terminal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The terminal achieved LEED certification in December 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In 2016 the pre-1981 parts of Terminal 1 and the 2001 south-end extension were demolished due to expenses and lack of practical use of these ends of the building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminal_2">Terminal 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raleigh%E2%80%93Durham_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Terminal 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RDU_Terminal_2_Concourse_C.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4d/RDU_Terminal_2_Concourse_C.jpg/220px-RDU_Terminal_2_Concourse_C.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4d/RDU_Terminal_2_Concourse_C.jpg/330px-RDU_Terminal_2_Concourse_C.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4d/RDU_Terminal_2_Concourse_C.jpg/440px-RDU_Terminal_2_Concourse_C.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RDU_Terminal_2_Concourse_C.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Terminal 2</div>

<p>Terminal 2 occupies the site of the former Terminal C, built between 1985 and 1987 for the American Airlines hub. In planning the terminal, American assumed that only around 20% of passengers would originate or terminate their trips in Raleigh–Durham; rather, Terminal C was optimized for the exchange of passengers between connecting flights, with a relatively small check-in and baggage claim area. In reality, connecting passengers only accounted for around two-thirds of the terminal's passengers. After the American and Midway hubs closed, the airport faced a decision about the future of Terminal C and how to eliminate the inconveniences it imposed on local passengers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airportbusiness_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-airportbusiness-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2003, the Airport Authority announced plans to expand and renovate the 300,000-square-foot (28,000 m²) building, transforming it into a new 900,000-ft² (84,000 m²) facility. In January 2006, the airport instead decided to replace the building entirely.</p>
<p>Terminal 2 was designed by <a href="/wiki/Fentress_Architects" title="Fentress Architects">Fentress Architects</a> under a philosophy of contextual regionalism, related to <a href="/wiki/Critical_regionalism" title="Critical regionalism">Critical regionalism</a>. The terminal, invoking the flowing hills and culture of North Carolina's <a href="/wiki/Piedmont_(United_States)" title="Piedmont (United States)">Piedmont region</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> consisted of two phases of construction. The first, larger Phase 1 opened on October 26, 2008 while Phase 2 opened on January 23, 2011 in time for the <a href="/wiki/58th_National_Hockey_League_All-Star_Game" title="58th National Hockey League All-Star Game">2011 NHL All Star Game</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rdu.com_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-rdu.com-18">[18]</a></sup> Terminal 2 has 36 gates; three configured for international flights. All gates feature adjustable jetbridges that can accommodate aircraft from <a href="/wiki/Regional_jet" title="Regional jet">regional jets</a> to <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747s</a>. The federal inspection area has 16 stations.</p>

<p>The airport incorporates two cargo areas, North Cargo and South Cargo. The North Cargo terminal area is used by cargo airlines. The largest cargo operators are FedEx and UPS. The South Cargo terminal area is used by commercial airlines for cargo operations.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Runways">Runways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raleigh%E2%80%93Durham_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Runways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>RDU has three runways. Two parallel runways are designated 5L/23R and 5R/23L, and a cross-wind runway designated 14/32. Both parallel runways have been equipped with LED lights.</p>
<ul>
<li>Runway 5L/23R: 10,000 feet (3,000 m) x 150 feet (concrete)</li>
<li>Runway 5R/23L: 7,500 feet (2,300 m) x 150 feet (asphalt)</li>
<li>Runway 14/32: 3,570 feet (1,100 m) x 100 feet (asphalt)</li>
</ul>
<p>Prior to the September 11 attacks in 2001, the RDU Airport Authority and <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> planned a fourth runway at the airport, but with the demise of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midway_Airlines_(JI)" title="Midway Airlines (JI)">Midway Airlines</a> hub and the airline industry downturn following 9/11 terrorist attacks, this plan was placed on hold. During the period between May 27, 2008 and June 24, 2008, and between May 19, 2010 and June 17, 2010, runway 5R/23L was closed for renovation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li>Lot 1 is a "cell-phone waiting" lot</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Observation_areas">Observation areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raleigh%E2%80%93Durham_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Observation areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>RDU maintains two public observation decks.</p>
<ul>
<li>One deck overlooks runway 5L/23R near the air traffic control tower and park-and-ride lot 2. It has a playground with a simplistic model of RDU's runways for kids and air traffic communications are broadcast via a loudspeaker for the curious public.</li>
<li>The second deck is located at the General Aviation Terminal. It includes a café called "CrossWinds Cafe". This observation deck allows for both inside and outside viewing.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ground_transportation">Ground transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raleigh%E2%80%93Durham_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Ground transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_transit">Public transit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raleigh%E2%80%93Durham_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Public transit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Public transportation to and from RDU is provided by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triangle_Transit" title="Triangle Transit">Triangle Transit</a> (TTA), which offers scheduled, fixed-route regional and commuter bus service between the airport, <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a> and the principal cities of Raleigh, Durham and town of Chapel Hill (where TTA connects with the respective local urban transit systems), as well as to and from Research Triangle Park and several of the region's larger suburban communities.</p>
<ul>
<li>TTA Route 100</li>
<li>Taxicab service at RDU is operated by RDU Taxi Inc., with a contract extension that runs through December 31, 2014.</li>
<li>Shuttle services: As in most regions, numerous hotels throughout the area offer guest shuttles to and from the airport. Service varies by location, however most are available from approximately 6 a.m. daily through the final arrival/departure of the evening.</li>
<li>Major rental car providers operate from the airport.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raleigh%E2%80%93Durham_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>On February 19, 1988, <a href="/wiki/AVAir_Flight_3378" title="AVAir Flight 3378">AVAir Flight 3378</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Fairchild_Swearingen_Metroliner" title="Fairchild Swearingen Metroliner">Fairchild Swearingen Metroliner</a> was on a regularly scheduled flight between Raleigh and <a href="/wiki/Richmond_International_Airport" title="Richmond International Airport">Richmond</a> when it crashed into a reservoir about a mile from the airport, where it had departed in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Cary,_North_Carolina" title="Cary, North Carolina">Cary, North Carolina</a>: The aircraft departed during low ceiling, low visibility and night conditions. Analysis of radar data indicated the aircraft was in a 45-degree descending turn. Both crew members and all 10 passengers were killed. It was revealed during the investigation that the pilot had complained of illness but decided to continue the flight.</li>
<li>On December 13, 1994, American Eagle Flight 3379 operated by AMR's regional airline <a href="/wiki/Flagship_Airlines" title="Flagship Airlines">Flagship Airlines</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jetstream_31" title="Jetstream 31">Jetstream 31</a> was on a regularly scheduled service of Raleigh–<a href="/wiki/Piedmont_Triad_International_Airport" title="Piedmont Triad International Airport">Greensboro</a>-Raleigh when it crashed into a wooded area about 4 miles (6.4 km) SW of the airport, in the vicinity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morrisville,_NC" title="Morrisville, NC">Morrisville, NC</a>. Of the 20 onboard (18 passengers and 2 crewmembers) 15 were killed while the 5 survivors received serious injuries. The probable cause of the crash was the pilot not following proper procedure when it came to an engine failure situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
