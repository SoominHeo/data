<p><b>Operation Rolling Thunder</b> was the title of a gradual and sustained aerial bombardment campaign conducted by the U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2nd_Air_Division" title="2nd Air Division">2nd Air Division</a> (later <a href="/wiki/Seventh_Air_Force" title="Seventh Air Force">Seventh Air Force</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Vietnam_Air_Force" title="Republic of Vietnam Air Force">Republic of Vietnam Air Force</a> (VNAF) against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Vietnam" title="Democratic Republic of Vietnam">Democratic Republic of Vietnam</a> (North Vietnam) from 2 March 1965 until 2 November 1968, during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>.</p>
<p>The four objectives of the operation (which evolved over time) were to boost the sagging morale of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saigon" title="Saigon">Saigon</a> regime in the Republic of Vietnam, to persuade North Vietnam to cease its support for the communist insurgency in South Vietnam without actually taking any ground forces into communist North Vietnam, to destroy North Vietnam's transportation system, industrial base, and air defenses, and to halt the flow of men and material into South Vietnam. Attainment of these objectives was made difficult by both the restraints imposed upon the U.S. and its allies by <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> exigencies and by the military aid and assistance received by North Vietnam from its communist allies, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and the <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">People's Republic of China</a> (PRC).</p>
<p>The operation became the most intense air/ground battle waged during the Cold War period; it was the most difficult such campaign fought by the U.S. Air Force since the aerial bombardment of Germany during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Supported by communist allies, <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">North Vietnam</a> fielded a potent mixture of sophisticated air-to-air and surface-to-air weapons that created one of the most effective air defenses ever faced by American military aviators.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gradually_escalating_action">Gradually escalating action</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Rolling_Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gradually escalating action">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on the origins of American involvement in Vietnam, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Tonkin_Incident" title="Gulf of Tonkin Incident">Gulf of Tonkin Incident</a>.</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Rolling_Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In response to President <a href="/wiki/Ngo_Dinh_Diem" title="Ngo Dinh Diem">Ngo Dinh Diem</a>'s abrogation of the 1956 reunification election and suppression of communists during the late 1950s, <a href="/wiki/Hanoi" title="Hanoi">Hanoi</a> had begun sending arms and material to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guerrillas" title="Guerrillas">guerrillas</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Front_for_the_Liberation_of_South_Vietnam" title="National Front for the Liberation of South Vietnam">National Front for the Liberation of South Vietnam</a> (NLF), who were fighting an insurgency to topple the American-supported <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saigon" title="Saigon">Saigon</a> government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> To combat the NLF and to shore up the government in the south, the U.S. initially delivered monetary aid, military advisors, and supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Between 1957 and 1963, the U.S. found itself committed, through its acceptance of the policy of <a href="/wiki/Containment" title="Containment">containment</a> and belief in the <a href="/wiki/Domino_theory" title="Domino theory">domino theory</a>, to defending South Vietnam from what it saw as expansive communist aggression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[a]</a></sup></p>
<p>U.S. policy was for a time dictated by its perception of improvement in the Saigon government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[b]</a></sup> No further commitment by the Americans would occur without tangible proof of the regime's survivability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup> Events in Vietnam, however, outraced this policy. By the beginning of 1965, it was stood upon its head—it was believed that without further American action the Saigon government could not survive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup> However, as late as February 8, in a cable to US Ambassador to South Vietnam <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maxwell_Taylor" title="Maxwell Taylor">Maxwell Taylor</a>, Johnson stressed that the paramount goal of a bombing campaign would be to boost Saigon's morale, not to influence Hanoi, expressing hope "that the building of a minimum government will benefit by ... assurances from us to the highest levels [of the South Vietnamese government] that we ... intend to take continuing action."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[c]</a></sup></p>
<p>Questions then arose among the U.S. administration and military leadership as to the best method by which Hanoi (the perceived locus of the insurgency) could be dissuaded from its course of action. The answer seemed to lie in the application of air power. By 1964 most of the civilians surrounding President <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> shared the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff" title="Joint Chiefs of Staff">Joint Chiefs of Staff</a>'s collective faith in the efficacy of strategic bombing to one degree or another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[14]</a></sup> They reasoned that a small nation like North Vietnam, with a tiny industrial base that was just emerging after the <a href="/wiki/First_Indochina_War" title="First Indochina War">First Indochina War</a>, would be reluctant to risk its new-found economic viability to support the insurgency in the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[15]</a></sup> Constantly affecting this decision-making process were fears of possible counter moves or outright intervention by the Soviet Union, the PRC, or both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[16]</a></sup> The civilians and the military were divided, however, on the manner of affecting Hanoi's will to support the southern insurgency. The civilians thought in terms of changing the regime's behavior while the military men were more concerned with breaking its will.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 1964, as a result of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Tonkin_Incident" title="Gulf of Tonkin Incident">Gulf of Tonkin Incident</a>, in which U.S. naval vessels claimed to have been attacked by North Vietnamese patrol boats, President Johnson ordered retaliatory air strikes (<a href="/wiki/Operation_Pierce_Arrow" title="Operation Pierce Arrow">Operation Pierce Arrow</a>) launched against the north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[d]</a></sup> This did not, however, satisfy the military chiefs, who demanded a wider and more aggressive campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation">Implementation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Rolling_Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Implementation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In March 1964 the CinC Pacific began developing plans for a sustained eight-week air campaign designed to escalate in three stages. This was published at the end of August as CINCPAC OPLAN 37-64, which included the "94 target list". Bridges, rail yards, docks, barracks and supply dumps were all targeted, and selected based on a criterion system considering:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>(a) reducing North Vietnamese support of communist operations in Laos and South Vietnam, (b) limiting North Vietnamese capabilities to take direct action against Laos and South Vietnam, and finally (c) impairing North Vietnam’s capacity to continue as an industrially viable state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[19]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>There was widespread concern that an air campaign could lead to a wider conflict involving the Chinese or Soviets. Westmoreland referred to "an almost paranoid fear of nuclear confrontation with the Soviet Union" and a "phobia" that the Chinese would invade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drew_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Drew-24">[20]</a></sup> Johnson later noted:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>By keeping a lid on all the designated targets, I knew I could keep the control of the war in my own hands. If China reacted to our slow escalation by threatening to retaliate, we’d have plenty of time to ease off the bombing. But this control—so essential for preventing World War III—would be lost the moment we unleashed a total assault on the North—for that would be rape rather than seduction—and then there would be no turning back. The Chinese reaction would be instant and total.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drew_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Drew-24">[20]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>For a time, no overt action was taken, and the plans continued to evolve. A further refinement of the plan was developed by <a href="/wiki/William_Bundy" title="William Bundy">William</a> and <a href="/wiki/McGeorge_Bundy" title="McGeorge Bundy">McGeorge Bundy</a> on 29 November 1964, with a more moderate target list, which the Joint Chiefs opposed. No action was taken while these, and other, plans were considered. But matters came to a head with the <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Camp_Holloway" title="Attack on Camp Holloway">attack on Camp Holloway</a> on 7 February 1965, which demanded immediate action, and resulted in a reprisal raid known as <a href="/wiki/Operation_Flaming_Dart" title="Operation Flaming Dart">Operation Flaming Dart</a>. A sapper raid against an American enlisted men's billet at Qui Nhon on the 10th<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[21]</a></sup> led to Flaming Dart II. These small-scale operations were launched against the southern region of the country, where the bulk of North Vietnam's ground forces and supply dumps were located.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>These actions led to the plans for a sustained air campaign being reconsidered. On 13 February a new plan, merging targets and priorities from the lists produced by the Bundys and the JSC, was approved and given the name <i>Rolling Thunder</i>. This campaign was not aimed at specific actions on the part of the North Vietnamese, but was intended as a larger response to the growing hostilities as a whole. Although some within the administration believed that the campaign would be costly, and that it might not work, they reasoned that it was "an acceptable risk, especially when considered against the alternative of introducing American combat troops."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[e]</a></sup> <i>Rolling Thunder</i> called for an eight-week air campaign consistent with the restrictions that Johnson and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">Secretary of Defense</a> Robert S. McNamara had imposed upon it. If the insurgency continued "with DRV support, strikes against the DRV would be extended with intensified efforts against targets north of the 19th parallel."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was believed that selective pressure, controlled by Washington, combined with diplomatic overtures, would prevail and compel Hanoi to end its aggression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[25]</a></sup> The military was still not satisfied, since, for the time being, the bombing campaign was to be limited to targets below the 19th parallel, each of which would have to be cleared individually by the President and McNamara.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[f]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first mission of the new operation was launched on 2 March against an ammunition storage area near Xom Bang. On the same day, 19 VNAF <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-1_Skyraider" title="A-1 Skyraider">A-1 Skyraiders</a> struck the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Quang_Khe_Naval_Base&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Quang Khe Naval Base (page does not exist)">Quang Khe Naval Base</a>. The Americans were shocked when six of their aircraft were shot down during the mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[27]</a></sup> Five of the downed crewmen were rescued, but it was a portent of things to come.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Over_the_north">Over the north</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Rolling_Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Over the north">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategic_persuasion">Strategic persuasion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Rolling_Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Strategic persuasion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In keeping with the doctrine of "gradualism", in which threatening destruction would serve as a more influential signal of American determination than destruction itself, it was better to hold important targets "hostage" by bombing trivial ones. From the beginning of <i>Rolling Thunder</i>, Washington dictated which targets would be struck, the day and hour of the attack, the number and types of aircraft and the tonnages and types of ordnance utilized, and sometimes even the direction of the attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[29]</a></sup> Airstrikes were strictly forbidden within 30 nautical miles (60 km) of Hanoi and within ten nautical miles (19 km) of the port of <a href="/wiki/Haiphong" title="Haiphong">Haiphong</a>. A thirty-mile buffer zone also extended along the length of the Chinese frontier. According to air force historian Earl Tilford:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Targeting bore little resemblance to reality in that the sequence of attacks was uncoordinated and the targets were approved randomly – even illogically. The North's airfields, which, according to any rational targeting policy, should have been hit first in the campaign, were also off-limits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[30]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>Although some of these restrictions were later loosened or rescinded, Johnson (with McNamara's support) kept a tight rein on the campaign, which continuously infuriated the American military commanders, right-wing members of Congress, and even some within the administration itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[31]</a></sup> One of the primary objectives of the operation, at least to the military, should have been the closure of Haiphong and other ports by aerial mining, thereby slowing or halting the flow of seaborne supplies entering the north. President Johnson refused to take such a provocative action, however, and such an operation was not implemented until 1972. There was also little consultation between Johnson and the military chiefs during the target selection process. Even the chairman of the Joint Chiefs, General Earl G. Wheeler, was not present for most of the critical discussions of 1965 and participated only occasionally thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The majority of strikes during <i>Rolling Thunder</i> were launched from four air bases, in <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>: <a href="/wiki/Nakhon_Ratchasima" title="Nakhon Ratchasima">Korat</a>, Takhli, <a href="/wiki/Udon_Thani" title="Udon Thani">Udon Thani</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ubon" title="Ubon">Ubon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[g]</a></sup> The aircraft refueled from aerial tankers over <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a> before flying on to their targets in the DRV. After attacking their targets (usually by dive-bombing) the strike forces would either fly directly back to Thailand or exit over the relatively safe waters of the Gulf of Tonkin. It was quickly decided that, in order to limit airspace conflicts between air force and naval strike forces, North Vietnam was divided into six target regions called "<a href="/wiki/Route_Package" title="Route Package">route packages</a>", each of which was assigned to either the air force or navy and into which the other was forbidden to intrude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Navy strikes were launched from the aircraft carriers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Task_Force_77_(U.S._Navy)" title="Task Force 77 (U.S. Navy)">Task Force 77</a>, cruising off the North Vietnamese coast at <a href="/wiki/Yankee_Station" title="Yankee Station">Yankee Station</a>. Naval aircraft, which had shorter ranges (and carried lighter bomb loads) than their air force counterparts, approached their targets from seaward with the majority of their strikes flown against coastal targets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[h]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 3 April the Joint Chiefs persuaded McNamara and Johnson to launch a four-week attack on North Vietnam's lines of communications, which would isolate that nation from its overland sources of supply in the PRC and the Soviet Union. About one-third of the North's imports came down the northeast railroad from the PRC, while the remaining two-thirds came by sea through Haiphong and other ports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[34]</a></sup> For the first time in the campaign, targets were to be chosen for their military, rather than their psychological, significance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[35]</a></sup> During the four weeks, 26 bridges and seven ferries were destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[36]</a></sup> Other targets included the extensive North Vietnamese radar system, barracks, and ammunition depots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drew_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Drew-24">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>The panhandle of southern North Vietnam, however, remained the primary focus of operations and total sorties flown there rose from 3,600 in April to 4,000 in May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morocco.2C_p._63_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morocco.2C_p._63-45">[37]</a></sup> Slowly moving away from the destruction of fixed targets, "armed reconnaissance" missions, in which small formations of aircraft patrolled highways, railroads, and rivers, searching for targets of opportunity, were authorized. These missions increased from two to 200 sorties per week by the end of 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morocco.2C_p._63_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morocco.2C_p._63-45">[37]</a></sup> Eventually, armed reconnaissance missions constituted 75 percent of the total bombing effort, in part because the system through which fixed targets were requested, selected, and authorized was so complicated and unwieldy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Changing_priorities_and_POL_strikes">Changing priorities and POL strikes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Rolling_Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Changing priorities and POL strikes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If <i>Rolling Thunder</i> was supposed to "send signals" to Hanoi to desist in its actions, it did not seem to be working. On 8 April, responding to requests for peace negotiations, North Vietnamese premier, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pham_Van_Dong" title="Pham Van Dong">Pham Van Dong</a>, stated that they could only begin when: the bombing was halted; the U.S. had removed all of its troops from the south; the Saigon government recognized the demands of the NLF; and it was agreed that the reunification of Vietnam would be settled by the Vietnamese themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>As part of a large attack on the <a href="/wiki/Thanh_H%C3%B3a_Bridge" title="Thanh Hóa Bridge">Thanh Hóa Bridge</a> on 3 April, the VPAF first appeared with two flights of four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MiG-17" title="MiG-17">MiG-17s</a> launched from <a href="/wiki/Ph%C3%BAc_Y%C3%AAn_Air_Base" title="Phúc Yên Air Base">Noi Bai airbase</a> and shot down an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-8_Crusader" title="F-8 Crusader">F-8 Crusader</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[i]</a></sup> for the cost of one of their own aircraft written off when it landed on a river bed after running short of fuel. A repeat the next day resulted in a classic <a href="/wiki/Dogfight" title="Dogfight">dogfight</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-100_Super_Sabre" title="F-100 Super Sabre">F-100 Super Sabres</a> and F-105s fighting with more MiG-17s. In total, the USAF lost eleven aircraft to air and ground forces, while the VPAF lost three of their fighters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The entire complexion of the American effort was altered on 8 March 1965, when 3,500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Marines" title="U.S. Marines">U.S. Marines</a> came ashore at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Da_Nang" title="Da Nang">Da Nang</a>, ostensibly to defend the southern airfields committed to prosecuting <i>Rolling Thunder</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[41]</a></sup> The mission of the ground forces was expanded to combat operations and, from that point onward, the aerial campaign became a secondary operation, overwhelmed by troop deployments and the escalation of ground operations in South Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[42]</a></sup> Until the third week of April, <i>Rolling Thunder</i> had enjoyed at least equal status with air missions conducted in the south. After that time, strikes that interfered with requirements for the southern battlefield were either cut back or cancelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 24 December 1965, 170 U.S. aircraft had been lost during the campaign (85 Air Force, 94 Navy, and one Marine Corps). Eight VNAF aircraft had also been lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hobson15-166_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hobson15-166-53">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[j]</a></sup> Air force aircrews had flown 25,971 sorties and dropped 32,063 tons of bombs. Naval aviators had flown 28,168 sorties and dropped 11,144 tons. The VNAF had contributed 682 missions with unknown ordnance tonnages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[45]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:F-105_hit_by_SA-2_over_Vietnam.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1070" data-file-width="1800" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/F-105_hit_by_SA-2_over_Vietnam.jpg/220px-F-105_hit_by_SA-2_over_Vietnam.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/F-105_hit_by_SA-2_over_Vietnam.jpg/330px-F-105_hit_by_SA-2_over_Vietnam.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/F-105_hit_by_SA-2_over_Vietnam.jpg/440px-F-105_hit_by_SA-2_over_Vietnam.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

An F-105D hit by an <a href="/wiki/S-75_Dvina" title="S-75 Dvina">SA-2</a> missile</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>U.S. reconnaissance discovered on 5 April 1966, that the North Vietnamese were constructing positions for what could only be <a href="/wiki/Surface-to-air_missile" title="Surface-to-air missile">surface-to-air missile</a> (SAM) batteries. The air force and navy then filed a joint appeal to Washington for permission to strike the sites, but they were refused since most of the sites were near the restricted urban areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[46]</a></sup> Then, on 24 July, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-105_Thunderchief" title="F-105 Thunderchief">F-105</a> was shot down by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SA-2_Guideline" title="SA-2 Guideline">SA-2 Guideline</a> missile. Three days later, a one-time strike was authorized against the two offending missile sites. The Americans, however, fell for an elaborate trap when the sites turned out to be dummies surrounded by anti-aircraft artillery defenses. One American pilot described the action which followed as "looking like the end of the world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morocco.2C_p._109_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morocco.2C_p._109-57">[47]</a></sup> Six of the strike craft were destroyed (two of the pilots were killed, one missing, two captured, and one rescued) during the ambush.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morocco.2C_p._109_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morocco.2C_p._109-57">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 29 June 1966, airstrikes against the North's petroleum, oil, and lubricants (POL) storage areas were authorized by Johnson. The American military had advocated such strikes since the inception of the operation, believing that to deny North Vietnam its POL would cause its military effort to grind to a halt. At first, the strikes appeared highly successful, destroying tank farms near Hanoi and Haiphong and leading the <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">Central Intelligence Agency</a> (CIA) to estimate that 70 percent of North Vietnam's oil facilities had been destroyed for the loss of 43 aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[48]</a></sup> The loss of the oil storage tank farms and refineries proved to be only a short-term inconvenience for North Vietnam, however, since Hanoi had anticipated just such a campaign and had during that time dispersed the majority of its POL stocks in 50-gallon drums across the length of the country. The POL attacks were halted on 4 September, after U.S. intelligence admitted that there was "no evidence yet of any shortages of POL in North Vietnam."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Rolling_Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems">Problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Rolling_Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Rolling Thunder</i> exposed many problems within the American military services committed to it and tended to exacerbate others. A key interservice issue (and one which was not solved until 1968) was the command and control arrangement in Southeast Asia. The Air Force's 2nd Air Division (replaced by the Seventh Air Force on 1 April 1966) was ostensibly responsible for aerial operations over North and South Vietnam. It was subordinate, however, to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MACV" title="MACV">MACV</a> and its commander, U.S. Army General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_C._Westmoreland" title="William C. Westmoreland">William C. Westmoreland</a>, who tended to see his problems centered in the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[50]</a></sup> The U.S. <a href="/wiki/Seventh/Thirteenth_United_States_Air_Force" title="Seventh/Thirteenth United States Air Force">Seventh/Thirteenth Air Force</a>, based in Thailand (which carried out the majority of the Air Force's strikes in North Vietnam), had a dual command structure. It reported to the Seventh on operational matters and to the Thirteenth Air Force (whose headquarters was in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>) for logistical and administrative concerns. These command and control complexities grew even more tangled with the division of the aerial effort into four competing operational areas (those in South Vietnam, North Vietnam, and Laos (both north and south).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Navy's Task Force 77 took its orders via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/7th_Fleet" title="7th Fleet">7th Fleet</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CINCPAC" title="CINCPAC">CINCPAC</a>, a Navy admiral based in <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">Honolulu</a>, through his subordinate, the Air Force commander of Pacific Air Forces (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PACAF" title="PACAF">PACAF</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[52]</a></sup> Due to their influence, the Navy could not be persuaded to integrate its air operations over North Vietnam with those of the Air Force. General William Momyer, commander of the Seventh, had the impression that CINCPAC and PACAF wanted to keep the Thai-based aircraft out of his hands. "By denying Momyer, they were really denying Westmoreland and keeping air operations against the DRV under their control."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson15_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson15-63">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[k]</a></sup> To complicate matters, the U.S. ambassadors to Thailand (<a href="/wiki/Graham_Martin" title="Graham Martin">Graham Martin</a>) and Laos (<a href="/wiki/William_H._Sullivan" title="William H. Sullivan">William H. Sullivan</a>) exerted undue influence over operational and command arrangements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson15_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson15-63">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>This bizarre command structure went against the grain of the Air Force's single air manager concept, which dictated that one commander was to control and coordinate all aircraft within a combat theater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[l]</a></sup> The chain through which operational strike requests had to flow gave some indication of the growing overcomplexity of the campaign. Requests for airstrikes originated with the 2nd Air Division and Task Force 77 in Vietnam and then proceeded to CINCPAC, who in turn reported to his superiors, the Joint Chiefs, at <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">the Pentagon</a>. After input from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Department" title="State Department">State Department</a> and the CIA, the requests then proceeded to the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a>, where the president and his "Tuesday Cabinet" made decisions on the strike requests on a weekly basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[m]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A-6As_VA-196_dropping_Mk_82_bombs_Vietnam.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
U.S. Navy <a href="/wiki/Grumman_A-6_Intruder" title="Grumman A-6 Intruder">A-6A Intruder</a> all-weather bombers, in 1968</div>

</div>
<p>Another problem exposed by <i>Rolling Thunder</i> was the unpreparedness of the Air Force for the operations it was undertaking. Its aircraft had been designed and its pilots trained for strategic operations against the Soviet Union – for nuclear, not conventional war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tilford.2C_p._113_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tilford.2C_p._113-68">[55]</a></sup> The new campaign exposed years of neglect in conventional tactics, while aircraft capabilities and armament were ill-suited to the task at hand. The Air Force was also embarrassed by the fact that the Navy was better prepared. It possessed the only all-weather bomber in the U.S. inventory in the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-6_Intruder" title="A-6 Intruder">A-6 Intruder</a> and was also responsible for the development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-4_Phantom" title="F-4 Phantom">F-4 Phantom</a> fighter-bomber, which became ubiquitous during the Vietnam War.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[n]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once air-to-air combat began over North Vietnam, the Air Force was again found lacking. The mainstay missiles of the air war turned out to be the Navy-developed <a href="/wiki/AIM-9_Sidewinder" title="AIM-9 Sidewinder">AIM-9 Sidewinder</a> and <a href="/wiki/AIM-7_Sparrow" title="AIM-7 Sparrow">AIM-7 Sparrow</a>, not its own <a href="/wiki/AIM-4_Falcon" title="AIM-4 Falcon">AIM-4 Falcon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[56]</a></sup> The Air Force continuously opposed adapting to the war in Southeast Asia, since its leadership believed that it was an aberration that would be quickly resolved. It could then turn its attention (and its more modern weapons) against the greater threat posed by the Soviet Union. None in the Air Force high command foresaw that the war would drag on for nearly a decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tilford.2C_p._113_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tilford.2C_p._113-68">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Air Force did possess an aircraft which had an all-weather capability, radar-guided bombing equipment, and awesome destructive potential – the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-52_Stratofortress" title="B-52 Stratofortress">B-52 Stratofortress</a>. The civilian administration, however, never considered utilizing the big bombers (whose operations remained under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Air_Command" title="Strategic Air Command">Strategic Air Command</a>) very far north of the DMZ, believing that it was too overt an escalation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[57]</a></sup> Air Force Chief of Staff <a href="/wiki/John_P._McConnell" title="John P. McConnell">John P. McConnell</a> also opposed sending the bombers into the air defense environment in the north and limited B-52 strikes to Route Package One.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[o]</a></sup></p>
<p>Compounding these issues was the one-year rotation policy adopted by the Pentagon in Southeast Asia. Although the first aircrews arriving in-theater were highly experienced, the rapidly growing tempo and ever-expanding length of the operation demanded more personnel. This exacerbated a growing lack of experienced aircrews. This dilemma was further compounded by an Air Force policy which dictated universal pilot training while proscribing involuntary second combat tours, which combined, had the effect of rotating personnel to different aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[p]</a></sup> Conversely, the Navy tended to maintain its aircrews within the same community for the duration of their careers, thereby retaining their expertise, but also incurring greater losses among experienced crews undergoing multiple combat tours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another factor was the weather within the operational theater. The cyclical monsoon patterns meant that the weather was deplorable for flight operations eight months of the year (from late September to early May) when rain and fog tended to conceal targets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tilford.2C_p._113_68-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tilford.2C_p._113-68">[55]</a></sup> Lack of adequate all-weather and night-bombing capability made it necessary for the majority of U.S. missions to be conducted during daylight hours, thereby easing the burden on the air defense forces of North Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson.2C_p._41_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson.2C_p._41-77">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="People.27s_War_in_the_air">People's War in the air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Rolling_Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: People's War in the air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hanoi_POL_site_burning_1966.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="766" data-file-width="609" height="239" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Hanoi_POL_site_burning_1966.jpg/190px-Hanoi_POL_site_burning_1966.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Hanoi_POL_site_burning_1966.jpg/285px-Hanoi_POL_site_burning_1966.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Hanoi_POL_site_burning_1966.jpg/380px-Hanoi_POL_site_burning_1966.jpg 2x" width="190"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hanoi_POL_site_burning_1966.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Hanoi POL facility burning after it was attacked by the U.S. Air Force on 29 June 1966</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Before <i>Rolling Thunder</i> even began the North Vietnamese leadership knew what was coming. It issued a February 1965 directive to the military and the population to "maintain communication and transportation and to expect the complete destruction of the entire country, including Hanoi and Haiphong."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[62]</a></sup> The communist leadership declared "a people's war against the air war of destruction...each citizen is a soldier, each village, street, and plant a fortress on the anti-American battlefront."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[63]</a></sup> All except those deemed "truly indispensable to the life of the capital" were evacuated to the countryside. By 1967, Hanoi's population had been reduced by half.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since gaining air superiority over U.S. forces was out of the question, the northern leadership decided to implement a policy of air deniability. At the beginning of the campaign, North Vietnam possessed approximately 1,500 anti-aircraft weapons, most of which were of the light 37 and 57mm variety. Within one year, however, the U.S. estimated that the number had grown to over 5,000 guns, including 85 and 100mm radar-directed weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morocco.2C_p._102_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morocco.2C_p._102-81">[65]</a></sup> That estimate was later revised downward from a high of 7,000 in early 1967 to less than a thousand by 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson.2C_p._40_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson.2C_p._40-82">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[q]</a></sup> Regardless, during <i>Rolling Thunder</i>, 80 percent of U.S. aircraft losses were attributed to anti-aircraft fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>Backing up the guns were the fighter aircraft of the North Vietnamese Air Force, which originally consisted of only 53 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MiG-17_Fresco" title="MiG-17 Fresco">MiG-17 Fresco</a> fighter aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morocco.2C_p._102_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morocco.2C_p._102-81">[65]</a></sup> Though considered antiquated by the Americans when compared to their supersonic jets, the North Vietnamese turned their aircraft's weaknesses into strengths. They were fast enough for hit and run ambush operations and they were also maneuverable enough to shock the American fighter community by shooting down more advanced F-8 Crusaders and F-105 Thunderchiefs, which had to quickly develop new tactics. The newer missile-armed F-4 Phantom would become the Americans' primary dogfighting platform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>The simple appearance of MiGs could often accomplish their mission by causing American pilots to jettison their bomb loads as a defensive measure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[r]</a></sup> In 1966, the MiG-17 were joined by more modern Soviet-built <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MiG-21_Fishbed" title="MiG-21 Fishbed">MiG-21 Fishbeds</a>, which could fight on a more equal footing with the U.S. aircraft. By 1967, the North Vietnamese Air Force was maintaining an interceptor force of 100 aircraft, many of which were based on PRC airfields and out of reach of American air attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>The northern economy was decentralized for its protection and large factories, located in the heavily populated Red River Delta region, were broken up and scattered into caves and small villages throughout the countryside. In the more heavily bombed southern panhandle, entire villages moved into underground tunnel complexes for the duration. Food shortages in North Vietnam became widespread, especially in the urban areas, as rice farmers went into the military or volunteered for service repairing bomb damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[71]</a></sup> When the nation's transportation system came under attack, destroyed bridges were repaired or replaced by dirt fords, ferries, and underwater or pontoon bridges. The system proved to be durable, well built, easily repaired, and practically impossible to shut down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>Perhaps North Vietnam's ultimate resource was its population. During 1965, 97,000 North Vietnamese civilians volunteered to work full-time in repairing the damage inflicted by U.S. bombs. Another 370,000–500,000 civilians worked part-time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[73]</a></sup> When the nation's lines of communication came under attack, railroad supply trains and truck convoys were split into smaller elements which traveled only at night. The logistical effort was supported by citizens on <a href="/wiki/Sampan" title="Sampan">sampans</a>, driving carts, pushing wheelbarrows, or man-portering supplies on their backs to keep the war effort going. They were motivated by slogans like "Each kilogram of goods...is a bullet shot into the head of the American pirates."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[74]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biggest_shooting_gallery_on_Earth">Biggest shooting gallery on Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Rolling_Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Biggest shooting gallery on Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SAMs_and_Wild_Weasels">SAMs and Wild Weasels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Rolling_Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: SAMs and Wild Weasels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>North Vietnam's deployment of SAMs forced American pilots to make hard choices: either approach targets at higher altitudes (to avoid anti-aircraft fire) and become prey to SAMs, or fly lower to avoid the missiles and become the target of anti-aircraft batteries. Due to altered tactics and the increased use of electronic radar jamming, the record of SAM kills decreased over time. The already dismal missile success rate fell from one kill for 30 launches to less than one kill for 50.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[75]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A-7Bs_VA-25_during_Ironhand_mission_1969.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1446" data-file-width="1872" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/A-7Bs_VA-25_during_Ironhand_mission_1969.jpg/220px-A-7Bs_VA-25_during_Ironhand_mission_1969.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/A-7Bs_VA-25_during_Ironhand_mission_1969.jpg/330px-A-7Bs_VA-25_during_Ironhand_mission_1969.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/A-7Bs_VA-25_during_Ironhand_mission_1969.jpg/440px-A-7Bs_VA-25_during_Ironhand_mission_1969.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A-7Bs_VA-25_during_Ironhand_mission_1969.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
U.S. Navy <a href="/wiki/LTV_A-7_Corsair_II" title="LTV A-7 Corsair II">A-7B Corsairs</a> armed with <a href="/wiki/AGM-45_Shrike" title="AGM-45 Shrike">Shrike</a> anti-radiation missiles, 1969</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The nature of the gradual escalation had given Hanoi time to adapt to the situation. By 1967, North Vietnam had formed an estimated 25 SAM battalions (with six missile launchers each) which rotated among approximately 150 sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson.2C_p._40_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson.2C_p._40-82">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[s]</a></sup> With the assistance of the Soviet Union, the North Vietnamese had also quickly integrated an early warning radar system of more than 200 facilities which covered the entire country, tracking incoming U.S. raids, and then coordinating SAMs, anti-aircraft batteries, and MiGs to attack them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson.2C_p._41_77-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson.2C_p._41-77">[61]</a></sup> During 1967 U.S. losses totaled 248 aircraft (145 Air Force, 102 Navy, and one Marine Corps).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hobson15-166_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hobson15-166-53">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>To survive in this ever more lethal air defense zone, the U.S. had to adopt newer, more specialized tactics. Large-scale strikes, known as force packages in the Air Force and multi-carrier "<a href="/wiki/Alpha_strike_(United_States_Navy)" title="Alpha strike (United States Navy)">Alpha strikes</a>" by the Navy, were assigned numerous support aircraft to protect the fighter-bombers. First into the target areas were specialized <i>Iron Hand</i> flak suppression missions. These consisted of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-105_Thunderchief" title="F-105 Thunderchief">F-105</a> <a href="/wiki/Wild_Weasel" title="Wild Weasel">Wild Weasel</a> hunter/killer teams configured with sophisticated electronic equipment to detect and locate the emissions associated with SAM guidance and control radars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Wild Weasels also carried <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_countermeasures" title="Electronic countermeasures">electronic countermeasures</a> (ECM) equipment to protect themselves. They directed flak suppression strikes and carried <a href="/wiki/AGM-45_Shrike" title="AGM-45 Shrike">AGM-45 Shrike</a> anti-radiation missiles (another Navy development), which homed in on the radar systems of the SAMs. The SA-2 had greater range than the Shrike, but if the Shrike was launched and the radar operator stayed on the air, the American missile would home in on the signal and destroy the radar source. A sophisticated cat and mouse game then ensued between North Vietnamese radar operators and the Wild Weasel pilots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[77]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KC-135A_refueling_Wild_Weasel_team_Oct_1972.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="954" data-file-width="1727" height="122" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/KC-135A_refueling_Wild_Weasel_team_Oct_1972.jpg/220px-KC-135A_refueling_Wild_Weasel_team_Oct_1972.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/KC-135A_refueling_Wild_Weasel_team_Oct_1972.jpg/330px-KC-135A_refueling_Wild_Weasel_team_Oct_1972.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/KC-135A_refueling_Wild_Weasel_team_Oct_1972.jpg/440px-KC-135A_refueling_Wild_Weasel_team_Oct_1972.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

A USAF "Iron Hand" SAM-suppression team late in the war</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Next came the bomb-laden strike aircraft protected by escort fighters (Combat Air Patrol or MIGCAP) and electronic jamming aircraft to degrade enemy radar. New ECM devices had hurriedly been deployed to protect aircraft from missile attacks, but they remained subject to frequent breakdowns because of climate conditions in Southeast Asia. Also included in the missions were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KC-135_Stratotanker" title="KC-135 Stratotanker">KC-135</a> aerial tankers and Search and Rescue (SAR) helicopters, which were, in turn, protected by propeller-driven A-1 escorts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>From mid-1966 until the end of 1967, President Johnson continued to dole out sensitive targets one by one to the generals while simultaneously trying to placate the doves in Congress and within his own administration with periodic cutbacks and half-hearted peace initiatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[t]</a></sup> In the end, this erratic course satisfied no one and did little to alter the course of the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>The nature of the targets and the risks involved in striking (and re-striking) them began to take a toll. <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_Naval_Operations" title="Chief of Naval Operations">Chief of Naval Operations</a> <a href="/wiki/David_L._McDonald" title="David L. McDonald">David McDonald</a> reported to his co-chiefs after a trip to South Vietnam in September 1966, that <i>Rolling Thunder</i> aircrews were angered with the targeting process and that they faulted the campaign due to "guidelines requiring repetitive air programs that seemed more than anything else to benefit enemy gunners."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[80]</a></sup> During 1967, the second full year of <i>Rolling Thunder</i> operations, 362 U.S. aircraft had been lost over North Vietnam (208 Air Force, 142 Navy, and 12 Marine Corps).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hobson15-166_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hobson15-166-53">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MiGs_and_interdiction">MiGs and interdiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Rolling_Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: MiGs and interdiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Rolling Thunder</i> reached the last stage of its operational evolution during 1967 and 1968. The chief purpose of the American air effort in the higher Route Packages of North Vietnam was slowly transformed into that of interdicting the flow of supplies and material and the destruction of those segments of the north's infrastructure that supported its military effort. Although most U.S. aircraft losses continued to be inflicted by anti-aircraft fire, U.S. Air Force F-105s and Navy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-4_Skyhawk" title="A-4 Skyhawk">A-4 Skyhawks</a> increasingly encountered SAMs and MiGs. North Vietnamese fighters also became a particular problem because of the lack of radar coverage in the Red River Delta region, which allowed the MiGs to surprise the strike forces. Airborne early warning aircraft had difficulty detecting the fighters at low altitudes and the aircraft themselves were difficult to see visually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[81]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VPAF_MiG-21_landing_with_chute.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="665" data-file-width="1800" height="81" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/VPAF_MiG-21_landing_with_chute.jpg/220px-VPAF_MiG-21_landing_with_chute.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/VPAF_MiG-21_landing_with_chute.jpg/330px-VPAF_MiG-21_landing_with_chute.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/VPAF_MiG-21_landing_with_chute.jpg/440px-VPAF_MiG-21_landing_with_chute.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VPAF_MiG-21_landing_with_chute.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A missile-armed VPAF <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MiG-21_Fishbed" title="MiG-21 Fishbed">MiG-21PF</a> landing, using its <a href="/wiki/Drogue_parachute" title="Drogue parachute">drogue parachute</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>While F-105s did score 27 air-to-air victories, the overall exchange ratio was near parity. On January 2, 1967, the Americans sprang a surprise on the MiGs when they launched <a href="/wiki/Operation_Bolo" title="Operation Bolo">Operation Bolo</a>. F-4 Phantoms, using the same radio call signs, direction of approach, altitude, and speed as a typical flight of bomb-laden F-105s, lured a group of MiG-21s toward what the MiG pilots thought would be easy prey. The result was seven MiG-21s shot down within 12 minutes for no U.S. losses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later in the year, the U.S. launched its most intense and sustained attempt to force North Vietnam into peace negotiations. Almost all of the targets on the Joint Chiefs' list had been authorized for attack, including airfields that had been previously off limits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morocco.2C_p._159_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morocco.2C_p._159-103">[83]</a></sup> Only central Hanoi, Haiphong, and the PRC border area remained prohibited from attack. A major effort was made to isolate the urban areas by downing bridges and attacking LOCs. Also struck were the Thai Nguyen steel complex (origin of the <a href="/wiki/Pardo%27s_Push" title="Pardo's Push">Pardo's Push</a>), thermal and electrical power plants, ship and rail repair facilities, and warehouses. North Vietnamese MiGs entered the battle <i>en masse</i>, as their capital was threatened and kill ratios fell to one U.S. aircraft lost for every two MiGs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morocco.2C_p._159_103-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morocco.2C_p._159-103">[83]</a></sup> During 1968, MiGs accounted for 22 percent of the 184 American aircraft (75 Air Force, 59 Navy, and five Marine Corps) lost over the north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[84]</a></sup> As a result, operations against the last of North Vietnam's airfields, previously off-limits to attack, were authorized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the best interdiction efforts of <i>Rolling Thunder</i>, however, the NLF and PAVN launched their largest offensive thus far in the war on 30 January 1968, striking throughout South Vietnam during the lunar new year holiday. The <a href="/wiki/Tet_Offensive" title="Tet Offensive">Tet Offensive</a> concluded as a military disaster for North Vietnam and its NLF allies, but it also adversely affected U.S. public opinion, which in turn affected the will of Washington.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[u]</a></sup> Fortunately for North Vietnam, many U.S. bombing advocates (including Air Force Chief of Staff McConnell) did not want to risk the one aircraft capable of delivering a lot of bombs in bad weather – the B-52. Without them, there was little that could be done over the north in response to Tet, since bad weather minimized fighter operations until the beginning of April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[86]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_the_line">End of the line</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Rolling_Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: End of the line">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition">Opposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Rolling_Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Opposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on the positions of the secretary of defense, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_S._McNamara" title="Robert S. McNamara">Robert S. McNamara</a>.</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on the negotiations, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Peace_Talks" title="Paris Peace Talks">Paris Peace Talks</a>.</div>
<p>Writing after the war, Robert McNamara stated that by spring 1967 he and other civilians in the administration had become convinced that both <i>Rolling Thunder</i> and the ground war in South Vietnam were not working.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[87]</a></sup> McNamara claimed that he and others within the administration continuously opposed the Joint Chief's recommendations for an increased tempo of bombing and the loosening of target restrictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[88]</a></sup> The generals found themselves on the horns of a dilemma of their own making. They continuously claimed that the campaign was working, yet they also had to continuously demand greater latitude in order to make the campaign succeed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[v]</a></sup> The limited goals entailed in American foreign policy and the military's goal of total victory were simply not reconcilable. The great conundrum had then become how to defeat North Vietnam without defeating North Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 9 August 1967 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senate_Armed_Services_Committee" title="Senate Armed Services Committee">Senate Armed Services Committee</a> opened hearings on the bombing campaign. Complaints from the armed services had sparked the interest of some of the most vocal hawks on Capitol Hill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[91]</a></sup> The military chiefs testified before the committee, complaining about the gradual nature of the air war and its civilian-imposed restrictions. It was obvious that McNamara, the only civilian subpoenaed and the last to testify before the committee, was to be the scapegoat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[92]</a></sup> The Secretary of Defense marshaled his objections to an indiscriminate air war and adeptly rebutted the charges of the military chiefs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[93]</a></sup> He bluntly admitted that there was "no basis to believe that any bombing campaign...would by itself force <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh" title="Ho Chi Minh">Ho Chi Minh</a>'s regime into submission, short, that is, of the virtual annihilation of North Vietnam and its people."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p>It had now become clear to President Johnson that McNamara had become a liability to the administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[95]</a></sup> In February 1968, McNamara resigned his position and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Clark_Clifford" title="Clark Clifford">Clark Clifford</a>, who was chosen because of his personal friendship with Johnson and his previous opposition to McNamara's suggestions that the number of troops in the South Vietnam be stabilized and that <i>Rolling Thunder</i> be ended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[96]</a></sup> McNamara's position, however was almost immediately taken up by Secretary of State Dean Rusk, (until then an ardent advocate of the bombing campaign). Rusk proposed limiting the campaign to the panhandle of North Vietnam without preconditions and awaiting Hanoi's reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[97]</a></sup> Within months Clifford too began to adopt the views of the man he had replaced, gradually becoming convinced that the U.S. had to withdraw from an open-ended commitment to the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[98]</a></sup> Disappointed by perceived political defeats at home and hoping that Hanoi would enter into negotiations, President Johnson announced on 31 March 1968, that all bombing north of the 19th parallel would cease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[99]</a></sup> As a result of that decision, into the area between the 17th and 19th parallels, the Air Force and Navy began to pour all the firepower that they had formerly spread throughout North Vietnam. The Air Force doubled the number of sorties sent into Route Package One to more than 6,000 per month with the campaign concentrated on interdiction "choke points", road closing, and truck hunting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[100]</a></sup> Once again, the military commanders were faced a familiar dilemma: having opposed the bombing cutback, they then decided that the new policy had a lot of merit, especially when considering the alternative of no bombing at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[101]</a></sup> The North Vietnamese responded by doubling the number of anti-aircraft batteries in the panhandle, but most of their SAM batteries remained deployed around Hanoi and Haiphong.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[102]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hanoi, which had continuously stipulated that it would not conduct negotiations while the bombing continued, finally agreed to meet with the Americans for preliminary talks in Paris. As a result, President Johnson declared that a complete bombing halt over North Vietnam would go into effect on 1 November 1968, just prior to the U.S. presidential election. Although the bombing halt was to be linked to progress in the peace talks, the Joint Chiefs were skeptical that the administration would reopen the bombing campaign under any circumstances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[103]</a></sup> They were correct. North Vietnam was not the target of intense bombing again for another three and half years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[104]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conclusions">Conclusions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Rolling_Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Conclusions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Between March 1965 and November 1968, aircraft of the U.S. Air Force had flown 153,784 attack sorties against North Vietnam, while the Navy and Marine Corps had added another 152,399.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[105]</a></sup> On 31 December 1967, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> announced that 864,000 tons of American bombs had been dropped on North Vietnam during <i>Rolling Thunder</i>, compared with 653,000 tons dropped during the entire <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> and 503,000 tons in the Pacific theater during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[106]</a></sup></p>
<p>The CIA estimated on 1 January 1968 that damage inflicted in the north totaled $370 million in physical destruction, including $164 million worth of damage to capital assets (such as factories, bridges, and power plants). The agency also estimated that approximately 1,000 casualties had been inflicted on the North Vietnamese population per week, or approximately 90,000 for the 44-month period, 72,000 of whom were civilians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[107]</a></sup> Due to combat and operational circumstances, 506 U.S. Air Force, 397 Navy, and 19 Marine Corps aircraft were lost over or near North Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlight53_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schlight53-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hobson15-116_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hobson15-116-4">[4]</a></sup> During the operation, of the 745 crewmen shot down, the U.S. Air Force recorded 145 rescued, 255 killed, 222 captured (23 of whom died in captivity), and 123 missing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlight53_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Schlight53-3">[3]</a></sup> Figures on U.S. Navy and Marine Corps casualties were harder to come by. During the 44-month time frame, 454 naval aviators were killed, captured, or missing during combined operations over North Vietnam and Laos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[108]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Rolling Thunder</i> had begun as a campaign of psychological and strategic persuasion, but it changed very quickly to interdiction, a tactical mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[109]</a></sup> Its ultimate failure had two sources, both of which lay with the civilian and military policy-makers in Washington: first, neither group could ever conceive that the North Vietnamese would endure under the punishment that they would unleash upon it. The civilians, moreover, did not understand air power well enough to know that their policies might be crippling it; second, the American military leadership failed to initially propose and develop, or later to adapt, an appropriate strategy for the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[110]</a></sup></p>
<p>Along the way, <i>Rolling Thunder</i> also fell prey to the same dysfunctional managerial attitude as did the rest of the American military effort in Southeast Asia. The <i>process</i> of the campaign became an end unto itself, with sortie generation as the standard by which progress was measured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[111]</a></sup> Sortie rates and the number of bombs dropped, however, equaled efficiency, not effectiveness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[112]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Rolling_Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Studying the outcome of the events in Rolling Thunder, the Air Force and Navy came to very different conclusions on how to adapt. The Air Force noted that most of their air-to-air losses were due to unseen attacks from the rear, and thus the problem could be addressed through additional technology that would provide early warning of such attacks. They began modifying their aircraft with built-in <a href="/wiki/M61_Vulcan" title="M61 Vulcan">M61 Vulcans</a> for close-in use, adopted the Sidewinder and began upgrading them to improve their performance, and introduced new ground and air-based radars to provide an overall watch over the battlefield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[113]</a></sup> The Navy concluded that the primary problem was that their pilots had not been given proper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_combat_maneuvering" title="Air combat maneuvering">air combat maneuvering</a> training, and were forced to rely on missiles that were not performing as expected. In 1968 they introduced the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TOPGUN" title="TOPGUN">TOPGUN</a> program, a move that was welcomed by the F-8 pilots who had been campaigning for this all along.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[114]</a></sup></p>
<p>Which of these two policies was more effective was immediately clear: during Rolling Thunder the US maintained a 3.7:1 kill ratio over the VPAF as a whole, but the Air Force's portion of that was closer to 2:1., By 1970 the Navy's ratio had climbed to 13:1. The Air Force, however, saw its ratio stagnate and actually decrease, for a short time being less than one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[115]</a></sup> More critically, in 1970 the VPAF inflicted a kill on the USAF every three times they tried, while it took six missions to do the same against the Navy, and inversely, the VPAF lost a MiG every two engagements with the Air Force, but every time they engaged the Navy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[116]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was not until <a href="/wiki/Operation_Linebacker" title="Operation Linebacker">Operation Linebacker</a> in 1972 that the problem became acute enough for the Air Force to finally take note. In the three months following the start of Linebacker in May 1972, the U.S. lost 48 aircraft, 21 to VNAF MiGs and 27 to improved ground defenses. In the same period, only 31 MiGs were shot down by U.S. aircraft and things worsened in the summer with 13 U.S. aircraft lost to MiGs and only 11 MiGs shot down. General John Vogt, commander of the Seventh Air Force, reported to the AF Chief of Staff that they were losing the air war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[117]</a></sup> One immediate outcome was <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Teaball&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Operation Teaball (page does not exist)">Operation Teaball</a>, which reorganized the entire operational side of the Air Force's early warning systems, and tying them with the Navy's, so that every aircraft had a channel providing immediate warning of incoming aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[118]</a></sup> It was not until 1975, however, that the Air Force introduced <a href="/wiki/Exercise_Red_Flag" title="Exercise Red Flag">Exercise Red Flag</a> to match the performance of the Navy's TOPGUN.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[119]</a></sup></p>
