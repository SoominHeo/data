<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lolcat</h1>

<p>A <b>lolcat</b> (pronounced <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">LOL</span></span></b>-kat</i></a></span>) is an <a href="/wiki/Image_macro" title="Image macro">image macro</a> of one or more <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a>. The image's text is often <a href="/wiki/Idiosyncrasy" title="Idiosyncrasy">idiosyncratic</a> and grammatically incorrect, and is known as <i>lolspeak</i>.</p>
<p>Lolcat is a <a href="/wiki/Compound_(linguistics)" title="Compound (linguistics)">compound word</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acronymic" title="Acronymic">acronymic</a> abbreviation for <a href="/wiki/LOL" title="LOL">LOL (laugh out loud)</a> and the word cat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silverman_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Silverman-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-2">[2]</a></sup> A synonym for lolcat is <b>cat macro</b>, since the images are a type of <a href="/wiki/Image_macro" title="Image macro">image macro</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beacon_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-beacon-3">[3]</a></sup> Lolcats are commonly designed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photo_sharing" title="Photo sharing">photo sharing</a> <a href="/wiki/Imageboard" title="Imageboard">imageboards</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internet_forums" title="Internet forums">Internet forums</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lolcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Harry_Whittier_Frees_-_What%27s_Delaying_My_Dinner.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="575" data-file-width="381" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Harry_Whittier_Frees_-_What%27s_Delaying_My_Dinner.jpg/170px-Harry_Whittier_Frees_-_What%27s_Delaying_My_Dinner.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Harry_Whittier_Frees_-_What%27s_Delaying_My_Dinner.jpg/255px-Harry_Whittier_Frees_-_What%27s_Delaying_My_Dinner.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Harry_Whittier_Frees_-_What%27s_Delaying_My_Dinner.jpg/340px-Harry_Whittier_Frees_-_What%27s_Delaying_My_Dinner.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Harry_Whittier_Frees_-_What%27s_Delaying_My_Dinner.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1905 cat postcard by <a href="/wiki/Harry_Whittier_Frees" title="Harry Whittier Frees">Harry Whittier Frees</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>British portrait <a href="/wiki/Photographer" title="Photographer">photographer</a> Harry Pointer created a <a href="/wiki/Carte_de_visite" title="Carte de visite">carte de visite</a> series featuring cats posed in various situations in the early 1870s. To these he usually added amusing text intended to further enhance their appeal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Other notable early figures include <a href="/wiki/Harry_Whittier_Frees" title="Harry Whittier Frees">Harry Whittier Frees</a> and (using mounted animals) <a href="/wiki/Walter_Potter" title="Walter Potter">Walter Potter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first recorded use of the term "lolcat" is from the anonymous <a href="/wiki/Imageboard" title="Imageboard">imageboard</a> <a href="/wiki/4chan" title="4chan">4chan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The word "Lolcat" was in use as early as June 2006, and the domain name "LOLcats.com" was registered on June 14, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Their popularity was spread through usage on forums such as <a href="/wiki/Something_Awful" title="Something Awful">Something Awful</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_News_Journal" title="The News Journal">The News Journal</a></i> states that "some trace the lolcats back to the site 4chan, which features bizarre cat pictures on Saturdays, or 'Caturdays'." Ikenburg adds that the images have been "slinking around the Internet for years under various labels, but they did not become a sensation until early 2007 with the advent of <a href="/wiki/I_Can_Has_Cheezburger%3F" title="I Can Has Cheezburger?">I Can Has Cheezburger?</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The first image on "I CAN HAZ CHEEZBURGER?" was posted on January 11, 2007, and was allegedly from the <a href="/wiki/Something_Awful" title="Something Awful">Something Awful</a> website."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Lev Grossman of <i>Time</i> wrote that the oldest known example "probably dates to 2006,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> but later corrected himself in a blog post<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> where he recapitulated the anecdotal evidence readers had sent him, placing the origin of "Caturday" and many of the images now known by a few as "lolcats" in early 2005. The domain name "caturday.com" was registered on April 30, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <i>lolcat</i> gained national media attention in the United States when it was covered by <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i>, which wrote that non-commercialized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenomena" title="Phenomena">phenomena</a> of the sort are increasingly rare, stating that lolcats have "a distinctly old-school, early 1990s, <a href="/wiki/Usenet" title="Usenet">Usenet</a> feel to [them]".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Time-17">[17]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> put them on its end-of-the-decade, "best-of" list, saying, "Da cutest distractshun of da decaid? Y, lolcats of corse! We can neber haz enuf of deez capshioned pics of cuddlie kittehs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> "Lolcat" was also a runner-up under the "Most Creative" category under the <a href="/wiki/American_Dialect_Society" title="American Dialect Society">American Dialect Society</a> Word of the Year Awards, losing out to "Googlegänger".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2014, the word <i>lolcat</i> entered the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a>, appearing in its online version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Format">Format</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lolcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Format">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lolcat_in_folder.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="333" data-file-width="442" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Lolcat_in_folder.jpg/220px-Lolcat_in_folder.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Lolcat_in_folder.jpg/330px-Lolcat_in_folder.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Lolcat_in_folder.jpg/440px-Lolcat_in_folder.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lolcat_in_folder.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A lolcat image using the "I'm in ur..." format</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Lolcat is a composite of two words, "lol" and "cat". "<a href="/wiki/LOL" title="LOL">Lol</a>" stands for "Laugh out Loud" or "Laughing out Loud"; hence, lolcats are intended to be funny and to include jokes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Lolcats images comprise a photo of a cat with a large caption characteristically set in a heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sans_serif" title="Sans serif">sans serif</a> font such as <a href="/wiki/Impact_(typeface)" title="Impact (typeface)">Impact</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arial_Black" title="Arial Black">Arial Black</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cats_has_grammar_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-cats_has_grammar-22">[22]</a></sup> The image is, on occasion, <a href="/wiki/Image_editing" title="Image editing">digitally edited</a> for effect.</p>
<p>The caption generally acts as a <a href="/wiki/Speech_balloon" title="Speech balloon">speech balloon</a> encompassing a comment from the cat, or as a description of the depicted scene. The caption is <a href="/wiki/Sensational_spelling" title="Sensational spelling">intentionally written with deviations</a> from standard English spelling and grammar,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cats_has_grammar_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-cats_has_grammar-22">[22]</a></sup> featuring "strangely-conjugated verbs, but a tendency to converge to a new set of rules in spelling and grammar".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cats_has_grammar_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-cats_has_grammar-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newitz_2007_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newitz_2007-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kitty_pidgin_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-kitty_pidgin-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (April 2011)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> The text parodies the grammar-poor <a href="/wiki/Patois" title="Patois">patois</a> stereotypically attributed to <a href="/wiki/Internet_slang" title="Internet slang">Internet slang</a>. Frequently, lolcat captions take the form of <a href="/wiki/Phrasal_template" title="Phrasal template">phrasal templates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kitty_pidgin_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-kitty_pidgin-24">[24]</a></sup> Some phrases have a known source, usually a well-known <a href="/wiki/Internet_meme" title="Internet meme">Internet meme</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/All_your_base_are_belong_to_us" title="All your base are belong to us">All your base are belong to us</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Do_not_want" title="Do not want">Do not want</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-virgin_myths_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-virgin_myths-25">[25]</a></sup> while others don't. The language of lolcats has also been likened to <a href="/wiki/Baby_talk" title="Baby talk">baby talk</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> however it draws on a variety of linguistic resources, not just the imitation of baby talk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Common themes include jokes of the form "Im in ur [noun], [verb]-ing ur [related noun]."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tampa_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-tampa-28">[28]</a></sup> Many lolcat images capture cats performing characteristically human actions or appearing to use modern technology, such as computers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>There are several well-known lolcat images and single-word captions that have spawned many variations and imitations, including "Ceiling Cat" (see below). Others include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fail_(Internet_meme)" title="Fail (Internet meme)">Fail</a> (a cat with a slice of <a href="/wiki/Processed_cheese" title="Processed cheese">processed cheese</a> on its face)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_Can_Has_Cheezburger" title="I Can Has Cheezburger">I Can Has Cheezburger</a>" (a portrait of a blue <a href="/wiki/British_Shorthair" title="British Shorthair">British Shorthair</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Another popular format is "[Adjective] cat is [adjective/noun]."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recurring_characters">Recurring characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lolcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recurring characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Ceiling Cat" is a character spawned by the meme. The original image was an <a href="/wiki/Image_macro" title="Image macro">image macro</a> with a picture of a cat looking out of a hole in a ceiling, captioned "Ceiling Cat is watching you <a href="/wiki/Masturbation" title="Masturbation">masturbate</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> There followed numerous image macros with the format "Ceiling Cat is watching you [<i>verb</i> ending in/rhyming with -ate]" with Ceiling Cat superimposed in the upper left hand corner of an image macro depicting the appropriate action. The underlying theme is that the cat is looking down on you, almost in a form of judgement. The character is also featured in a <a href="/wiki/LOLCat_Bible_Translation_Project" title="LOLCat Bible Translation Project">project to translate the Bible to <i>lolspeak</i></a>. "Ceiling Cat" and the corresponding "Basement Cat" (a <a href="/wiki/Black_cat" title="Black cat">black cat</a> who lives in the basement) represent good and evil (sometimes God and Satan) in the lolcat universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Offshoots_and_parodies">Offshoots and parodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lolcat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Offshoots and parodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Variations on the lolcat concept include captioning photos of other animals in a similar style (e.g. <i>loldogs</i> for dogs, etc.).</p>
<p>The syntax of lolcat captions was used as the basis for <a href="/wiki/LOLCODE" title="LOLCODE">LOLCODE</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Esoteric_programming_language" title="Esoteric programming language">esoteric programming language</a> with interpreters and compilers available in <a href="/wiki/.NET_Framework" title=".NET Framework">.NET Framework</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perl" title="Perl">Perl</a>, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silverman_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Silverman-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the game Minecraft, there is an option to change language settings to LOLCAT.</p>
