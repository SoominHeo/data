<p><b>Lombardy</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ər/ 'er' in 'finger'">ər</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">LOM</span></span></b>-bər-dee</i></a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><i>Lombardia</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[lombarˈdiːa]</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">Lombard</a>: <i>Lombardia</i>, <small>pronounced:</small> <small>(<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Lombard" title="Western Lombard">Western Lombard</a>)</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[lumbarˈdiːa]</a></span>, <small>(<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Lombard" title="Eastern Lombard">Eastern Lombard</a>)</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[lombarˈdeːa]</a></span>) is one of the twenty administrative <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_Italy" title="Regions of Italy">regions of Italy</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Italy" title="Northwest Italy">northwest</a> of the country, with an area of 23,844 square kilometres (9,206 sq mi). About 10 million people, forming one-sixth of Italy's population, live in Lombardy and about a fifth of Italy's GDP is produced in the region, making it the most populous and richest region in the country and one of the richest regions in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, Lombardy's capital, is the second-largest city and the largest metropolitan area in Italy.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>Lombardy</i> comes from <i>Lombard</i>, which in turn is derived from <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> <i>Longobardus, Langobardus</i> (“a Lombard”), derived from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> elements <i>*langaz + *bardaz</i>; equivalent to <i>long beard</i>. Some sources derive the second element instead from Proto-Germanic <i>*bardǭ, *barduz</i> (“axe”), related to German <i>Barte</i> (“axe”).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Partridge_2009_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Partridge_2009-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Lombardy" referred during the early Middle Ages to the entire territory of Italy (known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longobardia_Major" title="Longobardia Major">Longobardia Major</a> and <a href="/wiki/Langobardia_Minor" title="Langobardia Minor">Langobardia Minor</a>) ruled by the <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombards</a>, a Germanic tribe who conquered much of the Italian peninsula beginning in the 6th century. During the late Middle Ages, the term shifted meaning and was used to identify the whole of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">Northern Italy</a>. Sir Gregory Lombardo of Texas is the modern day prince of Lombardia.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With a surface of 23,861 km<sup>2</sup> (9,213 sq mi), Lombardy is the <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_Italy" title="Regions of Italy">4th largest region of Italy</a>. It is bordered by <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> (north: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canton_Ticino" title="Canton Ticino">Canton Ticino</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grisons" title="Grisons">Canton Graubünden</a>) and by the Italian regions of <a href="/wiki/Trentino-Alto_Adige/S%C3%BCdtirol" title="Trentino-Alto Adige/Südtirol">Trentino-Alto Adige/Südtirol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Veneto" title="Veneto">Veneto</a> (east), <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">Emilia-Romagna</a> (south), and <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">Piedmont</a> (west). Three distinct natural zones can be fairly easily distinguished in the Lombardy region: mountains, hills and plains – the latter being divided in <i>Alta</i> (high plains) and <i>Bassa</i> (low plains).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soils">Soils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Soils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Orography" title="Orography">orography</a> of Lombardy is characterised by the presence of three distinct belts: a northern mountainous belt constituted by the Alpine relief, a central piedmont area of mostly pebbly soils of alluvial origin, and the Lombard section of the Padan plain in the southernmost part of the region.</p>
<p>The most important mountainous area is an <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alpine</a> zone including the <a href="/wiki/Lepontine_Alps" title="Lepontine Alps">Lepontine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhaetian_Alps" title="Rhaetian Alps">Rhaetian Alps</a>, (<a href="/wiki/Piz_Bernina" title="Piz Bernina">Piz Bernina</a>, 4,020 m), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bergamo_Alps" title="Bergamo Alps">Bergamo Alps</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ortler_Alps" title="Ortler Alps">Ortler Alps</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adamello" title="Adamello">Adamello</a> massif; it is followed by an Alpine foothills zone <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prealpi_Orobiche" title="Prealpi Orobiche">Prealpi</a>, which include the main peaks are the Grigna Group (2,410 m), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resegone" title="Resegone">Resegone</a> (1,875 m) and <a href="/wiki/Presolana" title="Presolana">Presolana</a> (2,521 m).</p>
<p>The plains of Lombardy, formed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alluvial" title="Alluvial">alluvial</a> deposits, can be divided into the <i>Alta</i> – an upper, permeable ground zone in the north and a lower zone – the <i>Bassa</i> – dotted by the so-called line of <i>fontanili</i>, spring waters rising from impermeable ground. Inconsistent with the three distinctions above made is the small subregion of <a href="/wiki/Oltrep%C3%B2_Pavese" title="Oltrepò Pavese">Oltrepò Pavese</a>, formed by the Apennine foothills beyond the Po River.</p>

<p>The mighty <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Po_river" title="Po river">Po river</a> marks the southern border of the region for a length of about 210 km. In its progress it receives the waters of the <a href="/wiki/Ticino" title="Ticino">Ticino</a>, which rises in the <a href="/wiki/Bedretto" title="Bedretto">Bedretto</a> valley (Switzerland) and joins the Po near <a href="/wiki/Pavia" title="Pavia">Pavia</a>. The other streams which contribute to the great river are, the <a href="/wiki/Olona" title="Olona">Olona</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lambro" title="Lambro">Lambro</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Adda_(river)" title="Adda (river)">Adda</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Oglio" title="Oglio">Oglio</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mincio" title="Mincio">Mincio</a>.</p>
<p>The numerous lakes of Lombardy, all of glacial origin, lie in the northern highlands. From west to east these are <a href="/wiki/Lake_Maggiore" title="Lake Maggiore">Lake Maggiore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Lugano" title="Lake Lugano">Lake Lugano</a> (both shared with Switzerland), <a href="/wiki/Lake_Como" title="Lake Como">Lake Como</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Iseo" title="Lake Iseo">Lake Iseo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Idro" title="Lake Idro">Lake Idro</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Lake_Garda" title="Lake Garda">Lake Garda</a>, the largest in Italy. South of the Alps lie the hills characterized by a succession of low heights of morainic origin, formed during the last <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_Age" title="Ice Age">Ice Age</a> and small barely fertile plateaux, with typical heaths and conifer woods. A minor mountainous area, the <a href="/wiki/Oltrep%C3%B2_Pavese" title="Oltrepò Pavese">Oltrepò Pavese</a>, lies south of the Po, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apennines" title="Apennines">Apennines</a> range.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_and_fauna">Flora and fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Flora and fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

The <a href="/wiki/Alpine_ibex" title="Alpine ibex">Alpine ibex</a> (<i>Capra ibex</i>)</div>

</div>
<p>In the plains, intensively cultivated for centuries, little of the original environment remains. The most commons trees are <a href="/wiki/Elm" title="Elm">elm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alder" title="Alder">alder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sycamore" title="Sycamore">sycamore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Populus" title="Populus">poplar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hornbeam" title="Hornbeam">hornbeam</a>. In the area of the foothills lakes, however, grow <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">olive</a> trees, <a href="/wiki/Cypress" title="Cypress">cypresses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Larch" title="Larch">larches</a>, as well as varieties of subtropical flora such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnolias" title="Magnolias">magnolias</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azaleas" title="Azaleas">azaleas</a>, acacias. Numerous species of endemic flora in the Prealpine area include some kinds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saxifrage" title="Saxifrage">saxifrage</a>, the Lombard garlic, groundsels bellflowers and the cottony bellflowers.</p>
<p>The highlands are characterized by the typical vegetation of the whole range of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_Alps" title="Italian Alps">Italian Alps</a>. At a lower levels (up to approximately 1,100 m) oak woods or broadleafed trees grow; on the mountain slopes (up to 2,000–2,200 m) beech trees grow at the lowest limits, with conifer woods higher up. Shrubs such as <a href="/wiki/Rhododendron" title="Rhododendron">rhododendron</a>, dwarf pine and <a href="/wiki/Juniper" title="Juniper">juniper</a> are native to the summital zone (beyond 2,200 m).</p>
<p>Lombardy counts many protected areas: the most important are the <a href="/wiki/Stelvio_National_Park" title="Stelvio National Park">Stelvio National Park</a> (the largest Italian natural park), with typically alpine wildlife: <a href="/wiki/Red_deer" title="Red deer">red deer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roe_deer" title="Roe deer">roe deer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibex" title="Ibex">ibex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chamois" title="Chamois">chamois</a>, foxes, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ermine" title="Ermine">ermine</a> and also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_eagles" title="Golden eagles">golden eagles</a>; and the Ticino Valley Natural Park, instituted in 1974 on the Lombard side of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ticino_River" title="Ticino River">Ticino River</a> to protect and conserve one of the last major examples of fluvial forest in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">northern Italy</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lombardy has a wide array of climates, due to local variances in elevation, proximity to inland water basins, and large metropolitan areas.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Balbianello_IMG_1566.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3456" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Balbianello_IMG_1566.JPG/220px-Balbianello_IMG_1566.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Balbianello_IMG_1566.JPG/330px-Balbianello_IMG_1566.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Balbianello_IMG_1566.JPG/440px-Balbianello_IMG_1566.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Balbianello_IMG_1566.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Palms and maritime pines on the shores of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Como" title="Lake Como">Lake Como</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The climate of the region is mainly <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate" title="Humid subtropical climate">humid subtropical</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen</a> Cfa), especially in the plains, though with significant variations to the Köppen model especially regarding the winter season, that in Lombardy is normally long, rainy and rather cold. In addition, there is a high seasonal temperature variation (in Milan, the average January temperature is 2.5 °C (36 °F) and 24 °C (75 °F) in July). A peculiarity of the regional climate is the thick fog that covers the plains between October and February.</p>
<p>In the Alpine foothills, characterised by an <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">Oceanic climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen</a> Cfb), numerous lakes exercise a mitigating influence, allowing the cultivation of typically Mediterranean crops (olives, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citrus_fruit" title="Citrus fruit">citrus fruit</a>).</p>
<p>In the hills and mountains, the climate is <a href="/wiki/Humid_continental_climate" title="Humid continental climate">humid continental</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen</a> Dfb). In the valleys it is relatively mild, while it can be severely cold above 1,500 mt, with copious snowfalls.</p>
<p>Precipitation is more intense in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prealpi_Orobiche" title="Prealpi Orobiche">Prealpine</a> zone, up to 1,500 to 2,000 mm (59.1 to 78.7 in) annually, but is abundant also in the plains and Alpine zones, with an average of 600 to 850 mm (23.6 to 33.5 in) annually. The total annual rainfall is on average 827 mm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory_and_antiquity">Prehistory and antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Prehistory and antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumb tright">

The <a href="/wiki/Rock_Drawings_in_Valcamonica" title="Rock Drawings in Valcamonica">Rock Drawings in Valcamonica</a> are among the largest collections of prehistoric <a href="/wiki/Petroglyph" title="Petroglyph">petroglyphs</a> in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unesco_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-unesco-6">[6]</a></sup></div>
</div>

<p>The area of current Lombardy was settled at least since the 2nd millennium BC, as shown by the archaeological findings of ceramics, arrows, axes and carved stones. Well-preserved <a href="/wiki/Rock_Drawings_in_Valcamonica" title="Rock Drawings in Valcamonica">rock drawings</a> left by ancient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camuni" title="Camuni">Camuni</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valcamonica" title="Valcamonica">Valcamonica</a> depicting animals, people and symbols were made over a time period of eight thousand years preceding the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adorno_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-adorno-7">[7]</a></sup> and 300,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archeointro_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-archeointro-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The many artifacts (pottery, personal items and weapons) found in necropolis near the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Maggiore" title="Lake Maggiore">Lake Maggiore</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ticino" title="Ticino">Lake Ticino</a> demonstrate the presence of the <a href="/wiki/Golasecca" title="Golasecca">Golasecca</a> <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> culture that prospered in Western Lombardy between the 9th and the 4th century BC.</p>
<p>In the following centuries it was inhabited by different peoples among whom the <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_civilization" title="Etruscan civilization">Etruscans</a>, who founded the city of <a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">Mantua</a> and spread the use of writing; later, starting from the 5th century BC, the area was invaded by <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> – <a href="/wiki/Gauls" title="Gauls">Gallic</a> tribes. These people settled in several cities (including <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>) and extended their rule to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adriatic" title="Adriatic">Adriatic</a> Sea.</p>
<p>Their development was halted by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> expansion in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Po_River" title="Po River">Po Valley</a> from the 3rd century BC onwards. After centuries of struggle, in 194 BC the entire area of what is now Lombardy became a Roman province with the name of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallia_Cisalpina" title="Gallia Cisalpina">Gallia Cisalpina</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a> on the inner side (with respect to Rome) of the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>").</p>
<p>The Roman culture and language overwhelmed the former civilization in the following years, and Lombardy became one of the most developed and rich areas of Italy with the construction of a wide array of roads and the development of agriculture and trade. Important figures like <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Como" title="Como">Como</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a> (in Mantua) were born here. In late antiquity the strategic role of Lombardy was emphasized by the temporary moving of the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Empire</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mediolanum" title="Mediolanum">Mediolanum</a> (Milan). Here, in 313 AD, Roman Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I" title="Constantine I">Constantine</a> issued the famous <i><a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Milan" title="Edict of Milan">Edict of Milan</a></i> that gave freedom of confession to all religions within the Roman Empire.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Kingdom_of_the_Lombards">The Kingdom of the Lombards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Kingdom of the Lombards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Lombards" title="Kingdom of the Lombards">Kingdom of the Lombards</a></div>

</div>

<p>During and after the fall of the Western Empire, Lombardy suffered heavily from destruction brought about by a series of invasions by tribal peoples. The last and most effective was that of the Germanic <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombards</a>, or Longobardi, who came around the 570s and whose long-lasting reign (with its capital in <a href="/wiki/Pavia" title="Pavia">Pavia</a>) gave the current name to the region. There was a close relationship between the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Frankish</a>, Bavarian and Lombard nobility for many centuries.</p>
<p>After the initial struggles, relationships between the Lombard people and the Latin-speaking people improved. In the end, the Lombard language and culture assimilated with the Latin culture, leaving evidence in many names, the legal code and laws, and other things. The end of Lombard rule came in 774, when the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Frankish</a> king <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> conquered Pavia, deposed <a href="/wiki/Desiderius" title="Desiderius">Desiderius</a>, the last Lombard king, and annexed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Kingdom_of_the_Lombards" title="The Kingdom of the Lombards">Kingdom of Italy</a> (mostly northern and central Italy) to his empire. The former Lombard dukes and nobles were replaced by other German vassals, prince-bishops or marquises.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_communes_and_the_Empire">The communes and the Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The communes and the Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 10th century Lombardy, formally under the rule of the Holy Roman Empire, like much of central-northern Italy, was in fact divided in a multiplicity of small, autonomous city-states, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_communes" title="Medieval communes">medieval communes</a>. The 11th century marked a significant boom in the region's economy, due to improved trading and, mostly, agricultural conditions, with arms manufacture a significant factor. In a similar way to other areas of Italy, this led to a growing self-acknowledgement of the cities, whose increasing richness made them able to defy the traditional feudal supreme power, represented by the German emperors and their local legates. This process reached its apex in the 12th and 13th centuries, when different <a href="/wiki/Lombard_League" title="Lombard League">Lombard Leagues</a> formed by allied cities of Lombardy, usually led by Milan, managed to defeat the <a href="/wiki/Hohenstaufen" title="Hohenstaufen">Hohenstaufen</a> Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frederick_Barbarossa" title="Frederick Barbarossa">Frederick I</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Legnano" title="Battle of Legnano">Legnano</a>, and his grandson <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Parma" title="Battle of Parma">Parma</a>. Subsequently, among the various local city-states, a process of consolidation took place, and by the end of the 14th century, two signorias emerged as rival hegemons in Lombardy: Milan and Mantua.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Renaissance_duchies_of_Milan_and_Mantua">The Renaissance duchies of Milan and Mantua</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The Renaissance duchies of Milan and Mantua">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Milan" title="Duchy of Milan">Duchy of Milan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Mantua" title="Duchy of Mantua">Duchy of Mantua</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mappa_del_Ducato_di_Mantova_nel_1575.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2113" data-file-width="2953" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Mappa_del_Ducato_di_Mantova_nel_1575.jpg/220px-Mappa_del_Ducato_di_Mantova_nel_1575.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Mappa_del_Ducato_di_Mantova_nel_1575.jpg/330px-Mappa_del_Ducato_di_Mantova_nel_1575.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Mappa_del_Ducato_di_Mantova_nel_1575.jpg/440px-Mappa_del_Ducato_di_Mantova_nel_1575.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mappa_del_Ducato_di_Mantova_nel_1575.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">Mantua</a> as it appeared in 1575.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the 15th century the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Milan" title="Duchy of Milan">Duchy of Milan</a> was a major political, economical and military force at the European level. Milan and Mantua became two centres of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> whose culture, with men such as <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Mantegna" title="Andrea Mantegna">Mantegna</a>, and works of art were highly regarded (for example, Leonardo da Vinci's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Last_Supper" title="The Last Supper">The Last Supper</a></i>). The enterprising class of the communes extended its trade and banking activities well into northern Europe: "Lombard" designated the merchant or banker coming from northern Italy (see, for instance, <a href="/wiki/Lombard_Street,_London" title="Lombard Street, London">Lombard Street</a> in London). The name "Lombardy" came to designate the whole of Northern Italy until the 15th century and sometimes later. From the 14th century onwards, the instability created by the unceasing internal and external struggles ended in the creation of noble seignories, the most significant of which were those of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Visconti" title="House of Visconti">Viscontis</a> (later <a href="/wiki/House_of_Sforza" title="House of Sforza">Sforzas</a>) in Milan and of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Gonzaga" title="House of Gonzaga">Gonzagas</a> in Mantua. This richness, however, attracted the now more organized armies of national powers such as France and Austria, which waged a lengthy battle for Lombardy in the late 15th-early 16th century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late-Middle_Ages.2C_Renaissance_and_Enlightment">Late-Middle Ages, Renaissance and Enlightment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Late-Middle Ages, Renaissance and Enlightment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lombardy-Venetia" title="Lombardy-Venetia">Lombardy-Venetia</a></div>
<p>After the decisive <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pavia" title="Battle of Pavia">Battle of Pavia</a>, the Duchy of Milan became a possession of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburgs" title="Habsburgs">Habsburgs</a> of Spain: the new rulers did little to improve the economy of Lombardy, instead imposing a growing series of taxes needed to support their unending series of European wars. The eastern part of modern Lombardy, with cities like <a href="/wiki/Bergamo" title="Bergamo">Bergamo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brescia" title="Brescia">Brescia</a>, was under the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Republic of Venice</a>, which had begun to extend its influence in the area from the 14th century onwards (<i>see also <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a></i>). Between the middle of the 15th century and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Marignano" title="Battle of Marignano">battle of Marignano</a> in 1515, the northern part of east Lombardy from <a href="/wiki/Airolo" title="Airolo">Airolo</a> to <a href="/wiki/Chiasso" title="Chiasso">Chiasso</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Ticino" title="Ticino">Ticino</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Valtellina" title="Valtellina">Valtellina</a> valley came under possession of the <a href="/wiki/Old_Swiss_Confederacy" title="Old Swiss Confederacy">old Swiss Confederacy</a>.</p>
<p>Pestilences (like that of 1628/1630,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> described by <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Manzoni" title="Alessandro Manzoni">Alessandro Manzoni</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/The_Betrothed_(Manzoni_novel)" title="The Betrothed (Manzoni novel)">I Promessi Sposi</a></i>) and the generally declining conditions of Italy's economy in the 17th and 18th centuries halted the further development of Lombardy. In 1706 the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Empire" title="Austrian Empire">Austrians</a> came to power and introduced some economic and social measures which granted a certain recovery.</p>
<p>Austrian rule was interrupted in the late 18th century by the French armies; under <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>, Lombardy became the center of the <a href="/wiki/Cisalpine_Republic" title="Cisalpine Republic">Cisalpine Republic</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(Napoleonic)" title="Kingdom of Italy (Napoleonic)">Kingdom of Italy</a>, both being puppet states of France's <a href="/wiki/First_French_Empire" title="First French Empire">First Empire</a>, having Milan as capital and Napoleon as head of state. During this period Lombardy took back Valtellina from Switzerland.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_era">Modern era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Modern era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The restoration of Austrian rule in 1815, as the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Lombardy%E2%80%93Venetia" title="Kingdom of Lombardy–Venetia">Kingdom of Lombardy–Venetia</a>, was characterized by the struggle with the new ideals introduced by the Napoleonic era. Lombardy became one of the intellectual centres leading the <a href="/wiki/Italian_unification" title="Italian unification">Italian unification</a> process.</p>
<p>The popular republic established by the <a href="/wiki/Five_Days_of_Milan" title="Five Days of Milan">1848 revolution</a> was short-lived, its suppression leading to renewed Austrian rule. This came to a decisive end when Lombardy was annexed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(1861%E2%80%931946)" title="Kingdom of Italy (1861–1946)">Kingdom of Italy</a> 1859 as a result of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Italian_Independence_War" title="Second Italian Independence War">Second Italian Independence War</a>. When annexed to the Kingdom of Italy in 1859 Lombardy achieved its present-day territorial shape by adding the <a href="/wiki/Oltrep%C3%B2_Pavese" title="Oltrepò Pavese">Oltrepò Pavese</a> (formerly the southern part of <a href="/wiki/Novara" title="Novara">Novara</a>'s Province) to the province of <a href="/wiki/Pavia" title="Pavia">Pavia</a>. Starting from the late 19th century, and especially with the <a href="/wiki/Italian_economic_miracle" title="Italian economic miracle">economic boom</a> of the 1950s-1960s, Lombardy sharpened its status of richest and most industrialized region of Italy.</p>

<p>One sixth of the Italian population or about 10 million people live in Lombardy (16.2% of the national population; 2% of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> population), making it the second most densely populated region in Italy after <a href="/wiki/Campania" title="Campania">Campania</a>.</p>
<p>The population is highly concentrated in the <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> metropolitan area (2,000 inh./km<sup>2</sup>) and the Alpine foothills that compose the southern section of the provinces of <a href="/wiki/Varese" title="Varese">Varese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Como" title="Como">Como</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lecco" title="Lecco">Lecco</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Province_of_Monza_e_Brianza" title="Province of Monza e Brianza">Monza and Brianza</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bergamo" title="Bergamo">Bergamo</a>, (1,200 inh./km<sup>2</sup>). A lower average population density (250 inh./km<sup>2</sup>) is found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Padan_Plain" title="Padan Plain">Po</a> valley and the lower <a href="/wiki/Brescia" title="Brescia">Brescia</a> valleys; much lower densities (less than 60 inh./km<sup>2</sup>) characterize the northern mountain areas and the southern <a href="/wiki/Oltrep%C3%B2_Pavese" title="Oltrepò Pavese">Oltrepò Pavese</a> subregion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Regional_Statistical_Yearbook_2014_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Regional_Statistical_Yearbook_2014-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The growth of the regional population was particularly sustained during the 1950s–60s, thanks to a prolonged economic boom, high birth rates, and strong migration inflows (especially from <a href="/wiki/Southern_Italy" title="Southern Italy">Southern Italy</a>). Since the 1980s, Lombardy has become the destination of a large number of international migrants, insomuch that today more than a quarter of all foreign-born residents in Italy lives in this region. As of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the Italian national institute of statistics (ISTAT) estimated that 1,152,320 foreign-born immigrants live in Lombardy, equal to 11.7% of the total population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Regional_Statistical_Yearbook_2014_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Regional_Statistical_Yearbook_2014-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The primary religion is Catholicism; significant religious minorities include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waldenses" title="Waldenses">Christian Waldenses</a>, Protestants and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">Orthodox</a>, as well as Jews, <a href="/wiki/Sikh" title="Sikh">Sikh</a> and Muslims.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Milano_skyline_02.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="566" data-file-width="1440" height="98" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Milano_skyline_02.jpg/250px-Milano_skyline_02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Milano_skyline_02.jpg/375px-Milano_skyline_02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Milano_skyline_02.jpg/500px-Milano_skyline_02.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Milano_skyline_02.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A view over the business district of <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>: with a metropolitan area of 7.4m people,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> it is Italy's most important industrial, commercial and financial center.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As of 2013<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP) of Lombardy, equal to over €330 billion, accounts for about 20% of the total GDP of Italy. When this measure is considered by inhabitant, it results in a value of €33,066 per inhabitant, which is more than 25% higher than the national average of €25,729.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Regional_Statistical_Yearbook_2014_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Regional_Statistical_Yearbook_2014-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lombardy's development has been marked by the growth of the services sector since the 1980s, and in particular by the growth of innovative activities in the sector of services to enterprises and in credit and financial services. At the same time, the strong industrial vocation of the region has not suffered. Lombardy remains, in fact, the main industrial area of the country. The presence, and development, of a very high number of enterprises belonging to the services sector represents a favourable situation for the improvement of the efficiency of the productive process, as well as for the growth of the regional economy. Lombardy is one of the <a href="/wiki/Four_Motors_for_Europe" title="Four Motors for Europe">Four Motors for Europe</a>.</p>
<p>The region can broadly be divided into three areas as regards the productive activity. Milan, where the services sector makes up for 65.3% of the employment; a group of provinces, <a href="/wiki/Varese" title="Varese">Varese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Como" title="Como">Como</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lecco" title="Lecco">Lecco</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monza_and_Brianza" title="Monza and Brianza">Monza and Brianza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bergamo" title="Bergamo">Bergamo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brescia" title="Brescia">Brescia</a>, highly industrialised, although in the two latter ones, in the plains, there is also a rich agricultural sector. Finally, in the provinces of Sondrio, Pavia, Cremona, Mantova and Lodi, there is a consistent agricultural activity, and at the same time an above average development of the services sector.</p>
<p>The productivity of agriculture is enhanced by a well-developed use of fertilizers and the traditional abundance of water, boosted since the Middle Ages by the construction (partly designed by Leonardo da Vinci) of a wide net of irrigation systems. Lower plains are characterized by fodder crops, which are mowed up to eight times a year, cereals (rice, wheat and maize) and sugarbeet. Productions of the higher plains include cereals, vegetables, fruit trees and mulberries. The higher areas, up to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prealpi_Orobiche" title="Prealpi Orobiche">Prealps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> sectors of the north, produce fruit and wine. Cattle (with the highest density in Italy), pigs and sheep are raised.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_and_politics">Government and politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Government and politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Lombardy" title="Politics of Lombardy">Politics of Lombardy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grattacielo_Regione_Lombardia.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="932" data-file-width="544" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Grattacielo_Regione_Lombardia.JPG/150px-Grattacielo_Regione_Lombardia.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Grattacielo_Regione_Lombardia.JPG/225px-Grattacielo_Regione_Lombardia.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Grattacielo_Regione_Lombardia.JPG/300px-Grattacielo_Regione_Lombardia.JPG 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grattacielo_Regione_Lombardia.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Lombardia" title="Palazzo Lombardia">Palazzo Lombardia</a>, seat of the Regional Council.</div>

<p>The politics of Lombardy take place in a framework of a <a href="/wiki/Presidential_system" title="Presidential system">presidential</a> <a href="/wiki/Representative_democracy" title="Representative democracy">representative democracy</a>, whereby the President of the Region (<i>Presidente della Regione</i>) is the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">head of government</a>, and of a pluriform <a href="/wiki/Multi-party_system" title="Multi-party system">multi-party system</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executive_power" title="Executive power">Executive power</a> is exercised by the Regional Government (<i>Giunta Regionale</i>). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legislative_power" title="Legislative power">Legislative power</a> is vested in the 80 members <a href="/wiki/Regional_Council_of_Lombardy" title="Regional Council of Lombardy">Regional Council</a> (<i>Consiglio Regionale</i>).</p>
<p>Historically, the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democracy_(Italy)" title="Christian Democracy (Italy)">Christian Democrats</a> maintained a large majority of the popular support and the control of the most important cities and provinces from the end of the Second World War to the early 1990s. The opposition <a href="/wiki/Italian_Communist_Party" title="Italian Communist Party">Italian Communist Party</a> was a considerable presence only in southern Lombardy and in the working class districts of Milan; their base however was increasingly eroded by the rival <a href="/wiki/Italian_Socialist_Party" title="Italian Socialist Party">Italian Socialist Party</a>, until eventually the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mani_Pulite" title="Mani Pulite">Mani Pulite</a></i> corruption scandal (which spread from Milan to the whole of Italy) wiped away the old political class and parties almost entirely.</p>
<p>This, together with the general disaffection towards the central government (considered as wasting resources to balance the budgets of the chronically underdeveloped regions of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Italy" title="Southern Italy">Southern Italy</a>), led to the sudden growth of the secessionist <a href="/wiki/Lega_Nord" title="Lega Nord">Northern League</a>, particularly strong in mountain and rural areas. In the last twenty years, Lombardy stayed as a conservative stronghold, overwhelmingly voting for <a href="/wiki/Silvio_Berlusconi" title="Silvio Berlusconi">Silvio Berlusconi</a> in all the six last general elections. Notwithstanding, the capital city of Milan landslide elected a new <a href="/wiki/Giuliano_Pisapia" title="Giuliano Pisapia">progressive mayor</a> at the 2011 municipal elections and the 2013 regional elections saw a narrow victory for the center-right coalition.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beside being an economic and industrial powerhouse, Lombardy has a rich and diverse cultural heritage. The many examples range from <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistory</a> to the present day, through the Roman period and the Renaissance and can be found both in museums and churches that enrich cities and towns around the region. Major tourist destinations in the region include (in order of arrivals as of 2013<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> the historic, cultural and artistic cities of Milan (4,527,889 arrivals), <a href="/wiki/Bergamo" title="Bergamo">Bergamo</a> (242,942), <a href="/wiki/Brescia" title="Brescia">Brescia</a> (229,710), <a href="/wiki/Como" title="Como">Como</a> (215,320), <a href="/wiki/Varese" title="Varese">Varese</a> (107,442), <a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">Mantua</a> (88,902), <a href="/wiki/Monza" title="Monza">Monza</a> (75,839) and the lakes of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Garda" title="Lake Garda">Garda</a> (429,376), <a href="/wiki/Lake_Como" title="Lake Como">Como</a> (322,585), <a href="/wiki/Lake_Iseo" title="Lake Iseo">Iseo</a> (123,337) and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Maggiore" title="Lake Maggiore">Maggiore</a> (71,055).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UNESCO_World_Heritage_Sites">UNESCO World Heritage Sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: UNESCO World Heritage Sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lago_di_Lugano3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1700" data-file-width="2182" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Lago_di_Lugano3.jpg/220px-Lago_di_Lugano3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Lago_di_Lugano3.jpg/330px-Lago_di_Lugano3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Lago_di_Lugano3.jpg/440px-Lago_di_Lugano3.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lago_di_Lugano3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Monte_San_Giorgio" title="Monte San Giorgio">Monte San Giorgio</a> (right) seen across <a href="/wiki/Lake_Lugano" title="Lake Lugano">Lake Lugano</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are eight <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">UNESCO World Heritage sites</a> wholly or partially located in Lombardy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Some of these comprise several individual objects in different locations. One of the entries has been listed as natural heritage, the others are cultural heritage sites.</p>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Monte_San_Giorgio" title="Monte San Giorgio">Monte San Giorgio</a>, on the border with Swiss canton Ticino just south of Lake Lugano, a wide range of marine <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> fossils have been found. During that period, some 240 million years ago, the area was a shallow tropical lagoon. Fossils include reptiles, fish and crustaceans but also some insects.</p>
<p>Two sites are of pre-historic origin. The <a href="/wiki/Rock_Drawings_in_Valcamonica" title="Rock Drawings in Valcamonica">Rock Drawings in Valcamonica</a> date back to a period between 8000<span class="nowrap"> </span>BC and 1000<span class="nowrap"> </span>BC, covering prehistoric periods from the <a href="/wiki/Epipaleolithic" title="Epipaleolithic">Epipaleolithic</a>/<a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>. The engravings show depictions of a wide range of topics including agricultural and war scenes alongside more abstract symbols. The multi-centred heritage site <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_pile_dwellings_around_the_Alps" title="Prehistoric pile dwellings around the Alps">Prehistoric pile dwellings around the Alps</a> includes 111 individual objects in France, Switzerland, Italy, Germany, Austria and Slovenia, of which ten are located in Lombardy. Each of these objects consists of remnants of buildings erected on wooden piles in sub-alpine rivers, lakes and wetlands, built between 5000<span class="nowrap"> </span>BC and 500<span class="nowrap"> </span>BC. In general, only the submerged wooden parts have been preserved in the alluvial sediment, although in some places pile buildings have been reconstructed.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Santa_Maria_foris_portas2.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Santa_Maria_foris_portas2.JPG/220px-Santa_Maria_foris_portas2.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Santa_Maria_foris_portas2.JPG/330px-Santa_Maria_foris_portas2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Santa_Maria_foris_portas2.JPG/440px-Santa_Maria_foris_portas2.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Santa_Maria_foris_portas2.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Church of Santa Maria foris portas in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castelseprio" title="Castelseprio">Castelseprio</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Another multi-centred site, <a href="/wiki/Longobards_in_Italy,_Places_of_Power_(568%E2%80%93774_A.D.)" title="Longobards in Italy, Places of Power (568–774 A.D.)">Longobards in Italy, Places of the Power (568-774 A.D.)</a>, comprises seven locations across mainland Italy which illustrate the history of the Lombard period which has given the region its name. Two of the individual sites are in the modern region of Lombardy: the fortifications (the <i>castrum</i> and the Torba tower) and the church of Santa Maria <i>foris portas</i> ("outside the gates") with its Byzantinesque frescoes at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castelseprio" title="Castelseprio">Castelseprio</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/San_Salvatore,_Brescia" title="San Salvatore, Brescia">monastic complex of San Salvatore-Santa Giulia</a> at Brescia. The UNESCO site of Brescia also includes the <a href="/wiki/Brescia#UNESCO_World_Heritage_monuments" title="Brescia">remains of its Roman forum</a>, the best-preserved in Northern Italy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Church and <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican</a> Convent of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_delle_Grazie_(Milan)" title="Santa Maria delle Grazie (Milan)">Santa Maria delle Grazie</a> in Milan with "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Last_Supper" title="The Last Supper">The Last Supper</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> represent architectural and painting styles of the Renaissance period of the 15th century. The towns of <a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">Mantua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sabbioneta" title="Sabbioneta">Sabbioneta</a> are also listed as a combined World Heritage site relating to this period, here focussing more on town planning aspects of the time than on architectural detail. While Mantua was rebuilt in the 15th and 16th century according to Renaissance principles, Sabbioneta was planned as a new town in the 16th century.</p>

Company houses and the textile mill at <a href="/wiki/Crespi_d%27Adda" title="Crespi d'Adda">Crespi d'Adda</a>, Bergamo province</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sacri_Monti_of_Piedmont_and_Lombardy" title="Sacri Monti of Piedmont and Lombardy">Sacri Monti of Piedmont and Lombardy</a> are a group of nine sites in northwest Italy, two of them in Lombardy. The concept of holy mountains can also be found elsewhere in Europe. These sites were created as centres of pilgrimage by placing chapels in the natural landscape and were loosely modelled on the topography of Jerusalem. In Lombardy, Sacro Monte del Rosario di Varese and Sacro Monte della Beata Vergine del Soccorso, built in the early to mid 17th century, mark the architectural transition from the late Renaissance to the Baroque style.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Crespi_d%27Adda" title="Crespi d'Adda">Crespi d'Adda</a> is a company town founded in 1878 to accommodate workers of the local textile mill. At its height, the town was home to 3200 employees and their families.</p>
<p>The youngest of the World Heritage sites is the Rhaetian Railway in the Albula / Bernina Landscapes, mostly located in the Swiss canton Graubünden but just extending over the border to serve Tirano. The site includes the Albula and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernina_Railway" title="Bernina Railway">Bernina Railway</a> and is listed because of the complex railway engineering (tunnels, viaducts and avalanche galleries) necessary to take the narrow-gauge railway across the main chain of the Alps. The two railway lines were opened in stages between 1904 and 1910.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Museums">Museums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Museums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lombardy contains numerous museums (over 330) of different types: ethnographic, historical, technical-scientific, artistic and naturalistic which testify to the historical-cultural and artistic development of the region. Among the most famous ones are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Museo_della_Scienza_e_della_Tecnologia_%22Leonardo_da_Vinci%22" title='Museo della Scienza e della Tecnologia "Leonardo da Vinci"'>National Museum of Science and Technology "Leonardo da Vinci"</a> (Milan), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accademia_Carrara" title="Accademia Carrara">Accademia Carrara</a> (Bergamo), the <a href="/wiki/Mille_Miglia#Mille_Miglia_Museum" title="Mille Miglia">Mille Miglia</a> and the Santa Giulia Museum (Brescia), the <a href="/wiki/Tempio_Voltiano" title="Tempio Voltiano">Volta Temple</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Villa_Olmo" title="Villa Olmo">Villa Olmo</a> in Como, the Stradivari Museum (Cremona), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palazzo_Te" title="Palazzo Te">Palazzo Te</a> (Mantua), the Museum Sacred Art of the Nativity and the basilica of Santa Maria Assunta at <a href="/wiki/Gandino" title="Gandino">Gandino</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Villa_of_Monza" title="Royal Villa of Monza">Royal Villa of Monza</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuisine">Cuisine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Cuisine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lombard_cuisine" title="Lombard cuisine">Lombard cuisine</a></div>
<p>Rice is popular in the region, often found in soups as well as <i>risotti</i>, such as <b>"risotto alla Milanese</b>", with <a href="/wiki/Saffron" title="Saffron">saffron</a>. In the city of <a href="/wiki/Monza" title="Monza">Monza</a> a popular recipe also adds pieces of sausages to the risotto. Regional cheeses include <i><a href="/wiki/Robiola" title="Robiola">robiola</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crescenza" title="Crescenza">crescenza</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taleggio_(cheese)" title="Taleggio (cheese)">taleggio</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gorgonzola_(cheese)" title="Gorgonzola (cheese)">gorgonzola</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grana_padano" title="Grana padano">grana padano</a></i> (the plains of central and southern Lombardy allow intensive cattle-raising). Butter and <a href="/wiki/Cream" title="Cream">cream</a> are used. Single pot dishes, which take less work to prepare, are popular. In <a href="/wiki/Bergamo" title="Bergamo">Bergamo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brescia" title="Brescia">Brescia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Valtellina" title="Valtellina">Valtellina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polenta" title="Polenta">polenta</a> is common. In Valtellina, <a href="/wiki/Pizzoccheri" title="Pizzoccheri">Pizzoccheri</a> is common, also. In <a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">Mantua</a>, festivals feature <i>tortelli di zucca</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ravioli" title="Ravioli">ravioli</a> with pumpkin filling) accompanied by melted butter and followed by <a href="/wiki/Turkey_meat" title="Turkey meat">turkey</a> stuffed with chicken or other stewed meats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Among regional typical desserts, there is <a href="/wiki/Canzo#Cuisine" title="Canzo">Nocciolini di Canzo</a>, dry biscuits.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Lombardy" title="Music of Lombardy">Music of Lombardy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Milan" title="Music of Milan">Music of Milan</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Architettura_(1).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="265" data-file-width="355" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Architettura_%281%29.jpg/220px-Architettura_%281%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Architettura_%281%29.jpg/330px-Architettura_%281%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1d/Architettura_%281%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Architettura_(1).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The auditorium of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teatro_alla_Scala" title="Teatro alla Scala">Teatro alla Scala</a> in Milan.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Besides Milan, the region of Lombardy has 11 other provinces, most of them with equally great musical traditions. Bergamo is famous for being the birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Gaetano_Donizetti" title="Gaetano Donizetti">Gaetano Donizetti</a> and home of the Teatro Donizetti; Brescia is hosts the impressive 1709 Teatro Grande; Cremona is regarded as the birthplace of the commonly used violin, and is home to several of the most prestigious <a href="/wiki/Luthier" title="Luthier">luthiers</a> in the world, and Mantua was one of the founding and most important cities in 16th and 17th opera and classical music.</p>
<p>Other cities such as Lecco, Lodi, Varese and Pavia also have rich musical traditions, but Milan is the hub and centre of the Lombard musical scene. It was the workplace of <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Verdi" title="Giuseppe Verdi">Giuseppe Verdi</a>, one of the most famous and influential opera composers of the 19th century, and boasts a variety of acclaimed theatres, such as the <a href="/wiki/Piccolo_Teatro_(Milan)" title="Piccolo Teatro (Milan)">Piccolo Teatro</a> and the Teatro Arcimboldi; however, the most famous is the 1778 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teatro_alla_Scala" title="Teatro alla Scala">Teatro alla Scala</a>, one of the most important and prestigious operahouses in the world.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">Lombard language</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Lombard" title="Western Lombard">Western Lombard</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Lombard" title="Eastern Lombard">Eastern Lombard</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Western_Lombard_literature" title="Western Lombard literature">Western Lombard literature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Lombard_writers" title="Western Lombard writers">Western Lombard writers</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plural_inflection_in_Western_Lombard" title="Plural inflection in Western Lombard">Plural inflection in Western Lombard</a></div>
<p>Apart from standardized Italian, <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">Lombard</a> is the local language of Lombardy. Lombard is a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic" title="Gallo-Italic">Gallo-Italic</a> group within the <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance languages</a>. It is spoken natively in Northern <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> (most of Lombardy and some areas of neighbouring regions, notably the eastern side of <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">Piedmont</a>) and Southern <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ticino" title="Ticino">Ticino</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graub%C3%BCnden" title="Graubünden">Graubünden</a>).</p>
<p>The two main varieties (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Lombard_language" title="Western Lombard language">Western Lombard language</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Lombard_language" title="Eastern Lombard language">Eastern Lombard language</a>) show differences and are often, but not always, mutually comprehensible. The union of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Lombard" title="Western Lombard">Western Lombard or Insubric</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Lombard" title="Eastern Lombard">Eastern Lombard</a> and intermediate varieties under the denomination of "Lombard" is a matter of debate, and it has been argued that the two might potentially form separate languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<a href="/wiki/Dolce_%26_Gabbana" title="Dolce &amp; Gabbana">Dolce &amp; Gabbana</a> is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>.</div>

<p>Lombardy has always been an important centre for silk and textile production, notably the cities of <a href="/wiki/Pavia" title="Pavia">Pavia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vigevano" title="Vigevano">Vigevano</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cremona" title="Cremona">Cremona</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> is the region's most important centre for clothing and high fashion. In 2009, Milan was regarded as the world <a href="/wiki/Fashion_capital" title="Fashion capital">fashion capital</a>, even surpassing New York, Paris and London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-languagemonitor.com_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-languagemonitor.com-19">[19]</a></sup> Most of the major <a href="/wiki/Italian_fashion" title="Italian fashion">Italian fashion</a> brands, such as <a href="/wiki/Valentino_SpA" title="Valentino SpA">Valentino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Versace" title="Versace">Versace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prada" title="Prada">Prada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armani" title="Armani">Armani</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dolce_%26_Gabbana" title="Dolce &amp; Gabbana">Dolce &amp; Gabbana</a> (to name a few), are currently headquartered in the city.</p>
