<p>The <b>Battle of Long Island</b> is also known as the <b>Battle of Brooklyn</b> and the <b>Battle of Brooklyn Heights</b>. It was fought on August 27, 1776 and was the first major battle of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a> to take place after the United States declared its independence on July 4, 1776. It was a victory for the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> and the beginning of a successful campaign that gave them control of the strategically important <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">city of New York</a>. In terms of troop deployment and fighting, it was the largest battle of the entire war.</p>
<p>After defeating the British in the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Boston" title="Siege of Boston">Siege of Boston</a> on March 17, 1776, commander-in-chief <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General" title="General">General</a> <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> brought the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> to defend the port city of New York, then limited to the southern end of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manhattan_Island" title="Manhattan Island">Manhattan Island</a>. Washington understood that the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Harbor" title="New York Harbor">city's harbor</a> would provide an excellent base for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">British Navy</a> during the campaign, so he established defenses there and waited for the British to attack. In July, the British under the command of <a href="/wiki/William_Howe,_5th_Viscount_Howe" title="William Howe, 5th Viscount Howe">General William Howe</a> landed a few miles across the harbor from Manhattan on the sparsely-populated <a href="/wiki/Staten_Island" title="Staten Island">Staten Island</a>, where they were slowly reinforced by ships in <a href="/wiki/Lower_New_York_Bay" title="Lower New York Bay">Lower New York Bay</a> during the next month and a half, bringing their total force to 32,000 troops. Washington knew the difficulty in holding the city with the British fleet in control of the entrance to the harbor at <a href="/wiki/The_Narrows" title="The Narrows">the Narrows</a>, and he moved the bulk of his forces to Manhattan, believing that it would be the first target.</p>
<p>On August 22, the British landed on the shores of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravesend_Bay" title="Gravesend Bay">Gravesend Bay</a> in southwest <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Kings County</a>, across the Narrows from Staten Island and more than a dozen miles south from the established <a href="/wiki/East_River" title="East River">East River</a> crossings to Manhattan. After five days of waiting, the British attacked American defenses on the <a href="/wiki/Heights_of_Guan" title="Heights of Guan">Guan Heights</a>. Unknown to the Americans, however, Howe had brought his main army around their rear and attacked their flank soon after. The Americans panicked, resulting in twenty percent losses through <a href="/wiki/Casualty_(person)" title="Casualty (person)">casualties</a> and captures, although a stand by 400 Maryland troops prevented a larger portion of the army from being lost. The remainder of the army retreated to the main defenses on <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Heights" title="Brooklyn Heights">Brooklyn Heights</a>. The British dug in for a siege but, on the night of August 29–30, Washington evacuated the entire army to Manhattan without the loss of supplies or a single life. Washington and the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> were driven out of New York entirely after several more defeats and forced to retreat through New Jersey and into Pennsylvania.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boston_to_New_York">Boston to New York</span></h3>
<p>In the first stage of the war, the <a href="/wiki/British_Army_during_the_American_War_of_Independence" title="British Army during the American War of Independence">British Army</a> was <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Boston" title="Siege of Boston">trapped</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Peninsula" title="Peninsula">peninsular</a> city of Boston and they abandoned it on March 17, setting sail to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_of_Halifax" title="City of Halifax">Halifax, Nova Scotia</a> to await reinforcements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m101_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-m101-7">[7]</a></sup> Washington then began to transfer regiments to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> which he believed the British would next attack because of its strategic importance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m112_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-m112-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l128_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-l128-9">[9]</a></sup> Washington left Boston on April 4, arrived at New York on April 13,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m121_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-m121-10">[10]</a></sup> and established headquarters at the <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Kennedy,_11th_Earl_of_Cassilis" title="Archibald Kennedy, 11th Earl of Cassilis">former home of Archibald Kennedy</a> on <a href="/wiki/Broadway_(Manhattan)" title="Broadway (Manhattan)">Broadway</a> facing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bowling_Green_(Manhattan)" title="Bowling Green (Manhattan)">Bowling Green</a>. Washington had sent his second in command <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lee_(general)" title="Charles Lee (general)">Charles Lee</a> ahead to New York the previous February to establish the city's defenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l129_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-l129-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lee remained in New York City until March, when the <a href="/wiki/Second_Continental_Congress" title="Second Continental Congress">Continental Congress</a> sent him to <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>; the defenses' construction was left to General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Alexander_(American_general)" title="William Alexander (American general)">William Alexander</a> (Lord Stirling).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m121_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-m121-10">[10]</a></sup> Troops were in limited supply, so Washington found the defenses incomplete,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> but Lee had concluded that it would be impossible to hold the city with the British commanding the sea. He reasoned that the defenses should be located with the ability to inflict heavy casualties upon the British if any move was made to take and hold ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l129_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-l129-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Barricade" title="Barricade">Barricades</a> and <a href="/wiki/Redoubt" title="Redoubt">redoubts</a> were established in and around the city, and the <a href="/wiki/Bastion" title="Bastion">bastion</a> of Fort Stirling across the East River in <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Heights" title="Brooklyn Heights">Brooklyn Heights</a>, facing the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l131_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-l131-13">[13]</a></sup> Lee also saw that the immediate area was cleared of <a href="/wiki/Loyalist_(American_Revolution)" title="Loyalist (American Revolution)">Loyalists</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategy">Strategy</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BrooklynMap1766.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1629" data-file-width="1882" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/BrooklynMap1766.jpg/220px-BrooklynMap1766.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/BrooklynMap1766.jpg/330px-BrooklynMap1766.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/BrooklynMap1766.jpg/440px-BrooklynMap1766.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BrooklynMap1766.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
American strategy called for the first line of defense to be based on the <a href="/wiki/Heights_of_Guan" title="Heights of Guan">Heights of Guan</a>, a series of hills which stretched northeast across <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">King's County</a>. The main defensive works were a series of forts and entrenchments located in the northwest of the county, in and around the Dutch-founded town of Brookland. The "Road to Narrows" is the Gowanus Road. No. 5 is the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Stone_House_(Brooklyn,_New_York)" title="Old Stone House (Brooklyn, New York)">Old Stone House</a>". Map by <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Ratzer" title="Bernard Ratzer">Bernard Ratzer</a> based on his 1766-1767 survey.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Washington began moving troops to Brooklyn in early May,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m127_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-m127-15">[15]</a></sup> and there were several thousand of them there in a short time. Three more forts were under construction on the eastern side of the East River to support Fort Stirling, which stood to the west of the hamlet of Brooklyn Heights. These new fortifications were Fort Putnam<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> (named for <a href="/wiki/Rufus_Putnam" title="Rufus Putnam">Rufus Putnam</a> and not to be confused with the later <a href="/wiki/Fort_Putnam" title="Fort Putnam">Fort Putnam</a> at West Point), Fort Greene<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> (named for <a href="/wiki/Nathanael_Greene" title="Nathanael Greene">Nathanael Greene</a>), and Fort Box<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> (named for Major Daniel Box).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m128_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-m128-19">[19]</a></sup> They lay from north to south, with Fort Putnam farthest to the north, Greene slightly to the southwest, and Box slightly farther southwest. Each of these defensive structures was surrounded by a large ditch, all connected by a line of <a href="/wiki/Entrenchment_(fortification)" title="Entrenchment (fortification)">entrenchments</a> and a total of 36 cannons (mostly 18-pounders).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fort Defiance was also being constructed at this time, located farther southwest, past Fort Box, near present-day <a href="/wiki/Red_Hook,_Brooklyn" title="Red Hook, Brooklyn">Red Hook</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m128_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-m128-19">[19]</a></sup> In addition to these new forts, a mounted battery was established on <a href="/wiki/Governors_Island" title="Governors Island">Governors Island</a>, cannons were placed at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Amsterdam" title="Fort Amsterdam">Fort George</a> facing Bowling Green, and more cannons placed at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whitehall_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Whitehall Street (Manhattan)">Whitehall Dock</a>, which sat on the <a href="/wiki/East_River" title="East River">East River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m129_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-m129-21">[21]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hulk_(ship)" title="Hulk (ship)">Hulks</a> were sunk at strategic locations to deter the British from entering the East River and other waterways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCullough_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCullough-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Washington had been authorized by Congress to recruit an army of up to 28,501 troops, but he had only 19,000 when he reached New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l132_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-l132-23">[23]</a></sup> Military discipline was inadequate; routine orders were not carried out, <a href="/wiki/Brown_Bess" title="Brown Bess">muskets</a> were fired in camp, <a href="/wiki/Flintlock" title="Flintlock">flints</a> were ruined, <a href="/wiki/Bayonet" title="Bayonet">bayonets</a> were used as knives to cut food, and <a href="/wiki/Firearm" title="Firearm">firearm</a> readiness was lax.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l133_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-l133-24">[24]</a></sup> Petty internal conflict was common under the strain of a large number of people from different environments and temperaments in relative closeness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m159_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-m159-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Commander of the artillery <a href="/wiki/Henry_Knox" title="Henry Knox">Henry Knox</a> persuaded Washington to transfer 400 to 500 soldiers, who lacked muskets or guns, to crew the artillery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m129_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-m129-21">[21]</a></sup> In early June, Knox and Greene inspected the land at the north end of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> and decided to establish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Washington_(New_York)" title="Fort Washington (New York)">Fort Washington</a>. Fort Constitution, later renamed <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lee,_New_Jersey" title="Fort Lee, New Jersey">Fort Lee</a>, was planned opposite Fort Washington on the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m129_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-m129-21">[21]</a></sup> The forts were hoped to discourage the British ships from sailing up the Hudson River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m129_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-m129-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_arrival">British arrival</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_British_fleet_in_the_lower_bay_1876.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="582" data-file-width="760" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/The_British_fleet_in_the_lower_bay_1876.jpg/220px-The_British_fleet_in_the_lower_bay_1876.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/The_British_fleet_in_the_lower_bay_1876.jpg/330px-The_British_fleet_in_the_lower_bay_1876.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/The_British_fleet_in_the_lower_bay_1876.jpg/440px-The_British_fleet_in_the_lower_bay_1876.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_British_fleet_in_the_lower_bay_1876.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>The British fleet in the lower bay</i> (<i>Harpers Magazine</i>, 1876) depicts the British fleet amassing off the shores of Staten Island in the summer of 1776</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On June 28, Washington learned that the British fleet had set sail from Halifax on June 9 and were heading toward New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m133_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-m133-26">[26]</a></sup> On June 29, signals were sent from men stationed on <a href="/wiki/Staten_Island" title="Staten Island">Staten Island</a>, indicating that the British fleet had appeared. Within a few hours, 45 British ships dropped anchor in <a href="/wiki/Lower_New_York_Bay" title="Lower New York Bay">Lower New York Bay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m134_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-m134-27">[27]</a></sup> Less than a week later, there were 130 ships off Staten Island under the command of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Howe" title="Richard Howe">Richard Howe</a>, the brother of the general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l135_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-l135-28">[28]</a></sup> The population of New York went into panic at the sight of the British ships; alarms went off and troops immediately rushed to their posts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m134_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-m134-27">[27]</a></sup> On July 2, British troops began to land on Staten Island. The continental regulars on the island took a few shots at them before fleeing, and the citizens' <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a> switched over to the British side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l135_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-l135-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 6, news reached New York that Congress had voted for independence four days earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m135_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-m135-29">[29]</a></sup> On Tuesday, July 9, at 18:00, Washington had several brigades march onto the commons of the city to hear the <i><a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">Declaration of Independence</a></i> read. After the end of the reading, a mob ran down to <a href="/wiki/Bowling_Green_(New_York_City)" title="Bowling Green (New York City)">Bowling Green</a> with ropes and bars, where they tore down the gilded lead equestrian statue of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_III_of_Great_Britain" title="George III of Great Britain">George III of Great Britain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m137_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-m137-30">[30]</a></sup> In their fury, the crowd cut off the statue's head, severed the nose, and mounted what remained of the head on a spike outside a tavern, and the rest of the statue was dragged to Connecticut and melted down into <a href="/wiki/Musket#Ammunition" title="Musket">musket balls</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m138_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-m138-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 12, the British ships <i>Phoenix</i> and <a href="/wiki/HMS_Rose_(1757)" title="HMS Rose (1757)"><i>Rose</i></a> sailed up the harbor toward the mouth of the Hudson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m138_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-m138-31">[31]</a></sup> The American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_(artillery)" title="Battery (artillery)">batteries</a> opened fire at <a href="/wiki/Fort_George,_New_York" title="Fort George, New York">Fort George</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_Hook,_Brooklyn" title="Red Hook, Brooklyn">Red Hook</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Governors_Island" title="Governors Island">Governors Island</a>, and the British returned fire into the city. The ships sailed along the New Jersey shore and continued up the Hudson, sailing past Fort Washington and arriving by nightfall at <a href="/wiki/Tarrytown,_New_York" title="Tarrytown, New York">Tarrytown</a>, the widest part of the Hudson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m139_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-m139-32">[32]</a></sup> The goals of the British ships were to cut off American supplies and to encourage Loyalist support. The only casualties of the day were six Americans who were killed when their own cannon blew up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m139_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-m139-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:British_flat-bottomed_boat_American_Revolution.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="402" data-file-width="615" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/British_flat-bottomed_boat_American_Revolution.jpg/220px-British_flat-bottomed_boat_American_Revolution.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/British_flat-bottomed_boat_American_Revolution.jpg/330px-British_flat-bottomed_boat_American_Revolution.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/British_flat-bottomed_boat_American_Revolution.jpg/440px-British_flat-bottomed_boat_American_Revolution.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The next day, July 13, Howe attempted to open negotiations with the Americans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m144_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-m144-33">[33]</a></sup> He sent a letter to Washington delivered by Lieutenant Philip Brown, who arrived under a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flag_of_truce" title="Flag of truce">flag of truce</a>. The letter was addressed "George Washington, Esq."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m144_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-m144-33">[33]</a></sup> Brown was met by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Reed_(jurist)" title="Joseph Reed (jurist)">Joseph Reed</a>, who had hurried to the waterfront on Washington's orders, accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Knox" title="Henry Knox">Henry Knox</a> and Samuel Webb. Washington asked his officers whether it should be received or not, as it did not recognize his rank as general, and they unanimously said no.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Reed told Brown that there was no one in the army with that address. On July 16, Howe tried again, this time with the address "George Washington, Esq., etc., etc.", but it was again declined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m145_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-m145-35">[35]</a></sup> The next day, Howe sent Captain Nisbet Balfour to ask if Washington would meet with Howe's <a href="/wiki/Adjutant" title="Adjutant">adjutant</a> face to face, and a meeting was scheduled for July 20.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m145_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-m145-35">[35]</a></sup> Howe's adjutant was Colonel James Patterson. Patterson told Washington that Howe had come with powers to grant pardons, but Washington said, "Those who have committed no fault want no pardon."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m145_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-m145-35">[35]</a></sup> Patterson departed soon after.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m145_35-3"><a href="#cite_note-m145-35">[35]</a></sup> Washington's performance during the meeting was praised in parts of the colonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l138_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-l138-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, British ships continued to arrive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m146_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-m146-37">[37]</a></sup> On August 1, 45 ships arrived with generals <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Clinton_(American_War_of_Independence)" title="Henry Clinton (American War of Independence)">Henry Clinton</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Cornwallis" title="Charles Cornwallis">Charles Cornwallis</a>, along with 3,000 troops. By August 12, 3,000 more British troops and another 8,000 Hessians had arrived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m148_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-m148-38">[38]</a></sup> At this point, the British fleet numbered over 400 ships, including 73 war ships, and 32,000 troops were camped on Staten Island. Faced with this large force, Washington was unsure as to where the British would attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m152_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-m152-39">[39]</a></sup> Both Greene and Reed thought that the British would attack Long Island, but Washington felt that a British attack on Long Island might be a diversion for the main attack on Manhattan. He broke his army in half, stationing half of it on Manhattan, and the other half on Long Island; the army on Long Island was commanded by Greene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m152_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-m152-39">[39]</a></sup> On August 20, Greene became ill and was forced to move to a house in Manhattan where he rested to recover. <a href="/wiki/John_Sullivan_(general)" title="John Sullivan (general)">John Sullivan</a> was placed in command until Greene was well enough to resume command.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m153_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-m153-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Invasion_of_Long_Island">Invasion of Long Island</span></h3>
<p>At 05:10 on August 22, an advance guard of 4,000 British troops left Staten Island under the command of Clinton and Cornwallis to land on Long Island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m156_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-m156-41">[41]</a></sup> At 08:00, all 4,000 troops landed unopposed on the shore of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravesend_Bay" title="Gravesend Bay">Gravesend Bay</a>. Colonel <a href="/wiki/Edward_Hand" title="Edward Hand">Edward Hand</a>'s Pennsylvanian riflemen had been stationed on the shore, but they did not oppose the landings and fell back, killing cattle and burning farmhouses on the way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m157_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-m157-42">[42]</a></sup> By noon, 15,000 troops had landed on shore along with 40 pieces of artillery, as hundreds of Loyalists came to greet the British troops. Cornwallis pushed on with the advance guard, advancing six miles onto the island and establishing a camp at the village of <a href="/wiki/Flatbush,_Brooklyn" title="Flatbush, Brooklyn">Flatbush</a>. He was given orders to advance no further.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m157_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-m157-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Washington received word of the landings the same day, but was informed that the number was 8,000 to 9,000 troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m158_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-m158-44">[44]</a></sup> This convinced him that it was the feint which he had predicted and therefore he only sent 1,500 more troops to Brooklyn, bringing the total number of troops on Long Island to 6,000. On August 24, Washington replaced Sullivan with <a href="/wiki/Israel_Putnam" title="Israel Putnam">Israel Putnam</a> who commanded the troops on Long Island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m160_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-m160-45">[45]</a></sup> Putnam arrived on Long Island the next day along with six battalions. Also that day, the British troops on Long Island received 5,000 Hessian reinforcements, bringing their total to 20,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m161_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-m161-46">[46]</a></sup> There was little fighting on the days immediately after the landing, although some small skirmishes did take place with American <a href="/wiki/Marksman" title="Marksman">marksmen</a> armed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_long_rifle" title="Pennsylvania long rifle">rifles</a> picking off British troops from time to time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_Brooklyn.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="699" data-file-width="630" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Battle_of_Brooklyn.gif/220px-Battle_of_Brooklyn.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Battle_of_Brooklyn.gif/330px-Battle_of_Brooklyn.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Battle_of_Brooklyn.gif/440px-Battle_of_Brooklyn.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_Brooklyn.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
British military map from 1776 showing the marching routes and battle sites during the Battle of Long Island.</div>
</div>

<p>The American plan was that Putnam would direct the defenses from Brooklyn Heights, while Sullivan and Stirling and their troops would be stationed on the Guan Heights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m162_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-m162-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l141_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-l141-49">[49]</a></sup> The Guan (hills) were up to 150 feet high and blocked the most direct route to Brooklyn Heights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m162_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-m162-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l141_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-l141-49">[49]</a></sup> Washington believed that, by stationing men on the heights, heavy casualties could be inflicted on the British before the troops fell back to the main defenses at Brooklyn Heights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l142_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-l142-50">[50]</a></sup> There were three main passes through the heights; the Gowanus Road farthest to the west, the <a href="/wiki/Flatbush_Avenue" title="Flatbush Avenue">Flatbush Road</a> slightly farther to the east, in the center of the American line where it was expected that the British would attack, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bedford_Avenue_(Brooklyn)" title="Bedford Avenue (Brooklyn)">Bedford Road</a> farthest to the east. Stirling was responsible for defending the Gowanus Road with 500 men, and Sullivan was to defend the Flatbush and Bedford roads where there were 1,000 and 800 men respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m162_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-m162-48">[48]</a></sup> Six-thousand troops were to remain behind at Brooklyn Heights. There was one lesser-known pass through the heights farther to the east called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jamaica_Pass" title="Jamaica Pass">Jamaica Pass</a>, which was defended by just five militia officers on horses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m163_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-m163-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the British side, General Clinton learned of the almost undefended Jamaica Pass from local Loyalists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m165_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-m165-52">[52]</a></sup> He drew up a plan and gave it to <a href="/wiki/Sir_William_Erskine,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir William Erskine, 1st Baronet">William Erskine</a> to propose to Howe. Clinton's plan had the main army making a night march and going through the Jamaica Pass to turn the American flank, while other troops would keep the Americans busy in front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m166_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-m166-53">[53]</a></sup> On August 26, Clinton received word from Howe that the plan would be used, and that Clinton was to command the advance guard of the main army of 10,000 men on the march through the Jamaica Pass. While they made the night march, General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Grant_(British_Army_officer)" title="James Grant (British Army officer)">James Grant</a>'s British troops along with some Hessians, a total of 4,000 men, were to attack the Americans in front to distract them from the main army coming on their flank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m166_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-m166-53">[53]</a></sup> Howe told Clinton to be ready to move out that night, August 26.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m166_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-m166-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle">Battle</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Night_march">Night march</span></h3>
<p>At 21:00, the British moved out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m168_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-m168-54">[54]</a></sup> No one except the commanders knew of the plan. Clinton led a crack brigade of light infantry with fixed bayonets in front, followed by Cornwallis who had eight battalions and 14 artillery pieces. Cornwallis was followed by Howe and <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Percy,_2nd_Duke_of_Northumberland" title="Hugh Percy, 2nd Duke of Northumberland">Hugh Percy</a> with six battalions, more artillery, and baggage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m168_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-m168-54">[54]</a></sup> The column consisted of 10,000 men who stretched out over two miles. Three Loyalist farmers led the column toward the Jamaica Pass. The British had left their campfires burning to deceive the Americans into thinking that nothing was happening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m168_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-m168-54">[54]</a></sup> The column headed northeast until it reached what later became the village of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Lots,_Brooklyn" title="New Lots, Brooklyn">New Lots</a>, when it headed directly north toward the heights.</p>
<p>The column had yet to run into any American troops when they reached Howard's Tavern (also known as Howard's Half-Way House), just a few hundred yards from the Jamaica Pass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m169_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-m169-55">[55]</a></sup> Tavern keeper William Howard and his son William Jr. were forced to act as guides to show the British the way to the Rockaway Foot Path, an old <a href="/wiki/Lenape" title="Lenape">Indian</a> trail that skirted the Jamaica Pass to the west (located today in the <a href="/wiki/Cemetery_of_the_Evergreens" title="Cemetery of the Evergreens">Cemetery of the Evergreens</a>). Five minutes after leaving the tavern, the five American militia officers stationed at the pass were captured without a shot fired, as they thought that the British were Americans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m170_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-m170-56">[56]</a></sup> Clinton interrogated the men and they informed him that they were the only troops guarding the pass. By dawn, the British were through the pass and stopped so that the troops could rest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m170_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-m170-56">[56]</a></sup> At 09:00, they fired two heavy cannons to signal the Hessian troops below Battle Pass to begin their frontal assault against Sullivan's men deployed on the two hills flanking the pass, while Clinton's troops simultaneously flanked the American positions from the east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m170_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-m170-56">[56]</a></sup></p>

<p>William Howard Jr. describes meeting Howe:<br/></p>
<blockquote>
<p>It was about two in the morning of August 27 that I was awakened by seeing a soldier at the side of my bed. I got up and dressed and went down to the barroom, where I saw my father standing in one corner with three British soldiers before him with muskets and bayonets fixed. The army was then lying in the field in front of the house.... General Howe and another officer were in the barroom. General Howe wore a <a href="/wiki/Camlet" title="Camlet">camlet</a> cloak over his regimentals. After asking for a glass of liquor from the bar, which was given him, he entered into familiar conversation with my father, and among other things said, "I must have some one of you to show me over the Rockaway Path around the pass." My father replied, "We belong to the other side, General, and can’t serve you against our duty." General Howe replied, "That is alright; stick to your country, or stick to your principles, but Howard, you are my prisoner and must guide my men over the hill." My father made some further objection, but was silenced by the general, who said, "You have no alternative. If you refuse I shall shoot you through the head."</p>
</blockquote>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grant.27s_diversionary_attack">Grant's diversionary attack</span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div>

<p>At about 23:00 on August 26, the first shots were fired in the Battle of Long Island, near the <a href="/wiki/Red_Lion_Inn_(Brooklyn)" title="Red Lion Inn (Brooklyn)">Red Lion Inn</a> (near present-day 39th Street and 4th Avenue). American pickets from <a href="/wiki/Samuel_John_Atlee" title="Samuel John Atlee">Samuel John Atlee</a>'s Pennsylvania regiment fired upon two British soldiers who were foraging in a watermelon patch near the inn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around 01:00 on August 27, the British approached the vicinity of the Red Lion with 200–300 troops. The American troops fired upon the British; after approximately two fusillades, they fled up the Gowanus Road toward the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Stone_House_(Brooklyn,_New_York)" title="Old Stone House (Brooklyn, New York)">Vechte-Cortelyou House</a>. Major <a href="/wiki/Edward_Burd" title="Edward Burd">Edward Burd</a> had been in command, but he was captured along with a lieutenant and 15 privates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> This first engagement was fought in the vicinity of 38th and 39th streets between 2nd and 3rd avenues near a swamp located adjacent to the Gowanus Road.</p>
<p>Brigadier General <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Holden_Parsons" title="Samuel Holden Parsons">Samuel Holden Parsons</a> and Colonel Atlee were stationed farther north on the Gowanus Road. Parsons was a lawyer from Connecticut who had recently secured a commission in the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a>; Atlee was a veteran of the <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">French and Indian War</a> in command of the First Regiment of Pennsylvania Musketry. Putnam had been awakened by a guard at 03:00 and told that the British were attacking through the Gowanus Pass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m171_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-m171-60">[60]</a></sup> He lit signals to Washington, who was on Manhattan, and then rode south to warn Stirling of the attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l143_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-l143-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stirling led two units of <a href="/wiki/John_Haslet" title="John Haslet">Colonel John Haslet</a>'s 1st Delaware Regiment under the immediate command of Major Thomas Macdonough, and <a href="/wiki/William_Smallwood" title="William Smallwood">Colonel William Smallwood</a>'s <a href="/wiki/1st_Maryland_Regiment" title="1st Maryland Regiment">1st Maryland Infantry</a> under the immediate command of <a href="/wiki/Mordecai_Gist" title="Mordecai Gist">Major Mordecai Gist</a>; both Haslet and Smallwood were on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Courts-martial" title="Courts-martial">courts-martial</a> duty in Manhattan. Following close behind was Parson's Connecticut regiment with 251 men. Stirling led this combined force to reinforce Parsons and Atlee and stop the British advance. Stirling had a total of 1,600 troops at his command.</p>
<p>Stirling placed Atlee's men in an apple orchard owned by Wynant Bennett on the south side of the Gowanus Road near present-day 3rd Avenue and 18th Street. Upon the approach of the British, the Americans:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>took possession of a hill about two miles from camp, and detached Colonel Atlee to meet them further on the road; in about sixty <a href="/wiki/Rod_(unit)" title="Rod (unit)">rods</a> he drew up and received the enemy's fire and gave them a well-directed fire from his regiment, which did great execution, and then retreated to the hill. - General Parsons</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Stirling took up positions with the Delaware and Maryland regiments just to the north of Atlee's men on the slopes of a rise of land between 18th and 20th streets. Some of the Maryland troops were positioned on a small hill near 23rd Street, which the local Dutch called "<i>Blokje Berg</i>" (Dutch for cube or block hill). At the base of this hill, the Gowanus Road crossed a small bridge over a ditch which drained a marshy area. When the British advanced up the Gowanus Road, the American troops fired upon them from positions on the north side of the ditch. To the left was Colonel Peter Kachline's Pennsylvania regiment.</p>
<p>Just to the southeast of <i>Blokje Berg</i> were a few hills; amongst them was a hill which is the highest point in King's County at 220 feet which came to be known as "<a href="/wiki/Battle_Hill_(Brooklyn)" title="Battle Hill (Brooklyn)">Battle Hill</a>," in what is today <a href="/wiki/Green-Wood_Cemetery" title="Green-Wood Cemetery">Greenwood Cemetery</a> by the cemetery's boundary of 23rd Street and 7th Avenue. The British attempted to outflank the American positions by taking this hill. The Americans tried to prevent the British move, sending troops under Parsons and Atlee to take the hill. The British got there first but the Americans were able to dislodge them in fierce fighting.</p>
<p>Battle Hill was the site of especially brutal fighting, with the Americans inflicting the highest number of casualties against the British troops during the entire Battle of Long Island. Among those killed was British Colonel James Grant, which led the Americans to believe that they had killed General James Grant. He was alleged to have been shot by a Pennsylvanian rifleman who had been sniping at the British from up in a tree. Among the American dead was Pennsylvania Colonel Caleb Parry, who was killed while rallying his troops.</p>
<p>The Americans were still unaware that this was not the main British attack, in part due to the ferocity of the fighting and the number of British troops engaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m172_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-m172-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle_Pass">Battle Pass</span></h3>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brooklyn_Museum_-_Battle_Pass_Vally_Grove_-_Hayward_and_Lepine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="503" data-file-width="768" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Brooklyn_Museum_-_Battle_Pass_Vally_Grove_-_Hayward_and_Lepine.jpg/220px-Brooklyn_Museum_-_Battle_Pass_Vally_Grove_-_Hayward_and_Lepine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Brooklyn_Museum_-_Battle_Pass_Vally_Grove_-_Hayward_and_Lepine.jpg/330px-Brooklyn_Museum_-_Battle_Pass_Vally_Grove_-_Hayward_and_Lepine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Brooklyn_Museum_-_Battle_Pass_Vally_Grove_-_Hayward_and_Lepine.jpg/440px-Brooklyn_Museum_-_Battle_Pass_Vally_Grove_-_Hayward_and_Lepine.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Battle Pass - also known as "Flatbush Pass" - is located in modern-day <a href="/wiki/Prospect_Park_(Brooklyn)" title="Prospect Park (Brooklyn)">Prospect Park</a>. Here <a href="/wiki/John_Sullivan_(general)" title="John Sullivan (general)">General Sullivan</a> and his troops were outflanked by the British who attacked from the rear while the Hessians attacked up Battle Pass. <p>The Hessians, in the center under the command of General von Heister, began to bombard the American lines stationed at Battle Pass under the command of <a href="/wiki/John_Sullivan_(general)" title="John Sullivan (general)">General John Sullivan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m173_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-m173-63">[63]</a></sup> The Hessian brigades did not attack, as they were waiting for the pre-arranged signal from the British, who were in the process of outflanking the American lines at that time. The Americans were still under the assumption that Grant's attack up the Gowanus Road was the main thrust, and Sullivan sent four-hundred of his men to reinforce Stirling.</p>
<p>Howe fired his signal guns at 09:00 and the Hessians began to attack up Battle Pass, while the main army came at Sullivan from the rear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m173_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-m173-63">[63]</a></sup> Sullivan left his advance guard to hold off the Hessians while he turned the rest of his force around to fight the British. Heavy casualties mounted between the Americans and the British, and men on both sides fled out of fear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m173_63-2"><a href="#cite_note-m173-63">[63]</a></sup> Sullivan attempted to calm his men and tried to lead a retreat. By this point, the Hessians had overrun the advance guard on the heights and the American left had completely collapsed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m174_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-m174-64">[64]</a></sup> Hand-to-hand fighting followed, with the Americans swinging their muskets and rifles like clubs to save their own lives. Many of the Americans who surrendered were bayoneted by the Hessians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l145_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-l145-65">[65]</a></sup> Sullivan, despite the chaos, managed to evacuate most of his men to Brooklyn Heights though he himself was captured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m174_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-m174-64">[64]</a></sup></p>

<p>At 09:00, Washington arrived from Manhattan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m175_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-m175-67">[67]</a></sup> He realized that he had been wrong about a feint on Long Island and he ordered more troops to Brooklyn from Manhattan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m175_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-m175-67">[67]</a></sup> His location on the battlefield is not known because accounts differ, but most likely he was at Brooklyn Heights where he could view the battle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m176_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-m176-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stirling still held the line against Grant on the American right, to the west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m176_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-m176-68">[68]</a></sup> He held on for four hours, still unaware of the British flanking maneuver, and some of his own troops thought that they were winning the day because the British had been unable to take their position. However, Grant was reinforced by 2,000 marines, and he hit Stirling's center by 11:00, and Stirling was attacked on his left by the Hessians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l145_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-l145-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m176_68-2"><a href="#cite_note-m176-68">[68]</a></sup> Stirling pulled back, but British troops were coming at him from the rear, south down the Gowanus Road. The only escape route left was across a Brouwer's millpond on the Gowanus Creek which was 80 yards wide, on the other side of Brooklyn Heights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m177_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-m177-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Maryland_400">Maryland 400</span></h4>
<p>Stirling ordered all of his troops to cross the creek, except a contingent of Maryland troops under the command of Gist. This group became known to history as the "<a href="/wiki/Maryland_400" title="Maryland 400">Maryland 400</a>", although they numbered about 260–270 men. Stirling and Gist led the troops in a rear-guard action against the overwhelming numbers of British troops, which surpassed 2,000 supported by two cannons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m177_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-m177-69">[69]</a></sup> Stirling and Gist led the Marylanders in two attacks against the British, who were in fixed positions in and in front of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Stone_House_(Brooklyn,_New_York)" title="Old Stone House (Brooklyn, New York)">Vechte-Cortelyou House</a> (known today as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Stone_House_(Brooklyn,_New_York)" title="Old Stone House (Brooklyn, New York)">Old Stone House</a>). After the last assault, the remaining troops retreated across the Gowanus Creek. Some of the men who tried to cross the marsh were bogged down in the mud and under musket fire, and others who could not swim were captured. Stirling was surrounded and, unwilling to surrender, broke through the British lines to von Heister's Hessians and surrendered to them. Two hundred fifty six Maryland troops were killed in the assaults in front of the Old Stone House, and fewer than a dozen made it back to the American lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l146_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-l146-70">[70]</a></sup> Washington watched from a redoubt on nearby Cobble Hill (intersection of today's Court Street and Atlantic Avenue) and reportedly said, "Good God, what brave fellows I must this day lose."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m177_69-2"><a href="#cite_note-m177-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[note 1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disengagement">Disengagement</span></h3>
<p>The American troops who were not killed or captured escaped behind the fortified American positions centered on Brooklyn Heights. Howe then ordered all of his troops to halt the attack, in a move considered controversial to this day, despite the protests of many officers in his command who believed that they should push on to Brooklyn Heights. Howe had decided against a direct frontal assault against the entrenched American positions, choosing instead to begin a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_warfare" title="Siege warfare">siege</a> and setting up lines of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circumvallation" title="Circumvallation">circumvallation</a> around the American positions. He believed the Americans to be essentially trapped, with his troops blocking escape by land and the Royal Navy in control of the East River, which they would have to cross to reach Manhattan Island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m178_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-m178-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-f99_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-f99-73">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>Howe's failure to press the attack and the reasons for it have been disputed. He may have wished to avoid the casualties that his army suffered when attacking the continentals under similar circumstances at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bunker_Hill" title="Battle of Bunker Hill">Battle of Bunker Hill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-f99_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-f99-73">[72]</a></sup> He may also have been giving Washington an opportunity to conclude that his position was hopeless and surrender, in the European gentleman-officer tradition. Howe told Parliament in 1779 that his essential duty was to avoid excessive British casualties for insufficient purpose, and capturing Brooklyn Heights would likely not have meant capturing the entire American army. "The loss of 1,000, or perhaps 1,500 British troops, in carrying those lines, would have been but ill repaid by double that number of the enemy, could it have been supposed they would have suffered in that proportion." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howe_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Howe-74">[73]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retreat_to_Manhattan">Retreat to Manhattan</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Washington and the army were surrounded on Brooklyn Heights with the East River to their backs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m182_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-m182-76">[75]</a></sup> As the day went on, the British began to dig trenches, slowly coming closer to the American defenses. By doing this, the British would not have to cross over open ground to assault the American defenses as they did in Boston the year before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l148_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-l148-77">[76]</a></sup> Despite this perilous situation, Washington ordered 1,200 more men from Manhattan to Brooklyn on August 28.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m182_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-m182-76">[75]</a></sup> The men that came over were two Pennsylvania regiments and Colonel <a href="/wiki/John_Glover_(general)" title="John Glover (general)">John Glover</a>'s regiment from <a href="/wiki/Marblehead,_Massachusetts" title="Marblehead, Massachusetts">Marblehead, Massachusetts</a>. In command of the Pennsylvanian troops was <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Mifflin" title="Thomas Mifflin">Thomas Mifflin</a> who, after arriving, volunteered to inspect the outer defenses and report back to Washington.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m183_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-m183-78">[77]</a></sup> In these outer defenses, small skirmishes were still taking place. On the afternoon of August 28, it began to rain and Washington had his cannons bombard the British well into the night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m184_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-m184-79">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the rain continued, Washington sent a letter instructing General <a href="/wiki/William_Heath" title="William Heath">William Heath</a>, who was at <a href="/wiki/Kingsbridge,_Bronx" title="Kingsbridge, Bronx">Kings Bridge</a> between Manhattan and what is now the Bronx, to send every flat-bottomed boat and sloop without delay, in case battalions of infantry from New Jersey came to reinforce their position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m185_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-m185-80">[79]</a></sup> At 16:00, on August 29, Washington held a meeting with his generals. Mifflin advised Washington to retreat to Manhattan while Mifflin and his Pennsylvania regiments made up the rear guard, holding the line until the rest of the army had withdrawn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m185_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-m185-80">[79]</a></sup> The generals agreed unanimously with Mifflin that <a href="/wiki/Withdrawal_(military)" title="Withdrawal (military)">retreat</a> was the best option and Washington had orders go out by the evening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m186_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-m186-81">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p>The troops were told that they were to gather up all their ammunition and baggage and prepare for a night attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m186_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-m186-81">[80]</a></sup> By 21:00, the sick and wounded began to move to the Brooklyn Ferry in preparation for evacuation. At 23:00, Glover and his Massachusetts men, who were sailors and fishermen, began to evacuate the troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m188_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-m188-82">[81]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Washington_Battle_of_Brooklyn_3c_1951_issue.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="821" data-file-width="1314" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Washington_Battle_of_Brooklyn_3c_1951_issue.JPG/220px-Washington_Battle_of_Brooklyn_3c_1951_issue.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Washington_Battle_of_Brooklyn_3c_1951_issue.JPG/330px-Washington_Battle_of_Brooklyn_3c_1951_issue.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Washington_Battle_of_Brooklyn_3c_1951_issue.JPG/440px-Washington_Battle_of_Brooklyn_3c_1951_issue.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>As more troops were evacuated, more were ordered to withdraw from the lines and march to the ferry landing. Wagon wheels were muffled, and men were forbidden to talk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m188_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-m188-82">[81]</a></sup> Mifflin's rear guard was tending campfires to deceive the British and convince them that nothing was going on. At 04:00, on August 30, Mifflin was informed that it was his unit's turn to evacuate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m189_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-m189-83">[82]</a></sup> Mifflin told the man who had been sent to order him to leave, Major <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Scammell" title="Alexander Scammell">Alexander Scammell</a>, that he must be mistaken, but Scammell insisted that he was not and Mifflin ordered his troops to move out. When Mifflin's troops were within a half mile of the ferry landing, Washington rode up and demanded to know why they were not at their defenses. <a href="/wiki/Edward_Hand" title="Edward Hand">Edward Hand</a>, who was leading the troops, tried to explain what had happened, but Mifflin arrived shortly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m190_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-m190-84">[83]</a></sup> Washington exclaimed "Good God. General Mifflin, I am afraid you have ruined us." Mifflin explained that he had been told that it was his turn to evacuate by Scammell; Washington told him it had been a mistake. Mifflin then led his troops back to the outer defenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m190_84-1"><a href="#cite_note-m190-84">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>Artillery, supplies, and troops were all being evacuated across the river at this time but it was not going as fast as Washington had anticipated and daybreak soon came.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m190_84-2"><a href="#cite_note-m190-84">[83]</a></sup> A fog settled in and concealed the evacuation from the British. British patrols noticed that there did not seem to be any American pickets and thus began to search the area. While they were doing this, Washington, the last man left, stepped onto the last boat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l148_77-1"><a href="#cite_note-l148-77">[76]</a></sup> At 07:00, the last American troops landed in Manhattan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m191_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-m191-85">[84]</a></sup> All 9,000 troops had been evacuated with no loss of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m191_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-m191-85">[84]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conclusion_of_the_campaign">Conclusion of the campaign</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Englishfleetrevolution.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="488" data-file-width="760" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Englishfleetrevolution.jpeg/220px-Englishfleetrevolution.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Englishfleetrevolution.jpeg/330px-Englishfleetrevolution.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Englishfleetrevolution.jpeg/440px-Englishfleetrevolution.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Englishfleetrevolution.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The British fleet in <a href="/wiki/New_York_Harbor" title="New York Harbor">New York Harbor</a> just after the battle</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The British were stunned to find that Washington and the army had escaped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m191_85-2"><a href="#cite_note-m191-85">[84]</a></sup> Later in the day, August 30, the British troops occupied the American fortifications. When news of the battle reached London, it caused many festivities to take place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m195_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-m195-86">[85]</a></sup> Bells were rung across the city, candles were lit in windows and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_George_III" title="King George III">King George III</a> gave Howe the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Bath" title="Order of the Bath">Order of the Bath</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m196_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-m196-87">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>Washington's defeat revealed his deficiencies as a <a href="/wiki/Strategist" title="Strategist">strategist</a> who split his forces, his inexperienced generals who misunderstood the situation, and his raw troops that fled in disorder at the first shots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[87]</a></sup> However, his daring nighttime retreat has been seen by some historians as one of his greatest military feats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[88]</a></sup> Other historians concentrate on the failure of British naval forces to prevent the withdrawal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>Howe remained inactive for the next half month, not attacking until September 15 when he <a href="/wiki/Landing_at_Kip%27s_Bay" title="Landing at Kip's Bay">landed a force</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kip%27s_Bay" title="Kip's Bay">Kip's Bay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[90]</a></sup> The British quickly occupied the city. On September 21, a <a href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_New_York_(1776)" title="Great Fire of New York (1776)">fire</a> of uncertain origin destroyed a quarter of New York City. In the immediate aftermath of the fire <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Hale" title="Nathan Hale">Nathan Hale</a> was executed for spying. Although American troops delivered an unexpected check to the British at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Harlem_Heights" title="Battle of Harlem Heights">Harlem Heights</a> in mid-September, Howe defeated Washington in battle again at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_White_Plains" title="Battle of White Plains">White Plains</a> and then again at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fort_Washington" title="Battle of Fort Washington">Fort Washington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[91]</a></sup> Because of these defeats, Washington and the army retreated across New Jersey and into Pennsylvania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[92]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casualties">Casualties</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Old_Sugar_House_and_Middle_Dutch_Church.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="526" data-file-width="823" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Old_Sugar_House_and_Middle_Dutch_Church.jpg/220px-Old_Sugar_House_and_Middle_Dutch_Church.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Old_Sugar_House_and_Middle_Dutch_Church.jpg/330px-Old_Sugar_House_and_Middle_Dutch_Church.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Old_Sugar_House_and_Middle_Dutch_Church.jpg/440px-Old_Sugar_House_and_Middle_Dutch_Church.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Old_Sugar_House_and_Middle_Dutch_Church.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Middle Dutch Church is where some of the enlisted men captured at the Battle of Long Island were imprisoned. The Sugar House also became a prison as the British captured more of Washington's soldiers during the retreat from New York. The sight today is the location of the Chase Manhattan Bank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CO_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-CO-94">[93]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At the time, it was by far the largest battle ever fought in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m178_71-2"><a href="#cite_note-m178-71">[71]</a></sup> If the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> is included, over 40,000 men took part in the battle. Howe reported his losses as 59 killed, 268 wounded and 31 missing. The Hessian casualties were 5 killed and 26 wounded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m179_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-m179-5">[5]</a></sup> The Americans suffered much heavier losses. About 300 had been killed and over 1,000 captured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m180_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-m180-4">[4]</a></sup> As few as half of the prisoners survived. Kept on prison ships, then transferred to locations such as the Middle Dutch Church, they were starved and denied medical attention. In their weakened condition, many succumbed to smallpox.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CO_94-1"><a href="#cite_note-CO-94">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:191</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dongan_Oak_eagle_plaque_jeh.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3594" data-file-width="2313" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Dongan_Oak_eagle_plaque_jeh.JPG/120px-Dongan_Oak_eagle_plaque_jeh.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Dongan_Oak_eagle_plaque_jeh.JPG/180px-Dongan_Oak_eagle_plaque_jeh.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Dongan_Oak_eagle_plaque_jeh.JPG/240px-Dongan_Oak_eagle_plaque_jeh.JPG 2x" width="120"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dongan_Oak_eagle_plaque_jeh.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Thomas_Dongan,_2nd_Earl_of_Limerick" title="Thomas Dongan, 2nd Earl of Limerick">Dongan</a> Oak memorial in <a href="/wiki/Prospect_Park_(Brooklyn)" title="Prospect Park (Brooklyn)">Prospect Park</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The most significant legacy of the Battle of Long Island was that it showed there would be no easy victory, and that the war would be long and bloody.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CO_94-2"><a href="#cite_note-CO-94">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:2</sup></p>
<p>Commemorations of the battle include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <i>Altar to Liberty: Minerva</i> monument: The battle is commemorated with a monument, which includes a bronze statue of <a href="/wiki/Minerva" title="Minerva">Minerva</a> near the top of Battle Hill, the highest point of Brooklyn, in Green-Wood Cemetery. The statue was sculpted by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Ruckstull" title="Frederick Ruckstull">Frederick Ruckstull</a> and unveiled in 1920. The statue stands in the northwest corner of the cemetery and gazes directly at the <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_Liberty" title="Statue of Liberty">Statue of Liberty</a> in New York Harbor. In 2006, the Minerva statue was invoked in a successful defense to prevent a building from blocking the <a href="/wiki/Sightline" title="Sightline">line of sight</a> from the cemetery to the Statue of Liberty in the harbor. The annual Battle of Long Island commemoration begins inside the main Gothic arch entrance to Green-Wood Cemetery and marches up Battle Hill to ceremonies at the monument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[94]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Prison_Ship_Martyrs%27_Monument" title="Prison Ship Martyrs' Monument">Prison Ship Martyrs' Monument</a>: A freestanding Doric column in Fort Greene memorializing all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prisoners_in_the_American_Revolutionary_War" title="Prisoners in the American Revolutionary War">those who died while kept prisoner on the British ships just off the shore of Brooklyn</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Wallabout_Bay" title="Wallabout Bay">Wallabout Bay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[95]</a></sup></li>
<li>Soldiers' Monument - <a href="/wiki/Milford,_Connecticut" title="Milford, Connecticut">Milford, Connecticut</a>. Memorializes the 200 seriously ill prisoners of the Battle of Long Island who were dumped on the beach at Milford the night of January 3, 1777.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CO_94-3"><a href="#cite_note-CO-94">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:195</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Old_Stone_House_(Brooklyn)" title="Old Stone House (Brooklyn)">The Old Stone House</a>: A re-constructed farmhouse (c.1699) that was at the center of the Marylanders' delaying actions serves as a museum of the battle. It is located in J.J. Byrne Park, at Third Street and Fifth Avenue, Brooklyn, and features models and maps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[96]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prospect_Park_(Brooklyn)" title="Prospect Park (Brooklyn)">Prospect Park</a>, Brooklyn, Battle Pass: along the eastern side of Center Drive in Prospect Park, Brooklyn, is a large granite boulder with a brass plaque affixed, and another marker lies near the road for the Dongan Oak, a very large and old tree felled to block the pass from the British advance. In addition, in the park resides the Line of Defense marker erected by the <a href="/wiki/Sons_of_the_American_Revolution" title="Sons of the American Revolution">Sons of the American Revolution</a> and, near the eastern edge of Long Meadow, the Maryland Monument&amp; Maryland Memorial<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[97]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corinthian_column" title="Corinthian column">corinthian column</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[98]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>There are only thirty currently existing <a href="/wiki/Army_National_Guard_and_Active_Regular_Army_Units_with_Colonial_Roots" title="Army National Guard and Active Regular Army Units with Colonial Roots">units in the U.S. Army with lineages that go back to the colonial and revolutionary eras</a>. Five Army National Guard units (<a href="/wiki/Army_National_Guard_and_Active_Regular_Army_Units_with_Colonial_Roots#101st_Engineer_Battalion.2C_ARNG_MA_.28East_Regiment.29" title="Army National Guard and Active Regular Army Units with Colonial Roots">101st Eng Bn</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[99]</a></sup> 125th MP Co,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[100]</a></sup> 175th Inf,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[101]</a></sup> 181st Inf<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[102]</a></sup> and 198th Sig Bn)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[103]</a></sup> and one Regular Army Field Artillery battalion (1-5th FA)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[104]</a></sup> are derived from American units that participated in the Battle of Long Island.</p>
