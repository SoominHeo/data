<p><b>Abu Abdollah Jafar ibn Mohammad Rudaki</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">ابوعبدالله جعفر ابن محمد رودکی</span>‎‎, <a href="/wiki/Tajik_language" title="Tajik language">Tajik</a>: <span lang="tg" xml:lang="tg">Абӯабдуллоҳ Ҷаъфар Ибни Муҳаммад</span>, entitled <span lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">آدم الشعرا</span> <i>Ādam ul-Shoara</i> or Adam of Poets), also written as <b>Rudagi</b> (858 - c. 941), was a <a href="/wiki/Persian_people" title="Persian people">Persian</a> poet regarded as the first great literary genius of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_Persian" title="Modern Persian">Modern Persian</a> language. Rudaki composed poems in the <a href="/wiki/Persian_alphabet" title="Persian alphabet">"New Persian" alphabet</a> and is considered a founder of <a href="/wiki/Persian_literature" title="Persian literature">classical Persian literature</a>. His poetry contains many of the oldest genres of Persian poetry including the <a href="/wiki/Quatrain" title="Quatrain">quatrain</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> however, only a small percentage of his extensive poetry has survived. Rudaki's "Nahr and 'Ayn," "Khing- but and Surkhbut," and "Wamiq and 'Azra" have prospered on the riches of the oral tradition of folklores. </p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rudaki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rudaki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rudaki was born in 858 in <a href="/wiki/Rudaki,_Tajikistan" title="Rudaki, Tajikistan">Rudak</a> (Panjrud), a village located in the <a href="/wiki/Samanid_Empire" title="Samanid Empire">Samanid Empire</a> which is now <a href="/wiki/Panjakent" title="Panjakent">Panjakent</a>, located in modern-day <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a>. Even though most of his biographers assert that he was completely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blindness" title="Blindness">blind</a>, some early biographers are silent about this or do not mention him as being born blind. His accurate knowledge and description of colors, as evident in his poetry, renders this assertion very doubtful. He was the court poet to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samanid" title="Samanid">Samanid</a> ruler <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasr_II_of_Samanid" title="Nasr II of Samanid">Nasr II</a> (914–943) in <a href="/wiki/Bukhara" title="Bukhara">Bukhara</a>, although he eventually fell out of favour; his life ended in poverty.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="At_the_Samanid_court">At the Samanid court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rudaki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: At the Samanid court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Early in his life, the fame of his accomplishments reached the ear of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samanid" title="Samanid">Samanid</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasr_II_of_Samanid" title="Nasr II of Samanid">Nasr II ibn Ahmad</a>, the ruler of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Khorasan" title="Greater Khorasan">Khorasan</a> and Transoxiana, who invited the poet to his court. Rudaki became his daily companion, amassed great wealth, and become highly honored. It is claimed that he well deserves the title of the father of <a href="/wiki/Persian_literature" title="Persian literature">Persian literature</a>, or the Adam or the Sultan of poets even though he had various predecessors, because he was the first who impressed upon every form of epic, lyric and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Didactic" title="Didactic">didactic</a> poetry its peculiar stamp and its individual character. He is also said to have been the founder of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deewan" title="Deewan">diwan</a></i>, or the typical form of the complete collection of a poet's lyrical compositions in a more or less alphabetical order, which all <a href="/wiki/Persian_literature" title="Persian literature">Persian</a> writers use even today. He was also very adept singer and instrumentalist (<a href="/wiki/Harp" title="Harp">Harp</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rudaki.27s_blindness">Rudaki's blindness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rudaki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rudaki's blindness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The common opinion was that Rudaki was born blind or was blind from his childhood. However, some of early biographies, like Samani and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nezami_Aruzi" title="Nezami Aruzi">Nezami Aruzi</a> do not emphasis his blindness as natural-born. <a href="/wiki/Ferdowsi" title="Ferdowsi">Ferdowsi</a> just mentions in his <a href="/wiki/Shahnameh" title="Shahnameh">Shahnameh</a> that they recited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pancatantra" title="Pancatantra">Kelileh o Demneh</a> to him and he rendered it into poem. Also using some of his poems we can see that he had sight:</p>

<p>The contemporary Iranian scholar, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Said_Nafisi" title="Said Nafisi">Said Nafisi</a>, has a book about Rudaki called <i>Biography, Environment and Time of Rudaki</i>. In pages 394-404, he refers to historical events and references in Persian books and poems, as well as the forensic findings of Russians in early 20th century including <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Mikhaylovich_Gerasimov" title="Mikhail Mikhaylovich Gerasimov">Mikhail Gerasimov</a> (who reconstructed Rudaki's face based on his bones found in his tomb, see above picture), concludes that Rudaki and Amir Nasr Samani were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ismaili" title="Ismaili">Ismailis</a> and there was a revolt against Ismalis around 940, a few years before Rudaki's death. This revolt led to the overthrow of the Samanid king and Rudaki, as his close companion, was tortured and blinded and his back was broken while they were blinding him. After this, Rudaki went back to the small town where he was born and died shortly after that. He was buried there.</p>

<p>Of the 1,300,000 verses attributed to him, only 52 <a href="/wiki/Qasida" title="Qasida">qasidas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ghazal" title="Ghazal">ghazals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ruba%27i" title="Ruba'i">rubais</a> survived; of his epic masterpieces we have nothing beyond a few stray lines in native dictionaries. However, the most serious loss is that of his translation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abdullah_Ibn_al-Muqaffa" title="Abdullah Ibn al-Muqaffa">Abdullah Ibn al-Muqaffa</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> version of the old <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> fable book <i>Kalila and Dimna</i> (<a href="/wiki/Panchatantra" title="Panchatantra">Panchatantra</a>), which he put into <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> verse at the request of his royal patron. Numerous fragments, however, are preserved in the Persian lexicon of <a href="/wiki/Asadi_Tusi" title="Asadi Tusi">Asadi Tusi</a> (the Lughat al-Furs, ed. P. Horn, <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingen" title="Göttingen">Göttingen</a>, 1897). In his qasidas - all of which are devoted to the praise of his sovereign and friend - unequalled models of a refined and delicate taste, very different from the often bombastic compositions of later Persian encomiasts, have survived. His didactic <a href="/wiki/Ode" title="Ode">odes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Epigram" title="Epigram">epigrams</a> expressed in well-measured lines a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epicurean" title="Epicurean">Epicurean</a> philosophy of life and human happiness, and more charming still are the purely lyrical pieces that glorify love and wine.</p>
<p>There is a complete edition of all the extant poems of Rudaki which were known at the end of the 19th century, in Persian text and metrical <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> translation, together with a biographical account, based on forty-six Persian manuscripts, in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hermann_Eth%C3%A9&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hermann Ethé (page does not exist)">Hermann Ethé</a>'s <i>Rudagi der Samanidendichter</i> (<i>Göttinger Nachrichten</i>, 1873, pp. 663–742); see also:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Neupersische Literatur</i> in <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Geiger" title="Wilhelm Geiger">Wilhelm Geiger</a>'s <i>Grundriss der iranischen Philologie</i> (ii.</li>
<li>Paul Horn, <i>Geschichte der persischen Literatur</i> (1901), p. 73</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E._G._Browne" title="E. G. Browne">E. G. Browne</a>, <i>Literary History of Persia</i>, i. (1902)</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=C._J._Pickering&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="C. J. Pickering (page does not exist)">C. J. Pickering</a>, <i>A Persian Chaucer</i> in <i>National Review</i> (May 1890).</li>
</ul>
<p>More recently, in 1963, Saʻīd Nafīsī identified more fragments to be attributed to Rudaki and has assembled them, together with an extensive biography, in <i>Muḥīṭ-i zindagī va aḥvāl va ashʻār-i Rūdakī.</i></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poetry">Poetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rudaki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Poetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sample Poetry:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>Look at the cloud, how it cries like a grieving man</dd>
<dd>Thunder moans like a lover with a broken heart.</dd>
<dd>Now and then the sun peeks from behind the clouds</dd>
<dd>Like a prisoner hiding from the guard.</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Celebrations">Celebrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rudaki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Celebrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For the 1100th anniversary of his birth, the <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iranian</a> government published a series of stamps showing his picture. An international seminar was held at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vahdat_Hall" title="Vahdat Hall">Vahdat Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> on 21 December 2008, to mark his 1150th birth anniversary, with President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahmadinejad" title="Ahmadinejad">Ahmadinejad</a> and "Culture minister" of Tajikistan in attendance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In this seminar, Rudaki was celebrated as the father of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_Persian" title="Modern Persian">Modern Persian</a> literature.</p>

<td class="navbox-abovebelow" colspan="2" style="padding:0.2em 0; background:lavender; line-height:1.2em;">
<div>Contemporary Persian and Classical Persian are the same language, but writers since 1900 are classified as contemporary. At one time, Persian was a common cultural language of much of the non-Arabic Islamic world. Today it is the official language of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a> and one of the two official languages of <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>.</div>
