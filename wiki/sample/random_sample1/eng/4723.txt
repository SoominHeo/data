<p><b>Sir Rudolf Ernst Peierls</b>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">CBE</a> (5 June 1907 – 19 September 1995)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was a German-born British <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> who had a major role in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons_and_the_United_Kingdom" title="Nuclear weapons and the United Kingdom">Britain's nuclear programme</a>, and also had a role in many modern sciences. His obituary in <i><a href="/wiki/Physics_Today" title="Physics Today">Physics Today</a></i> describes him as "a major player in the drama of the eruption of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">nuclear physics</a> into world affairs...".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edwards_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rudolf_Peierls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The son of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> parents, he assisted <a href="/wiki/Egon_Orowan" title="Egon Orowan">Egon Orowan</a> in understanding the <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> required to move a <a href="/wiki/Dislocation" title="Dislocation">dislocation</a> which would be expanded on by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Nabarro" title="Frank Nabarro">Frank Nabarro</a> and called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peierls%E2%80%93Nabarro_force" title="Peierls–Nabarro force">Peierls–Nabarro force</a>. In 1929, he studied <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_physics" title="Solid-state physics">solid-state physics</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zurich,_Switzerland" title="Zurich, Switzerland">Zurich</a> under the tutelage of <a href="/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg" title="Werner Heisenberg">Werner Heisenberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli" title="Wolfgang Pauli">Wolfgang Pauli</a>. His early work on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_physics" title="Quantum physics">quantum physics</a> led to the theory of <a href="/wiki/Electron_hole" title="Electron hole">positive carriers</a> to explain the thermal and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">electrical conductivity</a> behaviours of <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductors</a>. He was a pioneer of the concept of "holes" in semiconductors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He actually established "zones" before <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Brillouin" title="Léon Brillouin">Léon Brillouin</a>, despite Brillouin's name being currently attached to the idea, and applied it to <a href="/wiki/Phonon" title="Phonon">phonons</a>. Doing this, he discovered the <a href="/wiki/Boltzmann_equation" title="Boltzmann equation">Boltzmann equations</a> for phonons and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umklapp_process" title="Umklapp process">Umklapp process</a>. Physics Today states "His many papers on <a href="/wiki/Electronic_band_structure" title="Electronic band structure">electrons in metals</a> have now passed so deeply into <a href="/wiki/Scientific_literature" title="Scientific literature">the literature</a> that it is hard to identify his contribution to conductivity in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_fields" title="Magnetic fields">magnetic fields</a> and to the <a href="/wiki/Electron_hole" title="Electron hole">concept of a hole</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_electrons_in_solids" title="Theory of electrons in solids">theory of electrons in solids</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edwards_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leading_up_to_World_War_II">Leading up to World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rudolf_Peierls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Leading up to World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Poynting_Physics_building_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Poynting_Physics_building_2.jpg/220px-Poynting_Physics_building_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Poynting_Physics_building_2.jpg/330px-Poynting_Physics_building_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Poynting_Physics_building_2.jpg/440px-Poynting_Physics_building_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Poynting_Physics_building_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birmingham_University" title="Birmingham University">Birmingham University</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>He was studying on a Rockefeller Scholarship at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" title="Cambridge University">Cambridge University</a> when <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> came to power in his native Germany. Granted leave to remain in Britain, he worked in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> under a fund set up for refugees, with <a href="/wiki/Hans_Bethe" title="Hans Bethe">Hans Bethe</a> on <a href="/wiki/Photodisintegration" title="Photodisintegration">photodisintegration</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">statistical mechanics</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alloys" title="Alloys">alloys</a> when asked by <a href="/wiki/James_Chadwick" title="James Chadwick">James Chadwick</a>. Their results still serve as the basis for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean-field_theory" title="Mean-field theory">mean-field theories</a> of <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">structural phase changes</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">complete alloys</a>. Moving back to Cambridge, he worked with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=P._G._L._Kapur&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="P. G. L. Kapur (page does not exist)">P. G. L. Kapur</a> at the Mond Laboratory on <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconductivity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_helium" title="Liquid helium">liquid helium</a>. The group derived the dispersion formula for <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reaction" title="Nuclear reaction">nuclear reactions</a> originally given in <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_theory_(quantum_mechanics)" title="Perturbation theory (quantum mechanics)">perturbation theory</a> by <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Breit" title="Gregory Breit">Gregory Breit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Wigner" title="Eugene Wigner">Eugene Wigner</a>, but now included generalising conditions. This is now known as the Kapur–Peierls derivation. In 1937, he became Professor of Mathematical Physics at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Birmingham" title="University of Birmingham">University of Birmingham</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edwards_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rudolf_Peierls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1939, he started working on atomic research with <a href="/wiki/Otto_Robert_Frisch" title="Otto Robert Frisch">Otto Robert Frisch</a> and James Chadwick. Ironically, both Peierls and Frisch were excluded from working on <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> (then known as RDF) as it was considered too secret for scientists with foreign backgrounds.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frisch.E2.80.93Peierls_memorandum">Frisch–Peierls memorandum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rudolf_Peierls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Frisch–Peierls memorandum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Frisch%E2%80%93Peierls_memorandum" title="Frisch–Peierls memorandum">Frisch–Peierls memorandum</a></div>
<p>In March 1940, he co-authored the Frisch–Peierls memorandum with <a href="/wiki/Otto_Robert_Frisch" title="Otto Robert Frisch">Otto Robert Frisch</a>. This short paper was the first to set out how one could construct an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_bomb" title="Atomic bomb">atomic bomb</a> from a small amount of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fissile" title="Fissile">fissionable</a> <a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235">uranium-235</a>. They calculated that about 1 kg would be needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Until then it had been assumed that such a bomb would require many tons of uranium, and consequently was impractical to build and use. The paper was pivotal in igniting the interest of first the British and later the American authorities in atomic weapons. In 1941 its findings made their way to the United States through the report of the <a href="/wiki/MAUD_Committee" title="MAUD Committee">MAUD Committee</a>, an important trigger in the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a> and the subsequent development of the atomic bomb. He was also responsible for the recruitment of his compatriot <a href="/wiki/Klaus_Fuchs" title="Klaus Fuchs">Klaus Fuchs</a> to <a href="/wiki/British_contribution_to_the_Manhattan_Project" title="British contribution to the Manhattan Project">the British project</a>, an action which was to result in Peierls falling under suspicion when Fuchs was exposed as a Soviet spy in 1950. In 1999, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spectator" title="The Spectator">The Spectator</a></i> garnered outrage from his family when they alleged Rudolf Peierls was a spy codenamed "perls" for the Soviet Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manhattan_project">Manhattan project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rudolf_Peierls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Manhattan project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Quebec_Agreement" title="Quebec Agreement">Quebec Agreement</a> in August 1943, Peierls joined the Manhattan Project, located in the United States. Peierls was part of the British team, along with Klaus Fuchs, whom he recruited for the project. Peierls was initially located in New York and later at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos Laboratory">Los Alamos Laboratory</a>, where he played an important role in the development of the atomic bomb.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war">Post-war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rudolf_Peierls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Post-war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the war, Peierls reassumed his position in the physics department at the University of Birmingham where he worked until 1963 before joining the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> as Wykeham Professor of Physics. At Birmingham he worked on <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_force" title="Nuclear force">nuclear forces</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scattering" title="Scattering">scattering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theories</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_structure" title="Nuclear structure">collective motion in nuclei</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transport_theory_(statistical_physics)" title="Transport theory (statistical physics)">transport theory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">statistical mechanics</a>. Also while at Birmingham, he worked as a consultant to the British atomic programme at Harwell. He retired from Oxford in 1974. He wrote several books including <i>Quantum Theory of Solids</i>, <i>The Laws of Nature</i> (1955), <i>Surprises in Theoretical Physics</i> (1979), <i>More Surprises in Theoretical Physics</i> (1991) and an autobiography, <i>Bird of Passage</i> (1985). Concerned with the nuclear weapons he had helped to unleash, he worked on the <a href="/wiki/Bulletin_of_the_Atomic_Scientists" title="Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists">Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists</a>, was President of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_Scientists%27_Association_in_the_UK" title="Atomic Scientists' Association in the UK">Atomic Scientists' Association in the UK</a>, and was a major player in the <a href="/wiki/Pugwash_Conferences_on_Science_and_World_Affairs" title="Pugwash Conferences on Science and World Affairs">Pugwash movement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edwards_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours">Honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rudolf_Peierls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Peierls was appointed a <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">Commander of the Order of the British Empire (Civil Division)</a> in 1945<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and was <a href="/wiki/Knight_Bachelor" title="Knight Bachelor">knighted</a> in 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He was invited to deliver the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rutherford_Memorial_Lecture" title="Rutherford Memorial Lecture">Rutherford Memorial Lecture</a> in 1952, was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_Medal" title="Lorentz Medal">Lorentz Medal</a> in 1962, and in 1980 he received the <a href="/wiki/Enrico_Fermi_Award" title="Enrico Fermi Award">Enrico Fermi Award</a> from the United States Government for exceptional contribution to the science of atomic energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rudolf_Peierls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He attended a physics congress in <a href="/wiki/Odessa" title="Odessa">Odessa</a> in August 1930 and met a young physics student, Eugenia Kannegiesser (b.1908), from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leningrad" title="Leningrad">Leningrad</a>. English was their shared language though Eugenia's English was limited. During a visit to lecture in Leningrad in 1931 they married. However, she had to wait for a passport and exit visa. They finally left for Zürich that summer. They had four children, Gaby Ellen (b.1933), Ronald Frank (b.1935), Catherine (Kitty; b.1948), and Joanna (b.1949). Eugenia died in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODNB_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
