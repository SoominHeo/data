<p><b>Ludvík Vaculík</b> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Czech" title="Help:IPA for Czech">[ˈludviːk ˈvatsuˌliːk]</a></span> (23 July 1926 – 6 June 2015) was a <a href="/wiki/Czechs" title="Czechs">Czech</a> <a href="/wiki/Writer" title="Writer">writer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Journalist" title="Journalist">journalist</a>. He was born in <a href="/wiki/Brumov-Bylnice" title="Brumov-Bylnice">Brumov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moravian_Wallachia" title="Moravian Wallachia">Moravian Wallachia</a>. A prominent <a href="/wiki/Samizdat" title="Samizdat">samizdat</a> writer, he was best known as the author of the "<a href="/wiki/The_Two_Thousand_Words" title="The Two Thousand Words">Two Thousand Words</a>" <a href="/wiki/Manifesto" title="Manifesto">manifesto</a> of June 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-1968">Pre-1968</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludv%C3%ADk_Vacul%C3%ADk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pre-1968">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>President of <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Communist Party of Czechoslovakia">Communist Party</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Anton%C3%ADn_Novotn%C3%BD" title="Antonín Novotný">Antonín Novotný</a> and his fellow conservatives had begun taking a more repressive approach toward intellectuals and writers after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Six_Day_War" title="Six Day War">Six Day War</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> of June 1967. The following month, Vaculík, then still a member of the Communist Party, attended the Fourth Congress of the Union of Writers. Others in attendance included communists <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Kohout" title="Pavel Kohout">Pavel Kohout</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Kl%C3%ADma" title="Ivan Klíma">Ivan Klíma</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Milan_Kundera" title="Milan Kundera">Milan Kundera</a>, as well as non-Party member <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A1clav_Havel" title="Václav Havel">Václav Havel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Vaculík made an inflammatory speech in which he rejected the leading role of the party as unnecessary and criticized it for its restrictive cultural policies and failure to address social issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crampton.2C_Eastern_Europe.2C_323_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crampton.2C_Eastern_Europe.2C_323-4">[4]</a></sup> Havel recalled the mixed response of the fellow writers to Vaculík's remarks: on the one hand, they were “delighted that someone had spoken the truth… but [their] delight was tempered by doubts about whether direct confrontation on the political level would lead anywhere, and by fears that it could stimulate a counterattack by the power center.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Novotný and his supporters did indeed try to bring the writers' union under their control after the congress, but failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crampton.2C_Eastern_Europe.2C_323_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Crampton.2C_Eastern_Europe.2C_323-4">[4]</a></sup> Vaculík's and other writers' speeches at the conference, with their anti-Novotný sentiments, increased the gap between the conservative Novotný supporters and more moderate members of the party leadership,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> a division that would contribute to Novotný's eventual fall.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Prague_Spring_and_the_.22Two_Thousand_Words.22">The Prague Spring and the "Two Thousand Words"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludv%C3%ADk_Vacul%C3%ADk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title='Edit section: The Prague Spring and the "Two Thousand Words"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vaculík was among the most progressive members of the Communist Party and thereby more radical than <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek" title="Alexander Dubček">Alexander Dubček</a>, who had become Party leader in January 1968. Hence, Vaculík and others generally felt that the reforms of the <a href="/wiki/Action_Programme_(1968)" title="Action Programme (1968)">April Action Programme</a> were the minimum necessary and that they should be quickly and firmly enforced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In hopes of influencing voters in upcoming party congress elections, Vaculík released the manifesto "Two Thousand Words to Workers, Farmers, Scientists, Artists, and Everyone" in several major <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> newspapers, complete with signatures of other public figures. The date was 27 June 1968, the day after preliminary censorship was abolished by the national assembly.</p>
<p>In the "Two Thousand Words," Vaculík asked that the public “demand the resignation of people who have misused their power” by criticism, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demonstration_(people)" title="Demonstration (people)">demonstrations</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Strike_action" title="Strike action">strikes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He also expressed concern over the "recent apprehension" regarding the reforms due to "the possibility that foreign forces"—those of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warsaw_Treaty_Organization" title="Warsaw Treaty Organization">Warsaw Treaty Organization</a>—"may intervene in Czechoslovakia's internal development."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vacul.C3.ADk.2C_Two_Thousand_Words.2C_130_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vacul.C3.ADk.2C_Two_Thousand_Words.2C_130-9">[9]</a></sup> If this were to happen, Vaculík argued:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>…the only thing we can do is to hold our own and not indulge in any provocation. We can assure our government—with weapons if need be—as long as it does what we give it a mandate to do.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vacul.C3.ADk.2C_Two_Thousand_Words.2C_130_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vacul.C3.ADk.2C_Two_Thousand_Words.2C_130-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_of_the_.22Two_Thousand_Words.22">Impact of the "Two Thousand Words"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludv%C3%ADk_Vacul%C3%ADk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title='Edit section: Impact of the "Two Thousand Words"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite the overall moderate tone and <a href="/wiki/Marxism%E2%80%93Leninism" title="Marxism–Leninism">Marxist–Leninist</a> orthodoxy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crampton.2C_Eastern_Europe.2C_334_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crampton.2C_Eastern_Europe.2C_334-10">[10]</a></sup> the "Two Thousand Words" called for action on the part of the public in case of military intervention and therefore denied the leading role of the party, as Vaculík's 1967 speech had. It was popular throughout Czechoslovakia with both intellectuals and workers, and its popularity only increased after the party officially condemned it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crampton.2C_Eastern_Europe.2C_334_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Crampton.2C_Eastern_Europe.2C_334-10">[10]</a></sup> It also significantly increased the concerns of the Soviet Union. Following the “Two Thousand Words,” <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Brezhnev" title="Leonid Brezhnev">Leonid Brezhnev</a>'s party leadership, seeing a situation similar to that in <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_Revolution_of_1956" title="Hungarian Revolution of 1956">1956 Hungary</a> developing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> used the term "counterrevolution" to describe the <a href="/wiki/Prague_Spring" title="Prague Spring">Prague Spring</a> for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> If a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counterrevolution" title="Counterrevolution">counterrevolution</a> was taking place (and the Soviet Union was increasingly disposed to categorising the events in Czechoslovakia as such, as other radicals continued to act and Dubček failed to gain their confidence), <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a> as the Soviet Union saw it was threatened and invasion by Warsaw Treaty Organisation troops, as occurred 20–21 August 1968, was deemed justified. This policy of the acceptability of using force wherever socialism was thought to be threatened would become known as the <a href="/wiki/Brezhnev_Doctrine" title="Brezhnev Doctrine">Brezhnev Doctrine</a>, and Vaculík's "Two Thousand Words" was an integral step toward this early application of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vacul.C3.ADk_as_a_dissident">Vaculík as a dissident</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludv%C3%ADk_Vacul%C3%ADk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vaculík as a dissident">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Gust%C3%A1v_Hus%C3%A1k" title="Gustáv Husák">Gustáv Husák</a> came to power in 1969 and censorship increased, Vaculík (now no longer a party member) was part of the circle of <a href="/wiki/Dissident" title="Dissident">dissident</a> writers in Czechoslovakia. In 1973, he started <i>Edice Petlice</i> (<i>Edition Padlock</i>), a samizdat series that he ran until 1979. Others followed with their own series, despite harassment from the party's secret police.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Some samizdat authors, including Vaculík, were also published in the west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The core of the samizdat authors eventually developed and signed the foundation document of <a href="/wiki/Charter_77" title="Charter 77">Charter 77</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Vaculík attended the second of the planning meetings in December 1976.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> On 6 January 1977, Vaculík, along with Havel and <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Landovsk%C3%BD" title="Pavel Landovský">Pavel Landovský</a>, an actor, attempted to take a copy of the charter to the post office to mail to the Czechoslovak government. Their car was pulled over by the Party secret police, and all three were taken in for interrogation. Other signatories were subsequently subjected to interrogations and searches of their homes, as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 1978, however, Vaculík published the article "Remarks on Courage", a piece that helped set the tone for criticism of charterists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-19">[19]</a></sup> Of the original signatories, most were from the intelligentsia in Prague and <a href="/wiki/Brno" title="Brno">Brno</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and Vaculík and others warned against them becoming so isolated that average citizens could no longer relate to Charter 77. His criticism worked against a <a href="/wiki/Mythology#Myths_as_depictions_of_historical_events" title="Mythology">mythologisation</a> of the Charter and ensured a continued discussion of its position and role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="After_communism">After communism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludv%C3%ADk_Vacul%C3%ADk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: After communism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vaculík continued to write; the official ban on his works was lifted in late 1989. He had a weekly column in <i><a href="/wiki/Lidov%C3%A9_noviny" title="Lidové noviny">Lidové noviny</a></i> that featured <a href="/wiki/Feuilleton" title="Feuilleton">feuilletons</a> addressing various Czech political and cultural issues, just as much of his underground work during Communism had.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludv%C3%ADk_Vacul%C3%ADk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vaculík died of natural causes at the age of 88 on 6 June 2015 in Prague.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
