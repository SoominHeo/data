<p><b>Comet Lulin</b> (official designation <b>C/2007 N3 (Lulin)</b>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese" title="Traditional Chinese">Traditional Chinese</a>:鹿林彗星) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-periodic_comet" title="Non-periodic comet">non-periodic comet</a>. It was discovered by Ye Quanzhi and Lin Chi-Sheng from <a href="/wiki/Lulin_Observatory" title="Lulin Observatory">Lulin Observatory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gwkc_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-gwkc-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aerith_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-aerith-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skynews1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Skynews1-7">[7]</a></sup> It peaked in brightness and arrived at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">perigee</a> for observers on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> on February 24, 2009,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpl-close_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-jpl-close-8">[8]</a></sup> at <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">magnitude</a> +5,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aerith_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-aerith-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-magnitude_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-magnitude-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skyandtelescope_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Skyandtelescope-11">[11]</a></sup> and at 0.411 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> from Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpl-close_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-jpl-close-8">[8]</a></sup> The comet was near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjunction_(astronomy_and_astrology)" title="Conjunction (astronomy and astrology)">conjunction</a> with <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a> on February 23, and passed near <a href="/wiki/Regulus" title="Regulus">Regulus</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Leo_(constellation)" title="Leo (constellation)">Leo</a> on February 26 and 27, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aerith_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-aerith-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skynews1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Skynews1-7">[7]</a></sup> It was expected to pass near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comet_Cardinal" title="Comet Cardinal">Comet Cardinal</a> on May 12, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The comet became visible to the naked eye from dark-sky sites around February 7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It passed near the <a href="/wiki/Double_star" title="Double star">double star</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zubenelgenubi" title="Zubenelgenubi">Zubenelgenubi</a> on February 6, near <a href="/wiki/Spica" title="Spica">Spica</a> on February 15 and 16, near <a href="/wiki/Gamma_Virginis" title="Gamma Virginis">Gamma Virginis</a> on February 19 and near the <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">star cluster</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Messier_44" title="Messier 44">M44</a> on March 5 and 6. It also passed near the <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nebula" title="Planetary nebula">planetary nebula</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NGC_2392" title="NGC 2392">NGC 2392</a> on March 14, and near the <a href="/wiki/Double_star" title="Double star">double star</a> <a href="/wiki/Delta_Geminorum" title="Delta Geminorum">Wasat</a> around March 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, Comet Lulin's <a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">green</a> color comes from a combination of gases that make up its local atmosphere, primarily <a href="/wiki/Diatomic_carbon" title="Diatomic carbon">diatomic carbon</a>, which appears as a green glow when illuminated by <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> of space. When <a href="/wiki/Swift_Gamma-Ray_Burst_Mission" title="Swift Gamma-Ray Burst Mission">SWIFT</a> observed comet Lulin on 28 January 2009, the comet was shedding nearly 800 gallons (approximately 3028 liters) of water each second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SWIFT_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-SWIFT-16">[16]</a></sup> Comet Lulin was <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>-rich.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comet_Lulin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The comet was first photographed by <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> Lin Chi-Sheng (林啟生) with a 0.41-metre (16 in) telescope at the Lulin Observatory in <a href="/wiki/Nantou_County" title="Nantou County">Nantou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> on July 11, 2007. However, it was the 19-year-old Ye Quanzhi (葉泉志) from <a href="/wiki/Sun_Yat-sen_University" title="Sun Yat-sen University">Sun Yat-sen University</a> in China who identified the new object from three of the photographs taken by Lin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC8857_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-IAUC8857-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Initially, the object was thought to be a magnitude 18.9 <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a>, but images taken a week after the discovery with a larger 0.61-metre (24 in) telescope revealed the presence of a faint <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coma_(astronomy)" title="Coma (astronomy)">coma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gwkc_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-gwkc-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEC2007-O05_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-MPEC2007-O05-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC8857_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-IAUC8857-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The discovery occurred as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lulin_Sky_Survey" title="Lulin Sky Survey">Lulin Sky Survey</a> project to identify small objects in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-Earth_Object" title="Near-Earth Object">Near-Earth Objects</a>. The comet was named "Comet Lulin" after the observatory, and its official designation is Comet C/2007 N3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-space-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">Orbit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comet_Lulin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Astronomer <a href="/wiki/Brian_G._Marsden" title="Brian G. Marsden">Brian Marsden</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Astrophysical_Observatory" title="Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory">Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory</a> calculated that Comet Lulin reached its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a> on January 10, 2009, at a distance of 113 million miles (182 million kilometers) from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>.</p>
<p>The orbit of Comet Lulin is very nearly a <a href="/wiki/Parabola" title="Parabola">parabola</a> (<a href="/wiki/Parabolic_trajectory" title="Parabolic trajectory">parabolic trajectory</a>), according to Marsden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-space-18">[18]</a></sup> The comet had an <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(astronomy)" title="Epoch (astronomy)">epoch</a> 2009 eccentricity of 0.9999<u>86</u>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU-MPC_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-IAU-MPC-6">[6]</a></sup> and has an epoch 2010 eccentricity of 0.9999<u>98</u>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU-MPC_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-IAU-MPC-6">[6]</a></sup> It is moving in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">retrograde orbit</a> at a very low inclination of just 1.6° from the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-space-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Given the extreme <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">orbital eccentricity</a> of this object, different <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(astronomy)" title="Epoch (astronomy)">epochs</a> can generate quite different heliocentric unperturbed <a href="/wiki/Two-body_problem" title="Two-body problem">two-body</a> <a href="/wiki/Curve_fitting" title="Curve fitting">best-fit</a> solutions to the aphelion distance (maximum distance) of this object. For objects at such high eccentricity, the Suns <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barycentric_coordinates_(astronomy)" title="Barycentric coordinates (astronomy)">barycentric coordinates</a> are more stable than heliocentric coordinates. Using <a href="/wiki/JPL_Horizons_On-Line_Ephemeris_System" title="JPL Horizons On-Line Ephemeris System">JPL Horizons</a>, the barycentric orbital elements for epoch 2014-Jan-01 generate a semi-major axis of about 1200 AU and a period of about 42,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barycenter_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-barycenter-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disconnected_tail">Disconnected tail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comet_Lulin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Disconnected tail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On February 4, 2009, a team of <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italian</a> astronomers witnessed "an intriguing phenomenon in Comet Lulin's tail". Team leader Ernesto Guido explains: "We photographed the comet using a remotely controlled telescope in New Mexico, and our images clearly showed a disconnection event. While we were looking, part of the comet's plasma tail was torn away."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disconnect_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-disconnect-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Guido and colleagues believe the event was caused by a magnetic disturbance in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> hitting the comet. Magnetic mini-storms in comet tails have been observed before—most famously in 2007 when NASA's <a href="/wiki/STEREO" title="STEREO">STEREO</a> spacecraft watched a <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">coronal mass ejection</a> crash into <a href="/wiki/Comet_Encke" title="Comet Encke">Comet Encke</a>. Encke lost its tail in dramatic fashion, much as Comet Lulin did on February 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disconnect_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-disconnect-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
