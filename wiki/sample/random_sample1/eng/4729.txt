<p><b>Lubyanka</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Лубя́нка</span>) is a station on the <a href="/wiki/Sokolnicheskaya_Line" title="Sokolnicheskaya Line">Sokolnicheskaya Line</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Metro" title="Moscow Metro">Moscow Metro</a>, located under <a href="/wiki/Lubyanka_Square" title="Lubyanka Square">Lubyanka Square</a>. It opened in 1935 as part of the first stage of the Metro.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lubyanka_(Moscow_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Construction work on the station began in December 1933, and the engineers were faced with extremely difficult soil conditions from the outset. The area under Lubyanka square is made of <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>, beneath which are layers of <a href="/wiki/Quicksand" title="Quicksand">quicksand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> clay. The Metro station was planned to rest on top of the Carboniferous clay, which was thought to be firm enough to support its weight. It was quickly discovered, however, that the clay was much softer than anticipated due to the proximity of an underground channel of the <a href="/wiki/Neglinnaya_River" title="Neglinnaya River">Neglinnaya River</a> and also tended to swell greatly when exposed to air. This meant that the tunnels had to be built one section at a time, very quickly, in order to allow the <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> to set before the pressure exerted by the expanding clay increased to the point where the wooden forms could no longer contain it.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Ladovsky" title="Nikolai Ladovsky">Nikolai Ladovsky</a>'s initial design had to be significantly modified in order to cope with these problems. In order to minimize the amount of excavation required, the planned full-length central hall was abandoned in favour of a short passage at the end of the station connecting the two platform tubes, similar to many <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a> stations. This simplified the construction of the station while still allowing the planned hall to be built in the future.</p>
<p>Even after the design change, the station's construction was plagued by difficulties. Quicksand from between the two layers of clay began to seep into the construction site almost immediately, and due to the unexpected softness of the Carboniferous clay the station began to slowly sink. Nevertheless, the builders of the station were able to surmount these problems and Lubyanka was opened as planned on May 15, 1935. The heroic efforts of the Metro builders are memorialized in a sculptural composition in the vestibule on Nikolskaya street.</p>
<p>In 1965 it was revealed that Lubyanka was to become a transfer point to the planned <a href="/wiki/Tagansko-Krasnopresnenskaya_Line" title="Tagansko-Krasnopresnenskaya Line">Tagansko-Krasnopresnenskaya Line</a>, making it clear that the station would need to be completed. Technology had advanced to the point where the building of the central hall, which had been deemed impossible in the 1930s, could finally be accomplished, though this project still took more than seven years to complete. The first phase of the station's expansion was the construction of a second entrance at the northern end, which was completed in 1968.</p>
<p>The construction of the northern half of the central hall was simplified by using the new technique of soil freezing, but this could not be used on the southern half. Workers were forced to go back to the original method of quickly building one tunnel segment at a time before the expansion of the clay could crush them. Once the central hall was finished passages to the platforms were blown through using explosives. In 1972 the station was finally reopened.</p>
<p>The reconstruction of the station was an engineering triumph, but it was much less impressive aesthetically with blocky white <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a> <a href="/wiki/Pylon_station" title="Pylon station">pylons</a> and white tiled walls replacing the strikingly patterned dark marble used in the old station. Though the old section of the central hall still exists, the overall effect has been lost. The architects of the expansion were <a href="/wiki/Nina_Alexandrovna_Aleshin" title="Nina Alexandrovna Aleshin">Nina Alexandrovna Aleshin</a> and A.F. Strelkov. In 1975, <a href="/wiki/Escalator" title="Escalator">escalators</a> were added in the centre of the platform for the transfer to the new <a href="/wiki/Kuznetsky_Most_(Moscow_Metro)" title="Kuznetsky Most (Moscow Metro)">Kuznetsky Most</a> station.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Entrances_and_transfers">Entrances and transfers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lubyanka_(Moscow_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Entrances and transfers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The station has numerous entrances, but only two vestibules. The original western vestibule is subterranean and its entrance pavilion is built into the ground floor of a building that stands at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Nikolskaya_Street" title="Nikolskaya Street">Nikolskaya Street</a> on one side and Malyy Cherkassky side street on the other. The distinctive facade, facing the Lubyanka square, with twin arches was designed by architect Iosif Loveyko, to remind the public of the historic Vladimir gates of the <a href="/wiki/Kitay-gorod" title="Kitay-gorod">Kitay-gorod</a> wall that was demolished a few years prior. In 1957 a subway was built connecting the vestibule and the new Detsky Mir (Children's World) shop, under the Teatralny drive. The original 3 N-type escalators were replaced in 1997 by ET-3M type (21.8 metres high), during which the vestibule underwent renovation.</p>
<p>The second entrance was opened in 1968, due to the landscape and the axis of the station platform to vestibule is done via two escalators and an interim mezzanine station that rotates the passenger traffic by 180 degrees. The large incline from platform to the mezzanine retains the three LT-4 21.4 metre high escalators. The 7 metre high smaller incline to the subterranean vestibule originally had three LP-6I escalators, but as this model proved troublesome after long use, it was prematurely replaced throughout the Metro during the 1990s. Lubyanka's turn came in 1995 and three ET-5Ms now soldier in their place.</p>
<p>Building of the vestibule included a reconstruction of the whole square, that feature a long subway under the square's square's eastern and southern perimeter: two entrances on the corner of the <a href="/wiki/Lubyanka_Building" title="Lubyanka Building">FSB headquarters</a> and a service drive along its western facade, two on the corner of FSB's computing centre (Lubyasky drive and Myasnitskaya street), a further two to the small garden before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polytechnical_Museum" title="Polytechnical Museum">Polytechnical Museum</a> the containing the <a href="/wiki/Solovetsky_Stone" title="Solovetsky Stone">Solovetsky Stone</a> memorial (Lubyansky drive and Novaya square) and a final two right in front of the original entrance. From the new subway, a small passageway was added linking the old vestibule underground. It is thus possible to circumvent the whole square underground and walk from one vestibule to the other without descending to the platforms.</p>
<p>In 2008 the Detsky Mir was closed for reconstruction, and the original building that the entrance was built into was demolished in 2010 and a <a href="/wiki/Kempinski" title="Kempinski">Kempinski</a> hotel is being erected in its place, though the ground floor and facade are retained. Thus the only way to access the original vestibule is via the mentioned passageway.</p>
<p>The station serves as a transfer point to <a href="/wiki/Kuznetsky_Most_(Moscow_Metro)" title="Kuznetsky Most (Moscow Metro)">Kuznetsky Most</a>, which linked by a passenger tunnel under the station. Access to which is provided by a pair of tandem escalators in the middle of the central hall. Like on the entrance, the original four LP-6I units, in place since 1975, were replaced in 1993 by ET-5M series.</p>
<p>The station was originally named <b>Dzerzhinskaya</b> after Dzerzhinsky Square, but it was changed on 5 November 1990 after the square's original name, Lubyanka, was restored. There is still a bust of <a href="/wiki/Felix_Dzerzhinsky" title="Felix Dzerzhinsky">Felix Dzerzhinsky</a> in the station vestibule.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_bombing">2010 bombing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lubyanka_(Moscow_Metro)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2010 bombing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/2010_Moscow_Metro_bombings" title="2010 Moscow Metro bombings">2010 Moscow Metro bombings</a>.</div>
<p>On 29 March 2010 a bomb was reported to have gone off at the Lubyanka station, on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sokolnicheskaya" title="Sokolnicheskaya">Sokolnicheskaya</a> (Сокольническая), during rush hour, at 7:56 AM local time. Early reports suggest that 40 people have been killed, 14 of whom were in the second train carriage where the explosion is believed to have taken place.</p>
<p>A second explosion was reported at <a href="/wiki/Park_Kultury_(Sokolnicheskaya_Line)" title="Park Kultury (Sokolnicheskaya Line)">Park Kultury station</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
