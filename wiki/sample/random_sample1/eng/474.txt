<p>The <b>2014–15 South-West Indian Ocean cyclone season</b> was an event of the annual cycle of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclone</a> formation. It began on November 15, 2014, and ended on April 30, 2015, with the exception for <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a>, for which it ended on May 15, 2015. These dates conventionally delimit the period of each year when most tropical and subtropical cyclones form in the basin, which is west of <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">90°E</a> and south of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a>. Tropical and subtropical cyclones in this basin are monitored by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regional_Specialised_Meteorological_Centre" title="Regional Specialised Meteorological Centre">Regional Specialised Meteorological Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a>.</p>

<p>During October 2014, the Mauritius Meteorological Services issued its seasonal outlook for the summer of 2014–15 and predicted that between ten and twelve named storms would develop during the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The first tropical disturbance of the season developed on November 16, and quickly developed into the first named storm of the season and was named Adjali by Mauritius.</p>

<p>An area of low pressure developed close to <a href="/wiki/Diego_Garcia" title="Diego Garcia">Diego Garcia</a> on November 14. It slowly organized as it made a northwest-south-southeast loop over the next two days. By the evening of November 16, the center of the storm became more well defined with convective <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainbands" title="Rainbands">rainbands</a> wrapped tightly into it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Around that time, RSMC La Réunion started tracking the system as a tropical disturbance,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and subsequently upgraded it to a Tropical Depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Later that day, the JTWC issued a tropical cyclone formation alert on the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The depression strengthened further and became the first named tropical storm of the year, on the same day. Mauritius Meteorological Service, which usually names storms in the region, named the system <i>Adjali</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The JTWC also initiated advisories on Adjali.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> On November 17, MFR upgraded it to a Severe Tropical Storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">On November 20, Adjali turned west until reached the northern tip of Madagascar on November 23 and rapidly dissipated.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>During November 25, RSMC La Reunion reported that Tropical Disturbance 2, had developed within a marginal environment for further development to the northeast of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mascarene_islands" title="Mascarene islands">Mascarene islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCFW_1125_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCFW_1125-9">[9]</a></sup> <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">During that day despite having a low-level circulation centre that was located to the east of the deepest atmospheric convection, the system developed into a tropical depression. The depression continued to intensified as it was classified as a tropical storm by the JTWC early on November 28. The next day, the system moved in a southwestward direction and cooler waters. With this, the MFR downgraded it to a tropical disturbance again on November 29, with the JTWC downgrading it to a tropical depression.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>On January 9, the MFR upgraded a low-pressure system east of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> to a zone of disturbed weather, and the system became a tropical disturbance late on the next day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> On January 11, the MFR upgraded the system to a tropical depression. Later that day, it intensified into a moderate tropical storm, receiving the name Bansi, whist the JTWC upgraded it to a tropical storm. In the next day, the MFR upgraded Bansi to a tropical cyclone, as the system formed a ragged <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">On January 13, Bansi <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">explosively intensified</a> into a Category 5 cyclone. However, it soon weakened to a Category 2 on the SSHWS (intense tropical cyclone for MFR) due to an eyewall replacement cycle.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">It re-intensified slowly to a Category 4 on the SSHWS as it moved East-Southeast until January 13. Then it slowly curved Southeast and impacted <a href="/wiki/Rodrigues" title="Rodrigues">Rodrigues</a> on January 14.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Approximately 90 percent of the island was left without power as a result of the storm. Some flooding occurred and 115 people sought refuge in shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> From this moment onwards it started weakening gradually at first, but then deteriorated quickly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">On January 16, Bansi began to undergo an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extratropical_transition" title="Extratropical transition">extratropical transition</a>. Consequently, the MFR and JTWC issued Bansi's final warning, as Bansi was absorbed by an extratropical cyclone to the northeast.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p><span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">On January 11, the JTWC started to monitor a weak tropical disturbance inland over <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>. On January 14, the MFR upgraded a low-pressure system east of Mozambique to a zone of disturbed weather, and the system became a tropical disturbance late on the same day. On January 15, the MFR upgraded the system to a tropical depression. Later next day, it intensified into a moderate tropical storm, receiving the name Chedza, whist the JTWC upgraded it to a tropical storm. On the same day, MFR upgraded it to a Severe Tropical Storm. Later that day, it made landfall over <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>. On January 17, it left Madagascar and went to the South-West Indian Ocean. On the next day, it re-intensified to a Severe Tropical Storm. On January 19, Chedza began to undergo an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extratropical_transition" title="Extratropical transition">extratropical transition</a>. Consequently, the MFR issued Chedza's final warning.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The formative stages of Chedza brought rainfall to an already flooded region across southeastern Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reut128_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-reut128-19">[19]</a></sup> Weeks of heavy rainfall killed 117 people in Mozambique and 104 in neighboring <a href="/wiki/Malawi" title="Malawi">Malawi</a>, where it was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2015_Malawi_floods" title="2015 Malawi floods">worst floods</a> in 24 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-f29_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-f29-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mag_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-mag-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In Madagascar, Chedza struck after weeks of heavy rainfall,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> causing rivers to increase and flooding widespread areas of crop fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdf2_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-pdf2-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In the capital city of <a href="/wiki/Antananarivo" title="Antananarivo">Antananarivo</a>, the deluge damaged the main water pump that controlled water levels in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rc22_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-rc22-26">[26]</a></sup> The rainfall caused mudslides and damaged roads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdf2_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-pdf2-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afp127_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-afp127-27">[27]</a></sup> Across the country, flooding from Chedza displaced 54,792 people, after destroying 4,430 houses and flooding another 3,442, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Vatovavy-Fitovinany" title="Vatovavy-Fitovinany">Vatovavy-Fitovinany</a> in the southeastern portion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdf2_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-pdf2-24">[24]</a></sup> Chedza killed 80 people and caused about $40 million in damage (2015 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reut128_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-reut128-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-f29_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-f29-20">[20]</a></sup> Later, the storm brought heavy rainfall and strong winds to the mountainous peaks of Réunion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mf_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-mf-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p><span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">On January 26, a tropical low formed east-southeast of <a href="/wiki/Diego_Garcia" title="Diego Garcia">Diego Garcia</a>. It slowly moved towards it, before the MFR upgraded the low into a zone of disturbed weather. Later that day, the area of disturbed weather intensified into a depression, as the MFR and JTWC issued their initial advisories, and designated it as <i>08S</i>. On January 27, MFR reported that Tropical Depression 08S intensified into a moderate tropical storm, and so was issued the name <i>Diamondra</i>. Later that day, Diamondra continued to strengthen, and late on January 28, Diamondra reached moderate tropical storm intensity. Diamondra weakened, after moving over cooler waters. Late on January 29, Diamondra continued to weaken, so JTWC issued Diamondra's final warning. On January 30, Diamondra began to undergo an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extratropical_transition" title="Extratropical transition">extratropical transition</a>. Two days later, it was reported that Diamondra had become extratropical, and MFR announced their final advisory on the system soon after.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p><span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">On March 7, a tropical disturbance formed east of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>. It intensified into a zone of disturbed weather and became a tropical disturbance. It became Tropical Depression 12 by MFR, within the JTWC's designation <i>16S</i> on March 8, and became Moderate Tropical Storm Haliba. The next day, Haliba continued to intensify.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>During the early stages of Haliba's development it produced heavy rains across eastern Madagascar, resulting in severe flooding. A total of 26 people lost their lives on the island while approximately 96,000 people were affected, 39,000 of whom were rendered homeless. Roughly 13,000 hectares (32,000 acres) of rice fields were destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Torrential rains also affected Mauritius for three days, leading to damaging floods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> A 24‑hour rainfall of 135.6 mm (5.34 in) was observed at <a href="/wiki/Ganga_Talao" title="Ganga Talao">Ganga Talao</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> While passing near Réunion, the cyclone produced torrential rain over the northern areas of the island. Total accumulations peaked at 796 mm (31.3 in) in <a href="/wiki/Salazie" title="Salazie">Salazie</a>. Although heavy, the rains were noted as normal for a tropical cyclone. Wind gusts were not as strong as initially forecast and did not exceed 100 km/h (62 mph); a peak gust of 91 km/h (57 mph) was measured in Bellevue Bras Panon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Agricultural damage in the region amounted to <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">€</a>6 million (US$6.4 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

<p><span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">On April 5, Tropical Depression 14 developed just west of the border of the Australian region basin. Later that day, the JTWC designated it as <i>21S</i>. Due to a favorable environment, the system strengthened quickly, with MFR upgrading it to a tropical cyclone and was named Ikola as it crossed into the Australian region the next day.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_systems">Other systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2014%E2%80%9315_South-West_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Other systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Early on December 13, Tropical Cyclone Bakung moved into the basin from the Australian region as a Category 1 tropical cyclone on the Australian tropical cyclone intensity scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITCZ_Dec_13_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-ITCZ_Dec_13-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jakarta_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jakarta-39">[39]</a></sup> However, during that day the system's low-level circulation centre became exposed and displaced about 280 km (175 mi) from the deep convection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITCZ_Dec_13_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-ITCZ_Dec_13-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCFW_Bakung_1_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCFW_Bakung_1-40">[40]</a></sup> As a result, TCWC Jakarta and the JTWC issued their final warnings on the system, while RSMC La Reunion declared it to be a remnant low in their first and only warning on the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jakarta_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jakarta-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCFW_Bakung_1_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCFW_Bakung_1-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_13.2F12_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_13.2F12-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storm_names">Storm names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2014%E2%80%9315_South-West_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Storm names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Within the South-West Indian Ocean, tropical depressions and subtropical depressions that are judged to have 10-minute sustained windspeeds of 65 km/h, (40 mph) by the <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Center" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Center">Regional Specialized Meteorological Center</a> on <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">La Réunion Island</a>, France (RSMC La Réunion) are usually assigned a name. However, it is the Sub-Regional Tropical Cyclone Advisory Centers in Mauritius and Madagascar who name the systems. The Sub-Regional Tropical Cyclone Advisory Center in Mauritius names the storm should it intensify into a moderate tropical storm between <a href="/wiki/55th_meridian_east" title="55th meridian east">55°E</a> and <a href="/wiki/90th_meridian_east" title="90th meridian east">90°E</a>, if the storm should intensify into a moderate tropical storm between <a href="/wiki/30th_meridian_east" title="30th meridian east">30°E</a> and <a href="/wiki/55th_meridian_east" title="55th meridian east">55°E</a> then the Sub-Regional Tropical Cyclone Advisory Center in Madagascar assigns the appropriate name to the storm. New name lists are used every year, while a name is normally only used once so there are currently no names are retired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOP-SWIO_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCOP-SWIO-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of note is the presence of the name Nathan in this list. This is unrelated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclone_Nathan" title="Cyclone Nathan">Cyclone Nathan</a> which coincidentally formed during the same cyclone season in the neighbouring Australian region.</p>
