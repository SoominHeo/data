<p><b>Louis XII</b> (27 June 1462 – 1 January 1515) was a monarch of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Valois" title="House of Valois">House of Valois</a> who ruled as <a href="/wiki/List_of_French_monarchs" title="List of French monarchs">King of France</a> from 1498 to 1515 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Naples" title="King of Naples">King of Naples</a> from 1501 to 1504. The son of <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Duke_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Charles, Duke of Orléans">Charles, Duke of Orléans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Maria_of_Cleves" title="Maria of Cleves">Maria of Cleves</a>, he succeeded his cousin <a href="/wiki/Charles_VIII_of_France" title="Charles VIII of France">Charles VIII</a>, who died without a closer heir in 1498.</p>
<p>Before his accession to the throne of France, he was known as <b>Louis of Orléans</b> and was compelled to be married to his disabled and supposedly <a href="/wiki/Sterility_(physiology)" title="Sterility (physiology)">sterile</a> cousin <a href="/wiki/Joan_of_France,_Duchess_of_Berry" title="Joan of France, Duchess of Berry">Joan</a> by his second cousin, king <a href="/wiki/Louis_XI_of_France" title="Louis XI of France">Louis XI</a>. By doing so, Louis XI hoped to extinguish the Orléans <a href="/wiki/Cadet_branch" title="Cadet branch">cadet branch</a> of the House of Valois.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Louis of Orléans was one of the great feudal lords who opposed the French monarchy in the conflict known as the <a href="/wiki/Mad_War" title="Mad War">Mad War</a>. At the royal victory in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Saint-Aubin-du-Cormier_(1488)" title="Battle of Saint-Aubin-du-Cormier (1488)">Battle of Saint-Aubin-du-Cormier</a> in 1488, Louis was captured, but Charles VIII pardoned him and released him. He subsequently took part in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_War_of_1494%E2%80%931498" title="Italian War of 1494–1498">Italian War of 1494–1498</a> as one of the French commanders.</p>
<p>When Louis XII became king in 1498, he had his marriage with Joan annulled by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_VI" title="Pope Alexander VI">Pope Alexander VI</a> and instead married <a href="/wiki/Anne_of_Brittany" title="Anne of Brittany">Anne of Brittany</a>, the widow of his cousin Charles VIII. This marriage allowed Louis to reinforce the personal <a href="/wiki/Union_of_Brittany_and_France" title="Union of Brittany and France">Union of Brittany and France</a>.</p>
<p>Louis persevered in the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a>, initiating a <a href="/wiki/Italian_War_of_1499%E2%80%931504" title="Italian War of 1499–1504">second Italian campaign</a> for the control of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">Kingdom of Naples</a>. Louis conquered the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Milan" title="Duchy of Milan">Duchy of Milan</a> in 1500 and pushed forward to the Kingdom of Naples, which fell to him in 1501. Proclaimed King of Naples, Louis faced a new coalition gathered by <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand II of Aragon</a> and was forced to cede Naples to <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Spain" title="Habsburg Spain">Spain</a> in 1504.</p>
<p>Louis XII did not encroach on the power of local governments or the privileges of the nobility, in opposition with the long tradition of the French kings to attempt to impose <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy_in_France" title="Absolute monarchy in France">absolute monarchy in France</a>. A popular king, Louis was proclaimed "<b>Father of the People</b>" (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Le Père du Peuple</i></span>) in 1506 by the <a href="/wiki/Estates_General_(France)" title="Estates General (France)">Estates-General</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tours" title="Tours">Tours</a> for his reduction of the tax known as <a href="/wiki/Taille" title="Taille">taille</a>, legal reforms, and civil peace within France.</p>
<p>Louis, who remained <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_of_Milan" title="Duke of Milan">Duke of Milan</a> after the second Italian War, was interested in further expansion in the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Peninsula" title="Italian Peninsula">Italian Peninsula</a> and launched a <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_League_of_Cambrai" title="War of the League of Cambrai">third Italian War</a> (1508–1516), which was marked by the military prowess of the <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Terrail,_seigneur_de_Bayard" title="Pierre Terrail, seigneur de Bayard">Chevalier de Bayard</a>.</p>
<p>Louis XII died in 1515 without a male heir. He was succeeded by his cousin <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis</a> from the Angoulême cadet branch of the House of Valois.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_XII_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Louis was born on 27 June 1462 in the <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Blois" title="Château de Blois">Château de Blois</a>, Touraine (in the modern French <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">department</a> of <a href="/wiki/Loir-et-Cher" title="Loir-et-Cher">Loir-et-Cher</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The son of <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Duke_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Charles, Duke of Orléans">Charles, Duke of Orléans</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marie_of_Cleves" title="Marie of Cleves">Marie of Cleves</a>, he succeeded his father as <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Duke of Orléans">Duke of Orléans</a> in the year 1465.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Louis XI, who had become king of France in 1461, became highly distrustful of the close relationship between the Orleanists and the Burgundians and began to oppose the idea of an Orleanist ever coming to the throne of France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baumgartner3_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baumgartner3-5">[5]</a></sup> However, Louis XI may have been more influenced in this opinion by his opposition to the entire Orleanist faction of the royal family than by the actual facts of this paternity case.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Despite any alleged doubts that King Louis XI may have had, the King, nevertheless, became "godfather" of the newborn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baumgartner3_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baumgartner3-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>King Louis XI died on 30 August 1483.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He was succeeded to the throne of France by his thirteen (13) year-old son, Charles VIII.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Nobody knew the direction which the new king (or more accurately his regent and oldest sister, Anne of France) would take in leading the kingdom. Accordingly, on 24 October 1483, a call went out for a convocation of the Estates General of the French kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In January 1484, deputies of the <a href="/wiki/Estates_General_(France)" title="Estates General (France)">Estates General</a> began to arrive in Tours, France. The deputies represented three different "estates" in society. The First Estate was the Church; in France this meant the Roman Catholic Church. The Second Estate was composed of the nobility and the royalty of France. The Third Estate was generally composed of commoners and the class of traders and merchants in France. Louis, the current Duke of Orleans and future Louis XII, attended as part of the Second Estate. Each estate brought their chief complaints to the Estates General in hopes to have some impact on the policies that the new King would pursue. The First Estate (the Church) wanted a return to the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pragmatic_Sanction" title="Pragmatic Sanction">Pragmatic Sanction</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baumgartner22_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baumgartner22-9">[9]</a></sup> The Pragmatic Sanction had been first instituted by King Charles VII, the current King Charles VIII's grandfather. The Pragmatic Sanction eliminated the papacy from the process of appointing bishops and abbots in France. Instead, these positions would be filled by appointment made by the cathedrals and monastery chapters themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baumgartner22_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baumgartner22-9">[9]</a></sup> All church prelates within France would be appointed by the King of France without reference to the pope.</p>
<p>The deputies representing the Second Estate (the nobility) at the Estates General of 1484 wanted all foreigners to be prohibited from command positions in the military.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baumgartner22_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Baumgartner22-9">[9]</a></sup> The deputies of the Third Estate (the merchants and traders) wanted taxes to be drastically reduced and that the revenue needs of the crown be met by reducing royal pensions and the number offices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baumgartner22_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Baumgartner22-9">[9]</a></sup> All three of the estates were in agreement on the demand for an end to the sale of government offices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baumgartner22_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Baumgartner22-9">[9]</a></sup> By 7 March 1484, the King announced that he was leaving Tours because of poor health. Five days later the deputies were told that there was no more money to pay their salaries, and the Estates General meekly concluded its business and went home. The Estates General of 1484 is called, by historians, the most important Estates General until the Estates General of 1789.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Important as they were, many of the reforms suggested at the meeting of the Estates General were not immediately adopted. Rather the reforms would only be acted on when Louis XII came to the throne.</p>
<p>Since Charles VIII was only thirteen years of age when he became king, his older sister <a href="/wiki/Anne_of_France" title="Anne of France">Anne</a> was to serve as regent until Charles VIII became 20 years old. From 1485 through 1488, there was another war against the royal authority of France conducted by a collections of nobles. This war was the <a href="/wiki/Mad_War" title="Mad War">Mad War</a> (1485-1488), Louis's war against Anne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Allied with <a href="/wiki/Francis_II,_Duke_of_Brittany" title="Francis II, Duke of Brittany">Francis II, Duke of Brittany</a>, Louis confronted the royal army at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Saint-Aubin-du-Cormier_(1488)" title="Battle of Saint-Aubin-du-Cormier (1488)">Battle of Saint-Aubin-du-Cormier</a> on 28 July 1488 but was defeated and captured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Pardoned three years later, Louis joined his cousin <a href="/wiki/Charles_VIII_of_France" title="Charles VIII of France">King Charles VIII</a> in campaigns in Italy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>All four children of Charles VIII died in infancy. The French interpretation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salic_Law" title="Salic Law">Salic Law</a> permitted claims to the French throne only by male agnatic descendants of French kings. This made Louis, the great-grandson of King <a href="/wiki/Charles_V_of_France" title="Charles V of France">Charles V</a>, the most senior claimant as heir of Charles VIII. Thus, Louis, Duke of Orleans, succeeded to the throne on 7 April 1498 as Louis XII upon the death of King Charles VIII.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reign">Reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_XII_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_XII_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Although he came late<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> (and unexpectedly) to power, Louis acted with vigour, reforming the French legal system,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> reducing taxes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and improving government<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> much like his contemporary <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII</a> did in England. To meet his budget after having reduced taxes, Louis XII reduced the pensions for the nobility and for foreign princes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In religious policy, Louis XII re-instituted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pragmatic_Sanction" title="Pragmatic Sanction">Pragmatic Sanction</a>, which established the Roman Catholic Church in France as a "Gallic Church" with most of the power of appointment in the hands of the king or other French officials. As noted above, these reforms had been proposed at the meeting of the Estates General in 1484.</p>
<p>Louis was also skilled in managing his nobility, including the powerful <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">Bourbon</a> faction, greatly contributing to the stability of French government. In the Ordinance of Blois of 1499<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and the Ordinance of Lyon issued in June 1510<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> he extended the powers of royal judges and made efforts to curb corruption in the law. Highly complex French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Customary_law" title="Customary law">customary law</a> was codified and ratified by the royal proclamation of the Ordinance of Blois of 1499.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The Ordinance of Lyon tightened up the tax collection system requiring, for instance, that tax collectors forward all money to the government within eight days after they collected it from the people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Fines and loss of office were prescribed for violations of this ordinance.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_early_wars">The early wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_XII_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The early wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The French Kingdom under Charles VIII <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Italian_War" title="First Italian War">invaded Italy in 1494</a> to protect the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Milan" title="Duchy of Milan">Duchy of Milan</a> from the threats of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Republic of Venice</a>. At the time, the Duchy of Milan was one of the most prosperous regions of Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Louis, the current Duke of Orleans and future King Louis XII, joined Charles VIII on this campaign. The French Kingdom was responding to an appeal for assistance from <a href="/wiki/Ludovico_Sforza" title="Ludovico Sforza">Ludovico Sforza</a>, Duke of Milan. The invasion set off a series of wars that would last from 1494 until 1559 and would become known as the "<a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a>".</p>

<p>In 1495, Ludovico Sforza betrayed the French by changing sides in the war and joining the anti-French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/League_of_Venice" title="League of Venice">League of Venice</a> (sometimes called the "Holy League").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This left Louis, the Duke of Orleans, in an awkward and inferior military position at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fornovo" title="Battle of Fornovo">Battle of Fornovo</a> on 6 July 1495. As a result, Louis had come to hate Ludovico Sforza.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Accordingly, even before he became King of France, Louis began to claim the Duchy of Milan as his own inheritance, which should have come to his by right of his paternal grandmother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valentina_Visconti" title="Valentina Visconti">Valentina Visconti</a>.</p>
<p>After becoming king in 1499, Louis XII pursued his ambition to claim Milan in what is known as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_War_of_1499-1504" title="Italian War of 1499-1504">Great Italian War</a>" (1499–1504) or "King Louis XII's War". However, before initiating any war Louis XII needed to deal with the international threats that he faced. In August 1498, he signed a peace treaty with the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor">Maximillian I</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner106_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner106-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>With Maximillian I neutralized, Louis wanted to turn his attention to King <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII of England</a>. However, Henry was then pursuing a marriage between his eldest son, Arthur, and <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Aragon" title="Catherine of Aragon">Catherine of Aragon</a>, the Infanta of Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner106_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner106-27">[27]</a></sup> Thus he needed to detach Spain from its close relations with England before he could deal with Henry VII. Furthermore, Spain was then a member of the anti-French League of Venice. <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand of Aragon</a>, king of the newly unified Spain, directed all relations between Spain and the French on behalf of himself and his queen, <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Castile" title="Isabella I of Castile">Isabella I of Castile</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner106_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner106-27">[27]</a></sup> Ferdinand was so hostile to France that he had founded the anti-French League of Venice in 1495.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In August 1498, Louis XII succeeded in signing a treaty with Spain that ignored all the territorial disputes between France and Spain and merely pledged mutual friendship and non-aggression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner107_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner107-29">[29]</a></sup> This allowed enough freedom for Louis XII to start negotiating with <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> for an alliance. Actually, Louis was merely seeking to reinstitute an old alliance between France and Scotland that had been in existence since King Philippe IV of France first recognised Robert the Bruce (1306–1329) as "King of Scotland in 1309. In early 1499, the old alliance between Scotland and France was renewed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner106_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner106-27">[27]</a></sup> and the attentions of England were drawn northward toward Scotland rather than southward toward continental Europe.</p>
<p>With the major powers preoccupied or pledged to peace with France, Louis XII could attend to two other neighbors on his border: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swiss_Confederation" title="Swiss Confederation">Swiss Confederation</a> and the Duchy of Savoy. In March 1499, Louis signed an agreement with the Swiss Confederation that promised 20,000 <a href="/wiki/Franc" title="Franc">francs</a> as an annual subsidy for simply allowing the French to recruit an unspecified number of troops in the Confederation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner107_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner107-29">[29]</a></sup> In exchange, Louis promised to protect the Confederation from any aggression from Maximillian and the Holy Roman Empire. Louis opened negotiations with the Duchy of Savoy and by May 1499 had hammered out an agreement that allowed French troops to cross Savoy to reach the Duchy of Milan. The agreement with Savoy also allowed France to purchase supplies and to recruit troops in Savoy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Finally, Louis was ready to march into Italy.</p>
<p>The French army had been a potent force in 1494 when Charles VIII had first invaded Italy. However, during the remainder of Charles VIII's reign, the army had been allowed to deteriorate through neglect. Ever since becoming king, Louis XII had been rebuilding the French army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Now he could put it to use.</p>
<p>On 10 August 1499, after marching across Savoy and through the town of <a href="/wiki/Asti" title="Asti">Asti</a>, the French army crossed the border into the Duchy of Milan. Contrary to the wishes of the Second Estate (the nobles and royalty of France), expressed at the Estates General in 1484, this French army was being led by a non-Frenchman, <a href="/wiki/Gian_Giacomo_Trivulzio" title="Gian Giacomo Trivulzio">Gian Giacomo Trivulzio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner113_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner113-32">[32]</a></sup> Marshall Trivulzio had been in the service of the French throne since the reign of Louis XI, but he had been born and raised in Milan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner113_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner113-32">[32]</a></sup> The French army that Marshal Trivulzio now commanded consisted of 27,000 men of which 10,000 were mounted. The French army was also supplied with 5,000 Swiss mercenaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner113_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner113-32">[32]</a></sup> In the campaign of 1499, the French army surrounded the fortified town of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rocca_di_Arazzo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rocca di Arazzo (page does not exist)">Rocca di Arazzo</a> in the western part of the Duchy of Milan. After five hours of bombardment by the French artillery batteries, the walls of Rocca di Arazzo were breached and the town was taken by the French. Louis XII had ordered his army to massacre the garrison and many civilians as a message to the other towns in the Duchy against resistance to the French army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner113_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner113-32">[32]</a></sup> The legal rationale for the massacre at Rocca di Arazzo was that defenders of the town were traitors because they had risen up in arms against their rightful lord, Louis XII. The French repeated the episode at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annone" title="Annone">Annone</a>, the next fortified town on the road to the city of Milan. This time the massacre had the desired effect, as three more fortified towns surrendered without a fight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner114_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner114-33">[33]</a></sup> Marshall Trivulzio then brought the French Army up to the gates of town of Alessandro, and his batteries began battering the walls of the town on 25 August 1499. At first, a vigorous defense was mounted by the garrison, but on 29 August 1499, the city gave up and the garrison and the governor of the city slipped out of town before dawn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner114_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner114-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Marshal Trivulzio now became aware that the <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venetian</a> army, allies of the Duchy of Milan, were crossing into the Duchy from the east in an attempt to aid the Milanese army before it was too late. Accordingly, Marshal Trivulzio marched his army to <a href="/wiki/Pavia" title="Pavia">Pavia</a>, the last fortified town in the Duchy of Milan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner114_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner114-33">[33]</a></sup> With French troops already near Pavia, a short distance west of the city of Milan, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lodovico_Sforza" title="Lodovico Sforza">Lodovico Sforza</a> determined that it was useless to continue resisting the French. Accordingly, on the night of 2 September 1499, Sforza and a band of cavalry fled Milan, heading northward to the Holy Roman Empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner114_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner114-33">[33]</a></sup> Louis XII, staying in <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a>, heard about the surrender of Milan on 17 September 1499. He immediately left Lyon and on 6 October 1499, Louis XII made his triumphant entry into Milan. Marshal Trevulzio presented the key to the city to Louis, who in turn appointed Marshal Trivulzio as the temporary French governor of Milan. Later, Louis appointed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georges_d%27_Amboise" title="Georges d' Amboise">Georges d' Amboise</a> as the permanent governor of Milan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In an attempt to win popularity with the public in Milan, Louis lowered the old Sforza taxes by as much as one-third.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, Ludovico Sforza had been gathering an army, mainly among the Swiss, to take Milan back. In mid-January 1500, his army crossed the border into the Duchy of Milan and marched toward the city of Milan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Upon hearing the news of Sforza's return, some of his partisans in the city rose up. On 1 February 1500, Marshal Trivulzio decided that he could not hold the city, and the French retreated to the fortresses west of the city. Sforza was welcomed back into the city by a joyous crowd of his supporters on 5 February 1500.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner116_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner116-37">[37]</a></sup> Louis XII raised another army under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_de_La_Tr%C3%A9moille" title="Louis de La Trémoille">Louis de La Trémoille</a> and sent him to recapture Milan. By the time Trémoille reached the forts west of Milan where Marshal Trivulzio and his force were holding out, the French army had swollen to 30,000 men by recruitment along the way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner116_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner116-37">[37]</a></sup> Many of these new recruits in the French army were Swiss mercenaries. The government of the Swiss Confederation heard about the coming battle and forbade any Swiss soldier from fighting against a fellow Swiss, which effectively subtracted all the Swiss from both sides for this particular battle. These troops then started to march back home to Switzerland. This had a much more damaging effect on Sforza's army, because his army was composed of a larger proportion of Swiss than the French army under La Trémoille.</p>
<p>Faced with the return of the French and his own greatly reduced force, Sforza decided to slip out of Milan as he had done previously. This time, however, Sforza was captured<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> and spent the rest of his life in a French prison. Despite Milan's openly warm welcome of Sforza (which Louis XII regarded as "treasonous"), Louis XII was very generous to the city in victory. While Sforza had been in charge of Milan, the export of grain had been forbidden. Now the French reopened the trade in grain, setting off a decade of prosperity in Milan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Milan was to remain a French stronghold in Italy for twelve years.</p>
<p>Using Milan as his firmly established base, Louis XII began to turn his attention to other parts of Italy. The city of <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a> agreed to the appointment of <a href="/wiki/Philip_of_Cleves,_Lord_of_Ravenstein" title="Philip of Cleves, Lord of Ravenstein">Philip of Cleves</a>, a cousin of Louis XII, as its new governor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner114_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner114-33">[33]</a></sup> Additionally, the French king now began to espouse his claim to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">Kingdom of Naples</a>, though the legal rationale for this claim was weaker than for his claim to Milan, stemming only from his position as the successor to Charles VIII.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner119_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner119-40">[40]</a></sup> Nonetheless, Louis XII pursued the claim with vigor.</p>
<p>The presence of several French garrisons in southern Italy, the remnants of Charles VIII's first invasion of Italy, provided Louis XII with a toehold in southern Italy from which he hoped to enforce his claim to the Kingdom of Naples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner119_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner119-40">[40]</a></sup> However, Louis first had to deal with a recurring problem in northern Italy. In 1406, the city of <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a> was conquered by <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> but had been in constant revolt almost ever since. In 1494, the Pisans successfully overthrew the Florentine governors of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner119_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner119-40">[40]</a></sup> The Florentines requested aid from the French to recapture Pisa, as the city of Florence had long been an ally of France in Italian affairs. However, Louis and his advisers were miffed at Florence because in the recent fight against Sforza, Florence had chosen to abandon France and remain strictly neutral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner119_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner119-40">[40]</a></sup> The French knew that they would need Florence in the coming campaign in the Kingdom of Naples — French troops would need to cross Florentine territory on their way to Naples and they would need Florentine agreement to do so. Accordingly, a French army including 600 knights and 6,000 Swiss infantrymen under the command of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sire_de_Beaumont&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sire de Beaumont (page does not exist)">Sire de Beaumont</a> was sent to Pisa. On 29 June 1500, a combined French and Florentine force laid siege to Pisa and set up batteries around the town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner120_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner120-41">[41]</a></sup> Within a day of opening fire, the French batteries had knocked down 100 feet of the old medieval walls surrounding the city. Even with the breach in their walls, the Pisans put up such a determined resistance that Beaumont despaired of ever taking Pisa. On 11 July 1500, the French broke camp and retreated north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner120_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner120-41">[41]</a></sup> The diversion to Pisa and the failure there emboldened opponents of the French in Italy. Pursuing the claim to the Kingdom of Naples had become politically impossible until some of the opponents were neutralized. One opponent in particular was Spain. It was at this point, in 1500, that Louis XII pursued the claim of his immediate predecessor to the Kingdom of Naples with <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand II</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Aragon" title="King of Aragon">King of Aragon</a> and with Queen Isabel of Castile, ruler of Spain.</p>
<p>On 11 November 1500, Ferdinand II and Louis XII signed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Granada_(1500)" title="Treaty of Granada (1500)">Treaty of Granada</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner122_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner122-42">[42]</a></sup> which brought Spain into Italian politics in a big way for the first time. Louis XII was severely criticized by contemporary historians including <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccolò Machiavelli">Niccolò Machiavelli</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner122_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner122-42">[42]</a></sup> Machiavelli's criticism of Louis XII is contained in his work <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prince" title="The Prince">The Prince</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_portrayed_in_Machiavelli.27s_The_Prince">As portrayed in Machiavelli's <i>The Prince</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_XII_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: As portrayed in Machiavelli's The Prince">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Louis's failure to hold on to Naples prompted a commentary by <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccolò Machiavelli">Niccolò Machiavelli</a> in his famous <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">opus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prince" title="The Prince">The Prince</a></i>:</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="margin: auto; width:80%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>King Louis was brought into Italy by the ambition of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venetians</a>, who expected by his coming to get control of half the state of <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a>. I don't mean to blame the king for his part in the scheme; he wanted a foothold in Italy, and not only had no friends in the province, but found all doors barred against him because of King Charles's behavior. Hence he had to take what friendships he could get; and if he had made no further mistakes in his other arrangements, he might have carried things off very successfully. By taking Lombardy, the king quickly regained the reputation lost by Charles. <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a> yielded, and the Florentines turned friendly, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marquis_of_Mantua" title="Marquis of Mantua">Marquis of Mantua</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_of_Ferrara" title="Duke of Ferrara">Duke of Ferrara</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bentivogli" title="Bentivogli">Bentivogli</a> (of Bologna), the countess <a href="/wiki/Forl%C3%AC" title="Forlì">Forlì</a>, the lords of <a href="/wiki/Faenza" title="Faenza">Faenza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pesaro" title="Pesaro">Pesaro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rimini" title="Rimini">Rimini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camerino" title="Camerino">Camerino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piombino" title="Piombino">Piombino</a>, and the people of <a href="/wiki/Lucca" title="Lucca">Lucca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Siena" title="Siena">Siena</a> all sought him out with professions of friendship. At this point the Venetians began to see the folly of what they had done, since in order to gain for themselves a couple of districts in Lombardy, they had now made the king master of a third of Italy.</p>
<p>Consider how easy it would have been for the king to maintain his position in Italy if he had observed the rules [of not worrying about weaker powers, decreasing the strength of a major power, not introducing a very powerful foreigner in the midst of his new subjects and taking up residence among his new subjects and/or setting up colonies], and become the protector and defender of his new friends. They were many, they were weak, some of them were afraid of the Venetians, others of the Church, hence they were bound to stick by him; and with their help, he could easily have protected himself against the remaining great powers. But no sooner was he established in Milan than he took exactly the wrong tack, helping <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_VI" title="Pope Alexander VI">Pope Alexander</a> to occupy the <a href="/wiki/Romagna" title="Romagna">Romagna</a>. And he never realized that by this decision he was weakening himself, driving away his friends and those who had flocked to him, while strengthening the Church by adding vast temporal power to the spiritual power which gives it so much authority. Having made this first mistake, he was forced into others. To limit the ambition of Alexander and keep him from becoming master of <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a>, he was forced to come to Italy himself [in 1502]. Not satisfied with having made the Church powerful and deprived himself of his friends, he went after the kingdom of Naples and divided it with the king of Spain (<a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand II</a>). And where before he alone had been the arbiter of Italy, he brought in a rival to whom everyone in the kingdom who was ambitious on his own account or dissatisfied with Louis could have recourse. He could have left in Naples a caretaker king of his own, but he threw him out, and substituted a man capable of driving out Louis himself.</p>
<p>If France could have taken Naples with her own power, she should have done so; if she could not, she should not have split the kingdom with the Spaniards. The division of Lombardy that she made with the Venetians was excusable, since it gave Louis a foothold in Italy; the division of Naples with Spain was an error, since there was no such necessity for it. [When Louis made the final mistake of] depriving the Venetians of their power (who never would have let anyone else into Lombardy unless they were in control), he thus lost Lombardy.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_campaigns_against_the_Kingdom_of_Naples_.281501.E2.80.931508.29">Military campaigns against the Kingdom of Naples (1501–1508)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_XII_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Military campaigns against the Kingdom of Naples (1501–1508)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To assert his claim to his half of the Kingdom of Naples, Louis XII sent an army under the command of <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Stewart,_4th_Lord_of_Aubigny" title="Bernard Stewart, 4th Lord of Aubigny">Bernard Stuart of Aubigny</a> composed of 1,000 lances, 10,000 infantrymen including 5,000 Swiss troops to Naples in early June 1501.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner123_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner123-44">[44]</a></sup> In May 1501, Louis had obtained free passage for his troops to march through Bologne on the way to Naples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner123_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner123-44">[44]</a></sup> As the army approached Rome, Spanish and French ambassadors notified Pope Alexander VI of the thus far secret Treaty of Grenada, signed 11 November 1500, which divided the Kingdom of Naples between France and Spain. The Pope was pleased and enthusiastically issued a bull naming the two kings — Louis XII of France and Ferdinand II of Spain — as the Pope's vassals in Naples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner123_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner123-44">[44]</a></sup> Indeed, the public announcement of the treaty in the Vatican was the first news that Federico IV, the current king of Naples, had received about his fate and his betrayal by his own cousin, King Ferdinand II of Spain.</p>
<p>Being a stern disciplinarian, Lord Stuart held the troops of his army to strict decorum during most of the march to Naples. However, discipline fell apart when the army passed through <a href="/wiki/Capua" title="Capua">Capua</a>. The French army plundered and raped Capua mercilessly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baumgartner123_44-3"><a href="#cite_note-baumgartner123-44">[44]</a></sup> However, when news of the rape of Capua spread throughout southern Italy, resistance to the French vanished. King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frederick_IV_of_Naples" title="Frederick IV of Naples">Federigo, King of Naples</a> fled and the French Army entered Naples unopposed. Louis XII claimed the throne of Naples and pursunt to the sharing agreement with Ferdinand II shared half the income of Naples with Spain. However, as Machiavelli had said, the agreement could not last and in early 1502 relations between France and Spain had gone sour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Negotiations were started between France and Spain over their disagreements about Naples. However, in April 1502, without waiting for the conclusion of these negotiations, Louis sent an army under the command of <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV,_Duke_of_Alen%C3%A7on" title="Charles IV, Duke of Alençon">Louis d' Armagnac, Duke of Nemours</a> against the Spanish in <a href="/wiki/Apulia" title="Apulia">Apulia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_War_of_the_League_of_Cambrai">The War of the League of Cambrai</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_XII_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The War of the League of Cambrai">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Louis's greatest success came in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_League_of_Cambrai" title="War of the League of Cambrai">War of the League of Cambrai</a> (1508-1516), his final war, fought against the Venetians, who had again become his enemy. The French army won the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Agnadello" title="Battle of Agnadello">Battle of Agnadello</a> on 14 May 1508. However, things became much more difficult in 1510, when the army of Pope Julius II intervened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Julius II founded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/League_of_Cambrai#Holy_League" title="League of Cambrai">Holy League</a> of the League of Cambrai specifically to thwart the ambitions of France. The French were eventually driven from Milan in 1513 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Swiss_Confederation" title="Old Swiss Confederation">Swiss</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_XII_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the end of his reign the crown deficit was no greater than it had been when he succeeded Charles VIII in 1498, despite several expensive military campaigns in Italy. His fiscal reforms of 1504 and 1508 tightened and improved procedures for the collection of taxes.</p>
<p>In spite of his military and diplomatic failures, Louis proved to be a popular king. He duly earned the title of <i>Father of the People</i> ("<i>Le Père du Peuple</i>") conferred upon him by the Estates in 1506.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_XII_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriages">Marriages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_XII_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Marriages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Joan_of_Valois_Queen_of_France.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="150" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f2/Joan_of_Valois_Queen_of_France.jpg" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Joan_of_Valois_Queen_of_France.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Joan_of_France,_Duchess_of_Berry" title="Joan of France, Duchess of Berry">Queen Joan of France</a></div>

<p>In 1476, <a href="/wiki/Louis_XI_of_France" title="Louis XI of France">Louis XI</a> forced Louis (his second cousin) to marry his daughter <a href="/wiki/Joan_of_France,_Duchess_of_Berry" title="Joan of France, Duchess of Berry">Joan of France</a>. The son of Louis XI, Charles VIII, succeeded to the throne of France in 1483, but died childless in 1498, whereupon the throne passed to Louis XII. Charles had been married to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anne,_Duchess_of_Brittany" title="Anne, Duchess of Brittany">Anne, Duchess of Brittany</a>, in order to unite the quasi-sovereign <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_of_Brittany" title="Duke of Brittany">Duchy of Brittany</a> with the Kingdom of France. To sustain this union, Louis XII had his marriage to Joan annulled (December 1498) after he became king so that he could marry Charles VIII's widow, Anne of Brittany.</p>
<p>The annulment, described as "one of the seamiest lawsuits of the age", was not simple. Louis did not, as one might have expected, argue the marriage to be void due to consanguinity (the general allowance for the dissolution of a marriage at that time). Though he could produce witnesses to claim that the two were closely related due to various linking marriages, there was no documentary proof, merely the opinions of courtiers. Likewise, Louis could not argue that he had been below the legal <a href="/wiki/Age_of_consent" title="Age of consent">age of consent</a> (fourteen) to marry: no one was certain when he had been born, with Louis claiming to have been twelve at the time, and others ranging in their estimates between eleven and thirteen. As there was no real proof, he had perforce to bring forward other arguments.</p>

<p>Accordingly, Louis (much to the dismay of his wife) claimed that Joan was physically malformed (providing a rich variety of detail precisely how) and that he had therefore been unable to consummate the marriage. Joan, unsurprisingly, fought this uncertain charge fiercely, producing witnesses to Louis's boast of having "mounted my wife three or four times during the night". Louis also claimed that his sexual performance had been inhibited by <a href="/wiki/Witchcraft" title="Witchcraft">witchcraft</a>. Joan responded by asking how he was able to know what it was like to try to make love to her.</p>
<p>Had the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papacy" title="Papacy">Papacy</a> been a neutral party, Joan would likely have won, for Louis's case was exceedingly weak. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_VI" title="Pope Alexander VI">Pope Alexander VI</a>, however, had political reasons to grant the annulment, and ruled against Joan accordingly. He granted the annulment on the grounds that Louis did not freely marry, but was forced to marry by Joan's father Louis XI. Outraged, Joan reluctantly submitted, saying that she would pray for her former husband. She became a nun; she was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canonized" title="Canonized">canonized</a> in 1950.</p>

<p>Louis married the reluctant queen dowager, Anne, in 1499. They had four stillborn sons, and two daughters. The elder daughter, <a href="/wiki/Claude_of_France" title="Claude of France">Claude</a> (1499–1524), was betrothed by her mother's arrangement to the future <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Charles V</a> in 1501. But after Anne failed to produce a living son, Louis dissolved the betrothal and betrothed Claude to his <a href="/wiki/Heir_presumptive" title="Heir presumptive">heir presumptive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis of Angoulême</a>, thereby insuring Brittany would remain united with France. Anne opposed this marriage, which took place only after her death in 1514. Claude succeeded her mother in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> and became queen consort to Francis. The younger daughter, <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9e_of_France" title="Renée of France">Renée</a> (1510–1575), married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ercole_II_d%27Este" title="Ercole II d'Este">Duke Ercole II of Ferrara</a>.</p>
<p>After the death of Anne, Louis married <a href="/wiki/Mary_Tudor,_Queen_of_France" title="Mary Tudor, Queen of France">Mary Tudor</a>, the sister of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII of England</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Abbeville" title="Abbeville">Abbeville</a>, France, on 9 October 1514. This represented a final attempt to produce an heir to his throne, for despite two previous marriages the king had no living sons. Louis died on 1 January 1515, less than three months after he married Mary, reputedly worn out by his exertions in the bedchamber, but more likely from the effects of gout. Their union produced no children.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_XII_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 24 December 1514, Louis was reportedly suffering from a severe case of <a href="/wiki/Gout" title="Gout">gout</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-51">[51]</a></sup> In the early hours of 1 January 1515, he had received the final sacraments and died later that evening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-51">[51]</a></sup> Louis was interred in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Denis_Basilica" title="Saint Denis Basilica">Saint Denis Basilica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Succession">Succession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_XII_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Succession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The succession to the throne of France followed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salic_Law" title="Salic Law">Salic Law</a>, which did not allow women to inherit the throne. As a result, Louis XII was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis I</a>. Born to <a href="/wiki/Louise_of_Savoy" title="Louise of Savoy">Louise of Savoy</a>, on 12 September 1494 Francis I was the son of <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Count_of_Angoul%C3%AAme" title="Charles, Count of Angoulême">Charles, Count of Angoulême</a>. Francis I would also become Louis XII's' son-in-law upon Francis I's marriage to Louis XII's daughter <a href="/wiki/Claude_of_France" title="Claude of France">Claude of France</a>.</p>
<p>The succession to the ducal crown of Brittany followed semi-Salic tradition, allowing women to inherit the crown in their own right (<i><a href="/wiki/Suo_jure" title="Suo jure">suo jure</a></i>). Anne of Brittany predeceased Louis XII. Thus, Anne's eldest daughter, <a href="/wiki/Claude_of_France" title="Claude of France">Claude of France</a>, inherited the Duchy of Brittany directly in her own right (<i>suo jure</i>) before Louis's death. When Claude married Francis I, Francis also became the administrator of Brittany <a href="/wiki/Jure_uxoris" title="Jure uxoris">in right of his wife</a>. This assured that Brittany would remain part of the Kingdom of France and the unity of the Kingdom would be upheld.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_XII_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>As Duke of Orleans, he is a recurring character in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Walter_Scott" title="Sir Walter Scott">Sir Walter Scott</a>'s 1823 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Quentin_Durward" title="Quentin Durward">Quentin Durward</a></i>, where he is portrayed as attempting to break his marriage contract to Joan.</li>

He continues the claim on Naples by Charles VIII, and is also crowned Duke of Milan by <a href="/wiki/Cesare_Borgia" title="Cesare Borgia">Cesare Borgia</a>. Despite his initial friendship with Cesare, their relations are strained by Cesare's conflicts with French interests, as well as Cesare's heavy handed methods. After Della Rovere becomes <a href="/wiki/Pope_Julius_II" title="Pope Julius II">Pope Julius II</a> and Cesare's downfall begins, Louis offers him exile in France, but Cesare's ambition refuses to consider defeat.</li>
</ul>
