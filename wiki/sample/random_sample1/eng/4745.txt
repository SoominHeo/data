<p><b>Louis VI</b> (1 December 1081 – 1 August 1137), called <b>the Fat</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>le Gros</i></span>), was <a href="/wiki/List_of_French_monarchs" title="List of French monarchs">King of the Franks</a> from 1108 until his death (1137). Chronicles called him "roi de Saint-Denis".</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:S%C3%89RIE_M%C3%89TALLIQUE_DES_ROIS_DE_FRANCE,_Louis_VI_le_Gros.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="589" data-file-width="1200" height="108" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/S%C3%89RIE_M%C3%89TALLIQUE_DES_ROIS_DE_FRANCE%2C_Louis_VI_le_Gros.jpg/220px-S%C3%89RIE_M%C3%89TALLIQUE_DES_ROIS_DE_FRANCE%2C_Louis_VI_le_Gros.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/S%C3%89RIE_M%C3%89TALLIQUE_DES_ROIS_DE_FRANCE%2C_Louis_VI_le_Gros.jpg/330px-S%C3%89RIE_M%C3%89TALLIQUE_DES_ROIS_DE_FRANCE%2C_Louis_VI_le_Gros.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/S%C3%89RIE_M%C3%89TALLIQUE_DES_ROIS_DE_FRANCE%2C_Louis_VI_le_Gros.jpg/440px-S%C3%89RIE_M%C3%89TALLIQUE_DES_ROIS_DE_FRANCE%2C_Louis_VI_le_Gros.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:S%C3%89RIE_M%C3%89TALLIQUE_DES_ROIS_DE_FRANCE,_Louis_VI_le_Gros.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Louis VI</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Louis was the first member of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Capet" title="House of Capet">House of Capet</a> to make a lasting contribution to the centralizing institutions of royal power,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He spent almost all of his twenty-nine-year reign fighting either the "<a href="/wiki/Robber_baron" title="Robber baron">robber barons</a>" who plagued Paris<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> or the <a href="/wiki/List_of_English_monarchs" title="List of English monarchs">Norman kings of England</a> for their continental possession of <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>. Nonetheless, Louis VI managed to reinforce his power considerably and became one of the first strong kings of France since the division of the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Empire" title="Carolingian Empire">Carolingian Empire</a> in 843.</p>
<p>Louis was a warrior king but by his forties his weight had become so great that it was increasingly difficult for him to lead in the field. A biography - <i>The Deeds of Louis the Fat</i>, prepared by his loyal advisor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbot_Suger" title="Abbot Suger">Abbot Suger</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saint-Denis,_Seine-Saint-Denis" title="Saint-Denis, Seine-Saint-Denis">Saint Denis</a> - offers a fully developed portrait of his character, in contrast to what little historians know about most of his predecessors.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_VI_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Louis was born on 1 December 1081 in Paris, the son of <a href="/wiki/Philip_I_of_France" title="Philip I of France">Philip I</a> and his first wife, <a href="/wiki/Bertha_of_Holland" title="Bertha of Holland">Bertha of Holland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Suger tells us: "In his youth, growing courage matured his spirit with youthful vigour, making him bored with hunting and the boyish games with which others of his age used to enjoy themselves and forget the pursuit of arms." And..."How valiant he was in youth, and with what energy he repelled the king of the English, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Rufus" title="William Rufus">William Rufus</a>, when he attacked Louis' inherited kingdom."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Louis married <a href="/wiki/Lucienne_de_Rochefort" title="Lucienne de Rochefort">Lucienne de Rochefort</a>, a French crown princess, in 1104, but repudiated her three years later. They had no children.</p>
<p>On 3 August 1115 Louis married <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_of_Maurienne" title="Adelaide of Maurienne">Adelaide of Maurienne</a>, daughter of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humbert_II_of_Savoy" title="Humbert II of Savoy">Humbert II of Savoy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gisela_of_Burgundy" title="Gisela of Burgundy">Gisela of Burgundy</a>, and niece of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Callixtus_II" title="Pope Callixtus II">Pope Callixtus II</a>. They had eight children. Adelaide was one of the most politically active of all France's medieval queens. Her name appears on 45 royal charters from the reign of Louis VI. During her tenure as queen, royal charters were dated with both her regnal year and that of the king.</p>
<p>Suger became Louis's adviser before he became king and he succeeded his father at the age of 26 on 29 July 1108. Louis's half-brother prevented him from reaching <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rheims" title="Rheims">Rheims</a>, and so <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daimbert,_Archbishop_of_Sens" title="Daimbert, Archbishop of Sens">Daimbert, Archbishop of Sens</a>, crowned him in the cathedral of <a href="/wiki/Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Orléans">Orléans</a> on 3 August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naus112_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Naus112-5">[5]</a></sup> Ralph the Green, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Rheims" title="Archbishop of Rheims">Archbishop of Rheims</a>, sent envoys to challenge the validity of the coronation and anointing, but to no avail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naus112_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Naus112-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges_to_royal_authority">Challenges to royal authority</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_VI_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Challenges to royal authority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When Louis ascended the throne the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">Kingdom of France</a> was a collection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feudal" title="Feudal">feudal</a> principalities. Beyond the <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele-de-France" title="Île-de-France">Isle de France</a> the French Kings had little authority over the great <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dukes" title="Dukes">Dukes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counts" title="Counts">Counts</a> of the realm but slowly Louis began to change this and assert Capetian rights. This process would take two centuries to complete but began in the reign of Louis VI.</p>
<p>The second great challenge facing Louis was to counter the rising power of the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Normans" title="Anglo-Normans">Anglo-Normans</a> under their capable new King, <a href="/wiki/Henry_I_of_England" title="Henry I of England">Henry I of England</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Struggles_with_the_robber_barons">Struggles with the robber barons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_VI_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Struggles with the robber barons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From early in his reign (and during his father's reign) Louis faced the problem of the <a href="/wiki/Robber_baron" title="Robber baron">robber barons</a> who resisted the King's authority and engaged in brigandry, making the area around <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> unsafe.</p>
<p>From their castles, such as <a href="/wiki/Le_Puiset" title="Le Puiset">Le Puiset</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teaufort,_Yvelines" title="Châteaufort, Yvelines">Chateaufort</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montlhery" title="Montlhery">Montlhery</a>, these barons would charge tolls, waylay merchants and pilgrims, terrorize the peasantry and loot churches and abbeys, the latter deeds drawing the ire of the writers of the day, who were mostly clerics.</p>
<p>In 1108, soon after he ascended the throne, Louis engaged in war with Hugh of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crecy" title="Crecy">Crecy</a>, who was plaguing the countryside and had captured Eudes, Count of Corbeil, and imprisoned him at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Ferte-Alais" title="La Ferte-Alais">La Ferte-Alais</a>. Louis besieged that fortress to free Eudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halphen596_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Halphen596-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In early 1109, Louis besieged his half-brother, Philip, the son of <a href="/wiki/Bertrade_de_Montfort" title="Bertrade de Montfort">Bertrade de Montfort</a>, who was involved in brigandry and conspiracies against the King, at <a href="/wiki/Mantes-la-Jolie" title="Mantes-la-Jolie">Mantes-la-Jolie</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halphen596_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Halphen596-6">[6]</a></sup> Philip's plots included the lords of <a href="/wiki/Montfort-l%27Amaury" title="Montfort-l'Amaury">Montfort-l'Amaury</a>. <a href="/wiki/Amaury_III_of_Montfort" title="Amaury III of Montfort">Amaury III of Montfort</a> held many castles which, when linked together, formed a continuous barrier between Louis and vast swathes of his domains, threatening all communication south of Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halphen596_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Halphen596-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1108-1109 a <a href="/wiki/Seigneur" title="Seigneur">seigneur</a> named Aymon Vaire-Vache seized the lordship of Bourbon from his nephew, Archambaud, a minor. Louis demanded the boy be restored to his rights but Aymon refused the summons. Louis raised his army and besieged Aymon at his castle at Germigny-sur-l'Aubois, forcing its surrender and enforcing the rights of Archambaud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p598_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p598-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1122, Aimeri, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Clermont" title="Bishop of Clermont">Bishop of Clermont</a>, appealed to Louis after <a href="/wiki/William_VI,_Count_of_Auvergne" title="William VI, Count of Auvergne">William VI, Count of Auvergne</a>, had driven him from his episcopal town. When William refused Louis' summons, Louis raised an army at <a href="/wiki/Bourges" title="Bourges">Bourges</a>, and marched into Auvergne, supported by some of his leading vassals, such as the Counts of <a href="/wiki/Anjou" title="Anjou">Anjou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nevers" title="Nevers">Nevers</a>. Louis seized the fortress of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pont-du-Chateau" title="Pont-du-Chateau">Pont-du-Chateau</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Allier" title="Allier">Allier</a>, then attacked Clermont, which William was forced to abandon. Aimeri was restored. Four years later William rebelled again and Louis, though his increasing weight made campaigning difficult, marched again. He burned Montferrand and seized Clermont a second time, captured William, and brought him before the court at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orleans" title="Orleans">Orleans</a> to answer for his crimes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p598_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p598-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of the outlaws became notorious for their cruelty, the most notable being <a href="/wiki/Thomas,_Lord_of_Coucy" title="Thomas, Lord of Coucy">Thomas, Lord of Coucy</a>, who was reputed to indulge in torture of his victims, including hanging men by their testicles, cutting out eyes, and chopping off feet. <a href="/wiki/Guibert_of_Nogent" title="Guibert of Nogent">Guibert of Nogent</a> noted of him, "No one can imagine the number of those who perished in his dungeons, from starvation, from torture, from filth."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another notable brigand was Hugh, Lord of <a href="/wiki/Le_Puiset" title="Le Puiset">Le Puiset</a>, who was ravaging the lands around <a href="/wiki/Chartres" title="Chartres">Chartres</a>. In March 1111,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Louis heard charges against Hugh at his court at <a href="/wiki/Melun" title="Melun">Melun</a> from <a href="/wiki/Theobald_II,_Count_of_Champagne" title="Theobald II, Count of Champagne">Theobald II, Count of Champagne</a>, the Archbishop of <a href="/wiki/Sens" title="Sens">Sens</a>, and also from bishops and abbots. Louis commanded Hugh to appear before him to answer these charges, but Hugh evaded the summons. Louis stripped him of his lands and titles and laid siege to Le Puiset. After a fierce struggle, Louis took the castle and burned it to the ground, taking Hugh prisoner.</p>

<p>Rashly, Louis released Hugh, and while Louis was engaged in war with <a href="/wiki/Henry_I_of_England" title="Henry I of England">Henry I of England</a> and Theobald, Hugh raised another band of brigands and began ravaging the country again. When Louis returned his attention to Hugh, he found Le Puiset rebuilt and Hugh receiving aid from Theobald. Hugh held out against the King until Theobald abandoned him. Once again Louis razed Le Puiset and Hugh, who had sworn never to return to his brigandage, rebuilt the castle and resumed terrorizing his neighbours. At the third attempt, Louis finally defeated Hugh and stripped him of his possessions for the last time. Hugh later died on an expiatory <a href="/wiki/Pilgrimage" title="Pilgrimage">pilgrimage</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Land" title="Holy Land">Holy Land</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>These were just some of the recalcitrant nobles Louis was forced to contend with. There were many more, and Louis was in constant motion against them, leading his army from castle to castle, bringing law and order to his domains. The result was increased recognition of the King's authority and the Crown's ability to impose its will, so that all sectors of French society began to see the King as their protector.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="War_with_Henry_I_over_Gisors">War with Henry I over Gisors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_VI_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: War with Henry I over Gisors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chateau-de-Gisors.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1916" data-file-width="2874" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Chateau-de-Gisors.jpg/220px-Chateau-de-Gisors.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Chateau-de-Gisors.jpg/330px-Chateau-de-Gisors.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Chateau-de-Gisors.jpg/440px-Chateau-de-Gisors.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chateau-de-Gisors.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Motte and castle at <a href="/wiki/Gisors" title="Gisors">Gisors</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After seizing the English Crown, <a href="/wiki/Henry_I_of_England" title="Henry I of England">Henry I of England</a> deprived his brother, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Curthose" title="Robert Curthose">Robert Curthose</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Normandy" title="Duchy of Normandy">Duchy of Normandy</a> and quickly took possession of the castle at <a href="/wiki/Gisors" title="Gisors">Gisors</a>, a fortress of strategic importance on the right bank of the <a href="/wiki/Epte" title="Epte">Epte</a>, commanding the road between <a href="/wiki/Rouen" title="Rouen">Rouen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>. This violated an earlier agreement between Henry and the French King that Gisors should remain in the hands of a neutral <a href="/wiki/Castellan" title="Castellan">castellan</a>, or else be demolished.</p>
<p>This move threatened the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capetian" title="Capetian">Capetian</a> domain and Louis was outraged, demanding Henry, as his <a href="/wiki/Vassal" title="Vassal">vassal</a>, appear before him to account for his actions. The two kings met, in force, in March 1109<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup> at the borders of their respective territories at the bridge of Neauphle on the Epte.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup> Henry refused to relinquish Gisors. Louis challenged the English King to <a href="/wiki/Single_combat" title="Single combat">single combat</a> to settle the issue. When Henry refused, war was inevitable, a war which would last, on and off, for twenty years.</p>
<p>The first years of the war went well for Louis until the influential <a href="/wiki/Theobald_II,_Count_of_Champagne" title="Theobald II, Count of Champagne">Theobald II, Count of Champagne</a>, switched to Henry's side. By early 1112<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup> Theobald had succeeded in bringing together a coalition of barons with grievances against Louis: Lancelin of Bulles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup> Ralph of Beaugency,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup> Milo of Bray-sur-Seine,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup> Hugh of Crecy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup> Guy of Rochfort,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup> Hugh of Le Puiset<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-8"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup> and Hugh, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Count_of_Troyes" title="Count of Troyes">Count of Troyes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-9"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Louis defeated Theobald's coalition but the additional effort meant he could not defeat the English monarch as well or force him to abandon Gisors, and in March 1113<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-10"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup> Louis was forced to sign a treaty recognizing Henry I as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suzerain" title="Suzerain">suzerain</a> of Brittany and Maine. Peace of sorts lasted three years until April 1116<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-11"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup> when hostilities renewed in the French and Norman <a href="/wiki/Vexin" title="Vexin">Vexins</a>, with each king making gains from his rival.</p>
<p>By 1119, buoyed by several successes and the capture (through treachery) of <a href="/wiki/Les_Andelys" title="Les Andelys">Les Andelys</a>, Louis felt ready for a final encounter to end the war. In the fierce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bremule" title="Battle of Bremule">Battle of Bremule</a>, in August 1119,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-12"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup> Louis's troops broke and routed, abandoning the royal banner and sweeping the King along with them in retreat to Les Andelys. A counter-attack through Evreaux to seize Breteuil failed and Louis, health failing, looked for peace.</p>
<p>He appealed to <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calixtus_II" title="Calixtus II">Calixtus II</a>, who agreed to help and met with Henry at Gisors in November 1120.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-13"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup> The terms of the peace included Henry's heir, <a href="/wiki/William_Adelin" title="William Adelin">William Adelin</a>, doing homage to Louis for Normandy, a return of all territories captured by both kings with the painful exception of Gisors itself, which Louis was forced to concede to Henry.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Intervention_in_Flanders">Intervention in Flanders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_VI_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Intervention in Flanders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blessed_Charles_the_Good.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2828" data-file-width="1326" height="469" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Blessed_Charles_the_Good.jpg/220px-Blessed_Charles_the_Good.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Blessed_Charles_the_Good.jpg/330px-Blessed_Charles_the_Good.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Blessed_Charles_the_Good.jpg/440px-Blessed_Charles_the_Good.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Blessed_Charles_the_Good.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Statue of Blessed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_the_Good" title="Charles the Good">Charles the Good</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_the_Holy_Blood" title="Basilica of the Holy Blood">Basilica of the Holy Blood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruges" title="Bruges">Bruges</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 2 March 1127, the <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Flanders" title="Count of Flanders">Count of Flanders</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_the_Good" title="Charles the Good">Charles the Good</a>, was assassinated in <a href="/wiki/St._Donatian%27s_Cathedral" title="St. Donatian's Cathedral">St. Donatian's Cathedral</a> at <a href="/wiki/Bruges" title="Bruges">Bruges</a>. It was a scandal in itself but made worse because Charles had no heir.</p>
<p>Soon a variety of claimants were abroad, including <a href="/wiki/William_of_Ypres" title="William of Ypres">William of Ypres</a>, son of Charles's uncle and popularly thought to be complicit in the murder, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thierry_of_Alsace" title="Thierry of Alsace">Thierry of Alsace</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/Gertrude_of_Flanders,_Duchess_of_Lorraine" title="Gertrude of Flanders, Duchess of Lorraine">Gertrude of Flanders, Duchess of Lorraine</a>, Arnold of <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, nephew of Charles the Good, who seized <a href="/wiki/Saint-Omer" title="Saint-Omer">Saint-Omer</a>. Baldwin, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Count_of_Hainault" title="Count of Hainault">Count of Hainault</a>, who seized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oudenarde" title="Oudenarde">Oudenarde</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Godfrey_I,_Count_of_Louvain" title="Godfrey I, Count of Louvain">Godfrey I, Count of Louvain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Brabant" title="Duke of Brabant">Duke of Brabant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p599_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p599-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Louis had his own candidate in mind and marched into Flanders with an army and urged the barons to elect <a href="/wiki/William_Clito" title="William Clito">William Clito</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Curthose" title="Robert Curthose">Robert Curthose</a>, who had been disinherited of <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a> by his uncle <a href="/wiki/Henry_I_of_England" title="Henry I of England">Henry I of England</a>, as their new Count. He had no better claim to Flanders than being the King's candidate but on 23 March 1127 he was elected Count by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flemings" title="Flemings">Flemings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p599_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p599-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Louis then moved decisively to secure Flanders, apprehending the murderers of Charles the Good and ousting the rival claimants. On 2 April he took <a href="/wiki/Ghent" title="Ghent">Ghent</a>, on 5 April Bruges, on 26 April he took <a href="/wiki/Ypres" title="Ypres">Ypres</a>, capturing William of Ypres and imprisoning him at <a href="/wiki/Lille" title="Lille">Lille</a>. He then quickly took Aire, Cassel and all the towns still loyal to William of Ypres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p599_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p599-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Louis's final act before leaving for France was to witness the execution of Charles the Good's murderers. They were hurled from the roof of the church of Saint Donatian where they had committed their crime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p599_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p599-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was a triumph for Louis and demonstrated how far the Crown had come under his leadership, but it was a brief triumph. The new young Count William Clito fared badly, relying on heavy handed feudal ways not suited to the more socially advanced and mercantile Flemings. William's knights ran amok and the Flemings rebelled against Louis's candidate. Ghent and Bruge appealed to Thierry of Alsace and Saint-Omer to Arnold of Denmark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p599_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p599-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Louis attempted to intervene again but the moment was gone. The people of Bruge rejected him and recognized Thierry of Alsace as their Count, and he quickly moved to enforce his claim. Louis called a great assembly at Arras and had Thierry excommunicated but it was a gesture. Louis abandoned William of Clito, who died during a siege at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alost" title="Alost">Alost</a> on 27 July 1128, and after the whole country finally submitted to Thierry, Louis was obliged to confirm his claim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p599_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p599-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Invasion_of_Henry_V">Invasion of Henry V</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_VI_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Invasion of Henry V">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paschalis.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="672" data-file-width="417" height="355" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Paschalis.jpg/220px-Paschalis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Paschalis.jpg/330px-Paschalis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/54/Paschalis.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Paschalis.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Henry_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry V, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry V, Holy Roman Emperor</a>, with Ruthard, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Mainz" title="Archbishop of Mainz">Archbishop of Mainz</a>. Paint on vellum. Parker Library, Corpus Christi College, Cambridge.</div>

<p>On 25 November 1120, Louis' fortunes against Henry I of England were raised when Henry's heir, William Adelin, drunkenly perished aboard the <i><a href="/wiki/White_Ship" title="White Ship">White Ship</a></i> en route from Normandy to England, putting the future of Henry's dynasty and his position in doubt.</p>
<p>By 1123 Louis was involved with a coalition of Norman and French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seigneurs" title="Seigneurs">seigneurs</a> opposed to Henry. The plan was to drive the English King from Normandy and replace him with William Clito. Henry, however, easily defeated this coalition then instigated his son-in-law, <a href="/wiki/Henry_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry V, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry V, Holy Roman Emperor</a>, to invade France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p604_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p604-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Henry V had married the <a href="/wiki/Empress_Matilda" title="Empress Matilda">Empress Matilda</a>, the English King's daughter and the future mother of <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II of England</a>, 9 years earlier, in hopes of creating an Anglo-German empire, though the couple remained childless. Like Louis, Henry V had designs on the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a> and an invasion of Northern France would enable him to strengthen his ambitions in Flanders, as well as support his father-in-law.</p>
<p>Thus in 1124, Henry V assembled an army to march on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rheims" title="Rheims">Rheims</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p604_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p604-13">[13]</a></sup> It never arrived. In testament to how far Louis had risen as national protector, all of France rose to his appeal against the threat. Henry V was unwilling to see the French barons united behind their King, who now identified himself as the vassal of St <a href="/wiki/Denis" title="Denis">Denis</a>, the patron saint of Paris, whose banner he now carried,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and the proposed invasion was abandoned.</p>
<p>Henry V died a year after the aborted campaign.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alliance_of_the_Anglo-Normans_and_Anjou">Alliance of the Anglo-Normans and Anjou</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_VI_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Alliance of the Anglo-Normans and Anjou">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1128 Henry I married his sole surviving legitimate child, the <a href="/wiki/Dowager" title="Dowager">dowager</a> Empress Matilda, to <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Plantagenet,_Count_of_Anjou" title="Geoffrey Plantagenet, Count of Anjou">Geoffrey Plantagenet, Count of Anjou</a>. This was a very dangerous alliance for Louis and would prove so during the reign of his successor, <a href="/wiki/Louis_VII_of_France" title="Louis VII of France">Louis VII of France</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years">Final years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_VI_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Final years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As Louis VI approached his end, there seemed to be reasons for optimism. Henry I of England had died on 1 December 1135 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stephen_of_Blois" title="Stephen of Blois">Stephen of Blois</a> had seized the English crown, reneging on the oath he had sworn to Henry I to support Matilda. Stephen was thus in no position to bring the combined Anglo-Norman might against the French crown.</p>
<p>Louis had also made great strides in exercising his royal authority over his barons, and even Theobald II had finally rallied to the Capetian cause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p604_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_Medieval_History_Volume_V_p604-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Finally, on 9 April 1137, a dying <a href="/wiki/William_X,_Duke_of_Aquitaine" title="William X, Duke of Aquitaine">William X, Duke of Aquitaine</a> appointed Louis VI guardian of his fifteen-year-old daughter and heiress, <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_of_Aquitaine" title="Eleanor of Aquitaine">Eleanor of Aquitaine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fawtier21_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fawtier21-15">[15]</a></sup> Eleanor was suddenly the most eligible heiress in Europe, and Louis wasted no time in marrying her to his own heir, the future Louis VII, at the Cathedral of Saint-André in <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux" title="Bordeaux">Bordeaux</a> on 25 July 1137.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fawtier21_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fawtier21-15">[15]</a></sup> At a stroke Louis had added one of the most powerful <a href="/wiki/Duchy" title="Duchy">duchies</a> in France to the Capetian domains.</p>
<p>Louis died of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dysentry" title="Dysentry">dysentry</a> 7 days later, on 1 August 1137. Despite his achievements, it would be the growing power of the soon to be <a href="/wiki/Angevin_Empire" title="Angevin Empire">Angevin Empire</a> that would come to overshadow his successor, its seeds sown in the marriage between the Empress Matilda and Geoffrey Plantagenet and realised through their son, <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II of England</a>.</p>
<p>Louis VI was interred in the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_St_Denis" title="Basilica of St Denis">Basilica of St Denis</a> in Paris.</p>

<p>He married in 1115: 2) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ad%C3%A9laide_de_Maurienne" title="Adélaide de Maurienne">Adélaide de Maurienne</a> (1092–1154)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradbury132_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bradbury132-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<li>Their children:

<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philip_of_France_(1116-1131)" title="Philip of France (1116-1131)">Philip</a> (29 August 1116 – 13 October 1131), King of France (1129–31), not to be confused with his brother of the same name; he died as a result of a fall from a horse.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Louis_VII_of_France" title="Louis VII of France">Louis VII</a> (1120 – 18 September 1180), King of France.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_of_France_(1121-1175)" title="Henry of France (1121-1175)">Henry</a> (1121 – 13 November 1175), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Reims" title="Archbishop of Reims">Archbishop of Reims</a>.</li>
<li>Hugues (<i>ca</i> 1122 – died young).</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_I_of_Dreux" title="Robert I of Dreux">Robert</a> (<i>ca</i> 1123 – 11 October 1188), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Count_of_Dreux" title="Count of Dreux">count of Dreux</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Peter_I_of_Courtenay" title="Peter I of Courtenay">Peter</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> (September 1126 – 10 April 1183), married Elizabeth, <a href="/wiki/House_of_Courtenay" title="House of Courtenay">Lady of Courtenay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constance_of_France_(1124%E2%80%931176)" title="Constance of France (1124–1176)">Constance</a> (<i>ca</i> 1128 – 16 August 1176), married first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eustace_IV_of_Boulogne" title="Eustace IV of Boulogne">Eustace IV</a>, <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Boulogne" title="Count of Boulogne">count of Boulogne</a>, and then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raymond_V_of_Toulouse" title="Raymond V of Toulouse">Raymond V of Toulouse</a>.</li>
<li>Philip (<i>ca</i> 1132/33 -1161), <a href="/wiki/Archdeacon" title="Archdeacon">Archdeacon</a> of <a href="/wiki/Paris_in_the_Middle_Ages" title="Paris in the Middle Ages">Paris</a>, not to be confused with his elder brother.</li>

</li>

<ul>
