<p><b>Louis Nicolas Vauquelin</b> (16 May 1763 – 14 November 1829), was a French <a href="/wiki/Pharmacist" title="Pharmacist">pharmacist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_Nicolas_Vauquelin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vauquelin was born at <a href="/wiki/Saint-Andr%C3%A9-d%27H%C3%A9bertot" title="Saint-André-d'Hébertot">Saint-André-d'Hébertot</a> in <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>, France. His first acquaintance with chemistry was gained as laboratory assistant to an apothecary in <a href="/wiki/Rouen" title="Rouen">Rouen</a> (1777–1779), and after various vicissitudes he obtained an introduction to <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Fran%C3%A7ois,_comte_de_Fourcroy" title="Antoine François, comte de Fourcroy">A. F. Fourcroy</a>, in whose laboratory he was an assistant from 1783 to 1791.</p>
<p>Moving to Paris, he became a laboratory assistant at the Jardin du Roi and was befriended by a professor of chemistry. In 1791 he was made a member of the Academy of Sciences and from that time he helped to edit the journal <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annales_de_Chimie" title="Annales de Chimie">Annales de Chimie</a></i> <i>(Chemical annals)</i>, although he left the country for a while during the height of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>. In 1798 Vauquelin discovered <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">beryllium</a> by extracting it from an <a href="/wiki/Emerald" title="Emerald">emerald</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Beryl" title="Beryl">beryl</a> variety).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributions_to_chemistry">Contributions to chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_Nicolas_Vauquelin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Contributions to chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At first his work appeared as that of his master and patron, then in their joint names; in 1790 he began to publish on his own, and between that year and 1833 his name is associated with 376 papers. Most of these were simple records of patient and laborious analytical operations, and it is perhaps surprising that among all the substances he analysed he only detected two new elements, <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">beryllium</a> in 1798 in beryl and <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> in 1797 in a red lead ore from <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. He also managed to get liquid <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> at atmospheric pressure.</p>
<p>Either together or successively he held the offices of inspector of mines, professor at the School of Mines and at the Polytechnic School, assayer of gold and silver articles, professor of chemistry in the College de France and at the <a href="/wiki/Jardin_des_Plantes" title="Jardin des Plantes">Jardin des Plantes</a>, member of the Council of Industry and Commerce, commissioner on the pharmacy laws, and finally professor of chemistry to the Medical Faculty, to which he succeeded on Fourcroy's death in 1809. His lectures, which were supplemented with practical laboratory teaching, were attended by many chemists who subsequently attained distinction.</p>
<p>A lesser known contribution and finding of his included the study of hens fed a known amount of mineral. "Having calculated all the lime in oats fed to a hen, found still more in the shells of its eggs. Therefore, there is a creation of matter. In that way, no one knows."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_achievements.2C_days_and_legacy">Final achievements, days and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_Nicolas_Vauquelin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Final achievements, days and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From 1809 he was professor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">University of Paris</a>. In 1816, he was elected a foreign member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Swedish_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences">Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences</a>. He was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Chamber_of_Deputies" title="Chamber of Deputies">Chamber of Deputies</a> in 1828. In 1806, working with <a href="/wiki/Asparagus" title="Asparagus">asparagus</a>, he and <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Jean_Robiquet" title="Pierre Jean Robiquet">Pierre Jean Robiquet</a> (future discoverer of the famous red dye <a href="/wiki/Alizarin" title="Alizarin">alizarin</a>, then a young chemist and his assistant) isolated the amino acid <a href="/wiki/Asparagine" title="Asparagine">asparagine</a>, the first one to be discovered. He also discovered <a href="/wiki/Pectin" title="Pectin">pectin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malic_acid" title="Malic acid">malic acid</a> in <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apples</a>, and isolated <a href="/wiki/Camphoric_acid" title="Camphoric acid">camphoric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quinic_acid" title="Quinic acid">quinic acid</a>.</p>
<p>His death occurred while he was on a visit to his birthplace.</p>
<p>Among his best known works is <i>"Manuel de l'essayeur"</i> (Manual of the assayer).</p>
<p>The plant genus <i><a href="/wiki/Vauquelinia" title="Vauquelinia">Vauquelinia</a></i> is named in his honor, as is the <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Vauquelin" title="Vauquelin">Vauquelin</a>, an egg white foam associated with <a href="/wiki/Molecular_gastronomy" title="Molecular gastronomy">molecular gastronomy</a>, and the mineral <a href="/wiki/Vauquelinite" title="Vauquelinite">vauquelinite</a>, discovered at the same mine as the <a href="/wiki/Crocoite" title="Crocoite">crocoite</a> from which Vauquelin isolated chromium.</p>
