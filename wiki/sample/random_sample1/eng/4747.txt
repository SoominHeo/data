<p><b>Louis de Luxembourg, Count of Saint-Pol, of Brienne, de Ligny, and Conversano</b> (1418 – 19 December 1475) belonged to the Ligny branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Luxemburg" title="House of Luxemburg">House of Luxemburg</a> and was <a href="/wiki/Constable_of_France" title="Constable of France">Constable of France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaughan_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vaughan-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Saint-Pol was the eldest son of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_of_Luxembourg,_Count_of_Saint-Pol" title="Peter of Luxembourg, Count of Saint-Pol">Peter of Luxembourg</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Margaret_de_Baux" title="Margaret de Baux">Margaret de Baux</a>. His older sister Jacqueline, better known as <a href="/wiki/Jacquetta_of_Luxembourg" title="Jacquetta of Luxembourg">Jacquetta of Luxembourg</a>, married <a href="/wiki/John_of_Lancaster,_1st_Duke_of_Bedford" title="John of Lancaster, 1st Duke of Bedford">John of Lancaster, 1st Duke of Bedford</a>, and Louis was initially a supporter of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lancaster" title="House of Lancaster">Lancastrian</a> cause in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=English_Wars_of_the_Roses&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="English Wars of the Roses (page does not exist)">English Wars of the Roses</a>.</p>
<p>He was brought up by his uncle, <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_Luxembourg,_Count_of_Ligny" title="John II of Luxembourg, Count of Ligny">John II of Luxembourg, Count of Ligny</a>, who named Louis as heir to his estates. However, King <a href="/wiki/Charles_VII_of_France" title="Charles VII of France">Charles VII of France</a> <a href="/wiki/Sequestration_(law)" title="Sequestration (law)">sequestrated</a> the estates on John's death in 1441. As a result, Saint-Pol sought a rapprochement with the French king and duly had his inheritance restored to him. However, the county of <a href="/wiki/Guise" title="Guise">Guise</a> was claimed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_of_Le_Maine" title="Charles of Le Maine">Charles, Count of Maine</a>. The affair was settled by an agreement that Saint-Pol's sister Isabelle would marry the Count of Maine and receive the disputed lands as her <a href="/wiki/Dowry" title="Dowry">dowry</a>.</p>
<p>Saint-Pol became a close friend of the <a href="/wiki/Dauphin_of_France" title="Dauphin of France">Dauphin</a> Louis, the future King <a href="/wiki/Louis_XI_of_France" title="Louis XI of France">Louis XI of France</a> and fought with him in <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>. However, in 1465 Saint-Pol broke with his friend, now King, to join with the King's brother, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_de_Valois,_Duke_of_Berry" title="Charles de Valois, Duke of Berry">Charles, Duke of Berry</a> in the <a href="/wiki/League_of_the_Public_Weal" title="League of the Public Weal">League of the Public Weal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaughan_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vaughan-1">[1]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Montlh%C3%A9ry" title="Battle of Montlhéry">battle of Montlhéry</a>, he commanded the van of <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Bold" title="Charles the Bold">Charles the Bold</a>'s army, yet later was made <a href="/wiki/Constable_of_France" title="Constable of France">constable of France</a> by Louis XI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaughan_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Vaughan-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Conflans" title="Treaty of Conflans">Treaty of Conflans</a> ended the war, while Saint-Pol received the hand of the King's sister-in-law, Maria of Savoy.</p>
<p>After this, he was persistently disloyal to the King, conspiring with Charles, Count of Charolais, and with <a href="/wiki/Edward_IV_of_England" title="Edward IV of England">Edward IV of England</a>, his nephew by marriage. The final treason came in 1474 when Saint-Pol approached <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Bold" title="Charles the Bold">Charles the Bold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Burgundy" title="Duke of Burgundy">Duke of Burgundy</a>, who had already entered into a compact with Edward IV of England to dismember France in a renewal of the Hundred Years' War. The scheme envisaged the murder of Louis and the sub-division of France between Saint-Pol, the Dukes of Burgundy, Brittany, Bourbon and Namours, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Count_of_Maine" title="Count of Maine">Count of Maine</a> and King Edward. Saint-Pol then proceeded to draw other magnates into the conspiracy.</p>
<p>The whole thing started to unravel after Louis and Edward concluded the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Picquigny" title="Treaty of Picquigny">Treaty of Picquigny</a> in August 1475. Angered by this, Saint-Pol was imprudent enough to write to Edward, upbraiding him as a "cowardly, dishonoured and beggarly king". Edward promptly forwarded the letter to Louis, who now had all the proof he needed. A messenger was sent to the conspirator, in which he was informed that the King had 'need of a head such as his.' He was arrested in September 1475, and later imprisoned in the <a href="/wiki/Bastille" title="Bastille">Bastille</a>. Execution followed in December. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippe_de_Commynes" title="Philippe de Commynes">Philippe de Commynes</a>, the chief chronicler of Louis' reign, was to write that Saint-Pol had been "abandoned by God because he had tried with all his might to prolong the hostilities between the King and the Duke of Burgunday."</p>
<p>From 1468 to 1472 his <a href="/wiki/Chaplain" title="Chaplain">chaplain</a> was the well-known translator, author and scribe <a href="/wiki/Jean_Mi%C3%A9lot" title="Jean Miélot">Jean Miélot</a>.</p>
<p>Louis de Luxembourg married twice, first to <a href="/wiki/Jeanne_de_Bar,_Countess_of_Marle_and_Soissons" title="Jeanne de Bar, Countess of Marle and Soissons">Jeanne de Bar, Countess of Marle and Soissons</a> (died 1462), and secondly to <a href="/wiki/Marie_of_Savoy,_Countess_of_Saint-Pol" title="Marie of Savoy, Countess of Saint-Pol">Marie of Savoy</a>. He left at least nine legitimate children, including:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_of_Luxembourg,_Count_of_Soissons" title="John of Luxembourg, Count of Soissons">John of Luxembourg, Count of Soissons</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pierre_II_de_Luxembourg" title="Pierre II de Luxembourg">Pierre II de Luxembourg</a></li>
<li>Jacqueline of Luxembourg, married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philip_I_of_Croy" title="Philip I of Croy">Philip I of Croy</a> (died 1511)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anthony_I,_Count_of_Ligny" title="Anthony I, Count of Ligny">Anthony I, Count of Ligny</a></li>
</ul>
