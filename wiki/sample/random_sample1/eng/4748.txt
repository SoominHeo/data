<p><b>Louis David Riel</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small>English</small> <span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uː</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[lwi ʁjɛl]</a></span>; 22 October 1844 – 16 November 1885) was a <a href="/wiki/Canadians" title="Canadians">Canadian</a> politician, a founder of the province of <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a>, and a political leader of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_people_(Canada)" title="Métis people (Canada)">Métis</a> people of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_prairies" title="Canadian prairies">Canadian prairies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He led two resistance movements against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_government" title="Canadian government">Canadian government</a> and its first post-Confederation prime minister, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_John_A._Macdonald" title="Sir John A. Macdonald">Sir John A. Macdonald</a>. Riel sought to preserve Métis rights and culture as their homelands in the Northwest came progressively under the Canadian sphere of influence. Over the decades, he has been made a folk hero by the Francophones, the Catholic nationalists, the native rights movement, and the <a href="/wiki/New_Left" title="New Left">New Left</a> student movement. Riel has received more scholarly attention than practically any other figure in Canadian history.</p>
<p>His first resistance was the <a href="/wiki/Red_River_Rebellion" title="Red River Rebellion">Red River Rebellion</a> of 1869–1870.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-martyr_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-martyr-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Provisional_government" title="Provisional government">provisional government</a> established by Riel ultimately negotiated the terms under which the modern province of Manitoba entered the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Confederation" title="Canadian Confederation">Canadian Confederation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Riel ordered the execution of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Scott_(Orangeman)" title="Thomas Scott (Orangeman)">Thomas Scott</a>, and fled to the United States to escape prosecution. Despite this, he is frequently referred to as the "Father of Manitoba".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> While a fugitive, he was elected three times to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_House_of_Commons" title="Canadian House of Commons">Canadian House of Commons</a>, although he never assumed his seat. During these years, he was frustrated by having to remain in exile despite his growing belief that he was a divinely chosen leader and prophet, a belief which would later resurface and influence his actions. Because of this new religious conviction, Catholic leaders who had supported him before increasingly repudiated him. He married in 1881 while in exile in <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a> in the United States; he fathered three children.</p>
<p>In 1884 Riel was called upon by the Métis leaders in Saskatchewan to articulate their grievances to the Canadian government. Instead he organized a military resistance that escalated into a military confrontation, the <a href="/wiki/North-West_Rebellion" title="North-West Rebellion">North-West Rebellion</a> of 1885. Ottawa used the new rail lines to send in thousands of combat soldiers. It ended in his arrest and conviction for <a href="/wiki/Treason_Act_1351" title="Treason Act 1351">high treason</a>. Rejecting many protests and popular appeals, Prime Minister MacDonald decided to hang him. Riel was seen as a heroic victim by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Canada" title="French Canada">francophone Canadians</a>; his execution had a lasting negative impact on Canada, polarizing the new nation along ethno-religious lines. Although only a few hundred people were directly affected by the Rebellion in Saskatchewan, the long-term result was that the Prairie provinces would be controlled by the Anglophones, not the Francophones. An even more important long-term impact was the bitter alienation Francophones across Canada showed and anger against the repression of their countrymen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Riel's historical reputation has long been polarized between portrayals as a dangerous half-insane religious fanatic and rebel against the Canadian nation, or by contrast a heroic rebel who fought to protect his Francophone people from the unfair encroachments of an Anglophone national government. He is increasingly celebrated as a proponent of multiculturalism, although that downplays his primary commitment to Métis nationalism and political independence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCE_Riel_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCE_Riel-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Red_River_Colony" title="Red River Colony">Red River Settlement</a> was a community in <a href="/wiki/Rupert%27s_Land" title="Rupert's Land">Rupert's Land</a> nominally administered by the <a href="/wiki/Hudson%27s_Bay_Company" title="Hudson's Bay Company">Hudson's Bay Company</a> (HBC), and largely inhabited by <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a> tribes and the Métis, an ethnic group of mixed <a href="/wiki/Cree" title="Cree">Cree</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ojibwa" title="Ojibwa">Ojibwa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saulteaux" title="Saulteaux">Saulteaux</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Canadian" title="French Canadian">French Canadian</a>, Scottish, and English descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Louis Riel was born there in 1844, near modern <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg" title="Winnipeg">Winnipeg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a>, to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_Riel,_Sr." title="Louis Riel, Sr.">Louis Riel, Sr.</a> and Julie Lagimodière.</p>
<p>Riel was the eldest of eleven children in a locally well-respected family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> His father, who was of Franco-Ojibwa Métis descent, had gained prominence in this community by organizing a group that supported <a href="/wiki/Guillaume_Sayer" title="Guillaume Sayer">Guillaume Sayer</a>, a Métis imprisoned for challenging the HBC's historical trade monopoly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Sayer's eventual release due to agitations by Louis Sr.'s group effectively ended the monopoly, and the name Riel was therefore well known in the Red River area. His mother was the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Lagimodi%C3%A8re" title="Jean-Baptiste Lagimodière">Jean-Baptiste Lagimodière</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marie-Anne_Gaboury" title="Marie-Anne Gaboury">Marie-Anne Gaboury</a>, one of the earliest <a href="/wiki/White_people" title="White people">white</a> families to settle in the Red River Settlement in 1812. The Riels were noted for their devout Catholicism and strong family ties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Riel was first educated by Roman Catholic priests at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Boniface,_Manitoba" title="Saint Boniface, Manitoba">St. Boniface</a>. At age 13 he came to the attention of <a href="/wiki/Alexandre-Antonin_Tach%C3%A9" title="Alexandre-Antonin Taché">Alexandre Taché</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suffragan_Bishop" title="Suffragan Bishop">Suffragan Bishop</a> of St. Boniface, who was eagerly promoting the priesthood for talented young Métis. In 1858 Taché arranged for Riel to attend the Petit Séminaire of the <a href="/wiki/Coll%C3%A8ge_de_Montr%C3%A9al" title="Collège de Montréal">Collège de Montréal</a>, under the direction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Society_of_Saint-Sulpice" title="Society of Saint-Sulpice">Sulpician order</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Descriptions of him at the time indicate that he was a fine scholar of languages, science, and philosophy, but exhibited a frequent and unpredictable moodiness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following news of his father's premature death in 1864, Riel lost interest in the priesthood and he withdrew from the college in March 1865. For a time he continued his studies as a day student in the convent of the <a href="/wiki/Grey_Nuns" title="Grey Nuns">Grey Nuns</a>, but was soon asked to leave following breaches of discipline. He remained in Montreal over a year, living at the home of his aunt, Lucie Riel. Impoverished by the death of his father, Riel took employment as a law clerk in the Montreal office of <a href="/wiki/Rodolphe_Laflamme" title="Rodolphe Laflamme">Rodolphe Laflamme</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCE_Riel_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCE_Riel-6">[6]</a></sup> During this time he was involved in a failed romance with a young woman named Marie–Julie Guernon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This progressed to the point of Riel having signed a contract of marriage, but his fiancée's family opposed her involvement with a Métis, and the engagement was soon broken. Compounding this disappointment, Riel found legal work unpleasant, and by early 1866 he had resolved to leave <a href="/wiki/Canada_East" title="Canada East">Canada East</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Some of his friends said later that he worked odd jobs in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, Illinois, while staying with poet <a href="/wiki/Louis-Honor%C3%A9_Fr%C3%A9chette" title="Louis-Honoré Fréchette">Louis-Honoré Fréchette</a>, and wrote poems himself in the manner of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lamartine" title="Lamartine">Lamartine</a>; also that he was then for a time employed as a clerk in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Paul,_Minnesota" title="Saint Paul, Minnesota">Saint Paul, Minnesota</a>, before returning to the Red River Settlement on 26 July 1868.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Red_River_Rebellion">Red River Rebellion</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Red_River_Rebellion" title="Red River Rebellion">Red River Rebellion</a></div>
<p>The majority population of the Red River had historically been Métis and First Nation people. Upon his return, Riel found that religious, nationalistic, and racial tensions were exacerbated by an influx of <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">Anglophone</a> Protestant settlers from Ontario. The political situation was also uncertain, as ongoing negotiations for the transfer of Rupert's Land from the Hudson's Bay Company to Canada had not addressed the political terms of transfer. Finally, despite warnings to the Macdonald government from Bishop Taché<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and the HBC governor <a href="/wiki/William_Mactavish" title="William Mactavish">William Mactavish</a> that any such activity would precipitate unrest, the Canadian minister of public works, <a href="/wiki/William_McDougall_(politician)" title="William McDougall (politician)">William McDougall</a>, ordered a survey of the area. The arrival on 20 August 1869 of a survey party headed by Colonel <a href="/wiki/John_Stoughton_Dennis" title="John Stoughton Dennis">John Stoughton Dennis</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> increased anxiety among the Métis. The Métis did not possess title to their land, which was in any case laid out according to the <a href="/wiki/Seigneurial_system_of_New_France" title="Seigneurial system of New France">seigneurial system</a> rather than in English-style square lots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Riel_emerges_as_a_leader">Riel emerges as a leader</span></h3>
<p>In late August, Riel denounced the survey in a speech, and on 11 October 1869, the survey's work was disrupted by a group of Métis that included Riel. This group organized itself as the "Métis National Committee" on 16 October, with Riel as secretary and <a href="/wiki/John_Bruce_(Canada)" title="John Bruce (Canada)">John Bruce</a> as president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> When summoned by the HBC-controlled <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Assiniboia" title="Council of Assiniboia">Council of Assiniboia</a> to explain his actions, Riel declared that any attempt by Canada to assume authority would be contested unless Ottawa had first negotiated terms with the Métis. Nevertheless, the non-bilingual McDougall was appointed the <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_governor" title="Lieutenant governor">lieutenant governor</a>-designate, and attempted to enter the settlement on 2 November. McDougall's party was turned back near the Canada–US border, and on the same day, Métis led by Riel seized <a href="/wiki/Fort_Garry" title="Fort Garry">Fort Garry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 6 November, Riel invited Anglophones to attend a convention alongside Métis representatives to discuss a course of action, and on 1 December he proposed to this convention a list of rights to be demanded as a condition of union. Much of the settlement came to accept the Métis point of view, but a passionately pro-Canadian minority began organizing in opposition. Loosely constituted as the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Party" title="Canadian Party">Canadian Party</a>, this group was led by <a href="/wiki/John_Christian_Schultz" title="John Christian Schultz">John Christian Schultz</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Mair" title="Charles Mair">Charles Mair</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Colonel John Stoughton Dennis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and a more reticent Major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Boulton" title="Charles Boulton">Charles Boulton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> McDougall attempted to assert his authority by authorizing Dennis to raise a contingent of armed men, but the Anglophone settlers largely ignored this call to arms. Schultz, however, attracted approximately fifty recruits and fortified his house and store. Riel ordered Schultz's home surrounded, and the outnumbered Canadians soon surrendered and were imprisoned in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_Fort_Garry" title="Upper Fort Garry">Upper Fort Garry</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Provisional_government">Provisional government</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ProvisionalMetisGovernment.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="380" data-file-width="640" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/ProvisionalMetisGovernment.jpg/300px-ProvisionalMetisGovernment.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/ProvisionalMetisGovernment.jpg/450px-ProvisionalMetisGovernment.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/ProvisionalMetisGovernment.jpg/600px-ProvisionalMetisGovernment.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ProvisionalMetisGovernment.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Métis provisional government</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Hearing of the unrest, Ottawa sent three emissaries to the Red River, including <a href="/wiki/Hudson%27s_Bay_Company" title="Hudson's Bay Company">HBC</a> representative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Donald_Alexander_Smith,_1st_Baron_Strathcona_and_Mount_Royal" title="Donald Alexander Smith, 1st Baron Strathcona and Mount Royal">Donald Alexander Smith</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> While they were en route, the Métis National Committee declared a provisional government on 8 December, with Riel becoming its president on 27 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Meetings between Riel and the Ottawa delegation took place on 5 and 6 January 1870, but when these proved fruitless, Smith chose to present his case in a public forum. Smith assured large audiences of the Government's goodwill in meetings on 19 and 20 January, leading Riel to propose the formation of a new convention split evenly between French and English settlers to consider Smith's instructions. On 7 February, a new list of rights was presented to the Ottawa delegation, and Smith and Riel agreed to send representatives to Ottawa to engage in direct negotiations on that basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BioOnline_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-BioOnline-27">[27]</a></sup> The provisional government established by Louis Riel published its own newspaper titled <i>New Nation</i> and established the <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_of_Assiniboia" title="Legislative Assembly of Assiniboia">Legislative Assembly of Assiniboia</a> to pass laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newnation_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-newnation-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canadian_resistance_and_the_execution_of_Scott">Canadian resistance and the execution of Scott</span></h3>
<p>Despite the apparent progress on the political front, the Canadian party continued to plot against the provisional government. However, they suffered a setback on 17 February, when forty-eight men, including Boulton and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Scott_(Orangeman)" title="Thomas Scott (Orangeman)">Thomas Scott</a>, were arrested near Fort Garry.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ShootingThomasScott.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="376" data-file-width="432" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/ShootingThomasScott.jpg/250px-ShootingThomasScott.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/ShootingThomasScott.jpg/375px-ShootingThomasScott.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6c/ShootingThomasScott.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ShootingThomasScott.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The execution of Thomas Scott</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Boulton was tried by a tribunal headed by <a href="/wiki/Ambroise-Dydime_L%C3%A9pine" title="Ambroise-Dydime Lépine">Ambroise-Dydime Lépine</a> and sentenced to death for his interference with the provisional government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> He was pardoned, but Scott interpreted this as weakness by the Métis, whom he regarded with open contempt. After Scott repeatedly quarreled with his guards, they insisted that he be tried for insubordination. At his court martial he was found guilty and was sentenced to death. Riel was repeatedly entreated to commute the sentence, but Riel responded, "I have done three good things since I have commenced: I have spared Boulton's life at your instance, I pardoned Gaddy, and now I shall shoot Scott.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scott was executed by firing squad on 4 March. Riel's motivations have been the cause of much speculation, but his own justification was that he felt it necessary to demonstrate to the Canadians that the Métis must be taken seriously. Protestant Canada did take notice, swore revenge, and set up a "<a href="/wiki/Canada_First" title="Canada First">Canada First</a>" movement to mobilize their anger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation_of_Manitoba_and_the_Wolseley_expedition">Creation of Manitoba and the Wolseley expedition</span></h3>
<p>The delegates representing the provisional government departed for Ottawa in March. Although they initially met with legal difficulties arising from the execution of Scott, they soon entered into direct talks with Macdonald and <a href="/wiki/George-%C3%89tienne_Cartier" title="George-Étienne Cartier">George-Étienne Cartier</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> An agreement enshrining the demands in the list of rights was quickly reached, and this formed the basis for the <a href="/wiki/Manitoba_Act" title="Manitoba Act">Manitoba Act</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> of 12 May 1870, which formally admitted Manitoba into the Canadian confederation. However, the negotiators could not secure a general <a href="/wiki/Amnesty" title="Amnesty">amnesty</a> for the provisional government.</p>
<p>As a means of exercising Canadian authority in the settlement and dissuading <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manifest_Destiny" title="Manifest Destiny">American expansionists</a>, a Canadian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolseley_Expedition" title="Wolseley Expedition">military expedition</a> under Colonel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garnet_Wolseley" title="Garnet Wolseley">Garnet Wolseley</a> was dispatched to the Red River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Although the government described it as an <i>"errand of peace"</i>, Riel learned that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonial_Militia_in_Canada" title="Colonial Militia in Canada">Canadian militia</a> elements in the expedition meant to lynch him, and he fled as the expedition approached the Red River. The arrival of the expedition on 20 August marked the effective end of the Red River Rebellion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Intervening_years">Intervening years</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amnesty_question">Amnesty question</span></h3>
<p>It was not until 2 September 1870 that the new lieutenant-governor <a href="/wiki/Adams_George_Archibald" title="Adams George Archibald">Adams George Archibald</a> arrived and set about the establishment of civil government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Without an amnesty, and with the Canadian militia beating and intimidating his sympathisers, Riel fled to the safety of the St. Joseph's mission across the Canada–US border in the <a href="/wiki/Dakota_Territory" title="Dakota Territory">Dakota Territory</a>. However the results of the first provincial election in December 1870 were promising for Riel, as many of his supporters came to power. Nevertheless, stress and financial troubles precipitated a serious illness—perhaps a harbinger of his future mental afflictions—that prevented his return to Manitoba until May 1871.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LouisRielPortrait2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="443" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/LouisRielPortrait2.jpg/200px-LouisRielPortrait2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/LouisRielPortrait2.jpg/300px-LouisRielPortrait2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/LouisRielPortrait2.jpg/400px-LouisRielPortrait2.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LouisRielPortrait2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Louis Riel circa 1875</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The settlement now faced a possible threat, from cross-border <a href="/wiki/Fenian_raids#Pembina_Raid_.281871.29" title="Fenian raids">Fenian raids</a> coordinated by his former associate <a href="/wiki/William_Bernard_O%27Donoghue" title="William Bernard O'Donoghue">William Bernard O'Donoghue</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Archibald proclaimed a general call to arms on 4 October. Companies of armed horsemen were raised, including one led by Riel. When Archibald reviewed the troops in St. Boniface, he made the significant gesture of publicly shaking Riel's hand, signaling that a rapprochement had been affected. This was not to be—when this news reached Ontario, Mair and members of the <a href="/wiki/Canada_First" title="Canada First">Canada First</a> movement whipped up anti-Riel (and anti-Archibald) sentiment. With Federal elections coming in 1872, Macdonald could ill afford further rift in Quebec-Ontario relations and so he did not offer an amnesty. Instead he quietly arranged for Taché to offer Riel a bribe of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_dollars" title="Canadian dollars">$</a>1,000 to remain in voluntary exile. This was supplemented by an additional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pound_Sterling" title="Pound Sterling">£</a>600 from Smith for the care of Riel's family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nevertheless, by late June Riel was back in Manitoba and was soon persuaded to run as a member of parliament for the electoral district of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Provencher_(electoral_district)" title="Provencher (electoral district)">Provencher</a>. However, following the early September defeat of <a href="/wiki/George-%C3%89tienne_Cartier" title="George-Étienne Cartier">George-Étienne Cartier</a> in his home riding in Quebec, Riel stood aside so that Cartier—on record as being in favour of amnesty for Riel—might secure a seat in Provencher. Cartier won by acclamation, but Riel's hopes for a swift resolution to the amnesty question were dashed following Cartier's death on 20 May 1873. In the ensuing by-election in October 1873, Riel ran unopposed as an Independent, although he had again fled, a warrant having been issued for his arrest in September. Lépine was not so lucky; he was captured and faced trial.</p>
<p>Riel made his way to Montreal and, fearing arrest or assassination, vacillated as to whether he should attempt to take up his seat in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_House_of_Commons" title="Canadian House of Commons">House of Commons</a>—<a href="/wiki/Edward_Blake" title="Edward Blake">Edward Blake</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_Ontario" title="Premier of Ontario">Premier of Ontario</a>, had announced a bounty of $5,000 for his arrest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Famously, Riel was the only Member of Parliament who was not present for the great <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Scandal" title="Pacific Scandal">Pacific Scandal</a> debate of 1873 that led to the resignation of the Macdonald government in November. <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada" title="Liberal Party of Canada">Liberal</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Mackenzie_(politician)" title="Alexander Mackenzie (politician)">Alexander Mackenzie</a> became the interim <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Canada" title="Prime Minister of Canada">prime minister</a>, and a general election was held in January 1874. Although the Liberals under Mackenzie formed the new government, Riel easily retained his seat. Formally, Riel had to sign a register book at least once upon being elected, and he did so under disguise in late January. He was nevertheless stricken from the rolls following a motion supported by Schultz, who had become the member for the electoral district of <a href="/wiki/Lisgar_(electoral_district)" title="Lisgar (electoral district)">Lisgar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Undeterred, Riel prevailed again in the resulting by-election, and although again expelled, his symbolic point had been made and public opinion in Quebec was strongly tipped in his favour.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exile_and_mental_illness">Exile and mental illness</span></h3>
<p>During this period, Riel had been staying with priests of the <a href="/wiki/Missionary_Oblates_of_Mary_Immaculate" title="Missionary Oblates of Mary Immaculate">Oblate order</a> in <a href="/wiki/Plattsburgh_(town),_New_York" title="Plattsburgh (town), New York">Plattsburgh</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, who introduced him to Father Fabien Martin <i><a href="/wiki/Dit_name" title="Dit name">dit</a></i> Barnabé in the nearby village of <a href="/wiki/Keeseville,_New_York" title="Keeseville, New York">Keeseville</a>. It was here that he received news of Lépine's fate: following his trial for the murder of Scott, which had begun on 13 October 1874, Lépine was found guilty and sentenced to death. This sparked outrage in the sympathetic Quebec press, and calls for amnesty for both Lépine and Riel were renewed. This presented a severe political difficulty for Mackenzie, who was hopelessly caught between the demands of Quebec and Ontario. However, a solution was forthcoming when, acting on his own initiative, the <a href="/wiki/Governor_General_of_Canada" title="Governor General of Canada">Governor General</a> <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Hamilton-Temple-Blackwood,_1st_Marquess_of_Dufferin_and_Ava" title="Frederick Hamilton-Temple-Blackwood, 1st Marquess of Dufferin and Ava">Lord Dufferin</a> commuted Lépine's sentence in January 1875. This opened the door for Mackenzie to secure from parliament an amnesty for Riel, on the condition that he remain in exile for five years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCE_Riel_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCE_Riel-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>During his time of exile, he was primarily concerned with religious rather than political matters. Spurred on by a sympathetic Roman Catholic priest in Quebec, he was increasingly influenced by his belief that he was a divinely chosen leader of the Métis. Modern biographers have speculated that he may have suffered from the <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychological</a> condition <a href="/wiki/Narcissism" title="Narcissism">megalomania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trial_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-trial-41">[41]</a></sup> His mental state deteriorated, and following a violent outburst he was taken to Montreal, where he was under the care of his uncle, John Lee, for a few months. But after Riel disrupted a religious service, Lee arranged to have him committed in an asylum in <a href="/wiki/Longue_Pointe" title="Longue Pointe">Longue Pointe</a> on 6 March 1876 under the assumed name "Louis R. David".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCE_Riel_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-TCE_Riel-6">[6]</a></sup> Fearing discovery, his doctors soon transferred him to the Beauport Asylum near <a href="/wiki/Quebec_City" title="Quebec City">Quebec City</a> under the name "Louis Larochelle".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> While he suffered from sporadic irrational outbursts, he continued his religious writing, composing theological tracts with an admixture of Christian and Judaic ideas. He consequently began calling himself Louis "David" Riel, prophet of the new world, and he would pray (standing) for hours, having servants help him to hold his arms in the shape of a cross. Nevertheless, he slowly recovered, and was released from the asylum on 23 January 1878<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asylum_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-asylum-43">[43]</a></sup> with an admonition to lead a quiet life. He returned for a time to Keeseville, where he became involved in a passionate romance with Evelina Martin <i><a href="/wiki/Dit_name" title="Dit name">dit</a></i> Barnabé,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BioOnline_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-BioOnline-27">[27]</a></sup> sister of his friend, the <a href="/wiki/Missionary_Oblates_of_Mary_Immaculate" title="Missionary Oblates of Mary Immaculate">oblate</a> father Fabien Barnabé. But with insufficient means to propose marriage, Riel returned to the west, hoping that she might follow. However, she decided that she would be unsuited to prairie life, and their correspondence soon ended.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Montana_and_family_life">Montana and family life</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jean-LouisAngeliqueRiel.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="310" height="310" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Jean-LouisAngeliqueRiel.jpg/200px-Jean-LouisAngeliqueRiel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Jean-LouisAngeliqueRiel.jpg/300px-Jean-LouisAngeliqueRiel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2d/Jean-LouisAngeliqueRiel.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jean-LouisAngeliqueRiel.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Jean-Louis and Marie-Angélique Riel, children of Louis Riel</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the fall of 1878, Riel returned to St. Paul, and briefly visited his friends and family. This was a time of rapid change for the Métis of the Red River—the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Bison" title="American Bison">buffalo</a> on which they depended were becoming increasingly scarce, the influx of settlers was ever-increasing, and much land was sold to unscrupulous land speculators. Like other Red River Métis who had left Manitoba, Riel headed further west to start a new life. Travelling to the <a href="/wiki/Montana_Territory" title="Montana Territory">Montana Territory</a>, he became a trader and interpreter in the area surrounding <a href="/wiki/Fort_Benton,_Montana" title="Fort Benton, Montana">Fort Benton</a>. Observing rampant alcoholism and its detrimental impact on the Native American and Métis people, he engaged in an unsuccessful attempt to curtail the <a href="/wiki/Whisky" title="Whisky">whisky</a> trade. In 1881, he married Marguerite Monet <i><a href="/wiki/Dit_name" title="Dit name">dit</a></i> Bellehumeur (1861–1886),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> a young Métis, "in the fashion of the country" on 28 April, an arrangement that was solemnized on 9 March 1882. They were to have three children: Jean-Louis (1882–1908); Marie-Angélique (1883–1897); and a boy who was born and died on 21 October 1885, less than one month before Riel was hanged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BioOnline_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-BioOnline-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Riel soon became involved in the politics of Montana, and in 1882, actively campaigned on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican Party</a>. He brought a suit against a <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrat</a> for rigging a vote, but was then himself accused of fraudulently inducing <a href="/wiki/British_subject" title="British subject">British subjects</a> to take part in the election. In response, Riel applied for United States citizenship and was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturalized" title="Naturalized">naturalized</a> on 16 March 1883.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> With two young children, he had by 1884 settled down and was teaching school at the <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Mission_Church_and_Cemetery" title="St. Peter's Mission Church and Cemetery">St. Peter's</a> <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuit</a> mission in the <a href="/wiki/Sun_River" title="Sun River">Sun River</a> district of Montana.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_North-West_Rebellion">The North-West Rebellion</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/North-West_Rebellion" title="North-West Rebellion">North-West Rebellion</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grievances_in_the_Saskatchewan_territory">Grievances in the Saskatchewan territory</span></h3>
<p>Following the Red River Rebellion, Métis travelled west and settled in the <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan_Valley" title="Saskatchewan Valley">Saskatchewan Valley</a>, especially along the south branch of the river in the country surrounding the Saint-Laurent mission (near modern <a href="/wiki/St._Laurent_de_Grandin,_Saskatchewan" title="St. Laurent de Grandin, Saskatchewan">St. Laurent de Grandin, Saskatchewan</a>). But by the 1880s, it had become clear that westward migration was no panacea for the troubles of the Métis and the plains Indians. The rapid collapse of the buffalo herd was causing near starvation among the <a href="/wiki/Plains_Cree" title="Plains Cree">Plains Cree</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackfoot" title="Blackfoot">Blackfoot</a> <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a>. This was exacerbated by a reduction in government assistance in 1883, and by a general failure of Ottawa to live up to its treaty obligations. The Métis were likewise obliged to give up the hunt and take up agriculture—but this transition was accompanied by complex issues surrounding land claims similar to those that had previously arisen in Manitoba. Moreover, settlers from Europe and the eastern provinces were also moving into the Saskatchewan territories, and they too had complaints related to the administration of the territories. Virtually all parties therefore had grievances, and by 1884 English settlers, <a href="/wiki/Anglo-M%C3%A9tis" title="Anglo-Métis">Anglo-Métis</a> and Métis communities were holding meetings and petitioning a largely unresponsive government for redress. In the electoral district of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorne_(N.W.T._electoral_district)" title="Lorne (N.W.T. electoral district)">Lorne</a>, a meeting of the south branch Métis was held in the village of <a href="/wiki/Batoche,_Saskatchewan" title="Batoche, Saskatchewan">Batoche</a> on 24 March, and thirty representatives voted to ask Riel to return and represent their cause. On 6 May a joint "Settler's Union" meeting was attended by both the Métis and English-speaking representatives from <a href="/wiki/Prince_Albert,_Saskatchewan" title="Prince Albert, Saskatchewan">Prince Albert</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_Jackson" title="Honoré Jackson">William Henry Jackson</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> an Ontario settler sympathetic to the Métis and known to them as Honoré Jackson, and <a href="/wiki/James_Isbister" title="James Isbister">James Isbister</a> of the Anglo-Métis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> It was here resolved to send a delegation to ask Riel's assistance in presenting their grievances to the Canadian government.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_of_Riel">Return of Riel</span></h3>
<p>The head of the delegation to Riel was <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Dumont_(M%C3%A9tis_leader)" title="Gabriel Dumont (Métis leader)">Gabriel Dumont</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DumontCE_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-DumontCE-48">[48]</a></sup> a respected buffalo hunter and leader of the Saint-Laurent Métis who had known Riel in Manitoba. <a href="/wiki/James_Isbister" title="James Isbister">James Isbister</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> was the lone <a href="/wiki/Anglo-M%C3%A9tis" title="Anglo-Métis">Anglo-Métis</a> delegate. Riel was easily swayed to support their cause—which was perhaps not surprising in view of Riel's continuing conviction that he was the divinely selected leader of the Métis and the prophet of a new form of Christianity. Riel also intended to use the new position of influence to pursue his own land claims in Manitoba. The party departed 4 June, and arrived back at Batoche on 5 July. Upon his arrival Métis and English settlers alike formed an initially favourable impression of Riel following a series of speeches in which he advocated moderation and a reasoned approach. During June 1884, the Plains Cree leaders <a href="/wiki/Big_Bear" title="Big Bear">Big Bear</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poundmaker" title="Poundmaker">Poundmaker</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> were independently formulating their complaints, and subsequently held meetings with Riel. However, the Native grievances were quite different from those of the settlers, and nothing was then resolved. Inspired by Riel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Honoré Jackson and representatives of other communities set about drafting a petition,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> and Jackson on 28 July released a manifesto detailing grievances and the settler's objectives. A joint English-Métis central committee with Jackson acting as secretary worked to reconcile proposals from different communities. In the interim, Riel's support began to waver. As Riel's religious pronouncements became increasingly heretical the clergy distanced themselves, and father <a href="/wiki/Alexis_Andr%C3%A9" title="Alexis André">Alexis André</a> cautioned Riel against mixing religion and politics. Also, in response to bribes by territorial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant-governor" title="Lieutenant-governor">lieutenant-governor</a> and Indian commissioner <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Dewdney" title="Edgar Dewdney">Edgar Dewdney</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> local English-language newspapers adopted an editorial stance critical of Riel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BioOnline_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-BioOnline-27">[27]</a></sup> Nevertheless, the work continued, and on 16 December Riel forwarded the committee's petition to the government, along with the suggestion that delegates be sent to Ottawa to engage in direct negotiation. Receipt of the petition was acknowledged by <a href="/wiki/Joseph-Adolphe_Chapleau" title="Joseph-Adolphe Chapleau">Joseph-Adolphe Chapleau</a>, Macdonald's Secretary of State, although Macdonald himself would later deny having ever seen it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BioOnline_27-4"><a href="#cite_note-BioOnline-27">[27]</a></sup> By then many original followers had left; only 250 remained at Batoche when it fell in May 1885.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Break_with_the_church">Break with the church</span></h3>
<p>Historian Donald Creighton has argued that Riel's had become a changed man:</p>
<dl>
<dd>In the 15 years since he had left Red River, his megalomania had grown greater than ever. His ungovernable rages, delusions of grandeur, messianic claims, and dictatorial impulses had all become more extreme; but these violent excesses were not the only symptoms of his curious mental and moral decline. He had lost his shrewd appreciation of realities. His sense of direction was confused in his purposes were equivocal. He showed, at intervals, a cynical selfishness and the ruthless cupidity.... although in public he professed that his sole aim was the redress of the Métis grievances, and private he was quite ready to promise that if the government made him a satisfactory personal payment of a few thousand dollars he would induce his credulous followers to accept almost any settlement the federal authorities desired, and would quietly leave Canada forever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>While Riel awaited news from Ottawa he considered returning to Montana, but had by February resolved to stay. Without a productive course of action, Riel began to engage in obsessive prayer, and was experiencing a significant relapse of his mental agitations. This led to a deterioration in his relationship with the Catholic hierarchy, as he publicly espoused an increasingly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_heresy" title="Christian heresy">heretical</a> doctrine. On 11 February 1885, a response to the petition was received. The government proposed to take a census of the North-West Territories, and to form a commission to investigate grievances. This angered a faction of the Métis who saw it as a mere delaying tactic; they favoured taking up arms at once. Riel became the leader of this faction, but he lost the support practically all the Anglophones (who included many Métis), the Catholic Church, and the great majority of Indians. He also lost the support of the Métis faction supporting local leader <a href="/wiki/Charles_Nolin" title="Charles Nolin">Charles Nolin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> But Riel, undoubtedly influenced by his messianic delusions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> became increasingly supportive of this course of action. In the church at Saint-Laurent on 15 March, Riel disrupted a sermon to argue for this position, following which he was barred from receiving the sacraments. He took more and more about his "divine revelations". But disenchanted with the status quo, and swayed by Riel's charisma and eloquent rhetoric, hundreds of Métis remained loyal to Riel, despite his proclamations that Bishop <a href="/wiki/Ignace_Bourget" title="Ignace Bourget">Ignace Bourget</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> should be accepted as pope, and that <i>"Rome has fallen"</i>.</p>
<p>At his trial, Riel denied allegations that his religious beliefs were as irrational as was being (and continue to be) alleged. He explained as follows:</p>
<p>"I wish to leave Rome aside, inasmuch as it is the cause of division between Catholics and Protestants. I did not wish to force my views...If I could have any influence in the new world it would be to help in that way, even if it takes 200 years to become practical...so my children's children can shake hands with the Protestants of the new world in a friendly manner. I do not wish those evils which exist in Europe to be continued, as much as I can influence it, among the (Metis). I do not wish that to be repeated in America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_rebellion">Open rebellion</span></h3>
<p>On 18 March it became known that the <a href="/wiki/North-West_Mounted_Police" title="North-West Mounted Police">North-West Mounted Police</a> garrison at Battleford was being reinforced. Although only 100 men had been sent in response to warnings from father <a href="/wiki/Alexis_Andr%C3%A9" title="Alexis André">Alexis André</a> and NWMP superintendent <a href="/wiki/Leif_Newry_Fitzroy_Crozier" title="Leif Newry Fitzroy Crozier">L.N.F. Crozier</a>, a rumour soon began to circulate that 500 heavily armed troops were advancing on the territory. Métis patience was exhausted, and Riel's followers seized arms, took hostages, and cut the telegraph lines between Batoche and Battleford. The <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Government_of_Saskatchewan" title="Provisional Government of Saskatchewan">Provisional Government of Saskatchewan</a> was declared at Batoche on 19 March, with Riel<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> as the political and spiritual leader and with Dumont assuming responsibility for military affairs. Riel formed a council called the <a href="/wiki/Exovedate" title="Exovedate">Exovedate</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> (a <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologism</a> meaning "those who have left the flock"), and sent representatives to court Poundmaker and Big Bear. On 21 March, Riel's emissaries demanded that Crozier surrender <a href="/wiki/Fort_Carlton" title="Fort Carlton">Fort Carlton</a>, but this was refused. The situation was becoming critical, and on 23 March Dewdney sent a telegraph to Macdonald indicating that military intervention might be necessary. Scouting near Duck Lake on 26 March, a force led by <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Dumont_(M%C3%A9tis_leader)" title="Gabriel Dumont (Métis leader)">Gabriel Dumont</a> unexpectedly chanced upon a party from Fort Carlton. In the ensuing <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Duck_Lake" title="Battle of Duck Lake">Battle of Duck Lake</a>, the police were routed, and the Natives also rose up once the news became known. The die was cast for a violent outcome, and the <a href="/wiki/North-West_Rebellion" title="North-West Rebellion">North-West Rebellion</a> was begun in earnest.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Louis_Riel_prison.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="610" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Louis_Riel_prison.jpg/220px-Louis_Riel_prison.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Louis_Riel_prison.jpg/330px-Louis_Riel_prison.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Louis_Riel_prison.jpg/440px-Louis_Riel_prison.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Louis_Riel_prison.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Louis Riel imprisoned in Middleton's camp at Batoche, 16 May 1885</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Riel had counted on the Canadian government being unable to effectively respond to another uprising in the distant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North-West_Territories" title="North-West Territories">North-West Territories</a>, thereby forcing them to accept political negotiation. This was essentially the same strategy that had worked to such great effect during the 1870 rebellion. But in that instance, the first troops did not arrive until three months after Riel seized control. However, Riel had completely overlooked the significance of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Pacific_Railway" title="Canadian Pacific Railway">Canadian Pacific Railway</a>. Despite some uncompleted gaps, the first Canadian regular and militia units, under the command of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major-General" title="Major-General">Major-General</a> <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Dobson_Middleton" title="Frederick Dobson Middleton">Frederick Dobson Middleton</a>, arrived in Duck Lake less than two weeks after Riel had made his demands. Knowing that he could not defeat the Canadians in direct confrontation, Dumont had hoped to force the Canadians to negotiate by engaging in a long-drawn out campaign of <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare" title="Guerrilla warfare">guerrilla warfare</a>; Dumont realised a modest success along these lines at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fish_Creek" title="Battle of Fish Creek">Battle of Fish Creek</a> on 24 April 1885.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Riel, however, insisted on concentrating forces at Batoche to defend his "city of God". The outcome of the ensuing <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Batoche" title="Battle of Batoche">Battle of Batoche</a> which took place from 9 to 12 May<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> was never in doubt, and on 15 May a disheveled Riel surrendered to Canadian forces. Although <a href="/wiki/Big_Bear" title="Big Bear">Big Bear</a>'s forces managed to hold out until the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Loon_Lake" title="Battle of Loon Lake">Battle of Loon Lake</a> on 3 June,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> the rebellion was a dismal failure for Métis and Natives alike, as they surrendered or fled.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trial_for_treason">Trial for treason</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Trial_of_Louis_Riel" title="Trial of Louis Riel">Trial of Louis Riel</a></div>
<p>Several individuals closely tied to the government requested that the trial be held in Winnipeg in July 1885. There are historians who contend that the trial was moved to Regina because of concerns with the possibility of an ethnically mixed and sympathetic jury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tom_Flanagan_(political_scientist)" title="Tom Flanagan (political scientist)">Tom Flanagan</a> states that an amendment of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=North-West_Territories_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="North-West Territories Act (page does not exist)">North-West Territories Act</a> (which dropped the provision that trials with crimes punishable by death should be tried in Manitoba) meant that the trial could be convened within the North-West Territories and did not have to be held in Winnipeg.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RielAtTrial.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="284" data-file-width="501" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/RielAtTrial.jpg/300px-RielAtTrial.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/RielAtTrial.jpg/450px-RielAtTrial.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7b/RielAtTrial.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RielAtTrial.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Louis Riel testifies at his trial</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Prime Minister Sir <a href="/wiki/John_A._Macdonald" title="John A. Macdonald">John A. Macdonald</a> ordered the trial to be convened in <a href="/wiki/Regina,_Saskatchewan" title="Regina, Saskatchewan">Regina</a>, where Riel was tried before a jury of six English and Scottish Protestants, all from the area surrounding the city. The trial began on 28 July 1885, and lasted five days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-martyr_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-martyr-2">[2]</a></sup> Riel delivered two long speeches during his trial, defending his own actions and affirming the rights of the Métis people. He rejected his lawyer's attempt to argue that he was <a href="/wiki/Insanity_defense" title="Insanity defense">not guilty by reason of insanity</a>, asserting,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Life, without the dignity of an intelligent being, is not worth having.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The jury found him guilty but recommended mercy; nonetheless, Judge <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Richardson_(magistrate)" title="Hugh Richardson (magistrate)">Hugh Richardson</a> sentenced him to death, with the date of his execution initially set for 18 September 1885.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trial_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-trial-41">[41]</a></sup> "We tried Riel for treason," one juror later said, "And he was hanged for the murder of Scott." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Execution">Execution</span></h2>
<p>Boulton writes in his memoirs that, as the date of his execution approached, Riel regretted his opposition to the defence of insanity and vainly attempted to provide evidence that he was not sane. Requests for a retrial and an appeal to the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a> in Britain were denied. Sir John A. Macdonald, who was instrumental in upholding Riel's sentence, is famously quoted as saying:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>He shall die though every dog in Quebec bark in his favour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Before his execution, Riel was reconciled with the Catholic Church, and assigned Father André as his spiritual advisor. He was also given writing materials so that he could employ his time in prison to write a book. Louis Riel was <a href="/wiki/Hanging" title="Hanging">hanged</a> for treason on 16 November 1885 at the North West Mounted Police barracks in Regina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Martin-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LouisRielTombstone.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1984" data-file-width="1488" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/LouisRielTombstone.jpg/220px-LouisRielTombstone.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/LouisRielTombstone.jpg/330px-LouisRielTombstone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/LouisRielTombstone.jpg/440px-LouisRielTombstone.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LouisRielTombstone.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Riel's tombstone at the St. Boniface Cathedral</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Boulton writes of Riel's final moments,</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>... Père André, after explaining to Riel that the end was at hand, asked him if he was at peace with men. Riel answered <i>"Yes."</i> The next question was, <i>"Do you forgive all your enemies?"</i> <i>"Yes."</i> Riel then asked him if he might speak. Father André advised him not to do so. He then received the kiss of peace from both the priests, and Father André exclaimed in French, <i>"Alors, allez au ciel!"</i> meaning "so, to heaven!"</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd> ... [Riel's] last words were to say good-bye to Dr. Jukes and thank him for his kindness, and just before the white cap was pulled over his face he said, <i>"Remerciez, Madame Forget."</i> meaning "thank, Ms. Forget".</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>The cap was pulled down, and while he was praying the trap was pulled. Death was not instantaneous. Louis Riel's pulse ceased four minutes after the trap-door fell and during that time the rope around his neck slowly strangled and choked him to death. The body was to have been interred inside the gallows' enclosure, and the grave was commenced, but an order came from the Lieutenant-Governor to hand the body over to Sheriff Chapleau which was accordingly done that night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Following the execution, Riel's body was returned to his mother's home in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Vital,_Manitoba" title="St. Vital, Manitoba">St. Vital</a>, where it lay in state. On 12 December 1886, his remains were laid in the churchyard of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint-Boniface_Cathedral" title="Saint-Boniface Cathedral">Saint-Boniface Cathedral</a> following the celebration of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Requiem_mass" title="Requiem mass">requiem mass</a>.</p>
<p>The trial and execution of Riel caused a bitter and prolonged reaction which convulsed Canadian politics for decades. The Orange Irish Protestant element in Ontario had demanded the execution as the punishment for Riel's treason and his execution of Thomas Scott in 1870. With their revenge satisfied, the Orange turned their attention to other matters (especially the <a href="/wiki/Jesuit_Estates_Act" title="Jesuit Estates Act">Jesuit Estates</a> proposal). In Quebec there was no forgetting, and the politician <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_Mercier" title="Honoré Mercier">Honoré Mercier</a> rose to power by mobilizing the opposition in 1886.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span></h2>
<p>Riel remains controversial. J. M. Bumsted in 2000 said that for Manitoba historian James Jackson, the murder of Scott – "perhaps the result of Riel's incipient madness – was the great blemish on Riel's achievement, depriving him of his proper role as the father of Manitoba."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political">Political</span></h3>
<p>The Saskatchewan Métis' requested land grants were all provided by the government by the end of 1887, and the government resurveyed the Métis river lots in accordance with their wishes. The Métis did not understand the long term value of their new land, however, and it was soon bought by speculators who later turned huge profits from it. Riel's worst fears were realised—following the failed rebellion, the French language and Roman Catholic religion faced increasing marginalisation in both Saskatchewan and Manitoba, as exemplified by the controversy surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Manitoba_Schools_Question" title="Manitoba Schools Question">Manitoba Schools Question</a>. The Métis themselves were increasingly forced to live on undesirable land or in the shadow of <a href="/wiki/Indian_reserve" title="Indian reserve">Indian reserves</a> (as they did not themselves have treaty status). Saskatchewan did not attain provincehood until 1905.</p>
<p>Riel's execution and Macdonald's refusal to commute his sentence caused lasting discord in Quebec, and led to a fundamental alteration in the Canadian political order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> In Quebec, <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_Mercier" title="Honoré Mercier">Honoré Mercier</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> exploited the discontent to reconstitute the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parti_National" title="Parti National">Parti National</a>. This party, which promoted <a href="/wiki/Quebec_nationalism" title="Quebec nationalism">Quebec nationalism</a>, won a majority in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1886_Quebec_election" title="1886 Quebec election">1886 Quebec election</a> by winning a number of seats formerly controlled by the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_of_Quebec_(historical)" title="Conservative Party of Quebec (historical)">Quebec Conservative Party</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_1887" title="Canadian federal election, 1887">federal election of 1887</a> likewise saw significant gains by the federal Liberals, again at the expense of the Conservatives. This led to the victory of the Liberal party under Sir <a href="/wiki/Wilfrid_Laurier" title="Wilfrid Laurier">Wilfrid Laurier</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_1896" title="Canadian federal election, 1896">federal election of 1896</a>, which in turn set the stage for the domination of Canadian federal politics by the Liberal party in the 20th century.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revoking_Riel.27s_conviction">Revoking Riel's conviction</span></h2>
<p>That Riel's name still has resonance in Canadian politics was evidenced on 16 November 1994, when <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Tremblay" title="Suzanne Tremblay">Suzanne Tremblay</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Bloc_Qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois" title="Bloc Québécois">Bloc Québécois</a> member of parliament, introduced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Private_Member%27s_Bill" title="Private Member's Bill">private members' bill</a> C-228, "An Act to revoke the conviction of Louis David Riel".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> The unsuccessful bill was widely perceived in English Canada as an attempt to arouse support for Quebec nationalism before the 1995 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1995_Quebec_referendum" title="1995 Quebec referendum">referendum</a> on <a href="/wiki/Quebec_sovereignty_movement" title="Quebec sovereignty movement">Quebec sovereignty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Bill C-213 or Louis Riel Day Act and Bill C-417 Louis Riel Act are the more notable acts which have gone through parliament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> Bill C-297 to revoke the conviction of Louis Riel was introduced to the House of Commons 21 October and 22 November 1996, however the motion lacked unanimous consent from the House and was dropped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Bill C-213<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> or the Louis Riel Day Act of 1997 attempted to revoke the conviction of Louis Riel for high treason and establish a National Day in his honour on 16 November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Bill C-417<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> or the Louis Riel Act which also had a first reading in parliament to revoke the conviction of Louis Riel, and establish 15 July as <i>Louis Riel Day</i> was tabled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historiography">Historiography</span></h3>
<p>Historians have debated the Riel case so often and so passionately that he is the most written-about person in all of Canadian history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Interpretations have varied dramatically over time. The first amateur English language histories hailed the triumph of civilization, represented by English-speaking Protestants, over savagery represented by the half-breed Métis who were Catholic and spoke French. Riel was portrayed as an insane traitor and an obstacle to the expansion of Canada to the West.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> By the mid-20th century academic historians had dropped the theme of savagery versus civilization, deemphasized the Métis, and focused on Riel, presenting his execution as a major cause of the bitter division in Canada along ethnocultural and geographical lines of religion and language. <a href="/wiki/W._L._Morton" title="W. L. Morton">W. L. Morton</a> says the execution:</p>
<dl>
<dd>gave rise to a bitter and prolonged reaction which convulsed the course of national politics for the next decade. In Ontario it had been demanded and applauded by the Orange element as the punishment of treason and a vindication of loyalty. In Quebec Riel was defended, despite his apostasy and megalomania, as the symbol, indeed as a hero of his race.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Morton argued that Riel's demands were unrealistic:</p>
<dl>
<dd>[They] did touch on some real grievances, such as the need for increased representation of the people in the Council of the Territories, but they did not present a program of practical substance which the government might have granted without betrayal of its responsibilities....the Canadian government can hardly be blamed for refusing to continue its private negotiations with him, or for sending in the troops to suppress rebellion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The Catholic bishops had originally supported the Métis but reversed themselves when they realized that Riel was leading a heretical movement. They made sure that he was not honored as a martyr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> However the bishops lost their influence during the <a href="/wiki/Quiet_Revolution" title="Quiet Revolution">Quiet Revolution</a>, and activists in Québec found in Riel the perfect hero, with the image now of a freedom fighter who stood up for his people against an oppressive government in the face of widespread racist bigotry. His insanity was ignored and he was made a folk hero by the Francophones, the Catholic nationalists, the native rights movement, and the <a href="/wiki/New_Left" title="New Left">New Left</a> student movement. Activists who espoused violence embraced his image; in the 1960s, the Quebec terrorist group, the <a href="/wiki/Front_de_lib%C3%A9ration_du_Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Front de libération du Québec">Front de libération du Québec</a> adopted the name "Louis Riel" for one of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrorist_cell" title="Terrorist cell">terrorist cells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> Riel has received more scholarly attention than practically any other figure in Canadian history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p>Across Canada there emerged a new interpretation of reality in his rebellion, holding that the Métis had major unresolved grievances; the government Was unresponsive; that Riel resorted to violence only as a last resort; and he was given a questionable trial then executed by vengeful government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> John Foster said in 1985 that:</p>
<dl>
<dd>the interpretive drift of the last half-century...has witnessed increasingly shrill though frequently uncritical condemnations of Canadian government culpability and equally uncritical identification with the "victimization" of the "innocent" Métis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>However, a leading specialist <a href="/wiki/Tom_Flanagan_(political_scientist)" title="Tom Flanagan (political scientist)">Thomas Flanagan</a> reversed his views after editing Riel's writings:</p>
<dl>
<dd>As I sifted the evidence this became less and less convincing to me until I concluded that the opposite was closer to the truth: that the Métis grievances were at least partly of their own making; that the government was on the verge of resolving them when the Rebellion broke out; That Riel's resort to arms could not be explained by the failure of constitutional agitation and that he received a surprisingly fair trial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>As for the insanity, historians have noted that many religious leaders the past have exhibited behavior that looks exactly like insanity. Flanigan emphasizes that Riel exemplified the tradition of religious mystics involved in politics, especially those with a sense that the world was about to be totally transformed by their religious vision. In his case it meant his delivering the Métis from colonial domination. More broadly, Flanagan argues that Riel was devoutly religious and rejected equalitarianism (which he equated with secularism), concluding he was "a millenarian theocrat, sympathetic to the 'ancien régime' and opposed to the French Revolution, democracy, individualism, and secular society."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<p>Métis scholars have noted that Riel is a more important figure to non-Métis than to Métis; he is the only Métis figure most non-Métis are aware of. Political scientists such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Flanagan_(political_scientist)" title="Thomas Flanagan (political scientist)">Thomas Flanagan</a> have pointed out certain parallels between Riel's following during the North-West Rebellion and <a href="/wiki/Millenarianism" title="Millenarianism">millenarian</a> cults.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commemorations">Commemorations</span></h3>

<p>A resolution was passed by Parliament on 10 March 1992 citing that Louis Riel was the founder of Manitoba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proceeedings_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-proceeedings-100">[100]</a></sup> Two statues of Riel are located in Winnipeg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> One of the Winnipeg statues, the work of architect <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Gaboury" title="Étienne Gaboury">Étienne Gaboury</a> and sculptor <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Marcien_Lemay&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Marcien Lemay (page does not exist)">Marcien Lemay</a>, depicts Riel as a naked and tortured figure. It was unveiled in 1970 and stood in the grounds of the <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_of_Manitoba" title="Legislative Assembly of Manitoba">Legislative Assembly of Manitoba</a> for 23 years. After much outcry (especially from the Métis community) that the statue was an undignified misrepresentation, the statue was removed and placed at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coll%C3%A8ge_universitaire_de_Saint-Boniface" title="Collège universitaire de Saint-Boniface">Collège universitaire de Saint-Boniface</a>. It was replaced in 1994 with a statue designed by <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Joyal" title="Miguel Joyal">Miguel Joyal</a> depicting Riel as a dignified statesman. The unveiling ceremony was on 16 May 1996, in Winnipeg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proceeedings_100-1"><a href="#cite_note-proceeedings-100">[100]</a></sup></p>

<p>A statue of Riel on the grounds of the Saskatchewan legislative building in Regina was installed and later removed for similar reasons.</p>
<p>In numerous communities across Canada, Riel is commemorated in the names of streets, schools, neighbourhoods, and other buildings. Examples in Winnipeg include the landmark <a href="/wiki/Esplanade_Riel" title="Esplanade Riel">Esplanade Riel</a> pedestrian bridge linking Old Saint-Boniface with Winnipeg, the <a href="/wiki/Louis_Riel_School_Division" title="Louis Riel School Division">Louis Riel School Division</a>, Louis Riel Avenue in Old Saint-Boniface, and Riel Avenue in <a href="/wiki/St._Vital,_Winnipeg" title="St. Vital, Winnipeg">St. Vital</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Minnetonka,_Winnipeg" title="Minnetonka, Winnipeg">Minnetonka</a> neighbourhood (which is sometimes called Riel). The student centre and campus pub at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan" title="University of Saskatchewan">University of Saskatchewan</a> in Saskatoon are named after Riel (<i>Place Riel</i> and <i>Louis'</i>, respectively).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan_Highway_11" title="Saskatchewan Highway 11">Highway 11</a>, stretching from Regina to just south of Prince Albert, has been named <i>Louis Riel Trail</i> by the province; the roadway passes near locations of the 1885 rebellion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> One of the student residences at <a href="/wiki/Simon_Fraser_University" title="Simon Fraser University">Simon Fraser University</a> in Burnaby, British Columbia is named Louis Riel House. There is a Louis Riel School in <a href="/wiki/Calgary" title="Calgary">Calgary</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> On 26 September 2007, Manitoba legislature passed a bill establishing a statutory holiday on the third Monday in February as <i>Louis Riel Day</i>, the same day some other provinces celebrate <a href="/wiki/Family_Day_(Canada)" title="Family Day (Canada)">Family Day</a>, beginning in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> The first Louis Riel Day was celebrated on 18 February 2008. This new statutory holiday coincides with the celebration on 15–24 February of the Festival du Voyageur.</p>
<p>In the spring of 2008, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_Saskatchewan" title="Government of Saskatchewan">Government of Saskatchewan</a> Tourism, Parks, Culture and Sport Minister Christine Tell proclaimed in Duck Lake that "the 125th commemoration, in 2010, of the 1885 Northwest Resistance is an excellent opportunity to tell the story of the prairie Métis and First Nations peoples' struggle with Government forces and how it has shaped Canada today."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> One of three <a href="/wiki/Territorial_evolution_of_Canada" title="Territorial evolution of Canada">Territorial</a> Government Buildings remains on Dewdney Avenue in the Saskatchewan capital city of <a href="/wiki/Regina,_Saskatchewan" title="Regina, Saskatchewan">Regina, Saskatchewan</a> which was the site of the Trial of Louis Riel, where the drama the "Trial of Louis Riel" is still performed. Following the May trial, Louis Riel was hanged 16 November 1885. The RCMP Heritage Centre, in Regina, opened in May 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> The Métis brought his body to his mother's home, now the Riel House National Historic Site, and then interred at the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Boniface_Cathedral" title="Saint Boniface Cathedral">St. Boniface Basilica</a> in Manitoba, his birthplace, for burial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts.2C_literature_and_popular_culture">Arts, literature and popular culture</span></h3>
<p>In 1925, the French writer <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Constantin-Weyer" title="Maurice Constantin-Weyer">Maurice Constantin-Weyer</a> who lived 10 years in <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a> published in French a fictionalized biography of Louis Riel titled <i>La Bourrasque</i>. An English translation/adaptation was published in 1930 : <i>A Martyr's Folly</i> (Toronto, The Macmillan Company), and a new version in 1954, <i>The Half-Breed</i> (New York, The Macaulay Compagny).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup></p>
<p>Portrayals of Riel's role in the Red River Rebellion include the 1979 <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Canadian Broadcasting Corporation">CBC</a> television film <i>Riel</i> and Canadian <a href="/wiki/Cartoonist" title="Cartoonist">cartoonist</a> <a href="/wiki/Chester_Brown" title="Chester Brown">Chester Brown</a>'s acclaimed 2003 <a href="/wiki/Graphic_novel" title="Graphic novel">graphic novel</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Louis_Riel_(comics)" title="Louis Riel (comics)">Louis Riel: A Comic-Strip Biography</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1940 film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_West_Mounted_Police" title="North West Mounted Police">North West Mounted Police</a></i> Riel is portrayed by <i><a href="/wiki/Francis_McDonald" title="Francis McDonald">Francis McDonald</a></i>.</p>
<p>An opera about Riel entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Louis_Riel_(opera)" title="Louis Riel (opera)">Louis Riel</a></i> was commissioned for <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Centennial" title="Canadian Centennial">Canada's centennial</a> celebrations in 1967. It was an opera in three acts, written by <a href="/wiki/Harry_Somers" title="Harry Somers">Harry Somers</a>, with an English and French <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">libretto</a> by <a href="/wiki/Mavor_Moore" title="Mavor Moore">Mavor Moore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Languirand" title="Jacques Languirand">Jacques Languirand</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Opera_Company" title="Canadian Opera Company">Canadian Opera Company</a> produced and performed the first run of the opera in September and October 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup></p>

<p>From the late 1960s until the early 1990s, the city of <a href="/wiki/Saskatoon" title="Saskatoon">Saskatoon</a> hosted "Louis Riel Day", a summer celebration that included a relay race that combined running, backpack carrying, <a href="/wiki/Canoeing" title="Canoeing">canoeing</a>, hill climbing, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horseback_riding" title="Horseback riding">horseback riding</a> along the <a href="/wiki/South_Saskatchewan_River" title="South Saskatchewan River">South Saskatchewan River</a> in the city's downtown core. Traditionally, the event also included a <a href="/wiki/Cabbage_roll" title="Cabbage roll">cabbage roll</a> eating contest and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tug-of-war" title="Tug-of-war">tug-of-war</a> competition, as well as live musical performances. Although not affiliated with the Saskatoon Exhibition, for years Louis Riel Day was scheduled for the day before the start of the fair, and as such came to be considered the Exhibition's unofficial kick-off (the scheduling of the two events was separated in later years). The event was discontinued when major sponsors pulled out.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Billy_Childish" title="Billy Childish">Billy Childish</a> wrote a song entitled "Louis Riel", which was performed by <a href="/wiki/Thee_Headcoats" title="Thee Headcoats">Thee Headcoats</a>. Texas musician <a href="/wiki/Doug_Sahm" title="Doug Sahm">Doug Sahm</a> wrote a song entitled "Louis Riel," which appeared on the album <i>S.D.Q. '98</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> In the song, Sahm likens the lore surrounding Riel to David Crockett's legend in his home state, spinning an abridged tale of Riel's life as a revolutionary: "...but you gotta respect him for what he thought was right... And all around Regina they talk about him still – why did they have to kill Louis Riel?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Seattle-based Indie rock band <a href="/wiki/Grand_Archives" title="Grand Archives">Grand Archives</a> also wrote a song entitled "Louis Riel" that appears on their 2008 <a href="/wiki/The_Grand_Archives" title="The Grand Archives">self-titled album</a>.</p>
<p>A track entitled <i>Snowin' Today: A Lament for Louis Riel</i> appears on the 2009 album <i>Live: Two Nights In March</i> by Saskatchewan singer/guitarist Little Miss Higgins; a studio version features on her 2010 release <i>Across The Plains</i>.</p>
<p>On 22 October 2003, the Canadian news channel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CBC_Newsworld" title="CBC Newsworld">CBC Newsworld</a> and its French-language equivalent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%C3%A9seau_de_l%27information" title="Réseau de l'information">Réseau de l'information</a>, staged a simulated retrial of Riel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup> Viewers were invited to enter a verdict on the trial over the internet, and more than 10,000 votes were received—87% of which were "not guilty".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> The results of this straw poll led to renewed calls for Riel's posthumous <a href="/wiki/Pardon" title="Pardon">pardon</a>. Also on the basis of a public poll, the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Canadian Broadcasting Corporation">CBC's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Greatest_Canadian" title="The Greatest Canadian">Greatest Canadian</a></i> project ranked Riel as the 11th "Greatest Canadian".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup></p>
<p>An episode of the TV-series <i><a href="/wiki/How_the_West_Was_Won_(TV_series)" title="How the West Was Won (TV series)">How the West Was Won</a></i> from 1979 was named <i>L'Affaire Riel</i>, featuring Louis Riel while in exile in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, Canadian sketch comedy troupe <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Air_Farce" title="Royal Canadian Air Farce">Royal Canadian Air Farce</a> featured Riel in its send-up of the CBC documentary series <i>Canada: A People's History.</i> Significant parallels were drawn between Riel's actions and those of modern-day Québécois separatists, and the comedian who portrayed Riel was made up to look like then-Premier <a href="/wiki/Lucien_Bouchard" title="Lucien Bouchard">Lucien Bouchard</a>.</p>
