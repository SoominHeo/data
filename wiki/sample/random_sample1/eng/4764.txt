<p><b>Luigi Di Biagio</b> (<small>Italian pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[luˈidʒi diˈbjadʒo]</a></span>; born June 3, 1971), is an <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Association_football_manager" title="Association football manager">manager</a> and former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Association_footballer" title="Association footballer">footballer</a>, who currently coaches the <a href="/wiki/Italy_national_under-21_football_team" title="Italy national under-21 football team">Italy U-21 team</a>. A former <a href="/wiki/Midfielder#Defensive_midfielder" title="Midfielder">defensive midfielder</a>, Di Biagio last played for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascoli_Calcio_1898" title="Ascoli Calcio 1898">Ascoli Calcio 1898</a> (2006–07), and also played for several other Italian clubs throughout his career, including <a href="/wiki/A.S._Roma" title="A.S. Roma">Roma</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internazionale_Milano_F.C." title="Internazionale Milano F.C.">Inter</a>, in particular. At international level, he also played 31 times for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_national_football_team" title="Italian national football team">Italian national side</a> between 1998 and 2002, scoring 2 goals, representing his country at the <a href="/wiki/1998_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1998 FIFA World Cup">1998</a> and <a href="/wiki/2002_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2002 FIFA World Cup">2002 World Cups</a>, as well as at <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2000" title="UEFA Euro 2000">Euro 2000</a>, where Italy reached the final.</p>
<p>In 2010 he temporarily worked as a football pundit. On 19 September, he assisted Riccardo Trevisani in the commentary for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palermo_calcio" title="Palermo calcio">Palermo</a>–Inter, during the third matchday of the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_Serie_A" title="2010–11 Serie A">2010–11 Serie A</a> season, which was broadcast through <a href="/wiki/Sky_Sport_(Italy)" title="Sky Sport (Italy)">Sky Sport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Club_career">Club career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luigi_Di_Biagio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Club career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luigi_Di_Biagio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Di Biagio was born in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>. He initially played for <a href="/wiki/S.S._Lazio" title="S.S. Lazio">Lazio</a> (1988–89), making his <a href="/wiki/Serie_A" title="Serie A">Serie A</a> debut with the side, and later played for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A.C._Monza_Brianza_1912" title="A.C. Monza Brianza 1912">Monza</a> (1989–92) in <a href="/wiki/Serie_B" title="Serie B">Serie B</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serie_C1" title="Serie C1">Serie C1</a>, winning the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coppa_Italia_Serie_C" title="Coppa Italia Serie C">Coppa Italia C1</a> in 1991. He later moved on to play for <a href="/wiki/Foggia_Calcio" title="Foggia Calcio">Foggia</a> (1992–95) under <a href="/wiki/Zden%C4%9Bk_Zeman" title="Zdeněk Zeman">Zeman</a>, where he gained promotion to Serie A once again, establishing himself in the starting lineup of the club as a defensive minded central midfielder, who also served as a playmaker; he notably reached the semi-final of the <a href="/wiki/Coppa_Italia" title="Coppa Italia">Coppa Italia</a> with Foggia during the <a href="/wiki/1994%E2%80%9395_Coppa_Italia" title="1994–95 Coppa Italia">1994–95 season</a>, attracting the attention of bigger Italian clubs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legaseriea.it_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-legaseriea.it-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enciclopediagiallorossa.com_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-enciclopediagiallorossa.com-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emergence_with_Roma">Emergence with Roma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luigi_Di_Biagio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Emergence with Roma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Di Biagio began to achieve greater international exposure and fame in Italy due to his excellent and consistent performances whilst playing for <a href="/wiki/A.S._Roma" title="A.S. Roma">Roma</a> (1995–99), where he remained for five seasons, under managers <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Mazzone" title="Carlo Mazzone">Carlo Mazzone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Bianchi" title="Carlos Bianchi">Carlos Bianchi</a>, and Zeman once again; he would make his career debut in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UEFA_club_competitions" title="UEFA club competitions">European Competitions</a> with the club. During his time at Roma, he was able to help the squad to a fourth-place finish during the <a href="/wiki/1997%E2%80%9398_Serie_A" title="1997–98 Serie A">1997–98 season</a>, and managed to subsequently reach the quarter-finals of the <a href="/wiki/1998%E2%80%9399_UEFA_Cup" title="1998–99 UEFA Cup">1998–99 UEFA Cup</a> and the <a href="/wiki/1997%E2%80%9398_Coppa_Italia" title="1997–98 Coppa Italia">1997–98 Coppa Italia</a>. His consistent performances led him to become a permanent member of the Italian national side during this period. Di Biagio would make 140 appearances for Roma in all competitions, scoring 18 goals. 15 of his goals were scored in Serie A in 114 appearances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legaseriea.it_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-legaseriea.it-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enciclopediagiallorossa.com_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-enciclopediagiallorossa.com-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_prominence_with_Inter">International prominence with Inter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luigi_Di_Biagio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: International prominence with Inter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_Serie_A" title="1999–2000 Serie A">1999–2000 Serie A season</a>, Di Biagio transferred to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internazionale_Milano_F.C." title="Internazionale Milano F.C.">Internazionale</a>, and became a regular first team member due to his continued high standard of performance. During the <a href="/wiki/2001%E2%80%9302_Serie_A" title="2001–02 Serie A">2001–02 season</a>, he narrowly missed out on winning the Serie A title to <a href="/wiki/Juventus_F.C." title="Juventus F.C.">Juventus</a> on the final match-day; he received criticism, however, for his despondent performance in a 4–2 defeat to <a href="/wiki/S.S._Lazio" title="S.S. Lazio">Lazio</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Stadio_Olimpico" title="Stadio Olimpico">Stadio Olimpico</a> in Rome, on his final league appearance of the season, despite initially scoring a goal during the match.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Di Biagio remained at Inter until 2003, and during his four years at the club, he amassed 163 appearances, scoring a total of 18 goals in all competitions.</p>
<p>Although he was unable to win a title during his time with the club, he did manage to reach the semi-finals of the <a href="/wiki/2001%E2%80%9302_UEFA_Cup" title="2001–02 UEFA Cup">2001–02 UEFA Cup</a> with Inter, losing out to eventual Champions <a href="/wiki/Feyenoord" title="Feyenoord">Feyenoord</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He also reached the semi-finals of the <a href="/wiki/2002%E2%80%9303_UEFA_Champions_League" title="2002–03 UEFA Champions League">2002–03 Champions League</a> with Inter, under manager <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hector_Cuper" title="Hector Cuper">Hector Cuper</a>, losing out to <a href="/wiki/Derby_della_Madonnina" title="Derby della Madonnina">cross-city rivals</a> and eventual Champions <a href="/wiki/A.C._Milan" title="A.C. Milan">Milan</a> on away goals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He also managed to win runners up medals in <a href="/wiki/Serie_A" title="Serie A">Serie A</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serie_A_2002%E2%80%9303" title="Serie A 2002–03">2002–03 season</a> under Cuper,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Italy_2002.2F03_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Italy_2002.2F03-8">[8]</a></sup> as well as in the <a href="/wiki/Coppa_Italia" title="Coppa Italia">Coppa Italia</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_Coppa_Italia" title="1999–2000 Coppa Italia">1999–2000 season</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raisport.rai.it_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-raisport.rai.it-9">[9]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_Supercup" title="Italian Supercup">Italian Supercup</a> in <a href="/wiki/2000_Supercoppa_Italiana" title="2000 Supercoppa Italiana">2000</a>, under <a href="/wiki/Marcello_Lippi" title="Marcello Lippi">Marcello Lippi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canaleinter.it_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-canaleinter.it-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brescia">Brescia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luigi_Di_Biagio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Brescia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Di Biagio subsequently moved to <a href="/wiki/Brescia_Calcio" title="Brescia Calcio">Brescia Calcio</a> (2003–2006) during his later career, where he played alongside <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Baggio" title="Roberto Baggio">Roberto Baggio</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serie_A_2003%E2%80%9304" title="Serie A 2003–04">2003–04 season</a>, frequently playing as a sweeper or as a centreback/secondary defensive playmaker in Brecia's three-man defence. Although Di Biagio managed a personal best of 9 goals in Serie A the following year, Brescia were relegated to Serie B during the <a href="/wiki/2004%E2%80%9305_Serie_A" title="2004–05 Serie A">2004–05 season</a>, and Di Biagio finished his final season at Brescia playing in the Italian second division during the <a href="/wiki/2005%E2%80%9306_Serie_B" title="2005–06 Serie B">2005–06 season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legaseriea.it_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-legaseriea.it-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enciclopediagiallorossa.com_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-enciclopediagiallorossa.com-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_career_and_retirement">Later career and retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luigi_Di_Biagio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later career and retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Di Biagio signed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascoli_Calcio_1898" title="Ascoli Calcio 1898">Ascoli</a> in November 2006, but the bid was not considered to be valid by the federation, since the player was not released for free by Brescia before the June 30 deadline. The bid was therefore postponed until January 2007, and in the meantime Di Biagio went on training with Ascoli, and played from November to December with <a href="/wiki/Promozione" title="Promozione">Promozione</a> club <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Polisportiva_La_Storta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Polisportiva La Storta (page does not exist)">Polisportiva La Storta</a> from Rome, coached by his friend and former <a href="/wiki/Dundee_F.C." title="Dundee F.C.">Dundee</a> and Lazio footballer <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Romano" title="Alessandro Romano">Alessandro Romano</a>. Di Biagio played his first Ascoli match on January 14, 2007, against <a href="/wiki/Cagliari_Calcio" title="Cagliari Calcio">Cagliari</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He collected only 7 appearances that season, scoring two goals in 523 minutes. Ascoli finished second last in <a href="/wiki/2006%E2%80%9307_Serie_A" title="2006–07 Serie A">Serie A</a> that season and were relegated to Serie B. retired at the end of the season, and returned to Polisportiva La Storta as a youth coach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legaseriea.it_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-legaseriea.it-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acmonzabrianza.it_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-acmonzabrianza.it-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite his reputation and ability as a midfielder,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Il_Corriere_della_Sera_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Il_Corriere_della_Sera-13">[13]</a></sup> Di Biagio had an unfortunate club and international career, failing to win a major trophy, and only winning the Coppa Italia Serie C1 with Monza.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_career">International career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luigi_Di_Biagio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: International career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Di Biagio was a regular member of the national side during the late 1990s and early 2000s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maldini_prova_i_soliti_noti_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maldini_prova_i_soliti_noti-14">[14]</a></sup> and was capped 31 times for <a href="/wiki/Italy_national_football_team" title="Italy national football team">Italy</a> between 1998 and 2002, scoring two goals. He represented <a href="/wiki/Italy_national_under-21_football_team" title="Italy national under-21 football team">Italy at Under-23 level</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Football_at_the_1993_Mediterranean_Games_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_team_squads" title="Football at the 1993 Mediterranean Games – Men's team squads">1993 Mediterranean games</a>, where they reached the final under manager Cesare Maldini.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acmonzabrianza.it_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-acmonzabrianza.it-12">[12]</a></sup> He made his senior debut for Italy on 28 January 1998, in a 3–0 friendly home win against <a href="/wiki/Slovakia_national_football_team" title="Slovakia national football team">Slovakia</a>, under <a href="/wiki/Cesare_Maldini" title="Cesare Maldini">Cesare Maldini</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-figc.it_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-figc.it-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>After a strong season with Roma, Di Biagio represented his country in the <a href="/wiki/1998_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1998 FIFA World Cup">1998</a> World Cup, appearing in each of Italy's five matches, and notably scoring a headed goal from a <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Baggio" title="Roberto Baggio">Roberto Baggio</a> cross in the second group stage match against <a href="/wiki/Cameroon_national_football_team" title="Cameroon national football team">Cameroon</a>, which ended in a 3–0 win for Italy;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> his goal being the <a href="/wiki/Italy_national_football_team_records#Goalscorers" title="Italy national football team records">100th goal for Italy in World Cup matches</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Record_e_Curiosit.C3.A0_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Record_e_Curiosit.C3.A0-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In the round of 16 match against <a href="/wiki/Norway_national_football_team" title="Norway national football team">Norway</a>, Di Biagio set up <a href="/wiki/Christian_Vieri" title="Christian Vieri">Vieri's</a> match winning goal, and helped Italy to keep a clean sheet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Despite a strong tournament, he missed the decisive penalty in the quarter-final shootout against hosts and eventual champions <a href="/wiki/France_national_football_team" title="France national football team">France</a>, after a 0–0 deadlock following extra time, hitting the crossbar, as Italy were eliminated from the tournament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-figc.it_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-figc.it-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Di Biagio continued to be a key player for Italy under <a href="/wiki/Dino_Zoff" title="Dino Zoff">Dino Zoff</a>, and he went on to represent Italy at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2000_UEFA_European_Football_Championship" title="2000 UEFA European Football Championship">Euro 2000</a>, starting alongside <a href="/wiki/Demetrio_Albertini" title="Demetrio Albertini">Albertini</a> in midfield, and winning a runners-up medal as Italy lost 2–1 to France in the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2000_Final" title="UEFA Euro 2000 Final">final</a> on a golden goal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UEFA_Euro_2000_Final_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-UEFA_Euro_2000_Final-21">[21]</a></sup> Di Biagio had another successful tournament for Italy, and he scored Italy's first goal against <a href="/wiki/Sweden_national_football_team" title="Sweden national football team">Sweden</a>, heading in a <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Del_Piero" title="Alessandro Del Piero">Del Piero</a> corner, in Italy's final group match, which ended in a 2–1 win, and which allowed Italy to top their group and progress to the quarter-finals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In the victorious semi-final penalty shootout against co-hosts the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_national_football_team" title="Netherlands national football team">Netherlands</a>, he took Italy's first penalty, and was able to convert it successfully on this occasion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acmonzabrianza.it_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-acmonzabrianza.it-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> It was the first penalty he had taken after the infamous miss which struck the crossbar during the 1998 World Cup, which ultimately eliminated Italy from the competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-figc.it_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-figc.it-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Trapattoni" title="Giovanni Trapattoni">Giovanni Trapattoni</a>, Di Biagio was also a member of Italy's <a href="/wiki/2002_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2002 FIFA World Cup">2002</a> World Cup squad that was eliminated by co-hosts <a href="/wiki/South_Korea_national_football_team" title="South Korea national football team">South Korea</a> in the Round of 16 on a golden goal. Di Biagio made only one appearance in the tournament, however, playing in Italy's 2–0 opening win against <a href="/wiki/Ecuador_national_football_team" title="Ecuador national football team">Ecuador</a>, where he assisted Vieri's second goal of the match with a long ball, in the 27th minute; he was substituted by <a href="/wiki/Gennaro_Gattuso" title="Gennaro Gattuso">Gattuso</a> in the 69th minute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> He made his final appearance for Italy in a friendly match against <a href="/wiki/Turkey_national_football_team" title="Turkey national football team">Turkey</a> on 20 November 2002, in Pescara.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-figc.it_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-figc.it-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Managerial_career">Managerial career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luigi_Di_Biagio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Managerial career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Di_biagio-Under_21.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1878" data-file-width="1457" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Di_biagio-Under_21.JPG/220px-Di_biagio-Under_21.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Di_biagio-Under_21.JPG/330px-Di_biagio-Under_21.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Di_biagio-Under_21.JPG/440px-Di_biagio-Under_21.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Following his retirement from Ascoli in 2007, Di Biagio returned to football, signing a contract with the amateur 1993 youth side La Polisportiva La Storta as a youth coach, in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acmonzabrianza.it_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-acmonzabrianza.it-12">[12]</a></sup> In August 2007, he signed a contract with Cisco Roma as a youth coach. In July 2008, he had managed to officially obtain his first degree coaching licence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 25 July 2011, he was named the coach of Italy's under-20 side. On 2 July 2013, Luigi Di Biagio stepped up an age group to replace <a href="/wiki/Devis_Mangia" title="Devis Mangia">Devis Mangia</a> as Italy's under-21 coach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> He made his debut as the Italy under-21 coach on 14 August 2013, in a friendly match against <a href="/wiki/Slovakia_national_under-21_football_team" title="Slovakia national under-21 football team">Slovakia</a>, which was won 4–1 by <a href="/wiki/Italy_national_under-21_football_team" title="Italy national under-21 football team">Italy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> He eventually helped the u-21 side to qualify for the upcoming <a href="/wiki/2015_UEFA_European_Under-21_Championship" title="2015 UEFA European Under-21 Championship">2015 Under-21 European Championship</a> in the Czech Republic, as Italy defeated Slovakia once again in the play-offs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the final tournament, Italy were placed in a group containing <a href="/wiki/England_national_under-21_football_team" title="England national under-21 football team">England</a> and eventual finalists <a href="/wiki/Portugal_national_under-21_football_team" title="Portugal national under-21 football team">Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sweden_national_under-21_football_team" title="Sweden national under-21 football team">Sweden</a>, who would go on to win the tournament. Italy finished third in their group, and were eliminated in the first round with 4 points.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style_of_play">Style of play</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luigi_Di_Biagio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Style of play">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Di Biagio was one of Italy's best and most consistent <a href="/wiki/Midfielder" title="Midfielder">midfielders</a> during the later 1990s and early 2000s, and one of the best players in the world in his position during his prime,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> consistently providing excellent performances for Roma, Inter and the Italian National side;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maldini_prova_i_soliti_noti_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Maldini_prova_i_soliti_noti-14">[14]</a></sup> he often stood out throughout his career for his leadership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enciclopediagiallorossa.com_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-enciclopediagiallorossa.com-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Il_Corriere_della_Sera_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Il_Corriere_della_Sera-13">[13]</a></sup> Di Biagio was a complete, tenacious and combative <a href="/wiki/Midfielder#Defensive_midfielder" title="Midfielder">defensive midfielder</a>, whose best strengths involved breaking up the opposition's attacks, although he was also capable of getting forward and making attacking runs when needed; furthermore, he was known for his ability to either set the tempo of his team's build-up plays with short exchanges, or start counter-attacks with long balls after winning back possession.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moriero.2C_amore_al_primo_dribbling_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moriero.2C_amore_al_primo_dribbling-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Throughout his career, Di Biagio earned a reputation as a strong, aggressive, and hard-tackling midfielder, who had a knack for picking up cards; after <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Montero" title="Paolo Montero">Paolo Montero</a>, he is the player with the most red cards in <a href="/wiki/Football_records_in_Italy" title="Football records in Italy">Serie A history</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although he was primarily a <a href="/wiki/Midfielder#Central_midfielder" title="Midfielder">central</a> or defensive midfielder, he was capable of playing anywhere in midfield due to his tactical versatility, and even occasionally played in an <a href="/wiki/Midfielder#Attacking_midfielder" title="Midfielder">attacking role</a> behind the <a href="/wiki/Forward_(association_football)" title="Forward (association football)">forwards</a>; he also played as a <a href="/wiki/Defender_(association_football)" title="Defender (association football)">defender</a> later in his career, once he lost his pace, functioning as a <a href="/wiki/Sweeper_(association_football)" title="Sweeper (association football)">sweeper</a>, as a <a href="/wiki/Defender_(association_football)#Full-back" title="Defender (association football)">full-back</a>, or as a <a href="/wiki/Defender_(association_football)#Centre-back" title="Defender (association football)">central defender</a>. A hardworking player, Di Biagio was gifted with power, stamina, and tactical intelligence, as well as an acute defensive awareness and positional sense. He combined these attributes with a surprising technical ability, and was also capable of functioning creatively as a <a href="/wiki/Playmaker#Deep-lying_playmakers" title="Playmaker">deep-lying playmaker</a> in midfield, due to his ball control, vision, and <a href="/wiki/Passing_(association_football)" title="Passing (association football)">passing</a> range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moriero.2C_amore_al_primo_dribbling_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Moriero.2C_amore_al_primo_dribbling-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> He also possessed a powerful shot from distance, and was a dangerous <a href="/wiki/Direct_free_kick" title="Direct free kick">free kick</a> and penalty kick taker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enciclopediagiallorossa.com_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-enciclopediagiallorossa.com-4">[4]</a></sup> Additionally, he excelled in the air, which made him lethal in the box during set-pieces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acmonzabrianza.it_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-acmonzabrianza.it-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
