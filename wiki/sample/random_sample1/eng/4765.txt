<p><a href="/wiki/Marshal_of_Italy" title="Marshal of Italy">Marshal of Italy</a> <b>Luigi Cadorna</b>, <span style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saints_Maurice_and_Lazarus" title="Order of Saints Maurice and Lazarus">OSML</a>, <a href="/wiki/Military_Order_of_Savoy" title="Military Order of Savoy">OMS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Crown_of_Italy" title="Order of the Crown of Italy">OCI</a></span> (4 September 1850 – 21 December 1928) was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_people" title="Italian people">Italian</a> General and <a href="/wiki/Marshal_of_Italy" title="Marshal of Italy">Marshal of Italy</a>, most famous for being the <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_Staff_of_the_Italian_Army" title="Chief of Staff of the Italian Army">Chief of Staff of the Italian Army</a> during the first part of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luigi_Cadorna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Luigi Cadorna was born to General <a href="/wiki/Raffaele_Cadorna" title="Raffaele Cadorna">Raffaele Cadorna</a> in <a href="/wiki/Verbania" title="Verbania">Verbania Pallanza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">Piedmont</a> in 1850. In 1860 Cardona became a student at the "Teuliè" Military School in Milan. At fifteen he entered the Turin Military Academy. Upon graduation he was commissioned as a second lieutenant of artillery in 1868. In 1870, as an officer in the 2nd Regiment of Artillery, Cadorna participated in the occupation of Rome as part of a force commanded by his father. As major he was appointed to the staff of General Pianell, afterwards taking the post of Chief of Staff of the Verona Divisional Command. As Colonel commanding the 10th Regiment of <a href="/wiki/Bersaglieri" title="Bersaglieri">Bersaglieri</a> from 1892 Cadorna acquired a reputation for strict discipline and harsh punishment. He wrote a manual of infantry tactics which laid stress on the doctrine of the offensive. Promoted to lieutenant general in 1898 Cadorna subsequently held a number of senior staff and divisional/corps command positions. On the eve of Italy's entry into World War (1915) he was close to peace-time retirement age and had a history of differences with his political and military superiors.</p>
<p>Cadorna had been offered the post of Chief of Staff for the first time in 1908, which he had rejected over the issue of political control during wartime. He was again offered the position in July 1914, as the <a href="/wiki/Triple_Entente" title="Triple Entente">Triple Entente</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Powers" title="Central Powers">Central Powers</a> girded for war. When Italy entered the war in May 1915 on the side of the Entente, Cadorna fielded thirty-six infantry divisions composed of 875,000 men, but with only 120 modern artillery pieces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>Cadorna inherited a difficult political and military situation. The government of Premier <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Salandra" title="Antonio Salandra">Antonio Salandra</a> favored initial neutrality over Italy's treaty commitments under the <a href="/wiki/Triple_Alliance_(1882)" title="Triple Alliance (1882)">Triple Alliance</a>. Cadorna was accordingly obliged to reverse long established strategic plans while discovering that the army was ill-prepared for war against Austria-Hungary and Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In particular large numbers of men and quantities of equipment had been deployed to <a href="/wiki/Tripolitania" title="Tripolitania">Tripolitania</a> leaving the home army disorganized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cadorna launched four offensives in 1915, all along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isonzo_River" title="Isonzo River">Isonzo River</a>. The goal of these offensives was the fortress of <a href="/wiki/Gorizia" title="Gorizia">Gorizia</a>, the capture of which would permit the Italian armies to pivot south and march on <a href="/wiki/Trieste" title="Trieste">Trieste</a>, or continue on to the <a href="/wiki/Ljubljana_Gap" title="Ljubljana Gap">Ljubljana Gap</a>. All four offensives failed, resulting in some 250,000 Italian casualties for little material gain. Cadorna would ultimately fight eleven battles on the Isonzo between 1915 and 1917. Additional forces were arrayed along <a href="/wiki/Trentino" title="Trentino">the Trentino salient</a>, attacking treaty commitments as towards <a href="/wiki/Rovereto" title="Rovereto">Rovereto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trento" title="Trento">Trento</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bolzano" title="Bolzano">Bolzano</a>. These attacks also failed. The terrain along the Isonzo and Trentino was completely unsuited for offensive warfare–mountainous and broken, with no room for maneuver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 24 October 1917 a combined Austro-Hungarian/German army struck across the Soča (the Slovene name for the Isonzo River) at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Caporetto" title="Battle of Caporetto">Kobarid</a> (called <i>Caporetto</i> in Italian) and by 12 November had advanced all the way to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piave_River" title="Piave River">Piave River</a>. Cadorna's disposition of most of his troops far forward, with little defense in depth, contributed greatly to the disaster;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> but graver still were the responsibilities of other officers, notably <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Badoglio" title="Pietro Badoglio">Pietro Badoglio</a>, then corps commander in a sector overrun by the Austro-German attack. Cadorna himself had been on leave for most of October and his immediate subordinate was seriously ill.</p>
<p>The Italian Army fled in disarray and seemed on the verge of total collapse; 275,000 soldiers surrendered. Italy's allies Britain and France sent eleven divisions to reinforce the Italian front, and insisted on the dismissal of Cadorna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The General was relieved of command on 9 November 1917.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herald_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herald-7">[7]</a></sup> The new Italian Prime Minister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vittorio_Orlando" title="Vittorio Orlando">Vittorio Orlando</a> appointed the respected General <a href="/wiki/Armando_Diaz" title="Armando Diaz">Armando Diaz</a> as Chief of General Staff,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> with Badoglio named as his second-in-command.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herald_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Herald-7">[7]</a></sup> Cadorna was reassigned as the Italian representative to the Allied <a href="/wiki/Supreme_War_Council" title="Supreme War Council">Supreme War Council</a> set up in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Versailles_(city)" title="Versailles (city)">Versailles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herald_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Herald-7">[7]</a></sup> The Italian forces thereafter rallied behind the Piave and Monte Grappa and, with the help of the Allied divisions, reversed the course of the conflict.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_war">Post war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luigi_Cadorna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Post war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the war, there was an enquiry held by the Italian government to investigate the defeat at Caporetto. It was published in 1919 and was highly critical of Cadorna, at that time a bitter man busy with writing his memoirs who claimed that he had no responsibility for the defeat, despite fleeing to Padua during the battle and abandoning the entire Italian Second Army to its fate. Nevertheless, he was made a Field Marshal (Maresciallo d'Italia) in 1924 after <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a> seized power.</p>
<p>Field Marshal Cadorna died in <a href="/wiki/Bordighera" title="Bordighera">Bordighera</a> in 1928.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_reputation">Personal reputation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luigi_Cadorna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal reputation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historians record Cadorna as an unimaginative <a href="/wiki/Martinet" title="Martinet">martinet</a> who was ruthless with his troops and dismissive of his country's political authorities. David Stevenson, Professor of International History at the London School of Economics, describes him as earning "opprobrium as one of the most callous and incompetent of First World War commanders."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In manner he appeared a reserved and aristocratic officer of the old-fashioned Piedmontese school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> During the course of the war Cadorna dismissed 217 officers, and during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Caporetto" title="Battle of Caporetto">Battle of Caporetto</a>, he ordered the summary execution of officers whose units retreated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Six percent of Italian soldiers under his leadership faced a disciplinary charge during the war and 61 percent of them were found guilty. About 750 were executed, the highest number in any army in World War I.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Claims have been made that he also reintroduced the ancient Roman practice of <a href="/wiki/Decimation_(Roman_army)" title="Decimation (Roman army)">decimation</a>—the killing of every tenth man—for units which failed to perform in battle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, the military historian John Keegan records that his "judicial savagery" took the form of the summary executions of individual stragglers rather than the formalized winnowing of entire detachments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[Note 1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luigi_Cadorna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was the father of <a href="/wiki/Raffaele_Cadorna,_Jr." title="Raffaele Cadorna, Jr.">Raffaele Cadorna, Jr</a>, an Italian general who fought during World War I and World War II, who was famous as one of the commanders of the Italian Resistance against German occupying forces in north Italy after 1943.</p>
