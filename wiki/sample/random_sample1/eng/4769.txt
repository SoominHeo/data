<p>The <b>canton of Lucerne</b> (German: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Kanton Luzern</i></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_Switzerland" title="Cantons of Switzerland">canton</a> of <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>. It is located in the centre of Switzerland. The population of the canton (as of 31 December 2015) is 398,762.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> As of 2007<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the population included 57,268 foreigners, or about 15.8% of the total population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The cantonal capital is <a href="/wiki/Lucerne" title="Lucerne">Lucerne</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The canton of Lucerne comprises territories acquired by its capital <a href="/wiki/Lucerne" title="Lucerne">Lucerne</a>, either by treaty, armed occupation or purchase. The first town acquired was <a href="/wiki/Weggis" title="Weggis">Weggis</a> (in 1380), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rothenburg,_Switzerland" title="Rothenburg, Switzerland">Rothenburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kriens" title="Kriens">Kriens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horw" title="Horw">Horw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sempach" title="Sempach">Sempach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hochdorf,_Lucerne" title="Hochdorf, Lucerne">Hochdorf</a> (all in 1394), <a href="/wiki/Wolhusen" title="Wolhusen">Wolhusen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Entlebuch" title="Entlebuch">Entlebuch</a> (1405), the so-called "Habsburger region" to the northeast of the town of Lucerne (1406), <a href="/wiki/Willisau" title="Willisau">Willisau</a> (1407), <a href="/wiki/Sursee" title="Sursee">Sursee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berom%C3%BCnster" title="Beromünster">Beromünster</a> (1415), <a href="/wiki/Malters" title="Malters">Malters</a> (1477) and <a href="/wiki/Littau" title="Littau">Littau</a> (1481), while in 1803, in exchange for <a href="/wiki/Hitzkirch" title="Hitzkirch">Hitzkirch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merenschwand" title="Merenschwand">Merenschwand</a> (held since 1397) was given up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_canton_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB_canton-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

</div>
<p>The oldest traces of humans in the Lucerne area are stone artifacts and <a href="/wiki/Cave_bear" title="Cave bear">cave bear</a> bones found in the Steigelfadbalm cave on Mt. <a href="/wiki/Rigi" title="Rigi">Rigi</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Paleolithic" title="Middle Paleolithic">Middle Paleolithic</a> or about 30,000 BC. Other animal bones including mammoth, reindeer and giant deer from the <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">local glacial maximum</a> have also been found in the canton. Around 17,000 BC the glaciers disappeared from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swiss_plateau" title="Swiss plateau">Swiss plateau</a> and recolonization is likely at that time.</p>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> settlement discovered in the canton is in the Wauwilermoos, which is now a Swiss <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swiss_inventory_of_cultural_property_of_national_and_regional_significance" title="Swiss inventory of cultural property of national and regional significance">heritage site of national significance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A number of other settlements have since been found, mainly on sandy, dry elevations in the immediate vicinity of water. The settlements of <a href="/wiki/Wauwilermoos_pile_dwelling_settlement_(Egolzwil_3)" title="Wauwilermoos pile dwelling settlement (Egolzwil 3)">Egolzwil 3</a> in Wauwilermoos in <a href="/wiki/Egolzwil" title="Egolzwil">Egolzwil</a>, Seematte at <a href="/wiki/Hitzkirch" title="Hitzkirch">Hitzkirch</a> and Halbinsel in <a href="/wiki/Sursee" title="Sursee">Sursee</a> are part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Pile_dwellings_around_the_Alps" title="Prehistoric Pile dwellings around the Alps">Prehistoric Pile dwellings around the Alps</a> a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNESCO_World_Heritage_Site" title="UNESCO World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNESCO_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Wauwilermoos houses had wooden or bark floors and hearths of clay. The villages had ceramic vessels and wood, bone, antler, stone and flint tools as well as textiles. Copper ax blades and knives provide the first evidence of metal use in Switzerland. Imported mollusks show that there were trade connections to the Mediterranean. The bones at Egolzwil 3 are over two thirds from domestic animals with the remainder from wild animals. The main domesticated animals were sheep, goats and pigs with only a few domestic cattle. The animals hunted included deer, roe deer, wild boar and elk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-HDS-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> the canton was quite settled. There were a number of settlements on the shores of Lake Sempach and Lake Baldegg along with hilltop settlements, graves and scattered items throughout the area. At Hochdorf-Baldegg a fenced village from the early Bronze Age (2200–1500 BC) was uncovered. The single-story houses all had clay or stone hearths. During the Middle Bronze Age (1550–1350 BC) most of the villages were not located directly on the lake shores. The Late Bronze Age settlement at Sursee-Zellmoos on Lake Sempach featured houses arranged in rows with mortared stone. The walls were timber lined with clay. Another Late Bronze Age settlement near the village of Schötz was densely populated between 1350 and 800 BC.</p>
<p>While numerous individual <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> items have been found, almost no settlements have been discovered. From the <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_culture" title="Hallstatt culture">Hallstatt period</a> (800–480 BC) mainly graves have been discovered. Very little is known about the <a href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne_culture" title="La Tène culture">La Tène period</a> (480–30 BC) in Lucerne. Some iron tools, gold coins, ceramic vessels and a glass bangle as well as a burial ground with at least four graves have been found.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Switzerland_in_the_Roman_era" title="Switzerland in the Roman era">Roman era</a>, the canton was once again thickly settled. A number of farms were built in the north-south running valleys (<a href="/wiki/Wigger_(river)" title="Wigger (river)">Wigger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suhre" title="Suhre">Suhre</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wynental" title="Wynental">Wyna</a> and See valleys). During the 1st century AD, the farms provided food for the <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">Legion</a> camp in <a href="/wiki/Vindonissa" title="Vindonissa">Vindonissa</a> and for the larger settlements located in the Swiss plateau.</p>
<p>Towards the end of the 1st century there was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vicus_(Rome)" title="Vicus (Rome)">vicus</a> at <a href="/wiki/Sursee" title="Sursee">Sursee</a>. The remains of houses show that there were a number of small shops and manufacturing buildings in the town. The west bank of the Suhre was fortified with a stone slip and may have served as a ship or raft berth. Imports from various regions of the Romans Empire (southern Spain, southern Italy, Lipari, southern and eastern Gaul and the Germanic areas) provide evidence of extensive trade relations. Sursee was likely a freight hub for trade with the entire Alpine region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-HDS-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_middle_ages">Early middle ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early middle ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Picswiss_LU-20-40.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="333" data-file-width="499" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Picswiss_LU-20-40.jpg/220px-Picswiss_LU-20-40.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Picswiss_LU-20-40.jpg/330px-Picswiss_LU-20-40.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Picswiss_LU-20-40.jpg/440px-Picswiss_LU-20-40.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Following the collapse of the Roman Empire, the canton of Lucerne was settled by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alamanni" title="Alamanni">Alamanni</a>, who generally settled away from the Roman settlements. Some exceptions include a Roman manor at <a href="/wiki/B%C3%BCron" title="Büron">Büron</a> and the Roman town of Sursee. An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alamannic_graves#Alemannic_grave_fields" title="Alamannic graves">Alamannic grave field</a> was found at <a href="/wiki/Aesch,_Lucerne" title="Aesch, Lucerne">Aesch</a> with 61 graves with wooden coffins from before the 7th century before. In the women's graves there were necklaces with glass and amber beads, while swords were found in the men's graves. Belt buckles and small iron knives were found in the graves of both sexes. A few items from the southern Alpine region, southern Germany and Burgundy indicate that trade continued.</p>
<p>Two early medieval stone grave vaults, both of which were used for multiple burials, were found in the church of <a href="/wiki/Altishofen" title="Altishofen">Altishofen</a>. In the treasury of <a href="/wiki/Berom%C3%BCnster" title="Beromünster">Beromünster</a> Abbey there is a 7th-century ornate <a href="/wiki/Reliquary" title="Reliquary">reliquary</a> of gilded copper plates, which probably came from northern Italy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDS_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-HDS-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation_of_Lucerne_city">Foundation of Lucerne city</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Foundation of Lucerne city">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lucerne grew up around a Benedictine monastery, founded about 750 on the right bank of the Reuss by <a href="/wiki/Murbach_Abbey" title="Murbach Abbey">Murbach Abbey</a> in Alsace, of which it long remained a "cell". It is first mentioned in a charter of 840 under the name of <i>Luciaria</i>, which is probably derived from the patron saint of the monastery, St <a href="/wiki/Leodegar" title="Leodegar">Leodegar</a>. The name <i>Lucerrun</i> is first mentioned in 1252. At some point, a small village grew up around the monastery. The first signs of a municipal constitution appear in 1252.</p>
<p>With the growing power of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburg" title="Habsburg">Habsburgs</a> in the area the ties that bound Lucerne to Murbach weakened. In 1291 the Habsburgs finally purchased Lucerne from Murbach. The purchase of Lucerne by the Habsburgs drove the three forest cantons (<a href="/wiki/Canton_of_Uri" title="Canton of Uri">Uri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canton_of_Schwyz" title="Canton of Schwyz">Schwyz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unterwalden" title="Unterwalden">Unterwalden</a>) to form an <a href="/wiki/Federal_Charter_of_1291" title="Federal Charter of 1291">Eternal Alliance</a>, an act that is considered to be the foundation of Switzerland. In 1332 Lucerne became the fourth member of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eidgenossen" title="Eidgenossen">Eidgenossen</a></i> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Swiss_Confederation" title="Old Swiss Confederation">Swiss Confederation</a>, and the first town to join the rural forest cantons.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sempach" title="Battle of Sempach">Battle of Sempach</a> (1386) near Sempach (which while allied wouldn't become part of the canton for 8 more years) drove the Habsburgs out of the region and strengthened the Confederation. That victory led also to the gradual acquisition of territory ruled by and from the town.</p>
<p>Amstalden, a wealthy innkeeper <a href="/wiki/Sch%C3%BCpfheim" title="Schüpfheim">Schüpfheim</a> in the Entlebuch valley of the canton of Lucerne, was the leader of a planned rebellion of the valley against the city of Lucerne. In a plot to limit the power of the city, officials from the neighbouring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canton_of_Obwalden" title="Canton of Obwalden">canton of Obwalden</a> had promised their support. The conspiracy was detected before they could stage the planned coup. On 24 August 1478, Amstalden was arrested, tortured and interrogated, and finally beheaded in November 1478.</p>
<p>The incident furthered the distrust amongst rural and urban cantons in the <a href="/wiki/Old_Swiss_Confederacy" title="Old Swiss Confederacy">Old Swiss Confederacy</a> and was one of the reasons for the conclusion of the <a href="/wiki/Stanser_Verkommnis" title="Stanser Verkommnis">Stanser Verkommnis</a> (Treaty of <a href="/wiki/Stans" title="Stans">Stans</a>) in 1481, an important coalition treaty of the cantons of the Old Swiss Confederacy</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a> Lucerne remained attached to Roman Catholicism, of which ever since it has been the great stronghold in Switzerland. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papal_nuncio" title="Papal nuncio">papal nuncio</a> resided here from 1601 to 1873. In the 16th century, during the <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_Switzerland" title="Early Modern Switzerland">early modern age</a>, the town government fell into the hands of an aristocratic <a href="/wiki/Oligarchy" title="Oligarchy">oligarchy</a>, whose power, though shaken by the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_peasant_war_of_1653" title="Swiss peasant war of 1653">Swiss peasant war of 1653</a> in the Entlebuch, lasted till 1798. Under the <a href="/wiki/Campaigns_of_1798_in_the_French_Revolutionary_Wars#Other_campaigns" title="Campaigns of 1798 in the French Revolutionary Wars">French</a>-supported <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helvetic_republic" title="Helvetic republic">Helvetic republic</a> (1798–1803) Lucerne was the seat of the central government. When the republic collapsed, under the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Mediation" title="Act of Mediation">Act of Mediation</a> (1803) it was one of the six "<a href="/wiki/Directorial_system" title="Directorial system">Directorial</a>" cantons and during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Restauration_(Switzerland)" title="Restauration (Switzerland)">Restoration</a> (from 1815 to 1848) it was one of the three ruling cantons.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Patrician_(post-Roman_Europe)" title="Patrician (post-Roman Europe)">patrician</a> government was swept away by the cantonal constitution of 1831. But in 1841 the Conservatives regained power. They quickly recalled the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit" title="Jesuit">Jesuits</a>, who had been expelled by earlier radical governments, to head the cantonal school system. The riots that followed brought about the <a href="/wiki/Sonderbund_War" title="Sonderbund War">Sonderbund War</a> (1847) in which the Conservatives were defeated, the decisive battle taking place at Gisikon, not far from Lucerne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_city_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB_city-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The canton of Lucerne is located in the centre of Switzerland. It is drained by the <a href="/wiki/Reuss_(river)" title="Reuss (river)">Reuss</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kleine_Emme" title="Kleine Emme">Kleine Emme</a>. The lands of the canton lie on the northern foothills of the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Alps" title="Swiss Alps">Swiss Alps</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urner_Alps" title="Urner Alps">Urner Alps</a>). The highest elevation of the canton of Lucerne is at the <i>Brienzer Rothorn</i> at 2,350 m (7,710 ft).</p>
<p>The area of the canton is 1,493 km<sup>2</sup> (576 sq mi). 817.7 km<sup>2</sup> (315.7 sq mi), about 55% of the total land area, is used for agriculture. An additional 449.0 km<sup>2</sup> (173.4 sq mi) (about 30%) of the canton is wooded. The remainder of the canton is either developed, 125.3 km<sup>2</sup> (48.4 sq mi) (8.4%), or unproductive (lakes, rivers or mountains), 101.5 km<sup>2</sup> (39.2 sq mi) (6.8%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cantonal_politics_and_government">Cantonal politics and government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cantonal politics and government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Constitution of Lucerne of 2007 entered into force on 1 January 2008, replacing the 1875 constitution.</p>
<p>With the new constitution, the legislative body <i>Grosser Rat</i> (Grand Council) was renamed to <i>Kantonsrat</i> (Cantonal Council). It is composed of 120 members elected by proportional representation.</p>
<p>The executive body is the <i>Regierungsrat</i> (Government Council), composed of 5 members.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_People%27s_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Christian Democratic People's Party of Switzerland">Christian Democratic People's Party</a> (CVP) is traditionally the largest party, having won 38 seats in the Cantonal Council as of the 2015 elections, and having two out of five members in the Government Council as of 2016.</p>

<p>Lucerne has a population (as of December 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>) of 398,762.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> As of 2013<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, 18.1% of the population are resident foreign nationals, of which 15.5% are from <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, 1.4% from <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, 0.7% from <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, and 0.5% from <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lustat2013_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lustat2013-13">[13]</a></sup> Over the last 10 years (2000–2010) the population has changed at a rate of −0.4%. Migration accounted for −1.2%, while births and deaths accounted for 1.3%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFSO_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the population (as of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>) speaks <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> (91%) as their first language, while <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">Serbo-Croatian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_Language" title="Italian Language">Italian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">Albanian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Language" title="English Language">English</a> are the second most common languages (3%), followed by <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> with 2% each, and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> with 1%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lustat2010Lang_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lustat2010Lang-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of the population in the canton, 115,233 or about 32.9% were born in Lucerne and lived there in 2000. There were 101,980 or 29.1% who were born in the same canton, while 66,486 or 19.0% were born somewhere else in Switzerland, and 55,314 or 15.8% were born outside of Switzerland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAT2000_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-STAT2000-17">[17]</a></sup> As of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, children and teenagers (0–19 years old) make up 25% of the population, while adults (20–64 years old) make up 58.6% and seniors (over 64 years old) make up 16.4%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFSO_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 158,345 people who were single and never married in the canton. There were 159,152 married individuals, 18,853 widows or widowers and 14,154 individuals who are divorced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAT2000_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-STAT2000-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 13,430 private households in the canton, and an average of 2.5 persons per household.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFSO_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-15">[15]</a></sup> There were 47,012 households that consist of only one person and 12,952 households with five or more people. As of 2009<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the construction rate of new housing units was 4.7 new units per 1000 residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFSO_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2003<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the average price to rent an average apartment in Lucerne city was 1150.31 <a href="/wiki/Swiss_franc" title="Swiss franc">Swiss francs</a> (CHF) per month (US$920, £520, €740 approx. exchange rate from 2003). The average rate for a one-room apartment was 646.07 CHF (US$520, £290, €410), a two-room apartment was about 879.64 CHF (US$700, £400, €560), a three-room apartment was about 1033.60 CHF (US$830, £470, €660) and a six or more room apartment cost an average of 2032.38 CHF (US$1630, £910, €1300). The average apartment price in Lucerne was 103.1% of the national average of 1116 CHF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BFS_rent_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-BFS_rent-18">[18]</a></sup> The vacancy rate for the canton, in 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, was 0.77%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFSO_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The largest population centres are <a href="/wiki/Lucerne" title="Lucerne">Lucerne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emmen,_Switzerland" title="Emmen, Switzerland">Emmen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kriens" title="Kriens">Kriens</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Central_Switzerland.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="682" data-file-width="1022" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Central_Switzerland.jpg/220px-Central_Switzerland.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Central_Switzerland.jpg/330px-Central_Switzerland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Central_Switzerland.jpg/440px-Central_Switzerland.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Central_Switzerland.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Lucerne area and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Lucerne" title="Lake Lucerne">Lake Lucerne</a> from <a href="/wiki/Pilatus_(mountain)" title="Pilatus (mountain)">Pilatus</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Much of the land in the canton is used for agriculture. Agriculture is the most significant source of income, but industry is also developed. The most important agricultural products are <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">crops</a>, fruit and cattle breeding. Industry concentrates on textiles, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machinery" title="Machinery">machinery</a>, paper, wood, tobacco and metallurgical goods.</p>
<p>Tourism is of great importance. The canton of Lucerne is a gateway to holiday resorts in the nearby Alps, and much of the transit traffic between Germany and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> crosses the region.</p>
<p>As of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Luzern had an unemployment rate of 1.4%. As of 2008<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 1,764 people employed in the <a href="/wiki/Primary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Primary sector of the economy">primary economic sector</a> and about 703 businesses involved in this sector. 5,388 people were employed in the <a href="/wiki/Secondary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Secondary sector of the economy">secondary sector</a> and there were 324 businesses in this sector. 9,431 people were employed in the <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Tertiary sector of the economy">tertiary sector</a>, with 1,113 businesses in this sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFSO_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the total number of <a href="/wiki/Full-time_equivalent" title="Full-time equivalent">full-time equivalent</a> jobs was 160,133. The number of jobs in the primary sector was 9,608, of which 9,462 were in agriculture, 119 were in forestry or lumber production and 27 were in fishing or fisheries. The number of jobs in the secondary sector was 48,191 of which 31,698 or (65.8%) were in manufacturing, 142 or (0.3%) were in mining and 14,990 (31.1%) were in construction. The number of jobs in the tertiary sector was 102,334. In the tertiary sector; 25,105 or 24.5% were in the sale or repair of motor vehicles, 8,182 or 8.0% were in the movement and storage of goods, 8,146 or 8.0% were in a hotel or restaurant, 3,835 or 3.7% were in the information industry, 7,000 or 6.8% were the insurance or financial industry, 10,979 or 10.7% were technical professionals or scientists, 8,111 or 7.9% were in education and 15,924 or 15.6% were in health care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of the working population, 12.1% used public transportation to get to work, and 48.5% used a private car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFSO_15-6"><a href="#cite_note-SFSO-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From the 2000 census<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, 248,545 or 70.9% were Roman Catholic, while 39,426 or 11.2% belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Reformed_Church" title="Swiss Reformed Church">Swiss Reformed Church</a>. Of the rest of the population, there were 7,801 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Christianity" title="Orthodox Christianity">members of an Orthodox church</a> (or about 2.23% of the population), there were 471 individuals (or about 0.13% of the population) who belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Catholic_Church_of_Switzerland" title="Christian Catholic Church of Switzerland">Christian Catholic Church</a>, and there were 7,564 individuals (or about 2.16% of the population) who belonged to another Christian church. There were 399 individuals (or about 0.11% of the population) who were Jewish, and 13,227 (or about 3.77% of the population) who were Islamic. There were 875 individuals who were <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist</a>, 1,715 individuals who were <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hindu</a> and 293 individuals who belonged to another church. 20,681 (or about 5.90% of the population) belonged to no church, are agnostic or <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheist</a>, and 13,007 individuals (or about 3.71% of the population) did not answer the question.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAT2000_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-STAT2000-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canton_of_Lucerne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luzern" title="Luzern">Luzern</a> about 127,331 or (36.3%) of the population have completed non-mandatory <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Switzerland#Secondary" title="Education in Switzerland">upper secondary education</a>, and 42,391 or (12.1%) have completed additional <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">higher education</a> (either <a href="/wiki/List_of_universities_in_Switzerland" title="List of universities in Switzerland">universities</a> or a <i><a href="/wiki/Fachhochschule" title="Fachhochschule">Fachhochschule</a></i>). Of the 42,391 who completed tertiary schooling, 65.0% were Swiss men, 23.6% were Swiss women, 7.0% were non-Swiss men and 4.4% were non-Swiss women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAT2000_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-STAT2000-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Institutions of <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">higher education</a> include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucerne_University_of_Applied_Sciences" title="Lucerne University of Applied Sciences">Lucerne University of Applied Sciences</a> which also has campuses in <a href="/wiki/Lucerne" title="Lucerne">Lucerne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Horw" title="Horw">Horw</a>.</p>
