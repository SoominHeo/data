<p><b>Lucia of Syracuse</b> (283–304), also known as <b>Saint Lucy</b>, or <b>Saint Lucia</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Sancta Lucia</i></span>), was a young <a href="/wiki/Christian_martyrs" title="Christian martyrs">Christian martyr</a> who died during the <a href="/wiki/Diocletianic_Persecution" title="Diocletianic Persecution">Diocletianic Persecution</a>. She is venerated as a <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican" title="Anglican">Anglican</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy" title="Eastern Orthodoxy">Orthodox</a> Churches. She is one of eight women, who along with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veneration_of_Mary_in_Roman_Catholicism" title="Veneration of Mary in Roman Catholicism">Blessed Virgin Mary</a>, are commemorated by name in the <a href="/wiki/Canon_of_the_Mass" title="Canon of the Mass">Canon of the Mass</a>. Her feast day, known as <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucy%27s_Day" title="Saint Lucy's Day">Saint Lucy's Day</a>, is celebrated in the West on 13 December. St. Lucia of Syracuse was honored in the Middle Ages and remained a well-known saint in early modern England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Lucy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All that is really known for certain of Lucy is that she was a martyr in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syracuse,_Italy" title="Syracuse, Italy">Syracuse</a> during the Diocletianic Persecution of 304 AD. Her veneration spread to Rome, and by the 6th century to the whole Church. The oldest archaeological evidence comes from the Greek inscriptions from the catacombs of St. John in Syracuse.</p>
<p>The oldest record of her story comes from the fifth-century <i>Acts</i>. <a href="/wiki/Jacobus_de_Voragine" title="Jacobus de Voragine">Jacobus de Voragine</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Golden_Legend" title="Golden Legend">Legenda Aurea</a></i> was the most widely read version of the Lucy legend in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. In medieval accounts, Saint Lucy's eyes are gouged out prior to her execution.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Lucy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All the details of her life are the conventional ones associated with female martyrs of the early 4th century. John Henry Blunt views her story as a Christian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_(heroic_literature)" title="Romance (heroic literature)">romance</a> similar to the Acts of other virgin martyrs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blunt_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-blunt-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the traditional story, Lucy was born of rich and noble parents about the year 283. Her father was of Roman origin, but died when she was five years old,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scranton_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-scranton-5">[5]</a></sup> leaving Lucy and her mother without a protective guardian. Her mother's name Eutychia, seems to indicate that she came of Greek stock.</p>
<p>Like many of the early martyrs, Lucy had consecrated her virginity to God, and she hoped to distribute her dowry to the poor. However, Eutychia, not knowing of Lucy's promise and suffering from a bleeding disorder feared for Lucy's future. She arranged Lucy's marriage to a young man of a wealthy pagan family.</p>

<p>Saint <a href="/wiki/Agatha_of_Sicily" title="Agatha of Sicily">Agatha</a> had been martyred fifty-two years before during the Decian persecution. Her shrine at Catania, less than fifty miles from Syracuse attracted a number of pilgrims; many miracles were reported to have happened through her intercession. Eutychia was persuaded to make a pilgrimage to <a href="/wiki/Catania" title="Catania">Catania</a>, in hopes of a cure. While there, St. Agatha came to Lucy in a dream and told her that because of her faith her mother would be cured and that Lucy would be the glory of Syracuse, as she was of Catania. With her mother cured, Lucy took the opportunity to persuade her mother to allow her to distribute a great part of her riches among the poor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bridge_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bridge-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eutychia suggested that the sums would make a good bequest, but Lucy countered, "...whatever you give away at death for the Lord's sake you give because you cannot take it with you. Give now to the true Savior, while you are healthy, whatever you intended to give away at your death."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skeat_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-skeat-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>News that the patrimony and jewels were being distributed came to Lucy's betrothed, who denounced her to Paschasius, the Governor of Syracuse. Paschasius ordered her to burn a sacrifice to the emperor's image. When she refused Paschasius sentenced her to be defiled in a brothel. The Christian tradition states that when the guards came to take her away, they could not move her even when they hitched her to a team of oxen. Bundles of wood were then heaped about her and set on fire, but would not burn. Finally, she met her death by the sword.</p>

<p>Absent in the early narratives and traditions, at least until the 15th century, is the story of Lucia tortured by eye-gouging. According to later accounts, before she died she foretold the punishment of Paschasius and the speedy end of the persecution, adding that Diocletian would reign no more, and Maximian would meet his end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bridge_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-bridge-1">[1]</a></sup> This so angered Paschasius that he ordered the guards to remove her eyes. Another version has Lucy taking her own eyes out in order to discourage a persistent suitor who admired them. When her body was prepared for burial in the family mausoleum it was discovered that her eyes had been miraculously restored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scranton_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-scranton-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Veneration">Veneration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Lucy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Veneration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>By the 6th century, her story was sufficiently widespread that she appears in the Sacramentary of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_I" title="Pope Gregory I">Pope Gregory I</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blunt_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-blunt-4">[4]</a></sup> She is also commemorated in the ancient Roman Martyrology. St. Aldhelm (English, died in 709) and later the Venerable Bede (English, died in 735) attest that her popularity had already spread to England, where her festival was kept in England until the Protestant Reformation, as a holy day of the second rank, in which no work but tillage or the like was allowed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scranton_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-scranton-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sigebert_of_Gembloux" title="Sigebert of Gembloux">Sigebert of Gembloux</a> wrote a mid-eleventh century <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passio" title="Passio">passio</a></i>, to support a local cult of Lucy at <a href="/wiki/Metz" title="Metz">Metz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/General_Roman_Calendar" title="General Roman Calendar">General Roman Calendar</a> formerly had a commemoration of Saints Lucy and Geminianus on 16 September. This was removed in 1969, as a duplication of the feast of her <i><a href="/wiki/Calendar_of_saints" title="Calendar of saints">dies natalis</a></i> on 13 December and because the Geminianus in question, mentioned in the <i>Passio</i> of Saint Lucy, seems to be a fictitious figure,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CR_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-CR-2">[2]</a></sup> unrelated to the <a href="/wiki/Geminianus" title="Geminianus">Geminianus</a> whose feast is on 31 January.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relics">Relics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Lucy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Relics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sigebert_of_Gembloux" title="Sigebert of Gembloux">Sigebert</a> (1030–1112), a monk of <a href="/wiki/Gembloux" title="Gembloux">Gembloux</a>, in his <i>sermo de Sancta Lucia</i>, chronicled that her body lay undisturbed in Sicily for 400 years, before <a href="/wiki/Faroald_II_of_Spoleto" title="Faroald II of Spoleto">Faroald II, Duke of Spoleto</a>, captured the island and transferred the body to <a href="/wiki/Corfinium" title="Corfinium">Corfinium</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Abruzzo" title="Abruzzo">Abruzzo</a>, Italy. From there it was removed by the <a href="/wiki/Otto_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto I, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Otho I</a> in 972 to <a href="/wiki/Metz" title="Metz">Metz</a> and deposited in the church of St. Vincent. It was from this shrine that an arm of the saint was taken to the monastery of Luitburg in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Speyer" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Speyer">Diocese of Speyer</a> – an incident celebrated by Sigebert in verse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bridge_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-bridge-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The subsequent history of the relics is not clear. According to Umberto Benigni, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Stephen_II" title="Pope Stephen II">Stephen II</a> (768) sent the relics of St. Lucy to Constantinople for safety against the Saracen incursions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> On their capture of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> in 1204, the French found some relics attributed to Saint Lucy in the city, and <a href="/wiki/Enrico_Dandolo" title="Enrico Dandolo">Enrico Dandolo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doge_of_Venice" title="Doge of Venice">Doge of Venice</a>, secured them for the monastery of St. George at <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>. In 1513 the Venetians presented to <a href="/wiki/Louis_XII_of_France" title="Louis XII of France">Louis XII of France</a> the saint's head, which he deposited in the cathedral church of <a href="/wiki/Bourges" title="Bourges">Bourges</a>. Another account, however, states that the head was brought to Bourges from Rome, where it had been transferred during the time when the relics rested in Corfinium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lucia_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-lucia-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The remainder of the relics remain in Venice: they were transferred to the church of <a href="/wiki/San_Geremia" title="San Geremia">San Geremia</a> when the church of Santa Lucia was demolished in 1861 to make way for the new railway terminus. A century later, on 7 November 1981, thieves stole all her bones, except her head. Police recovered them five weeks later, on her feast day. Other parts of the corpse have found their way to Rome, Naples, Verona, Lisbon, Milan, as well as Germany, France and Sweden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lucia_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-lucia-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patronage">Patronage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Lucy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Patronage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lucy's Latin name <i>Lucia</i> shares a root (<i>luc-</i>) with the Latin word for light, <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lux#Latin" title="wikt:lux">lux</a></i>. This has played a large part of Saint Lucy being named as the patron saint of the blind and those with eye-trouble.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stlucychurch_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-stlucychurch-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>She is also the patroness of Syracuse in Sicily, Italy. At the Piazza Duomo in Syracuse, the church of Santa Lucia alla Badia houses the painting "<a href="/wiki/Burial_of_St._Lucy_(Caravaggio)" title="Burial of St. Lucy (Caravaggio)">Burial of St. Lucy (Caravaggio)</a>".</p>
<p>Saint Lucy is also the patron saint of the Caribbean island of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucia" title="Saint Lucia">Saint Lucia</a>, one of the Windward Islands in the Lesser Antilles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Iconography">Iconography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Lucy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Iconography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The emblem of eyes on a cup or plate apparently reflects popular devotion to her as protector of sight, because of her name, Lucia (from the Latin word "lux" which means "light").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In paintings St. Lucy is frequently shown holding her eyes on a golden plate. Lucy was represented in Gothic art holding a dish with two eyes on it. She also holds the palm branch, symbol of victory over evil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scranton_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-scranton-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature">In literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Lucy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dante" title="Dante">Dante</a> also mentions Lucia in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Divine_Comedy#Inferno" title="The Divine Comedy">Inferno</a></i> Canto II as the messenger "of all cruelty the foe" sent to <a href="/wiki/Beatrice_Portinari" title="Beatrice Portinari">Beatrice</a> from "The blessed Dame" (Divine Mercy), to rouse Beatrice to send Virgil to Dante's aid. She has instructed Virgil to guide Dante through Hell and Purgatory. Lucia is only referred to indirectly in Virgil's discourse within the narrative and does not appear. According to Robert Harrison, Professor in Italian Literature at Stanford University, and Rachel Jacoff, Professor of Italian Studies at Wellesley, Lucia's appearance in this intermediary role is to reinforce the scene in which Virgil tries to fortify Dante's courage to begin the journey through the Inferno.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stlucychurch_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-stlucychurch-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Purgatorio" title="Purgatorio">Purgatorio</a></i> IX:52–63, Lucy carries the sleeping Dante to the entrance to Purgatory. Then in <i><a href="/wiki/Paradiso_(Dante)" title="Paradiso (Dante)">Paradiso</a></i> XXXII Dante places her opposite Adam within the Mystic Rose in Canto XXXII of the Paradiso. Lucy may also be seen as a figure of Illuminating Grace or Mercy or even Justice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dc_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-dc-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lucia is also the protagonist of a Swedish novel: "Ett ljus i mörkret" ("A light in the darkness") by Agneta Sjödin.</p>
<p>Her feast day was commonly described as the shortest day of the year, as it is in <a href="/wiki/John_Donne" title="John Donne">John Donne</a>'s poem, "A Nocturnal upon St. Lucie's Day, being the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shortest_day" title="Shortest day">shortest day</a>" (1627). The poem begins with: "'Tis the year's midnight, and it is the day's".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stlucychurch_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-stlucychurch-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In JM Coetzee's Booker Award winning novel <i><a href="/wiki/Disgrace" title="Disgrace">Disgrace</a></i>, Lucy Lurie plays a central role as the protagonist's daughter.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_celebration">Popular celebration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Lucy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Popular celebration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucy%27s_Day" title="Saint Lucy's Day">Saint Lucy's Day</a></div>
<p>Lucy's feast is on 13 December, in <a href="/wiki/Advent" title="Advent">Advent</a>. Her feast once coincided with the Winter Solstice, the shortest day of the year, before calendar reforms, so her feastday has become a festival of light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scranton_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-scranton-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>This is particularly seen in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> countries, with their long dark winters. There, a young girl dressed in a white dress and a red sash (as the symbol of martyrdom) carries palms and wears a crown or wreath of candles on her head. In both Norway and Sweden, girls dressed as Lucy carry rolls and cookies in procession as songs are sung. It is said that to vividly celebrate St. Lucy's Day will help one live the long winter days with enough light.</p>
<p>A special devotion to St. Lucy is practiced in the Italian regions of <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">Emilia-Romagna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Veneto" title="Veneto">Veneto</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friuli_Venezia_Giulia" title="Friuli Venezia Giulia">Friuli Venezia Giulia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trentino-Alto_Adige/S%C3%BCdtirol" title="Trentino-Alto Adige/Südtirol">Trentino-Alto Adige</a>, in the North of the country, and <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">Calabria</a>, in the South, as well as in Croatian coastal region of <a href="/wiki/Dalmatia" title="Dalmatia">Dalmatia</a>. The feast is a Catholic-celebrated holiday with roots that can be traced to Sicily. On 13th of every December it is celebrated with large traditional feasts of home made pasta and various other Italian dishes, with a special dessert of wheat in hot chocolate milk. The large grains of soft wheat are representative of her eyes and are a treat only to be indulged in once a year.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Omaha,_Nebraska" title="Omaha, Nebraska">Omaha, Nebraska</a>, the Santa Lucia Festival is celebrated each summer. Founded in 1925 by the Italian immigrant Grazia Buonafede Caniglia, it continues to this day. Members of the ethnic Italian community process with a statue of Saint Lucy through the streets of downtown Omaha, carrying also a first-class relic of Saint Lucy.</p>
<p>A Hungarian custom is to plant wheat in a small pot on St. Lucy's feast. By Christmas green sprouts appear, signs of life coming from death. The wheat is then carried to the manger scene as the symbol of Christ in the Eucharist.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, villagers from <a href="/wiki/Barangay" title="Barangay">Barangay</a> Sta. Lucia in <a href="/wiki/Magarao,_Camarines_Sur" title="Magarao, Camarines Sur">Magarao</a>, Camarines Sur, hold a <a href="/wiki/Novena" title="Novena">novena</a> to St. Lucy nine days before her feast. A <a href="/wiki/Procession" title="Procession">procession</a> of the saint's image is held every morning at the <a href="/wiki/Poblacion" title="Poblacion">poblacion</a> or village centre during the nine days leading up to St. Lucy's Day, attracting devotees from other parts of the <a href="/wiki/Bicol_Region" title="Bicol Region">Bicol Region</a>. Hymns to the saint, known as the <i>Gozos</i>, as well as the Spanish version of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ave_Maria" title="Ave Maria">Ave Maria</a> are chanted during the dawn procession, which is followed by a <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">Mass</a>.</p>
