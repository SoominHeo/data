<p><b>Lucas Oil Stadium</b> is a multi-purpose stadium in downtown <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis" title="Indianapolis">Indianapolis</a>, United States. It replaced the <a href="/wiki/RCA_Dome" title="RCA Dome">RCA Dome</a> as the home field of the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">NFL</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis_Colts" title="Indianapolis Colts">Indianapolis Colts</a>. The stadium also hosted <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XLVI" title="Super Bowl XLVI">Super Bowl XLVI</a> in 2012. The stadium had its ribbon-cutting ceremony August 16, 2008, and then officially opened to the public eight days later on August 24, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wishtv.com_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-wishtv.com-12">[12]</a></sup> The stadium was constructed to allow the removal of the RCA Dome and expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Convention_Center" title="Indiana Convention Center">Indiana Convention Center</a> on its site. The stadium is on the south side of South Street, the block south of the site of the former RCA Dome. The stadium is often referred to as "The House That Manning Built", even though <a href="/wiki/Peyton_Manning" title="Peyton Manning">Peyton Manning</a> no longer plays for the <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis_Colts" title="Indianapolis Colts">Indianapolis Colts</a>.</p>
<p>In addition to serving as a football stadium, Lucas Oil Stadium is also the well-known site for two famous music competitions in the United States: the <a href="/wiki/Bands_of_America" title="Bands of America">Bands of America</a> Grand National Championships, which feature some of the highest achieving high school marching bands, and the <a href="/wiki/Drum_Corps_International" title="Drum Corps International">Drum Corps International</a> Championships. Both organizations serve corps style bands. On February 28, 2006, prior to the construction of the stadium, <a href="/wiki/Forrest_Lucas" title="Forrest Lucas">Forrest Lucas</a> announced that <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Oil" title="Lucas Oil">Lucas Oil</a> had purchased the <a href="/wiki/Naming_rights" title="Naming rights">naming rights</a> for $121 million over 20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rights_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-rights-13">[13]</a></sup> The architectural firm <a href="/wiki/HKS,_Inc." title="HKS, Inc.">HKS, Inc.</a> was responsible for the stadium's design, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walter_P_Moore_Engineers_and_Consultants" title="Walter P Moore Engineers and Consultants">Walter P Moore</a> working as the Structural Engineer of Record. The stadium features a retractable roof and window wall, thus allowing the Colts to play both indoors and outdoors. The implementation of these elements of <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_architecture" title="Kinetic architecture">kinetic architecture</a> provides for quick conversion of the facility to accommodate a variety of events. The field surface is <a href="/wiki/FieldTurf" title="FieldTurf">FieldTurf</a>.</p>
<p>The exterior of the new stadium is faced with a reddish-brown <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">brick</a> trimmed with <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Limestone" title="Indiana Limestone">Indiana Limestone</a>, similar to several other sports venues in the area such as <a href="/wiki/Bankers_Life_Fieldhouse" title="Bankers Life Fieldhouse">Bankers Life Fieldhouse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hinkle_Fieldhouse" title="Hinkle Fieldhouse">Hinkle Fieldhouse</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairgrounds_Coliseum" title="Fairgrounds Coliseum">Fairgrounds Coliseum</a>. The architectural style complements other older structures in the downtown area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turf_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-turf-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucas_Oil_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Seating capacity for football games is 62,421; an increase of more than 5,000 over the RCA Dome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-facts_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-facts-4">[4]</a></sup> For football, the stadium can be expanded to a capacity of 70,000 for large events, such as the <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl" title="Super Bowl">Super Bowl</a>. The stadium's basketball configuration can exceed the 70,000 minimum <a href="/wiki/Seating_capacity" title="Seating capacity">seating capacity</a> required to host the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NCAA_Men%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Championship" title="NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Championship">NCAA Final Four</a>.</p>
<p>Lucas Oil Stadium offers 137 luxury suites, including 8 field suites, and 12 super suites. In addition, it contains the Quarterback Suite, a semi-private 200-seat party room.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turf_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-turf-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The stadium contains two massive high definition scoreboards, each one 97 feet (30 m) wide and 53 feet (16 m) tall, which are situated in the northwest and southeast corners of the stadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanized_retractable_roof">Mechanized retractable roof</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucas_Oil_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mechanized retractable roof">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lucas Oil Stadium has a retractable roof designed by <a href="/wiki/Uni-Systems" title="Uni-Systems">Uni-Systems</a> that, like the <a href="/wiki/Veltins-Arena" title="Veltins-Arena">Veltins-Arena</a>, divides lengthwise into two retractable panels, with each half sliding down the sloping roof of the stadium into the open position. The stadium roof is gabled, with the peak in the center of the field, paralleling the sidelines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uni_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-uni-15">[15]</a></sup> A cable drum drive system drives the retractable roof panels up and down the sloped track. Rather than dragging the 1½" diameter galvanized cables across the fixed roof, this system's patented design lays the roof cable down, and then picks it back up. In nine minutes, the roof panels will simultaneously move to the open position at the touch of a button. To guard the stadium's interior from weather conditions the roof is designed with a large cap that will run the length of a sealed overlap between the parting roof panels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-facts_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-facts-4">[4]</a></sup> Just beneath the sealed overlap is a large trough, finalizing the retractable roof's layers of protection. It is the only retractable roof in the country with two moving panels that will meet in a peak above the center of the stadium. The roof boasts the largest opening—a 4.5-acre (1.8 ha) hole to the sky—of all current and planned NFL stadiums with retractable roofs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Lucas Oil Stadium retractable roof system is operated by 32 cables, each 1½" in diameter, with galvanized right and left hand lay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uni_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-uni-15">[15]</a></sup> They were manufactured specifically for this project by Wire Rope Corporation of America and furnished by The Tway Company Inc. located in Indianapolis. The lengths vary from 232’6" to 245’ and include a Johnson Wedge Socket installed on one end that terminates the cables at the roof peak 288’ above the stadium floor.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="NFL_rules_for_roof_opening">NFL rules for roof opening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucas_Oil_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: NFL rules for roof opening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The home team determines if the roof is to be opened or closed 90 minutes before kickoff. The roof remains open unless <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> is within the vicinity of the stadium, the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> drops below 40 °F (4 °C), or <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">wind gusts</a> are greater than 40 miles per hour (64 km/h), in which case the roof operators will close the roof. Once the roof is closed, it is not allowed to be reopened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-facts_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-facts-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Movable_window_wall">Movable window wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucas_Oil_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Movable window wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lucas_Oil_Stadium_-_opening.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Lucas_Oil_Stadium_-_opening.JPG/220px-Lucas_Oil_Stadium_-_opening.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Lucas_Oil_Stadium_-_opening.JPG/330px-Lucas_Oil_Stadium_-_opening.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Lucas_Oil_Stadium_-_opening.JPG/440px-Lucas_Oil_Stadium_-_opening.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lucas_Oil_Stadium_-_opening.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Attendees can see the Indianapolis skyline through this movable window</div>

<p>A large windowed gate at the northern end of the stadium allows additional light while closed and allows for a more open feel while open. It was the largest movable glass wall in the world until <a href="/wiki/AT%26T_Stadium" title="AT&amp;T Stadium">AT&amp;T Stadium</a> was completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nfl_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-nfl-16">[16]</a></sup> The transportable window wall is 244 by 88 feet (74 by 27 m), and composed of six 88 ft (27 m) × 38 ft (12 m) glass-clad panels. Each panel rides on a steel rail while the wall opens and closes, and is supported by two hardened steel wheels. The window separates at the center, with three panels amassed on each side when in the open position. The six wall panels move simultaneously during opening and closing in six minutes. The windows seal when closed, fully shielding building occupants from all weather conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uni_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-uni-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The retractable window offers a view of downtown Indianapolis during games, concerts and other events due to the stadium's angled position on the city block.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gate_sponsorship">Gate sponsorship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucas_Oil_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gate sponsorship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The four gates leading into Lucas Oil Stadium are each named for sponsoring corporation: Lucas Oil, Verizon, HHGregg, and Huntington Bank. The ground-level concourses of their respective gates are feature banners and floor coverings with the corporations' logos, advertisements and merchandise displays. Each corporation is reported to have paid in excess of $1 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sponsor_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-sponsor-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Football">Football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucas_Oil_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lucas Oil Stadium and the city of Indianapolis made a bid to host <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XLVI" title="Super Bowl XLVI">Super Bowl XLVI</a> in 2012. On May 20, 2008, the bid was successful, defeating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston, Texas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glendale,_Arizona" title="Glendale, Arizona">Glendale, Arizona</a> for that right. In July 2012, Indianapolis mayor <a href="/wiki/Greg_Ballard" title="Greg Ballard">Greg Ballard</a> announced that the city was intending on hosting another Super Bowl in 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The stadium is also host to the annual <a href="/wiki/NFL_Scouting_Combine" title="NFL Scouting Combine">NFL Scouting Combine</a> in February.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2008_NFL_season" title="2008 NFL season">2008 NFL season</a> featured the first <a href="/wiki/NBC_Sunday_Night_Football" title="NBC Sunday Night Football">NBC Sunday Night Football</a> game of the season in the stadium, as the Colts faced the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Bears" title="Chicago Bears">Chicago Bears</a> in a rematch of <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XLI" title="Super Bowl XLI">Super Bowl XLI</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-opener_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-opener-19">[19]</a></sup> The Colts lost the game 29-13. Indianapolis won its first 2 NFL playoff games held at Lucas Oil Stadium, beating the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Ravens" title="Baltimore Ravens">Baltimore Ravens</a> 20-3 in a 2009 AFC divisional playoff and the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Jets" title="New York Jets">New York Jets</a> 30-17 in the 2009 AFC Championship Game to reach <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XLIV" title="Super Bowl XLIV">Super Bowl XLIV</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="College_and_high_school">College and high school</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucas_Oil_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: College and high school">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The stadium hosted the semifinal and final rounds of the <a href="/wiki/2010_NCAA_Men%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Tournament" title="2010 NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Tournament">Men's Final Four</a> in 2010, 2015 and will in 2021, with the <a href="/wiki/2016_NCAA_Women%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Tournament" title="2016 NCAA Women's Division I Basketball Tournament">Women's Final Four</a> scheduled to be hosted there in 2016. Historically, Indianapolis has been a popular choice for the Final Four. The NCAA has its headquarters there, and the event comes on a 5-year rotation.</p>
<p>Also, the <a href="/wiki/Big_Ten_Conference" title="Big Ten Conference">Big Ten Conference</a> has played the <a href="/wiki/Big_Ten_Football_Championship_Game" title="Big Ten Football Championship Game">Big Ten Football Championship Game</a> at Lucas Oil Stadium since its inception in 2011. In the inaugural edition on December 3, the <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_Badgers_football" title="Wisconsin Badgers football">Wisconsin Badgers</a> defeated the <a href="/wiki/Michigan_State_Spartans_football" title="Michigan State Spartans football">Michigan State Spartans</a> 42-39.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-espn_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-espn-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first games played at Lucas Oil Stadium occurred on August 22, 2008 and were part of the PeyBack Classic, featuring Indiana High School Football games played between <a href="/wiki/Noblesville_High_School" title="Noblesville High School">Noblesville High School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fishers_High_School" title="Fishers High School">Fishers High School</a> in Game 1, followed by <a href="/wiki/New_Palestine_High_School" title="New Palestine High School">New Palestine High School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whiteland_Community_High_School" title="Whiteland Community High School">Whiteland Community High School</a> in Game 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inside_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-inside-21">[21]</a></sup> On November 26, 2008, <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Ritter_High_School" title="Cardinal Ritter High School">Cardinal Ritter High School</a> became the first high school to win a state championship on the field, beating <a href="/wiki/Sheridan_High_School_(Indiana)" title="Sheridan High School (Indiana)">Sheridan High School</a> 34-27 for the class A state title.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soccer">Soccer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucas_Oil_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Soccer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The stadium hosted its first soccer game on August 1, 2013, when <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_F.C." title="Chelsea F.C.">Chelsea</a> played <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internazionale" title="Internazionale">Inter Milan</a> in a first-round game of the <a href="/wiki/International_Champions_Cup" title="International Champions Cup">International Champions Cup</a>, drawing almost 40,000 fans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Entertainment">Entertainment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucas_Oil_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Entertainment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Drum_Corps_International" title="Drum Corps International">Drum Corps International</a> (DCI) announced on August 9, 2006 that it would move its corporate offices to Indianapolis and that the DCI World Championships would be the inaugural event for the stadium and would be held at Lucas Oil Stadium every year through 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dci_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-dci-23">[23]</a></sup> Because the stadium would not be completed in time, DCI announced on April 4, 2008 that it would move the event to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memorial_Stadium,_Bloomington" title="Memorial Stadium, Bloomington">Memorial Stadium</a> on the campus of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indiana_University_(Bloomington)" title="Indiana University (Bloomington)">Indiana University</a> for 2008. The competition was held for the first time at Lucas Oil Stadium in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-relocate_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-relocate-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other regular events include the <a href="/wiki/Bands_of_America" title="Bands of America">Bands of America</a> Grand National Championships<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mfa_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-mfa-25">[25]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Marching_Band_State_Finals" title="Indiana Marching Band State Finals">Indiana Marching Band State Finals</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-issma_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-issma-26">[26]</a></sup> both major events for the city in <a href="/wiki/Marching_band" title="Marching band">Marching band</a> competitions. The Drum Corps International World Championships are scheduled the site annually through the 2018 season, and the Circle City Classic that is an annual American football game featuring two historically black colleges/universities (HBCUs) that is held in October.</p>
<p>On September 13, 2008, country music singer <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Chesney" title="Kenny Chesney">Kenny Chesney</a> held the first public concert at the stadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chesney_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-chesney-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Financing">Financing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucas_Oil_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Financing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Groundbreaking for the stadium took place on September 20, 2005. It was originally referred to as Indiana Stadium until Lucas Oil purchased the naming rights. The total cost of Lucas Oil Stadium was $720 million. The stadium is being financed with funds raised by the State of Indiana and the City of Indianapolis, with the Indianapolis Colts providing $100 million. <a href="/wiki/Marion_County,_Indiana" title="Marion County, Indiana">Marion County</a> has raised taxes for food and beverage sales, auto rental taxes, innkeeper's taxes, and admission taxes for its share of the costs. Meanwhile, there has been an increase in food and beverage taxes in the 8 surrounding <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis_metropolitan_area" title="Indianapolis metropolitan area">doughnut counties</a> (with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Morgan_County,_Indiana" title="Morgan County, Indiana">Morgan County</a>) and the sale of Colts license plates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-facts_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-facts-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The County Commissioners of each county voted whether to levy the 1% food and beverage tax proposed by Marion County. Sweetening the deal for those counties was the fact that half of the revenue from the tax would stay in the respective county. <a href="/wiki/Morgan_County,_Indiana" title="Morgan County, Indiana">Morgan County</a> was the only county to turn down the offer, yet in a later vote, it levied its own 1% tax – thus keeping <i>all</i> of its additional generated revenue.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Budget_shortfall">Budget shortfall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucas_Oil_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Budget shortfall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 2006, the Capital Improvement Board, which operates the stadium, estimated that daily operating expenses of the new stadium would be $10 million more per year than the RCA Dome. The board urged the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_General_Assembly" title="Indiana General Assembly">Indiana General Assembly</a> to authorize funding to cover the shortfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pacers_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-pacers-28">[28]</a></sup> The Indiana Legislature considered a bill to raise sales taxes statewide to cover the shortfall, however this plan faces stiff opposition from legislators outside the Indianapolis metro area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bailout_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-bailout-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The assembly ultimately authorized a tax increase in Indianapolis-Marion County. In addition, the CIB trimmed staff and cut $10 million from its budget. Still, the agency anticipated a $20 million operating deficit for Lucas Oil Stadium in 2009. Anticipated expenses are $27.7 million—far outstripping the $7.7 million CIB expects to collect from its share of revenue from stadium events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibj_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ibj-30">[30]</a></sup> The Colts organization has been criticized for the favorable lease terms and the high percentage of revenue it can keep under the terms of its agreements with the stadium authorities and there have been calls for the team to cover the shortfalls of the CIB. The Colts responded to these criticisms in an open letter to fans on September 16, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-letter_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-letter-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Complications">Complications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucas_Oil_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Complications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On September 8, 2013 after the Colts defeated the Oakland Raiders in the season opener, a rail over the opposing team tunnel collapsed injuring two fans. One fan was transferred to the hospital for evaluation. No serious injuries were reported.</p>
<p>On September 3, 2015, three fans were injured by a bolt that fell from the roof of the stadium as it was being opened during an NFL preseason game against the Cincinnati Bengals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bolt_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-bolt-32">[32]</a></sup> The stadium was pronounced safe by officials, but the roof was to remain closed for events until a final investigation was completed as to why the bolt fell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-investigation_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-investigation-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
