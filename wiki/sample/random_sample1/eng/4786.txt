<p><b>Luchino <a href="/wiki/House_of_Visconti" title="House of Visconti">Visconti</a> di <a href="/wiki/Vimodrone" title="Vimodrone">Modrone</a></b>, Count of Lonate Pozzolo (2 November 1906 â 17 March 1976), was an Italian theatre, opera and cinema director, as well as a screenwriter. He is best known for his films <i><a href="/wiki/The_Leopard_(1963_film)" title="The Leopard (1963 film)">The Leopard</a></i> (1963) and <i><a href="/wiki/Death_in_Venice_(film)" title="Death in Venice (film)">Death in Venice</a></i> (1971).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luchino_Visconti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Luchino Visconti was born into a prominent noble family in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, one of seven children of Giuseppe Visconti di Modrone, Duke of Grazzano Visconti and Count of <a href="/wiki/Lonate_Pozzolo" title="Lonate Pozzolo">Lonate Pozzolo</a>, and his wife Carla<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (nÃ©e Erba, heiress to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Erba_Pharmaceuticals&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Erba Pharmaceuticals (page does not exist)">Erba Pharmaceuticals</a>). He was formally known as Count don Luchino Visconti di Modrone, and his family is a branch of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Visconti" title="House of Visconti">House of Visconti</a>. In his early years, he was exposed to art, music and theatre: he studied cello with the Italian cellist and composer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lorenzo_de_Paolis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lorenzo de Paolis (page does not exist)">Lorenzo de Paolis</a> (1890â1965) and met the composer <a href="/wiki/Giacomo_Puccini" title="Giacomo Puccini">Giacomo Puccini</a>, the conductor <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Toscanini" title="Arturo Toscanini">Arturo Toscanini</a> and the writer <a href="/wiki/Gabriele_D%27Annunzio" title="Gabriele D'Annunzio">Gabriele D'Annunzio</a>.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Visconti joined the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Communist_Party" title="Italian Communist Party">Italian Communist Party</a>.</p>
<p>Visconti made no secret of his <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">homosexuality</a>. His last partner was the <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a> actor <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Berger" title="Helmut Berger">Helmut Berger</a>, who played Martin in Visconti's film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Damned_(1969_film)" title="The Damned (1969 film)">The Damned</a></i>. Berger also appeared in Visconti's <i><a href="/wiki/Ludwig_(film)" title="Ludwig (film)">Ludwig</a></i> in 1972 and <i><a href="/wiki/Conversation_Piece_(film)" title="Conversation Piece (film)">Conversation Piece</a></i> in 1974, along with <a href="/wiki/Burt_Lancaster" title="Burt Lancaster">Burt Lancaster</a>. Other lovers included <a href="/wiki/Franco_Zeffirelli" title="Franco Zeffirelli">Franco Zeffirelli</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> who also worked as part of the crew in production design, as assistant director, and other roles in a number of Visconti's films, operas, and theatrical productions.</p>
<p>Visconti suffered a <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a> in 1972, but continued to smoke heavily. He died in Rome of another stroke at the age of 69. There is a museum dedicated to the director's work in <a href="/wiki/Ischia" title="Ischia">Ischia</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luchino_Visconti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Films">Films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luchino_Visconti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He began his filmmaking career as an assistant director on <a href="/wiki/Jean_Renoir" title="Jean Renoir">Jean Renoir</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Toni_(1935_film)" title="Toni (1935 film)">Toni</a></i> (1935) and <i><a href="/wiki/Partie_de_campagne" title="Partie de campagne">Partie de campagne</a></i> (1936), thanks to the intercession of their common friend, <a href="/wiki/Coco_Chanel" title="Coco Chanel">Coco Chanel</a>. After a short tour of the United States, where he visited <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a>, he returned to Italy to be Renoir's assistant again, this time for <i>La <a href="/wiki/Tosca" title="Tosca">Tosca</a></i> (1939), a production that was interrupted and later completed by German director <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karl_Koch_(director)" title="Karl Koch (director)">Karl Koch</a> because of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.</p>
<p>Together with <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Rossellini" title="Roberto Rossellini">Roberto Rossellini</a>, Visconti joined the <i><a href="/wiki/Salon_(gathering)" title="Salon (gathering)">salotto</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Vittorio_Mussolini" title="Vittorio Mussolini">Vittorio Mussolini</a> (the son of <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito</a>, who was then the national arbitrator for cinema and other arts). Here he presumably also met <a href="/wiki/Federico_Fellini" title="Federico Fellini">Federico Fellini</a>. With Gianni Puccini, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Pietrangeli" title="Antonio Pietrangeli">Antonio Pietrangeli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_De_Santis" title="Giuseppe De Santis">Giuseppe De Santis</a>, he wrote the screenplay for his first film as director: <i><a href="/wiki/Ossessione" title="Ossessione">Ossessione</a></i> (<i>Obsession</i>, 1943), the first <a href="/wiki/Italian_neorealism" title="Italian neorealism">neorealist</a> movie and an unofficial adaptation of the novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Postman_Always_Rings_Twice_(novel)" title="The Postman Always Rings Twice (novel)">The Postman Always Rings Twice</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1948, he wrote and directed <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_terra_trema" title="La terra trema">La terra trema</a></i> (<i>The Earth Trembles</i>), based on the novel <i>I Malavoglia</i> by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Verga" title="Giovanni Verga">Giovanni Verga</a>. In the book by Silvia Iannello, <i>Le immagini e le parole dei Malavoglia</i>, the author selects some passages of the Verga novel, adds original comments and Acitrezza's photographic images, and devotes a chapter to the origins, remarks and frames taken from the movie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Visconti continued working throughout the 1950s, although he veered away from the neorealist path with his 1954 film, <i><a href="/wiki/Senso_(film)" title="Senso (film)">Senso</a></i>, shot in colour. Based on the novella by <a href="/wiki/Camillo_Boito" title="Camillo Boito">Camillo Boito</a>, it is set in Austrian-occupied <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> in 1866. In this film, Visconti combines <a href="/wiki/Realism_(arts)" title="Realism (arts)">realism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">romanticism</a> as a way to break away from neorealism. However, as one biographer notes, "Visconti without <a href="/wiki/Italian_neorealism" title="Italian neorealism">neorealism</a> is like <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Lang" title="Fritz Lang">Lang</a> without <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">expressionism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Eisenstein" title="Sergei Eisenstein">Eisenstein</a> without <a href="/wiki/Formalist_film_theory" title="Formalist film theory">formalism</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He describes the film as the "most Viscontian" of all Visconti's films. Visconti returned to neorealism once more with <i><a href="/wiki/Rocco_and_His_Brothers" title="Rocco and His Brothers">Rocco e i suoi fratelli</a></i> (<i>Rocco and His Brothers</i>, 1960), the story of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Italy" title="Southern Italy">Southern Italians</a> who migrate to Milan hoping to find financial stability. In 1961, he was a member of the jury at the <a href="/wiki/2nd_Moscow_International_Film_Festival" title="2nd Moscow International Film Festival">2nd Moscow International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moscow1961_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moscow1961-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout the 1960s, Visconti's films became more personal. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Leopard_(1963_film)" title="The Leopard (1963 film)">Il Gattopardo</a></i> (<i>The Leopard</i>, 1963), is based on <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Tomasi_di_Lampedusa" title="Giuseppe Tomasi di Lampedusa">Lampedusa</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Leopard" title="The Leopard">novel of the same name</a> about the decline of the <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicilian</a> aristocracy at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_unification" title="Italian unification">Risorgimento</a>. It starred American actor <a href="/wiki/Burt_Lancaster" title="Burt Lancaster">Burt Lancaster</a> in the role of Prince Don Fabrizio. This film was distributed in America and Britain by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twentieth-Century_Fox" title="Twentieth-Century Fox">Twentieth-Century Fox</a>, which deleted important scenes. Visconti repudiated the Twentieth-Century Fox version.</p>
<p>It was not until <i><a href="/wiki/The_Damned_(1969_film)" title="The Damned (1969 film)">The Damned</a></i> (1969) that Visconti received a nomination for an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a>, for "Best Screenplay". The film, one of Visconti's best-known works, concerns a German industrialist's family which slowly begins to disintegrate during the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi</a> consolidation of power in the 30s. Its decadence and lavish beauty are characteristic of Visconti's aesthetic.</p>
<p>Visconti's final film was <i><a href="/wiki/The_Innocent_(1976_film)" title="The Innocent (1976 film)">The Innocent</a></i> (1976), in which he returns to his recurring interest in infidelity and betrayal.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatre">Theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luchino_Visconti&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Visconti was also a celebrated theatre and <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a> director. During the years 1946 to 1960 he directed many performances of the <a href="/wiki/Rina_Morelli" title="Rina Morelli">Rina Morelli</a>-<a href="/wiki/Paolo_Stoppa" title="Paolo Stoppa">Paolo Stoppa</a> Company with actor <a href="/wiki/Vittorio_Gassman" title="Vittorio Gassman">Vittorio Gassman</a> as well as many celebrated productions of operas.</p>
<p>Visconti's love of opera is evident in the 1954 <i>Senso</i>, where the beginning of the film shows scenes from the fourth act of <i><a href="/wiki/Il_trovatore" title="Il trovatore">Il trovatore</a></i>, which were filmed at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teatro_La_Fenice" title="Teatro La Fenice">Teatro La Fenice</a> in Venice. Beginning when he directed a production at Milan's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teatro_alla_Scala" title="Teatro alla Scala">Teatro alla Scala</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/La_vestale" title="La vestale">La vestale</a></i> in December 1954, his career included a famous revival of <i><a href="/wiki/La_traviata" title="La traviata">La traviata</a></i> at <a href="/wiki/La_Scala" title="La Scala">La Scala</a> in 1955 with <a href="/wiki/Maria_Callas" title="Maria Callas">Maria Callas</a> and an equally famous <i><a href="/wiki/Anna_Bolena" title="Anna Bolena">Anna Bolena</a></i> (also at La Scala) in 1957 with Callas. A significant 1958 <a href="/wiki/Royal_Opera_House" title="Royal Opera House">Royal Opera House</a> (London) production of Verdi's five-act Italian version of <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Carlos" title="Don Carlos">Don Carlos</a></i> (with <a href="/wiki/Jon_Vickers" title="Jon Vickers">Jon Vickers</a>) followed, along with a <i><a href="/wiki/Macbeth_(opera)" title="Macbeth (opera)">Macbeth</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Spoleto" title="Spoleto">Spoleto</a> in 1958 and a famous black-and-white <i><a href="/wiki/Il_trovatore" title="Il trovatore">Il trovatore</a></i> with scenery and costumes by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Filippo_Sanjust&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Filippo Sanjust (page does not exist)">Filippo Sanjust</a> at the Royal Opera House in 1964. In 1966 Visconti's luscious <i><a href="/wiki/Falstaff_(opera)" title="Falstaff (opera)">Falstaff</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_State_Opera" title="Vienna State Opera">Vienna State Opera</a> conducted by <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Bernstein" title="Leonard Bernstein">Leonard Bernstein</a> was critically acclaimed. On the other hand, his austere 1969 <i><a href="/wiki/Simon_Boccanegra" title="Simon Boccanegra">Simon Boccanegra</a></i> with the singers clothed in geometrical costumes provoked controversy.</p>
