<p><b>Lookism</b> is a term used to describe discriminatory treatment towards physically unattractive people; mainly in the workplace but also in social settings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> While not classified in the same way as racial, cultural, sexual discrimination, "lookism" is widespread and affects how people are perceived as well as affecting their opportunities in terms of relationships, job opportunities, etc.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Physical_attractiveness" title="Physical attractiveness">Physical attractiveness</a> is associated with good things; in contrast, physical unattractiveness is associated with negative things. Many people make judgments of others based on their physical appearance that influence how they respond to those people. Research on the "What is beautiful is good" stereotype shows that, overall, those who are physically attractive benefit from their good looks: physically attractive individuals are perceived more positively and physical attractiveness has a strong influence on judgement of a person’s competence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eagly_et_al..2C_1991.29._2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eagly_et_al..2C_1991.29.-2">[2]</a></sup> In return, physically attractive people benefit from these stereotypical beliefs. Research shows that on average, physically attractive individuals have more friends, better social skills, and more active sex lives. However, attractiveness does not have any effect on the level of happiness experienced by the individual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rhodes.2C_Simmons.2C_.26_Peters.2C_2005.29._3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rhodes.2C_Simmons.2C_.26_Peters.2C_2005.29.-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lookism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though the term "lookism" is of recent coinage, cultures and traditions worldwide have often warned against placing undue value on physical appearance:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>To judge by appearances is to get entangled in the Veil of Maya [in Buddhist thought] [...] From ancient times until relatively recently, there was widespread worry about lookism, because the appearance of others may deceive, especially in romance, or it may be personally or politically imprudent to judge or act on appearances. Judging by appearances was prohibited by monotheistic religions (“no graven images”) and criticized in ancient and medieval philosophies. <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_skepticism" title="Philosophical skepticism">Skeptics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoics" title="Stoics">Stoics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cynicism_(philosophy)" title="Cynicism (philosophy)">Cynics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epicureans" title="Epicureans">Epicureans</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scholastics" title="Scholastics">Scholastics</a> elaborated various reasons to avoid or subordinate the role of appearances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>However, the term "Lookism" was first coined in the 1970s within the <a href="/wiki/Fat_acceptance_movement" title="Fat acceptance movement">fat acceptance movement</a>. It was used in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> Magazine in 1978, which asserted that the term was coined by "fat people" who created the word to refer to "discrimination based on looks."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The word appears in several major English language dictionaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lookism has received scholarly attention both from a <a href="/wiki/Cultural_studies" title="Cultural studies">cultural studies</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> perspective. In the former context, lookism relates to preconceived notions of <a href="/wiki/Beauty" title="Beauty">beauty</a> and cultural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereotyping" title="Stereotyping">stereotyping</a> based on appearance as well as <a href="/wiki/Gender_role" title="Gender role">gender roles</a> and expectations. Important economic considerations include the question of income gaps based on looks, as well as increased or decreased productivity from workers considered beautiful or ugly by their co-workers. Due to this, new problems arise that are tied to other social issues like racism (preferring white people over people of color) and agism (young over old). The idea of beauty is also linked directly to social class because people who have more free time and money have the ability to work on their appearance. Weight is also linked to social class because people who are overweight do not have the exercise equipment or the healthy food choices that wealthier people do. Judging people on the basis of attractiveness decreases a person's self-esteem leading to a negative self-image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some writers have examined this phenomenon among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gay_men" title="Gay men">gay men</a>. Author <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo_Signorile" title="Michelangelo Signorile">Michelangelo Signorile</a> described it as "the setting of a rigid set of standards of physical beauty that pressures everyone within a particular group to conform to them. Any person who doesn't meet those very specific standards is deemed physically unattractive and sexually undesirable. In a culture in which the physical body is held in such high esteem and given such power, body fascism then not only deems those who don't or can't conform to be sexually less desirable, but in extreme sometimes dubbed lookism also deems a person completely worthless <i>as a person</i>, based solely on his exterior. In this sense it is not unlike racism or sexism, or homophobia itself."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morrison_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morrison-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Empirical_support">Empirical support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lookism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Empirical support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nancy_Etcoff" title="Nancy Etcoff">Nancy Etcoff</a>, a psychologist at <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_General_Hospital" title="Massachusetts General Hospital">Massachusetts General Hospital</a>, "we face a world where lookism is one of the most pervasive but denied prejudices."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Referring to several studies, Angela Stalcup writes that "The evidence clearly indicates that not only is there a premium for prettiness in Western culture, there is also penalty for plainness."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Studies on newborns have found that human infants as young as 14 hours from birth prefer to look at attractive faces rather than unattractive faces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gaze_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-gaze-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newborn_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-newborn-12">[12]</a></sup> The preference also extends to non-human animals such as cats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conspecifics_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-conspecifics-13">[13]</a></sup> These findings indicate that lookism is an innate product of how the human visual system functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asa_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-asa-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Research by <a href="/wiki/Dan_Ariely" title="Dan Ariely">Dan Ariely</a> found that American women exhibit a marked preference for dating taller men, and that for shorter men to be judged attractive by women, they must earn substantially more money than taller men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the article "Is Lookism Unjust", Louis Tietje and Steven Cresap discuss when discrimination based on looks can legitimately be described as unjust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xxx_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-xxx-16">[16]</a></sup> Tietje and Cresap quote evidence that suggests there exists "a 7–to–9 percent 'penalty' for being in the lowest 9 percent of looks among all workers, and a 5 percent 'premium' for being in the top 33 percent". While accepting that the evidence indicates that such discrimination does occur, the authors argue that it has been pervasive throughout history. Therefore, there can be no clear model of injustice in such discrimination, nor would legislation to address it be practicable. The authors conclude: "We do not see how any policy interventions to redress beauty discrimination can be justified."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xxx_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-xxx-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_lookism">Political lookism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lookism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Political lookism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lookism has been an issue in politics for centuries, with a long tradition in the United Kingdom of "mercilessly exaggerat[ing]" the physical flaws of politicians in newspaper cartoons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1960#Debates" title="United States presidential election, 1960">1960 Presidential race between John F. Kennedy and Richard Nixon</a>, it was often believed that Kennedy's more conventionally handsome appearance contributed to his winning more approval in their first televised debate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-museum.tv_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-museum.tv-18">[18]</a></sup> but some researchers have challenged this widespread idea and argued that Kennedy's appearance had little or no influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are several variables that might contribute to the objectification of masculinity and femininity in politics. Scholar Charlotte Hooper argued that “gender intersects with other social divisions such as class, race and sexuality to produce complex hierarchies of (gendered) identities[.]”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Hooper argues that institutional practices, such as military combat in war, have greatly defined what it means to be a man. Furthermore, the symbolic dimension, which includes sports, media, current affairs, etc. has “disseminate[d] a wealth of popular iconography which links Western masculinities to the wider world beyond the borders of the state.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This is where the ideology of lookism is firmly entrenched according to Hooper. Similarly, Laura Shepherd suggests that men are required to fit into the “matrix of intelligibility”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> by acting a certain way, dressing a certain way, and have a mentality that is devoid of emotion or anything effeminate; if they are successful in becoming the ultimate “man’s man” then they are virtually untouchable. However, others have suggested that there is only an explicit interest in the analysis of masculinity within this political sphere, it will be impossible to develop a reliable analysis of femininity within this same sphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (October 2013)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> argue that lookism affects women in an even more detrimental way than men. Drawing examples from <a href="/wiki/Madeleine_Albright" title="Madeleine Albright">Madeleine Albright</a>’s TED talk in 2010, “On Being a Woman and Diplomat”, Albright expressed her frustrations with how her male colleagues and media commentators would pick apart her appearance. Being the first female Secretary of State for the United States, Albright was in the spotlight on the domestic and international stage; everything from her age, weight, hairstyle and choice of dress were scrutinized; yet ironically, the policy positions she believed to be her most important accomplishments (initiation of the G7, attempts to promote gender equality, etc.) were hardly taken into account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The fact that Albright’s general appearance didn't fit into the narrow category of “attractive” made it even more difficult for her to navigate the space between being a woman and a diplomat. Albright is not the only woman in a position of power, or otherwise, that has been discriminated against because of her appearance. An article published in <i>The Washington Pos</i>t in 2005 labeled Secretary of State, <a href="/wiki/Condoleezza_Rice" title="Condoleezza Rice">Condoleezza Rice</a>, as a “<a href="/wiki/Dominatrix" title="Dominatrix">dominatrix</a>” when she stepped out in knee-high black-heeled boots during a visit to Wiesbaden Military Base in Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Although the article was meant to give credit to Rice for “challeng[ing] expectations and assumptions”,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (October 2013)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> argue that the article gave her a hyper-sexualized image, and further removed the audience from focusing on the purpose of her visit to the military place. Similarly, media commentators have often chosen to report on Hillary Clinton’s “man suits” and Julia Gillard’s short hairstyle, instead of focusing on these women’s professional accomplishments.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Sarah Palin, former governor of Alaska and 2008 Republican Vice-Presidential candidate, was the subject of much media attention due to her conventionally attractive appearance,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> with Palin suggesting that the focus on her appearance ignored her professional and policy accomplishments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Law">Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lookism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lookism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Until the 1970s, lookism in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> was sometimes codified into law. In many jurisdictions, so-called "<a href="/wiki/Ugly_law" title="Ugly law">ugly laws</a>" barred people from appearing in public if they had diseases or disfigurements that were considered unsightly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-brown-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Today, the <a href="/wiki/Equal_Employment_Opportunity_Commission" title="Equal Employment Opportunity Commission">Equal Employment Opportunity Commission</a> considers extreme <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a> to be a <a href="/wiki/Disability" title="Disability">disability</a> protected by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Americans_with_Disabilities_Act" title="Americans with Disabilities Act">Americans with Disabilities Act</a>, and a few cities protect against discrimination based on appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Otherwise, there is no federal law protecting against discrimination based on physical appearance.</p>
