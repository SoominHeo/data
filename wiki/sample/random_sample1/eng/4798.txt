<p><b>Ruch Chorzów</b> (<small>Polish pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Polish" title="Help:IPA for Polish">[ˈrux ˈxɔʐuf]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> association football club based in <a href="/wiki/Chorz%C3%B3w" title="Chorzów">Chorzów</a>, <a href="/wiki/Upper_Silesia" title="Upper Silesia">Upper Silesia</a>. It is one of the most successful football teams in Poland: fourteen-time national champions, and three-time winners of the Polish Cup. Currently the team plays in the top Polish league, the <a href="/wiki/Ekstraklasa" title="Ekstraklasa">Ekstraklasa</a>. Their stadium capacity is 10,000 seats.</p>
<p>Ruch Chorzów has also had a very successful female handball team (9 times national champions).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruch_Chorz%C3%B3w&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The club was founded on 20 April 1920 in <a href="/wiki/Chorz%C3%B3w_Batory" title="Chorzów Batory">Bismarkhuta</a> (German <i>Bismarckhütte</i>, historically <i>Hajduki</i>), one of the many heavily industrialised municipalities in the eastern part of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Silesia" title="Upper Silesia">Upper Silesia</a>, a disputed province between Poland and Germany. The main incentive was an appeal of the Polish Plebiscite Committee a few months earlier that led to creation of around one hundred sport associations. It took place in between the first and second <a href="/wiki/Silesian_Uprisings" title="Silesian Uprisings">Silesian Uprisings</a>, to which the name <i>Ruch</i> is a supposed <a href="/wiki/Double_entendre" title="Double entendre">cover reference</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The Polish word <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ruch" title="wiktionary:ruch">ruch</a> is however also a common noun for <i>movement</i>, not as strongly associated with Polishness as names of many other clubs established after the appeal (like Polonia, Powstaniec etc.). On the other hand, the club's first match, a 3:1 win against Orzeł Józefowiec, was played on 3 May 1920, the day of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_May_3,_1791" title="Constitution of May 3, 1791">first Polish Constitution</a>. After the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Silesia_plebiscite" title="Upper Silesia plebiscite">Upper Silesia plebiscite</a> and the third Silesian Uprising in 1921 Bismarkhuta became part of Poland and the <a href="/wiki/Silesian_Voivodeship_(1920%E2%80%9339)" title="Silesian Voivodeship (1920–39)">Silesian Voivodeship</a>. The municipality was renamed to <i>Wielkie Hajduki</i> on 1 January 1923,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> hence the club was known as Ruch Wielkie Hajduki until another merger into the town Chorzów (created in 1934 from amalgamation of <i>Królewska Huta</i>, <i>Chorzów</i> and <i>Hajduki Nowe</i>) in the early 1939, with a short period in 1923 after the fusion with the older local German club <i>Bismarckhütter Ballspiel Club</i>, when it was known as <i>Ruch BBC Wielkie Hajduki</i>. After the merger the team played its games on the former BBC's pitch known as <i>na Kalinie</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The popular nickname of the club <i>Niebiescy</i> (<i>The Blues</i>) clung to the team already in the 1920s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>In autumn of 1920 Ruch won the promotion to the nascent Silesian <i>Klasa A</i> (see also: <a href="/wiki/Lower_Level_Football_Leagues_in_Interwar_Poland" title="Lower Level Football Leagues in Interwar Poland">Lower Level Football Leagues in Interwar Poland</a>). The Blues were third out of fourteen teams in its first season, unfinished due to the third Silesian Uprising. The next year Ruch won the championship of the Silesian <i>Klasa A</i> and represented the region in the <a href="/wiki/1922_Polish_Football_Championship" title="1922 Polish Football Championship">1922 Polish Football Championship</a>. In 1924 the club finished second in the regional top league, behind <a href="/wiki/AKS_Chorz%C3%B3w" title="AKS Chorzów">AKS Królewska Huta</a>, before 1924 considered German and known as <i>Verein für Rasenspiele Königshütte</i>, the first team Ruch had developed a local rivalry with. In 1925 the Silesian Klasa A did not play, instead <i>Stanisław Flieger's Cup</i> took place, ultimately won by Ruch, which gave the side a start in the only interwar Polish Cup competition in <a class="extiw" href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puchar_Polski_w_pi%C5%82ce_no%C5%BCnej_(1925/1926)" title="pl:Puchar Polski w piłce nożnej (1925/1926)">1926</a>. On 4 July 1926 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J%C3%B3zef_Sobota&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Józef Sobota (page does not exist)">Józef Sobota</a>, before 1920 a BBC's player, became the first Ruch's player (and the fourth from Upper Silesia) of the <a href="/wiki/Poland_national_football_team" title="Poland national football team">Polish National Team</a>, who also scored a goal (against <a href="/wiki/Estonia_national_football_team" title="Estonia national football team">Estonia</a>). In the same year, two weeks after the national Cup Ruch won for the second time the regional Klasa A, firmly establishing itself as one of the strongest football clubs in this densely populated region and as such it was among the founding clubs of the Polish national league in <a href="/wiki/1927_Ekstraklasa" title="1927 Ekstraklasa">1927</a>. In <a href="/wiki/1933_Ekstraklasa" title="1933 Ekstraklasa">1933</a> Ruch won its first Championship as the first side from Silesia, interestingly with all the players who were born not further as a few kilometers from the <i>na Kalinie</i> pitch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Thus the first truly golden era began. The local steel mill (since 1934 known as Huta Batory) began to financially support the side. In the winter of 1933 the most noteworthy players such as <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Giemsa" title="Edmund Giemsa">Edmund Giemsa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Teodor_Peterek" title="Teodor Peterek">Teodor Peterek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Wodarz" title="Gerard Wodarz">Gerard Wodarz</a> were joined by legendary <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Wilimowski" title="Ernst Wilimowski">Ernst Wilimowski</a>, bought from <a href="/wiki/1._FC_Kattowitz" title="1. FC Kattowitz">1. FC Kattowitz</a>, who with Peterek and Wodarz were collectively nicknamed <i>the three kings</i> and helped to win another 4 championships (<a href="/wiki/1933_Ekstraklasa" title="1933 Ekstraklasa">1934</a>, <a href="/wiki/1935_Ekstraklasa" title="1935 Ekstraklasa">1935</a>, <a href="/wiki/1936_Ekstraklasa" title="1936 Ekstraklasa">1936</a>, <a href="/wiki/1938_Ekstraklasa" title="1938 Ekstraklasa">1938</a>). On 1 November 1934 the club, as the last in the league, employed its first coach, Gustav Wieser.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The side was also a leader in the unfinished season <a href="/wiki/1939_Ekstraklasa" title="1939 Ekstraklasa">1939</a>. The successes rendered the club the most popular in the voivodeship and accelerated building of the new stadium in the years 1934-1935, the current <a href="/wiki/Stadion_Miejski_(Chorz%C3%B3w)" title="Stadion Miejski (Chorzów)">Stadion Miejski</a>.</p>

<p>After the German occupation of Poland in 1939, the club was officially discontinued but unofficially was simply renamed Bismarckhütter SV 99 and joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gauliga_Oberschlesien" title="Gauliga Oberschlesien">Gauliga Oberschlesien</a> in 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The club was oficially re-established after the war. In 1947 Ruch won the regional championships. In 1948, under communist pressure (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalinisation" title="Stalinisation">Stalinisation</a>), the club was renamed Unia Chorzów, in 1955 it became Unia-Ruch, and finally in 1956 returned to the name Ruch. As Unia the club finished third in the first season of the reactivated national league in 1948 and in 1950 as the second team. In 1951 the club won the reactivated Polish Cup edition and were rewarded with the title of the National Champions (eventhough they were only sixth in <a href="/wiki/1951_Ekstraklasa" title="1951 Ekstraklasa">the league</a>). The next two years the club also won the title, first in <a href="/wiki/1952_Ekstraklasa" title="1952 Ekstraklasa">1952</a> after final against <a href="/wiki/Polonia_Bytom" title="Polonia Bytom">Polonia Bytom</a>, another local bitter rival, and in <a href="/wiki/1953_Ekstraklasa" title="1953 Ekstraklasa">1953</a> after finishing the league on the top position. The most renowned player of that era was <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Cie%C5%9Blik" title="Gerard Cieślik">Gerard Cieślik</a>, who dedicated his whole life to the club and became its icon.</p>

<p>The years 1957-1966 are considered a lost decade, completely overshadowed by the successes of the new biggest regional rival, <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B3rnik_Zabrze" title="Górnik Zabrze">Górnik Zabrze</a>, even though the club won the championships in <a href="/wiki/1960_Ekstraklasa" title="1960 Ekstraklasa">1960</a>. A record of its kind in the national footbal history as the team consisted of only 14 players, 11 of whom originated in the town of Chorzów. The turn of the tide came in the season <a href="/wiki/1967%E2%80%9368_Ekstraklasa" title="1967–68 Ekstraklasa">1967/68</a> when Ruch won the 10th championship title breaking Górnik Zabrze's streak of five consecutive titles. Another golden era for <i>the Blues</i> arrived in the early 1970s with <a href="/wiki/Michal_Vi%C4%8Dan" title="Michal Vičan">Michal Vičan</a> as a coach. In <a href="/wiki/1972%E2%80%9373_Ekstraklasa" title="1972–73 Ekstraklasa">1972/73</a> the club finished second, in <a href="/wiki/1973%E2%80%9374_Ekstraklasa" title="1973–74 Ekstraklasa">1973/73</a> they won the only double in the history (the championship and the cup) and advanced up to <a href="/wiki/1973%E2%80%9374_UEFA_Cup" title="1973–74 UEFA Cup">the quarter-finals of the UEFA Cup</a>. In <a href="/wiki/1974%E2%80%9375_Ekstraklasa" title="1974–75 Ekstraklasa">1974/75</a> they again won the league and qualified to the <a href="/wiki/1974%E2%80%9375_European_Cup" title="1974–75 European Cup">quarter-finals of the European Cup</a>. The most praised players of that times were <a href="/wiki/Bronis%C5%82aw_Bula" title="Bronisław Bula">Bronisław Bula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zygmunt_Maszczyk" title="Zygmunt Maszczyk">Zygmunt Maszczyk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joachim_Marx" title="Joachim Marx">Joachim Marx</a>.</p>
<p>These successes were followed by a bad financial plight and mediocre results until <a href="/wiki/1978%E2%80%9379_Ekstraklasa" title="1978–79 Ekstraklasa">1978/79</a>, when the club won its 13th Championship title. In the 1980s the club was one of the poorest in the national league. The worst came in the season <a href="/wiki/1986%E2%80%9387_Ekstraklasa" title="1986–87 Ekstraklasa">1986/87</a>, when the club, the only one in the country which so far played all the seasons of the official national top league, was relegated to the second tier. Especially shifty were the circumstances of the relegation decider, against <a href="/wiki/Lechia_Gda%C5%84sk" title="Lechia Gdańsk">Lechia Gdańsk</a>, when Ruch's goalkeeper <a href="/wiki/Janusz_Jojko" title="Janusz Jojko">Janusz Jojko</a> scored an infamous and bizarre <a href="/wiki/Own_goal" title="Own goal">own goal</a> and the club lost the game 1:2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> After one year Ruch returned to the top flight as winners of the seconed league and won the 14th Championship title, as the second freshly-promoted club in the national history (the first was Cracovia in <a href="/wiki/1937_Ekstraklasa" title="1937 Ekstraklasa">1937</a>), a feat, especially as it was still one of the poorest clubs in the top tier and over half of the players were home-grown,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> including e.g. <a href="/wiki/Dariusz_G%C4%99sior" title="Dariusz Gęsior">Dariusz Gęsior</a> and the most renowned <a href="/wiki/Krzysztof_Warzycha" title="Krzysztof Warzycha">Krzysztof Warzycha</a>, who was also, with 24 goals, the top scorer of the season.</p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1989" title="Revolutions of 1989">political turnover in Poland in 1989</a> Ruch did not fare well for the first two seasons. The money from the transfer of Krzysztof Warzycha to <a href="/wiki/Panathinaikos_A.O." title="Panathinaikos A.O.">Panathinaikos A.O.</a> finished quickly. The team began to compete with the top teams first in <a href="/wiki/1991%E2%80%9392_Ekstraklasa" title="1991–92 Ekstraklasa">1991/92</a> finishing in the fifth spot, fourth the next year, furthermore the second team (<i>Ruch II</i>) reached the Polish cup final. Ruch was demoted for the second time in the history in the season <a href="/wiki/1994%E2%80%9395_Ekstraklasa" title="1994–95 Ekstraklasa">1994/95</a>. As before the stay in the second tier lasted one season. While playing in the second league Ruch won its third Polish Cup trophy. In 1998 Ruch reached the final of the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Intertoto_Cup" title="UEFA Intertoto Cup">UEFA Intertoto Cup</a> and in the season <a href="/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_Ekstraklasa" title="1999–2000 Ekstraklasa">1999/2000</a> finished third in the league. The crisis came in <a href="/wiki/2002%E2%80%9303_Ekstraklasa" title="2002–03 Ekstraklasa">2002/2003</a> when the club was relegated from the top tier for the third time and in the next season was for the first time in history in danger of being demoted to the third tier, however the club won the relegation play-offs against <a href="/wiki/Stal_Rzesz%C3%B3w" title="Stal Rzeszów">Stal Rzeszów</a> (1:1, 2:0). In 2005 the club was restructured as <a href="/wiki/Joint-stock_company" title="Joint-stock company">spółka akcyjna</a>. The Blues won the promotion to the top flight in the season 2006/2007. In 2009 the side reached Polish Cup final, the next year The Blues finished third in the league. The best season in the recent history was <a href="/wiki/2011%E2%80%9312_Ekstraklasa" title="2011–12 Ekstraklasa">2011/2012</a> when Ruch was vice-champion (only 1 point behind the champions, <a href="/wiki/%C5%9Al%C4%85sk_Wroc%C5%82aw" title="Śląsk Wrocław">Śląsk Wrocław</a>) and reached the final of the national cup (lost 0:3 against <a href="/wiki/Legia_Warsaw" title="Legia Warsaw">Legia Warsaw</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Supporters_and_rivalries">Supporters and rivalries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruch_Chorz%C3%B3w&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Supporters and rivalries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:S7007632.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/S7007632.JPG/200px-S7007632.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/S7007632.JPG/300px-S7007632.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/S7007632.JPG/400px-S7007632.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:S7007632.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Derby against Górnik Zabrze (2008)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Ruch's popularity exploded in the 1930s and remained strong up to this date, especially in Upper Silesia. A specific subculture of <i>szalikowcy</i> (the name derived from <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/szalik" title="wiktionary:szalik">szalik</a> - <i>scarf</i>) developed in the 1970s, as alsewhere in the country, but in the region only after it first appeared among the fans of <a href="/wiki/Polonia_Bytom" title="Polonia Bytom">Polonia Bytom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historiakibicow_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-historiakibicow-13">[12]</a></sup> There are also <a href="/wiki/Football_hooliganism_in_Poland" title="Football hooliganism in Poland">hooligans</a> (<i>Psycho Fans</i>, formed in the mid 1990s) and <a href="/wiki/Ultras" title="Ultras">ultras</a> („Nucleo Ultra '03" from 2003, replaced by „Ultras Niebiescy”, formed in 2008).</p>
<p>Expressions of Upper Silesian identity are often displayed with golden-blue flagues, on banners (like controversial „Oberschlesien”,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> now banned, or „To my Naród Śląski”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> - <i>It's us - Silesian Nation</i>) or in chants.</p>
<p>Ruch's supporters maintain friendships with fans of <a href="/wiki/Widzew_%C5%81%C3%B3d%C5%BA" title="Widzew Łódź">Widzew Łódź</a> (since 2005), <a href="/wiki/Elana_Toru%C5%84" title="Elana Toruń">Elana Toruń</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atletico_Madrid" title="Atletico Madrid">Atletico Madrid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> The biggest animosity is held against followers <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B3rnik_Zabrze" title="Górnik Zabrze">Górnik Zabrze</a> (<a href="/wiki/List_of_derbies_in_Poland#Silesian_Voivodeship" title="List of derbies in Poland">The Great Silesian Derby</a>), <a href="/wiki/GKS_Katowice" title="GKS Katowice">GKS Katowice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polonia_Bytom" title="Polonia Bytom">Polonia Bytom</a> (the oldest Silesian derby<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup>), <a href="/wiki/Zag%C5%82%C4%99bie_Sosnowiec" title="Zagłębie Sosnowiec">Zagłębie Sosnowiec</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legia_Warszawa" title="Legia Warszawa">Legia Warszawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lech_Pozna%C5%84" title="Lech Poznań">Lech Poznań</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historiakibicow_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-historiakibicow-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
