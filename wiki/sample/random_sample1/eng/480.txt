<p>The <b>2014 Winter Paralympics</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Зимние Паралимпийские игры 2014</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Russian" title="Romanization of Russian">tr.</a></small> <i>Zimniye Paralimpiyskiye igry 2014</i>), the eleventh <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paralympic_Winter_Games" title="Paralympic Winter Games">Paralympic Winter Games</a>, and also more generally known as the <b>Sochi 2014 Paralympic Winter Games</b>, were a major international <a href="/wiki/Multi-sport_event" title="Multi-sport event">multi-sport event</a> for <a href="/wiki/Disabled_sports" title="Disabled sports">athletes with disabilities</a> governed by the <a href="/wiki/International_Paralympic_Committee" title="International Paralympic Committee">International Paralympic Committee</a> (IPC), held in <a href="/wiki/Sochi" title="Sochi">Sochi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krasnodar_Krai" title="Krasnodar Krai">Krasnodar Krai</a>, Russia from 7–16 March 2014. 45 <a href="/wiki/National_Paralympic_Committee" title="National Paralympic Committee">National Paralympic Committees</a> (NPCs) participated in the Games, which marked the first time Russia ever hosted the Paralympics. The Games featured 72 medal events in five sports, and saw the debut of <a href="/wiki/Snowboarding" title="Snowboarding">snowboarding</a> at the Winter Paralympics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The lead-up to these Paralympics were met with concerns regarding Russia's recent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2014_Russian_military_intervention_in_Ukraine" title="2014 Russian military intervention in Ukraine">military intervention</a> in the nearby <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_Republic_of_Crimea" title="Autonomous Republic of Crimea">Crimean</a> peninsula of <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, which led to both the United Kingdom and United States choosing not to send governmental delegations to the Games, and British government officials intending to publicly boycott the games. While the IPC monitored the situation throughout the Games, these concerns did not affect the participation of athletes from these countries—although, the head of Ukraine's NPC threatened to pull its athletes from the Games if the situation escalated, and members of the Ukrainian team staged symbolic protests of the crisis throughout the Games.</p>
<p>With 80 medals, 30 of them being gold, the host country of Russia won the most medals during these Games, and set a record for the most medals won by a single country during a single Winter Paralympic Games. Additionally, Russian skier <a href="/wiki/Roman_Petushkov" title="Roman Petushkov">Roman Petushkov</a> won the most individual gold medals in Winter Paralympic history, with six golds across skiing and biathlon events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc-romanrecord_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-romanrecord-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rt-finalresults_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-rt-finalresults-3">[3]</a></sup> However, following the Games, the IPC discovered evidence that Russia's performance may have been aided by a wider <a href="/wiki/Doping_in_Russia" title="Doping in Russia">state-sponsored doping program</a>.</p>

<p>As with previous Paralympics, the 2014 Winter Paralympics used most of the same venues that were used by the Olympics, situated within the <a href="/wiki/Sochi_Olympic_Park" title="Sochi Olympic Park">Sochi Olympic Park</a> and the resort of <a href="/wiki/Krasnaya_Polyana,_Sochi,_Krasnodar_Krai" title="Krasnaya Polyana, Sochi, Krasnodar Krai">Krasnaya Polyana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup> Despite the IPC believing that Russia had "zero track record in terms of <a href="/wiki/Accessibility" title="Accessibility">accessibility</a>"—given that the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> passed on hosting the <a href="/wiki/1980_Summer_Paralympics" title="1980 Summer Paralympics">1980 Summer Paralympics</a> after claiming no disabled athletes lived in Russia, the venues were built to the IPC's standards, organizers were quick to fix some of the issues pointed out by officials during an audit of the venues in January 2014, and an IPC spokesperson stated that they were "confident and expect everything to be ready for Games time." Organizers and the IPC hoped that the Paralympics' legacy would make Sochi a role model for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclusive_design" title="Inclusive design">inclusive design</a> throughout the country, and IPC president <a href="/wiki/Philip_Craven" title="Philip Craven">Philip Craven</a> went as far as dubbing Sochi the first accessible city in Russia, commending the country's changed stance on disability since 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc-venues_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-venues-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian-ukraineoc_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-ukraineoc-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sochi_Olympic_Park_.28Coastal_Cluster.29">Sochi Olympic Park (Coastal Cluster)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2014_Winter_Paralympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sochi Olympic Park (Coastal Cluster)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sochi_Olympic_Park" title="Sochi Olympic Park">Sochi Olympic Park</a></div>
<p>The Sochi Olympic Park was built by the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> coast in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imeretin_Valley" title="Imeretin Valley">Imeretin Valley</a>, about 4 km (2.5 miles) from Russia's <a href="/wiki/Abkhazia%E2%80%93Russia_border" title="Abkhazia–Russia border">border</a> with <a href="/wiki/Abkhazia" title="Abkhazia">Abkhazia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The venues are clustered around a central water basin with a synchronized fountain, allowing all indoor venues to be within walking distance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Off these, the venues used during the Paralympics included:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fisht_Olympic_Stadium" title="Fisht Olympic Stadium">Fisht Olympic Stadium</a> – ceremonies (opening/closing) 40,000 spectators</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shayba_Arena" title="Shayba Arena">Shayba Arena</a> – sledge hockey, 7,000 spectators</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube_Curling_Center" title="Ice Cube Curling Center">Ice Cube Curling Center</a> – wheelchair curling, 3,000 spectators</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mascots">Mascots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2014_Winter_Paralympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mascots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>A shortlist of 10 Olympic and 3 Paralympic designs were shown to the public on 7 February 2011, while the winners were revealed on 26 February 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The Paralympic mascots, Ray of Light and Snowflake, represented <a href="/wiki/Contrast_(literary)" title="Contrast (literary)">contrasts</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclusion_(value_and_practice)" title="Inclusion (value and practice)">inclusivity</a>: their fictional backstories portrayed them as having respectively come to Earth from a hot <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> and a cold <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a>. After Ray of Light overcame his struggle to adapt to a winter environment, he bonded with Snowflake through a shared passion for winter sports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipc-newlook_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipc-newlook-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Games">The Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2014_Winter_Paralympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opening_ceremony">Opening ceremony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2014_Winter_Paralympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Opening ceremony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/2014_Winter_Paralympics_opening_ceremony" title="2014 Winter Paralympics opening ceremony">2014 Winter Paralympics opening ceremony</a> and <a href="/wiki/2014_Winter_Paralympics_Parade_of_Nations" title="2014 Winter Paralympics Parade of Nations">2014 Winter Paralympics Parade of Nations</a></div>
<p>The opening ceremony of the Games were held at <a href="/wiki/Fisht_Olympic_Stadium" title="Fisht Olympic Stadium">Fisht Olympic Stadium</a> on 7 March 2014. Entitled "Breaking the Ice", the ceremony was themed around equality and "breaking" barriers, featuring ballet sequences set to music by notable Russian composers, and animated segments by <a href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Petrov_(animator)" title="Aleksandr Petrov (animator)">Aleksandr Petrov</a> based on the story of the <a href="/wiki/Firebird_(Slavic_folklore)" title="Firebird (Slavic folklore)">firebird</a>. During the parade of nations, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine_at_the_2014_Winter_Paralympics" title="Ukraine at the 2014 Winter Paralympics">Ukraine</a>'s athletes declined to participate in protest of the <a href="#Crimean_crisis">situation in Crimea</a>, and was represented solely by its flagbearer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mykailo_Tkachenko&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mykailo Tkachenko (page does not exist)">Mykailo Tkachenko</a>. In his opening speech, IPC president <a href="/wiki/Philip_Craven" title="Philip Craven">Philip Craven</a> praised Russia for finally fulfilling its "dream" of hosting the Paralympics after previously passing on the opportunity in 1980. He called upon spectators to have a "barrier-free mind", stating that "the sport you witness here will change you. Not just for now, but forever." The Paralympic cauldron was jointly lit by Russian Paralympians <a href="/wiki/Olesya_Vladykina" title="Olesya Vladykina">Olesya Vladykina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sergey_Shilov" title="Sergey Shilov">Sergey Shilov</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBC_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBC-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rt-oc2014_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-rt-oc2014-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Participating_National_Paralympic_Committees">Participating National Paralympic Committees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2014_Winter_Paralympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Participating National Paralympic Committees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Athletes representing forty-five <a href="/wiki/National_Paralympic_Committee" title="National Paralympic Committee">National Paralympic Committees</a> (NPCs) competed at the 2014 Winter Paralympics, an increase of one NPC over 2010.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Brazil_at_the_Paralympics" title="Brazil at the Paralympics">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey_at_the_Paralympics" title="Turkey at the Paralympics">Turkey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan_at_the_Paralympics" title="Uzbekistan at the Paralympics">Uzbekistan</a> made their Winter Paralympic debut in Sochi, while <a href="/wiki/Hungary_at_the_Paralympics" title="Hungary at the Paralympics">Hungary</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa_at_the_Paralympics" title="South Africa at the Paralympics">South Africa</a>, who participated in Vancouver, did not send any athletes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2014_Winter_Paralympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Competitions in the 2014 Winter Paralympics were held in five <a href="/wiki/Paralympic_sports#Winter_Paralympics" title="Paralympic sports">Winter Paralympic sports</a>, with 72 medal events in total. New for 2014 was the addition of <a href="/wiki/Snowboarding" title="Snowboarding">snowboarding</a> events, which were incorporated into the alpine skiing program.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Closing_ceremony">Closing ceremony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2014_Winter_Paralympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Closing ceremony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2014_Winter_Paralympics_closing_ceremony" title="2014 Winter Paralympics closing ceremony">2014 Winter Paralympics closing ceremony</a></div>
<p>The closing ceremony of the 2014 Winter Paralympics was held on 16 March 2014 at Fisht Olympic Stadium. Entitled "Reaching the Impossible", the ceremony was themed around inclusivity, and featured sequences paying tribute to Russian <a href="/wiki/Abstract_art" title="Abstract art">abstract artist</a> <a href="/wiki/Wassily_Kandinsky" title="Wassily Kandinsky">Wassily Kandinsky</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alexey_Pajitnov" title="Alexey Pajitnov">Alexey Pajitnov</a>'s classic <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Tetris" title="Tetris">Tetris</a></i>. During his closing remarks, deputy Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Kozak" title="Dmitry Kozak">Dmitry Kozak</a> stated that the Games had become a "catalyst for our efforts to create a barrier-free environment in Russia", and promised that "this important work will continue throughout our vast country." Sir Phillip Craven congratulated Russia for hosting what he deemed to be the best Winter Paralympic Games, and stated that the Games' participants had "shown the world that absolutely anything is possible and that life is about amazing capabilities and not perceived deficiencies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipc-sochi2014cc_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipc-sochi2014cc-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RIA-closing_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-RIA-closing-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc-closing_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbc-closing-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medals">Medals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2014_Winter_Paralympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Medals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_2014_Winter_Paralympics_medal_winners" title="List of 2014 Winter Paralympics medal winners">List of 2014 Winter Paralympics medal winners</a></div>
<p>Sochi's Paralympic medal design was unveiled in May 2013 alongside its Olympic equivalent. The design incorporates a "<a href="/wiki/Patchwork_quilt" title="Patchwork quilt">patchwork quilt</a>" of diamonds, some of which are translucent, containing designs that reflect Russia's regions, and also contain inscriptions in <a href="/wiki/Braille" title="Braille">braille</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olympicorg-14medals_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-olympicorg-14medals-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paralympic.org-14medals_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-paralympic.org-14medals-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Broadcasting">Broadcasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2014_Winter_Paralympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Broadcasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In February 2013, following its successful broadcast of the <a href="/wiki/2012_Summer_Paralympics" title="2012 Summer Paralympics">2012 Summer Paralympics</a>, British broadcaster <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> announced on February 2014 that it had obtained the broadcast rights to further Games, including the 2014 Winter Paralympics, and <a href="/wiki/2016_Summer_Paralympics" title="2016 Summer Paralympics">2016 Summer Paralympics</a>. Channel 4 planned to broadcast more than 45 hours of coverage from Sochi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph-c4-2016_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-c4-2016-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Australian coverage was provided by the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Australian Broadcasting Corporation">Australian Broadcasting Corporation</a>, including live online streaming. However, aside from the opening ceremony (which was broadcast live), television coverage was limited to a daily half-hour highlights show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tvtn-sochiaus_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-tvtn-sochiaus-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, <a href="/wiki/NBC_Sports" title="NBC Sports">NBC Sports</a> acquired the rights to the 2014 and 2016 Paralympics in September 2013. Following criticism of its minimal coverage of past Paralympics (such as at the <a href="/wiki/2012_Summer_Paralympics" title="2012 Summer Paralympics">2012 Summer Paralympics</a>, where in contrast to its extensive <a href="/wiki/2012_Summer_Olympics" title="2012 Summer Olympics">Olympics</a> coverage, it broadcast only five-and-a-half hours of highlights), <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> announced plans for a total of 52 hours of coverage during the Games, with at least 27 hours of coverage broadcast live, and live coverage of the opening ceremony. The majority of its coverage was relegated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NBC_Sports_Network" title="NBC Sports Network">NBC Sports Network</a>, but NBC aired highlights of the opening ceremony the following afternoon, and live coverage of the sledge hockey finals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usoc-nbccoverage_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-usoc-nbccoverage-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph-nbcparalympics_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-nbcparalympics-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/CBC_Sports" title="CBC Sports">CBC Sports</a>, in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Paralympic_Committee" title="Canadian Paralympic Committee">Canadian Paralympic Committee</a>, produced and syndicated television and digital coverage of the Games in Canada for <a href="/wiki/CBC_Television" title="CBC Television">CBC Television</a> (30 hours), <a href="/wiki/Ici_Radio-Canada_T%C3%A9l%C3%A9" title="Ici Radio-Canada Télé">Ici Radio-Canada Télé</a> (French, 7 hours), <a href="/wiki/Sportsnet" title="Sportsnet">Sportsnet</a>, <a href="/wiki/AMI-tv" title="AMI-tv">AMI-tv</a> (coverage with open <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Described_video" title="Described video">described video</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Sports" title="Yahoo! Sports">Yahoo! Sports</a> Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bc-cbcpara_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-bc-cbcpara-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concerns_and_controversies">Concerns and controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2014_Winter_Paralympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Concerns and controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Concerns_and_controversies_at_the_2014_Winter_Olympics" title="Concerns and controversies at the 2014 Winter Olympics">Concerns and controversies at the 2014 Winter Olympics</a></div>
<p>Although concerns over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LGBT_rights" title="LGBT rights">LGBT rights</a> still had a minor effect on the Games, they were overshadowed by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2014_Crimean_crisis" title="2014 Crimean crisis">crisis in the Ukrainian region of Crimea</a> which began during the Olympics, but became more severe immediately prior to the Paralympics.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crimean_crisis">Crimean crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2014_Winter_Paralympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Crimean crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Annexation_of_Crimea_by_the_Russian_Federation" title="Annexation of Crimea by the Russian Federation">Annexation of Crimea by the Russian Federation</a></div>
<p>On 27–28 February 2014 in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/2014_Ukrainian_revolution" title="2014 Ukrainian revolution">Ukrainian revolution</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2014_Russian_military_intervention_in_Ukraine" title="2014 Russian military intervention in Ukraine">Russian troops seized control</a> of most of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crimean_Peninsula" title="Crimean Peninsula">Crimean</a> peninsula of <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> located on the northern coast of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>—roughly 480 kilometres (300 mi) from Sochi,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc--noboycott_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc--noboycott-27">[27]</a></sup> including civil buildings, airports, and military bases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> On 1 March 2014, the Russian legislature approved the use of the Russian military in Ukraine, and Russian officials stated that their military forces in Crimea were not a breach of existing agreements between Russia and Ukraine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The Ukrainian response has been muted trying to find a diplomatic solution, with no military action on the part of Ukraine's government, which was formed in <a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">Kiev</a> less than a week before the intervention. Russia vowed troops would stay until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2014_Crimean_crisis" title="2014 Crimean crisis">political situation</a> was "normalised".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-30">[30]</a></sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crimean_referendum,_2014" title="Crimean referendum, 2014">referendum</a> on whether Crimea would separate from Ukraine was held on 16 March 2014—the same day as the closing ceremony of these Games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian-crimeareferendum_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-crimeareferendum-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The effects of these developments led to several notable political actions surrounding the Paralympics. On 2 March 2014, British Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> announced that British government officials were planning to boycott the 2014 Winter Paralympics in response to the situation in Crimea, while <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward,_Earl_of_Wessex" title="Prince Edward, Earl of Wessex">Prince Edward</a> cancelled plans to travel to Sochi for the Games "on the advice of government."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-ukboycott_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-ukboycott-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-independent-ukboycott_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-independent-ukboycott-33">[33]</a></sup> On 3 March 2014, <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Security_Council" title="United States National Security Council">United States National Security Council</a> spokeswoman Caitlin Hayden announced that the United States would not send a presidential delegation to Sochi (which was to be led by <a href="/wiki/Tammy_Duckworth" title="Tammy Duckworth">Tammy Duckworth</a>) at all, "in addition to other measures we are taking in response to the situation in Ukraine." Neither of the boycotts affected the participation of their respective athletes in the Games; in particular, Hayden went on to say that "<a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">President Obama</a> continues to strongly support all of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_at_the_2014_Winter_Paralympics" title="United States at the 2014 Winter Paralympics">U.S. athletes</a> who will participate in the Paralympics and wishes them great success".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-ukboycott_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-ukboycott-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usatoday-usboycott_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday-usboycott-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>IPC communications head Craig Spence reported prior to the Games that there were not any boycott efforts among athletes, and stated that although the organization would keep an eye on the situation in the Crimea, "we've obviously expressed our disappointment with what's going on in the political situation, but at the end of the day, we're not here to do politics. We're here to organize a sporting event." He went on to emphasize that the same level of <a href="/wiki/2014_Winter_Olympics#Security" title="2014 Winter Olympics">security measures</a> in place during the Olympics were in place during the Paralympics, ensuring that Sochi would be the "safest place" in the country during the Games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc--noboycott_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc--noboycott-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc-sochisecurity_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbc-sochisecurity-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Ukraine team still participated in the Games, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valeriy_Sushkevich" title="Valeriy Sushkevich">Valeriy Sushkevich</a>, head of the country's National Paralympic Committee, warned that "if there is an escalation of the conflict, intervention on the territory of our country, God forbid the worst, we would not be able to stay here. We would go." Similarly to the Olympics, political protests and statements among athletes were forbidden during Paralympic events, although two major, symbolic protests by the Ukrainian team occurred during the Games. During the opening ceremonies, all but one of <a href="/wiki/Ukraine_at_the_2014_Winter_Paralympics" title="Ukraine at the 2014 Winter Paralympics">the country's 31 athletes</a> declined to participate in the parade of nations. The country was represented solely by Nordic skier and flagbearer Mykailo Tkachenko, whose entrance was greeted with an ovation from the audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian-ukraineoc_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-ukraineoc-7">[7]</a></sup> Some Ukrainian medalists were seen covering their medal with their hand during ceremonies as another form of silent protest; Sushkevich stated that the covering was "a demonstration that aggression and the high ideals of Paralympic sports are incompatible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-qz-medalcover_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-qz-medalcover-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian_doping_scandal">Russian doping scandal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2014_Winter_Paralympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Russian doping scandal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 18 July 2016, the <a href="/wiki/World_Anti-Doping_Agency" title="World Anti-Doping Agency">World Anti-Doping Agency</a> published a report covering significant state-sponsored <a href="/wiki/Doping_in_Russia" title="Doping in Russia">doping in Russia</a>. It found that Russia's <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Sport_(Russia)" title="Ministry of Sport (Russia)">Ministry of Sport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Federal_Security_Service" title="Federal Security Service">Federal Security Service</a> (FSB) had operated a "state-dictated" system to implement an extensive doping program across the Olympics and Paralympics, and engaged in swapping to cover up positive urine samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The IPC found evidence that swapping of positive samples had occurred during the 2014 Winter Paralympics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT160718_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT160718-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT160512_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT160512-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian-ipcban_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-ipcban-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcsport-russiabanned_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbcsport-russiabanned-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 7 August 2016, the International Paralympic Committee announced that it had suspended the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Paralympic_Committee" title="Russian Paralympic Committee">Russian Paralympic Committee</a>, and that the country would be banned from the <a href="/wiki/2016_Summer_Paralympics" title="2016 Summer Paralympics">2016 Summer Paralympics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcsport-russiabanned_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbcsport-russiabanned-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPC160807_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPC160807-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LGBT_rights">LGBT rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2014_Winter_Paralympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: LGBT rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Concerns surrounding <a href="/wiki/LGBT_rights_in_Russia" title="LGBT rights in Russia">LGBT rights in Russia</a> and the country's <a href="/wiki/Russian_gay_propaganda_law" title="Russian gay propaganda law">"gay propaganda" law</a> continued into the Paralympics. On 4 February 2014, Norway's openly gay health minister <a href="/wiki/Bent_H%C3%B8ie" title="Bent Høie">Bent Høie</a> announced he would attend the Games with his husband, as is common for cabinet officials to travel with their spouses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gnhm_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-gnhm-43">[43]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_Gay_Games" title="Federation of Gay Games">Federation of Gay Games</a> also started a petition calling for IPC president <a href="/wiki/Philip_Craven" title="Philip Craven">Philip Craven</a> not to attend the Games if Russian authorities did not allow the Russian Open Games, a multi-sport event for LGBT athletes which organizers attempted to hold prior to the Paralympics, to occur. These games were faced with a bomb threat, and its venues arbitrarily pulling out of providing their services for events, which was believed to be a result of government pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blade-postolympic_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-blade-postolympic-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
