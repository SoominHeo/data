<p>The <b>Chamber of Deputies</b> (<a href="/wiki/Luxembourgish_language" title="Luxembourgish language">Luxembourgish</a>: <span lang="lb"><i>D'Chamber</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr"><i>Chambre des Députés</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de"><i>Abgeordnetenkammer</i></span>), abbreviated to the <b>Chamber</b>, is the <a href="/wiki/Unicameralism" title="Unicameralism">unicameral</a> national <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislature</a> of <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>. <i>Krautmaart</i> (French: <i>Marché aux herbes</i>, English: "Herb Market") is sometimes used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metonym" title="Metonym">metonym</a> for the Chamber, after the <a href="/wiki/Krautmaart" title="Krautmaart">square</a> on which the <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B4tel_de_la_Chambre" title="Hôtel de la Chambre">Hôtel de la Chambre</a> (Luxembourgish: <i>Chambergebai</i>, English: "Hall of the Chamber of Deputies") is located.</p>
<p>The Chamber is made up of 60 seats. Deputies are elected to serve five-year terms by <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a> in four multi-seat <a href="/wiki/Legislative_circonscriptions_(Luxembourg)" title="Legislative circonscriptions (Luxembourg)">constituencies</a>. Voters may vote for as many candidates as the constituency elects deputies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chamber_of_Deputies_(Luxembourg)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The constitution of 1841 created the <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_Estates" title="Assembly of Estates">Assembly of Estates</a> (<i>Assemblée des États</i>), consisting of 34 members. Under the absolute monarchy of <a href="/wiki/William_II_of_the_Netherlands" title="William II of the Netherlands">William II</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_the_Netherlands" title="King of the Netherlands">King of the Netherlands</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Duke_of_Luxembourg" title="Grand Duke of Luxembourg">Grand Duke of Luxembourg</a>, the legislature's powers were very restricted: it could not take decisions and had a purely advisory role with respect to the monarch. Its consent was necessary in very few matters. Only the sovereign could propose laws. The assembly was only in session 15 days a year, and these sessions were held in secret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a climate marked by the democratic revolutionary movements in France and elsewhere, a new constitution was drafted in 1848 by a <a href="/wiki/Constituent_Assembly_of_Luxembourg" title="Constituent Assembly of Luxembourg">Constituent Assembly</a>. This introduced a constitutional monarchy: the King-Grand Duke only retained those powers specifically enumerated in the Constitution. The parliament, now called the <i>Chambre des Députés</i>, had the legislative power: it had the right to propose and amend laws. It would decide the budget, and received the power to investigate. The government became accountable to the Chamber. In addition, its sessions were now public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1853, <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_the_Netherlands" title="William III of the Netherlands">William III</a> called on the government to write a new constitution to limit the powers of the Chamber. The latter refused to approve the government's revisions, and the Grand Duke dissolved the legislature. There was then a brief return to absolutist monarchy, in what became known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Putsch_of_1856" title="Putsch of 1856">Putsch of 1856</a>. The parliament, now renamed the <i>Assemblée des Etats</i>, retained its legislative powers, but the Grand Duke was no longer required to approve and promulgate its laws within a certain period. Taxes no longer had to be voted on annually, and the permanent budget was re-introduced. The Council of State was created in 1856 as a check on the Chamber. Its role was to render opinions on proposed bills and regulations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Luxembourg's neutrality and independence had been affirmed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Treaty_of_London" title="Second Treaty of London">Second Treaty of London</a>, in 1868, the constitution was revised to obtain a compromise between the liberties of 1848 and the authoritarian charter of 1856. The parliament was renamed the <i>Chambre des Députés</i> and regained most of the rights it lost in 1856, such as the annual vote on the budget and taxes. However, the King Grand-Duke still kept wide-ranging powers: he exercised executive power, and wielded legislative power alongside the Chamber.</p>
<p>The constitutional changes of 1919 brought in universal suffrage and affirmed the principle of national sovereignty. These steps on a pathway of democratisation took place in a period of crisis of the monarchy, famine, and difficulties in supplying food. Grand Duchess Charlotte remained the head of state, and the co-wielder of legislative power.</p>
<p>During World War II, from 1940 to 1944 under German occupation of Luxembourg, the Chamber was dissolved by the Nazis and the country annexed under the name "Gau Moselland". The Grand Ducal family and the Luxembourgish government went into exile (at first to the United Kingdom, then to Canada and the United States).</p>
<p>The first post-war session was opened on 6 December 1944 and was limited to one public sitting, as there was no quorum. A consultative assembly sat from March to August 1945, and new elections were held in October 1945. The post-war Chamber proceeded to revise the constitution again, which abolished the country's state of neutrality.</p>
<p>1965 saw the introduction of parliamentary commissions. The establishment of specialised and permanent commissions would facilitate the work of the legislature. The previous organisation of the Chamber into sections, un-specialised and with members chosen at random, had not been effective. Another innovation concerned political groups. They were now officially recognised, and received premises, and subsidies based on their proportion of seat. These material means were dwarfed by those established in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Changes to the Chamber's rules in 1990 and 1991 substantially increased the material means available to political groups, and contributed to a professionalisation of politics. In addition, every Deputy had the right to an office close to the Chamber building. The Chamber reimbursed the Deputies' staff expenses. Funds were now also available to "technical groups", following the protests of the small parties at the start of the new session in 1989.</p>
<p>In 2003, a new law established the office of the mediator and ombudsman. This was attached to the Chamber, but would not receive instructions from any authority in exercising his or her functions. They would deal with citizens' complaints concerning the central or local government administration, and other public entities. They would attempt to resolve disputes between parties, acting as a mediator. Every year, they would present a report to the Chamber.</p>
<p>Since January 2008, the political parties have been directly funded by the state. Their accounts were to be strictly separate from those of the parliamentary political groups. There were to be two different structures, each with their own staff. In order to receive public funds, a party must provide evidence of regular political activity, present complete lists of candidates at the legislative and European elections, and have received at least 2% of the vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions">Functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chamber_of_Deputies_(Luxembourg)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The function of the Chamber of Deputies is covered under Chapter IV of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Luxembourg" title="Constitution of Luxembourg">Constitution of Luxembourg</a>, the first article of which states that the purpose of the Chamber is to <a href="/wiki/Representative_democracy" title="Representative democracy">represent</a> the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Luxembourg is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliamentary_democracy" title="Parliamentary democracy">parliamentary democracy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> in which the Chamber is elected by <a href="/wiki/Universal_suffrage" title="Universal suffrage">universal suffrage</a> under the <a href="/wiki/D%27Hondt_method" title="D'Hondt method">d'Hondt method</a> of <a href="/wiki/Party-list_proportional_representation" title="Party-list proportional representation">Party-list proportional representation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>All laws must be passed by the Chamber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Each bill must be submitted to two votes in the Chamber, with an interval of at least three months between the votes, for it to become law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Laws are passed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_majority" title="Absolute majority">absolute majority</a>, provided that a <a href="/wiki/Quorum" title="Quorum">quorum</a> of half of the deputies is present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chamber_of_Deputies_(Luxembourg)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Chamber is composed of sixty members, called <b><a href="/wiki/Deputy_(legislator)" title="Deputy (legislator)">Deputies</a></b> (Luxembourgish: <i>Deputéiert</i> ; French: <i>Députés</i>). They each represent one of <a href="/wiki/Legislative_circonscriptions_(Luxembourg)" title="Legislative circonscriptions (Luxembourg)">four constituencies</a>, which are each a combination of at least two <a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_Luxembourg" title="Cantons of Luxembourg">cantons</a>. Each constituency elects a number of deputies proportionate to its population, with the largest electing 23 and the smallest electing 7.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elections">Elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chamber_of_Deputies_(Luxembourg)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electoral_system">Electoral system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chamber_of_Deputies_(Luxembourg)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Electoral system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Deputies are elected by <a href="/wiki/Universal_suffrage" title="Universal suffrage">universal suffrage</a> every five years, with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luxembourgian_legislative_election,_2013" title="Luxembourgian legislative election, 2013">last election</a> having been held on 20 October 2013. Deputies are elected by <a href="/wiki/Open_list" title="Open list">open list</a> <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a>, whereby all electors may vote for as many candidates as their constituency has seats. Each party is allocated a number of seats in proportion to the total number of votes cast for its candidates in that constituency. These seats are then allocated to that party's candidates in descending order of votes that each candidate received.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="H.C3.B4tel_de_la_Chambre">Hôtel de la Chambre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chamber_of_Deputies_(Luxembourg)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hôtel de la Chambre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B4tel_de_la_Chambre" title="Hôtel de la Chambre">Hôtel de la Chambre</a></div>
<p>The Chamber of Deputies holds session in the <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B4tel_de_la_Chambre" title="Hôtel de la Chambre">Hôtel de la Chambre</a> (Luxembourgish: <i>Chambergebai</i>, English: Hall of the Chamber of Deputies), located on <a href="/wiki/Krautmaart" title="Krautmaart">Krautmaart</a> (French: <i>Marché aux herbes</i>, English: Herb Market), in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Uewerstad&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Uewerstad (page does not exist)">Uewerstad</a> quarter (French: <a href="/wiki/Ville_Haute" title="Ville Haute">Ville Haute</a>, English: Upper City), the oldest part of <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg_City" title="Luxembourg City">Luxembourg City</a>. It was originally built between 1858 and 1860 as an annex to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Ducal_Palace" title="Grand Ducal Palace">Grand Ducal Palace</a>, which had, until then, been used as one of many venues for the Chamber's convocations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H.C3.B4tel_de_la_Chambre_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-H.C3.B4tel_de_la_Chambre-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The building was designed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_Hartmann&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Antoine Hartmann (page does not exist)">Antoine Hartmann</a> in a unified <a href="/wiki/Historicism_(art)" title="Historicism (art)">historicist</a> style, combining elements of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-Gothic_architecture" title="Neo-Gothic architecture">neo-Gothic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-Renaissance" title="Neo-Renaissance">neo-Renaissance</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-classical_architecture" title="Neo-classical architecture">neo-classical</a> architectural genres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H.C3.B4tel_de_la_Chambre_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-H.C3.B4tel_de_la_Chambre-8">[8]</a></sup> The Grand Ducal Palace, by contrast, was built over time in several architectural styles (primarily <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">Renaissance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">Baroque</a>), but renovated in 1891 in a historicist neo-Renaissance manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
