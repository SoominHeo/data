<p><b>Lhuntse District</b> (<a href="/wiki/Dzongkha" title="Dzongkha">Dzongkha</a>: ལྷུན་རྩེ་རྫོང་ཁག་; <a href="/wiki/Wylie_transliteration" title="Wylie transliteration">Wylie</a>: <i>Lhun-rtse rdzong-khag</i>; previously "Lhuntshi") is one of the 20 <a href="/wiki/Dzongkhag" title="Dzongkhag">dzongkhag</a> (districts) comprising <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>. It consists of 2506 households.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Located in the northeast, Lhuntse is one of the least developed dzhongkhags of Bhutan. There are few roads, the first gas station was opened in September 2005, electricity is not well distributed, and the difficult terrain makes distribution of social welfare problematic. Despite its favourable climate, farming is hindered by the lack of infrastructure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FYP_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FYP-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lhuntse_District&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lhuntse is culturally part of eastern Bhutan. The <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Bhutan" title="Languages of Bhutan">languages</a> and lifestyle of its inhabitants may be contrasted against the dominant western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ngalop" title="Ngalop">Ngalop</a> culture.</p>
<p>This region is renowned as a textiles producing region and as the ancestral homeland of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Wangchuck" title="House of Wangchuck">Bhutanese royal family</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Essex_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Essex-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alcohol">Alcohol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lhuntse_District&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Alcohol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Eastern Bhutanese culture is distinctive in its high alcohol consumption in relation to other parts of Bhutan. <a href="/wiki/Ara_(drink)" title="Ara (drink)">Ara</a>, the traditional alcohol of Bhutan, is most often home made from <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> or <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>, either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(wine)" title="Fermentation (wine)">fermented</a> or <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distilled</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tri_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tri-4">[4]</a></sup> It may only be legally produced and consumed privately. Ara production is unregulated in method and quality. Its sale has been prohibited in Bhutan and enforced since a severe crackdown. However, because Ara returns far more profit than other forms of maize, many Bhutanese farmers have pressed for legal reform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BO1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BO1-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Bhutanese government, meanwhile, is intent on discouraging excessive alcohol consumption, abuse, and associated diseases through taxation and regulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Through government efforts to reduce <a href="/wiki/Ara_(drink)" title="Ara (drink)">ara</a> production and consumption in Lhuntse District, locals conceded in 2011 that something should be done to curb the distinctly eastern Bhutanese tradition of heavy drinking. The government's strategy is to reduce ara production and consumption gradually until it is eliminated. Alcoholism and ara production have been notable topics of political discussion Bhutan, especially at the local level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Ara, however, is culturally relevant for its religious and medicinal uses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2011, the government passed its Alcohol Control Regulation, which imposed up to three times the previous taxes on alcohol. As a result, alcohol sales have dropped and prices have risen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages">Languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lhuntse_District&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lhuntse is home to a variety of language groups. In the east, <a href="/wiki/Dzala_language" title="Dzala language">Dzala</a> an <a href="/wiki/East_Bodish_languages" title="East Bodish languages">East Bodish language</a>, is spoken. In southern Lhuntse, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chocangacakha" title="Chocangacakha">Chocangacakha</a>, a sister language to <a href="/wiki/Dzongkha" title="Dzongkha">Dzongkha</a>, is spoken. The northern and western parts of the district are known as the Kurtö region, where inhabitants speak the East Bodish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kurt%C3%B6p" title="Kurtöp">Kurtöp</a> language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vanDriem93_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-vanDriem93-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lhuntse_District&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most of Lhuntse District is part of the environmentally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protected_areas_of_Bhutan" title="Protected areas of Bhutan">protected areas of Bhutan</a>. The district contains parts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wangchuck_Centennial_Park" title="Wangchuck Centennial Park">Wangchuck Centennial Park</a> in the north (the <i>gewogs</i> of <a href="/wiki/Gangzur_Gewog" title="Gangzur Gewog">Gangzur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Khoma_Gewog" title="Khoma Gewog">Khoma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kurtoe_Gewog" title="Kurtoe Gewog">Kurtoe</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thrumshingla_National_Park" title="Thrumshingla National Park">Thrumshingla National Park</a> in the south (the <i>gewogs</i> of <a href="/wiki/Gangzur_Gewog" title="Gangzur Gewog">Gangzur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jaray_Gewog" title="Jaray Gewog">Jarey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metsho_Gewog" title="Metsho Gewog">Metsho</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Bumdeling_Wildlife_Sanctuary" title="Bumdeling Wildlife Sanctuary">Bumdeling Wildlife Sanctuary</a> in the east (the <i>gewogs</i> of <a href="/wiki/Khoma_Gewog" title="Khoma Gewog">Khoma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minjay_Gewog" title="Minjay Gewog">Minjay</a>). These three parks are connected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_corridor" title="Biological corridor">biological corridors</a> that crisscross the central and southern regions of the district.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BTF1_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-BTF1-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
