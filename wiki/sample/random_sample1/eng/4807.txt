<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lulism</h1>

<p><b>Lulism</b> (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><i>lulismo</i></span>) is a political ideology describing the 2006 consolidation of segments of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazilian</a> society previously hostile to <a href="/wiki/Social_movement" title="Social movement">social movements</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_Party_(Brazil)" title="Workers' Party (Brazil)">Workers' Party</a> behind political forces led by former president <a href="/wiki/Luiz_In%C3%A1cio_Lula_da_Silva" title="Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva">Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The controlled <a href="/wiki/Reformism" title="Reformism">reformism</a> and limited structural change focused on the poorest sections of society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brazil:_Is_.27Lulism.27_over.3F_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brazil:_Is_.27Lulism.27_over.3F-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Lumpenproletariat" title="Lumpenproletariat">lower classes</a>, who had distanced themselves from Lula, accepted his candidacy after his first term as president as the middle‑class turned from him. One explanation would be a new ideological configuration incorporating <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">left-</a> and <a href="/wiki/Right-wing_politics" title="Right-wing politics">right-wing</a> elements. The <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetoric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Praxis_(process)" title="Praxis (process)">praxis</a>, which united the maintenance of stability and state distributism are the origins of Lulism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Although distinct, it shares characteristics of <a href="/wiki/Chavism" title="Chavism">Chavism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kirchnerism" title="Kirchnerism">Kirchnerism</a>.</p>
<p>To build political consensus and secure social peace, Lulism has forged a coalition of interests across Brazilian society instead of pursuing an ideological agenda; gradual reformism was favored over direct confrontation with the elite. Brazilian manufacturers, banks and retailers benefited from the consumption-led, credit-fueled government economic model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECasanovaKassum201432_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECasanovaKassum201432-4">[4]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_V%C3%ADtor_Singer" title="André Vítor Singer">Andre Singer</a>, "The convergence of interests of the private industry sector on one side, and of the organized labor force on the other, led to the stability that allowed this political system to take the form of a sort of consensus".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This equilibrium allowed the government to gradually make significant changes in policy. Non-confrontation is, in the Lulism movement, a <i><a href="/wiki/Sine_qua_non" title="Sine qua non">sine qua non</a></i> for development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lulism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "Lulism" was coined by André Singer, Lula's press secretary from 2003 to 2005 and spokesperson during his presidency (from 2002 to 2007).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brazil:_Is_.27Lulism.27_over.3F_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brazil:_Is_.27Lulism.27_over.3F-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FSP.2F19-AGO-2012_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FSP.2F19-AGO-2012-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IG.2F30-SET-2012_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-IG.2F30-SET-2012-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Originating in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brazilian_presidential_election,_2002" title="Brazilian presidential election, 2002">2002 presidential campaign</a>, Lulism departed from the left-wing politics of the Workers Party until late 2001<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEBRAP.2F2009_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-CEBRAP.2F2009-10">[10]</a></sup> and abandoned the concepts of organization and mobilization. Since Lulism is a model of enforced change within order, mobilization is unnecessary and conflict is eliminated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IG.2F30-SET-2012_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-IG.2F30-SET-2012-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2009 article written for the <a href="/wiki/Instituto_Millenium" title="Instituto Millenium">Instituto Millenium</a> said that "liberals are cornered" after "more than six years of Lulism". <a href="/wiki/Patr%C3%ADcia_Carlos_de_Andrade" title="Patrícia Carlos de Andrade">Carlos de Andrade</a> adopted that view: "According to her, the term 'liberal' is mistranslated in Brazil as 'rightist' or 'supportive of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_dictatorships" title="Military dictatorships">military dictatorships</a>'. In the war for public opinion, the so-called left always got the better, Singer says".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lulism sought reconciliation between Lula's charisma and the large Brazilian conservative sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IG.2F30-SET-2012_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-IG.2F30-SET-2012-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VIOMUNDO.2F28-JUN-2013_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-VIOMUNDO.2F28-JUN-2013-12">[12]</a></sup> Ironically,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BF.2F03-JAN-2013_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-BF.2F03-JAN-2013-13">[13]</a></sup> it is a conservative social pact combining the economic policy of <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Henrique_Cardoso" title="Fernando Henrique Cardoso">Fernando Henrique Cardoso</a> (1995-2002) with the <a href="/wiki/Distributism" title="Distributism">distributive</a> policies of Lula's government (2002-2010).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FSP.2F19-AGO-2012_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FSP.2F19-AGO-2012-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under the auspices of conciliation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TEMPO-SOCIAL.2FNOV-2012_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-TEMPO-SOCIAL.2FNOV-2012-14">[14]</a></sup> Lulism represents an "appeasement of social conflicts, of which the <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">bourgeoisie</a> has always [been] too afraid, especially in a country of great inequality as is the case of Brazil" because it envisions a "reduction-agenda poverty and inequality, but under the aegis of a weak reformism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BF.2F03-JAN-2013_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-BF.2F03-JAN-2013-13">[13]</a></sup> This social-change model is explained as a "conservative variant of modernization" in which the state has a "prominent role in leveraging the poorest", ensuring that Brazilian social structural problems will not be touched (in other words, without conflicting with the financial interests of the conservative elite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TEMPO-SOCIAL.2FNOV-2012_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-TEMPO-SOCIAL.2FNOV-2012-14">[14]</a></sup> Lulism "concocted new ideological, under-union banners that seemed to combine" continuity of the Lula and Cardoso governments in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroeconomic" title="Macroeconomic">macroeconomic</a> policy based on three pillars: inflation control, a floating exchange rate and a budgetary surplus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEBRAP.2F2009_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-CEBRAP.2F2009-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another feature distinguishing Lulism as a political movement is its nonpartisan character. It overlaps the political parties, including the Workers Party founded by Lula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Although the movement was anchored in Lula's charisma, Lulism differs from other movements surrounding political leaders (such as <a href="/wiki/Peronism" title="Peronism">Peronism</a> in Argentina) in its lack of a <a href="/wiki/Cult_of_personality" title="Cult of personality">cult of personality</a> around the former Brazilian president.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy"><span id="Influences"></span>Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lulism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several Latin American politicians, such as <a href="/wiki/Ollanta_Humala" title="Ollanta Humala">Ollanta Humala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mujica" title="José Mujica">José Mujica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauricio_Funes" title="Mauricio Funes">Mauricio Funes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Lugo" title="Fernando Lugo">Fernando Lugo</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henrique_Capriles" title="Henrique Capriles">Henrique Capriles</a> have cited Lulism and <a href="/wiki/Chavism" title="Chavism">Chavism</a> as political models and alternatives to the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Consensus" title="Washington Consensus">Washington Consensus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CM.2F06-OUT.2F2012_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-CM.2F06-OUT.2F2012-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GLOBO.2F28-ABR-2012_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-GLOBO.2F28-ABR-2012-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
