<p>The <b>Rydberg formula</b> is used in <a href="/wiki/Atomic_physics" title="Atomic physics">atomic physics</a> to describe the wavelengths of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_lines" title="Spectral lines">spectral lines</a> of many <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical elements</a>. It was formulated by the Swedish <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Rydberg" title="Johannes Rydberg">Johannes Rydberg</a>, and presented on 5 November 1888.</p>
<p>In 1880, Rydberg worked on a formula describing the relation between the wavelengths in spectral lines of alkali metals. He noticed that lines came in series and he found that he could simplify his calculations by using the <a href="/wiki/Wavenumber" title="Wavenumber">wavenumber</a> (the number of waves occupying the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unit_length" title="Unit length">unit length</a>, equal to 1/<i>λ</i>, the inverse of the <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a>) as his unit of measurement. He plotted the wavenumbers (<i>n</i>) of successive lines in each series against consecutive integers which represented the order of the lines in that particular series. Finding that the resulting curves were similarly shaped, he sought a single function which could generate all of them, when appropriate constants were inserted.</p>

A frequency (or spectral energy) emitted in a transition from <i>n</i><sub>1</sub> to <i>n</i><sub>2</sub> therefore represents the photon energy emitted or absorbed when an electron makes a jump from orbital 1 to orbital 2.</p>
<p>Later models found that the values for <i>n</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>n</i><sub>2</sub> corresponded to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principal_quantum_numbers" title="Principal quantum numbers">principal quantum numbers</a> of the two orbitals.</p>

<p>It's important to notice that this formula can be directly applied only to hydrogen-like, also called <i>hydrogenic</i> atoms of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical elements</a>, i.e. atoms with only one electron being affected by an effective nuclear charge (which is easily estimated). Examples would include He<sup>+</sup>, Li<sup>2+</sup>, Be<sup>3+</sup> etc., where no other electrons exist in the atom.</p>
<p>But the Rydberg formula also provides correct wavelengths for distant electrons, where the effective nuclear charge can be estimated as the same as that for hydrogen, since all but one of the nuclear charges have been screened by other electrons, and the core of the atom has an effective positive charge of +1.</p>
<p>Finally, with certain modifications (replacement of <i><b>Z</b></i> by <b><i>Z</i>−1</b>, and use of the integers 1 and 2 for the <i>n</i>s to give a numerical value of <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span> for the difference of their inverse squares), the Rydberg formula provides correct values in the special case of <a href="/wiki/K-alpha" title="K-alpha">K-alpha</a> lines, since the transition in question is the K-alpha transition of the electron from the 1s orbital to the 2p orbital. This is analogous to the <a href="/wiki/Lyman-alpha_line" title="Lyman-alpha line">Lyman-alpha line</a> transition for hydrogen, and has the same frequency factor. Because the 2p electron is not screened by any other electrons in the atom from the nucleus, the nuclear charge is diminished only by the single remaining 1s electron, causing the system to be effectively a hydrogenic atom, but with a diminished nuclear charge <i>Z</i>−1. Its frequency is thus the Lyman-alpha hydrogen frequency, increased by a factor of (<i>Z</i>−1)<sup>2</sup>. This formula of <i>f</i> = <i>c</i>/<i>λ</i> = (Lyman-alpha frequency)⋅(<i>Z</i>−1)<sup>2</sup> is historically known as <a href="/wiki/Moseley%27s_law" title="Moseley's law">Moseley's law</a> (having added a factor <i><b>c</b></i> to convert wavelength to frequency), and can be used to predict wavelengths of the K<sub>α</sub> (K-alpha) X-ray spectral emission lines of chemical elements from aluminum to gold. See the biography of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Moseley" title="Henry Moseley">Henry Moseley</a> for the historical importance of this law, which was derived empirically at about the same time it was explained by the <a href="/wiki/Bohr_model" title="Bohr model">Bohr model</a> of the atom.</p>
<p>For other spectral transitions in multi-electron atoms, the Rydberg formula generally provides <i>incorrect</i> results, since the magnitude of the screening of inner electrons for outer-electron transitions is variable and not possible to compensate for in the simple manner above.</p>
