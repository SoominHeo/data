<p><b>Luc Besson</b> (<small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[lyk bɛsɔ̃]</a></span>; born 18 March 1959) is a French film director, screenwriter, and producer. He directed or produced the films <i><a href="/wiki/Subway_(film)" title="Subway (film)">Subway</a></i> (1985), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Blue" title="The Big Blue">The Big Blue</a></i> (1988), and <i><a href="/wiki/Nikita_(film)" title="Nikita (film)">Nikita</a></i> (1990), all using a distinctive style dubbed <i><a href="/wiki/Cin%C3%A9ma_du_look" title="Cinéma du look">Cinéma du look</a></i> by critics. Besson has been nominated for a <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Award" title="César Award">César Award</a> for Best Director and Best Picture for his films <i><a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on:_The_Professional" title="Léon: The Professional">Léon: The Professional</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Messenger:_The_Story_of_Joan_of_Arc" title="The Messenger: The Story of Joan of Arc">The Messenger: The Story of Joan of Arc</a></i>. He won Best Director and Best French Director for his sci-fi action film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fifth_Element" title="The Fifth Element">The Fifth Element</a></i> (1997). He wrote and directed the 2014 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sci-fi" title="Sci-fi">sci-fi</a> thriller film <i><a href="/wiki/Lucy_(2014_film)" title="Lucy (2014 film)">Lucy</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1980, he founded his own production company, called <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Les_Films_du_Loup&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Les Films du Loup (page does not exist)">Les Films du Loup</a>,</i> and later <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Les_Films_du_Dauphin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Les Films du Dauphin (page does not exist)">Les Films du Dauphin</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Les_Films_du_Dauphin" title="fr:Les Films du Dauphin">fr</a>)</span></i>. This was superseded in 2000 by his co-founding <a href="/wiki/EuropaCorp" title="EuropaCorp">EuropaCorp</a> film company with his longtime collaborator, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pierre-Ange_Le_Pogam&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pierre-Ange Le Pogam (page does not exist)">Pierre-Ange Le Pogam</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre-Ange_Le_Pogam" title="fr:Pierre-Ange Le Pogam">fr</a>)</span>. As writer, director, and/or producer, Besson has so far been involved in the creation of <a href="/wiki/Luc_Besson_filmography" title="Luc Besson filmography">more than 50 films</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luc_Besson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Besson was born in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, to parents who both worked as <a href="/wiki/Club_Med" title="Club Med">Club Med</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scuba-diving" title="Scuba-diving">scuba-diving</a> instructors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Influenced by this milieu, as a child Besson planned to become a <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology" title="Marine biology">marine biologist</a>. He spent much of his youth traveling with his parents to tourist resorts in Italy, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SFR_Yugoslavia" title="SFR Yugoslavia">SFR Yugoslavia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Greece" title="Kingdom of Greece">Kingdom of Greece</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The family returned to France when Besson was 10. His parents promptly divorced and each remarried.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Here there is two families, and I am the only bad souvenir of something that doesn't work," he said in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Herald_Tribune" title="International Herald Tribune">International Herald Tribune</a></i>. "And if I disappear, then everything is perfect. The rage to exist comes from here. I have to do something! Otherwise I am going to die."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>At the age of 17, Besson had a diving accident that left him unable to dive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hayward1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hayward1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>"I was 17 and I wondered what I was going to do. ... So I took a piece of paper and on the left I put everything I could do, or had skills for, and all the things I couldn't do. The first line was shorter and I could see that I loved writing, I loved images, I was taking a lot of pictures. So I thought maybe movies would be good. But I thought that to really know I should go to a set. And a friend of mine knew a guy whose brother was a third assistant on a short film. It's true," he said in a 2000 interview with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-film.guardian.co.uk_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-film.guardian.co.uk-5">[5]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>"So, I said: 'OK, let's go on the set.' So I went on the set...The day after I went back to see my mum and told her that I was going to make films and stop school and 'bye. And I did it! Very soon after I made a short film and it was very, very bad. I wanted to prove that I could do something, so I made a short film. That was in fact my main concern, to be able to show that I could do one."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-film.guardian.co.uk_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-film.guardian.co.uk-5">[5]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luc_Besson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Out of boredom, Besson started writing stories, including the background to what he later developed as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fifth_Element" title="The Fifth Element">The Fifth Element</a></i> (1997), one of his most popular movies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The film is inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Belgian_comics" title="Franco-Belgian comics">French comic books</a> which Besson read as a teenager. He reportedly worked on the first drafts of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_Grand_Bleu" title="Le Grand Bleu">Le Grand Bleu</a></i> while still in his teens. Besson directed and co-wrote the screenplay of this science fiction thriller with the screenwriter, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Mark_Kamen" title="Robert Mark Kamen">Robert Mark Kamen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luc_Besson_2000_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Luc_Besson_2000-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>At 18, Besson returned to his birthplace of Paris. There he took odd jobs in film to get a feel for the industry. He worked as an assistant to directors including <a href="/wiki/Claude_Faraldo" title="Claude Faraldo">Claude Faraldo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Grandperret" title="Patrick Grandperret">Patrick Grandperret</a>. Besson directed three short films, a commissioned documentary, and several commercials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>After this, he moved to the United States for three years, but returned to Paris, where he formed his own production company. He first named it <i>Les Films du Loup,</i> but changed it to <i>Les Films du Dauphin</i>. In the early 1980s, Besson met <a href="/wiki/%C3%89ric_Serra" title="Éric Serra">Éric Serra</a> and asked him to compose the score for his first short film, <i>L'Avant dernier</i>. He later used Serra as a composer for other films of his.</p>
<p>Since the late 20th century, Besson has written and produced numerous action movies, including the <i><a href="/wiki/Taxi_(1998_film)" title="Taxi (1998 film)">Taxi</a></i> (1998–2007) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Transporter" title="The Transporter">The Transporter</a></i> (2002–2008) series, and the <a href="/wiki/Jet_Li" title="Jet Li">Jet Li</a> films <i><a href="/wiki/Kiss_of_the_Dragon" title="Kiss of the Dragon">Kiss of the Dragon</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Unleashed_(film)" title="Unleashed (film)">Unleashed/Danny the Dog</a>.</i> His English-language films <i><a href="/wiki/Taken_(film)" title="Taken (film)">Taken</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Taken_2" title="Taken 2">Taken 2</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Taken_3" title="Taken 3">Taken 3</a></i>, all starring <a href="/wiki/Liam_Neeson" title="Liam Neeson">Liam Neeson</a>, have been major successes, with <i>Taken 2</i> becoming the largest-grossing export French film. Besson produced the promotional movie for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_2012_Olympic_bid" title="Paris 2012 Olympic bid">Paris</a> bid for the <a href="/wiki/2012_Summer_Olympics" title="2012 Summer Olympics">2012 Summer Olympics</a>.</p>
<p>Besson won Best Director and Best French Director for his film <i>The Fifth Element</i> (1997). he was nominated for Best Director and Best Picture <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Award" title="César Award">César Awards</a> for his films <i><a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on:_The_Professional" title="Léon: The Professional">Léon: The Professional</a></i> (1994) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Messenger:_The_Story_of_Joan_of_Arc" title="The Messenger: The Story of Joan of Arc">The Messenger: The Story of Joan of Arc</a></i> (1999).</p>
<p>French actor <a href="/wiki/Jean_Reno" title="Jean Reno">Jean Reno</a> has appeared in several films by Besson, including <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_dernier_combat" title="Le dernier combat">Le dernier combat</a></i> (1985), <i><a href="/wiki/Subway_(film)" title="Subway (film)">Subway</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Blue" title="The Big Blue">The Big Blue</a></i> (1988), <i><a href="/wiki/Nikita_(film)" title="Nikita (film)">Nikita</a></i> (1990), and <i><a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on:_The_Professional" title="Léon: The Professional">Léon: The Professional</a></i> (1994).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cin.C3.A9ma_du_look">Cinéma du look</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luc_Besson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cinéma du look">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Critics<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (June 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> cite Besson as a pivotal figure in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cinema_du_look" title="Cinema du look">Cinéma du look</a> movement, a specific, highly visual style produced from the 1980s into the early 1990s. <i><a href="/wiki/Subway_(film)" title="Subway (film)">Subway</a></i> (1985), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Blue" title="The Big Blue">The Big Blue</a></i> (1988) and <i><a href="/wiki/Nikita_(film)" title="Nikita (film)">Nikita</a></i> (1990) are all considered to be of this stylistic school. The term was coined by critic <a href="/wiki/Rapha%C3%ABl_Bassan" title="Raphaël Bassan">Raphaël Bassan</a> in a 1989 essay in <i>La Revue du Cinema n° 449.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A partisan of the experimental cinema and friend of the <a href="/wiki/French_New_Wave" title="French New Wave">New Wave</a> ("<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nouvelle_vague" title="Nouvelle vague">nouvelle vague</a></i>") directors, Bassan grouped Besson with <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Beineix" title="Jean-Jacques Beineix">Jean-Jacques Beineix</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leos_Carax" title="Leos Carax">Leos Carax</a> as three directors who shared the style of <i>"le look."</i> These directors were later described critically as favoring style over substance, and spectacle over narrative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Besson, along with most of the filmmakers so categorized, was uncomfortable with the label, particularly in light of the achievements of their forebears: France's <a href="/wiki/French_New_Wave" title="French New Wave">New Wave</a>. "<a href="/wiki/Jean-Luc_Godard" title="Jean-Luc Godard">Jean-Luc Godard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Truffaut" title="François Truffaut">François Truffaut</a> were rebelling against existing cultural values and used cinema as a means of expression simply because it was the most avant-garde medium at the time," said Besson in a 1985 interview in <i>The New York Times</i>. "Today, the revolution is occurring entirely within the industry and is led by people who want to change the look of movies by making them better, more convincing and pleasurable to watch.</p>
<p>"Because it's becoming increasingly difficult to break into this field, we have developed a psychological armor and are ready to do anything in order to work", he added in this same interview. "I think our ardor alone is going to shake the pillars of the moviemaking establishment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Besson directed a biopic of <a href="/wiki/Aung_San_Suu_Kyi" title="Aung San Suu Kyi">Aung San Suu Kyi</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lady_(2011_film)" title="The Lady (2011 film)">The Lady</a></i> (original title <i>Dans la Lumiere</i>), which was released in the fall of 2011. He also worked on <i><a href="/wiki/Lockout_(film)" title="Lockout (film)">Lockout</a></i>, which was released in April 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luc_Besson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many of Besson's films have achieved popular, if not critical, success. One such release was <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_Grand_Bleu" title="Le Grand Bleu">Le Grand Bleu</a></i>.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"When the film had its premiere on opening night at the <a href="/wiki/1988_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="1988 Cannes Film Festival">1988 Cannes Film Festival</a>, it was mercilessly drubbed, but no matter; it was a smash," observed the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Herald_Tribune" title="International Herald Tribune">International Herald Tribune</a></i> in a 2007 profile of Besson. "Embraced by young people who kept returning to see it again, the movie sold 10 million tickets and quickly became what the French call a 'film générationnel,' a defining moment in the culture."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Besson created the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_series_(Besson)" title="Arthur series (Besson)">Arthur series</a>, which comprises <i>Arthur and the Minimoys</i>, <i>Arthur and the Forbidden City</i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_and_the_Vengeance_of_Maltazard" title="Arthur and the Vengeance of Maltazard">Arthur and the Vengeance of Maltazard</a></i> and <i>Arthur and the War of the Two Worlds</i>. He directed <i><a href="/wiki/Arthur_and_the_Invisibles" title="Arthur and the Invisibles">Arthur and the Invisibles</a></i>, an adaptation of the first two books of the collection. A <a href="/wiki/Films_with_live_action_and_animation" title="Films with live action and animation">film with live action and animation</a>, it was released in the UK and the US and starred <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Highmore" title="Freddie Highmore">Freddie Highmore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mia_Farrow" title="Mia Farrow">Mia Farrow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_De_Niro" title="Robert De Niro">Robert De Niro</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_evaluation">Critical evaluation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luc_Besson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critical evaluation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Besson has been described as "the most Hollywood of French filmmakers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Scott Tobias wrote that his "slick, commercial" action movies were "so interchangeable—drugs, sleaze, chuckling <a href="/wiki/Supervillain" title="Supervillain">supervillainy</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_cinema" title="Hong Kong cinema">Hong Kong</a>-style effects—that each new project probably starts with white-out on the title page."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>American film critic <a href="/wiki/Armond_White" title="Armond White">Armond White</a> has praised Besson, whom he ranks as one of the best film producers, for refining and revolutionizing action film. He wrote that Besson dramatizes the struggle of his characters "as a conscientious resistance to human degradation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luc_Besson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Besson has been married four times; first, in 1986, to actress <a href="/wiki/Anne_Parillaud" title="Anne Parillaud">Anne Parillaud</a> who starred in Besson's <i><a href="/wiki/Nikita_(film)" title="Nikita (film)">Nikita</a></i> (1990).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Besson and Parillaud had a daughter, Juliette, born in 1987. The couple divorced in 1991.</p>
<p>Besson's second wife was actress <a href="/wiki/Ma%C3%AFwenn" title="Maïwenn">Maïwenn Le Besco</a>, who was 15 when they began dating in 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LeonDVDExtras_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-LeonDVDExtras-17">[17]</a></sup> They were married in late 1992 when Le Besco was pregnant with their daughter <a href="/wiki/Shanna_Besson" title="Shanna Besson">Shanna</a>, who was born on 3 January 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Le Besco later claimed that their relationship inspired Besson's film <i><a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on:_The_Professional" title="Léon: The Professional">Léon</a></i> (1994).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LeonDVDExtras_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-LeonDVDExtras-17">[17]</a></sup> Their marriage ended in 1997, when Besson became involved with actress <a href="/wiki/Milla_Jovovich" title="Milla Jovovich">Milla Jovovich</a> during the filming of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fifth_Element" title="The Fifth Element">The Fifth Element</a></i> (1997). He married the 22-year-old on 14 December 1997, at the age of 38, but they divorced in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 28 August 2004, at the age of 45, Besson married film producer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginie_Silla" title="Virginie Silla">Virginie Silla</a>. The couple has three children: Thalia, Satine, and Mao Besson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-karenr_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-karenr-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_honours">Legacy and honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luc_Besson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy and honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among Besson's awards are the Brussels International Festival of Fantasy Film Critics Prize, Fantasporto Audience Jury Award-Special Mention, Best Director, and Best Film, for <i>Le Dernier Combat</i> in 1983; the Italian National Syndicate of Film Journalists Silver Ribbon-Best Director-Foreign Film, for <i>La Femme Nikita</i>, 1990; the <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Korda" title="Alexander Korda">Alexander Korda</a> Award for Best British Film, <i>Nil by Mouth</i>, 1997; and the Best Director Cesar Award, for <i>The Fifth Element</i>, 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luc_Besson_2000_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Luc_Besson_2000-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_company">Film company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luc_Besson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Film company">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/EuropaCorp" title="EuropaCorp">EuropaCorp</a></div>
<p>In 2000, Besson superseded his production company by co-founding EuropaCorp with Pierre-Ange Le Pogam, with whom he had frequently worked since 1985. Le Pogam had then been Distribution Director with <a href="/wiki/Gaumont_Film_Company" title="Gaumont Film Company">Gaumont</a>. EuropaCorp has had strong growth based on several English-language films, with international distribution. It has production facilities in Paris, Normandy, and Hollywood, and is establishing distribution partnerships in Japan and China.</p>
