<p>The <b>Stadion Ljudski vrt</b> (English: People's Garden, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Volksgarten</i></span>; abbreviation: <b>LV</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lv_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-lv-8">[8]</a></sup>) is an <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a> <a href="/wiki/Stadium" title="Stadium">stadium</a> located on the left bank of the river <a href="/wiki/Drava" title="Drava">Drava</a> in the district of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koro%C5%A1ka_Vrata" title="Koroška Vrata">Koroška Vrata</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koroska_vrata_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Koroska_vrata-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Maribor" title="Maribor">Maribor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/Seating_capacity" title="Seating capacity">seating capacity</a> of 12,702. The ground has been the home of <a href="/wiki/NK_Maribor" title="NK Maribor">NK Maribor</a> for every season since their formation on 12 December 1960, with the exception of two short periods in early 1961 during the construction of the new stands and early 2008, when the stadium underwent a major reconstruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siol07092014_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-siol07092014-5">[5]</a></sup> Opened in 1952, it was originally the home of <a href="/wiki/NK_Branik_Maribor" title="NK Branik Maribor">Branik Maribor</a>, an association football club, which folded and was disbanded in 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vecer1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-vecer1-10">[10]</a></sup> Used as one of the main venues of the national team, the ground has hosted 23 <a href="/wiki/Slovenia_national_football_team" title="Slovenia national football team">Slovenia internationals</a> at senior level, the first in 1994 and the most recent in 2015. Ljudski vrt has hosted more Slovenian football cup finals than any other stadium, having hosted ten matches in total (six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slovenian_Cup" title="Slovenian Cup">Slovenian Cups</a> and four <a href="/wiki/Slovenian_Supercup" title="Slovenian Supercup">Slovenian Supercups</a>). In addition, the stadium was one of the four venues, which hosted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2012_UEFA_European_Under-17_Football_Championship" title="2012 UEFA European Under-17 Football Championship">2012 UEFA European Under-17 Football Championship</a>.</p>
<p>The area where the Ljudski vrt is situated was used for burial purpuses for centuries before it was first used for football in the early 1920s. Over the course of its history the stadium has gone through various stages of renovation and development, resulting in the current configuration. The record attendance at the stadium is 20,000, which was set in a match between Maribor and <a href="/wiki/FK_Proleter_Zrenjanin" title="FK Proleter Zrenjanin">Proleter Zrenjanin</a> in 1973. This record was set before the ground's conversion to an <a href="/wiki/All-seater_stadium" title="All-seater stadium">all-seater stadium</a> in 1998; the changes, a result of <a href="/wiki/UEFA" title="UEFA">UEFA</a> safety regulations, include greatly reduced capacity. Notable feature of the stadium includes the main stand 129,8 meters long and 18,4 meters high concrete arch that is protected by the Institute for the Protection of Cultural Heritage of Slovenia (<a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">Slovene</a>: <span lang="sl" xml:lang="sl"><i>Zavod za varstvo kulturne dediščine Slovenije</i></span>) as an architectural and historical landmark.</p>
<p>Future plans for the Ljudski vrt include the construction of a nearby underground parking garage that would solve the current parking problems and the redevelopment of the outdated main stand, which was built in 1962 and has not received a major renovation since then.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siol07092014_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-siol07092014-5">[5]</a></sup> The stadium is a landmark of the city of Maribor and is considered as one of the most beautiful smaller stadium in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siol07092014_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-siol07092014-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ljudski_vrt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The area now known as Ljudski vrt was originally located outside of <a href="/wiki/Maribor" title="Maribor">Maribor</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_walls" title="City walls">city walls</a> and served as a <a href="/wiki/Cemetery" title="Cemetery">cemetery</a> for centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siol07092014_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-siol07092014-5">[5]</a></sup> Around the year 1358 a small <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a> with a cemetery was built and remained there until 1522 when it was abandoned,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siol07092014_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-siol07092014-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cemetery1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-cemetery1-13">[13]</a></sup> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Growth_of_the_Ottoman_Empire" title="Growth of the Ottoman Empire">Turkish incursions</a> being the most likely reason.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voje_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Voje-14">[14]</a></sup> By 1571 the church was completely in <a href="/wiki/Ruins" title="Ruins">ruins</a>. The site served as a cemetery again between 1783 and 1914 when it was closed by a decision of the Maribor city council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cemetery1_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-cemetery1-13">[13]</a></sup> Some of the <a href="/wiki/Tomb" title="Tomb">tombs</a> were allowed to be in use until 1937 when they were transferred to a new site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cemetery1_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-cemetery1-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1873 a tree park (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Volksgarten</i></span>) was planted on the area from which Ljudski vrt received its present-day name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siol07092014_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-siol07092014-5">[5]</a></sup> Around the start of the 20th century, Ljudski vrt was starting to become the recreational centre of the city and records from 1901 show that tennis was already being played there during that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the whole area served as a <a href="/wiki/Shooting_range" title="Shooting range">shooting range</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Army" title="Austro-Hungarian Army">Austro-Hungarian Army</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ljudski_vrt_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ljudski_vrt-16">[16]</a></sup> Like in other Slovenian towns (<a href="/wiki/Ljubljana" title="Ljubljana">Ljubljana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Celje" title="Celje">Celje</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trbovlje" title="Trbovlje">Trbovlje</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> after World War I football boomed in Maribor with the establishment of new clubs, most notably <a href="/wiki/I._SSK_Maribor" title="I. SSK Maribor">I. SSK Maribor</a> (<a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">Slovene</a>: <span lang="sl" xml:lang="sl"><i>1. Slovenski Športni Klub Maribor</i></span>, English: 1. Slovene Sport Club Maribor), which was founded in 1919 by Slovenian youth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1.SSK_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-1.SSK-18">[18]</a></sup> Together with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athletics_(sport)" title="Athletics (sport)">athletics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a>, football was one of the main sports departments of the club and in 1920 they received their first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Association_football_pitch" title="Association football pitch">football field</a> at the Ljudski vrt area,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ljudski_vrt_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ljudski_vrt-16">[16]</a></sup> which was erected with the help of local volunteers and prison <a href="/wiki/Convict" title="Convict">convicts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1.SSK_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-1.SSK-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Yugoslavia" title="Invasion of Yugoslavia">invasion of Yugoslavia</a> in 1941, <a href="/wiki/Maribor" title="Maribor">Maribor</a> came under the occupation of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> and the Nazi regime immediately disbanded all Slovene cultural and sports societies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-okupacija_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-okupacija-19">[19]</a></sup> I. SSK Maribor was particularly affected with dozens of arrests and deportations of their members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-okupacija_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-okupacija-19">[19]</a></sup> The club ceased all operations and many of their members joined in the fight for their fatherland and a total of 51 perished while fighting the <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">Germans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-okupacija_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-okupacija-19">[19]</a></sup> Those victims were later commemorated with the erection of a statue located on the northwestern corner of the stadium today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-okupacija_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-okupacija-19">[19]</a></sup> By the end of the war <a href="/wiki/Maribor" title="Maribor">Maribor</a> was the most destroyed larger town in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SFR_Yugoslavia" title="SFR Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> and the whole Ljudski vrt area was devastated, completely in ruins and without an organization which would renovate and later manage the sporting infrastructure at the site. On 29 January 1949, an initiative led to the establishment of <a href="/wiki/NK_Branik_Maribor" title="NK Branik Maribor">Branik Maribor</a> football club.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-branik1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-branik1-20">[20]</a></sup> Two years later, in 1951, they became the flagship of the new sport organization, MŠD Branik (<i><span lang="sl" xml:lang="sl">Mariborsko Športno Društvo Branik</span></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ustanovitev_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ustanovitev-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>The renovation and construction of the sports infrastructure at the Ljudski vrt and throughout <a href="/wiki/Maribor" title="Maribor">Maribor</a> was the primary objective of the new sports organization during most of the late 1940s and early 1950s and on 12 July 1952 the Ljudski vrt stadium was opened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siol07092014_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-siol07092014-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vecer1_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-vecer1-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-izgradnja_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-izgradnja-22">[22]</a></sup> At the time the main pitch was fully enclosed by banking, surrounding the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athletic_track" title="Athletic track">athletic track</a>, with concrete terraces and seats located on the western side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-izgradnja_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-izgradnja-22">[22]</a></sup> By 1958 the concrete terraces, in length of 248 meters, were constructed throughout the banking around the pitch and served as the standing area for over 40 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-izgradnja_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-izgradnja-22">[22]</a></sup> Milan Černigoj was the main architect of the stadium and in the late 1950s he was joined by Boris Pipan with whom they designed a new main stand on the western side of the pitch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-izgradnja_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-izgradnja-22">[22]</a></sup> The construction began in May 1960 and was completed in 1962, with the new club offices, dressing rooms and gyms located beneath it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-izgradnja_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-izgradnja-22">[22]</a></sup> Notable feature of the stand includes 129,8 meters long and 18,4 meters high concrete arch that is protected by the Institute for the Protection of Cultural Heritage of Slovenia (<i><span lang="sl" xml:lang="sl">Zavod za varstvo kulturne dediščine Slovenije</span></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zvkds_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-zvkds-23">[23]</a></sup> The primary user of the stadium and the new club offices was to be NK Branik, however, the club was dissolved in 1960 due to a food poisoning affair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vecer1_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-vecer1-10">[10]</a></sup> After that, the city of <a href="/wiki/Maribor" title="Maribor">Maribor</a> was left without an association football club that would play on a professional level, which was one of the reasons why NK Maribor was established on 12 December 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vecer1_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-vecer1-10">[10]</a></sup> The new club found their home at Ljudski vrt and on 25 June 1961 they played their first match at the stadium, with the then main stand still under construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tekma1_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-tekma1-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ljudski vrt was to remain in much the same state for another 30 years with no major developments until the early 1990s and the <a href="/wiki/Ten-Day_War" title="Ten-Day War">independence</a> of <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ljudski_vrt_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ljudski_vrt-16">[16]</a></sup> After the opening of the main stand in 1962 the stadium's capacity increased to over 10,000. However, as most of the stadium had only concrete standing terraces it was possible to accommodate as much as 20,000 spectators during the club's important matches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maribor_Proleter_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Maribor_Proleter-4">[4]</a></sup> The wooden benches on the main stand were replaced by plastic seats in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ljudski_vrt_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ljudski_vrt-16">[16]</a></sup> During the same year, on 24 August, the stadium received four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floodlights_(sport)" title="Floodlights (sport)">floodlight</a> pylons and the first football match at night was played in a <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Cup_Winners%27_Cup" title="UEFA Cup Winners' Cup">UEFA Cup Winners' Cup</a> between <a href="/wiki/NK_Maribor" title="NK Maribor">NK Maribor</a> and <a href="/wiki/FC_Norma_Tallinn" title="FC Norma Tallinn">FC Norma Tallinn</a>; won by Maribor 10–0.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ljudski_vrt_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ljudski_vrt-16">[16]</a></sup> The capacity of the stadium was greatly reduced four years later, as a result of <a href="/wiki/UEFA" title="UEFA">UEFA</a> safety regulations, with the ground's conversion to an <a href="/wiki/All-seater_stadium" title="All-seater stadium">all-seater stadium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ljudski_vrt_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ljudski_vrt-16">[16]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_UEFA_Champions_League" title="1999–2000 UEFA Champions League">1999–2000 season</a> Maribor became the first Slovenian club which qualified to the elite <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">UEFA Champions League</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ljudski_vrt_16-6"><a href="#cite_note-Ljudski_vrt-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mb_pohorje_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-mb_pohorje-25">[25]</a></sup> As a result, the stadium received its first major redevelopment since 1962 with the renovation of the main stand's VIP box and dressing rooms and club offices beneath it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ljudski_vrt_16-7"><a href="#cite_note-Ljudski_vrt-16">[16]</a></sup> The terraces ring opposite of the main stand was enlarged and the total capacity of the stadium was brought to 10,160 seats, making Ljudski vrt the <a href="/wiki/List_of_football_stadiums_in_Slovenia" title="List of football stadiums in Slovenia">largest football stadium in the country</a> at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ljudski_vrt_16-8"><a href="#cite_note-Ljudski_vrt-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Maribor results in domestic and international competitions during the 1990s and their dedicated fanbase were the main reasons for political and sports officials in the city to start thinking about a new stadium. In 1996 the <a href="/wiki/OFIS_Architects" title="OFIS Architects">OFIS Architects</a> Project Ring was selected with a plan to fully redevelop and modernise the stadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siol07092014_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-siol07092014-5">[5]</a></sup> The project included the refurbishment of the main stand and the surrounding area, removal of the athletic track and the concrete terraces on the northern, southern and eastern side of the pitch and their replacement with the brand new covered stands. In addition, the project also included new club offices, gym's, dressing rooms and commercial premises such as shops and bars located beneath the new stands. However, it would take nearly a decade for the project to become a reality when in 2006 the City of Maribor and MŠD Branik, with the help of the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Slovenia" title="Government of Slovenia">Government of Slovenia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, finally amassed enough funds to start the first stage of the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siol07092014_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-siol07092014-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first stage of improvements, worth about 10 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euros" title="Euros">euros</a>, saw the removal of the athletic track and the uncovered stands that surrounded the pitch from the north, east and south which were replaced by new covered stands, built only couple of meters from the pitch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siol07092014_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-siol07092014-5">[5]</a></sup> Construction began in August 2006 and was finished by May 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siol07092014_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-siol07092014-5">[5]</a></sup> One of the improvements included the replacement of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Association_football_pitch" title="Association football pitch">turf</a>, which also included the installation of the <a href="/wiki/Under-soil_heating" title="Under-soil heating">underground heating system</a>, and during that time Maribor played couple of months at the <a href="/wiki/Ptuj_City_Stadium" title="Ptuj City Stadium">Ptuj City Stadium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ptuj" title="Ptuj">Ptuj</a>, about 30 kilometres from the city of Maribor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siol07092014_5-12"><a href="#cite_note-siol07092014-5">[5]</a></sup> In addition, this was the first time that Maribor had played home matches outside of Ljudski vrt since the opening of the main stand in 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siol07092014_5-13"><a href="#cite_note-siol07092014-5">[5]</a></sup> The new stands, which increased the stadium capacity by over 2,000 seats, were opened on 10 May 2008 during a league match against <a href="/wiki/NK_Nafta_Lendava" title="NK Nafta Lendava">Nafta Lendava</a>. The match was played in front of a sold-out crowd of 12,435 spectators and Maribor won 3–1. The second stage of the project started in 2010 and saw the completion of the premises under the eastern and southern stands. It included new club offices, dressing rooms and two <a href="/wiki/Gym" title="Gym">gyms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In addition, the total capacity of the stadium was brought to its present 12,702 seats.</p>
<p>In 2014, the stadium barely passed <a href="/wiki/UEFA_stadium_categories" title="UEFA stadium categories">UEFA stadium regulations</a> for the international competitions due to aging west stand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In 2015, the plans for re-developing of the west stand were released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Football">Football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ljudski_vrt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The stadium is mainly used for football and is the home ground of football club <a href="/wiki/NK_Maribor" title="NK Maribor">NK Maribor</a>. It is the symbol of the club and one of the most recognizable sport landmarks in <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>. Beside being the home ground of NK Maribor, the stadium has also hosted final matches of the <a href="/wiki/Slovenian_Football_Cup" title="Slovenian Football Cup">Slovenian Cup</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Slovenian_Supercup" title="Slovenian Supercup">Slovenian Supercup</a> on numerous occasions and is also the home venue of the <a href="/wiki/Slovenia_national_football_team" title="Slovenia national football team">Slovenia national football team</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ljudski_vrt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to excellent acoustics, the stadium is a popular choice for concerts and other cultural performances. One of the first on the renovated stadium was the <a href="/wiki/Zorba_(musical)" title="Zorba (musical)">Greek Zorba</a> musical, which had an attendance of around 6,000 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The stadium also hosted an annual concert <i>Piše se leto</i> several times, organized by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ve%C4%8Der_(Maribor)" title="Večer (Maribor)">Večer</a> newspaper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>

<p>The highest attendance recorded at Ljudski vrt is 20,000, for Maribor's match against <a href="/wiki/FK_Proleter_Zrenjanin" title="FK Proleter Zrenjanin">Proleter Zrenjanin</a> in the first leg of the promotional playoffs for the <a href="/wiki/Yugoslav_First_League" title="Yugoslav First League">Yugoslav First League</a>, on 8 July 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maribor_Proleter_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Maribor_Proleter-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siol07092014_5-15"><a href="#cite_note-siol07092014-5">[5]</a></sup> The stadium also shares the record with <a href="/wiki/Sto%C5%BEice_Stadium" title="Stožice Stadium">Stožice Stadium</a> for the highest attendance achieved in a <a href="/wiki/Slovenian_PrvaLiga" title="Slovenian PrvaLiga">Slovenian league</a> match.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> This was set in the final round of the <a href="/wiki/1996%E2%80%9397_Slovenian_PrvaLiga" title="1996–97 Slovenian PrvaLiga">1996–97</a> season on 1 June 1997, when 14,000 spectators were present for Maribor's match against <a href="/wiki/NK_Beltinci" title="NK Beltinci">Beltinci</a>, which secured the club's first league title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mb_beltinci1_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-mb_beltinci1-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mb_beltinci2_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-mb_beltinci2-49">[49]</a></sup> The record modern (all-seated) attendance is 12,702, for the second leg of the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2016_qualifying_play-offs" title="UEFA Euro 2016 qualifying play-offs">UEFA Euro 2016 qualifying play-offs</a> between Slovenia and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine_national_football_team" title="Ukraine national football team">Ukraine</a>, on 17 November 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slo_ukr2015_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-slo_ukr2015-45">[45]</a></sup> In addition, Ljudski vrt holds the record for the highest average attendance during the Slovenian league season to date (5,289).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Maribor did not lose a league match at Ljudski vrt during the <a href="/wiki/1960%E2%80%9361_Slovenian_Republic_League" title="1960–61 Slovenian Republic League">1960–61</a> (Div 3), 1965–66 (Div 2), 1966–67 (Div 2), <a href="/wiki/1970%E2%80%9371_Yugoslav_First_League" title="1970–71 Yugoslav First League">1970–71</a> (Div 1), 1972–73 (Div 2), 1977–78 (Div 2), <a href="/wiki/1980%E2%80%9381_Yugoslav_Second_League" title="1980–81 Yugoslav Second League">1980–81</a> (Div 2), <a href="/wiki/1981%E2%80%9382_Slovenian_Republic_League" title="1981–82 Slovenian Republic League">1981–82</a> (Div 3), <a href="/wiki/1983%E2%80%9384_Slovenian_Republic_League" title="1983–84 Slovenian Republic League">1983–84</a> (Div 3), <a href="/wiki/1985%E2%80%9386_Slovenian_Republic_League" title="1985–86 Slovenian Republic League">1985–86</a> (Div 3), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slovenian_Republic_League_1987-88" title="Slovenian Republic League 1987-88">1987–88</a> (Div 3), <a href="/wiki/1991%E2%80%9392_Slovenian_PrvaLiga" title="1991–92 Slovenian PrvaLiga">1991–92</a> (Div 1), <a href="/wiki/1992%E2%80%9393_Slovenian_PrvaLiga" title="1992–93 Slovenian PrvaLiga">1992–93</a> (Div 1), <a href="/wiki/1998%E2%80%9399_Slovenian_PrvaLiga" title="1998–99 Slovenian PrvaLiga">1998–99</a> (Div 1) and <a href="/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_Slovenian_PrvaLiga" title="1999–2000 Slovenian PrvaLiga">1999–2000</a> (Div 1) season. They won all their home games during the 1983–84 and 1985–86 seasons. Maribor's longest winning streak at home extended from October 1993 to November 1994, a period encompassing 17 league games, in which Maribor scored 52 goals and conceded 10.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ljudski_vrt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ljudski vrt's public transport links include rail and bus services, but it lacks dedicated parking facilities. The stadium is about 1,5 <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">kilometres</a> (1 <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">mile</a>) away from the Maribor bus station and the <a href="/wiki/Maribor_railway_station" title="Maribor railway station">Maribor railway station</a>, which lies on the <a href="/wiki/Pan-European_Corridor_Xa" title="Pan-European Corridor Xa">Pan-European Corridor Xa</a> (connecting <a href="/wiki/Zagreb" title="Zagreb">Zagreb</a> to <a href="/wiki/Graz" title="Graz">Graz</a>) and on <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-European_Corridor_V&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pan-European Corridor V (page does not exist)">Pan-European Corridor V</a>, which connects <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">Kiev</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ljubljana" title="Ljubljana">Ljubljana</a> - <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>). Several bus lines pass directly by the stadium with the nearest bus stations located less than one hundred meters from the ground. The connection to the <a href="/wiki/A1_motorway_(Slovenia)" title="A1 motorway (Slovenia)">A1 motorway</a>, that links to the <a href="/wiki/Highways_in_Slovenia" title="Highways in Slovenia">Slovenian motorway network</a>, is located about 3 kilometres to the east, while <a href="/wiki/Maribor_Edvard_Rusjan_Airport" title="Maribor Edvard Rusjan Airport">Maribor Edvard Rusjan Airport</a> is located about 13 kilometres to the southeast of the ground.</p>
