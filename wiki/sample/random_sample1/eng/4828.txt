<p><b>Rheumatism</b> or <b>rheumatic disorder</b> is an umbrella term for conditions causing <a href="/wiki/Chronic_pain" title="Chronic pain">chronic, often intermittent pain</a> affecting the <a href="/wiki/Joint" title="Joint">joints</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue" title="Connective tissue">connective tissue</a>. The study of, and therapeutic interventions in, such disorders is called <a href="/wiki/Rheumatology" title="Rheumatology">rheumatology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The term "rheumatism", however, does not designate any specific disorder, but covers at least 200<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> different conditions.</p>
<p>Sources dealing with rheumatism tend to focus on <a href="/wiki/Arthritis" title="Arthritis">arthritis</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but "rheumatism" may also refer to other conditions causing chronic pain, grouped as "non-articular rheumatism", also known as "regional pain syndrome" or "soft tissue rheumatism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urleMedicine_-_Nonarticular_Rheumatism.2FRegional_Pain_Syndrome_:_Article_by_Daniel_Muller_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-urleMedicine_-_Nonarticular_Rheumatism.2FRegional_Pain_Syndrome_:_Article_by_Daniel_Muller-2">[2]</a></sup> The term "Rheumatic Diseases" is used in <a href="/wiki/Medical_Subject_Headings" title="Medical Subject Headings">MeSH</a> to refer to <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue" title="Connective tissue">connective tissue</a> disorders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rheumatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>rheumatism</i> in the current sense has been in use since the late 17th century, as it was believed that chronic joint pain was caused by excessive flow of <i><a href="/wiki/Rheum" title="Rheum">rheum</a></i> or bodily fluids into a joint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <i>rheumatism</i> is somewhat older, adopted in the early 17th century in a different meaning, from Late Latin <i>rheumatismus</i>, ultimately from Greek ῥευματίζομαι "to suffer from a flux", i.e. any discharge of blood or bodily fluid (<a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Mark" title="Gospel of Mark">Mark</a> 5:25 uses ῥύσις, from the same root, for "flow of (menstrual) blood", translated as "flux" in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KJV" title="KJV">KJV</a>).</p>
<p>Before the 17th century, joint pain was thought to be caused by viscous <a href="/wiki/Humorism" title="Humorism">humours</a> seeping into the joints was named <a href="/wiki/Gout" title="Gout">gout</a>, a word adopted in <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> from <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>gote</i> "a drop; the gout, rheumatism".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rheumatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The major rheumatic disorders currently recognized <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2017)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> include</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ankylosing_spondylitis" title="Ankylosing spondylitis">Ankylosing spondylitis</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Back_pain" title="Back pain">Back pain</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bursitis" title="Bursitis">Bursitis</a>/<a href="/wiki/Tendinitis" title="Tendinitis">Tendinitis</a>, Shoulder pain, wrist, biceps, leg, knee (patellar), ankle, hip, and Achilles</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Capsulitis" title="Capsulitis">Capsulitis</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neck_pain" title="Neck pain">Neck pain</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Osteoarthritis" title="Osteoarthritis">Osteoarthritis</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Palindromic_rheumatism" title="Palindromic rheumatism">Palindromic rheumatism</a> has been theorized to be a form of <a href="/wiki/Rheumatoid_arthritis" title="Rheumatoid arthritis">rheumatoid arthritis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid12730510_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid12730510-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Although these disorders probably have little in common in terms of their <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology" title="Epidemiology">epidemiology</a>, they do share two characteristics: they cause chronic (though often intermittent) <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">pain</a>, and they are difficult to treat. They are also, collectively, very common.</p>
<p>Other rheumatic diseases which are caused by autoimmunity include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Relapsing_polychondritis" title="Relapsing polychondritis">Relapsing polychondritis</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Systemic_lupus_erythematosus" title="Systemic lupus erythematosus">systemic lupus erythematosus</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rheumatoid_arthritis" title="Rheumatoid arthritis">rheumatoid arthritis</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Gout" title="Gout">gout</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inflammatory_arthritis" title="Inflammatory arthritis">inflammatory arthritis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudogout" title="Pseudogout">pseudogout</a>)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juvenile_arthritis" title="Juvenile arthritis">juvenile arthritis</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sj%C3%B6gren_syndrome" title="Sjögren syndrome">Sjögren syndrome</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Scleroderma" title="Scleroderma">scleroderma</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Polymyositis" title="Polymyositis">Polymyositis</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dermatomyositis" title="Dermatomyositis">Dermatomyositis</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Beh%C3%A7et%27s_disease" title="Behçet's disease">Behçet's disease</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Reactive_arthritis" title="Reactive arthritis">Reactive arthritis</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Psoriatic_arthritis" title="Psoriatic arthritis">Psoriatic arthritis</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rheumatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A vast number of traditional <a href="/wiki/Herbalism" title="Herbalism">herbal</a> remedies were recommended for "rheumatism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Modern medicine, both conventional and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complementary_medicine" title="Complementary medicine">alternative</a>, recognises that the different rheumatic disorders have different causes (and several of them have multiple causes) and require different kinds of treatment.</p>
<p>Nevertheless, initial therapy of the major rheumatological diseases is with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analgesics" title="Analgesics">analgesics</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acetaminophen" title="Acetaminophen">paracetamol</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-steroidal_anti-inflammatory_drug" title="Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug">non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs</a> (NSAIDs), members of which are <a href="/wiki/Ibuprofen" title="Ibuprofen">ibuprofen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Naproxen" title="Naproxen">naproxen</a>. Often, stronger analgesics are required.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greeks</a> recorded that <a href="/wiki/Apitoxin" title="Apitoxin">bee venom</a> had some beneficial effects on some types of rheumatism. Bee and ant stings were known as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folk_remedy" title="Folk remedy">folk remedy</a> in the late 19th century, and at least one physician developed a treatment consisting of repeated <a href="/wiki/Formic_acid" title="Formic acid">formic acid</a> injections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Certain Amazonian tribes, including the <a href="/wiki/Zo%27%C3%A9" title="Zo'é">Zo'é</a>, use <a href="/wiki/Fire_ant" title="Fire ant">fire ant</a> stings as a remedy for aches and pains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cod_liver_oil" title="Cod liver oil">Cod liver oil</a> has also been used as a remedy.</p>
<p>Neem Tree Oil according to East Indian cultures has also been used as a remedy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>There has long been said to be a link between "rheumatic" pain and the weather. There appears to be no firm evidence in favour or against; a 1995 questionnaire given to 557 people by A. Naser and others at the Brigham and Women's Hospital's Pain Management Center concludes that "changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> are the main link between weather and pain. Low pressure is generally associated with cold, wet weather and an increase in pain. Clear, dry conditions signal high pressure and a decrease in pain."</p>
