<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Okinawan music</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Okinawan music</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">沖縄音楽</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Okinawa ongaku</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, also known as <b><a href="/wiki/Ryukyuan_music" title="Ryukyuan music">Ryukyuan music</a></b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">琉球音楽</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Ryūkyū ongaku</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, is the music of the <a href="/wiki/Okinawa_Islands" title="Okinawa Islands">Okinawa Islands</a> of southwestern <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. In modern Japan, it may also refer to the musical traditions of <a href="/wiki/Okinawa_Prefecture" title="Okinawa Prefecture">Okinawa Prefecture</a>, which also covers the <a href="/wiki/Miyako_Islands" title="Miyako Islands">Miyako</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yaeyama_Islands" title="Yaeyama Islands">Yaeyama Islands</a>. It derived from the larger <a href="/wiki/Ryukyuan_music" title="Ryukyuan music">musical traditions of the Southern Islands</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genres">Genres</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Okinawan_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Genres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A dichotomy widely accepted by people of Okinawa is the separation of musical traditions into <i>koten</i> (classical) and <i><a href="/wiki/Min%27y%C5%8D" title="Min'yō">min'yō</a></i> (folk). Okinawa was once independent as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ry%C5%ABky%C5%AB_Kingdom" title="Ryūkyū Kingdom">Ryūkyū Kingdom</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Pechin" title="Pechin">pechin</a> class in its capital <a href="/wiki/Shuri,_Okinawa" title="Shuri, Okinawa">Shuri</a> developed its <a href="/wiki/High_culture" title="High culture">high culture</a> while they frequently suppressed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folk_culture" title="Folk culture">folk culture</a> in rural areas. Susumu Kumada added another category "popular music" to describe songs that emerged after the kingdom was abolished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kumada2011c1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-kumada2011c1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_music">Classical music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Okinawan_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classical music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Uzagaku.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="559" data-file-width="1280" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Uzagaku.jpg/300px-Uzagaku.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Uzagaku.jpg/450px-Uzagaku.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Uzagaku.jpg/600px-Uzagaku.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Uzagaku.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Uzagaku</div>

</div>
<p>Ryukyuan classical music <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">琉球古典音楽</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>ryūkyū koten ongaku</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is the court music of Ryūkyū. <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Uzagaku&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Uzagaku (page does not exist)">Uzagaku</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">御座楽</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is the traditional chamber music of the royal palace of the Ryūkyū Kingdom.</p>
<p>The texture is essentially <a href="/wiki/Heterophony" title="Heterophony">heterophonic</a> using a single melodic line. Pitched accompaniment instruments each play a simultaneous variation on the vocal line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tokita.2C_A._.26_D._Hughes_2008_446_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tokita.2C_A._.26_D._Hughes_2008_446-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gillan_2012_238_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gillan_2012_238-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folk_music">Folk music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Okinawan_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Folk music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traditionally seen as "low culture" by the pechin class, folk music gained positive evaluation with the rise of folkloristics led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yanagita_Kunio" title="Yanagita Kunio">Yanagita Kunio</a>. Folk music is described by the term <i><a href="/wiki/Min%27y%C5%8D" title="Min'yō">min'yō</a></i>, which is also applied to other parts of Japan. Since the kingdom was abolished, some members of the former pechin class spread Shuri-based high culture to other areas of Okinawa. Some of such new elements are today seen as part of folk culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kumada2011c1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-kumada2011c1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Okinawa's folk songs are generally accompanied by one (or more) <a href="/wiki/Sanshin" title="Sanshin">sanshin</a>.</p>
<p><i>Yunta</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">ユンタ</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a genre of folk song originating in the <a href="/wiki/Yaeyama_Islands" title="Yaeyama Islands">Yaeyama Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gillan_2012_238_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gillan_2012_238-4">[4]</a></sup> The verses and choruses of the song are sung alternately by the men and the women. The word origin of <i>yunta</i> is said to be from <i>yumi uta</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">読み歌</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>"reading song"</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> or <i>yui uta</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">結い歌</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>"tying/knotting song"</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The suffixes <i><a href="/wiki/Ondo_(music)" title="Ondo (music)">-ondo</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Bushi_(music)" title="Bushi (music)">-bushi</a></i> may also be attached to the title of folk songs, however songs named without these clarifiers are more common.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Eisa_(dance)" title="Eisa (dance)">Eisā</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kach%C4%81sh%C4%AB" title="Kachāshī">kachāshī</a> are Okinawan dances with specific music styles that accompany them.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Warabe-uta</dt>
</dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Warabe_uta" title="Warabe uta">Warabe uta</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">童歌</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a general term for nursery rhymes and children's songs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_music">Popular music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Okinawan_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Popular music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="New_folk_songs">New folk songs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Okinawan_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: New folk songs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i>"New min'yō"</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">新民謡</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>shin min'yō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, composed in the style of traditional Okinawan min'yō, have been written by several contemporary Okinawan folk musicians such as <a href="/wiki/Rinsh%C5%8D_Kadekaru" title="Rinshō Kadekaru">Rinshō Kadekaru</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sadao_China&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sadao China (page does not exist)">Sadao China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shoukichi_Kina" title="Shoukichi Kina">Shōkichi Kina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seijin_Noborikawa" title="Seijin Noborikawa">Seijin Noborikawa</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tsuneo_Fukuhara&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tsuneo Fukuhara (page does not exist)">Tsuneo Fukuhara</a>. These songs are often heard in contemporary pop music arrangements. <i>Haisai ojisan</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">ハイサイおじさん</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, with music and lyrics by Shōkichi Kina, is typical of this genre.</p>
<p>Okinawa's (new) folk songs are sometimes referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Shima-uta" title="Shima-uta">shima-uta</a></i>. It is not a native term of Okinawa but was introduced from the <a href="/wiki/Amami_Islands" title="Amami Islands">Amami Islands</a> in the 1970s. It is originally a genre of songs originating from Amami.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-takahashi2002_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-takahashi2002-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Okinawa_pop">Okinawa pop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Okinawan_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Okinawa pop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The music of Okinawa came under the influence of <a href="/wiki/American_rock" title="American rock">American rock</a> music beginning with the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Many musicians began to blend the Okinawan folk music style and native instruments with those of American popular and rock music. This is called "Uchinaa pop."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instrumentation">Instrumentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Okinawan_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Instrumentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The instrument that defines Okinawan music is the <a href="/wiki/Sanshin" title="Sanshin">sanshin</a>. It is a three-stringed lute, very similar to the <a href="/wiki/Sanxian" title="Sanxian">sanxian</a> and a precursor to the <a href="/wiki/Shamisen" title="Shamisen">shamisen</a>. The body is covered in snake skin and it is plucked with a plectrum worn on the index finger.</p>
<p>Okinawan folk music is often accompanied by various <a href="/wiki/Taiko" title="Taiko">taiko drums</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Shime-daiko" title="Shime-daiko">shime-daiko</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">締太鼓</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, <i>hira-daiko</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">平太鼓</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=P%C4%81ranku&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pāranku (page does not exist)">pāranku</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">パーランク</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>. Pāranku, a small hand-held drum about the size of a tambourine, is often used in <a href="/wiki/Eisa_(dance)" title="Eisa (dance)">eisā</a> dancing.</p>
<p>Other percussion instruments such as <i><a href="/wiki/Sanba" title="Sanba">sanba</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">三板</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yotsutake&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yotsutake (page does not exist)">yotsutake</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">四つ竹</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hy%C5%8Dshigi" title="Hyōshigi">hyōshigi</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">拍子木</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> can often be heard in Okinawan music. <i>Sanba</i> are three small, flat pieces of wood or plastic that are used to make rapid clicking sounds, similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castanet" title="Castanet">castanets</a>. <i>Yotsutake</i> are two sets of rectangular <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> strips tied together, one set held in each hand, clapped together on the strong beat of the music. Traditionally they have been used in Ryukyuan classical music, but recently they have been used in <a href="/wiki/Eisa_(dance)" title="Eisa (dance)">eisā</a> dancing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>A group of singers called a <i>hayashi</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">囃子</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> often accompanies folk music, singing the chorus or interjecting shouts called <i><a href="/wiki/Kakegoe" title="Kakegoe">kakegoe</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">掛け声</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tonality">Tonality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Okinawan_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tonality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The following is described in terms used in Western disciplines of music.</i></p>
<p>Music from Okinawa uses tonal structure that is different in music from the mainland Japan and Amami, in particular the <a href="/wiki/Interval_(music)" title="Interval (music)">intervalic</a> content of the scales used.</p>
<p>The chief <a href="/wiki/Pentatonic_scale" title="Pentatonic scale">pentatonic scale</a> used in mainland Japan, for example, uses scale degrees 1, 2, 3, 5, and 6, also known as Do, Re, Mi, So, and La in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kod%C3%A1ly" title="Kodály">Kodály</a> system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solfeggio" title="Solfeggio">solfeggio</a>. This structure avoids <a href="/wiki/Semitone" title="Semitone">half step</a> intervals by eliminating the fourth and seventh scale degrees.</p>
<p>In contrast, music from Okinawa is abundant in the half steps. Common structures used in Okinawan music are a pentatonic scale utilizing scale degrees 1, 3, 4, 5, 7, or Do, Mi, Fa, So, Ti, or a <a href="/wiki/Hexatonic_scale" title="Hexatonic scale">hexatonic scale</a> with the addition of the second scale degree, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, or Do, Re, Mi, Fa, So, Ti. Half steps occur between the third and fourth (Mi and Fa), and also the seventh and first (Ti and Do) scale degrees. In particular, the interval from 7 to 1, or Ti to Do is very common. A folk tune can often be recognized as being Okinawan by noting the presence of this interval.</p>
