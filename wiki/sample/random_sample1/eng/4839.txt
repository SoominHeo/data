<p><b>René Goscinny</b> (<small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[ʁəne ɡosini]</a></span>; 14 August 1926 – 5 November 1977) was a <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> comics editor and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comics_writer" title="Comics writer">writer</a>, who is best known internationally for the <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">comic book</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ast%C3%A9rix" title="Astérix">Astérix</a></i>, which he created with illustrator <a href="/wiki/Albert_Uderzo" title="Albert Uderzo">Albert Uderzo</a>, and for his work on the comic series <i><a href="/wiki/Lucky_Luke" title="Lucky Luke">Lucky Luke</a></i> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morris_(comics)" title="Morris (comics)">Morris</a> (considered the series' golden age) and <i><a href="/wiki/Iznogoud" title="Iznogoud">Iznogoud</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Jean_Tabary" title="Jean Tabary">Jean Tabary</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Goscinny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Goscinny was born in Paris in 1926, to a family of <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jewish</a> immigrants from <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obs_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-obs-1">[1]</a></sup> His parents were Stanisław Simkha Gościnny (the surname means <i>hospitable</i> in Polish; Simkha is his Jewish name meaning <i>happiness</i>), a chemical engineer from <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>, Poland, and Anna (Hanna) Bereśniak-Gościnna from Chodorków, a small village near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C5%BBytomierz" title="Żytomierz">Żytomierz</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Polish_Republic" title="Second Polish Republic">Second Polish Republic</a> (now part of <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Claude, René's older brother was born six years earlier, on 10 December 1920. Stanisław and Anna had met in Paris and married in 1919. The Gościnnys moved to <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, two years after René's birth, because of a chemical engineer post Stanisław had obtained there. He spent a happy childhood in Buenos Aires, and studied in the French schools there. He had a habit of being the "class clown", probably to compensate for a natural shyness. He started drawing very early on, inspired by the illustrated stories which he enjoyed reading.</p>
<p>In December 1943, the year after he graduated from school, 17-year-old Goscinny lost his father to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cerebral_hemorrhage" title="Cerebral hemorrhage">cerebral hemorrhage</a>, forcing him to find a job. The next year, he got his first job, as an assistant accountant in a tire recovery factory, and when he was laid off the following year, he became a junior illustrator in an advertising agency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lambiek_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-lambiek-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Goscinny, along with his mother, left Argentina and went to New York in 1945, to join her brother Boris. To avoid service in the US military, he travelled to France to join the <a href="/wiki/French_Army" title="French Army">French Army</a> in 1946. He served at <a href="/wiki/Aubagne" title="Aubagne">Aubagne</a>, in the 141st Alpine Infantry Battalion. Promoted to senior <a href="/wiki/Corporal" title="Corporal">corporal</a>, he became the appointed artist of the regiment and drew illustrations and posters for the army.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_works">First works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Goscinny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: First works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following year, he illustrated the book <i>The Girl with The Eyes of Gold</i> and returned to New York. On his arrival Goscinny went through the most difficult period of his life. For a while, he was jobless, alone and totally broke. By 1948, though, he recovered and started working in a small studio where he became friends with future <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MAD_Magazine" title="MAD Magazine">MAD Magazine</a></i> contributors <a href="/wiki/Will_Elder" title="Will Elder">Will Elder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Davis_(cartoonist)" title="Jack Davis (cartoonist)">Jack Davis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Kurtzman" title="Harvey Kurtzman">Harvey Kurtzman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lambiek_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-lambiek-3">[3]</a></sup> Goscinny then became art director at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kunen_Publishers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kunen Publishers (page does not exist)">Kunen Publishers</a> where he wrote four books for children. Around this time he met Joseph Gillain, better known as <a href="/wiki/Jij%C3%A9" title="Jijé">Jijé</a>, and Maurice de Bevere, aka <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morris_(comics)" title="Morris (comics)">Morris</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cartoonist" title="Cartoonist">cartoonist</a> and author of the series <i><a href="/wiki/Lucky_Luke" title="Lucky Luke">Lucky Luke</a></i> (which Goscinny would write from 1955 to his death in 1977).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lambiek_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-lambiek-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Georges_Troisfontaines" title="Georges Troisfontaines">Georges Troisfontaines</a>, chief of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=World_Press_agency&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="World Press agency (page does not exist)">World Press agency</a>, convinced Goscinny to return to Paris and work for his agency as the head of the Paris office in 1951. There he met <a href="/wiki/Albert_Uderzo" title="Albert Uderzo">Albert Uderzo</a>, with whom he started a longtime collaboration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lambiek_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-lambiek-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lagardere_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-lagardere-4">[4]</a></sup> They started out with some work for <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bonnes_Soir%C3%A9es&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bonnes Soirées (page does not exist)">Bonnes Soirées</a></i>, a women's magazine for which Goscinny wrote <i>Sylvie</i>. Goscinny and Uderzo also launched the series <i>Jehan Pistolet</i> and <i>Luc Junior</i> in <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=La_Libre_Junior&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="La Libre Junior (page does not exist)">La Libre Junior</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1955, Goscinny, together with Uderzo, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Michel_Charlier" title="Jean-Michel Charlier">Jean-Michel Charlier</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_H%C3%A9brad&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jean Hébrad (page does not exist)">Jean Hébrad</a>, founded the syndicate Edipress/Edifrance. The syndicate launched publications like <i>Clairon</i> for the factory union and <i>Pistolin</i> for a chocolate company. Goscinny and Uderzo cooperated on the series <i>Bill Blanchart</i> in <i>Jeannot</i>, <i>Pistolet</i> in <i>Pistolin</i> and <i>Benjamin et Benjamine</i> in the magazine of the same name. Under the pseudonym Agostini, Goscinny wrote <i><a href="/wiki/Le_petit_Nicolas" title="Le petit Nicolas">Le Petit Nicolas</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Semp%C3%A9" title="Jean-Jacques Sempé">Jean-Jacques Sempé</a> in <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Le_Moustique&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Le Moustique (page does not exist)">Le Moustique</a></i> and later <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sud-Ouest_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sud-Ouest (magazine) (page does not exist)">Sud-Ouest</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pilote" title="Pilote">Pilote</a></i> magazines.</p>
<p>In 1956, Goscinny began a collaboration with <i><a href="/wiki/Tintin_(magazine)" title="Tintin (magazine)">Tintin</a></i> magazine. He wrote some short stories for <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jo_Angenot&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jo Angenot (page does not exist)">Jo Angenot</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Weinberg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Albert Weinberg (page does not exist)">Albert Weinberg</a>, and worked on <i>Signor Spaghetti</i> with <a href="/wiki/Dino_Attanasio" title="Dino Attanasio">Dino Attanasio</a>, <i>Monsieur Tric</i> with <a href="/wiki/Bob_de_Moor" title="Bob de Moor">Bob de Moor</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Prudence_Petitpas" title="Prudence Petitpas">Prudence Petitpas</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Mar%C3%A9chal_(comics_artist)" title="Maurice Maréchal (comics artist)">Maurice Maréchal</a>, <i>Globul le Martien</i> and <i>Alphonse</i> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tibet_(comics)" title="Tibet (comics)">Tibet</a>, <i>Strapontin</i> with <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Berckmans" title="Arthur Berckmans">Berck</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Modeste_et_Pompon" title="Modeste et Pompon">Modeste et Pompon</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Franquin" title="André Franquin">André Franquin</a>. An early creation with Uderzo, <i><a href="/wiki/Oumpah-pah" title="Oumpah-pah">Oumpah-pah</a></i>, was also adapted for serial publication in <i>Tintin</i> from 1958-1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asterix_int_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-asterix_int-5">[5]</a></sup> In addition, Goscinny appeared in the magazines <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paris-Flirt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paris-Flirt (page does not exist)">Paris-Flirt</a></i> (<i>Lili Manequin</i> with <a href="/wiki/Will_(comics)" title="Will (comics)">Will</a>) and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vaillant_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vaillant (magazine) (page does not exist)">Vaillant</a></i> (<i>Boniface et Anatole</i> with Jordom, <i>Pipsi</i> with Godard).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pilote_and_Ast.C3.A9rix"><i>Pilote</i> and <i>Astérix</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Goscinny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pilote and Astérix">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1959, the Édifrance/Édipresse syndicate started the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Belgian_comics" title="Franco-Belgian comics">Franco-Belgian comics</a> magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Pilote" title="Pilote">Pilote</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BDO-1959_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BDO-1959-6">[6]</a></sup> Goscinny became one of the most productive writers for the magazine. In the magazine's first issue, he launched his most famous creation, <i><a href="/wiki/Asterix" title="Asterix">Astérix</a></i>, with Uderzo. The series was an instant hit and remains popular worldwide. Goscinny also restarted the series <i>Le Petit Nicolas</i> and <i>Jehan Pistolet</i>, now called <i>Jehan Soupolet</i>. Goscinny also began <i>Jacquot le Mousse</i> and <i>Tromblon et Bottaclou</i> with Godard.</p>
<p>The magazine was bought by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Georges_Dargaud&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Georges Dargaud (page does not exist)">Georges Dargaud</a> in 1960, and Goscinny became editor-in-chief. He also began new series like <i>Les Divagations de Monsieur Sait-Tout</i> (with Martial), <i>La Potachologie Illustrée</i> (with <a href="/wiki/Cabu" title="Cabu">Cabu</a>), <i>Les Dingodossiers</i> (with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marcel_Gotlieb" title="Marcel Gotlieb">Gotlib</a>) and <i>La Forêt de Chênebeau</i> (with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mic_Delinx&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mic Delinx (page does not exist)">Mic Delinx</a>). With <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tabary" title="Tabary">Tabary</a>, he launched <i>Calife Haroun El Poussah</i> in <i>Record</i>, a series that was later continued in <i>Pilote</i> as <i><a href="/wiki/Iznogoud" title="Iznogoud">Iznogoud</a></i>. With <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Macherot" title="Raymond Macherot">Raymond Macherot</a> he created <i>Pantoufle</i> for <i><a href="/wiki/Spirou_(magazine)" title="Spirou (magazine)">Spirou</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Goscinny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Goscinny married Gilberte Pollaro-Millo in 1967. In 1968 their daughter <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anne_Goscinny&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anne Goscinny (page does not exist)">Anne Goscinny</a>, who also became an author, was born.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Goscinny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GoscinnyRen%C3%A9Grabstein.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/GoscinnyRen%C3%A9Grabstein.JPG/220px-GoscinnyRen%C3%A9Grabstein.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/GoscinnyRen%C3%A9Grabstein.JPG/330px-GoscinnyRen%C3%A9Grabstein.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/GoscinnyRen%C3%A9Grabstein.JPG/440px-GoscinnyRen%C3%A9Grabstein.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GoscinnyRen%C3%A9Grabstein.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
René Goscinny's tombstone in Nice (F)</div>
</div>

<p>Goscinny died at 51, in Paris of <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrest" title="Cardiac arrest">cardiac arrest</a> on 5 November 1977, during a routine <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_stress_test" title="Cardiac stress test">stress test</a> at his doctor's office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He was buried in the Jewish Cemetery of Nice. In accordance with his will, most of his money was transferred to the chief rabbinate of France.</p>
<p>After Goscinny's death, Uderzo continued to produce the Asterix series, although at a much slower pace, until passing the series over in 2011 to <a href="/wiki/Jean-Yves_Ferri" title="Jean-Yves Ferri">Jean-Yves Ferri</a> (script) and <a href="/wiki/Didier_Conrad" title="Didier Conrad">Didier Conrad</a> (art).</p>
<p>In a tribute to Goscinny, Uderzo gave his likeness to one of the characters in <i>L'Odyssée d'Astérix</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Asterix_and_the_Black_Gold" title="Asterix and the Black Gold">Asterix and the Black Gold</a>").</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Goscinny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>1974: <a href="/wiki/Adamson_Awards" title="Adamson Awards">Adamson Award</a> for best international comic strip artist, Sweden</li>
<li>2005: Inducted in the <a href="/wiki/Eisner_Award" title="Eisner Award">Will Eisner Hall of Fame</a> as a Judges' choice, U.S.</li>
</ul>
<p>Since 1996, the <a href="/wiki/Angoul%C3%AAme_International_Comics_Festival_Ren%C3%A9_Goscinny_award" title="Angoulême International Comics Festival René Goscinny award">René Goscinny Award</a> is presented at the yearly <a href="/wiki/Angoul%C3%AAme_International_Comics_Festival" title="Angoulême International Comics Festival">Angoulême International Comics Festival</a> in France as an encouragement for young comic writers.</p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Index_Translationum" title="Index Translationum">Index Translationum</a>, Goscinny, as of April 2008, was the 22nd most translated author, with 1,800 translations of his work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> (This figure does not take in account his additional work under pseudonyms.)</p>
