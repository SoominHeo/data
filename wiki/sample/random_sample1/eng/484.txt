<p><b>Presidential elections</b> were held in <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a> on 15 March 2014, with a second round on 29 March 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>After a first round, in which no candidate secured majority, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fico" title="Robert Fico">Robert Fico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andrej_Kiska" title="Andrej Kiska">Andrej Kiska</a> qualified to second round. On 29 March 2014, Kiska won the second round of the elections after securing 59.38% of popular vote, compared to Fico 40.61%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kiska succeeded incumbent <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Ga%C5%A1parovi%C4%8D" title="Ivan Gašparovič">Ivan Gašparovič</a>, who was unable to run due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Term_limits" title="Term limits">term limits</a> established by the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Slovakia" title="Constitution of Slovakia">Constitution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_round">First round</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slovak_presidential_election,_2014&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: First round">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Candidates">Candidates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slovak_presidential_election,_2014&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Candidates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>All candidates who wished to contest the presidency were required to collect no less than 15.000 individual signatures in order to qualify for the race. In addition they had to officially submit their candidacy to the election commission in writing no later than 9 January 2014. At least one would-be candidate, Leonid Chovanec, a citizen from <a href="/wiki/Ko%C5%A1ice" title="Košice">Košice</a> who intended to run as an independent candidate, gave up his candidacy on January 6 citing "unequal conditions for people in their access to the candidacy for the post of president”. He did not manage to collect the 15,000 signatures to support his presidential bid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> On January 9, Speaker of parliament <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pavol_Paska" title="Pavol Paska">Pavol Paska</a> officially announced that the following candidates had been approved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Campaign">Campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slovak_presidential_election,_2014&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The political right was fragmented; the People’s Platform (an alliance of three right-wing parties)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-7">[7]</a></sup> failed to agree on a common candidate. <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Movement" title="Christian Democratic Movement">Christian Democratic Movement</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Most-Hid" title="Most-Hid">Most-Hid</a> announced that they supported Pavol Hrušovský, but the third party, <a href="/wiki/Slovak_Democratic_and_Christian_Union_%E2%80%93_Democratic_Party" title="Slovak Democratic and Christian Union – Democratic Party">Slovak Democratic and Christian Union – Democratic Party</a> (SDKÚ-DS) originally refused to agree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-8">[8]</a></sup> According to multiple sources, SDKÚ would have preferred former Prime Minister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iveta_Radicova" title="Iveta Radicova">Iveta Radičová</a> as their candidate, however, she made clear that she did not want to run for president again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Later, after Fico announced its candidacy, SDKÚ-DS eventually decided to support Hrušovský. Shortly before first round of elections some members of SDKÚ-DS and Most-Híd suggested their supporters should choose different candidate in the upcoming elections, following polls which gave Hrušovský only small chance of progressing to second round.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Robert_Fico">Robert Fico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slovak_presidential_election,_2014&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Robert Fico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The ruling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smer" title="Smer">Smer</a> party selected incumbent Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fico" title="Robert Fico">Robert Fico</a> as its official candidate. According to opinion polls, Fico dominated his opponents, who were divided, and he would advance to the second round with a double digit lead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In addition, polls showed that he was most likely to win the presidential election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> However, his party would have had to replace him as PM and party leader, meaning that Smer would have had to produce a popular and credible leader to follow Fico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-7">[7]</a></sup> As early as February, Fico was endorsed by the <a href="/wiki/National_trade_union_center" title="National trade union center">National trade union center</a> when The Confederation of Labour Unions (KOZ) officially called upon its members to vote for Prime Minister Robert Fico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Andrej_Kiska">Andrej Kiska</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slovak_presidential_election,_2014&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Andrej Kiska">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Andrej Kiska was the first candidate to officially announce his presidential candidacy and his billboards appeared around Slovakia long before the names of his competitors were known. He was officially running as an independent candidate and had no political background whatsoever. Prior to the first round Kiskas campaign mostly stressed his independence, including his financial independence, which mean that he didn't have to report to any political parties. Furthermore, he promoted his lack of political involvement as one of his strengths, while his website featured the slogan “The First Independent President”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Eventually, Andrej Kiska won the election on 29 March with nearly 60% of the popular vote, becoming the first president in the history of Slovak republic never to have been a member of the Communist Party, nor any other political party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-auto-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Radoslav_Proch.C3.A1zka">Radoslav Procházka</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slovak_presidential_election,_2014&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Radoslav Procházka">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The young constitutional lawyer Prochazka, who campaigned as the conservative candidate, was formerly a member with the Christian Democratic Movement (KDH), but ran as an independent. His campaign mainly targeted the large catholic population, with issues such as abortion, same-sex marriage and traditional family values. His campaign also stressed his youth as a strength, as well as his background as a constitutional lawyer, with his official slogans reading: "A young president? Why not!" (<a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">Slovak</a>: <span lang="sk" xml:lang="sk"><i>Mladý prezident? Prečo nie!</i></span>) and "Strong president. I will protect your rights!" (<a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">Slovak</a>: <span lang="sk" xml:lang="sk"><i>Silný prezident. Budem chrániť vaše práva!</i></span>)</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Peter_Osusk.C3.BD">Peter Osuský</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slovak_presidential_election,_2014&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Peter Osuský">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Freedom_and_Solidarity" title="Freedom and Solidarity">Freedom and Solidarity</a> (SaS) party announced on 11 June 2013 that Peter Osuský would be their candidate for the presidency. Due to poor results in polls, Osuský decided to pull off his candidature. SaS reacted by supporting Radoslav Procházka for upcoming first round of elections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>During the second round all independent candidates and some right-wing parties supported Kiska. These candidates secured more than 60% votes including the ones received by Kiska in first round. Fico secured 12% more votes than in the first round. High turn out in second round helped Kiska defeat Fico by 20 points.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Campaign_2">Campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slovak_presidential_election,_2014&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The second round of the election, which pitted Robert Fico against Andrej Kiska, saw the candidates engage in a series of televised presidential debates. Fico repeatedly accused Kiska of having links to the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scientology" title="Church of Scientology">Church of Scientology</a>, but stopped short of calling him a member. Kiska however, confirmed his catholic faith, and filed criminal charges against Fico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> All the defeated candidates from the first round subsequently backed Kiska, while international social-democrats such as French president <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francois_Hollande" title="Francois Hollande">Francois Hollande</a> and Speaker of the European Parliament <a href="/wiki/Martin_Schulz" title="Martin Schulz">Martin Schultz</a> endorsed Fico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
