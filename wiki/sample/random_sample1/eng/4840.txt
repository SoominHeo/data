<p><b>René-Théophile-Hyacinthe Laennec</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> (<small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[laɛnɛk]</a></span>; 17 February 1781 – 13 August 1826) was a French <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a>. He invented the <a href="/wiki/Stethoscope" title="Stethoscope">stethoscope</a> in 1816, while working at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H%C3%B4pital_Necker" title="Hôpital Necker">Hôpital Necker</a>, and pioneered its use in diagnosing various chest conditions.</p>
<p>He became a lecturer at the <a href="/wiki/Coll%C3%A8ge_de_France" title="Collège de France">Collège de France</a> in 1822 and professor of medicine in 1823. His final appointments were that of Head of the Medical Clinic at the <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B4pital_de_la_Charit%C3%A9" title="Hôpital de la Charité">Hôpital de la Charité</a> and <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">Professor</a> at the Collège de France. He died of tuberculosis in 1826 at the age of 45.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_personality">Early life and personality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Laennec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and personality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Laennec was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quimper,_Brittany" title="Quimper, Brittany">Quimper</a> (<a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>). His mother died of tuberculosis when he was five years old, and he went to live with his great-uncle the <a href="/wiki/Abb%C3%A9" title="Abbé">Abbé</a> Laennec (a priest). <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>At the age of twelve, he proceeded to <a href="/wiki/Nantes" title="Nantes">Nantes</a> where his uncle, Guillaime-François Laennec, worked in the faculty of medicine at the university. Laennec was a gifted student. He learned English and German and began his medical studies under his uncle's direction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>His father (a lawyer) later discouraged him from continuing as a doctor and René then had a period of time where he took long walks in the country, danced, studied Greek and wrote poetry. However, in 1799 he returned to study. Laennec studied medicine at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">University of Paris</a> under several famous physicians, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dupuytren" title="Dupuytren">Dupuytren</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jean-Nicolas_Corvisart" title="Jean-Nicolas Corvisart">Jean-Nicolas Corvisart-Desmarets</a>. There he was trained to use sound as a diagnostic aid. Corvisart advocated the re-introduction of <a href="/wiki/Percussion_(medicine)" title="Percussion (medicine)">percussion</a> during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Invention_of_the_stethoscope">Invention of the stethoscope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Laennec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Invention of the stethoscope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>René Laennec wrote the classic treatise <i>De l'Auscultation Médiate</i>, published in August 1819<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laennec_1819_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Laennec_1819-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laennec_Forbes_translation_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Laennec_Forbes_translation-5">[5]</a></sup> The preface reads:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>In 1816, I was consulted by a young woman laboring under general symptoms of diseased heart, and in whose case percussion and the application of the hand were of little avail on account of the great degree of fatness. The other method just mentioned [direct auscultation] being rendered inadmissible by the age and sex of the patient, I happened to recollect a simple and well-known fact in acoustics, ... the great distinctness with which we hear the scratch of a pin at one end of a piece of wood on applying our ear to the other. Immediately, on this suggestion, I rolled a quire of paper into a kind of cylinder and applied one end of it to the region of the heart and the other to my ear, and was not a little surprised and pleased to find that I could thereby perceive the action of the heart in a manner much more clear and distinct than I had ever been able to do by the immediate application of my ear.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Laennec had discovered that the new <a href="/wiki/Stethoscope" title="Stethoscope">stethoscope</a> was superior to the normally used method of placing the ear over the chest, particularly if the patient was overweight. A stethoscope also avoided the embarrassment of placing the ear against the chest of a woman.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Laennec is said to have seen schoolchildren playing with long, hollow sticks in the days leading up to his innovation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scherer_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scherer-6">[6]</a></sup> The children held their ear to one end of the stick while the opposite end was scratched with a pin, the stick transmitted and amplified the scratch. His skill as a flautist may also have inspired him. He built his first instrument as a 25 cm by 2.5 cm hollow wooden cylinder, which he later refined to comprise three detachable parts.</p>
<p>His clinical work allowed him to follow chest patients from bedside to the autopsy table. He was therefore able to correlate sounds captured by his new instruments with specific pathological changes in the chest, in effect pioneering a new non-invasive diagnostic tool. Laennec was the first to classify and discuss the terms <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rales" title="Rales">rales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhonchi" title="Rhonchi">rhonchi</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crepitance" title="Crepitance">crepitance</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Egophony" title="Egophony">egophony</a> – terms that doctors now use on a daily basis during physical exams and diagnoses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scherer_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scherer-6">[6]</a></sup> In February 1818, he presented his findings in a talk at the Académie de Médecine, later publishing his findings in 1819.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Laennec coined the phrase <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediate_auscultation" title="Mediate auscultation">mediate auscultation</a></i> (indirect listening), as opposed to the popular practice at the time of directly placing the ear on the chest (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immediate_auscultation" title="Immediate auscultation">immediate auscultation</a>). He named his instrument the <a href="/wiki/Stethoscope" title="Stethoscope">stethoscope</a>, from <i>stethos</i> (chest), and <i>skopos</i> (examination).</p>

<p>Not all doctors readily embraced the new stethoscope. Although the <i>New England Journal of Medicine</i> reported the invention of the stethoscope two years later in 1821, as late as 1885, a professor of medicine stated, "He that hath ears to hear, let him use his ears and not a stethoscope." Even the founder of the <a href="/wiki/American_Heart_Association" title="American Heart Association">American Heart Association</a>, L. A. Connor (1866–1950) carried a silk handkerchief with him to place on the wall of the chest for ear auscultation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Laennec often referred to the stethoscope as "the cylinder," and as he neared death only a few years later, he bequeathed his own stethoscope to his nephew, referring to it as "the greatest legacy of my life."</p>
<p>The modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binaural_stethoscope" title="Binaural stethoscope">binaural stethoscope</a> with two ear pieces was invented in 1851 by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Leared" title="Arthur Leared">Arthur Leared</a>. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=George_Cammann&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="George Cammann (page does not exist)">George Cammann</a> perfected the design of the instrument for commercial production in 1852, which has become the standard ever since.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_medical_contributions">Other medical contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Laennec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other medical contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Laennec_-_Th%C3%A9obald_Chartran.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="842" data-file-width="937" height="198" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Laennec_-_Th%C3%A9obald_Chartran.jpg/220px-Laennec_-_Th%C3%A9obald_Chartran.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Laennec_-_Th%C3%A9obald_Chartran.jpg/330px-Laennec_-_Th%C3%A9obald_Chartran.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Laennec_-_Th%C3%A9obald_Chartran.jpg/440px-Laennec_-_Th%C3%A9obald_Chartran.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Laennec_-_Th%C3%A9obald_Chartran.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Laennec <a href="/wiki/Auscultation" title="Auscultation">auscultates</a> a patient before his students</div>

<p>He developed the understanding of <a href="/wiki/Peritonitis" title="Peritonitis">peritonitis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cirrhosis" title="Cirrhosis">cirrhosis</a>. Although the disease of cirrhosis was known, Laennec gave cirrhosis its name, using the Greek word (<i>kirrhos</i>, tawny) that referred to the tawny, yellow nodules characteristic of the disease.</p>
<p>He coined the term <a href="/wiki/Melanoma" title="Melanoma">melanoma</a> and described <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metastases" title="Metastases">metastases</a> of melanoma to the lungs. In 1804, while still a medical student, he was the first person to lecture on melanoma. This lecture was subsequently published in 1805. Laennec actually used the term 'melanose,' which he derived from the Greek (<i>mela</i>, <i>melan</i>) for "black." Over the years, there were bitter exchanges between Laennec and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dupuytren" title="Dupuytren">Dupuytren</a>, the latter objecting that there was no mention of his work in this area and his role in its discovery.</p>
<p>He also studied <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>. Coincidentally, his nephew, Mériadec Laennec, is said to have diagnosed tuberculosis in Laennec using Laennec’s stethoscope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scherer_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Scherer-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Laennec advocated objective scientific observation. Professor <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Ward_Richardson" title="Benjamin Ward Richardson">Benjamin Ward Richardson</a> stated in <i>Disciples of Aesculapius</i> that "the true student of medicine reads Laennec's treatise on mediate auscultation and the use of the stethoscope once in two years at least as long as he is in practice. It ranks with the original work of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vesalius" title="Vesalius">Vesalius</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Harvey" title="William Harvey">Harvey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a>." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_views">Religious views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Laennec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Religious views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Laennec "was intensely religious and was a devout Catholic all his life".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newadvent.org_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-newadvent.org-10">[10]</a></sup> He was noted as a very kind man and his charity to the poor became proverbial. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austin_Flint" title="Austin Flint">Austin Flint</a>, the 1884 president of the <a href="/wiki/American_Medical_Association" title="American Medical Association">American Medical Association</a>, said that "Laennec's life affords a striking instance among others disproving the vulgar error that the pursuit of science is unfavourable to religious faith." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newadvent.org_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-newadvent.org-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_John_Forbes" title="Sir John Forbes">Sir John Forbes</a>' annotated translation of Laennec's treatise, it is reported:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Laennec was a man of the greatest probity, habitually observant of his religious and social duties. He was a sincere Christian, and a good Catholic, adhering to his religion and his church through good report and bad report. " His death (says M. Bayle) was that of a Christian. Supported by the hope of a better life, prepared by the constant practice of virtue, he saw his end approach with much composure and resignation. His religious principles, imbibed with his earliest knowledge, were strengthened by the conviction of his maturer reason. He took no pains to conceal them when they were disadvantageous to his worldly interests; and he made no boast of them, when their avowal might have been a title to favour and advancement."</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <i>A Treatise on the Diseases of the Chest and on Mediate Auscultation</i> (1838 [1835]), Chapter "Life Of The Author", page xxvii<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laennec_Forbes_translation_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Laennec_Forbes_translation-5">[5]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Laennec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rene-Theophile-Hyacinthe_Laennec2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2877" data-file-width="2398" height="264" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Rene-Theophile-Hyacinthe_Laennec2.jpg/220px-Rene-Theophile-Hyacinthe_Laennec2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Rene-Theophile-Hyacinthe_Laennec2.jpg/330px-Rene-Theophile-Hyacinthe_Laennec2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Rene-Theophile-Hyacinthe_Laennec2.jpg/440px-Rene-Theophile-Hyacinthe_Laennec2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Laennec%27s_cirrhosis" title="Laennec's cirrhosis">Laennec's cirrhosis</a> refers to the appearance of regenerated liver, comprising small lobules separated by a fine, fibrous tissue; <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Laennec%27s_thrombus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Laennec's thrombus (page does not exist)">Laennec's thrombus</a> is an antenatal thrombus in the heart</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Laennec%27s_pearls&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Laennec's pearls (page does not exist)">Laennec's pearls</a> refer to sputum produced by asthmatics.</li>
<li><i>Hamman's murmur</i>, also known as <i>Laënnec–Hamman symptom</i>, <i>Laënnec–Müller–von Bergmann–Hamman symptom</i>, or <i>Hamman's crunch</i> is a crunching sound heard over the precordium due to spontaneous mediastinal emphysema.</li>
<li>At the <a href="/wiki/Claude_Bernard_University_Lyon_1" title="Claude Bernard University Lyon 1">Université Claude Bernard Lyon 1</a> one of the four medical schools is named after Laennec.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Laennec_in_fiction">Laennec in fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Laennec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Laennec in fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A Rene Laennec appears in Rudyard Kipling's <i><a href="/wiki/Rewards_and_Fairies" title="Rewards and Fairies">Rewards and Fairies</a></i>, the second of two books where two children, Dan and Una, encounter past inhabitants of England. In the short section "Marlake Witches", set during the Napoleonic Wars, Una meets a consumptive young lady who speaks of being treated by a French doctor, a <a href="/wiki/Parole#Prisoners_of_war" title="Parole">prisoner on parole</a>, one Rene Laennec. This prisoner discusses with a local herbalist the use of 'wooden trumpets' for listening to patients' chests, much to the distrust of the local doctor. Obviously, Kipling was aware of Laennec's work and invented an English connection.</p>
<p>He was the subject of a 1949 French film <i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_Laennec" title="Doctor Laennec">Doctor Laennec</a></i> in which he was played by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Blanchar" title="Pierre Blanchar">Pierre Blanchar</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Laennec.27s_landmarks_in_Paris">Laennec's landmarks in Paris</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Laennec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Laennec's landmarks in Paris">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the exterior wall of the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H%C3%B4pital_Necker" title="Hôpital Necker">Hôpital Necker</a> – Enfants Malades", where Laennec wrote <i>Mediate auscultation</i>, near the entrance of the hospital in 149, Rue de Sèvres, there is a marble memorial tablet with an engraved portrait of Laennec and this inscription: "Dans cet hôpital Laennec découvrit l'auscultation. 1781–1826". Some of the oldest buildings of the hospital can be seen on the same front of this large and modern medical area.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
