<p><b>René Frédéric Thom</b> (<small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[ʁəne tõ]</a></span>; 2 September 1923 – 25 October 2002) was a French <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a>. He made his reputation as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topologist" title="Topologist">topologist</a>, moving on to aspects of what would be called <a href="/wiki/Singularity_theory" title="Singularity theory">singularity theory</a>; he became world-famous among the wider academic community and the educated general public for one aspect of this latter interest, his work as founder of <a href="/wiki/Catastrophe_theory" title="Catastrophe theory">catastrophe theory</a> (later developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erik_Christopher_Zeeman" title="Erik Christopher Zeeman">Erik Christopher Zeeman</a>). He received the <a href="/wiki/Fields_Medal" title="Fields Medal">Fields Medal</a> in 1958.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ren%C3%A9_Thom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>René Thom was born in <a href="/wiki/Montb%C3%A9liard" title="Montbéliard">Montbéliard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doubs" title="Doubs">Doubs</a>. He was educated at the <a href="/wiki/Lyc%C3%A9e_Saint-Louis" title="Lycée Saint-Louis">Lycée Saint-Louis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Normale_Sup%C3%A9rieure" title="École Normale Supérieure">École Normale Supérieure</a>, both in Paris. He received his PhD in 1951 from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">University of Paris</a>. His thesis, titled <i>Espaces fibrés en sphères et carrés de Steenrod</i> (<i>Sphere bundles and Steenrod squares</i>), was written under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Henri_Cartan" title="Henri Cartan">Henri Cartan</a>. The foundations of <a href="/wiki/Cobordism" title="Cobordism">cobordism</a> theory, for which he received the Fields Medal at Edinburgh in 1958, were already present in his thesis.</p>
<p>After a fellowship in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, he went on to teach at the Universities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_Joseph_Fourier" title="Université Joseph Fourier">Grenoble</a> (1953–1954) and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Strasbourg" title="University of Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a> (1954–1963), where he was appointed Professor in 1957. In 1964, he moved to the <a href="/wiki/Institut_des_Hautes_%C3%89tudes_Scientifiques" title="Institut des Hautes Études Scientifiques">Institut des Hautes Études Scientifiques</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Bures-sur-Yvette" title="Bures-sur-Yvette">Bures-sur-Yvette</a>. He was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Brouwer_Medal" title="Brouwer Medal">Brouwer Medal</a> in 1970, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Prix_Scientifique_de_la_Ville_de_Paris&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Grand Prix Scientifique de la Ville de Paris (page does not exist)">Grand Prix Scientifique de la Ville de Paris</a> in 1974, and became a Member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academie_des_Sciences" title="Academie des Sciences">Academie des Sciences</a> of Paris in 1976.</p>
<p>While René Thom is most known to the public for his development of <a href="/wiki/Catastrophe_theory" title="Catastrophe theory">catastrophe theory</a> between 1968 and 1972, his earlier work was on <a href="/wiki/Differential_topology" title="Differential topology">differential topology</a>. In the early 1950s it concerned what are now called <a href="/wiki/Thom_space" title="Thom space">Thom spaces</a>, <a href="/wiki/Characteristic_class" title="Characteristic class">characteristic classes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobordism_theory" title="Cobordism theory">cobordism theory</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thom_transversality_theorem" title="Thom transversality theorem">Thom transversality theorem</a>. Another example of this line of work is the <a href="/wiki/Thom_conjecture" title="Thom conjecture">Thom conjecture</a>, versions of which have been investigated using <a href="/wiki/Gauge_theory" title="Gauge theory">gauge theory</a>. From the mid 1950s he moved into <a href="/wiki/Singularity_theory" title="Singularity theory">singularity theory</a>, of which catastrophe theory is just one aspect, and in a series of deep (and at the time obscure) papers between 1960 and 1969 developed the theory of <a href="/wiki/Topologically_stratified_space" title="Topologically stratified space">stratified sets</a> and stratified maps, proving a basic stratified isotopy theorem describing the local conical structure of <a href="/wiki/Whitney_conditions" title="Whitney conditions">Whitney stratified sets</a>, now known as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thom-Mather_isotopy_theorem&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thom-Mather isotopy theorem (page does not exist)">Thom-Mather isotopy theorem</a>. Much of his work on <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stratified_sets&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stratified sets (page does not exist)">stratified sets</a> was developed so as to understand the notion of topologically stable maps, and to eventually prove the result that the set of topologically stable mappings between two smooth manifolds is a <a href="/wiki/Dense_set" title="Dense set">dense set</a>. Thom's lectures on the stability of differentiable mappings, given at Bonn in 1960, were written up by <a href="/wiki/Harold_Levine" title="Harold Levine">Harold Levine</a> and published in the proceedings of a year long symposium on singularities at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liverpool_University" title="Liverpool University">Liverpool University</a> during 1969-70, edited by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terry_Wall" title="Terry Wall">Terry Wall</a>. The proof of the density of topologically stable mappings was completed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Mather_(mathematician)" title="John Mather (mathematician)">John Mather</a> in 1970, based on the ideas developed by Thom in the previous ten years. A coherent detailed account was published in 1976 by C. Gibson, K. Wirthmuller, <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Looijenga" title="Eduard Looijenga">E. Looijenga</a> and A. du Plessis.</p>
<p>During the last twenty years of his life Thom's published work was mainly in philosophy and epistemology, and he undertook a reevaluation of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>'s writings on science. In 1992, he was one of eighteen academics who sent a letter to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" title="Cambridge University">Cambridge University</a> protesting against plans to award <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Derrida" title="Jacques Derrida">Jacques Derrida</a> an honorary doctorate.</p>
<p>Beyond Thom's contributions to algebraic topology, he studied differentiable mappings, through the study of <a href="/wiki/Generic_property" title="Generic property">generic properties</a>.</p>
