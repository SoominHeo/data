<p><b>René-Robert Cavelier, Sieur de La Salle</b>, or <b>Robert de La Salle</b> (November 22, 1643 – March 19, 1687) was a <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_explorers" title="List of explorers">explorer</a>. He explored the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> region of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>. La Salle claimed the entire Mississippi River basin for France.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span></h2>
<p>Robert de La Salle was born on November 22, 1643, into a comfortably well-off family in <a href="/wiki/Rouen" title="Rouen">Rouen</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, in the parish Saint-Herbland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> When La Salle was younger he enjoyed science and nature. As a man, he studied with the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuit</a> religious order and became a member after taking initial vows in 1660.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[a]</a></sup> At his request on March 27, 1667, after he was in Canada, he was released from the Society of Jesus after citing "moral weaknesses."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bio-4">[3]</a></sup> Although he left the order, never took final vows in it, and later became hostile to it, historians sometimes described him incorrectly as a priest or a leader.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span></h2>
<p>La Salle never married,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> but has been linked to <a href="/wiki/Madeleine_de_Roybon_d%27Allonne" title="Madeleine de Roybon d'Allonne">Madeleine de Roybon d'Allonne</a>, an early settler of <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dupre_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dupre-6">[5]</a></sup> His older brother, Jean Cavelier, was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulpician" title="Sulpician">Sulpician</a> priest. His parents were Jean Cavelier and Catherine Geest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-bio-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Explorations">Explorations</span></h2>
<p>Required to reject his father's legacy when he joined the Jesuits, La Salle was nearly destitute when he traveled as a prospective colonist to North America. He sailed for <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a> in the spring of 1666.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> His brother Jean, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulpician_Order" title="Sulpician Order">Sulpician</a> priest, had moved there the year before. La Salle was granted a <i><a href="/wiki/Seigneurial_system_of_New_France" title="Seigneurial system of New France">seigneurie</a></i> on land at the western end of the <a href="/wiki/Island_of_Montreal" title="Island of Montreal">Island of Montreal</a>, which became known as <i><a href="/wiki/Lachine,_Quebec" title="Lachine, Quebec">Lachine</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> (This was apparently from the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i>la Chine</i>, meaning <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Some sources say the name referred to La Salle's desire to find a route to China, though the evidence for this claim is unclear and has been disputed.)</p>
<p>La Salle immediately began to issue land grants, set up a village and learn the languages of the native people, mostly Mohawk in this area. The Mohawk told him of a great river, called the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio</a>, which flowed into the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>. Thinking the river flowed into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, La Salle began to plan for expeditions to find a western passage to China. He sought and received permission from Governor <a href="/wiki/Daniel_de_R%C3%A9my_de_Courcelle" title="Daniel de Rémy de Courcelle">Daniel Courcelle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intendant" title="Intendant">Intendant</a> <a href="/wiki/Jean_Talon" title="Jean Talon">Jean Talon</a> to embark on the enterprise. He sold his interests in Lachine to finance the venture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> In 1682, he named the area Louisiana after King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_XIV" title="Louis XIV">Louis XIV</a> of France.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fort_Frontenac">Fort Frontenac</span></h3>
<p>On July 12, 1673, the Governor of New France, <a href="/wiki/Louis_de_Buade_de_Frontenac" title="Louis de Buade de Frontenac">Louis de Buade de Frontenac</a>, arrived at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Cataraqui_River" title="Cataraqui River">Cataraqui River</a> to meet with leaders of the Five Nations of the Iroquois to encourage them to trade with the French. While the groups met and exchanged gifts, Frontenac's men, led by René-Robert Cavelier de La Salle, hastily constructed a rough wooden palisade on a point of land by a shallow, sheltered bay. Originally the fort was named Fort Cataraqui but was later renamed <a href="/wiki/Fort_Frontenac" title="Fort Frontenac">Fort Frontenac</a> by de La Salle in honor of La Salle's patron. The purpose of Fort Frontenac was to control the lucrative fur trade in the Great Lakes Basin to the west. The fort was also meant to be a bulwark against the English and Dutch, who were competing with the French for control of the fur trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> La Salle was left in command of the fort in 1673.</p>
<p>Thanks to his powerful protector, the discoverer managed, during a voyage to France in 1674–75, to secure for himself the grant of Fort Cataraqui and acquired letters of nobility for himself and his descendants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-bio-4">[3]</a></sup> With Frontenac's support, he received not only a <a href="/wiki/Fur_trade" title="Fur trade">fur trade</a> concession, with permission to establish frontier forts, but also a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Title_of_nobility" title="Title of nobility">title of nobility</a>. He returned and rebuilt Frontenac in stone. An <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Heritage_Trust" title="Ontario Heritage Trust">Ontario Heritage Trust</a> plaque describes René-Robert Cavelier de La Salle at Cataraqui as "[a] major figure in the expansion of the French fur trade into the Lake Ontario region, Using the fort as a base, he undertook expeditions to the west and southwest in the interest of developing a vast fur-trading empire."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Henri_de_Tonti" title="Henri de Tonti">Henri de Tonti</a> joined his explorations as his lieutenant.</p>

<p>In early 1679, La Salle's expedition built <a href="/wiki/Fort_Conti" title="Fort Conti">Fort Conti</a> at the mouth of the Niagara River on Lake Ontario. There they loaded supplies from <a href="/wiki/Fort_Frontenac" title="Fort Frontenac">Fort Frontenac</a> into smaller boats (<a href="/wiki/Canoe" title="Canoe">canoes</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bateaux" title="Bateaux">bateaux</a>), so they could continue up the shallow and swiftly flowing lower <a href="/wiki/Niagara_River" title="Niagara River">Niagara River</a> to what is now the location of <a href="/wiki/Lewiston,_New_York" title="Lewiston, New York">Lewiston, New York</a>. There the Iroquois had a well-established <a href="/wiki/Portage" title="Portage">portage</a> route which bypassed the rapids and the <a href="/wiki/Waterfall#Types" title="Waterfall">cataract</a> later known as <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Falls" title="Niagara Falls">Niagara Falls</a>.</p>
<p>La Salle built <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Griffon" title="Le Griffon">Le Griffon</a></i>, a seven-cannon, 45-ton <a href="/wiki/Barque" title="Barque">barque</a>, on the upper Niagara River at or near <a href="/wiki/Cayuga_Creek" title="Cayuga Creek">Cayuga Creek</a>. She was launched on August 7, 1679.</p>
<p>La Salle sailed in <i>Le Griffon</i> up <a href="/wiki/Lake_Erie" title="Lake Erie">Lake Erie</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Huron" title="Lake Huron">Lake Huron</a>, then up Huron to <a href="/wiki/Michilimackinac" title="Michilimackinac">Michilimackinac</a> and on to present-day <a href="/wiki/Green_Bay,_Wisconsin" title="Green Bay, Wisconsin">Green Bay, Wisconsin</a>. <i>Le Griffon</i> left for Niagara with a load of furs, but was never seen again. La Salle continued with his men in canoes down the western shore of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a>, rounding the southern end to the mouth of the Miami River (now <a href="/wiki/St._Joseph_River_(Lake_Michigan)" title="St. Joseph River (Lake Michigan)">St. Joseph River</a>), where they built a stockade in January 1680. They called it <a href="/wiki/Fort_Miami_(Michigan)" title="Fort Miami (Michigan)">Fort Miami</a> (now known as <a href="/wiki/St._Joseph,_Michigan" title="St. Joseph, Michigan">St. Joseph, Michigan</a>). There they waited for Tonti and his party, who had crossed the Lower Michigan peninsula on foot.</p>
<p>Tonti arrived on November 20; on December 3, the entire party set off up the St. Joseph, which they followed until they had to take a portage at present-day <a href="/wiki/South_Bend,_Indiana" title="South Bend, Indiana">South Bend, Indiana</a>. They crossed to the <a href="/wiki/Kankakee_River" title="Kankakee River">Kankakee River</a> and followed it to the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_River" title="Illinois River">Illinois River</a>. There they built <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Cr%C3%A8vecoeur" title="Fort Crèvecoeur">Fort Crèvecoeur</a>, which later led to the development of present-day <a href="/wiki/Peoria,_Illinois" title="Peoria, Illinois">Peoria, Illinois</a>. La Salle set off on foot for Fort Frontenac for supplies. While he was gone, the soldiers at Ft. Crevecoeur <a href="/wiki/Mutiny" title="Mutiny">mutinied</a>, destroyed the fort, and exiled Tonti, whom La Salle had left in charge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FC_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FC-12">[11]</a></sup> Later La Salle captured the mutineers on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ontario" title="Lake Ontario">Lake Ontario</a>. He eventually rendezvoused with Tonti at <a href="/wiki/St._Ignace,_Michigan" title="St. Ignace, Michigan">St. Ignace, Michigan</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_expeditions">Final expeditions</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lasalle_au_Mississippi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="319" data-file-width="300" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Lasalle_au_Mississippi.jpg/220px-Lasalle_au_Mississippi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b5/Lasalle_au_Mississippi.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lasalle_au_Mississippi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Claiming Louisiana for France</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>La Salle reassembled a party for another major expedition. In 1682 he departed <a href="/wiki/Fort_Crevecoeur" title="Fort Crevecoeur">Fort Crevecoeur</a> with a group of Frenchmen and Indians and canoed down the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>. He named the Mississippi basin <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_(New_France)" title="Louisiana (New France)"><i>La Louisiane</i></a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HTsalle_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-HTsalle-13">[12]</a></sup> in honor of <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV</a> and claimed it for France. At what later became the site of <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis, Tennessee</a>, La Salle built the small <a href="/wiki/Fort_Prudhomme" title="Fort Prudhomme">Fort Prudhomme</a>. On April 9, 1682, at the mouth of the Mississippi River near modern <a href="/wiki/Venice,_Louisiana" title="Venice, Louisiana">Venice, Louisiana</a>, La Salle buried an engraved plate and a cross, claiming the territory for France.</p>
<p>In 1683, on his return voyage, La Salle established <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Saint_Louis_(Illinois)" title="Fort Saint Louis (Illinois)">Fort Saint Louis</a> of Illinois, at <a href="/wiki/Starved_Rock_State_Park" title="Starved Rock State Park">Starved Rock</a> on the Illinois River, to replace Fort Crevecoeur. He appointed Tonti to command the fort while La Salle traveled to France for supplies. On July 24, 1684,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HTsalle_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-HTsalle-13">[12]</a></sup> La Salle departed France and returned to America with a large expedition designed to establish a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_colony" title="French colony">French colony</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, at the mouth of the Mississippi River. They had four ships and 300 colonists. The expedition was plagued by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pirate" title="Pirate">pirates</a>, hostile Indians, and poor navigation. One ship was lost to pirates in the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>, a second sank in the inlets of <a href="/wiki/Matagorda_Bay" title="Matagorda Bay">Matagorda Bay</a>, and a third ran aground there. They founded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Saint_Louis_(Texas)" title="Fort Saint Louis (Texas)">Fort Saint Louis</a>, on <a href="/wiki/Garcitas_Creek" title="Garcitas Creek">Garcitas Creek</a> in present-day <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_Texas" title="Victoria, Texas">Victoria, Texas</a> vicinity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HTsalle_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-HTsalle-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>La Salle led a group eastward on foot on three occasions to try to locate the mouth of the Mississippi.</p>
<p>During a final search for the Mississippi River, some of La Salle's remaining 36 men mutinied, near the site of present <a href="/wiki/Navasota,_Texas" title="Navasota, Texas">Navasota, Texas</a>. On March 19, 1687, La Salle was slain by Pierre Duhaut during an ambush while talking to Duhaut's decoy, <a href="/wiki/Jean_L%27Archev%C3%AAque" title="Jean L'Archevêque">Jean L'Archevêque</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> They were "six leagues" from the westernmost village of the <a href="/wiki/Hasinai" title="Hasinai">Hasinai</a> (Tejas) Indians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HTsalle_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-HTsalle-13">[12]</a></sup> Duhaut was killed to avenge La Salle, while <a href="/wiki/Jean_L%27Archev%C3%AAque" title="Jean L'Archevêque">Jean L'Archevêque</a> was killed in 1720 by Indians during the <a href="/wiki/Villasur_expedition" title="Villasur expedition">Villasur expedition</a>—coincidentally in an ambush beside a river.</p>
<p>The Fort Saint Louis colony lasted only until 1688, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karankawa" title="Karankawa">Karankawa</a>-speaking Native Americans killed the 20 remaining adults and took five children as captives. Tonti sent out search missions in 1689 when he learned of the settlers' fate, but failed to find survivors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is some disagreement about accepting Navasota as the site of La Salle's death. The <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historian</a> Robert Weddle, for example, believes that La Salle's travel distances have been miscalculated. Weddle thinks that La Salle was murdered just east of the <a href="/wiki/Trinity_River_(Texas)" title="Trinity River (Texas)">Trinity River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>La Salle's major legacy was establishing the network of forts from Fort Frontenac to outposts along the Great Lakes, Ohio, Illinois and Mississippi rivers that came to define French territorial, diplomatic and commercial policy for almost a century between his first expedition and the 1763 cession of New France to Great Britain. In addition to the forts, which also served as authorized agencies for the extensive fur trade, LaSalle's visits to Illinois and other Indians cemented the French policy of alliance with Indians in the common causes of containing both Iroquois influence and Anglo-American settlement. He also gave the name Louisiana to the interior North American territory he claimed for France, which lives on in the name of a US state. His efforts to encompass modern-day Ontario and the eight American states that border the Great Lakes became a foundational effort in defining the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes_region" title="Great Lakes region">Great Lakes region</a>.</p>
<p>In 1995, La Salle's primary ship <i><a href="/wiki/La_Belle_(ship)" title="La Belle (ship)">La Belle</a></i> was discovered in the muck of <a href="/wiki/Matagorda_Bay" title="Matagorda Bay">Matagorda Bay</a>. It has been the subject of archeological research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> Through an international treaty, the artifacts excavated from La Belle are owned by France and held in trust by the Texas Historical Commission. The collection is held by the <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi,_Texas" title="Corpus Christi, Texas">Corpus Christi</a> Museum of Science and History. Artifacts from <i>La Belle</i> are shown at nine museums across Texas. The wreckage of La Salle's ship <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=L%27Aimable&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="L'Aimable (page does not exist)">L'Aimable</a></i> has yet to be located. A possible shipwreck of <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Griffon" title="Le Griffon">Le Griffon</a></i> in Lake Michigan is the subject of a lawsuit concerning ownership of artifacts. A more promising wreck has now been identified in the depths of northern Lake Michigan, divers Monroe and Dykster happened upon an ancient wreckage in 2011 while looking for Confederate gold. The bowsprit of their find includes what appears to be a carved wooden Griffin, similar to other examples of the French 17th Century. News of the find was not released to the public until December 2014, when it was published in the editor's note of issue #34 of "Wreck Diver" Magazine. Authentication of the find is expected to be painstaking, but forthcoming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
