<p><b>Lee Ann Womack</b> (born August 19, 1966) is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country music</a> singer and songwriter. Her 2000 single, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_Hope_You_Dance_(song)" title="I Hope You Dance (song)">I Hope You Dance</a>" was a major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crossover_(music)" title="Crossover (music)">crossover</a> music hit, reaching No. 1 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Country_Songs" title="Hot Country Songs">Billboard Country Chart</a> and the Top 15 of the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>, becoming her signature song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-askmen_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-askmen-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Womack emerged as a contemporary country artist in 1997, her material resembled that of <a href="/wiki/Dolly_Parton" title="Dolly Parton">Dolly Parton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tammy_Wynette" title="Tammy Wynette">Tammy Wynette</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oldies_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-oldies-2">[2]</a></sup> except for the way Womack's music mixed an old fashioned style with contemporary elements. Her 2000 album <i><a href="/wiki/I_Hope_You_Dance" title="I Hope You Dance">I Hope You Dance</a></i> had an entirely different sound, using <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop music</a> elements instead of traditional country. It wasn't until the release of <i><a href="/wiki/There%27s_More_Where_That_Came_From" title="There's More Where That Came From">There's More Where That Came From</a></i> in 2005 that Womack returned to recording traditional country music.</p>
<p>As of 2016, Womack has released a total of six studio albums and two compilations. Four of her studio albums have received a Gold certification or higher by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIAA" title="RIAA">RIAA</a>. Additionally, she has received five <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Country_Music_Awards" title="Academy of Country Music Awards">Academy of Country Music Awards</a>, six <a href="/wiki/Country_Music_Association_Awards" title="Country Music Association Awards">Country Music Association Awards</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a>. She has sold over 6 million albums worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lee_Ann_Womack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Womack was born and raised in <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Texas" title="Jacksonville, Texas">Jacksonville, Texas</a>. At an early age, she was interested in country music. Her father, a <a href="/wiki/Disc_jockey" title="Disc jockey">disc jockey</a>, often took his daughter to work with him to help choose records to play on the air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-4">[4]</a></sup> Womack was the second of two daughters. Her mother was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schoolteacher" title="Schoolteacher">schoolteacher</a> and her father was also a high school <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principal_(school)" title="Principal (school)">principal</a>. As a child, Womack studied the <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a> and later graduated from Jacksonville High School in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-musicianguide_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-musicianguide-5">[5]</a></sup> After graduating, Womack attended <a href="/wiki/South_Plains_College" title="South Plains College">South Plains Junior College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Levelland,_Texas" title="Levelland, Texas">Levelland, Texas</a>. The college was one of the first in the nation to offer country music degrees, and soon she became a member of the college band, <i>Country Caravan.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-4">[4]</a></sup> A year later, she left the college and after an agreement with her parents, Womack enrolled at <a href="/wiki/Belmont_University" title="Belmont University">Belmont University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville, Tennessee</a>, where she studied the commercial ways of the music business. In Nashville, she interned at the A&amp;R department of <a href="/wiki/MCA_Records" title="MCA Records">MCA Records</a>. She studied at the college until 1990, leaving the school a year before graduation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-musicianguide_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-musicianguide-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Womack spent a few years raising her children before reentering the music business in the mid 90s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-askmen_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-askmen-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1995 she began performing her music in songwriting demos and at showcase concerts. At one of these showcase concerts, she was spotted by Tree Publishings, who signed her after listening to one of her original demo recordings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-4">[4]</a></sup> Womack wrote songs with some popular Nashville songwriters, including <a href="/wiki/Bill_Anderson_(singer)" title="Bill Anderson (singer)">Bill Anderson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Skaggs" title="Ricky Skaggs">Ricky Skaggs</a>, who recorded her composition, "I Don't Remember Forgetting" for one of his albums. After divorcing her first husband around that time, Womack decided to pursue a career as a country music artist. She auditioned for MCA chairman, Bruce Hinton, who praised her talents. Shortly afterward, she accepted a contract from MCA's sister record company, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decca_Nashville" title="Decca Nashville">Decca Nashville</a> in 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-musicianguide_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-musicianguide-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_career">Music career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lee_Ann_Womack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Country_music_stardom:_1997.E2.80.931999">Country music stardom: 1997–1999</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lee_Ann_Womack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Country music stardom: 1997–1999">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Womack released her <a href="/wiki/Lee_Ann_Womack_(album)" title="Lee Ann Womack (album)">self-titled debut album</a> in May 1997, produced by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Wright_(record_producer)" title="Mark Wright (record producer)">Mark Wright</a>. The album consisted of self-penned material as well as songs written by other artists, including <a href="/wiki/Mark_Chesnutt" title="Mark Chesnutt">Mark Chesnutt</a>, Ricky Skaggs, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sharon_White_(singer)" title="Sharon White (singer)">Sharon White</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-4">[4]</a></sup> With the original release of the album, there was worry that the album wouldn't sell because some people wouldn't be able to differentiate between her and <a href="/wiki/LeAnn_Rimes" title="LeAnn Rimes">LeAnn Rimes</a>, who was also a successful artist at that time. However, Womack decided to keep her given name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-askmen_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-askmen-1">[1]</a></sup> The first single, "Never Again, Again" made the country charts and playlists by March 1997, which led to the release of the album's second single, "<a href="/wiki/The_Fool_(Lee_Ann_Womack_song)" title="The Fool (Lee Ann Womack song)">The Fool</a>" shortly afterward. More successful than her first single, "The Fool" reached the Top 5 on the Billboard Country chart that year. That year she won major awards from the country music community; Top New Female Vocalist from the Academy of Country Music Awards, Top Artist of the Year from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_Magazine" title="Billboard Magazine">Billboard Magazine</a></i>, and was nominated for the Horizon award by the Country Music Association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-musicianguide_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-musicianguide-5">[5]</a></sup> Decca Nashville decided to close its doors in 1998, moving Womack to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MCA_Nashville_Records" title="MCA Nashville Records">MCA Nashville Records</a> that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cmt_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-cmt-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1998, Womack released her second studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Some_Things_I_Know" title="Some Things I Know">Some Things I Know</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-musicianguide_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-musicianguide-5">[5]</a></sup> which was also produced by Mark Wright.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oldies_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-oldies-2">[2]</a></sup> The album's first two singles, "<a href="/wiki/A_Little_Past_Little_Rock" title="A Little Past Little Rock">A Little Past Little Rock</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/I%27ll_Think_of_a_Reason_Later" title="I'll Think of a Reason Later">I'll Think of a Reason Later</a>" both went to No. 2 on the Billboard Country Chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cmt_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-cmt-6">[6]</a></sup> Two additional singles, "(Now You See Me) Now You Don't" and "Don't Tell Me" were released in 1999, and the album was certified Gold by the RIAA soon after.</p>
<p>That year, she also won Favorite Country New Artist from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Music_Awards" title="American Music Awards">American Music Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-askmen_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-askmen-1">[1]</a></sup> Womack also contributed her vocals to the songs "If You're Ever Down in Dallas" and "The Man Who Made Mama Cry" in collaboration with her ex-husband and musician, <a href="/wiki/Jason_Sellers" title="Jason Sellers">Jason Sellers</a>. The material was promoted through shows through October to November before the birth of Womack's second child in January 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oldies_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-oldies-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pop_crossover_success:_2000.E2.80.932004">Pop crossover success: 2000–2004</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lee_Ann_Womack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pop crossover success: 2000–2004">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>She released her third studio album in 2000 entitled, <i><a href="/wiki/I_Hope_You_Dance" title="I Hope You Dance">I Hope You Dance</a></i>, which was met with major success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-musicianguide_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-musicianguide-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_Hope_You_Dance_(song)" title="I Hope You Dance (song)">title track</a> (which was released as the lead single), reached No. 1 on the Billboard Country chart for five weeks and crossed over to the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>, becoming a major crossover Pop hit, reaching No. 14. It also peaked at the top spot on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adult_contemporary" title="Adult contemporary">adult contemporary</a> chart and even reached the UK Singles Charts, peaking at No. 40. Both of Womack's daughters appeared in the song's video that year. Towards the end of 2000, "I Hope You Dance" won the Country Music Association's "Song of the Year" and "Single of the Year" awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cmt_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-cmt-6">[6]</a></sup> With the Pop success of "I Hope You Dance," Womack drew the attention of the magazines <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People_Magazine" title="People Magazine">People</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_Magazine" title="Time Magazine">Time</a></i>, both of which praised the single, calling it "one of her best." The song later won awards in 2001 from the Grammy and Academy of Country music awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-musicianguide_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-musicianguide-5">[5]</a></sup> The album of the same name has sold 3 million copies in the United States to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cmt_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-cmt-6">[6]</a></sup> The album's follow-up single, a cover of <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Crowell" title="Rodney Crowell">Rodney Crowell</a>'s "Ashes by Now" peaked at No. 4 on the Billboard Country Chart. The third single, "Why They Call it Falling" was also successful, reaching the country Top 15.</p>
<p>On December 11, 2000, Womack performed "I Hope You Dance" at the annual <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> concert. This song was also featured in the 2008 Tyler Perry film entitled "The Family that Preys."</p>
<p>In 2002, Womack's fourth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Something_Worth_Leaving_Behind" title="Something Worth Leaving Behind">Something Worth Leaving Behind</a></i> was released. The album made a stronger attempt at a pop-flavored style, however it did not react well, leading to poor record sales and only one major hit. Following its release, Womack's career stalled. Later that year, she released a <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Season_for_Romance" title="The Season for Romance">The Season for Romance</a></i> and also collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Willie_Nelson" title="Willie Nelson">Willie Nelson</a> on his single, "Mendocino County Line," which won a Grammy and Country Music award in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cmt_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-cmt-6">[6]</a></sup> In early 2003, she got a small recurring role on the popular <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> drama, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_District_(TV_series)" title="The District (TV series)">The District</a>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, Womack performed "I Hope You Dance" at the <a href="/wiki/Republican_National_Convention" title="Republican National Convention">Republican National Convention</a>, in which <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> was nominated for his second term as President of the United States. The other performers that night included <a href="/wiki/Sara_Evans" title="Sara Evans">Sara Evans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Larry_Gatlin" title="Larry Gatlin">Larry Gatlin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> She also collaborated with Red Dirt Music band, <a href="/wiki/Cross_Canadian_Ragweed" title="Cross Canadian Ragweed">Cross Canadian Ragweed</a> on their hit, "Sick and Tired" in 2004. Also that year, she also released her first <i><a href="/wiki/Greatest_Hits_(Lee_Ann_Womack_album)" title="Greatest Hits (Lee Ann Womack album)">Greatest Hits</a></i> album, which included two new songs; "The Wrong Girl" (the only song from the album released as a single) and "Time for Me to Go."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="There.27s_More_Where_That_Came_From_.26_hiatus:_2005.E2.80.932007"><i>There's More Where That Came From</i> &amp; hiatus: 2005–2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lee_Ann_Womack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: There's More Where That Came From &amp; hiatus: 2005–2007">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 2005, she released her fifth studio album aimed at traditional country music entitled, <i><a href="/wiki/There%27s_More_Where_That_Came_From" title="There's More Where That Came From">There's More Where That Came From</a>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cmt_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-cmt-6">[6]</a></sup> Many people in the music industry called the album, "a return to tradition," featuring songs about drinking and cheating with a distinctive older country twang, mixing strings and <a href="/wiki/Steel_guitar" title="Steel guitar">steel guitar</a>. The album won the Country Music Association's "Album of the Year" award in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-book-8">[8]</a></sup> Womack took inspiration from the records of the 60s and 70s, and according to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a>, the album sounded like albums by <a href="/wiki/Loretta_Lynn" title="Loretta Lynn">Loretta Lynn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Mandrell" title="Barbara Mandrell">Barbara Mandrell</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dolly_Parton" title="Dolly Parton">Dolly Parton</a> from the 70s. They also called it one of her best records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The lead single, "<a href="/wiki/I_May_Hate_Myself_in_the_Morning" title="I May Hate Myself in the Morning">I May Hate Myself in the Morning</a>" was a Top 10 hit in 2005, and also won "Single of the Year" by the CMA awards later that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cmt_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-cmt-6">[6]</a></sup> Two additional singles were released from the album in 2005 that became minor hits, "<a href="/wiki/He_Oughta_Know_That_by_Now" title="He Oughta Know That by Now">He Oughta Know That by Now</a>" and "Twenty Years and Two Husbands Ago." The album was released on vinyl LP as well as CD.</p>
<p>Womack can be heard on the track "If I Could Only Fly" from <a href="/wiki/Joe_Nichols" title="Joe Nichols">Joe Nichols</a>' album <i><a href="/wiki/Real_Things_(Joe_Nichols_album)" title="Real Things (Joe Nichols album)">Real Things</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Womack has also appeared on specials on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Country_Music_Television" title="Country Music Television">CMT</a> network, including their, "100 Greatest Duets", which featured Womack singing the duet with <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Rogers" title="Kenny Rogers">Kenny Rogers</a>, "<a href="/wiki/Every_Time_Two_Fools_Collide_(song)" title="Every Time Two Fools Collide (song)">Every Time Two Fools Collide</a>". The song had been originally recorded by Rogers and <a href="/wiki/Dottie_West" title="Dottie West">Dottie West</a> in 1978 and was a No. 1 Country hit that year. Womack took the place of West during that show since West had died in a serious car accident in September 1991. Womack's other honors includes being listed at No. 17 on CMT's 2002 special of their countdown of the <i>40 Greatest Women of Country Music</i>.</p>
<p>In 2006, Womack announced plans of a sixth studio album off of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury_Nashville_Records" title="Mercury Nashville Records">Mercury Nashville Records</a>. The lead single, "Finding My Way Back Home" was released in the late summer of that year and debuted at No. 46 on the Billboard Country Chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-s-11">[11]</a></sup> The single later peaked at No. 37 and was re-scheduled into 2007, because Womack found more songs that she wanted to record, however it was never released and Womack left Mercury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_music:_2008.E2.80.93present">Return to music: 2008–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lee_Ann_Womack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Return to music: 2008–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2008, Womack announced plans for a new single for the first time in three years, once again on MCA Nashville. "<a href="/wiki/Last_Call_(Lee_Ann_Womack_song)" title="Last Call (Lee Ann Womack song)">Last Call</a>" was released on June 30, 2008. It served as the lead-off single to Womack's seventh studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Call_Me_Crazy" title="Call Me Crazy">Call Me Crazy</a></i>, which was released on October 21, 2008. The album, released on vinyl and CD, was produced by <a href="/wiki/Tony_Brown_(record_producer)" title="Tony Brown (record producer)">Tony Brown</a>, has been described as a dark album with plenty of songs about drinking and losing love. It featured a duet with <a href="/wiki/George_Strait" title="George Strait">George Strait</a> titled "Everything But Quits," a re-recording of the Strait song "The King of Broken Hearts," which first appeared on the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pure_Country_(album)" title="Pure Country (album)">Pure Country</a></i> soundtrack. One track, "The Bees," features vocals from <a href="/wiki/Keith_Urban" title="Keith Urban">Keith Urban</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cmtnews1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-cmtnews1-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2009, Womack released "<a href="/wiki/There_Is_a_God" title="There Is a God">There Is a God</a>", as the lead-off single to her upcoming seventh studio album which never surfaced. The song debuted at No. 60 on the Billboard Hot Country Songs chart for the week of November 14, 2009, eventually peaking at No. 32 in early 2010. Womack has revealed a few of the tracks that she has recorded for the album, including: "Talking Behind Your Back", as well as "You Do Until You Don't".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2010, Womack contributed the new track "Liars Lie" to the <a href="/wiki/Country_Strong_(soundtrack)" title="Country Strong (soundtrack)">soundtrack</a> for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Country_Strong" title="Country Strong">Country Strong</a></i>. Womack also contributed guest vocals to Alan Jackson's cover of the song "<a href="/wiki/Ring_of_Fire_(song)" title="Ring of Fire (song)">Ring of Fire</a>", which was released in December 2010 as a single from his compilation album, <i><a href="/wiki/34_Number_Ones" title="34 Number Ones">34 Number Ones</a></i>. His version of the song was a minor hit, charting to number 45 on the Hot Country Songs charts. Though Womack is featured on the song, she was not given credit on the charts.</p>
<p>In August 2012, Womack parted ways with MCA Nashville.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In April 2014, Womack signed with <a href="/wiki/Sugar_Hill_Records" title="Sugar Hill Records">Sugar Hill Records</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sugarhill_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-sugarhill-16">[16]</a></sup> Her first album for the label, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Way_I%27m_Livin%27" title="The Way I'm Livin'">The Way I'm Livin'</a></i>, was released September 23, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sugarhill_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-sugarhill-16">[16]</a></sup> Critics cheered the progressive traditionalist’s return. In addition to a four out of four star review in USA Today, Rolling Stone proclaimed Livin’ “feels like something Merle Haggard or Waylon Jennings would have crafted back in the Seventies,” Spin deemed it “the best of her career” and Garden &amp; Gun offered, “Nashville is filled with artists making ‘the record they were born to make.’ With Livin’, Womack is one of the few who actually deliver.”</p>
<p>"Livin'" was the only country album to make Esquire magazine's Top Albums of 2014 list. It was nominated for two 2015 Grammy Awards for Best Country Album and for Best Engineered Album, Non-Classical. Womack also received two Americana Music Awards nominations for Album of the Year and Artist of the Year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 26, 2014, Womack collaborated with American <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a> singer <a href="/wiki/John_Legend" title="John Legend">John Legend</a> for an episode of <a href="/wiki/CMT_Crossroads" title="CMT Crossroads">CMT Crossroads</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crossroads_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-crossroads-18">[18]</a></sup> Ahead of her 2015 tour in support of <i>The Way I'm Livin'</i>, Womack appeared at the <a href="/wiki/C2C:_Country_to_Country" title="C2C: Country to Country">C2C: Country to Country</a> festival in the UK.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lee_Ann_Womack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Belmont_University" title="Belmont University">Belmont University</a>, Womack met and married singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Jason_Sellers" title="Jason Sellers">Jason Sellers</a> in 1990; they divorced in 1996. Together they had a daughter named <a href="/wiki/Aubrie_Sellers" title="Aubrie Sellers">Aubrie Lee Sellers</a> (b. 1991).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Womack gave birth to her second daughter, Annalise Liddell, in January 1999 after marrying record producer <a href="/wiki/Frank_Liddell" title="Frank Liddell">Frank Liddell</a>.</p>
