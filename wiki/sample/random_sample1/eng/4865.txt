<p>The <b>Peace of Riga</b>, also known as the <b>Treaty of Riga</b>; <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>: <span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl"><i>Traktat Ryski</i></span> was signed in <a href="/wiki/Riga" title="Riga">Riga</a> on 18 March 1921, between <a href="/wiki/Second_Polish_Republic" title="Second Polish Republic">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_Soviet_Federative_Socialist_Republic" title="Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic">Soviet Russia</a> (acting also on behalf of <a href="/wiki/Byelorussian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Byelorussian Soviet Socialist Republic">Soviet Belarus</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic">Soviet Ukraine</a>. The treaty ended the <a href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Soviet_War" title="Polish–Soviet War">Polish–Soviet War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Soviet-Polish borders established by the treaty remained in force until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>. They were later redrawn during the <a href="/wiki/Yalta_Conference" title="Yalta Conference">Yalta Conference</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Conference" title="Potsdam Conference">Potsdam Conference</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peace_of_Riga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish-Soviet_War" title="Polish-Soviet War">Polish-Soviet War</a>.</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> removed former imperial borders across Europe. In 1918, after the Russian Revolution had renounced Tsarist claims to Poland in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Brest-Litovsk" title="Treaty of Brest-Litovsk">Treaty of Brest-Litovsk</a> and the war had ended with Germany's surrender, Poland was able to re-establish its independence after <a href="/wiki/Partitions_of_Poland" title="Partitions of Poland">a century of foreign rule</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Russian Civil War presented an opportunity for Poland under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B3zef_Pi%C5%82sudski" title="Józef Piłsudski">Józef Piłsudski</a> to regain parts of the tsarist territories of the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish-Lithuanian_Commonwealth" title="Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth">Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth</a> which had been incorporated into the Russian Empire during the <a href="/wiki/Partitions_of_Poland" title="Partitions of Poland">Partitions of Poland</a>. Meanwhile, many in the Soviet leadership desired to respond to Piłsudski's moves into the Ukraine by using military force against Poland, which was seen by the Soviets as a land bridge to Western Europe, and thus to extend the revolution westwards. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish-Soviet_War" title="Polish-Soviet War">Polish-Soviet War</a> ensued, culminating in the Polish victory in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Warsaw_(1920)" title="Battle of Warsaw (1920)">Battle of Warsaw (1920)</a>, after which both sides became receptive to ending the conflict. Further military setbacks following their defeat near Warsaw made the Soviets eager to begin peace treaty negotiations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cienciala_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cienciala-2">[2]</a></sup> and the Poles, pressured by the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a>, were also willing to negotiate after the Polish army had regained control of most of the disputed territories in the war, but was nearing exhaustion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Negotiations">Negotiations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peace_of_Riga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Negotiations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Peace talks began in <a href="/wiki/Minsk" title="Minsk">Minsk</a> on 17 August 1920, but as the Polish <a href="/wiki/Counter-offensive" title="Counter-offensive">counter-offensive</a> drew near, the talks were moved to Riga, and resumed on 21 September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wandycz1962_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wandycz1962-3">[3]</a></sup> The Soviets proposed two solutions, the first on 21 September and the second on the 28th. The Polish delegation made a counter-offer on 2 October. Three days later the Soviets offered amendments to the Polish offer, which Poland accepted. An <a href="/wiki/Armistice" title="Armistice">armistice</a> was signed on 12 October and went into effect on 18 October 1920.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The chief negotiators were <a href="/wiki/Jan_D%C4%85bski" title="Jan Dąbski">Jan Dąbski</a> for Poland<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savchenko2009_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Savchenko2009-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Adolph_Joffe" title="Adolph Joffe">Adolph Joffe</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Soviet_Federative_Socialist_Republic" title="Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic">Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wandycz1962_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wandycz1962-3">[3]</a></sup> The Soviet side insisted, successfully, on excluding non-communist Ukrainian representatives from the negotiations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wandycz1962_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wandycz1962-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to their military setbacks, the Soviet delegation offered Poland substantial territorial concessions in the contested border areas. However, to many observers, it looked like the Polish side was conducting the Riga talks as if Poland had lost the war. The Polish delegation was dominated by members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endecja" title="Endecja">National Democrat</a> movement, who were Piłsudski's political opponents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savchenko2009_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Savchenko2009-5">[5]</a></sup> The National Democrats did not want non-Polish minorities in the reborn Polish state to constitute more than one third of the overall population, and were therefore prepared to accept a Polish-Soviet border which was substantially to the west of what was being offered by the Soviet side, even though this would leave hundreds of thousands of people who were ethnically Polish on the Soviet side of the border.</p>
<p>This decision was also motivated by political objectives. The National Democrats' base of public support was among Poles in central and western Poland. In the east of the country and in the disputed borderlands, support for the National Democrats was greatly outweighed by support for Piłsudski (and in the countryside outside of the cities, Poles were outnumbered by Ukrainians or Belarusians in these areas). So a border too far to the east was not just against the National Democrats' ideological objective of minimising the minority population of Poland, but would also be an electoral disadvantage to them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Snyderriga_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Snyderriga-6">[6]</a></sup> War-weary public opinion in Poland also favoured an end to the negotiations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wandycz1962_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wandycz1962-3">[3]</a></sup> and both sides remained under pressure from the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a> to reach a deal.</p>
<p>A special parliamentary delegation consisting of six members of the Polish <a href="/wiki/Sejm" title="Sejm">Sejm</a> held a vote on whether to accept the Soviets' far-reaching concessions, which would leave Minsk on the Polish side of the border. Pressured by the National Democrat ideologue, <a href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_Grabski" title="Stanisław Grabski">Stanisław Grabski</a>, the 100 km of extra territory was rejected, a victory for the nationalist doctrine and a stark defeat for Piłsudski's <a href="/wiki/Federalism" title="Federalism">federalism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wandycz1962_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Wandycz1962-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savchenko2009_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Savchenko2009-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Regardless, the peace negotiations dragged on for months due to Soviet reluctance to sign. However, the matter became more urgent for the Soviet leadership as it had to deal with increased internal unrest towards the end of 1920, which led to the <a href="/wiki/Tambov_Rebellion" title="Tambov Rebellion">Tambov Rebellion</a> and later the <a href="/wiki/Kronstadt_rebellion" title="Kronstadt rebellion">Kronstadt rebellion</a> against the Soviet authorities. As a result of this situation, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Lenin</a> ordered the Soviet plenipotentiaries to finalise the peace treaty with Poland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cienciala_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cienciala-2">[2]</a></sup> The Peace of Riga was signed on 18 March 1921, partitioning the disputed territories in <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> between Poland and the RSFSR, and ending the conflict.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terms">Terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peace_of_Riga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Treaty consisted of 26 articles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palij_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-palij-7">[7]</a></sup> Poland was to receive monetary compensation (30 million <a href="/wiki/Russian_ruble" title="Russian ruble">rubles</a> in gold) for its economic input into the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Partitions_of_Poland" title="Partitions of Poland">Partitions of Poland</a>. Under Article 14 Poland was also to receive railway materials (locomotives, rolling stock, etc.) with a value of 29 million gold roubles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Russia was to surrender works of art and other Polish national treasures acquired from Polish territories after 1772 (such as the <a href="/wiki/Jagiellonian_tapestries" title="Jagiellonian tapestries">Jagiellonian tapestries</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Za%C5%82uski_Library" title="Załuski Library">Załuski Library</a>). Both sides renounced claims to war compensation.</p>
<p>Article 3 stipulated that border issues between Poland and <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> would be settled by those states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palij_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-palij-7">[7]</a></sup> Article 6 created citizenship options for persons on either side of the new border.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palij_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-palij-7">[7]</a></sup> Article 7 consisted of a mutual guarantee that all nationalities would be permitted "free intellectual development, the use of their national language, and the exercise of their religion."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palij_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-palij-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treaty_aftermath">Treaty aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peace_of_Riga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Treaty aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Allied Powers were initially reluctant to recognise the treaty, which had been concluded without their participation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palij_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-palij-7">[7]</a></sup> Their postwar conferences had supported the <a href="/wiki/Curzon_Line" title="Curzon Line">Curzon Line</a> as the Polish-Russian border, and Poland's territorial gains in the treaty lay about 250 km east of that line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hup_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-hup-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> French support led to its recognition in March 1923 by France, Great Britain, Italy and Japan, followed by the US in April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palij_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-palij-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Poland, the Peace of Riga was met with criticism from the very beginning. Some characterised the treaty as short-sighted and argued that much of what Poland had gained during the Polish-Soviet war was lost during the peace negotiations. <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B3zef_Pi%C5%82sudski" title="Józef Piłsudski">Józef Piłsudski</a> had participated in the Riga negotiations only as an observer, and called the resulting treaty "an act of cowardice".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-11">[11]</a></sup> Piłsudski felt the agreement was a shameless and short-sighted political calculation, with Poland abandoning its Ukrainian allies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wandycz1962_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Wandycz1962-3">[3]</a></sup> On 15 May 1921 he apologised to Ukrainian soldiers during his visit to the internment camp at <a href="/wiki/Kalisz" title="Kalisz">Kalisz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruski_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bruski-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borz.C4.99cki2008_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Borz.C4.99cki2008-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The treaty substantially contributed to the failure of <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B3zef_Pi%C5%82sudski" title="Józef Piłsudski">Piłsudski</a>'s plan to create a Polish-led <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mi%C4%99dzymorze" title="Międzymorze">Intermarium</a> federation of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern European</a> countries, as portions of the territory proposed for the federation were ceded to the Soviets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Snyderriga_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Snyderriga-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lenin also considered the treaty unsatisfactory, as it forced him to put aside his plans for exporting the Soviet revolution to the West.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cienciala_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cienciala-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Belarusian and Ukrainian independence movements saw the treaty as a setback.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zap_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-zap-15">[15]</a></sup> Four million Ukrainians and over one million Belarusians lived within areas ceded to Poland; in one estimate, only 15% of the population was ethnically Polish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_People%27s_Republic" title="Ukrainian People's Republic">Ukrainian People's Republic</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Symon_Petliura" title="Symon Petliura">Symon Petliura</a> had been allied with Poland under the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Warsaw_(1920)" title="Treaty of Warsaw (1920)">Treaty of Warsaw</a>, which was abrogated by the Peace of Riga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cienciala_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cienciala-2">[2]</a></sup> The new treaty violated Poland's military alliance with the UPR, which had explicitly prohibited a separate peace. In doing so, it worsened relations between Poland and those Ukrainians who had supported Petliura. These supporters felt Ukraine had been betrayed by its Polish ally, a feeling that would be exploited by <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_Ukrainian_Nationalists" title="Organization of Ukrainian Nationalists">Ukrainian nationalists</a> and contribute to the growing tensions and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Massacres_of_Poles_in_Volhynia" title="Massacres of Poles in Volhynia">eventual anti-Polish violence</a> which erupted in the 1930s and 1940s. By the end of 1921, the majority of Poland-allied Ukrainian, Belarusian and <a href="/wiki/White_movement" title="White movement">White Russian</a> forces had either been annihilated by Soviet forces or crossed the border into Poland and laid down their arms.</p>
<p>According to Belarusian historian Andrew Savchenko, Poland's new eastern border was "military indefensible and economically unviable", and a source of growing ethnic tensions, as the resulting minorities in Poland were too large to be ignored or assimilated, and too small to win desired autonomy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savchenko2009_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Savchenko2009-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_consequences">Further consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peace_of_Riga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Further consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>While the Peace of Riga led to a two-decade stabilisation of Soviet-Polish relations, conflict was renewed with the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_invasion_of_Poland" title="Soviet invasion of Poland">Soviet invasion of Poland</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. The treaty was subsequently overridden after a decision by war's Allied powers to change Poland's borders once again and <a href="/wiki/Polish_population_transfers_(1944%E2%80%9346)" title="Polish population transfers (1944–46)">transfer the populations</a>.</p>
<p>In the view of some foreign observers, the treaty's incorporation of significant minority populations into Poland led to seemingly insurmountable challenges, because the newly formed organizations such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OUN" title="OUN">OUN</a> engaged in terror and sabotage actions across ethnically mixed areas to inflame conflict in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palij_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-palij-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hup_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-hup-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RJC_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-RJC-18">[18]</a></sup> Nevertheless, many groups representing national minorities welcomed Piłsudski's return to power in 1926 providing opportunities to play a role in the Polish government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PDS_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-PDS-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>The populations separated from Poland by the new Polish-Soviet border experienced different fate from their fellow citizens. Ethnic Poles left within Soviet borders were subjected to discrimination and property confiscation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-W.C5.82adze_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-W.C5.82adze-20">[20]</a></sup> Most of them (at least 111,000) were summarily killed in <a href="/wiki/Polish_Operation_of_the_NKVD_(1937%E2%80%9338)" title="Polish Operation of the NKVD (1937–38)">the NKVD operation</a> in 1937-38, genocide preceding ones perpetrated during WW2, or exiled to different regions of the Soviet Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Belarusians and Ukrainians, having failed to create their own states, were subjects of extermination in the Soviet Union, e.g. during <a href="/wiki/Holodomor" title="Holodomor">Holodomor</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Purge" title="Great Purge">Great Purge</a> (of which Poles were also victims).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Belarusians and Ukrainians living on the Polish side of the border were subjected to <a href="/wiki/Polonization" title="Polonization">Polonization</a>; which contributed to the rise of <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_Ukrainian_Nationalists" title="Organization of Ukrainian Nationalists">Ukrainian nationalist organisations</a> and the adoption of <a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terrorist</a> tactics by Ukrainian extremists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prybyla2010_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prybyla2010-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roshwald2001_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roshwald2001-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Soviet Union, thwarted in 1921, would see its sphere of influence expand as a result of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. After establishing its control over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_Poland" title="People's Republic of Poland">People's Republic of Poland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Territorial_changes_of_Poland_immediately_after_World_War_II" title="Territorial changes of Poland immediately after World War II">Polish-Soviet border</a> was moved westwards in 1945 to roughly coincide with the <a href="/wiki/Curzon_Line" title="Curzon Line">Curzon Line</a>. This shift was accompanied by large <a href="/wiki/Polish_population_transfers_(1944%E2%80%9346)" title="Polish population transfers (1944–46)">population transfers</a> which led to the expulsion of the Poles living east of the new border, and also <a href="/wiki/Operation_Vistula" title="Operation Vistula">moved most of the Ukrainian minority</a> remaining in Poland to the <a href="/wiki/Former_eastern_territories_of_Germany" title="Former eastern territories of Germany">former German territories</a> that were ceded to Poland in compensation. The unified Belarusian and Ukrainian territories were fully incorporated into the USSR.</p>
<p>However, in 1989, Poland would regain its full sovereignty, and soon afterwards, with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Fall of the Soviet Union">fall of the Soviet Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> would go on to become independent nations.</p>
