<p>The <b>main square</b> (<a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>: <span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl"><i>Rynek Główny</i></span> <small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Polish" title="Help:IPA for Polish">[ˈrɨnɛk ˈɡwuvnɨ]</a></span>) of the <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w_Old_Town" title="Kraków Old Town">Old Town</a> of <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Poland" title="Lesser Poland">Lesser Poland</a>, is the principal urban space located at the center of the city. It dates back to the 13th century, and at roughly 4,000 m<sup>2</sup> (43,000 ft<sup>2</sup>) is one of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_city_squares_by_size" title="List of city squares by size">largest</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> town squares in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steves_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steves-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walters2004_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walters2004-2">[2]</a></sup> The Project for Public Spaces (PPS) lists the square as the best public space in Europe due to its lively street life,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PPS_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-PPS-3">[3]</a></sup> and it was a major factor in the inclusion of Kraków as one of the top off-the-beaten-path destinations in the world in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main square is a square space surrounded by historic townhouses (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kamienica_(architecture)" title="Kamienica (architecture)"><i>kamienice</i></a>) and <a href="/wiki/Churches_of_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Churches of Kraków">churches</a>. The center of the square is dominated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cloth_Hall,_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Cloth Hall, Kraków">Cloth Hall</a> (<i>Sukiennice</i>), rebuilt in 1555 in the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> style, topped by a beautiful attic or <i>Polish parapet</i> decorated with carved masks. On one side of the cloth hall is the <a href="/wiki/Town_Hall_Tower,_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Town Hall Tower, Kraków">Town Hall Tower</a> (<i>Wieża ratuszowa</i>), on the other the 10th century <a href="/wiki/Church_of_St._Adalbert,_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Church of St. Adalbert, Kraków">Church of St. Adalbert</a> and 1898 <a href="/wiki/Adam_Mickiewicz_Monument,_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Adam Mickiewicz Monument, Kraków">Adam Mickiewicz Monument</a>. Rising above the square are the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> towers of <a href="/wiki/St._Mary%27s_Basilica,_Krak%C3%B3w" title="St. Mary's Basilica, Kraków">St. Mary's Basilica</a> (<i>Kościół Mariacki</i>). Kraków Main Square does not have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Town_hall" title="Town hall">town hall</a>, because it has not survived to the present day.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Main_Square,_Krak%C3%B3w&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The main function of the Market Square was commerce. After the city was destroyed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongol_invasion" title="Mongol invasion">Mongol invasion</a> in 1241, the Main Square was rebuilt in 1257 and its commercial role expanded with the <a href="/wiki/Magdeburg_rights" title="Magdeburg rights">Magdeburg rights</a> location of the city by the prince of Kraków, <a href="/wiki/Boles%C5%82aw_V_the_Chaste" title="Bolesław V the Chaste">Bolesław V the Chaste</a>. The Main Square was designed in its current state with each side repeating a pattern of three, evenly spaced streets set at right angles to the square. The exception is Grodzka Street which is much older and connects the Main Square with the <a href="/wiki/Wawel_Castle" title="Wawel Castle">Wawel Castle</a>. Originally the square was filled with low market stalls and administrative buildings and had a <i>ring</i> road running around it. It was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Poland" title="King of Poland">King</a> <a href="/wiki/Casimir_III_the_Great" title="Casimir III the Great">Casimir III the Great</a> who built the original <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w_Cloth_Hall" title="Kraków Cloth Hall">Cloth Hall</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w_Town_Hall" title="Kraków Town Hall">town hall</a> that filled nearly a quarter of the square. Kraków was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capital_(political)" title="Capital (political)">capital</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Poland_(1138%E2%80%931320)" title="Kingdom of Poland (1138–1320)">Kingdom of Poland</a> and a member of the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a> and the city flourished as an important European metropolis.</p>

<p>In addition to its original merchant functions the Main Square witnessed many historical events, and it was used to stage public executions of prisoners held in city Town Hall. It was a place of regal ceremonies as part of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Road,_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Royal Road, Kraków">Royal Road</a> (<i>Droga Królewska</i>), frequented by diplomats and dignitaries traveling to the <a href="/wiki/Wawel_Castle" title="Wawel Castle">Wawel Castle</a>. In 1364 King Casimir held the Pan-European <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Congress of Kraków">Congress of Kraków</a> there. On 10 April 1525, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_I,_Duke_of_Prussia" title="Albert I, Duke of Prussia">Albert I, Duke of Prussia</a> paid the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Homage" title="Prussian Homage">Prussian Homage</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_I_the_Old" title="Sigismund I the Old">Sigismund I the Old</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Poland" title="King of Poland">king of Poland</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Duke_of_Lithuania" title="Grand Duke of Lithuania">Grand Duke of Lithuania</a> accepting Polish kings' suzerainty, (<i>pictured</i>). In 1514 <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuanian</a> <a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">duke</a> <a href="/wiki/Konstanty_Ostrogski" title="Konstanty Ostrogski">Konstanty Ostrogski</a> held a victory parade over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscovy" title="Muscovy">Muscovy</a> and in 1531 nobleman <a href="/wiki/Jan_Tarnowski" title="Jan Tarnowski">Jan Tarnowski</a> celebrated another victory in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscovite_wars" title="Muscovite wars">Muscovite wars</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jan_III_Sobieski" title="Jan III Sobieski">Jan III Sobieski</a>, a King of Poland and Grand Duke of Lithuania, celebrated there his victory over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkish_Empire" title="Turkish Empire">Turkish Empire</a> in the 1683 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Vienna" title="Battle of Vienna">Battle of Vienna</a>.</p>
<p>In 1596 King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sigismund_III" title="Sigismund III">Sigismund III</a>, of the Swedish <a href="/wiki/House_of_Vasa" title="House of Vasa">House of Vasa</a>, moved the capital of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish-Lithuanian_Commonwealth" title="Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth">Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth</a> from Kraków to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warszawa" title="Warszawa">Warszawa</a> (<i>Warsaw</i>). Kraków remained the place of coronations and royal funerals. On 24 March 1794, at the Main Square <a href="/wiki/Tadeusz_Ko%C5%9Bciuszko" title="Tadeusz Kościuszko">Tadeusz Kościuszko</a> <a href="/wiki/Ko%C5%9Bciuszko%27s_proclamation" title="Kościuszko's proclamation">announced the general uprising</a> (<i>pictured</i>) and assumed the powers of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commander_in_Chief" title="Commander in Chief">Commander in Chief</a> of Polish armed forces, beginning the <a href="/wiki/Ko%C5%9Bciuszko_Uprising" title="Kościuszko Uprising">Kościuszko Uprising</a>. In 1848, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_of_Nations" title="Spring of Nations">Spring of Nations</a>, civilians clashed with the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Galicia_and_Lodomeria" title="Kingdom of Galicia and Lodomeria">Austrian</a> army and it was where, next to <i>Ratusz</i>, <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Austria" title="Coat of arms of Austria">Austrian eagles</a> were piled up as a symbol of regained independence in 1918.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guide_to_Krakow_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guide_to_Krakow-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>Jews traded on the square as early as the 15th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-findingyourjewishroots4_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-findingyourjewishroots4-6">[6]</a></sup> During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occupation_of_Poland_(1939%E2%80%931945)" title="Occupation of Poland (1939–1945)">occupation of Poland</a> by <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, the square was renamed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Streets_named_after_Adolf_Hitler" title="Streets named after Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler-Platz</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Adam_Mickiewicz_Monument,_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Adam Mickiewicz Monument, Kraków">Adam Mickiewicz monument</a> was destroyed along with historical commemorative plaques taken from buildings at the square. After the war, the monument was reconstructed.</p>
<p>In 1978 <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> placed the Main Square as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Town_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Old Town Kraków">Old Town Kraków</a> on the list of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Sites" title="World Heritage Sites">World Heritage Sites</a>. On 21 March 1980, in time of political tension and the run-up to the declaration of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martial_Law_in_Poland" title="Martial Law in Poland">Martial Law in Poland</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Walenty_Badylak&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Walenty Badylak (page does not exist)">Walenty Badylak</a>, retired baker and a veteran of Poland's wartime underground <a href="/wiki/Home_Army" title="Home Army">Home Army</a>, set himself alight chained to a well in the Main Square.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polskie_Radio_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Polskie_Radio-7">[7]</a></sup> Badylak was protesting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_Poland" title="People's Republic of Poland">communist</a> government's refusal to acknowledge the <a href="/wiki/Katyn_massacre" title="Katyn massacre">Katyn</a>'s war crime. Also, the Main Square was central in staging mass demonstrations of the <a href="/wiki/Solidarity_(Polish_trade_union)" title="Solidarity (Polish trade union)">Solidarity</a> movement. In 2013 Lonely Planet travel guides rated Kraków's Main Market Square as the most beautiful in the world.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Main_Square,_Krak%C3%B3w&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Main Square is located on the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Road,_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Royal Road, Kraków">Royal Road</a> once traversed during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_coronations_in_Poland" title="Royal coronations in Poland">Royal Coronations</a> at <a href="/wiki/Wawel_Cathedral" title="Wawel Cathedral">Wawel Cathedral</a>, between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w_barbican" title="Kraków barbican">Kraków barbican</a> to the north, and the <a href="/wiki/Wawel_Castle" title="Wawel Castle">Wawel Castle</a> to the south. Ever since its creation the square has been considered the center of the city.</p>
<p>The Main Square is surrounded by old brick buildings (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kamienica_(architecture)" title="Kamienica (architecture)">kamienica</a></i>) and palaces, almost all of them several centuries old. Most buildings have acquired a neoclassical look over time, but the basic structures are older and can be seen in their doorways, architectural details and interiors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guide_to_Krakow_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guide_to_Krakow-5">[5]</a></sup> Vast medieval cellars of the buildings are used as pubs, restaurants and cabarets. The square is lined with many restaurants and cafes. One of the most renowned, <i>Pod Palmą</i> (Under the Palm) at Krzysztofory Palace, was opened in 1876 by <a href="/wiki/Antoni_Hawe%C5%82ka" title="Antoni Hawełka">Antoni Hawełka</a>, a purveyor to the imperial court in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>. It is the location of the <a href="/wiki/Historical_Museum_of_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Historical Museum of Kraków">Historical Museum of Kraków</a>, above.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hawelka_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-hawelka-8">[8]</a></sup> Among the many tourism-oriented venues there is also the International Centre of Culture. Probably the most famous of the oldest establishments is the <a href="/wiki/Wierzynek" title="Wierzynek">Wierzynek</a>'s restaurant, remembered for the great feast of 1364 which, according to the legend, lasted for twenty one days and helped to reach a consensus between the monarchs of Europe.</p>
<p>Among the square's landmarks is the cloth hall, which was originally designed in the 14th century as a center for cloth trade. It was gutted by fire in 1555 and rebuilt in the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> style by Giovani il Mosca from Padua. The arcades were added in the 19th century. The ground floor is continually used for commerce with its many souvenir shops and cafés; upstairs houses the Gallery of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Museum_in_Krak%C3%B3w" title="National Museum in Kraków">National Museum</a>. Another landmark is <a href="/wiki/St._Mary%27s_Basilica,_Krak%C3%B3w" title="St. Mary's Basilica, Kraków">St. Mary's Basilica</a> with its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altar_of_Veit_Stoss" title="Altar of Veit Stoss">Altar</a> by <a href="/wiki/Veit_Stoss" title="Veit Stoss">Wit Stwosz</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Brick_Gothic" title="Brick Gothic">Brick Gothic</a> <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">church</a> built in the 14th century on the ruins of an earlier church destroyed by the Tartar raids of 1241. In the vicinity of the Market Square one can listen to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heynal" title="Heynal">heynal</a>, which is played each hour from the highest tower of St. Mary's Basilica. Other landmarks include the Church of St. Adalbert, <a href="/wiki/Town_Hall_Tower,_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Town Hall Tower, Kraków">Town Hall Tower</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Adam_Mickiewicz_Monument,_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Adam Mickiewicz Monument, Kraków">Adam Mickiewicz Monument</a>.</p>
<p>Originally the market square consisted of more structures that were crucial to the economy and political life of the city and thus created a microcosm of the city. The <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w_Town_Hall" title="Kraków Town Hall">Kraków Town Hall</a> existed since the medieval era and was destroyed in the 19th century. Also standing next to the Cloth Hall was the <a href="/wiki/Great_Weigh_House" title="Great Weigh House">Great Weigh House</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Small_Weigh_House" title="Small Weigh House">Small Weigh House</a>, which both existed until the 19th century. The foundations of the weigh houses were excavated in the early 21st century and incorporated into an underground museum.</p>
<p>Since its creation, the level of the Market Square has raised, in some places by over 5 metres (16 ft). Underneath there are large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basements" title="Basements">basements</a>, the most famous of which is the <a href="/wiki/Piwnica_pod_Baranami" title="Piwnica pod Baranami">Piwnica pod Baranami</a>. Many cellars are now transformed into pubs and <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">restaurants</a>; other include the Theater <i>Maszkaron</i> and a small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archeological" title="Archeological">archeological</a> <a href="/wiki/Museum" title="Museum">museum</a> in the basement of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_St._Wojciech" title="Church of St. Wojciech">St. Adalbert's church</a>. There are passages linking some of the basements, such as the one linking the Town Hall Tower with the Cloth Hall. The Hall itself has a little-known underground trading hall, 100 meters wide (328 ft) and 5 meters high (16.4 ft). Near Sienna Street there is another underground hall (<i>Kramy Bogate</i>), with 1,200 square metres (13,000 sq ft) of trading area.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events">Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Main_Square,_Krak%C3%B3w&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In December 2005 the <a href="/wiki/Project_for_Public_Spaces" title="Project for Public Spaces">Project for Public Spaces</a> selected Kraków's Rynek Główny as <i>the World's Best Square</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PPS_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-PPS-3">[3]</a></sup> It is the focal point of many public events and festivities, such as the annual <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w_szopka" title="Kraków szopka">Kraków szopka</a> Festival, <a href="/wiki/Lajkonik" title="Lajkonik">Lajkonik</a> celebrations, Festival of Military Bands, <a href="/wiki/Juwenalia" title="Juwenalia">Juwenalia Student Festival</a>, Gala Concert of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Orchestra_of_Christmas_Charity" title="Great Orchestra of Christmas Charity">Great Orchestra of Christmas Charity</a> and the largest <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Eve" title="New Year's Eve">New Year's Eve</a> party in Poland. Every year on Christmas Eve, the <a href="/wiki/Adam_Mickiewicz_Monument,_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Adam Mickiewicz Monument, Kraków">Adam Mickiewicz Monument</a> (Mickiewicz' saint's name day) is decorated with flowers by the florists of Kraków.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Krak%C3%B3w" title="Culture of Kraków">culture of Kraków</a> is deeply rooted in colorful traditions with the Main Square remaining most lively and crowded year-round. Like other notable old-town squares, Main Market Square in Kraków is also known for its large population of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_pigeon" title="Rock pigeon">rock pigeons</a>, florist stalls, gift-shops, beer-gardens and horse-drawn carriages.</p>
