<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Reed (mouthpiece)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>reed</b> is a thin strip of material which vibrates to produce a sound on a <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument" title="Musical instrument">musical instrument</a>. The reeds of most <a href="/wiki/Woodwind_instrument" title="Woodwind instrument">woodwind instruments</a> are made from <i><a href="/wiki/Arundo_donax" title="Arundo donax">Arundo donax</a></i> ("Giant cane") or synthetic material; tuned reeds (as in <a href="/wiki/Harmonica" title="Harmonica">harmonicas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Accordion" title="Accordion">accordions</a>) are made of <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> or synthetics.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Single_reeds">Single reeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_(mouthpiece)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Single reeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Single reeds are used on the mouthpieces of clarinets and saxophones. The back of the reed is flat and is placed against the mouthpiece, the rounded top side tapers to a thin tip. These reeds are roughly rectangular in shape except for the thin vibrating tip, which is curved to match the curve of the mouthpiece tip. All single reeds are shaped similarly but vary in size to fit each instrument's mouthpiece. Reeds designed for the same instrument may look identical to each other, but may vary in thickness ("hardness" or "strength"). Hardness is generally measured on a scale of 1 through 5 from softest to hardest. This is not a standardized scale and reed strengths vary by manufacturer. The thickness of the tip and heel and the profile in between affect the sound and playability. Cane of different grades (density, stiffness), even if cut with the same profile, will also respond differently.This is due to the natural differences in the density of the cane fibers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="How_single_reeds_are_made">How single reeds are made</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_(mouthpiece)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: How single reeds are made">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cane" title="Cane">cane</a> used to make reeds for <a href="/wiki/Saxophone" title="Saxophone">saxophone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinet</a>, and other <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Single_reed_instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Single reed instruments (page does not exist)">single reed instruments</a> is grown in the southern coastal regions of France. Once the cane is cut, it must lay out and dry in direct sunlight for about a month. The cane is rotated regularly to ensure proper and complete drying. Once dry, they are taken to be stored in a warehouse. As the cane is needed, it is pulled from the warehouse and taken to the factory. Once at the factory, the cane is taken to the cutting department where it is cut into tubes. The tubes are graded by diameter and wall density.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> The tubes are then cut into "splits" which are transformed into reed blanks. The blanks are tapered and profiled using blades or CNC machines into reeds. After the reeds are completed, they are taken to a machine that grades them for strength.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Double_reeds">Double reeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_(mouthpiece)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Double reeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Double_reed" title="Double reed">Double reed</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bassoon_Reeds.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="474" data-file-width="450" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Bassoon_Reeds.jpg/200px-Bassoon_Reeds.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Bassoon_Reeds.jpg/300px-Bassoon_Reeds.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Bassoon_Reeds.jpg/400px-Bassoon_Reeds.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bassoon_Reeds.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">Bassoon</a> double reeds.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Double_reed" title="Double reed">Double reeds</a> are used on the <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oboe_d%27amore" title="Oboe d'amore">oboe d'amore</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_horn" title="English horn">English horn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bass_oboe" title="Bass oboe">bass oboe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heckelphone" title="Heckelphone">Heckelphone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contrabassoon" title="Contrabassoon">contrabassoon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sarrusophone" title="Sarrusophone">sarrusophone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shawm" title="Shawm">shawm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bagpipes" title="Bagpipes">bagpipes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nadaswaram" title="Nadaswaram">nadaswaram</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shehnai" title="Shehnai">shehnai</a>. They are typically not used in conjunction with a mouthpiece; rather the two reeds vibrate against each other. However, in the case of the <a href="/wiki/Crumhorn" title="Crumhorn">crumhorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bagpipes" title="Bagpipes">bagpipes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rauschpfeife" title="Rauschpfeife">Rauschpfeife</a>, a reed cap that contains an airway is placed over the reeds and blown without the reeds actually coming in contact with the player's mouth. Reed strengths are graded from hard to soft.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="How_double_reeds_are_made">How double reeds are made</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_(mouthpiece)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: How double reeds are made">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The making of double reeds begins in the same way as how single reeds are made. The cane is collected from <i><a href="/wiki/Arundo_donax" title="Arundo donax">Arundo donax</a></i>, dried, processed and cut down to manageable sizes for reed-making. Similar to single reed production, the cane is separated into various diameters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> The most common diameters for American-style oboe reeds are as follows: 9.5mm-10mm, 10mm-10.5mm, and 10.5mm-11mm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Many American oboists prefer a specific diameter at one time of the year and a different diameter at other times, depending on the season and the weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The tubes are then split into three equal parts. Special attention is made to pick the pieces that are not warped. If a piece of tube cane is warped and made into a reed, the reed will not <a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">vibrate</a> consistently on both sides, thus affecting the finished sound quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> After the cane is split, the pieces will be gouged in a gouging machine to remove many cane layers to drastically decrease the thickness. This eases the process of scraping for the reed-maker and helps to maintain sharper knives. Reed makers not only masterfully learn to make reeds, but also learn how to <a href="/wiki/Knife_sharpening" title="Knife sharpening">sharpen knives</a> with great mastery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Finally, the gouged pieces of cane soaked and "shaped" on a shaper with razor blades and allowed to dry before the final steps. The shaped piece of cane is then re-soaked and tied onto a "staple" for <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboe</a> reeds and formed on a mandrel for <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoon</a> reeds. Bassoon reeds are wrapped with <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylon thread</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton thread</a>, depending on the musician's preference. Oboe reeds are most commonly tied with nylon thread. Finishing both bassoon and oboe reeds requires the reed-maker to scrape along the cane section of the reed with a scraping knife to specific dimensions and lengths depending on the reed style and the musician's preference. Bassoon and oboe reeds are finished when the reeds play in tune or can make a sufficient "crow"-like noise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quadruple_reeds">Quadruple reeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_(mouthpiece)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Quadruple reeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Double_reed" title="Double reed">Double reed</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lokesh_Anand_Shehnai_Player.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1438" data-file-width="1440" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Lokesh_Anand_Shehnai_Player.jpg/120px-Lokesh_Anand_Shehnai_Player.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Lokesh_Anand_Shehnai_Player.jpg/180px-Lokesh_Anand_Shehnai_Player.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Lokesh_Anand_Shehnai_Player.jpg/240px-Lokesh_Anand_Shehnai_Player.jpg 2x" width="120"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lokesh_Anand_Shehnai_Player.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The four reed shehnai is one of the most recognized instruments of India</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Quadruple_reed" title="Quadruple reed">Quadruple reed</a> instruments have four reeds, two on top and two on bottom. Examples of this include an archetypal instrument from <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Shehnai" title="Shehnai">Shehnai</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Pi_(instrument)" title="Pi (instrument)">Pi</a> from <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodian</a> <a href="/wiki/Sralai" title="Sralai">Sralai</a>. Having four reeds instead of two produces a very different tone and set of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harmonics" title="Harmonics">harmonics</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_reeds">Free reeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_(mouthpiece)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Free reeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Free_reed_aerophone" title="Free reed aerophone">Free reed aerophone</a></div>
<p>There are two types of <a href="/wiki/Free_reed_aerophone" title="Free reed aerophone">free reeds</a>: framed and unframed. Framed free reeds are used on ancient Asian instruments such as the Chinese <a href="/wiki/Sheng_(instrument)" title="Sheng (instrument)">shēng</a>, Japanese <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8D_(instrument)" title="Shō (instrument)">shō</a>, and Laotian <a href="/wiki/Khene" title="Khene">khene</a>, and modern European instruments such as the <a href="/wiki/Pump_organ" title="Pump organ">harmonium</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reed_organ" title="Reed organ">reed organ</a> (consisting of <a href="/wiki/Reed_pipe" title="Reed pipe">reed pipes</a>), <a href="/wiki/Harmonica" title="Harmonica">harmonica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Concertina" title="Concertina">concertina</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bandone%C3%B3n" title="Bandoneón">bandoneón</a>, <a href="/wiki/Accordion" title="Accordion">accordion</a>, and Russian <a href="/wiki/Bayan_(accordion)" title="Bayan (accordion)">bayan</a>. The reed is made from cane, willow, brass or steel, and is enclosed in a rigid frame. The <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">pitch</a> of the framed free reed is fixed.</p>
<p>The ancient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bullroarer_(music)" title="Bullroarer (music)">bullroarer</a> is an unframed free reed; it consists simply of a stone or board of wood tied to a rope which is swung around through the air to make a whistling sound. Another primitive unframed free-reed instrument is the leaf (the <i>bilu</i>), used in some traditional Chinese music ensembles. A leaf or long blade of grass is stretched between the sides of the thumbs and tensioned slightly by bending the thumbs to change the pitch. The tone can be modified by cupping the hands to provide a resonant chamber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">Materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_(mouthpiece)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most reeds for <a href="/wiki/Woodwind_instrument" title="Woodwind instrument">woodwind instruments</a> are made from <a href="/wiki/Arundo_donax" title="Arundo donax">cane</a>, but synthetic reeds are used by a small number of clarinetists, saxophonists, and <a href="/wiki/Double_reed" title="Double reed">double reed</a> players, as well as by bagpipers. Synthetic reeds are generally more durable than their natural counterparts and do not need to be moistened prior to playing. Many players consider them to have inferior <a href="/wiki/Timbre" title="Timbre">tone quality</a>.</p>
<p>Recent developments in synthetic reed technology have produced reeds made from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_polymer" title="Synthetic polymer">synthetic polymer</a> compounds <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.legere.com" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>, and as technology in this area has progressed, synthetic reeds have gained more acceptance. Synthetic reeds are useful when the instrument is played intermittently with long breaks in between, during which time a natural reed might become dry.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dizi_(musical_instrument)" title="Dizi (musical instrument)">dizi</a>, a <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> <a href="/wiki/Transverse_flute" title="Transverse flute">transverse flute</a>, has a distinctive kind of reed (a <a href="/wiki/Di_mo" title="Di mo">di mo</a>), which is made from a paper-like <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> membrane.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_vs._handmade">Commercial vs. handmade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_(mouthpiece)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Commercial vs. handmade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Musicians originally crafted reeds from cane using simple tools, a process which was time-consuming and painstaking. Specialized tools for cutting and trimming reeds by hand reduce the time needed to finish a reed. Today, nearly all players of single-reed instruments buy manufactured reeds, although many players adjust them by shaving or sanding. Some professionals make single reeds from "blanks", but this is time-consuming and can require expensive equipment. Among double reed players, advanced and professional players typically make their own reeds, while beginners and students often buy reeds either from their teachers or from commercial sources.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Care_and_maintenance">Care and maintenance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_(mouthpiece)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Care and maintenance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Wooden_reed_care" title="Wooden reed care">Wooden reed care</a></div>
<p>Reeds made from cane (used on woodwind instruments such as saxophones, clarinets, oboes, bassoons, etc.) are affected highly by climate changes. Sometimes musicians use the joke "my reed doesn't work because it's raining." While this is a joke, it's only a slight separation from the reality of the situation. As the temperature and amount of humidity changes, the reed also changes. It is a common occurrence for a musician's reed to behave vastly differently from the cold rehearsal room where he/she warms up, to the stage which is often hot due to onstage lights being used. While these changes in climate and the reeds are unavoidable, there are certain things that can be done to alter the reed.</p>
<p>First, it is important to explain that reeds are sold in different strengths (most commonly 2, 2.5, 3, 3.5, 4, 4.5, 5<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>). This is a number determined by a machine in the reed-making process that presses against the vamp (the part of the reed that includes the tip -where you place your tongue- and the heart -just behind the tip) of the reed and determines how stiff the reed is. They are given a number, and separated into boxes by that number. Reeds could be categorized by strength as far as 1/10 of a strength (3, 3.1, 3.2, 3.3, etc.), but too many strengths would not sell. What this means is that, for example, a box of 3 strength reeds would include all reeds from 2.75 to 3.25 strengths. In a box of 10 reeds, not every reed will be usable. This needs to be understood from the beginning.</p>
<p>Because reeds change with climate, reeds that are too soft can be kept in the hopes that they will eventually change with the climate and become thicker, but there is nothing else that can be done to them. If a reed is too stiff however, there are solutions. The most simple solution is to turn a piece of paper over (so that there is no ink) and gently rotate the reed around the paper while gently placing your fingers at the tip and the butt of the reed to ensure even distribution on the paper. This method works if the reed is just barely too stiff or the reed is warped (the tip is not flat). If the reed is more than just a little too stiff, you can use sand paper (preferably 300-500 grain) and repeat the same process as just described. Be careful not to damage the tip of the reed.</p>
<p>Sometimes the reason a reed doesn't play well, is because it was not cut properly (machine error). This cannot always be fixed, but it can be helped with a reed pen. Reed pens are expensive, but can be used to realign or re-cut the filing on the reed.</p>
<p>In order to get the longest life out of a reed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> the reed should be soaked in water for 2 minutes or until fully moist, but not soaked. They should be played for about 5 minutes the first day, 15 minutes the second, and 45 minutes on a third day. After this, they should continue to be soaked in water, but can be played as long as needed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Reed_players.22">"Reed players"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_(mouthpiece)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title='Edit section: "Reed players"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Especially in <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">musical theatre</a> orchestras, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woodwind" title="Woodwind">woodwind</a> players are commonly referred to as "reed players" or "reeds". These players are not restricted to one particular woodwind instrument group, but play ("double on") several different instruments. (Although the <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flutes</a> are not reed instruments, they are included as well.)</p>
<p>There are usually only four or five reed players in a pit orchestra who perform on all woodwind instruments (flute, oboe, clarinet, bassoon, saxophone). A basic reed part usually has three or four instruments (flutes, clarinets and saxophones being the most common), but can include up to eight instruments, such as the "Reed 3" part in Bernstein's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Side_Story_(musical)" title="West Side Story (musical)">West Side Story</a></i>, which calls for the player to use <a href="/wiki/Piccolo" title="Piccolo">piccolo</a>, flute, oboe, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_horn" title="English horn">English horn</a>, clarinet, <a href="/wiki/Bass_clarinet" title="Bass clarinet">bass clarinet</a>, and tenor and baritone saxophones. Through intricate doubling, the arranger can simulate the sound of a much larger woodwind section. (The <i>West Side Story</i> woodwind section would require twelve "classical" players instead of five "reed" players.)</p>
