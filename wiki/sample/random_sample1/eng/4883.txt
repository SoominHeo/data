<p><b>Reed valves</b> are a type of <a href="/wiki/Check_valve" title="Check valve">check valve</a> which restrict the flow of fluids to a single direction, opening and closing under changing pressure on each face. Modern versions often consist of flexible metal or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Composite_materials" title="Composite materials">composite materials</a> (<a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber">carbon fiber</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_valve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional">Traditional</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_valve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Traditional">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reed valves, normally a <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a> flap covering a hole, are amongst the earliest form of <a href="/wiki/Flow_control_valve" title="Flow control valve">automatic flow control</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquids" title="Liquids">liquids</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gases" title="Gases">gases</a>. They have been used for thousands of years in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_pump" title="Water pump">water pumps</a> and for hundreds of years in <a href="/wiki/Bellows" title="Bellows">bellows</a> for high-temperature <a href="/wiki/Forge" title="Forge">forges</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musical_instruments" title="Musical instruments">musical instruments</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_organ" title="Church organ">church organs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Accordion" title="Accordion">accordions</a>. In nature, <a href="/wiki/Heart_valve" title="Heart valve">heart valves</a> operate in a somewhat similar fashion.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pumps">Pumps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_valve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pumps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reed valves are used in some reciprocating compressor designs, and in the pumping element of some musical instruments, large and small.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Two-stroke_engines">Two-stroke engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_valve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Two-stroke engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Malossi_carbon_fibre_reed_valve_blades.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="468" data-file-width="875" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Malossi_carbon_fibre_reed_valve_blades.jpg/220px-Malossi_carbon_fibre_reed_valve_blades.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Malossi_carbon_fibre_reed_valve_blades.jpg/330px-Malossi_carbon_fibre_reed_valve_blades.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Malossi_carbon_fibre_reed_valve_blades.jpg/440px-Malossi_carbon_fibre_reed_valve_blades.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Malossi_carbon_fibre_reed_valve_blades.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A pair of <a href="/wiki/Malossi" title="Malossi">Malossi</a> reed valve blades made from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fibre" title="Carbon fibre">carbon fibre</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Reed valves are commonly used in high-performance versions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two-stroke" title="Two-stroke">two-stroke</a> engine, where they control the fuel-air mixture admitted to the cylinder. As the piston rises in the cylinder a vacuum is created in the <a href="/wiki/Crankcase" title="Crankcase">crankcase</a> beneath the piston. The resulting pressure differential opens the valve and the fuel-air mixture flows into the crankcase. As the piston descends, it raises the crankcase pressure causing the valve to close to retain the mixture and pressurize it for its eventual transfer through to the <a href="/wiki/Combustion_chamber" title="Combustion chamber">combustion chamber</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The Swedish motorcycle company <a href="/wiki/Husqvarna_Motorcycles" title="Husqvarna Motorcycles">Husqvarna</a> produced a two-stroke, 500 cc displacement single cylinder engine with a reed-valve controlled intake, one of the biggest in using this arrangement. Reed valves in two-stroke engines have been placed in the intake ports and also in controlling the intake to the crankshaft space.</p>
<p>Composite materials are preferred in racing engines, especially in <a href="/wiki/Kart_racing" title="Kart racing">kart racing</a>, because the stiffness of the petals can be easily tuned and they are relatively safe in failure. High-speed impact takes its toll on all reed valves, with metal valves suffering in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_fatigue" title="Metal fatigue">fatigue</a>. The physical inertia of reed valves means that they are not as entirely precise in action as <a href="/wiki/Rotary_valve" title="Rotary valve">rotary valves</a>, a rotary valve engine may run better than a reed valve engine at a small rpm range but the reed valve engine often runs better over a wider rpm range. More sophisticated designs partly address this by creating multi-stage reeds with smaller, more responsive reeds within larger ones that provide more volume later in the cycle. Nevertheless, current technology favors reed valves almost to the exclusion of rotary valves due to their simplicity and low implementation costs and less rotational mass.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wankel_rotary_combustion_engines">Wankel rotary combustion engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_valve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Wankel rotary combustion engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yanmar_Diesel" title="Yanmar Diesel">Yanmar Diesel</a>, a Japanese engine maker, was pioneer in introducing reed valves for flow control at intake ports of its small Wankel engines, showing an improvement in torque and performances at low rpm and under partial load of the engine. Toyota discovered the benefits of injecting fresh air into the Wankel RCE exhaust port, and also used a reed valve in prototypes where they tested the SCRE concept (Stratified Charge Rotary Engine). However, this kind of intake port arrangement never reached the production line for automobile size RCEs. According to David W. Garside, who developed the Norton line of Wankel-powered motorcycles, data from other RCE producers pointed that reed valves do improve performances at low rpm and under partial load, but reduce the high speed power output of the engine, a feature considered inconvenient for motorcycle engines.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pulse_jets">Pulse jets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_valve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pulse jets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reed valves are used in the cheap but inefficient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulse_jet_engine" title="Pulse jet engine">pulse jet engine</a>, such as the one used by the <a href="/wiki/Argus_As_014" title="Argus As 014">Argus As 014</a> engine in the German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V-1_(flying_bomb)" title="V-1 (flying bomb)">V-1 (flying bomb)</a>. The valves at the front of the cylindrical engine are opened by the low pressure in the combustion chamber caused by the resonance of the air column in the engine, <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> is squirted into the combustion chamber and ignited by the hot combustion gases of the previous cycle. Once the charge has expanded and mostly left the engine, pressure inside drops again to below-atmospheric values and the reed valve allows fresh air to enter and the cycle be repeated. Some ram-air pressure due to forward motion helps scavenging and filling the combustion chamber with the new, fresh air charge, thus improving the power of the engine at higher speeds.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_modelling">Design and modelling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_valve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Design and modelling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Reed valves are designed considering the <a href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient" title="Pressure gradient">pressure gradient</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mass_flow" title="Mass flow">mass flow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The pressure gradient is used to evaluate the valve lift during open condition; the lift and overall component geometry (considering also a pressure loss coefficient) are then used to calculate the mass flow. For high speed applications (compressors and engines) the dynamic response has to be considered. A simple approach consists in the evaluation of first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eigenvalue" title="Eigenvalue">eigenvalue</a> that is compared with exciting frequency. Design of reed valves can be refined using simulations. The dynamic of petals<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> can be studied neglecting the coupling between fluid and structure: in this case the evolution of the structural part are simulated using lumped parameters models or FEM models, discharge coefficients at various valve lift are evaluated with experiments or CFD simulations. The study of the complete system needs an integrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluid-structure_interaction" title="Fluid-structure interaction">Fluid-structure interaction</a> model.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reed_valves_for_compressors">Reed valves for compressors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reed_valve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reed valves for compressors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Modular_Reed_Valve_(Straightflo_Valve).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="458" data-file-width="507" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Modular_Reed_Valve_%28Straightflo_Valve%29.jpg/220px-Modular_Reed_Valve_%28Straightflo_Valve%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Modular_Reed_Valve_%28Straightflo_Valve%29.jpg/330px-Modular_Reed_Valve_%28Straightflo_Valve%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Modular_Reed_Valve_%28Straightflo_Valve%29.jpg/440px-Modular_Reed_Valve_%28Straightflo_Valve%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Modular_Reed_Valve_(Straightflo_Valve).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Modular Reed Valve (Straightflo Valve)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Reed valves, of the feather valve type, are used in small, single acting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compressors" title="Compressors">compressors</a> that have relatively high revolutions per minute. In air brake compressors, the cantilevered reed valve is used. These type of reed valves result in high losses and are difficult to service <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Compressor_Valve_Design_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Compressor_Valve_Design-4">[4]</a></sup> .Their application is restricted to smaller compressors where efficiency and uptime are not critical. In 2009, the Modular Reed Valve (MRV) technology was developed for compressors. The MRV has a flow path similar to reed valves used in two-stroke engines, but with design elements to handle the higher differential pressures seen in typical compressor applications. MRVs are drop in replacement reed valves for industrial reciprocating compressors, for speeds from 350 rpm to over 3600 rpm, pressure differential to 3500 psi (241 bar) across a valve, continuous application temperatures from -40 to 550 F (-40 C to 288 C), and for most gases including highly corrosive gases such as sour gas, <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acid_gas" title="Acid gas">acid gas</a> . Modular Reed Valves have been deployed for larger compressors in critical applications. Valves based on the MRV design such as the Straightflo valve, have been deployed in natural gas gathering, gas transmission, CO2 and gas injection, propane, air and CO2 applications. MRV has also been used in research involving repurposing automobile engines for compressed <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Compressed_Natural_gas_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Compressed_Natural_gas-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
