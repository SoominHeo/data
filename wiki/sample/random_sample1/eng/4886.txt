<p><b>Lydia Davis</b> (born July 15, 1947) is an American writer noted for literary works of extreme brevity (commonly called "<a href="/wiki/Flash_fiction" title="Flash fiction">flash fiction</a>").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lareviewofbooks.org_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-lareviewofbooks.org-6">[6]</a></sup> Davis is also a short story writer, novelist, essayist, and translator from French and other languages, and has produced several new translations of French literary classics, including <i><a href="/wiki/In_Search_of_Lost_Time" title="In Search of Lost Time">Swann’s Way</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Proust" title="Marcel Proust">Marcel Proust</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Madame_Bovary" title="Madame Bovary">Madame Bovary</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Flaubert" title="Gustave Flaubert">Gustave Flaubert</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lydia_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Davis was born in <a href="/wiki/Northampton,_Massachusetts" title="Northampton, Massachusetts">Northampton, Massachusetts</a>, on July 15, 1947.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ILFB_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ILFB-7">[7]</a></sup> She is the daughter of Robert Gorham Davis, a critic and professor of English, and Hope Hale Davis, a short-story writer, teacher, and memoirist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CL-Int_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-CL-Int-8">[8]</a></sup> Davis initially studied music—first piano, then violin—which was her first love. On becoming a writer, Davis has said, "I was probably always headed to being a writer, even though that wasn't my first love. I guess I must have always wanted to write in some part of me or I wouldn't have done it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> She studied at <a href="/wiki/Barnard_College" title="Barnard College">Barnard College</a>, where she mostly wrote poetry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1974 Davis married <a href="/wiki/Paul_Auster" title="Paul Auster">Paul Auster</a>, with whom she had a son named Daniel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CL-Int_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-CL-Int-8">[8]</a></sup> Auster and Davis later divorced and Davis is now married to the artist Alan Cote,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ind-Man_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ind-Man-11">[11]</a></sup> with whom she has another son, Theo Cote. She is a professor of creative writing at the <a href="/wiki/University_at_Albany,_SUNY" title="University at Albany, SUNY">University at Albany, SUNY</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ind-Man_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ind-Man-11">[11]</a></sup> and was a Lillian Vernon Distinguished Writer-in-Residence at <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a> in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYU_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYU-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lydia_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Davis has published six collections of fiction, including <i>The Thirteenth Woman and Other Stories</i> (1976) and <i>Break It Down</i> (1986), a finalist for the PEN/Hemingway Award. Her most recent collections were <i>Varieties of Disturbance</i>, a finalist for the National Book Award published by <a href="/wiki/Farrar,_Straus_and_Giroux" title="Farrar, Straus and Giroux">Farrar, Straus and Giroux</a> in 2007, and <i>Can't and Won't</i> (2013). <i>The Collected Stories of Lydia Davis</i> (2009) contains all her fiction up to 2008.</p>
<p>Davis has also translated <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Proust" title="Marcel Proust">Proust</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Flaubert" title="Gustave Flaubert">Flaubert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Blanchot" title="Maurice Blanchot">Blanchot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">Foucault</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michel_Leiris" title="Michel Leiris">Michel Leiris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Jean_Jouve" title="Pierre Jean Jouve">Pierre Jean Jouve</a> and other <a href="/wiki/French_literature" title="French literature">French writers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ILFB_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ILFB-7">[7]</a></sup> as well as the Dutch writer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A.L._Snijders&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A.L. Snijders (page does not exist)">A.L. Snijders</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_and_influence">Reception and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lydia_Davis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Davis has been described as "the master of a literary form largely of her own invention."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Many of her fictions are only one or two sentences. Davis has compared these fictions to skyscrapers in the sense that they are surrounded by an imposing blank expanse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Michael LaPointe writing in the <i>LA Review of Book</i>s goes so far as to say while "Lydia Davis did not invent <a href="/wiki/Flash_fiction" title="Flash fiction">flash fiction</a>, ... she is so far and away its most eminent contemporary practitioner".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lareviewofbooks.org_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-lareviewofbooks.org-6">[6]</a></sup> Her "distinctive voice has never been easy to fit into conventional categories", writes Kassia Boddy in the <i>Columbia Companion to the 21st Century Short Story</i>. Boddy writes: "Davis's parables are most successful when they examine the problems of communication between men and women, and the strategies each uses to interpret the other’s words and actions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of contemporary authors, only Davis, <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Dybek" title="Stuart Dybek">Stuart Dybek</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alice_Fulton" title="Alice Fulton">Alice Fulton</a> share the distinction of appearing in both <i><a href="/wiki/The_Best_American_Short_Stories" title="The Best American Short Stories">The Best American Short Stories</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Best_American_Poetry" title="The Best American Poetry">The Best American Poetry</a></i> series.</p>
<p>In October 2003, Davis received a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MacArthur_Fellowship" title="MacArthur Fellowship">MacArthur Fellowship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Believer_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Believer-1">[1]</a></sup> She was elected a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAAS_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-16">[16]</a></sup> Davis was a distinguished speaker at the 2004 <a href="/wiki/%26NOW_Festival" title="&amp;NOW Festival">&amp;NOW Festival</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Notre_Dame" title="University of Notre Dame">University of Notre Dame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.26Now_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-.26Now-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis was announced as the winner of the 2013 <a href="/wiki/Man_Booker_International_Prize" title="Man Booker International Prize">Man Booker International Prize</a> on 22 May 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013win_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-2013win-18">[18]</a></sup> The official announcement of Davis' award on the Man Booker Prize website described her work as having "the brevity and precision of poetry". The judging panel chair <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Ricks" title="Christopher Ricks">Christopher Ricks</a> commented that "There is vigilance to her stories, and great imaginative attention. Vigilance as how to realise things down to the very word or syllable; vigilance as to everybody's impure motives and illusions of feeling."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prize2013_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prize2013-19">[19]</a></sup> Davis won £60,000 as part of the biennial award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>She is widely considered "one of the most original minds in American fiction today."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
