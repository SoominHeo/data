<p><b>Limoges</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/ʒ/ 's' in 'pleasure'">ʒ</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-1">[1]</a></sup> <small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[li.mɔʒ]</a></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">Occitan</a>: <i>Lemòtges</i> or <i>Limòtges</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Occitan" title="Help:IPA for Occitan">[liˈmɔdʒes]</a></span>) is a city and <a href="/wiki/Communes_of_France" title="Communes of France">commune</a>, the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Haute-Vienne" title="Haute-Vienne">Haute-Vienne</a> <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">department</a> and the administrative capital of the <a href="/wiki/Limousin" title="Limousin">Limousin</a> in west-central <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.</p>
<p>Limoges is known for its medieval and Renaissance <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamels</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limoges_enamels" title="Limoges enamels">Limoges enamels</a>) on copper, for its 19th-century <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a> (<a href="/wiki/Limoges_porcelain" title="Limoges porcelain">Limoges porcelain</a>) and for its oak barrels which are used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognac_(drink)" title="Cognac (drink)">Cognac</a> and Bordeaux production. Some are even exported to wineries in California.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limoges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Limoges" title="Timeline of Limoges">Timeline of Limoges</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_and_medieval_history">Ancient and medieval history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limoges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient and medieval history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scarce remains of pre-urban settlements have been found in the area of Limoges. The capital of the <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaulish</a> people of the <a href="/wiki/Lemovices" title="Lemovices">Lemovices</a>, who lived in the area, was probably either near Villejoubert, some kilometres south-east of <a href="/wiki/Saint-L%C3%A9onard-de-Noblat" title="Saint-Léonard-de-Noblat">Saint-Léonard-de-Noblat</a>, or St Gence, just west of Limoges.</p>
<p>The city proper was founded as <b>Augustoritum</b> by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a>, around 10 BC: "rito-" is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaulish" title="Gaulish">Gaulish</a> for "<a href="/wiki/Ford_(crossing)" title="Ford (crossing)">ford</a>". The foundation was part of the reorganization of the province by the emperor <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>, hence the new name. The Roman city included an <a href="/wiki/Amphitheatre" title="Amphitheatre">amphitheatre</a> measuring 136 x 115 metres, a theatre, a <a href="/wiki/Forum_(Roman)" title="Forum (Roman)">forum</a>, baths and several sanctuaries. According to tradition, a temple consecrated to <a href="/wiki/Venus_(mythology)" title="Venus (mythology)">Venus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diana_(mythology)" title="Diana (mythology)">Diana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minerva" title="Minerva">Minerva</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_(mythology)" title="Jupiter (mythology)">Jupiter</a> was located near the modern cathedral. The city was on the typical Roman square plan, with two main streets crossing in the centre. It had a Senate and a currency of its own, a sign of its importance in the imperial age. Later, like many towns and cities in Gaul, it was renamed after the tribe (here the <a href="/wiki/Lemovices" title="Lemovices">Lemovices</a>) whose chief town it was; "Lemovices" subsequently evolved into "Limoges", and "Lemovicinus" for the area around changed into "<a href="/wiki/Limousin" title="Limousin">Limousin</a>".</p>
<p>Limoges was evangelized by <a href="/wiki/Saint_Martial" title="Saint Martial">Saint Martial</a>, who came to the city around 250 with two companions, Alpinianus and Austriclinienus. However, in the late 3rd century it was increasingly abandoned, due to unsafe conditions created by the invasions of various Germanic tribes. The population was concentrated instead in a more easily fortifiable site, the modern Puy Saint-Étienne, which is the centre of the modern Limoges. Starting from the construction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbey_of_St._Martial" title="Abbey of St. Martial">Abbey of St. Martial</a> (9th century), another settlement grew around the tomb of the saint, while a third area, next to the residence of the <a href="/wiki/Viscounty_of_Limoges" title="Viscounty of Limoges">viscount</a> (the future Castle of Saint Martial), seems to have been populated from the 10th century.</p>
<p>Starting from the 11th century, thanks to the presence of the Abbey of St. Martial and its large library, Limoges became a flourishing artistic centre. It was home to an important school of medieval music composition, which is usually called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Martial_School" title="St. Martial School">St. Martial School</a>; its most famous member was the 13th-century <a href="/wiki/Troubadour" title="Troubadour">troubadour</a> <a href="/wiki/Bertran_de_Born" title="Bertran de Born">Bertran de Born</a>.</p>

<p>In the 13th century, at the peak of its splendour, central Limoges consisted of two fortified settlements.</p>
<ul>
<li>The town proper, with a new line of walls encompassing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vienne_River" title="Vienne River">Vienne River</a>, inhabited mainly by clerks and workers. It has a bridge on the Vienne river named after Saint-Étienne, built by the bishops, and a developed port. Sacked in 1370, it never recovered entirely.</li>
<li>The castle, with 12 meter-high walls, including the abbey and controlled by the abbot, sometimes in contrast with the bishop-ruled town ("la Cité"). Traces of the walls can still be seen in the city centre. Outside the lines of walls were the popular quarters.</li>
</ul>
<p>In 1370, Limoges was occupied by <a href="/wiki/Edward,_the_Black_Prince" title="Edward, the Black Prince">Edward, the Black Prince</a>, who <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Massacre_of_Limoges" title="Massacre of Limoges">massacred</a> some 300 residents, "perhaps a sixth of the normal population", with another 60 members of the garrison of 140 dead as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">Modern history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limoges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city and castle were united in 1792 to form the single city of Limoges. During the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> several religious edifices, considered symbols of the <i><a href="/wiki/Ancien_R%C3%A9gime" title="Ancien Régime">Ancien Régime</a></i>, were destroyed by the population: these included the Abbey of St. Martial itself.</p>
<p>Some years later the porcelain industry started to develop, favoured by the presence of <a href="/wiki/Kaolinite" title="Kaolinite">kaolinite</a> which was discovered near Limoges in 1768<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indy_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-indy-4">[4]</a></sup> (near St Yrieix, south-west of Limoges). Many of the inhabitants became employed in the new sector or in connected activities (including the lumbering of wood needed for firing the porcelain) in manufacture and exporting needed for European distribution of <a href="/wiki/Limoges_Box" title="Limoges Box">Limoges Boxes</a>, dinnerware, and other porcelain wares.</p>

<p>. Because the Limousin region has had a long history of breeding (Baronet sheep and Limousine cows), the leather industry also settled in and around Limoges along the banks of the Vienne–the river providing the necessary water and power. Factories in Limoges and St Junien still produce luxury leather shoes, gloves, and bags.</p>
<p>In the 19th century Limoges saw strong construction activity, which included the destruction and rebuilding of much of the city centre. The unsafe conditions of the poorer population is highlighted by the outbreak of several riots, including <a href="/wiki/July_Revolution" title="July Revolution">that of July–November 1830</a>; April 1848. In early 1905 strikes began in another local industry, shoe factories soon followed in the porcelain factories. Barricades were built, the army intervened. There would be two casualties: a horse and a young porcelain worker, Camille Vardelle. The first French confederation of workers, Confédération Générale du Travail (CGT) (<a href="/wiki/General_Confederation_of_Labour_(France)" title="General Confederation of Labour (France)">General Confederation of Labour</a>), was created in Limoges in 1895.</p>
<p>During the World War II, many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Jews_in_Alsace" title="History of Jews in Alsace">Jews from Alsace</a> were evacuated to and around Limoges.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limoges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city is known for its basketball team <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CSP_Limoges" title="CSP Limoges">CSP Limoges</a> (Cercle St Pierre). Since 1983, the club has been French champion 5 times (1983, 1985, 1988, 1989, 1990) and three European titles (1982 (Korac Cup), 1988, 1993). It was the first French club team to become European champion in a collective sport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The team currently plays in Pro A, the French first basketball professional league.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limoges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Limoges experiences an <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> <i>Cfb</i>) common to much of Western France. Most precipitation occurs between October and February. On 27 December 1999, winds reached 148 km/h. On average, the city undergoes 41 days of frost and seven days of snow each winter. In June, July and August, precipitation tends to come only from violent thunderstorms coming from the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">Bay of Biscay</a>.</p>

<li>The <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_Saint_Martial,_Limoges" title="Abbey of Saint Martial, Limoges">Crypt of Saint Martial</a>, 10th century, including the tomb of the bishop who evangelized the city<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It was discovered in the 1960s while building an underground parking lot (place de la république).</li>
<li>Remains of the Gallo-Roman amphitheatre, one of the largest in ancient Gaul.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. It is noted for a fine loft built in 1534 and for the partly octagonal bell tower. The main artistic works are a Renaissance <a href="/wiki/Rood_screen" title="Rood screen">rood screen</a> and the tomb of the bishop Jean de Langeac, with sculpted scenes of the Apocalypse.</li>
<li>The <i>Chapelle Saint-Aurélien</i> (14th–17th centuries). <li>Church of <i>St-Michel-des-Lions</i>, begun in 1364. It houses the relics of St. Martial and has stained-glass windows from the 15th–16th century. The most striking feature is the 65 m-high tower, with a spire surmounted by a big bronze ball.</li>
<li>The bridges of Saint Martial (dating from the Roman era) and of St-Etienne (13th century).</li>
<li>The Limoges Fine Arts Museum (Musée des Beaux-Arts), housed in the 18th-century bishops' palace ('Palais de l'Évêché').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>

<li>The <i>Château de La Borie</i> (17th century), at 4 km (2.5 mi) from the city. It is home to the Centre Culturel de Rencontre de La Borie et l'Ensemble Baroque de Limoges.</li>
<li>The remains of the 12th-century Castle of Chalucet, 10 km (6.2 mi) south of the city. During the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years' War">Hundred Years' War</a> it was a base of the bands of pillagers which ravaged the country.</li>
<li>The city's <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical gardens</a> include the [[Jardin botanique de l'Evêché]next to the cathedral] and the <a href="/wiki/Jardin_botanique_alpin_%22Daniella%22" title='Jardin botanique alpin "Daniella"'>Jardin botanique alpin "Daniella"</a>.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_and_literature">Art and literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limoges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Art and literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Becket_Louvre_OA11333.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1250" data-file-width="2770" height="99" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Thomas_Becket_Louvre_OA11333.jpg/220px-Thomas_Becket_Louvre_OA11333.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Thomas_Becket_Louvre_OA11333.jpg/330px-Thomas_Becket_Louvre_OA11333.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Thomas_Becket_Louvre_OA11333.jpg/440px-Thomas_Becket_Louvre_OA11333.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>"Le marché de Limoges" (Limoges market) is the name of a section of <a href="/wiki/Pictures_at_an_Exhibition" title="Pictures at an Exhibition">Pictures at an Exhibition</a> by <a href="/wiki/Modest_Mussorgsky" title="Modest Mussorgsky">Modest Mussorgsky</a>.</p>
<p>In 1768,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indy_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-indy-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kaolinite" title="Kaolinite">kaolin</a>, a rock rich in fine, white clay which is used for making <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a>, was discovered at <a href="/wiki/Saint-Yrieix-la-Perche" title="Saint-Yrieix-la-Perche">Saint-Yrieix-la-Perche</a>, 30 km south of Limoges. Under the impetus of the progressive economist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anne_Robert_Jacques_Turgot,_Baron_de_Laune" title="Anne Robert Jacques Turgot, Baron de Laune">Anne Robert Jacques Turgot, Baron de Laune</a>, who had been appointed <i>intendant</i> of this impoverished and isolated region, a new <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_art" title="Ceramic art">ceramics</a> industry was developed, and <a href="/wiki/Limoges_porcelain" title="Limoges porcelain">Limoges porcelain</a> became famous during the 19th century. However, <a href="/wiki/Limoges_porcelain" title="Limoges porcelain">Limoges porcelain</a> is a generic term for porcelain produced in Limoges rather than at a specific factory (there are still several porcelain factories in and around Limoges). More than 50% of all porcelain made in France comes from Limoges<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indy_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-indy-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Limoges is mentioned in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T.S._Eliot" title="T.S. Eliot">T.S. Eliot</a>'s poem <a href="/wiki/Gerontion" title="Gerontion">Gerontion</a> (<a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> 1919), lines 23 to 25:</p>
<p>'...Mr. Silvero/ With caressing hands, at Limoges/ Who walked all night in the next room.'</p>
<p>Eliot's compatriot and mentor <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Pound" title="Ezra Pound">Ezra Pound</a> visited Limoges in 1912 when researching the landscape and the work of the 12th-century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Troubadours" title="Troubadours">troubadours</a>. As he states in his essay <i>Troubadours: Theirs Sorts and Conditions</i>: '...a man may walk the hill roads and river roads from Limoges and <a href="/wiki/Charente" title="Charente">Charente</a> to <a href="/wiki/Dordogne" title="Dordogne">Dordogne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Narbonne" title="Narbonne">Narbonne</a> and learn a little, or more than a little, of what the country meant to the wandering singers...'</p>
<p>There is also a reference to Limoges in <a href="/wiki/Jean-Paul_Sartre" title="Jean-Paul Sartre">Jean-Paul Sartre</a>'s novel <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">Nausea</a>, near the middle of the book in the <a href="/wiki/Shrove_Tuesday" title="Shrove Tuesday">Shrove Tuesday</a> section, when the magistrate says: "I had a similar case at the beginning of my career. It was in 1902. I was deputy magistrate at Limoges..."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limoges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main railway station of Limoges is the <a href="/wiki/Gare_de_Limoges-B%C3%A9n%C3%A9dictins" title="Gare de Limoges-Bénédictins">Gare de Limoges-Bénédictins</a>. It offers direct connections with Paris, and Toulouse, and several regional destinations. The motorway <a href="/wiki/A20_autoroute" title="A20 autoroute">A20</a> connects Limoges with Chateauroux, Vierzon, Orléans and Paris to the north, and Brive-la-Gaillarde, Cahors, Montauban and Toulouse to the south. The nearest airport is <a href="/wiki/Limoges_%E2%80%93_Bellegarde_Airport" title="Limoges – Bellegarde Airport">Limoges – Bellegarde Airport</a>.</p>
<p>Urban transport in Limoges and its metropolitan area is operated by Société de transports en commun de Limoges Métropole (STCL). The Limoges urban bus network includes the <a href="/wiki/Trolleybuses_in_Limoges" title="Trolleybuses in Limoges">Limoges trolleybus system</a>, one of only four such systems currently operating in France.</p>
