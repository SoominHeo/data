<p><b>Levi Parsons Morton</b> (May 16, 1824 – May 16, 1920) was a <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">Representative</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> and the 22nd <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President of the United States</a> (1889–93). He later served as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_New_York" title="List of Governors of New York">31st Governor of New York</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levi_P._Morton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Morton was born in <a href="/wiki/Shoreham,_Vermont" title="Shoreham, Vermont">Shoreham, Vermont</a>. His parents were the Reverend Daniel Oliver Morton (1788–1852), a <a href="/wiki/Congregational_church" title="Congregational church">Congregationalist</a> minister of old New England stock, and Lucretia Parsons (1789–1862). His older brother, David Oliver Morton (1815–59), was <a href="/wiki/List_of_mayors_of_Toledo,_Ohio" title="List of mayors of Toledo, Ohio">Mayor of Toledo, Ohio</a>, from 1849 to 1850.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>He left school early and worked as a clerk in a <a href="/wiki/General_store" title="General store">general store</a> in <a href="/wiki/Enfield,_Massachusetts" title="Enfield, Massachusetts">Enfield, Massachusetts</a>, taught school in <a href="/wiki/Boscawen,_New_Hampshire" title="Boscawen, New Hampshire">Boscawen, New Hampshire</a>, engaged in mercantile pursuits in <a href="/wiki/Hanover,_New_Hampshire" title="Hanover, New Hampshire">Hanover, New Hampshire</a>, moved to <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, entered the dry-goods business in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, and engaged in banking there. He was an unsuccessful candidate for election in 1876 to the 45th <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congress_of_the_United_States" title="Congress of the United States">Congress</a>, but he was appointed by President <a href="/wiki/Rutherford_B._Hayes" title="Rutherford B. Hayes">Rutherford B. Hayes</a> to be an honorary commissioner to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Exhibition_of_1878" title="Paris Exhibition of 1878">Paris Exhibition of 1878</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">Political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levi_P._Morton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Member_of_Congress">Member of Congress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levi_P._Morton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Member of Congress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Morton was elected, as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Republican_Party" title="United States Republican Party">Republican</a>, to the 46th and 47th Congresses representing <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>. He served from March 4, 1879, until his resignation, effective March 21, 1881. The 1880 Republican presidential nominee, <a href="/wiki/James_A._Garfield" title="James A. Garfield">James A. Garfield</a>, asked Morton to be his <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">vice presidential</a> running mate, but Morton declined the offer.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minister_to_France">Minister to France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levi_P._Morton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Minister to France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After Garfield's election, Morton asked to be appointed United States <a href="/wiki/Ambassador" title="Ambassador">ambassador</a> to either the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> or <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. He was U.S. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassador_to_France" title="United States Ambassador to France">Minister Plenipotentiary to France</a> from 1881 to 1885. (A deluded <a href="/wiki/Charles_J._Guiteau" title="Charles J. Guiteau">Charles J. Guiteau</a>, reportedly decided to murder Garfield after he was "passed over" as minister to France.)</p>
<p>Morton was very popular in France. He helped commercial relations between the two countries run smoothly during his term, and, in Paris on October 24, 1881, he placed the first rivet in the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_Liberty" title="Statue of Liberty">Statue of Liberty</a>. (It was driven into the big toe of Lady Liberty's left foot.)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vice_President">Vice President</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levi_P._Morton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vice President">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Levi_P._Morton_House.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1933" data-file-width="2833" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Levi_P._Morton_House.JPG/220px-Levi_P._Morton_House.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Levi_P._Morton_House.JPG/330px-Levi_P._Morton_House.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Levi_P._Morton_House.JPG/440px-Levi_P._Morton_House.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

</div>
<p>Morton was elected Vice President of the United States, on the Republican ticket with President <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Harrison" title="Benjamin Harrison">Benjamin Harrison</a>, in which capacity he served from March 4, 1889, to March 4, 1893. During his term, Harrison tried to pass the <a href="/wiki/Lodge_Bill" title="Lodge Bill">Lodge Bill</a>, an election law enforcing the voting rights of blacks in the South; the billed failed because Morton did little in his role as the Senate's presiding officer to support the bill against a <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_United_States_Democratic_Party" title="History of the United States Democratic Party">Democratic</a> filibuster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Harrison blamed Morton for the bill's eventual failure, and, at the Republican convention prior to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1892" title="United States presidential election, 1892">1892 election</a>, Morton decided not to run for a second term and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Whitelaw_Reid" title="Whitelaw Reid">Whitelaw Reid</a> as the vice-presidential candidate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Harrison and Reid went on to lose the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1892" title="United States presidential election, 1892">1892 election</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Grover_Cleveland" title="Grover Cleveland">Grover Cleveland</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adlai_E._Stevenson_I" title="Adlai E. Stevenson I">Adlai E. Stevenson</a>, the Democratic candidates.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Governor_of_New_York">Governor of New York</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levi_P._Morton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Governor of New York">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LMorton.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3294" data-file-width="2388" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/LMorton.png/170px-LMorton.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/LMorton.png/255px-LMorton.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/LMorton.png/340px-LMorton.png 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LMorton.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gubernatorial portrait of Levi P. Morton.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Levi Morton was <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_New_York" title="Governor of New York">Governor of New York</a> in 1895 and 1896. He was considered for the <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">presidential</a> <a href="/wiki/1896_Republican_National_Convention" title="1896 Republican National Convention">nomination</a> in <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1896" title="United States presidential election, 1896">1896</a>, but the Republican Party chose <a href="/wiki/William_McKinley" title="William McKinley">William McKinley</a> instead. After his public career was over, he became a <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a> investor.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriages_and_personal_life">Marriages and personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levi_P._Morton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Marriages and personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Morton married his first wife, Lucy Young Kimball (July 22, 1836 – July 11, 1871) on October 15, 1856, in <a href="/wiki/Flatlands,_Brooklyn" title="Flatlands, Brooklyn">Flatlands, Brooklyn</a>. They had one child, a daughter who died in infancy, in 1857.</p>
<p>His first wife died in 1871, and in 1873 Morton married <a href="/wiki/Anna_Morton" title="Anna Morton">Anna Livingston Reade Street</a>. She was <a href="/wiki/Second_Lady_of_the_United_States" title="Second Lady of the United States">Second Lady of the United States</a> during her husband's vice–presidency, and often handled entertaining duties for the administration due to <a href="/wiki/First_Lady_of_the_United_States" title="First Lady of the United States">First Lady</a> <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Harrison" title="Caroline Harrison">Caroline Harrison</a>'s ill health. She had five daughters with Morton, and a son who died in infancy.</p>
<p>In 1890 he became one of the first members of the District of Columbia Society of the <a href="/wiki/Sons_of_the_American_Revolution" title="Sons of the American Revolution">Sons of the American Revolution</a>. He was assigned national society membership number 1838 and district society number 38.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He was also a member of the <a href="/wiki/General_Society_of_Colonial_Wars" title="General Society of Colonial Wars">General Society of Colonial Wars</a>.</p>
<p>In retirement, he served as President of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Club" title="Metropolitan Club">Metropolitan Club</a> at One East Sixtieth Street, New York, between 1900 and 1911. He was preceded in that office by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._Pierpont_Morgan" title="J. Pierpont Morgan">J. Pierpont Morgan</a>; and succeeded by Frank Knight Sturgis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He was also a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_League_Club_of_New_York" title="Union League Club of New York">Union League Club of New York</a>, and served as President of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Zoological_Society" title="New York Zoological Society">New York Zoological Society</a> from 1897 to 1909.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levi_P._Morton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Morton died on May 16, 1920, at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhinebeck,_New_York" title="Rhinebeck, New York">Rhinebeck</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Dutchess_County,_New_York" title="Dutchess County, New York">Dutchess County</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He died on his 96th birthday, the only <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> to have died on his birthday. He is interred in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rhinebeck_Cemetery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rhinebeck Cemetery (page does not exist)">Rhinebeck Cemetery</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levi_P._Morton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Village of <a href="/wiki/Morton_Grove,_Illinois" title="Morton Grove, Illinois">Morton Grove, Illinois</a>, is named after Morton. He provided the funding necessary to allow Miller's Mill (now Lincoln Avenue) to pass through the upstart neighborhood, and provide goods to trade and sell. Morton Grove was incorporated in December 1895.</p>
<p>Morton owned property in <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport, Rhode Island</a>, spending his summers on fashionable Bellevue Avenue in his mansion called "Fairlawn," a building currently owned by <a href="/wiki/Salve_Regina_University" title="Salve Regina University">Salve Regina University</a>, housing the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pell_Center_of_International_Relations_and_Public_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pell Center of International Relations and Public Policy (page does not exist)">Pell Center of International Relations and Public Policy</a>. He left a nearby property to the city of Newport for use as a park. The park is at the corner of Coggeshall and Morton avenues (the latter formerly Brenton Road), and is named Morton Park.</p>
<p>Morton sold or donated property he owned in <a href="/wiki/Hanover,_New_Hampshire" title="Hanover, New Hampshire">Hanover, New Hampshire</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Dartmouth_College" title="Dartmouth College">Dartmouth College</a>, and the college built Webster Hall on the land. Morton was considered an honorary alumnus at alumni gatherings in New York. He also owned a summer retreat in the <a href="/wiki/Adirondack_Park" title="Adirondack Park">Adirondack Park</a>, on Eagle Island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The architecture is of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Camps" title="Great Camps">Great Camps</a> style, designed by the notable architect <a href="/wiki/William_L._Coulter" title="William L. Coulter">William L. Coulter</a>. Over the years, the island found its way into the ownership of the <a href="/wiki/Girl_Scouts_of_the_USA" title="Girl Scouts of the USA">Girl Scouts of the USA</a>, where it remains today as <a href="/wiki/Camp_Eagle_Island" title="Camp Eagle Island">Camp Eagle Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Morton was the second longest-lived <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President of the United States</a>, dying on his 96th birthday. Only <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>'s first Vice President, <a href="/wiki/John_Nance_Garner" title="John Nance Garner">John Nance Garner</a> (who died 15 days before his 99th birthday) lived longer. Morton survived five of his successors in the vice presidency: <a href="/wiki/Adlai_Stevenson_I" title="Adlai Stevenson I">Adlai E. Stevenson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garret_Hobart" title="Garret Hobart">Garret Hobart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_W._Fairbanks" title="Charles W. Fairbanks">Charles W. Fairbanks</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_S._Sherman" title="James S. Sherman">James S. Sherman</a>.</p>
