<p><b>Riverfront Stadium</b>, also known as <b>Cinergy Field</b> from 1996 to 2002, was a multi-purpose stadium in <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a>, Ohio, United States that was the home of the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds" title="Cincinnati Reds">Cincinnati Reds</a> of <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> from <a href="/wiki/1970_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="1970 Major League Baseball season">1970</a> through <a href="/wiki/2002_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="2002 Major League Baseball season">2002</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Bengals" title="Cincinnati Bengals">Cincinnati Bengals</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> from <a href="/wiki/1970_NFL_season" title="1970 NFL season">1970</a> to <a href="/wiki/1999_NFL_season" title="1999 NFL season">1999</a>. Located on the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Cincinnati" title="Downtown Cincinnati">downtown Cincinnati</a>, the stadium was best known as the home of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Big_Red_Machine" title="The Big Red Machine">The Big Red Machine</a>", as the Reds were often called in the 1970s.</p>
<p>Construction began on February 1, 1968, and was completed at a cost of less than $50 million. On June 30, 1970, the Reds hosted the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Braves" title="Atlanta Braves">Atlanta Braves</a> in their grand opening, with <a href="/wiki/Hank_Aaron" title="Hank Aaron">Hank Aaron</a> hitting the first ever home run at Riverfront. Two weeks later on July 14, 1970, Riverfront hosted the <a href="/wiki/1970_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="1970 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">1970 Major League Baseball All-Star Game</a>. This game is best remembered for the often-replayed collision at home plate between Reds star <a href="/wiki/Pete_Rose" title="Pete Rose">Pete Rose</a> and catcher <a href="/wiki/Ray_Fosse" title="Ray Fosse">Ray Fosse</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a>.</p>
<p>In September 1996, Riverfront Stadium was renamed "Cinergy Field" in a sponsorship deal with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greater_Cincinnati" title="Greater Cincinnati">Greater Cincinnati</a> energy company <a href="/wiki/Cinergy" title="Cinergy">Cinergy</a>. In <a href="/wiki/2001_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="2001 Major League Baseball season">2001</a>, to make room for <a href="/wiki/Great_American_Ball_Park" title="Great American Ball Park">Great American Ball Park</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Seating_capacity" title="Seating capacity">seating capacity</a> at Cinergy Field was reduced to 39,000. There was a huge wall in center field visible after the renovations, to serve as the <a href="/wiki/Batter%27s_eye" title="Batter's eye">batter's eye</a>. The stadium was demolished by implosion on December 29, 2002.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Riverfront_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Riverfront was a multi-purpose, circular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cookie-cutter_stadium" title="Cookie-cutter stadium">"cookie-cutter" stadium</a>, one of many built in the United States in the late 1960s and early 1970s as communities sought to save money by having their <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">football</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a> teams share the same facility. Riverfront, <a href="/wiki/Veterans_Stadium" title="Veterans Stadium">Veterans Stadium</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" title="Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Philadelphia, Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Busch_Memorial_Stadium" title="Busch Memorial Stadium">Busch Memorial Stadium</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlanta%E2%80%93Fulton_County_Stadium" title="Atlanta–Fulton County Stadium">Atlanta–Fulton County Stadium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Three_Rivers_Stadium" title="Three Rivers Stadium">Three Rivers Stadium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shea_Stadium" title="Shea Stadium">Shea Stadium</a> in New York and <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy_Memorial_Stadium" title="Robert F. Kennedy Memorial Stadium">Robert F. Kennedy Memorial Stadium</a> in Washington, D.C. all opened within a few years of each other and were largely indistinguishable from one another; in particular, it was often confused with fellow <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a> cookie-cutter Three Rivers Stadium by sportscasters because of the two stadium's similar names and similar designs.</p>
<p>One feature of Riverfront that was an improvement compared to some of its fellow "cookie-cutters" was the fact that the lower level field box ("blue") seats for baseball from home plate to the left field foul line were movable in an arc along curved tracks so that the field level seats on both sidelines in the football configuration were parallel to, and thus closer to, the football field than in some of the other cookie-cutter stadia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The astroturf panels covering the tracks could be seen in left field during Reds games. (Shea Stadium in New York had featured a similar movable field-level seats design from its debut in 1964 until the Jets moved to <a href="/wiki/Giants_Stadium" title="Giants Stadium">Giants Stadium</a> in New Jersey in 1984, after which the Mets retrofitted Shea for exclusive baseball use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A variation of this design was also used at <a href="/wiki/Three_Rivers_Stadium" title="Three Rivers Stadium">Three Rivers Stadium</a> in Pittsburgh.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The site on which Riverfront Stadium sat originally included the 2nd Street <a href="/wiki/Tenement" title="Tenement">tenement</a>, birthplace and boyhood home of <a href="/wiki/Cowboy" title="Cowboy">cowboy</a> singer and actor <a href="/wiki/Roy_Rogers" title="Roy Rogers">Roy Rogers</a>, who joked that he was born "somewhere between second base and center field."</p>
<p>Riverfront Stadium's scoreboard was designed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=American_Sign_and_Indicator&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="American Sign and Indicator (page does not exist)">American Sign and Indicator</a>, but in its last years was maintained by <a href="/wiki/Trans-Lux" title="Trans-Lux">Trans-Lux</a>. That scoreboard would be upgraded in the 1980s with the addition of an adjacent Sony <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JumboTron" title="JumboTron">JumboTron</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Big_Red_Machine">Big Red Machine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Riverfront_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Big Red Machine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Reds moved to Riverfront Stadium midway through the 1970 season, after spending over 86 years at the intersection of Findlay Street and Western Avenue – the last 57½ of those years at <a href="/wiki/Crosley_Field" title="Crosley Field">Crosley Field</a>. Riverfront quickly earned a place in Cincinnati's century-long baseball tradition as the home of one of the best teams in baseball history. The Reds had only won three pennants in their final 39 years at Crosley Field (1939, 1940, 1961) but made the <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a> in Riverfront's first year (1970) and a total of four times in the stadium's first seven years, with the Reds winning back-to-back championships in <a href="/wiki/1975_World_Series" title="1975 World Series">1975</a> and <a href="/wiki/1976_World_Series" title="1976 World Series">1976</a>. The World Series would return in <a href="/wiki/1990_World_Series" title="1990 World Series">1990</a>, with Cincinnati winning the first two of a four-game sweep of the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Athletics" title="Oakland Athletics">Oakland Athletics</a> at Riverfront.</p>
<p>Baseball purists disliked Riverfront's <a href="/wiki/Artificial_turf" title="Artificial turf">artificial turf</a>, but Reds' Manager <a href="/wiki/Sparky_Anderson" title="Sparky Anderson">Sparky Anderson</a> and General Manager <a href="/wiki/Bob_Howsam" title="Bob Howsam">Bob Howsam</a> took advantage of it by encouraging speed and line drive hitting that could produce doubles, triples and high-bouncing infield hits. Players who combined power and speed like <a href="/wiki/Joe_Morgan" title="Joe Morgan">Joe Morgan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pete_Rose" title="Pete Rose">Pete Rose</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ken_Griffey,_Sr." title="Ken Griffey, Sr.">Ken Griffey, Sr.</a> thrived there. On defense, the fast surface and virtually dirtless infield (see photo) rewarded range and quickness by both outfielders and infielders, like shortstop <a href="/wiki/Dave_Concepci%C3%B3n" title="Dave Concepción">Dave Concepción</a> who used the turf to bounce many of his long throws to first. <a href="/wiki/Catcher" title="Catcher">Catcher</a> <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Bench" title="Johnny Bench">Johnny Bench</a> and <a href="/wiki/First_baseman" title="First baseman">first baseman</a> <a href="/wiki/Tony_P%C3%A9rez" title="Tony Pérez">Tony Pérez</a> played here. The artificial turf covered not only the normal grass area of the ballpark but is usually the "skinned" portion of the infield. Only the pitcher's mound, the home plate area, and cutouts around first, second and third bases had dirt surfaces. This was the first stadium in the majors with this "sliding pit" configuration. The new stadiums that would follow (<a href="/wiki/Veterans_Stadium" title="Veterans Stadium">Veterans Stadium</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royals_Stadium" title="Royals Stadium">Royals Stadium</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louisiana_Superdome" title="Louisiana Superdome">Louisiana Superdome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Stadium_(Montreal)" title="Olympic Stadium (Montreal)">Olympic Stadium (Montreal)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exhibition_Stadium" title="Exhibition Stadium">Exhibition Stadium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdome" title="Kingdome">Kingdome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hubert_H._Humphrey_Metrodome" title="Hubert H. Humphrey Metrodome">Hubert H. Humphrey Metrodome</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B.C._Place" title="B.C. Place">B.C. Place</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SkyDome" title="SkyDome">SkyDome</a>) would install sliding pits as the original layout, and the existing artificial turf fields in <a href="/wiki/Candlestick_Park" title="Candlestick Park">San Francisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Astrodome" title="Astrodome">Houston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Three_Rivers_Stadium" title="Three Rivers Stadium">Pittsburgh</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Busch_Memorial_Stadium" title="Busch Memorial Stadium">St. Louis</a> would change to the cut-out configuration within the next few years.</p>
<p>Riverfront hosted the MLB All-Star Game twice. First on July 14, 1970 with President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> in attendance (51,838 total attendance), and again on July 12, 1988 (55,837 attendance).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_football">Professional football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Riverfront_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Professional football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite Cincinnati's love of baseball, it was the prospect of a professional football team that finally moved the city to end 20 years of discussion and build a new stadium on the downtown riverfront. After playing for two seasons at <a href="/wiki/Nippert_Stadium" title="Nippert Stadium">Nippert Stadium</a> on the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cincinnati" title="University of Cincinnati">University of Cincinnati</a> campus, the Bengals built on the Reds' success in the stadium's first year when they recorded their first winning season and first playoff appearance in 1970, just their third year of existence.</p>
<p>Perhaps the most memorable football game at Riverfront was the <a href="/wiki/AFC_Championship_Game" title="AFC Championship Game">AFC Championship</a> on January 10, 1982. The game became known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Freezer_Bowl" title="Freezer Bowl">Freezer Bowl</a></i> and was won by the Bengals over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego_Chargers" title="San Diego Chargers">San Diego Chargers</a>, 27–7. The air temperature during the game was −9 °F (−23 °C) and the <a href="/wiki/Wind_chill" title="Wind chill">wind chill</a> was −37 °F (−38 °C), the coldest in NFL history. The win earned the Bengals their first of two trips to the <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl" title="Super Bowl">Super Bowl</a> (<a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XVI" title="Super Bowl XVI">XVI</a>) while playing at Riverfront.</p>
<p>Riverfront Stadium hosted the 1988 AFC Championship, as the Bengals beat the <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Bills" title="Buffalo Bills">Buffalo Bills</a> 21–10 to advance to <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XXIII" title="Super Bowl XXIII">their second Super Bowl appearance</a>.</p>
<p>During the Bengals' tenure, they defeated every visiting franchise at least once, enjoying perfect records against the <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Cardinals" title="Arizona Cardinals">Arizona Cardinals</a> (4-0), <a href="/wiki/New_York_Giants" title="New York Giants">New York Giants</a> (4-0), and <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Eagles" title="Philadelphia Eagles">Philadelphia Eagles</a> (3-0). They posted a 5–1 record in playoff games played in Riverfront Stadium, with victories over the <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Bills" title="Buffalo Bills">Buffalo Bills</a> (twice), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego_Chargers" title="San Diego Chargers">San Diego Chargers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Seahawks" title="Seattle Seahawks">Seattle Seahawks</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston_Oilers" title="Houston Oilers">Houston Oilers</a>. Their only home playoff loss came to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Jets" title="New York Jets">New York Jets</a>.</p>
<p>For most of the Bengals' tenure at the stadium, the field contained only the basic markings required for play. Until the late 1990s, there wasn't a logo at midfield or any writing in the end zone, which had long become standard in NFL stadiums.</p>
<p>During the 1988 season as the Bengals were making a <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl" title="Super Bowl">Super Bowl</a> run, Riverfront Stadium was nicknamed the Jungle as the Bengals went a perfect 10-0 at home during the regular season and in the playoffs. With the new stadium nickname, the fans and team adopted the <a href="/wiki/Guns_N%27_Roses" title="Guns N' Roses">Guns N' Roses</a> song "<a href="/wiki/Welcome_to_the_Jungle" title="Welcome to the Jungle">Welcome to the Jungle</a>" as the unofficial theme song for the Bengals. When <a href="/wiki/Paul_Brown_Stadium" title="Paul Brown Stadium">Paul Brown Stadium</a> opened in 2000, the Jungle theme was incorporated into the stadium design.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="College_football">College football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Riverfront_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: College football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Between 1970 and 1990 Riverfront Stadium hosted some 25 <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Bearcats_football" title="Cincinnati Bearcats football">University of Cincinnati football</a> games to accommodate higher-caliber visiting teams and local rivals which would overwhelm demand in their usual home, <a href="/wiki/Nippert_Stadium" title="Nippert Stadium">Nippert Stadium</a> (which then could only hold 28,000). Among the Bearcats' opponents were the <a href="/wiki/Alabama_Crimson_Tide_football" title="Alabama Crimson Tide football">University of Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland_Terrapins_football" title="Maryland Terrapins football">University of Maryland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Wildcats_football" title="Kentucky Wildcats football">University of Kentucky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisville_Cardinals_football" title="Louisville Cardinals football">University of Louisville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston_College_Eagles_football" title="Boston College Eagles football">Boston College</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia_Mountaineers_football" title="West Virginia Mountaineers football">West Virginia University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Penn_State_Nittany_Lions_football" title="Penn State Nittany Lions football">Penn State University</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/1985_NCAA_Division_I-A_football_season" title="1985 NCAA Division I-A football season">1985</a> game took place with the Nittany Lions number one in the coaches' poll, and the <a href="/wiki/Miami_Hurricanes_football" title="Miami Hurricanes football">University of Miami</a> three times, twice while the Hurricanes were the defending national champions.</p>

<p>To allow room for the construction of <a href="/wiki/Great_American_Ball_Park" title="Great American Ball Park">Great American Ball Park</a> (which was being built largely over the grounds the stadium already sat on), a large section of the left and center field stands were removed and the distance to the fences was shortened by five feet. The new Great American Ball Park and old Riverfront Stadium were 26 inches apart at its closest point during this time. Consequently, in its last years, the stadium achieved an openness and a degree of aesthetic appeal that it had lacked for most of its existence. In the Reds' final two seasons in the stadium, ongoing construction on Great American was plainly visible just beyond the outfield walls while the team played their games.</p>

<p>The stadium was demolished by implosion on December 29, 2002. Any debris was removed and scrapped. Today, part of the site is now occupied by Great American Ball Park with some parking lots over the old site. A small portion of the Riverfront Stadium site is now occupied by the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Reds_Hall_of_Fame_and_Museum" title="Cincinnati Reds Hall of Fame and Museum">Reds' Hall of Fame and Museum</a> and Main Street, which was extended when the new park was built and when the old park was demolished.</p>

<li>First <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitcher</a> ever to pitch a No-hitter and hit two Home runs in the same game: <a href="/wiki/Rick_Wise" title="Rick Wise">Rick Wise</a>, June 23, 1971.</li>
<li>Hank Aaron ties the all-time home run record with number 714: April 4, 1974.</li>
<li>First stadium to display <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">Metric distances</a> on the outfield walls (100.58 meters down the lines, 114.30 to the alleys, 123.13 to center): <a href="/wiki/1976_in_baseball" title="1976 in baseball">1976</a>.</li>
<li>Highest season attendance, 2,629,708: <a href="/wiki/1976_in_baseball" title="1976 in baseball">1976</a>.</li>
<li>First <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baseball_slang" title="Baseball slang">rain checks</a> issued: August 30, 1978.</li>
<li>First player to <a href="/wiki/Hitting_for_the_cycle" title="Hitting for the cycle">Hit for the Cycle</a>: <a href="/wiki/Mike_Easler" title="Mike Easler">Mike Easler</a>, June 12, 1980.</li>
<li>Pete Rose breaks the all-time hit record with number 4,192: September 11, 1985.</li>
<li>First player ever to be <a href="/wiki/Caught_stealing" title="Caught stealing">caught stealing</a> four times in one game: <a href="/wiki/Robby_Thompson" title="Robby Thompson">Robby Thompson</a>, June 27, 1986.</li>
<li>Perfect Game: <a href="/wiki/Tom_Browning" title="Tom Browning">Tom Browning</a>, September 16, 1988.</li>
<li>Umpire <a href="/wiki/John_McSherry" title="John McSherry">John McSherry</a> collapsed and died on April 1, 1996.</li>
<li>Longest home run, 473': <a href="/wiki/Mark_McGwire" title="Mark McGwire">Mark McGwire</a>, May 5, <a href="/wiki/2000_Major_League_Baseball_season" title="2000 Major League Baseball season">2000</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Football_3">Football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Riverfront_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>First <a href="/wiki/Touchdown" title="Touchdown">Touchdown</a>: <a href="/wiki/Sam_Wyche" title="Sam Wyche">Sam Wyche</a>, September 20, <a href="/wiki/1970_NFL_season" title="1970 NFL season">1970</a></li>
<li>First <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_goal_(football)" title="Field goal (football)">Field goal</a>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horst_Muhlmann" title="Horst Muhlmann">Horst Muhlmann</a>, September 20, <a href="/wiki/1970_NFL_season" title="1970 NFL season">1970</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Freezer_Bowl" title="Freezer Bowl">Freezer Bowl</a>: Lowest wind chill game (2nd lowest temperature) in NFL history, January 10, <a href="/wiki/1982_NFL_season" title="1982 NFL season">1982</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Corey_Dillon" title="Corey Dillon">Corey Dillon</a> breaks the single-game rookie rushing record with 246 yards on December 4, <a href="/wiki/1997_NFL_season" title="1997 NFL season">1997</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concerts">Concerts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Riverfront_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Concerts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eagles_(band)" title="Eagles (band)">The Eagles</a> – <i><a href="/wiki/Hotel_California_(Eagles_album)" title="Hotel California (Eagles album)">Hotel California</a> Tour</i>, with <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Money" title="Eddie Money">Eddie Money</a> &amp; The <a href="/wiki/Steve_Miller_Band" title="Steve Miller Band">Steve Miller Band</a> – August 16, 1978 (51,855 people attended)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">The Rolling Stones</a> – <i><a href="/wiki/Steel_Wheels/Urban_Jungle_Tour" title="Steel Wheels/Urban Jungle Tour">Steel Wheels Tour</a></i>, with <a href="/wiki/Living_Colour" title="Living Colour">Living Colour</a> – September 14, 1989 (55,155 people attended) &amp; <i><a href="/wiki/Voodoo_Lounge_Tour" title="Voodoo Lounge Tour">Voodoo Lounge Tour</a></i>, with <a href="/wiki/Lenny_Kravitz" title="Lenny Kravitz">Lenny Kravitz</a> – August 30, 1994 (34,137 people attended)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/New_Kids_on_the_Block" title="New Kids on the Block">New Kids on the Block</a> – <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magic_Summer_Tour" title="The Magic Summer Tour">The Magic Summer Tour</a></i> – July 10, 1990 (48,000 people attended)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a> – <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_World_Tour" title="The New World Tour">The New World Tour</a></i> – May 5, 1993 (35,342 people attended)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Asleep_at_the_Wheel" title="Asleep at the Wheel">Asleep at the Wheel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lee_Ann_Womack" title="Lee Ann Womack">Lee Ann Womack</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Chesnutt" title="Mark Chesnutt">Mark Chesnutt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Chesney" title="Kenny Chesney">Kenny Chesney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martina_McBride" title="Martina McBride">Martina McBride</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/George_Strait" title="George Strait">George Strait</a> – May 21, 2000 (42,000 people attended)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%27N_Sync" title="'N Sync">'N Sync</a> – <i><a href="/wiki/No_Strings_Attached_Tour" title="No Strings Attached Tour">No Strings Attached Tour</a></i>, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sisqo" title="Sisqo">Sisqo</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Pink_(singer)" title="Pink (singer)">P!nk</a> – July 14, 2000 (48,234 people attended) &amp; <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop_Odyssey_Tour" title="Pop Odyssey Tour">Pop Odyssey Tour</a></i>, with <a href="/wiki/Dream_(American_group)" title="Dream (American group)">Dream</a> – June 6, 2001 (35,000 people attended)</li>
</ul>
<p>In addition, the Kool Jazz Festival (now the Macy's Music Festival) was an annual fixture.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_gatherings">Religious gatherings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Riverfront_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Religious gatherings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah's Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a> hosted three conventions in the stadium, in 1971, 1974 and 1978.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Promise_Keepers" title="Promise Keepers">Promise Keepers</a> held a meeting there in 1997.</li>
</ul>
