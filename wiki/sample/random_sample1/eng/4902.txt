<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Rebate (marketing)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>A <b>rebate</b> is an amount paid by way of reduction, return, or refund on what has already been paid or contributed. It is a type of <a href="/wiki/Sales_promotion" title="Sales promotion">sales promotion</a> that <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketers</a> use primarily as <a href="/wiki/Incentive" title="Incentive">incentives</a> or supplements to product sales. The <b>mail-in rebate</b> (<b>MIR</b>) is the most common. A MIR entitles the buyer to mail in a <a href="/wiki/Coupon" title="Coupon">coupon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Receipt" title="Receipt">receipt</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Barcode" title="Barcode">barcode</a> in order to receive a check for a particular amount, depending on the particular product, time, and often place of purchase. Rebates are offered by either the retailer or the manufacturer of the chosen product. Large stores often work in conjunction with manufacturers, usually requiring two or even three separate rebates for each item. Manufacturer rebates are sometimes valid only at a single store. Rebate forms and special receipts are sometimes printed by the <a href="/wiki/Cash_register" title="Cash register">cash register</a> at time of purchase on a separate receipt or available online for download. In some cases, the rebate may be available immediately, in which case it is referred to as an <a href="/wiki/Instant_rebate" title="Instant rebate">instant rebate</a>. Some rebate programs offer several payout options to consumers, including a paper check, a prepaid card that can be spent immediately without a trip to the bank, or even <a href="/wiki/PayPal" title="PayPal">PayPal</a> payout.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebate_(marketing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rebates are heavily used for advertising sales in retail stores. For example, an item might be advertised as "$39 after rebate" with the item costing $79 out-the-door with a $40 rebate that the customer would need to redeem. The <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">turnaround time</a> is generally four to eight weeks, though some rebates note a period of eight to twelve weeks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>, rebates are less common,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> with manufacturers and retailers preferring to give discounts at the point of sale rather than requiring mail-in or coupons. However, rebates are sometimes given in the form of "cashback offers" for <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone_cashback" title="Mobile phone cashback">mobile phone contracts</a> or other high value retail items sold alongside a credit agreement.</p>
<p>Rebates are commonly offered by retailers indirectly, through the use of a third party rebate or <a href="/wiki/Cashback_website" title="Cashback website">Cashback website</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rebate_clearinghouses">Rebate clearinghouses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebate_(marketing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rebate clearinghouses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most rebates are handled under <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contract</a> by rebate <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/clearing_house" title="wikt:clearing house">clearinghouses</a> that specialize in processing rebates and contest applications.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulations_and_laws">Regulations and laws</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebate_(marketing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Regulations and laws">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a> state regulations section 42-110b-19(e) require retailers who advertise the net price of an item after rebate to pay consumers the amount of that rebate at time of purchase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a> has similar legislation (Gen. Laws 6-13.1-1).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Otherwise, the after-rebate price cannot be advertised as the final price to be paid by the consumer. For example, retailers in Connecticut can advertise only "$40 with a $40 rebate," not "Free After Rebate," unless they give the rebate at the time of purchase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rationale">Rationale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebate_(marketing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rationale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rebates have become very popular in retail sales within the United States. Retailers and manufacturers have many reasons to offer them:</p>
<ul>
<li>The information given in the rebate form, such as name, address, method of payment, can be used for <a href="/wiki/Data_mining" title="Data mining">data mining</a> studies of consumer behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>The information can be used as evidence of a pre-existing business relationship for marketing purposes, such as <a href="/wiki/Do_not_call_list" title="Do not call list">do not call lists</a>.</li>
<li>Customers tend to notice price increases and react negatively. Rebates offer retailers the benefit of giving customers a temporary discount on an item, to stimulate sales, while allowing it to maintain its current price point. This method avoids the negative backlash that could be perceived with a price being lowered and then raised later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rebates:_An_ethical_issue_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rebates:_An_ethical_issue-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>Rebates also allow companies to "price protect" certain product lines by being selective in which models or brands to be discounted. This allows retailers and manufacturers to move some product at lower cost while maintaining prices of successful models. A straight price reduction on some models would have a domino effect on all products in a line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfgate_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-sfgate-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>During the turnaround time, the company can earn <a href="/wiki/Interest" title="Interest">interest</a> on the money.</li>
<li>If the turnaround time crosses into the next <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_year" title="Fiscal year">fiscal year</a> or quarter, a rebate offer can inflate sales in the current period, and not have to be accounted for until the next period and then it could be attributed as a cost reducing sales or expense for the next period, giving companies an accounting advantage with their <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a> projections.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Not all buyers will meet the criteria to receive the rebate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Companies often require the original UPC barcode, receipt, and additional information, which a buyer may forget to include when redeeming the rebate. Companies almost always add other <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/caveat" title="wikt:caveat">caveats</a> to the rebate as well, such as the redemption having to be <a href="/wiki/Postmark" title="Postmark">postmarked</a> by a certain date. Another tactic that might be used is to disguise the rebate mail to look like junk mail, so that the customer may overlook it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It works in the company's favor if buyers do not act quickly to redeem. However, a <a href="/wiki/University_of_Florida" title="University of Florida">University of Florida</a> study notes that shorter redemption periods actually increase the redemption rate in the consumer's favor because it gives them less time for <a href="/wiki/Procrastination" title="Procrastination">procrastination</a> to set in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>New companies that want to make a break into a market can offer substantial rebate savings on their new product as a means of capturing a customer's attention. Zeus Kerravala, vice president at the Yankee Group, has said, "For companies that haven't been in a particular market, the rebate that essentially refunds the customer's money is a great way to get people to pay attention to them. This is especially true in consumer electronics, where brand name does matter. It's a good way to get customers to take a chance on a new brand."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits_and_costs_for_consumers">Benefits and costs for consumers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebate_(marketing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Benefits and costs for consumers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rebates may offer customers lower pricing. Deal hunter sites frequently tout the benefits of rebates in making technology affordable: "Rebates are the meat and potatoes of the ultimate tech deal, no matter what you are buying… They are paying you money to buy their stuff. All you have to do is take it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to 2011 research, 47% of consumers submitted a rebate in the past 12 months, whereas similar research conducted in 2009 showed that only 37 percent of consumers had submitted a rebate in the prior year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Industry advisers claim that if mail-in rebates go away, they will not be replaced by "instant rebates" of the same value amount because of the loss of the tangible benefits listed above (fiscal accounting, price protection, etc.) Steve Baker, vice president of industry analysis for NPD Group, comments that "It's a case of be careful of what you ask for. You may see some great deals go away."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-articles.moneycentral.msn.com_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-articles.moneycentral.msn.com-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rebates take a certain amount of time and effort from the consumer – figuring out the rules, filling out the forms, preparing and dropping off the mailing, cashing in the check, keeping track of the paperwork while this is being done. Thus, a rebate can be thought of as being paid to do this paperwork and provide one's personal data to the company. Chances of rebate mailing being lost or failing some criteria may further reduce the expected return on this effort.</p>
<p>Consumers who are aware of this, and who value their time, effort, and opportunity costs above the value of the rebate may choose to intentionally ignore a non-instant rebate that requires such procedures and assume the out-the-door price when considering the purchase.</p>
<p>On the other hand, if the consumer does not see it this way, if the consumer's income and budget are extremely limited or non-existent, or if the consumer is more concerned with the price than his or her time for any reason, the rebate may be seen as a good deal.</p>
<p>Another potential disadvantage to receiving a rebate is that the rebate does not refund any of the sales tax charged at the time of purchase. Thus the consumer will pay more in tax than if the price had simply been lowered at the time of purchase.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Redemption_rate_inconsistencies">Redemption rate inconsistencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebate_(marketing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Redemption rate inconsistencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is difficult to get an account of redemption rates from most rebate companies, partly due to a reluctance on the part of rebate fulfillment houses to release confidential business information. Among different sources, radically different numbers on both ends of the spectrum can be cited. Part of the reason is that most "redemption rates" do not distinguish whether they are calculated as part of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_sales" title="Total sales">total sales</a></i> or <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Incremental_sales&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Incremental sales (page does not exist)">incremental sales</a></i>.</p>
<ul>
<li>PMA, a marketing firm, estimated that in 2005, $486.5 million worth of rebates were redeemed. The redemption rates averaged 21.1% when calculated as a percentage of <b>total sales</b>, and 67.6% when calculated as a percentage of <b>incremental sales</b>. PMA notes, "These statistics reveal that redemption rates calculated as a percentage of total sales can be misleading when diluted by non-incremental sales, consequently making redemption rates appear lower than they truly are."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>Not all buyers remember to mail the coupons, a phenomenon known in the industry as <i>breakage</i>, or the shoebox effect. Though it can be used interchangeably with <i>breakage</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc.ca_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbc.ca-15">[15]</a></sup> <i>slippage</i> is the phenomenon when a consumer has his or her rebate fulfilled, but he or she loses or forgets to cash the check. Some rebate companies could tout a higher "redemption rate" including the <i>breakage</i>, while not calculating the potential <i>slippage</i> of uncashed checks.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Some_redemption_estimates">Some redemption estimates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebate_(marketing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Some redemption estimates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>In November 2005, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BusinessWeek" title="BusinessWeek">BusinessWeek</a></i> estimated a return rate of 60 percent. Some estimates have been as low as 2%. For example, nearly half of the 100,000 new <a href="/wiki/TiVo" title="TiVo">TiVo</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subscriber" title="Subscriber">subscribers</a> in 2005 did not redeem their $100 rebates, allowing the company to keep $5,000,000 in additional <a href="/wiki/Profit_(accounting)" title="Profit (accounting)">profit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Great_Rebate_Runaround_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Great_Rebate_Runaround-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>PC Data in the Reston, VA estimates between "10 and 30 percent".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>PlusNetMarketing in Wilmington, DE quotes 80%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>A representative in 2005 from The Marco Corporation stated, "In some cases, we do have redemption programs that go as high as forty to fifty per cent, but generally it’s about one to five per cent". In the same article, John Challinor, advertising manager for Sony Canada remarks, "The industry average is less than ten percent.... and it can be as low as one percent."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc.ca_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-cbc.ca-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>NPD Group, a marketing firm, estimates 50% to 70%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-articles.moneycentral.msn.com_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-articles.moneycentral.msn.com-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_complaints">General complaints</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebate_(marketing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: General complaints">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>At some <a href="/wiki/Big-box_store" title="Big-box store">big box stores</a>, personal computers are regularly sold with sizable rebates attached, making the advertised price more attractive to buyers. Hardware manufacturers have come under fire, also. <a href="/wiki/Dell" title="Dell">Dell</a>, for one, has been the subject of rebate complaints involving misprinted receipts, potentially confusing expiration dates, and service representatives who are slow to react.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Rebate issues began to clog Dell's customer service forums, leading the company to shut down that portion of the website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/CompUSA" title="CompUSA">CompUSA</a> used rebates regularly until it started closing its remaining stores in December 2007.</p>
<p>Cell phone service companies, including major players like <a href="/wiki/T-Mobile" title="T-Mobile">T-Mobile</a>, as well as third-party retailers like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_Shack" title="Radio Shack">Radio Shack</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inphonic" title="Inphonic">Wirefly</a> and others have received growing attention due to complex rebate redemption rules. Both carriers and retailers make customers submit rebate claims during a 30-day window, often 6 months after cell phone activation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Some authorized dealers have responded by trying to make rebate requirements more transparent, explaining that the carrier will withdraw payment from them if a customer quits service before the end of the contract.</p>
<p>In 2009, Florida State Attorney General Bill McCollum filed suit against <a href="/wiki/TigerDirect" title="TigerDirect">TigerDirect</a>, OnRebate, and TigerDirect's parent company Systemax, charging the companies with failing to provide rebates to customers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rebates_as_a_form_of_price_discrimination">Rebates as a form of price discrimination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebate_(marketing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rebates as a form of price discrimination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A common complaint against rebates is the claim that rebates can be used as a form of "<a href="/wiki/Price_discrimination" title="Price discrimination">price discrimination</a>" against members of lower classes who are less likely to redeem rebates than a more educated middle class.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rebates:_An_ethical_issue_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rebates:_An_ethical_issue-7">[7]</a></sup> Sridhar Moorthy, marketing professor at the University of Toronto also advocates a "price discrimination" theory between "people who are price-sensitive and people who are not price-sensitive".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc.ca_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-cbc.ca-15">[15]</a></sup> A different view, as taken by the BusinessWeek article, is that rebates can be viewed as a "tax on the disorganized" that is paid by those who do not submit their rebates as opposed to those who do. As mentioned above, rebates are also less enticing the more the consumer values the opportunity costs (time and effort) involved in rebate submission.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_trends">Recent trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rebate_(marketing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Recent trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some retailers have taken a step forward with offering consumers new ways to submit their rebates easily over the Internet, completely or partially removing any mail-in requirements. <a href="/wiki/Staples_Inc." title="Staples Inc.">Staples</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sears" title="Sears">Sears</a>, TigerDirect, and <a href="/wiki/Rite_Aid" title="Rite Aid">Rite Aid</a> currently offer an online submission option for all or some of the rebates they offer. These special rebates are usually identified as such and have instructions for full or partial online submissions. This is touted as a more accurate processing of the rebate, reducing the potential for human or mechanical error and, in many cases, eliminating the postage costs associated with traditional mail-in rebates, although some require the UPC or proof of purchase to be mailed in. Most of these retailers still let consumers submit rebates by mail.</p>
<p>In 2006, <a href="/wiki/OfficeMax" title="OfficeMax">OfficeMax</a> stores announced that they were eliminating mail-in rebates from their sales promotion in favor of instant rebates for their sale prices. The decision came after a year of working with rebate vendors and manufacturers to improve the rebate process and receiving "overwhelmingly negative feedback" from their customers about their rebate program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Instead of checks, prepaid gift cards are being given by many stores. Merchants like these cards, as they cannot be redeemed for cash and must be spent. However, some states require that retailers redeem the card value for cash if it falls below a certain level, such as $5 in Washington state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Many sales people are not aware of this and will deny giving the refund. Consumers must be careful of expiration dates and read the fine print.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The typical American household that takes advantage of consumer rebates saves an average of $150 annually. More than $8 billion was issued back to American households in 2011 alone by rebate programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
