<p>The <b>ribosome</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span></span>, <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/-/ affix">-</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/-/ affix">-</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a complex <a href="/wiki/Molecular_machine" title="Molecular machine">molecular machine</a>, found within all living <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>, that serves as the site of <a href="/wiki/Translation_(biology)" title="Translation (biology)">biological protein synthesis</a> (translation). Ribosomes link <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amino_acids" title="Amino acids">amino acids</a> together in the order specified by <a href="/wiki/Messenger_RNA" title="Messenger RNA">messenger RNA</a> (mRNA) molecules. Ribosomes consist of two major components: the small ribosomal subunit, which reads the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribonucleic_acid" title="Ribonucleic acid">RNA</a>, and the large subunit, which joins amino acids to form a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polypeptide" title="Polypeptide">polypeptide</a> chain. Each subunit is composed of one or more <a href="/wiki/Ribosomal_RNA" title="Ribosomal RNA">ribosomal RNA</a> (rRNA) molecules and a variety of <a href="/wiki/Ribosomal_protein" title="Ribosomal protein">ribosomal proteins</a>. The ribosomes and associated molecules are also known as the <i>translational apparatus</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sequence of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>, which encodes the sequence of the amino acids in a protein, is copied into a messenger RNA chain. It may be copied many times into RNA chains. Ribosomes can bind to a messenger RNA chain and use its sequence for determining the correct sequence of amino acids. Amino acids are selected, collected, and carried to the ribosome by <a href="/wiki/Transfer_RNA" title="Transfer RNA">transfer RNA</a> (tRNA) molecules, which enter one part of the ribosome and bind to the messenger RNA chain. It is during this binding that the correct translation of nucleic acid sequence to amino acid sequence occurs. For each coding triplet in the messenger RNA there is a distinct transfer RNA that matches and which carries the correct amino acid for that coding triplet. The attached amino acids are then linked together by another part of the ribosome. Once the protein is produced, it can then <a href="/wiki/Protein_folding" title="Protein folding">fold</a> to produce a specific functional three-dimensional structure although during synthesis some proteins start folding into their correct form.</p>
<p>A ribosome is made from <a href="/wiki/Biomolecular_complex" title="Biomolecular complex">complexes</a> of RNAs and proteins and is therefore a <a href="/wiki/Ribonucleoprotein" title="Ribonucleoprotein">ribonucleoprotein</a>. Each ribosome is divided into two subunits: 1; a smaller subunit which binds to a larger subunit and the mRNA pattern, and 2; a larger subunit which binds to the tRNA, the amino acids, and the smaller subunit. When a ribosome finishes reading an mRNA molecule, these two subunits split apart. Ribosomes are <a href="/wiki/Ribozyme" title="Ribozyme">ribozymes</a>, because the <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalytic</a> <a href="/wiki/Peptidyl_transferase" title="Peptidyl transferase">peptidyl transferase</a> activity that links amino acids together is performed by the ribosomal RNA. Ribosomes are often associated with the intracellular membranes that make up the <a href="/wiki/Endoplasmic_reticulum#Rough_endoplasmic_reticulum" title="Endoplasmic reticulum">rough endoplasmic reticulum</a>.</p>
<p>Ribosomes from <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Three-domain_system" title="Three-domain system">three-domain system</a>, resemble each other to a remarkable degree, evidence of a common origin. They differ in their size, sequence, structure, and the ratio of protein to RNA. The differences in structure allow some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotics</a> to kill bacteria by inhibiting their ribosomes, while leaving human ribosomes unaffected. In bacteria and archaea, more than one ribosome may move along a single mRNA chain at one time, each "reading" its sequence and producing a corresponding protein molecule.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_ribosome" title="Mitochondrial ribosome">mitochondrial ribosomes</a> of eukaryotic cells, are produced from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_gene" title="Mitochondrial gene">mitochondrial genes</a>, and functionally resemble many features of those in bacteria, reflecting the likely evolutionary origin of mitochondria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ribosomes were first observed in the mid-1950s by <a href="/wiki/Romanians" title="Romanians">Romanian</a> cell biologist <a href="/wiki/George_Emil_Palade" title="George Emil Palade">George Emil Palade</a>, using an <a href="/wiki/Electron_microscope" title="Electron microscope">electron microscope</a>, as dense particles or granules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The term "ribosome" was proposed by scientist Richard B. Roberts in 1958:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>During the course of the symposium a semantic difficulty became apparent. To some of the participants, "microsomes" mean the ribonucleoprotein particles of the microsome fraction contaminated by other protein and lipid material; to others, the microsomes consist of protein and lipid contaminated by particles. The phrase "microsomal particles" does not seem adequate, and "ribonucleoprotein particles of the microsome fraction" is much too awkward. During the meeting, the word "ribosome" was suggested, which has a very satisfactory name and a pleasant sound. The present confusion would be eliminated if "ribosome" were adopted to designate ribonucleoprotein particles in sizes ranging from 35 to 100S.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Albert, Microsomal Particles and Protein Synthesis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Albert_Claude" title="Albert Claude">Albert Claude</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christian_de_Duve" title="Christian de Duve">Christian de Duve</a>, and <a href="/wiki/George_Emil_Palade" title="George Emil Palade">George Emil Palade</a> were jointly awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a>, in 1974, for the discovery of the ribosome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">Chemistry</a> 2009 was awarded to <a href="/wiki/Venkatraman_Ramakrishnan" title="Venkatraman Ramakrishnan">Venkatraman Ramakrishnan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_A._Steitz" title="Thomas A. Steitz">Thomas A. Steitz</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ada_E._Yonath" title="Ada E. Yonath">Ada E. Yonath</a> for determining the detailed structure and mechanism of the ribosome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ribosome_shape.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1289" data-file-width="2580" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Ribosome_shape.png/220px-Ribosome_shape.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Ribosome_shape.png/330px-Ribosome_shape.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Ribosome_shape.png/440px-Ribosome_shape.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ribosome_shape.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<b>Figure 2 :</b> Large (red) and small (blue) subunit fit together.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The ribosome is a cellular machine which is highly complex. It is largely made up of specialized RNA known as <a href="/wiki/Ribosomal_RNA" title="Ribosomal RNA">ribosomal RNA</a> (rRNA) as well as dozens of distinct proteins (the exact number varies slightly between species). [Note – these rRNAs do not carry instructions to make specific proteins like mRNAs do.] The ribosomal proteins and rRNAs are arranged into two distinct ribosomal pieces of different size, known generally as the large and small subunit of the ribosome. Ribosomes consist of two subunits that fit together (Figure 2) and work as one to translate the mRNA into a polypeptide chain during protein synthesis (Figure 1). Because they are formed from two subunits of non-equal size, they are slightly longer in the axis than in diameter. Prokaryotic ribosomes are around 20 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nm</a> (200 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">Å</a>) in diameter and are composed of 65% rRNA and 35% <a href="/wiki/Ribosomal_protein" title="Ribosomal protein">ribosomal proteins</a>. Eukaryotic ribosomes are between 25 and 30 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nm</a> (250–300 Å) in diameter with an rRNA-to-protein ratio that is close to 1. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterial" title="Bacterial">Bacterial</a> ribosomes are composed of one or two rRNA strands. Eukaryotic ribosomes contain one or three very large rRNA molecules and multiple smaller protein molecules. <a href="/wiki/Crystallography" title="Crystallography">Crystallographic</a> work has shown that there are no ribosomal proteins close to the reaction site for polypeptide synthesis. This proves that the protein components of ribosomes do not directly participate in peptide bond formation catalysis, but rather suggests that these proteins act as a scaffold that may enhance the ability of rRNA to synthesize protein (See: <a href="/wiki/Ribozyme" title="Ribozyme">Ribozyme</a>).</p>

<p>The ribosomal subunits of <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryotes</a> and eukaryotes are quite similar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alberts_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-alberts-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The unit of measurement is the <a href="/wiki/Svedberg" title="Svedberg">Svedberg</a> unit, a measure of the rate of <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">sedimentation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Centrifugation" title="Centrifugation">centrifugation</a> rather than size. This accounts for why fragment names do not add up: for example, prokaryotic 70S ribosomes are made of 50S and 30S subunits.</p>
<p>Prokaryotes have 70<a href="/wiki/Svedberg" title="Svedberg">S</a> ribosomes, each consisting of a small (<a href="/wiki/30S" title="30S">30S</a>) and a large (<a href="/wiki/50S" title="50S">50S</a>) subunit. Their small subunit has a <a href="/wiki/16S_ribosomal_RNA" title="16S ribosomal RNA">16S</a> RNA subunit (consisting of 1540 nucleotides) bound to 21 proteins. The large subunit is composed of a <a href="/wiki/5S_ribosomal_RNA" title="5S ribosomal RNA">5S</a> RNA subunit (120 nucleotides), a 23S RNA subunit (2900 nucleotides) and 31 <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alberts_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-alberts-9">[9]</a></sup> Affinity label for the tRNA binding sites on the E. coli ribosome allowed the identification of A and P site proteins most likely associated with the peptidyltransferase activity; labelled proteins are L27, L14, L15, L16, L2; at least L27 is located at the donor site, as shown by E. Collatz and A.P. Czernilofsky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Additional research has demonstrated that the S1 and S21 proteins, in association with the 3'-end of 16S ribosomal RNA, are involved in the initiation of translation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eukaryotes have 80S ribosomes, each consisting of a small (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/40S" title="40S">40S</a>) and large (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/60S" title="60S">60S</a>) subunit. Their 40S subunit has an <a href="/wiki/18S_ribosomal_RNA" title="18S ribosomal RNA">18S</a> RNA (1900 nucleotides) and 33 proteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ben-Shem_A.2C_Garreau_de_Loubresse_N.2C_Melnikov_S.2C_Jenner_L.2C_Yusupova_G.2C_Yusupov_M._13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ben-Shem_A.2C_Garreau_de_Loubresse_N.2C_Melnikov_S.2C_Jenner_L.2C_Yusupova_G.2C_Yusupov_M.-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rabl.2C_Leibundgut.2C_Ataide.2C_Haag.2C_Ban_2010_730.E2.80.93736_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rabl.2C_Leibundgut.2C_Ataide.2C_Haag.2C_Ban_2010_730.E2.80.93736-14">[14]</a></sup> The large subunit is composed of a <a href="/wiki/5S_ribosomal_RNA" title="5S ribosomal RNA">5S RNA</a> (120 nucleotides), <a href="/wiki/28S_ribosomal_RNA" title="28S ribosomal RNA">28S RNA</a> (4700 nucleotides), a <a href="/wiki/5.8S_ribosomal_RNA" title="5.8S ribosomal RNA">5.8S RNA</a> (160 nucleotides) subunits and 46 proteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alberts_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-alberts-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ben-Shem_A.2C_Garreau_de_Loubresse_N.2C_Melnikov_S.2C_Jenner_L.2C_Yusupova_G.2C_Yusupov_M._13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ben-Shem_A.2C_Garreau_de_Loubresse_N.2C_Melnikov_S.2C_Jenner_L.2C_Yusupova_G.2C_Yusupov_M.-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klinge.2C_Voigts-Hoffmann.2C_Leibundgut.2C_Arpagaus.2C_Ban_2011_941.E2.80.93948_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klinge.2C_Voigts-Hoffmann.2C_Leibundgut.2C_Arpagaus.2C_Ban_2011_941.E2.80.93948-15">[15]</a></sup> During 1977, Czernilofsky published research that used <a href="/wiki/Affinity_label" title="Affinity label">affinity labeling</a> to identify tRNA-binding sites on rat liver ribosomes. Several proteins, including L32/33, L36, L21, L23, L28/29 and L13 were implicated as being at or near the <a href="/wiki/Peptidyl_transferase" title="Peptidyl transferase">peptidyl transferase</a> center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ribosomes found in <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a> of eukaryotes also consist of large and small subunits bound together with <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> into one 70S particle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alberts_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-alberts-9">[9]</a></sup> These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organelles" title="Organelles">organelles</a> are believed to be descendants of <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endosymbiotic_theory" title="Endosymbiotic theory">Endosymbiotic theory</a>) and, as such, their ribosomes are similar to those of bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alberts_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-alberts-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The various ribosomes share a core structure, which is quite similar despite the large differences in size. Much of the RNA is highly organized into various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RNA_Tertiary_Structure" title="RNA Tertiary Structure">tertiary structural motifs</a>, for example pseudoknots that exhibit coaxial stacking. The extra <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> in the larger ribosomes is in several long continuous insertions, such that they form loops out of the core structure without disrupting or changing it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alberts_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-alberts-9">[9]</a></sup> All of the catalytic activity of the ribosome is carried out by the <a href="/wiki/Ribozyme" title="Ribozyme">RNA</a>; the proteins reside on the surface and seem to stabilize the structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alberts_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-alberts-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The differences between the bacterial and eukaryotic ribosomes are exploited by <a href="/wiki/Medicinal_chemistry" title="Medicinal chemistry">pharmaceutical chemists</a> to create <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotics</a> that can destroy a bacterial infection without harming the cells of the infected person. Due to the differences in their structures, the bacterial 70S ribosomes are vulnerable to these antibiotics while the eukaryotic 80S ribosomes are not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Recht_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Recht-17">[17]</a></sup> Even though <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a> possess ribosomes similar to the bacterial ones, mitochondria are not affected by these antibiotics because they are surrounded by a double membrane that does not easily admit these antibiotics into the <a href="/wiki/Organelle" title="Organelle">organelle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High-resolution_structure">High-resolution structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: High-resolution structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:10_large_subunit.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="320" data-file-width="320" height="320" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c6/10_large_subunit.gif" width="320"/></a>

Proteins are shown in blue and the two RNA chains in orange and yellow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ban_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ban-19">[19]</a></sup> The small patch of green in the center of the subunit is the active site.</div>

<p>The general molecular structure of the ribosome has been known since the early 1970s. In the early 2000s, the structure has been achieved at high resolutions, of the order of a few <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">Å</a>.</p>
<p>The first papers giving the structure of the ribosome at atomic resolution were published almost simultaneously in late 2000. The 50S (large prokaryotic) subunit was determined from the <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaeon</a> <i>Haloarcula marismortui</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ban_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ban-19">[19]</a></sup> and the bacterium <i>Deinococcus radiodurans</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schluenzen_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schluenzen-20">[20]</a></sup> and the structure of the 30S subunit was determined from <i><a href="/wiki/Thermus_thermophilus" title="Thermus thermophilus">Thermus thermophilus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Venki_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Venki-8">[8]</a></sup> These structural studies were awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 2009. In May 2001 these coordinates were used to reconstruct the entire <i><a href="/wiki/Thermus_thermophilus" title="Thermus thermophilus">T. thermophilus</a></i> 70S particle at 5.5 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">Å</a> resolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two papers were published in November 2005 with structures of the <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i> 70S ribosome. The structures of a vacant ribosome were determined at 3.5-<a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">Å</a> resolution using <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">x-ray crystallography</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Then, two weeks later, a structure based on cryo-<a href="/wiki/Electron_microscope" title="Electron microscope">electron microscopy</a> was published,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> which depicts the ribosome at 11–15<a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">Å</a> resolution in the act of passing a newly synthesized protein strand into the protein-conducting channel.</p>
<p>The first atomic structures of the ribosome complexed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TRNA" title="TRNA">tRNA</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRNA" title="MRNA">mRNA</a> molecules were solved by using X-ray crystallography by two groups independently, at 2.8 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">Å</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and at 3.7 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">Å</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> These structures allow one to see the details of interactions of the <i><a href="/wiki/Thermus_thermophilus" title="Thermus thermophilus">Thermus thermophilus</a></i> ribosome with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRNA" title="MRNA">mRNA</a> and with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TRNA" title="TRNA">tRNAs</a> bound at classical ribosomal sites. Interactions of the ribosome with long mRNAs containing <a href="/wiki/Shine-Dalgarno_sequence" title="Shine-Dalgarno sequence">Shine-Dalgarno sequences</a> were visualized soon after that at 4.5- to 5.5-<a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">Å</a> resolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, the first complete atomic structure of the eukaryotic 80S ribosome from the yeast <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i> was obtained by crystallography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ben-Shem_A.2C_Garreau_de_Loubresse_N.2C_Melnikov_S.2C_Jenner_L.2C_Yusupova_G.2C_Yusupov_M._13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ben-Shem_A.2C_Garreau_de_Loubresse_N.2C_Melnikov_S.2C_Jenner_L.2C_Yusupova_G.2C_Yusupov_M.-13">[13]</a></sup> The model reveals the architecture of eukaryote-specific elements and their interaction with the universally conserved core. At the same time, the complete model of a eukaryotic 40S ribosomal structure in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetrahymena_thermophila" title="Tetrahymena thermophila">Tetrahymena thermophila</a></i> was published and described the structure of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/40S" title="40S">40S subunit</a>, as well as much about the 40S subunit's interaction with <a href="/wiki/EIF1" title="EIF1">eIF1</a> during <a href="/wiki/Translation_(biology)" title="Translation (biology)">translation initiation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rabl.2C_Leibundgut.2C_Ataide.2C_Haag.2C_Ban_2010_730.E2.80.93736_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rabl.2C_Leibundgut.2C_Ataide.2C_Haag.2C_Ban_2010_730.E2.80.93736-14">[14]</a></sup> Similarly, the eukaryotic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/60S" title="60S">60S subunit</a> structure was also determined from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetrahymena_thermophila" title="Tetrahymena thermophila">Tetrahymena thermophila</a></i> in complex with <a href="/wiki/EIF6" title="EIF6">eIF6</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klinge.2C_Voigts-Hoffmann.2C_Leibundgut.2C_Arpagaus.2C_Ban_2011_941.E2.80.93948_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Klinge.2C_Voigts-Hoffmann.2C_Leibundgut.2C_Arpagaus.2C_Ban_2011_941.E2.80.93948-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ribosomes are organelles that synthesize proteins. Proteins are needed for many cellular functions such as repairing damage or directing chemical processes. Ribosomes can be found floating within the cytoplasm or attached to the <a href="/wiki/Endoplasmic_reticulum" title="Endoplasmic reticulum">endoplasmic reticulum</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Translation">Translation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Translation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Translation_(genetics)" title="Translation (genetics)">Translation (genetics)</a></div>
<p>Ribosomes are the workplaces of <a href="/wiki/Protein_biosynthesis" title="Protein biosynthesis">protein biosynthesis</a>, the process of translating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRNA" title="MRNA">mRNA</a> into <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>. The mRNA comprises a series of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Codon" title="Codon">codons</a> that dictate to the ribosome the sequence of the <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> needed to make the protein. Using the mRNA as a template, the ribosome traverses each codon (3 nucleotides) of the mRNA, pairing it with the appropriate amino acid provided by an <a href="/wiki/Aminoacyl-tRNA" title="Aminoacyl-tRNA">aminoacyl-tRNA</a>. Aminoacyl-tRNA contains a complementary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticodon" title="Anticodon">anticodon</a> on one end and the appropriate amino acid on the other. For fast and accurate recognition of the appropriate tRNA, the ribosome utilizes large conformational changes (<a href="/wiki/Conformational_proofreading" title="Conformational proofreading">conformational proofreading</a>) .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid23582332_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid23582332-27">[27]</a></sup> The small ribosomal subunit, typically bound to an aminoacyl-tRNA containing the amino acid <a href="/wiki/Methionine" title="Methionine">methionine</a>, binds to an AUG codon on the mRNA and recruits the large ribosomal subunit. The ribosome contains three RNA binding sites, designated A, P and E. The A site binds an aminoacyl-tRNA; the P site binds a peptidyl-tRNA (a tRNA bound to the peptide being synthesized); and the E site binds a free tRNA before it exits the ribosome. Protein synthesis begins at a <a href="/wiki/Start_codon" title="Start codon">start codon</a> AUG near the 5' end of the mRNA. mRNA binds to the P site of the ribosome first. The ribosome is able to identify the start codon by use of the <a href="/wiki/Shine-Dalgarno_sequence" title="Shine-Dalgarno sequence">Shine-Dalgarno sequence</a> of the mRNA in prokaryotes and <a href="/wiki/Kozak_consensus_sequence" title="Kozak consensus sequence">Kozak box</a> in eukaryotes.</p>
<p>Although catalysis of the <a href="/wiki/Peptide_bond" title="Peptide bond">peptide bond</a> involves the C2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> of RNA's P-site <a href="/wiki/Adenosine" title="Adenosine">adenosine</a> in a proton shuttle mechanism, other steps in protein synthesis (such as translocation) are caused by changes in protein conformations. Since their <a href="/wiki/Active_site" title="Active site">catalytic core</a> is made of RNA, ribosomes are classified as "<a href="/wiki/Ribozyme" title="Ribozyme">ribozymes</a>,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and it is thought that they might be remnants of the <a href="/wiki/RNA_world" title="RNA world">RNA world</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

The ribosome begins at the start codon of RNA (<span style="color:#00AA00;">AUG</span>) and ends at the stop codon (<span style="color:#00AA00;">UAG</span>).</div>

<p>In Figure 5, both ribosomal subunits (<span style="color:#0000AA;">small</span> and <span style="color:#AA0000;">large</span>) assemble at the start codon (towards the 5' end of the RNA). The ribosome uses <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> that matches the current codon (triplet) on the mRNA to append an <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acid</a> to the polypeptide chain. This is done for each triplet on the RNA, while the ribosome moves towards the 3' end of the mRNA. Usually in bacterial cells, several ribosomes are working parallel on a single RNA, forming what is called a <i>polyribosome</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Polysome" title="Polysome">polysome</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Addition_of_translation-independent_amino_acids">Addition of translation-independent amino acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Addition of translation-independent amino acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Presence of a ribosome quality control protein Rqc2 is associated with mRNA-independent protein elongation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> This elongation is a result of ribosomal addition (via tRNAs brought by Rqc2) of <i><b>CAT</b> tails</i>: ribosomes extend the <a href="/wiki/C-terminus" title="C-terminus"><i><b>C</b></i>-terminus</a> of a stalled protein with random, translation-independent sequences of <a href="/wiki/Alanine" title="Alanine"><i><b>a</b></i>lanines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Threonine" title="Threonine"><i><b>t</b></i>hreonines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ribosome_locations">Ribosome locations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ribosome locations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ribosomes are classified as being either "free" or "membrane-bound".</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Protein_translation.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="250" data-file-width="250" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/94/Protein_translation.gif" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Protein_translation.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<b>Figure 6 :</b> A ribosome translating a protein that is secreted into the <a href="/wiki/Endoplasmic_reticulum" title="Endoplasmic reticulum">endoplasmic reticulum</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Free and membrane-bound ribosomes differ only in their spatial distribution; they are identical in structure. Whether the ribosome exists in a free or membrane-bound state depends on the presence of an <a href="/wiki/Signal_peptide" title="Signal peptide">ER-targeting signal sequence</a> on the protein being synthesized, so an individual ribosome might be membrane-bound when it is making one protein, but free in the cytosol when it makes another protein.</p>
<p>Ribosomes are sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Organelle" title="Organelle">organelles</a>, but the use of the term <i>organelle</i> is often restricted to describing sub-cellular components that include a phospholipid membrane, which ribosomes, being entirely particulate, do not. For this reason, ribosomes may sometimes be described as "non-membranous organelles".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_ribosomes">Free ribosomes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Free ribosomes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Free ribosomes can move about anywhere in the <a href="/wiki/Cytosol" title="Cytosol">cytosol</a>, but are excluded from the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">cell nucleus</a> and other organelles. Proteins that are formed from free ribosomes are released into the cytosol and used within the cell. Since the cytosol contains high concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Glutathione" title="Glutathione">glutathione</a> and is, therefore, a <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reducing environment</a>, proteins containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disulfide_bonds" title="Disulfide bonds">disulfide bonds</a>, which are formed from oxidized cysteine residues, cannot be produced within it.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Membrane-bound_ribosomes">Membrane-bound ribosomes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Membrane-bound ribosomes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When a ribosome begins to synthesize proteins that are needed in some organelles, the ribosome making this protein can become "membrane-bound". In eukaryotic cells this happens in a region of the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) called the "rough ER". The newly produced polypeptide chains are inserted directly into the ER by the ribosome undertaking <a href="/wiki/Vectorial_synthesis" title="Vectorial synthesis">vectorial synthesis</a> and are then transported to their destinations, through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretory_pathway" title="Secretory pathway">secretory pathway</a>. Bound ribosomes usually produce proteins that are used within the plasma membrane or are expelled from the cell via <i><a href="/wiki/Exocytosis" title="Exocytosis">exocytosis</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biogenesis">Biogenesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Biogenesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ribosome_biogenesis" title="Ribosome biogenesis">Ribosome biogenesis</a></div>
<p>In bacterial cells, ribosomes are synthesized in the cytoplasm through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcription_(genetics)" title="Transcription (genetics)">transcription</a> of multiple ribosome gene <a href="/wiki/Operon" title="Operon">operons</a>. In eukaryotes, the process takes place both in the cell cytoplasm and in the <a href="/wiki/Nucleolus" title="Nucleolus">nucleolus</a>, which is a region within the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">cell nucleus</a>. The assembly process involves the coordinated function of over 200 proteins in the synthesis and processing of the four rRNAs, as well as assembly of those rRNAs with the ribosomal proteins.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ribosome may have first originated in an <a href="/wiki/RNA_world" title="RNA world">RNA world</a>, appearing as a self-replicating complex that only later evolved the ability to synthesize proteins when <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> began to appear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-35">[35]</a></sup> Studies suggest that ancient ribosomes constructed solely of <a href="/wiki/Ribosomal_RNA" title="Ribosomal RNA">rRNA</a> could have developed the ability to synthesize <a href="/wiki/Peptide_bond" title="Peptide bond">peptide bonds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In addition, evidence strongly points to ancient ribosomes as self-replicating complexes, where the rRNA in the ribosomes had informational, structural, and catalytic purposes because it could have coded for <a href="/wiki/Transfer_RNA" title="Transfer RNA">tRNAs</a> and proteins needed for ribosomal self-replication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-39">[39]</a></sup> As amino acids gradually appeared in the RNA world under prebiotic conditions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> their interactions with catalytic RNA would increase both the range and efficiency of function of catalytic RNA molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-35">[35]</a></sup> Thus, the driving force for the evolution of the ribosome from an ancient self-replicating machine into its current form as a translational machine may have been the selective pressure to incorporate proteins into the ribosome’s self-replicating mechanisms, so as to increase its capacity for self-replication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specialized_ribosomes">Specialized ribosomes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Specialized ribosomes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Heterogeneity in ribosome composition has been proposed to be involved in translational control of protein synthesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Vincent Mauro and <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Edelman" title="Gerald Edelman">Gerald Edelman</a> proposed the ribosome filter hypothesis to explain the regulatory functions of ribosomes. Emerging evidence has shown that specialized ribosomes specific to different cell populations can affect how genes are translated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
