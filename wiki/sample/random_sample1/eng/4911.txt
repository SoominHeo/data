<p><b>Metzgeriales</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> of <a href="/wiki/Marchantiophyta" title="Marchantiophyta">liverworts</a>. The group is sometimes called the <b>simple thalloid liverworts</b>: "<a href="/wiki/Thallus" title="Thallus">thalloid</a>" because the members lack structures resembling stems or leaves, and "simple" because their tissues are thin and relatively <a href="/wiki/Cellular_differentiation" title="Cellular differentiation">undifferentiated</a>. All species in the order have a small <a href="/wiki/Gametophyte" title="Gametophyte">gametophyte</a> stage and a smaller, relatively short-lived, <a href="/wiki/Sporophyte" title="Sporophyte">spore-bearing</a> stage. Although these plants are almost entirely restricted to regions with high humidity or readily available moisture, the group as a whole is widely distributed, and occurs on every continent except Antarctica.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metzgeriales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Members of the Metzgeriales typically are small and thin enough to be translucent, with most of the tissues only a single cell layer in thickness. Because these plants are thin and relatively undifferentiated, with little evidence of distinct tissues, the Metzgeriales are sometimes called the "simple thalloid liverworts".</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Noteroclada_GS197.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1308" data-file-width="764" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Noteroclada_GS197.png/160px-Noteroclada_GS197.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Noteroclada_GS197.png/240px-Noteroclada_GS197.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Noteroclada_GS197.png/320px-Noteroclada_GS197.png 2x" width="160"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Noteroclada_GS197.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Noteroclada" title="Noteroclada">Noteroclada</a></i>, a "leafy" member of the Metzgeriales</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There is considerable diversity in vegetative structure of the Metzgeriales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schofield_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schofield-3">[3]</a></sup> As a rule, simple thalloid liverworts do not have structures resembling leaves. However, a few genera, such as <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fossombronia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fossombronia (page does not exist)">Fossombronia</a></i>, and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Symphyogyna&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Symphyogyna (page does not exist)">Symphyogyna</a></i>, are "semileafy" and have a <a href="/wiki/Thallus" title="Thallus">thallus</a> that is very deeply lobed, thus giving the appearance of leafiness. The genus <i><a href="/wiki/Phyllothallia" title="Phyllothallia">Phyllothallia</a></i> has a more striking leafiness, with paired lobes of tissue spaced regularly at swollen nodes along a central, forked stem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The several semileafy groups within the Metzgeriales are not closely related to each other, and the currently accepted view is that the leafy condition evolved separately and independently in each of the groups where it occurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuster_V_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schuster_V-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Members of the Metzgeriales also differ from the related <a href="/wiki/Jungermanniales" title="Jungermanniales">Jungermanniales</a> in the location of their <a href="/wiki/Archegonium" title="Archegonium">archegonia</a> (female reproductive structures). Whereas archegonia in the Jungermanniales develop directly from the apical cell at the tip of a fertile branch, archegonia in the Metzgeriales develop from a cell that is behind the apical cell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dittmer_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dittmer-6">[6]</a></sup> As a result, the female reproductive organs, and the sporophytes that develop within them, are always located on the dorsal surface of the plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Because these structures do not develop at the apex of the branch, their development in the Metzgeriales is described as <i>anacrogynous</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%80-" title="wikt:ἀ-">ἀν-</a> (<i>an-</i>, "not") + <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%84%CE%BA%CF%81%CE%BF%CF%82" title="wikt:ἄκρος">ἄκρος</a> (<i>akros</i>, "tip") + <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B3%CF%85%CE%BD%CE%AE" title="wikt:γυνή">γυνή</a> (<i>gynē</i>, “female”). The group was accordingly known as the <b>Anacrogynae</b> prior to being recognized as a separate order.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_ecology">Distribution and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metzgeriales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although these plants are almost entirely restricted to regions with high humidity or readily available moisture, the group as a whole is widely distributed, and occurs on every continent except Antarctica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuster_V_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schuster_V-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>One simple thalloid liverwort is not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photosynthetic" title="Photosynthetic">photosynthetic</a>. The species <i><a href="/wiki/Cryptothallus_mirabilis" title="Cryptothallus mirabilis">Cryptothallus mirabilis</a></i> is white as a result of lacking <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">chlorophyll</a>, and has <a href="/wiki/Plastid" title="Plastid">plastids</a> that do not differentiate into <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This species is a <a href="/wiki/Myco-heterotrophy" title="Myco-heterotrophy">myco-heterotroph</a> that obtains its nutrients from <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a> growing among its tissues. These plants grow in bogs and are typically found under <a href="/wiki/Sphagnum" title="Sphagnum">peat moss</a> near <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a> trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metzgeriales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The beginning of modern liverwort nomenclature is marked with the 1753 publication of <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus'</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Species_Plantarum" title="Species Plantarum">Species Plantarum</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stotlers_2000_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stotlers_2000-10">[10]</a></sup> although this relied heavily upon the prior work of Micheli (1729)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and Dillenius (1741).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Linnaeus included all 25 known species of liverworts, together with mosses, algae, and fungi, within a single class <a href="/wiki/Cryptogam" title="Cryptogam">Cryptogamia</a>. Linnaeus' system was heavily revised by workers in the early nineteenth century, so that by the time <a href="/wiki/Stephan_Endlicher" title="Stephan Endlicher">Endlicher</a> published his <i>Enchiridion Botanicum</i> in 1841, five orders of liverworts were defined, and the "Frondosae" were segregated as a group that is congruent with the modern concept of the Metzgeriales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuster_I_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schuster_I-13">[13]</a></sup> Endlicher's "Frondosae" included five subgroups (Metzgerieae, Aneureae, Haplolaeneae, Diplomitrieae, and Codonieae) with no assigned <a href="/wiki/Taxonomic_rank" title="Taxonomic rank">taxonomic rank</a>, but these groups were termed <i>Familien</i> by Dědeček in 1886.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The same five subgroups of "Frondosae", without significant change, were used in the <i>Synopsis Hepaticarum</i> of Gottsche, Lindenberg, and Nees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>A more thorough understanding of the Metzgeriales was not achieved until the <a href="/wiki/Plant_morphology" title="Plant morphology">morphological</a> and developmental work of Leitgeb in the late nineteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuster_I_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schuster_I-13">[13]</a></sup> Leitgeb was among the first to recognize and appreciate the significance of development and reproductive morphology as a guide to distinguishing liverwort groups. His careful examinations guided revisions made in the classification published from 1893 to 1895 by Schiffner in <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Engler" title="Adolf Engler">Engler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karl_Anton_Eugen_Prantl" title="Karl Anton Eugen Prantl">Prantl</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Schiffner thus divided his "Jungermanniales" into two broad groups according to whether the archegonia were terminal on reproductive branches (<i>Jungermanniales akrogynae</i>) or sub-terminal (<i>Jungermanniales anakrogynae</i>). This latter group included what are now recognized as the Metzgeriales, <a href="/wiki/Sphaerocarpales" title="Sphaerocarpales">Sphaerocarpales</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Haplomitriales" title="Haplomitriales">Haplomitriales</a>.</p>
<p>The simple thalloids were not given ordinal status until 1930 by Chalaud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chalaud_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chalaud-2">[2]</a></sup> Although subsequent systems similarly treated the group as distinct, the name of the order was more often given as "Jungermanniales anacrogynae" (or similar),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> or the group was retained within the Jungermanniales as a suborder with either this name<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> or the name "Metzgerineae".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The highly influential and comprehensive 1966 classification found in Schuster's <i>Hepaticae and Anthocerotae of North America</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuster_I_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schuster_I-13">[13]</a></sup> firmly established the name "Metzgeriales" for the group, although he had used this name in his earlier works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuster_1953_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schuster_1953-20">[20]</a></sup> Schuster revised his system in 1972<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and again in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuster_1984_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schuster_1984-22">[22]</a></sup> The only change he made in the circumscription of the Metzgeriales was to remove the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treubiales" title="Treubiales">Treubiales</a> in accordance with that change made in the classification of Schljakov.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schljakov_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schljakov-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Schljakov's 1972 classification had elevated several subordinal groups within the simple thalloids to the rank of order, and treated the Metzgeriales itself as a superorder "Metzgerianae", but Schuster's 1984 system rejected most of these changes. The classification of Crandall-Stotler and Stotler (2000) adopted several of Schljakov's orders, while revising their membership and grouping them within a subclass "Metzgeriidae".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stotlers_2000_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stotlers_2000-10">[10]</a></sup> These changes reflected a morphological analysis of species that had been presented three years earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stotlers_1997_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stotlers_1997-24">[24]</a></sup> Although their system changed the rank and the Latin ending of the name, the composition was identical to the Metzgeriales of Schuster (1966), with only the addition of the <a href="/wiki/Haplomitriales" title="Haplomitriales">Haplomitriales</a> to its membership. Subsequent studies incorporating DNA sequence analysis have removed the <a href="/wiki/Haplomitriales" title="Haplomitriales">Haplomitriales</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treubiales" title="Treubiales">Treubiales</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Blasiales" title="Blasiales">Blasiales</a> and place those taxa elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nygr.C3.A9n_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nygr.C3.A9n-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forrest_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forrest-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heinrichs_2005_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heinrichs_2005-27">[27]</a></sup> The remnant of the group, after the removal of these taxa, consists of their Metzgeriales (7 families), Fossombroniales (4 families), and the Phyllothalliaceae.</p>

<p>Families marked with an asterisk <sup>*</sup> were classified in the separate order Fossombroniales by Crandall-Stotler and Stotler,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stotlers_2000_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stotlers_2000-10">[10]</a></sup> but this grouping is not supported by subsequent analysis using DNA sequences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forrest_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Forrest-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forrest_2006_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forrest_2006-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two additional families were formerly included within the Metzgeriales, but since have been transferred to other classes of liverwort. The <a href="/wiki/Blasiaceae" title="Blasiaceae">Blasiaceae</a> has been assigned its own order <a href="/wiki/Blasiales" title="Blasiales">Blasiales</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" title="Phylogenetic">phylogenetic</a> studies show that it is more closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Marchantiales" title="Marchantiales">Marchantiales</a> than to members of the Metzgeriales. Likewise, the <a href="/wiki/Treubiaceae" title="Treubiaceae">Treubiaceae</a> is now in its own order <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treubiales" title="Treubiales">Treubiales</a>, within the recently recognized class <a href="/wiki/Haplomitriopsida" title="Haplomitriopsida">Haplomitriopsida</a>.</p>
<p>A 2016 analysis of liverwort classification has further reduced the Metzgeriales to include only two families, <a href="/wiki/Metzgeriaceae" title="Metzgeriaceae">Metzgeriaceae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aneuraceae" title="Aneuraceae">Aneuraceae</a>, with all other previously included families dispersed into three additional orders: <a href="/wiki/Fossombroniales" title="Fossombroniales">Fossombroniales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pallaviciniales" title="Pallaviciniales">Pallaviciniales</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pelliales" title="Pelliales">Pelliales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Botanical_authority">Botanical authority</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metzgeriales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Botanical authority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In previous decades, there has been considerable confusion over the correct attribution of the name "Metzgeriales". The ordinal name Metzgeriales was first published by Chalaud in 1930 with the description “J’ai désigné très généralement les Jungermannniales anacrogynes sous le nom de Metzgériales,”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chalaud_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chalaud-2">[2]</a></sup> (that is: "I have designated very generally the <i>Jungermannniales anacrogynes</i> under the name of <i>Metzgeriales</i>"). Chalaud cited Underwood 1894<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> in support of his treatment, but Underwood himself used only the name <i>Metzgeriaceae</i> for the group, and considered the whole to represent a single family. The publication of the ordinal name by Chalaud was accepted as correct by Grolle in his 1983 synopsis of the generic and higher-rank names of liverworts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Writing in 1984, Rudolf M. Schuster questioned the correct authority for the ordinal name. He believed that Underwood had first used the term, but concluded that as neither Underwood nor Chalaud had provided a formal description, the order should be cited as "Metzgeriales Schust. emend. Schljak."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuster_1984_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schuster_1984-22">[22]</a></sup> He ascribed first use of the name with a description to his own 1953 work on the liverworts of <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuster_1953_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schuster_1953-20">[20]</a></sup> This work included a considerable description sufficient to distinguish the order from all others, but Schuster relied on the 1972 emendation by Schljakov<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schljakov_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schljakov-23">[23]</a></sup> to provide the Latin diagnosis required by the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Botanical_Nomenclature" title="International Code of Botanical Nomenclature">International Code of Botanical Nomenclature</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Schuster reaffirmed his position on the authority for publication in 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuster_V_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schuster_V-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the publication of their revised liverwort classification in 2000, Crandall-Stotler and Stotler agreed with Schuster as to the attribution of the name, although they restricted their circumscription of the order considerably.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stotlers_2000_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Stotlers_2000-10">[10]</a></sup> However, upon additional review of the Chalaud paper, they reversed their opinion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stotlers_2008_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stotlers_2008-34">[34]</a></sup> They concluded that Chalaud's diagnosis, although "terse", was nonetheless adequate to satisfy the requirements for publication of a <a href="/wiki/Botanical_name" title="Botanical name">botanical name</a>, and that the <i>Code'</i>s requirement for a Latin diagnosis did not apply, since the Chalaud paper was published prior to 1 January 1935.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Chalaud is therefore the correct authority in citing the name "Metzgeriales".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metzgeriales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phylogeny">Phylogeny</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metzgeriales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Phylogeny">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Metzgeriales are a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" title="Paraphyletic">paraphyletic</a> group of liverworts, in that they do not include all the descendants of their most recent common ancestor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nygr.C3.A9n_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nygr.C3.A9n-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forrest_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-Forrest-26">[26]</a></sup> Specifically, the <a href="/wiki/Jungermanniales" title="Jungermanniales">Jungermanniales</a> (leafy liverworts) are more closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Aneuraceae" title="Aneuraceae">Aneuraceae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metzgeriaceae" title="Metzgeriaceae">Metzgeriaceae</a> than are other simple thalloid groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stotlers_2000_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Stotlers_2000-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_record">Fossil record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metzgeriales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fossil record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Metzgeria_furcata_2005.04.02_15.29.56-p4020023.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="878" data-file-width="1269" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Metzgeria_furcata_2005.04.02_15.29.56-p4020023.jpg/240px-Metzgeria_furcata_2005.04.02_15.29.56-p4020023.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Metzgeria_furcata_2005.04.02_15.29.56-p4020023.jpg/360px-Metzgeria_furcata_2005.04.02_15.29.56-p4020023.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Metzgeria_furcata_2005.04.02_15.29.56-p4020023.jpg/480px-Metzgeria_furcata_2005.04.02_15.29.56-p4020023.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Metzgeria_furcata_2005.04.02_15.29.56-p4020023.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Metzgeria furcata</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Because plants belonging to the Metgeriales lack hard tissues, and the plants often decay or die back, preservation of <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> is dependent upon rapid burial by <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">floods</a> or <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">volcanic ash</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Bryophytes are considered "delicate" plants, and this characteristic is often cited as the reason for the apparently poor fossil record of the group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> However, fossils of the Metzgeriales are distributed widely, both geographically and stratigraphically, and the fossils which have been found are often highly detailed and well-preserved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lacey_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lacey-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The oldest fossil <a href="/wiki/Bryophyte" title="Bryophyte">bryophyte</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Compression_fossil" title="Compression fossil">compression fossil</a> of <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pallavicinites_devonicus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pallavicinites devonicus (page does not exist)">Pallavicinites devonicus</a></i> from Upper <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a> rocks<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stewart_.26_Rothwell_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stewart_.26_Rothwell-39">[39]</a></sup> that has been confidently assigned to the Metzgeriales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuster_I_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Schuster_I-13">[13]</a></sup> Portions of the fossil that have been isolated for microscopic examination reveal an extraordinary degree of cellular detail. The plant consisted of a thin, ribbon-like, bifurcating thallus with a thicker central midrib.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The plant is remarkably similar in structure to members of the extant liverwort family <a href="/wiki/Pallaviciniaceae" title="Pallaviciniaceae">Pallaviciniaceae</a>, but no reproductive structures have been found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuster_I_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Schuster_I-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Additional species assigned to the genus <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pallavicinites&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pallavicinites (page does not exist)">Pallavicinites</a></i> have been found in rocks dating from the Carboniferous to the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_and_Taylor_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_and_Taylor-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another early fossil probably belonging to the Metzgeriales is the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> fossil <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thallites_willsi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thallites willsi (page does not exist)">Thallites willsi</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> which has been compared to the modern genus <i><a href="/wiki/Metzgeria" title="Metzgeria">Metzgeria</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stewart_.26_Rothwell_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stewart_.26_Rothwell-39">[39]</a></sup> However, it has been assigned to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Form_taxon" title="Form taxon">form genus</a> <i>Thallites</i>, which is used for thalloid fossil plants and <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> of uncertain relationships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oostendorp_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oostendorp-1">[1]</a></sup> Most fossils belonging to the Metzgeriales are assigned to the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Metzgeriites" title="Metzgeriites">Metzgeriites</a></i>, which was established for this purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steere_1946_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steere_1946-44">[44]</a></sup> Specimens from the Upper Carboniferous of <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a>, England, have been named <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hepaticites_metzgerioides&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hepaticites metzgerioides (page does not exist)">Hepaticites metzgerioides</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> and (as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specific_name_(botany)" title="Specific name (botany)">specific epithet</a> indicates) these specimens strongly resemble the genus <i>Metzgeria</i>. However, they have been placed instead in the form genus <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hepaticites" title="Hepaticites">Hepaticites</a></i>, used for fossils believed to be liverworts but without confident affiliation with any extant order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oostendorp_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Oostendorp-1">[1]</a></sup> This species is not restricted to British localities, but has also been found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karagandy_Province" title="Karagandy Province">Karagandy Province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lacey_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lacey-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a> fossils of <i>Metzgeriites</i> have also been found. <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Metzgeriites_glebosus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Metzgeriites glebosus (page does not exist)">Metzgeriites glebosus</a></i> has been collected from <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> strata of <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oostendorp_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Oostendorp-1">[1]</a></sup> The type material, and so far only material collected, consists of a midrib with a thin lamina that is deeply and irregularly lobed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steere_1946_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Steere_1946-44">[44]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> fossil from <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> has been named <i>Metzgeriites infracretaceus</i>. Like <i>M. glebosus</i>, it possesses a midrib and thin lamina, but unlike that taxon the lamina of <i>M. infracretaceus</i> is not lobed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
