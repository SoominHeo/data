<p>The <b>Libyan Army</b> was the branch of the <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_of_the_Libyan_Arab_Jamahiriya" title="Armed Forces of the Libyan Arab Jamahiriya">Armed Forces of the Libyan Arab Jamahiriya</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libyan_Arab_Republic" title="Libyan Arab Republic">Libyan Arab Republic</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libyan_Kingdom" title="Libyan Kingdom">Libyan Kingdom</a> responsible for <a href="/wiki/Ground_warfare" title="Ground warfare">ground warfare</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libyan_Army_(1951%E2%80%932011)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When Libya gained its independence in 1951, veterans of the original Sanusi Army formed the nucleus of the Royal Libyan Army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Though the Libyan army has a large amount of fighting equipment at its disposal, the vast majority was bought from the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> in the 1970s and 1980s and was largely obsolete at the time of the Libyan civil war. A high percentage remains in storage and a large amount of equipment has also been sold to various African countries. The Libyan Army was generally regarded as neither efficient nor well trained.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Libyan_Arab_Jamahiriya_period">Libyan Arab Jamahiriya period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libyan_Army_(1951%E2%80%932011)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Libyan Arab Jamahiriya period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From the late seventies to the mid to late eighties the army was involved in four major incursions into Chadian Territory. The Libyan Army suffered great losses in these conflicts especially that of the <a href="/wiki/Toyota_War" title="Toyota War">Toyota War</a> of 1987 largely due to poor tactics and western aid to Chad. All of these incursions were eventually repulsed and Libya no longer occupies <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>. This conflict was known as the <a href="/wiki/Chadian%E2%80%93Libyan_conflict" title="Chadian–Libyan conflict">Chadian–Libyan conflict</a>. In February 2011, the Libyan civil war broke out and several units of the army mutinied and defected to the opposition, with battles taking place across much of the country.</p>
<p>In September 2011, the pre-civil war Libyan army had been effectively destroyed by a combination of NATO air strikes and combat with rebel forces, with the Libyan army forces still loyal to Gaddafi abandoning their posts in Tripoli as the rebels took the city, and the remnants of Gaddafi's loyalist army holed up in <a href="/wiki/Sirte" title="Sirte">Sirte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sabha,_Libya" title="Sabha, Libya">Sabha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bani_Walid" title="Bani Walid">Bani Walid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gaddafi's army was <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sirte_(2011)" title="Battle of Sirte (2011)">defeated in their last major stronghold of Sirte</a>. Muammar Gaddafi, along with his son <a href="/wiki/Mutassim_Gaddafi" title="Mutassim Gaddafi">Mutassim</a> and former defense minister <a href="/wiki/Abu-Bakr_Yunis_Jabr" title="Abu-Bakr Yunis Jabr">Abu-Bakr Yunis Jabr</a> were killed and the remnants of the Libyan Arab Jamahiriya army were completely destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Strength">Strength</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libyan_Army_(1951%E2%80%932011)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Strength">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2009 the <a href="/wiki/International_Institute_for_Strategic_Studies" title="International Institute for Strategic Studies">International Institute for Strategic Studies</a> estimated that the Ground Forces of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libyan_Arab_Jamahiriya" title="Libyan Arab Jamahiriya">Libyan Arab Jamahiriya</a> numbered 25,000 with an additional, estimated, 25,000 conscripts (total estimated 50,000). The IISS estimated that the Ground Forces were organised into 11 Border Defence and 4 Security Zones, one regime security brigade (the 32nd <a href="/wiki/Khamis_Brigade" title="Khamis Brigade">Khamis Brigade</a>), 10 Tank Battalions, 10 Mechanised Infantry Battalions, 18 Infantry Battalions, 6 Commando Battalions, 22 Artillery Battalions, 4 SSM Brigade and 7 Air Defence Artillery Battalions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Doctrine is a mixture of Egyptian doctrine which was adopted after the 1969 coup and socialist principles derived from the concepts of a People’s Army.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equipment">Equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libyan_Army_(1951%E2%80%932011)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1980s and 90s the high technological level of its huge amount of equipment demanded a level of technical competence in operation and maintenance that the Libyan Army lacked. Maintenance and repair problems were exacerbated by the diversity of arms sources. The numerous foreign advisers and technicians were insufficient to overcome low standards of support and logistics.</p>
<p>Recent years saw the Army undermined by the embargo, which deprived it of new weapons and caused major problems with equipment maintenance. In the 1990s Western agencies intercepted numerous shipments of spare parts and dual-use material being smuggled to Libya. After many years of sanctions, all major areas of the land forces equipment needed improvement, especially the replacement of obsolete main battle tanks and artillery. Over half of Libya's armoured forces were thought to be in storage due to the chronic shortage of spare parts and obsolescence.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tanks">Tanks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libyan_Army_(1951%E2%80%932011)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tanks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IISS" title="IISS">IISS</a> estimated tank numbers in 2009 as 1,514 (not including those proven to have been captured/destroyed by rebels during the Libyan civil war as of 6 June 2011): 181 <a href="/wiki/T-72" title="T-72">T-72</a>; 115 in store; 89 <a href="/wiki/T-62" title="T-62">T-62</a>; 70 in store; 495 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-55" title="T-55">T-55</a>; 1,040 T-54/T-55 in store. The IISS estimated there were 50 <a href="/wiki/BRDM-2" title="BRDM-2">BRDM-2</a> and 70 <a href="/wiki/EE-9_Cascavel" title="EE-9 Cascavel">EE-9 Cascavel</a> <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_vehicle" title="Reconnaissance vehicle">reconnaissance vehicles</a>, 986 <a href="/wiki/BMP-1" title="BMP-1">BMP-1s</a>, plus <a href="/wiki/BMD-1" title="BMD-1">BMD-1s</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Russian official sources reported in 2010 that T-72s would be modernized with help from <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> 540 <a href="/wiki/BTR-50" title="BTR-50">BTR-50</a> and <a href="/wiki/BTR-60" title="BTR-60">BTR-60s</a> were also reported by the IISS. Other reported wheeled vehicles in service include 100 <a href="/wiki/EE-11_Urutu" title="EE-11 Urutu">EE-11 Urutu</a>, and Polish-Czechoslovak <a href="/wiki/OT-64_SKOT" title="OT-64 SKOT">OT-64 SKOT</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Combat_experience">Combat experience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libyan_Army_(1951%E2%80%932011)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Combat experience">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A sharp series of border clashes occurred with Egypt in 1977, and Libyan forces were flown into Uganda in 1978 in an unsuccessful effort to defend Idi Amin's Uganda against invading Tanzanian forces. In addition, the Libyans conducted a series of campaigns in Northern Chad since 1980, launching a campaign against Chad that year and again in 1983. In April 1987, Libya suffered a disastrous defeat in Chad, losing nearly a quarter of its invasion force.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Egypt">Egypt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libyan_Army_(1951%E2%80%932011)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Egypt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Libyan%E2%80%93Egyptian_War" title="Libyan–Egyptian War">Libyan–Egyptian War</a></div>
<p>On 19 July 1977, after a protest march by Libyans was stopped by Egyptian border guards, Libyan artillery units fired into Egypt. After further border violations were alleged by both sides, fighting escalated on the same day with an artillery duel, and, two days later, a drive along the coast by Egyptian armor and infantry during which the Libyan army was engaged. Egypt claimed successful surprise air strikes against the Libyan air base at Al Adem, just south of Tobruk, and surface-to-air missile batteries and radar stations were knocked out as well.</p>
<p>When the Egyptians withdrew on 24 July, most foreign analysts agreed that the Egyptian units had prevailed, although Libyan forces responded more effectively than had been expected. Libyan army hailed the encounter as a victory, using the fight as a justification for further purchases of modern armaments.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Uganda">Uganda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libyan_Army_(1951%E2%80%932011)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Uganda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Uganda%E2%80%93Tanzania_War" title="Uganda–Tanzania War">Uganda–Tanzania War</a></div>
<p>In the case of Uganda, Libya had intervened on Idi Amin's behalf during his first confrontation with neighboring Tanzania in 1972 by airlifting a contingent of 4000 troops. During the invasion of Uganda by Tanzanian troops and Ugandan exiles in 1978, a new Libyan force estimated at 2,000 to 2,500 was sent, assisting in the defence of Entebbe and Kampala by covering road junctions with armored equipment.</p>
<p>Unprepared and undermotivated Libyan troops were quickly routed in attacks by foot soldiers. As many as 600 Libyans were estimated to have been killed during the Ugandan operation, and the remainder were hurriedly withdrawn. The troops had been led to believe that they were being airlifted into Uganda for training exercises with Ugandan units.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chad">Chad</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libyan_Army_(1951%E2%80%932011)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Chad">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chadian%E2%80%93Libyan_conflict" title="Chadian–Libyan conflict">Chadian–Libyan conflict</a></div>
<p>After nearly two decades, Col. Muammar Gaddafi's attempts to annex Northern Chad ended in 1987. In just the first three months of 1987, Libya lost almost all the territory it had held in Chad, between $500 million and $1 billion in weapons and one-third of its 15,000 troops. Over 4,494 Libyan soldiers were killed by Chad's forces between January and March 1987.</p>
<p>The Libyan Army was defeated by a force substantially inferior in numbers and equipment. Chad's victory was the result of a combination of Western funding, weapons and intelligence and Chadian courage, tactics and leadership. France provided air cover and troops to protect the Chadian rear areas, while the USA provided $240 million in equipment and weapons. The U.S. also contributed $75 million in emergency military aid, including transport aircraft and air defence systems.</p>
<p>The Chad forces displayed some remarkable tactical innovations: they used Toyota all-terrain vehicles, lightly armored French-made Panhard cars, and Milan antitank and Stinger antiaircraft missiles to destroy Libyan tanks and planes.</p>

<p>In 2011 protests against the rule of Gaddafi started in Libya. They were inspired by <a href="/wiki/Arab_Spring" title="Arab Spring">similar protests in other Arab countries</a>. Gaddafi used police and mercenary forces to violently suppress the protest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This resulted in an armed uprising in Libya between pro-government and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libyan_People%27s_Army" title="Libyan People's Army">anti-government</a> forces. Parts of the army joined the rebels and weapon depots were plundered by protesters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> After initial advances by the rebels, the Libyan Army began a counteroffensive and started pushing back the rebel fighters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> On 17 March 2011, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">United Nations Security Council</a> passed <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_Resolution_1973" title="United Nations Security Council Resolution 1973">United Nations Security Council Resolution 1973</a>, authorizing the use of "all necessary means" to protect civilians in Libya, "excluding a foreign occupation force".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN-1973_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-UN-1973-20">[20]</a></sup> On Saturday, 19 March 2011, France began enforcement of the resolution by deploying French fighter aircraft over Libyan airspace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_News_Website_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News_Website-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
