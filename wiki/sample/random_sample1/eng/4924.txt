<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Reshoring</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Reshoring</b> (also known as <b>onshoring</b>, <b>inshoring</b> and <b>backshoring</b>) is the act of reintroducing domestic manufacturing to a country. It is the reverse process of <a href="/wiki/Offshoring" title="Offshoring">offshoring</a>, where manufacturing is moved to another country where labor is cheaper.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reshoring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For the last twenty years, American companies have been "<a href="/wiki/Offshoring" title="Offshoring">offshoring</a>" manufacturing to low cost countries such as China, Malaysia and Vietnam. Even as recently as 2008 many companies were offshoring or <a href="/wiki/Nearshoring" title="Nearshoring">nearshoring</a> their manufacturing. In 2010 companies started looking into ways to keep costs low and how to bring jobs back to America. America was deep into a recession and American citizens were pressuring companies to keep jobs in country. The voice of America says that bringing manufacturing and jobs back to America is the right thing to do.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bsc4_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bsc4-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_response">Government response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reshoring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Government response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In response to the recession, President Obama launched the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SelectUSA" title="SelectUSA">SelectUSA</a> program in 2011,the first federal program to promote and facilitate U.S. investment in partnership with our states. This program and website helps companies connect with resources available on a Federal, State and local level. In January 2012, President Obama Issued a Call to Action to Invest in America at the White House "Insourcing American Jobs" Forum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitehouse1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-whitehouse1-2">[2]</a></sup> At this forum, he met with representatives from companies such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otis_Elevator" title="Otis Elevator">Otis Elevator</a>, <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont</a>, NOVO1, <a href="/wiki/Master_Lock" title="Master Lock">Master Lock</a> and many more, all of which had recently brought jobs back or made significant investments in the United States. In 2013, during his State of the Union speech President Obama stated that he wanted America to be a magnet for new jobs and manufacturing. He asked that manufacturers like <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a> bring manufacturing back the U.S. He talked about the new Innovation Institutes that are working on innovation and technology such as 3D printing that will help companies with Lean Manufacturing. He talked about the innovation in renewable and clean energy and how our future is dependent on both. The cameras panned the crowd and focused on Tim Cook, CEO of Apple, and explained that Apple had made a commitment to bring some of its manufacturing back to Silicon Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitehouse2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-whitehouse2-3">[3]</a></sup> Apple did open a US assembly operation to make the latest 21.5" iMac for the US market and in May 2013 shipped its first products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-arstech1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Success_stories">Success stories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reshoring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Success stories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Companies such as ET Water Systems, GE Appliances and Caterpillar found that with the increase of labor costs in Japan and China, the cost of shipping and custom fees, it cost only about 10% more to manufacture in America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-econ1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-econ1-5">[5]</a></sup> Advances in technology such as Digital Manufacturing and Automation have made bringing manufacturing back to the United States both cost effective and possible. With this advanced technology companies are creating new innovative products for the U.S. market.</p>
<p>Not all manufacturing should return to the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bsc1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-bsc1-6">[6]</a></sup> The rise of the middle class in China, India and other countries has created markets for the products made in those countries. As the U.S. has a "Made in U.S.A." program other countries support products made in their countries as well. Localization, the process of manufacturing products for the local market, is an approach to keeping some manufacturing offshore and bringing some of it back. Besides the cost savings of manufacturing closer to the market, the lead time for adapting to changes in the market is faster.</p>
<p>There have been several very successful stories of companies that have reshored including most of the companies mentioned above. In most cases hundreds if not thousands of jobs were created or reinstated. In the case of <a href="/wiki/Starbucks" title="Starbucks">Starbucks</a>, in 2012 it saved American Mug and Stein Company in East Liverpool, Ohio from bankruptcy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strb1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-strb1-7">[7]</a></sup> Initially only twelve people were put back to work but it was just the beginning for Starbucks. CEO Howard Schultz was compelled to take action; he teamed up with Opportunity Finance Network to found Create Jobs for USA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He seeded the foundation with an initial grant of $5 million. Later that year Starbucks announced that they are building another manufacturing facility in Georgia that will employ 140 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strb2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-strb2-9">[9]</a></sup> Although they originally only hired twelve people Starbucks got months of positive press.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Avoiding_failure">Avoiding failure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reshoring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Avoiding failure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There have been some cases of reshoring that weren't successful. Otis Elevators’ reshoring effort did not go well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bsc2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-bsc2-10">[10]</a></sup> Otis says it failed to consider the consequences of the new location and tried to do too much at once, including a supply-chain software implementation. This is not an uncommon reshoring scenario. Bringing manufacturing back to the United States isn’t so simple, and there are a lot of considerations and analyses that companies must do to determine the costs and feasibility of reshoring. Some companies pursue reshoring with their own internal staff. But reshoring projects are complicated and involve engineering, marketing, production, finance, and procurement. In addition, there are real estate concerns, government incentives and training requirements that require outreach to the community. To help with these projects, companies often turn to consultants that specialize in Reshoring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bsc3_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-bsc3-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reshoring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, companies have used the reintroduction of domestic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Call_centres" title="Call centres">call centres</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unique_selling_point" title="Unique selling point">unique selling point</a>. In 2014, the <a href="/wiki/RSA_Insurance_Group" title="RSA Insurance Group">RSA Insurance Group</a> completed a move of call centres back to Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The call centre industry in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> has been hit by reshoring, as businesses including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Telecom" title="British Telecom">British Telecom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santander_UK" title="Santander UK">Santander UK</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aviva" title="Aviva">Aviva</a> all announced they would move operations back to Britain in order to boost the economy and regain customer satisfaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
