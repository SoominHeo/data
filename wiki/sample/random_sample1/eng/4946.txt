<p><i><b>Cueva de las Manos</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> for <b>Cave of Hands</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">cave</a> or a series of caves located in the <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Argentina" title="Provinces of Argentina">province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_Province,_Argentina" title="Santa Cruz Province, Argentina">Santa Cruz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, 163 km (101 mi) south of the town of <a href="/wiki/Perito_Moreno,_Santa_Cruz" title="Perito Moreno, Santa Cruz">Perito Moreno</a>. It is famous for (and gets its name from) the <a href="/wiki/Cave_painting" title="Cave painting">paintings</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hand" title="Hand">hands</a>. The art in the cave dates from 13,000 to 9,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unesco_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-unesco-1">[1]</a></sup> Several waves of people occupied the cave, and early artwork has been <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">carbon-dated</a> to ca. 9300 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Before_present" title="Before present">BP</a> (about 7300 BC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unesco_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-unesco-1">[1]</a></sup> The age of the paintings was calculated from the remains of bone-made pipes used for spraying the paint on the wall of the cave to create <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silhouettes" title="Silhouettes">silhouettes</a> of hands.</p>
<p>The site was last inhabited around 700 AD, possibly by ancestors of the <a href="/wiki/Tehuelche_people" title="Tehuelche people">Tehuelche people</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unesco_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-unesco-1">[1]</a></sup> It was entered on the UNESCO World Heritage List in 1999.</p>

<p>The cave lies in the valley of the <a href="/wiki/Pinturas_River" title="Pinturas River">Pinturas River</a>, in an isolated spot in the <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonian</a> landscape. It is most easily reached by a gravel road (RP 41), which leaves <a href="/wiki/National_Route_40_(Argentina)" title="National Route 40 (Argentina)">Ruta 40</a> 3 km north of <a href="/wiki/Bajo_Caracoles" title="Bajo Caracoles">Bajo Caracoles</a> and runs 46 km northeast to the south side of the <a href="/wiki/Pinturas_River_Canyon" title="Pinturas River Canyon">Pinturas Canyon</a>. The north side of the canyon can also be reached by rough, but shorter, roads from Ruta 40. A 3 km path connects the two sides of the canyon, but there is no road link.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main cave measures 24 m (79 ft) in depth, with an entrance 15 m (49 ft) wide, and it is initially 10 m (33 ft) high. The ground inside the cave has an upward slope; inside the cave the height is reduced to no more than 2 m (7 ft).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artwork">Artwork</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cueva_de_las_Manos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Artwork">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The images of <a href="/wiki/Hand" title="Hand">hands</a> are negative painted, that is, <a href="/wiki/Stencil" title="Stencil">stencilled</a>. Most of the hands are left hands, which suggests that painters held the spraying pipe with their right hand or they put the back of their right hand to the wall and held the spraying pipe with their left hand.</p>
<p>Besides these there are also depictions of human beings, <a href="/wiki/Guanaco" title="Guanaco">guanacos</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Guanaco" title="Guanaco">Lama guanicoe</a></i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unesco_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-unesco-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rhea_(bird)" title="Rhea (bird)">rheas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Felidae" title="Felidae">felines</a> and other animals, as well as <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometric</a> shapes, <a href="/wiki/Zigzag" title="Zigzag">zigzag</a> patterns, representations of the sun, and <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a> scenes. The hunting scenes are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturalism_(arts)" title="Naturalism (arts)">naturalistic</a> portrayals of a variety of hunting techniques, including the use of <a href="/wiki/Bolas" title="Bolas">bolas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unesco_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-unesco-1">[1]</a></sup> Similar paintings, though in smaller numbers, can be found in nearby caves. There are also red dots on the ceilings, probably made by submerging their hunting bolas in ink, and then throwing them up.</p>
<p>The binder is unknown but the mineral pigments include iron oxides, producing reds and purples; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaolin" title="Kaolin">kaolin</a>, producing white; <a href="/wiki/Jarosite" title="Jarosite">natrojarosite</a>, producing yellow; and <a href="/wiki/Manganese_oxide" title="Manganese oxide">manganese oxide</a>, which makes black.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unesco_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-unesco-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
