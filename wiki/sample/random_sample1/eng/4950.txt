<p>The <b>Rioni</b> or <b>Rion River</b> (<a href="/wiki/Georgian_language" title="Georgian language">Georgian</a>: <span lang="ka" xml:lang="ka">რიონი</span> <i>Rioni</i>, <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%A6%E1%BE%B6%CF%83%CE%B9%CF%82" title="wikt:Φᾶσις">Φᾶσις</a></span> <i>Phasis</i>) is the main river of western <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>. It originates in the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus_Mountains" title="Caucasus Mountains">Caucasus Mountains</a>, in the region of <a href="/wiki/Racha" title="Racha">Racha</a> and flows west to the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, entering it north of the city of <a href="/wiki/Poti" title="Poti">Poti</a> (near ancient <a href="/wiki/Phasis_(town)" title="Phasis (town)">Phasis</a>). The city of <a href="/wiki/Kutaisi" title="Kutaisi">Kutaisi</a>, once the ancient city of <a href="/wiki/Colchis" title="Colchis">Colchis</a>, lies on its banks. It drains the western Transcaucasus into the Black Sea while its sister, the <a href="/wiki/Kura_River" title="Kura River">Kura River</a>, drains the eastern Transcaucasus into the Caspian Sea.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rioni_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soulier,_E.;_Andriveau-Goujon,_J._Anciens_Empires_Jusqua_Alexandre._1838_(BJ).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="324" data-file-width="625" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Soulier%2C_E.%3B_Andriveau-Goujon%2C_J._Anciens_Empires_Jusqua_Alexandre._1838_%28BJ%29.jpg/250px-Soulier%2C_E.%3B_Andriveau-Goujon%2C_J._Anciens_Empires_Jusqua_Alexandre._1838_%28BJ%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Soulier%2C_E.%3B_Andriveau-Goujon%2C_J._Anciens_Empires_Jusqua_Alexandre._1838_%28BJ%29.jpg/375px-Soulier%2C_E.%3B_Andriveau-Goujon%2C_J._Anciens_Empires_Jusqua_Alexandre._1838_%28BJ%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Soulier%2C_E.%3B_Andriveau-Goujon%2C_J._Anciens_Empires_Jusqua_Alexandre._1838_%28BJ%29.jpg/500px-Soulier%2C_E.%3B_Andriveau-Goujon%2C_J._Anciens_Empires_Jusqua_Alexandre._1838_%28BJ%29.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soulier,_E.;_Andriveau-Goujon,_J._Anciens_Empires_Jusqua_Alexandre._1838_(BJ).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> considered the Rioni river as a boundary between Europe and Asia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pp324_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-pp324-1">[1]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Known to the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greeks</a> as the <b>Phasis River</b>, Rioni was first mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a> in his <i>Theogony</i> (l.340); Plato has Socrates remark: "I believe that the earth is very large and that we who dwell between the <a href="/wiki/Pillars_of_Hercules" title="Pillars of Hercules">pillars of Hercules</a> and the river Phasis live in a small part of it about the sea, like ants or frogs about a pond" (<i><a href="/wiki/Phaedo" title="Phaedo">Phaedo</a></i>, 109a); later writers like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollonius_Rhodius" title="Apollonius Rhodius">Apollonius Rhodius</a> (<i>Argonautica</i> 2.12.61), <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a> (<i>Georgics</i> 4.367) and <a href="/wiki/Aelius_Aristides" title="Aelius Aristides">Aelius Aristides</a> (<i>Ad Romam</i> 82) considered it the easternmost limit of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navigable" title="Navigable">navigable</a> seas. Socrates, in <i>Phaedo</i> 109a referred to the portion of the world he knew of as between the <a href="/wiki/Pillars_of_Hercules" title="Pillars of Hercules">Pillars of Hercules</a> and the River Phasis, while <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> considered Rioni as a boundary between Europe and Asia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pp324_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-pp324-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term "<a href="/wiki/Pheasant" title="Pheasant">pheasant</a>" and the scientific name <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phasianus_colchicus" title="Phasianus colchicus">Phasianus colchicus</a></i> are derived from "Phasis" and "Colchis", as this was said to be the region from which the common pheasant was introduced to Europe<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> in ancient times (the ring-necked pheasants seen in the present day were later introduced from <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>; see <a href="/wiki/Common_pheasant" title="Common pheasant">Common pheasant</a> for details). It is said that "the failure of Kolkhis to emerge as a strong kingdom or to be maintained as a province of Rome has been blamed on the pestilential climate of the Phasis Valley, a situation remarked upon by travelers down to modern times, when the swamps were finally drained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rioni_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Rioni is the longest river wholly within the borders of Georgia. The river is 327 kilometres (203 mi) long, and its <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basin</a> covers about 13,400 square kilometres (5,200 sq mi). It starts on the southern slopes of the Caucasus Mountains at 2,960 metres (9,710 ft) above sea level.</p>
