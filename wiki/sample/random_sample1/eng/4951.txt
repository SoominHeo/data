<p><b>Li Wangyang</b> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">李旺阳</span>, 12 November 1950 – 6 June 2012) was a Chinese dissident <a href="/wiki/Labor_rights" title="Labor rights">labor rights</a> activist, member of the <a href="/wiki/Workers_Autonomous_Federation" title="Workers Autonomous Federation">Workers Autonomous Federation</a> and chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Shaoyang" title="Shaoyang">Shaoyang</a> WAF branch. Following his role in the <a href="/wiki/Tiananmen_Square_protests_of_1989" title="Tiananmen Square protests of 1989">Tiananmen Square protests of 1989</a>, he served twenty-one years in prison on charges of counterrevolutionary propaganda, incitement, and subversion. Of all Chinese pro-democracy activists from 1989, Li has spent the longest time in prison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> On 6 June 2012, one year after his release from prison, and a few days after a television interview in which he continued to call for vindication of the Tiananmen Square protests, Li was found hanged in a hospital room. Shaoyang city authorities initially claimed suicide was the cause of death, but it was revised to 'accidental death' after the autopsy.</p>
<p>Following a protest march attended by up to 25,000 people, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pan-democracy_camp" title="Pan-democracy camp">Pan Democrats</a> and senior establishment figures in Hong Kong publicly commented on the suspicious nature of the death, and said they had escalated the demands of citizens to politicians or "relevant departments" at national level for an independent investigation. The uproar in Hong Kong is said by commentators have put pressure on mainland authorities to order a criminal investigation so as not to overshadow the impending visit of president <a href="/wiki/Hu_Jintao" title="Hu Jintao">Hu Jintao</a> for the 15th anniversary celebrations of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handover_of_Hong_Kong" title="Handover of Hong Kong">handover of Hong Kong</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protests_and_imprisonment">Protests and imprisonment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_of_Li_Wangyang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Protests and imprisonment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A worker in a glass factory, Li Wangyang set up a labour union under inspiration of the <a href="/wiki/Democracy_Wall" title="Democracy Wall">Democracy Wall</a> in 1983. In 1989, China saw mass pro-democracy protests throughout the country, with the largest protests in <a href="/wiki/Tiananmen_Square" title="Tiananmen Square">Tiananmen Square</a>, Beijing. After hundreds died when the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army" title="People's Liberation Army">People's Liberation Army</a> suppressed the protests on 4 June, Li pasted a poster on a Shaoyang traffic sign urging a general strike in support of the protests. Two days later, Li organized a memorial for the victims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20120606LAT_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-20120606LAT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a result of his actions, Li was jailed on 9 June for "counterrevolutionary propaganda and incitement".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He was sentenced to ten years of imprisonment, but his sentence was increased to thirteen years when he appealed to the court. He was also sentenced to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_labour" title="Hard labour">hard labour</a>. As his health deteriorated, he was granted medical parole in June 1996, but the authorities cancelled his release after <a href="/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping" title="Deng Xiaoping">Deng Xiaoping</a> died in 1997, fearing calls for the other responsible officials to be held accounted for the 4 June Massacre. In June 2000 Li was released again for reasons of poor health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G76_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-G76-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_in_China_(organization)" title="Human Rights in China (organization)">Human Rights in China</a> (HRIC) alleged that Li was tortured while in custody, resulting in his losing both his sight and hearing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reut76_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reut76-5">[5]</a></sup> Li himself said in his last interview that the only explanation for his becoming blind was through nerve damage caused by being repeatedly beaten about the head.</p>
<p>During his spells in prison, Li was a frequent inmate in solitary confinement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt20120608jacobs_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt20120608jacobs-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2001, Li began a <a href="/wiki/Hunger_strike" title="Hunger strike">hunger strike</a> as part of a petition to authorities to repay his health care costs, and was given an additional ten-year sentence for "inciting subversion".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AI_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-AI-7">[7]</a></sup> Whilst attempting to force feed Li during his hunger strike, guards knocked out Li's front teeth. He finally gained freedom in May 2011, when he checked into Daxiang District Hospital to treat his medical conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt20120608jacobs_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt20120608jacobs-6">[6]</a></sup> His sister, who lived 7 km away from the hospital, visited him twice a day with his meals<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E5.8A.9B.E6.8A.97.E4.B8.AD.E5.85.B1.E3.80.80.E6.97.BA.E9.99.BD.E5.A6.B9.E6.B1.BA.E4.B8.8D.E7.81.AB.E5.8C.96.E5.93.A5.E5.93.A5_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E5.8A.9B.E6.8A.97.E4.B8.AD.E5.85.B1.E3.80.80.E6.97.BA.E9.99.BD.E5.A6.B9.E6.B1.BA.E4.B8.8D.E7.81.AB.E5.8C.96.E5.93.A5.E5.93.A5-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Li remained vocal in demanding vindication of the Tiananmen protesters up until his death: in an interview with <a href="/wiki/I-CABLE_News_Channel_(Hong_Kong)" title="I-CABLE News Channel (Hong Kong)">i-CABLE</a> broadcast four days before, he said "the souls of the martyrs deserve to finally find some peace".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reut76_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reut76-5">[5]</a></sup> He advocated a multi-party democracy for his country, and said he did not regret his fight for a better China, "even had [he] been beheaded"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guard20120608tania_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-guard20120608tania-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In another interview, broadcast on French radio the eve of the 23rd anniversary of the Tiananmen Square crackdown, he appealed to his compatriots "to observe 6-4".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20120606LAT_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-20120606LAT-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a> designated him a <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_conscience" title="Prisoner of conscience">prisoner of conscience</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AI_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-AI-7">[7]</a></sup> <i>Apple Daily</i> reported that he had no minders before the interviews. After his interviews were broadcast, Li was kept under around the clock surveillance by a team of 10 police officers. An account that was opened to receive donations from supporters from China and abroad to pay his medical fees was inexplicably blocked; he was also severely impeded from going to Beijing to see a specialist physician.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apple20120607tenk_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-apple20120607tenk-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_of_Li_Wangyang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Li was found dead on 6 June 2012, one year after his latest release from prison, in the Daxiang Hospital where he was being treated for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_disease" title="Heart disease">heart disease</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC76_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC76-12">[12]</a></sup> Li was reportedly found on his feet with a strip of cloth tied around his neck and connected to a window bar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G76_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-G76-4">[4]</a></sup> Li's next of kin, his sister Li Wangling and her husband Zhao Baozhu, rushed to the hospital shortly after they were telephoned at 6 am.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20120606LAT_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-20120606LAT-2">[2]</a></sup> His brother-in-law said that the body was "on its feet next to his bed, with a white strip of cloth tied tightly around its neck and connected to a window bar above".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G76_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-G76-4">[4]</a></sup> The authorities said to relatives that there would be an autopsy. Although the latter were denied permission to take photographs, some stills and one video made it onto the internet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20120606LAT_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-20120606LAT-2">[2]</a></sup> Later, i-Cable television in Hong Kong reported that officials had been attempting to persuade Li's family members to cremate his body immediately.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestic_reaction">Domestic reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_of_Li_Wangyang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Domestic reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Official statements that Li had committed suicide caused "outrage" among China's dissident community,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AI_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-AI-7">[7]</a></sup> as his family questioned how Li would have been capable of killing himself. They said that leaving aside his minders, Li was blind and nearly deaf and could "barely hold a bowl without his hands shaking".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20120606LAT_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-20120606LAT-2">[2]</a></sup> Friends and local activists strongly allege that Li was "suicided" (被自殺), and formed a 'Committee in quest of the truth about Li's death'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-udn_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-udn-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boxun_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-boxun-15">[15]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> commented that Chinese activists on Twitter had begun to tweet their own declarations that they had no plans to commit suicide (#我不自杀); dissident <a href="/wiki/Hu_Jia_(activist)" title="Hu Jia (activist)">Hu Jia (activist)</a> recommended that fellow dissidents who are frequently arrested and political prisoners prepare a notarised statement declaring one's lack of intention to commit suicide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> To avoid internet censorship, "suicided" became a web-search term of choice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt20120608jacobs_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-nyt20120608jacobs-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>HRIC said Li's sister had been detained by police and was taken to a hotel where they were being held.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guard20120608tania_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-guard20120608tania-9">[9]</a></sup> Media reported that Li's family was under pressure to consent to an autopsy, and that police had given an ultimatum of noon on 8 June. Family members were denied access to the body since police removed it from the hospital. The Information Centre for Human Rights and Democracy conveyed that Li's next of kin were prepared to grant an autopsy subject to the presence of a lawyer from outside Shaoyang.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp20120608ngtzewei_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-scmp20120608ngtzewei-17">[17]</a></sup> On 8 June, an autopsy of Li's body was carried out by authorities in Shaoyang, apparently against the wishes of his family, and the body was cremated on 9 June.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> According to the Information Centre for Human Rights and Democracy, an unnamed member of staff at a funeral parlour implied that the government ordered the cremation; another worker told Cable TV that Li's sister and brother-in-law had given signed consent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp20120610peterso_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-scmp20120610peterso-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Shaoyang city authorities, people on Li's wards saw him "acting strangely" by the window at around 3 am on the day he died. "Surveillance camera footage showed that on the night Li Wangyang died, no suspicious persons entered the ward except Li's wardmates and hospital staff ... The ward's door was not forced open", and there were no other bruises on his body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hks20120611cadres_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-hks20120611cadres-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shaoyang city authorities said the autopsy was conducted by four pathologists from the <a href="/wiki/Sun_Yat-sen_University" title="Sun Yat-sen University">Sun Yat-sen University</a> on 8 June. It is claimed that the entire process was videotaped and in the presence of the media, members of the National People's Congress and the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference. The university is expected to report the autopsy within four days. The city continued to assert that Li committed suicide, that his body was cremated at his family's request, and that his ashes were buried under the supervision of his sister and brother-in-law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hks20120611cadres_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-hks20120611cadres-20">[20]</a></sup> The media made a connection to the <a href="/wiki/Wukan_protests" title="Wukan protests">Wukan protests</a>, pointing out the medical examiner assigned to perform Li's autopsy was the same person who pronounced that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protests_of_Wukan#Death_of_Xue_Jinbo" title="Protests of Wukan">Xue Jingbo had died of "cardiac failure"</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_response">International response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_of_Li_Wangyang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: International response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By the day following news of Li's demise, over 2,700 people, including dissident <a href="/wiki/Ai_Weiwei" title="Ai Weiwei">Ai Weiwei</a>, had signed an online petition calling for an independent investigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reut76_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Reut76-5">[5]</a></sup> Human rights activist and lawyer <a href="/wiki/Teng_Biao" title="Teng Biao">Teng Biao</a> posted a picture of a calligraphy scrolls which read "An inch of blood for an inch of freedom, thousands of miles of rivers bring thousands of miles of woes" to his Twitter account, dedicating the post to Li.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G76_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-G76-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Information_Centre_for_Human_Rights_and_Democracy" title="Information Centre for Human Rights and Democracy">Information Centre for Human Rights and Democracy</a> raised the possibility that "security guards monitoring him tortured him to death and faked a suicide".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC76_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC76-12">[12]</a></sup> Amnesty International issued a statement urging the Chinese government to "thoroughly investigate the circumstances surrounding Li Wangyang's death and take seriously the claims made by his family and friends that this was not suicide".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AI_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-AI-7">[7]</a></sup> At the request of the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Confederation_of_Trade_Unions" title="Hong Kong Confederation of Trade Unions">Hong Kong Confederation of Trade Unions</a> (HKCTU), <a href="/wiki/LabourStart" title="LabourStart">LabourStart</a> launched a global online campaign demanding that the Chinese government open an investigation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Response_in_Hong_Kong">Response in Hong Kong</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_of_Li_Wangyang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Response in Hong Kong">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The death and its subsequent handling angered and sent shock waves through Hong Kong. The <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Alliance_in_Support_of_Patriotic_Democratic_Movements_in_China" title="Hong Kong Alliance in Support of Patriotic Democratic Movements in China">Hong Kong Alliance in Support of Patriotic Democratic Movements in China</a> rebuked claims that Li died of suicide. Its convener, <a href="/wiki/Lee_Cheuk-yan" title="Lee Cheuk-yan">Lee Cheuk-yan</a> argued that Li never regretted support for democracy in the course of his imprisonment, and that his poor health condition did not allow him to hang himself under surveillance of National Security Guards. Kinseng Lam, who gave Li the last interview, called it an assassination and believed it was his interview that triggered the authorities to kill him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Alan Leong, Civic Party legislator in Hong Kong, suggested Li 'paid the price' for his interview with iCable TV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp20120608ngtzewei_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-scmp20120608ngtzewei-17">[17]</a></sup> Several representatives to the National People's Congress (NPC), including Liberal Party chairman <a href="/wiki/Miriam_Lau" title="Miriam Lau">Miriam Lau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maria_Tam" title="Maria Tam">Maria Tam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Tien" title="Michael Tien">Michael Tien</a>, called on the central government to look into the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp20120608ngtzewei_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-scmp20120608ngtzewei-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp20120610peterso_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-scmp20120610peterso-19">[19]</a></sup> Several staunchly pro-establishment legislators argued against escalating the issue to Beijing. <a href="/wiki/Wong_Kwok-kin" title="Wong Kwok-kin">Wong Kwok-kin</a> argued that such incidents were "daily occurrences" and that it would be impractical to have them all investigated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E7.94.B0.E5.8C.97.E8.BE.B0.E5.B0.87.E5.8E.BB.E4.BF.A1.E4.BA.BA.E5.A4.A7.E5.B8.B8.E5.A7.94.E3.80.80.E4.BF.83.E6.9F.A5.E6.9D.8E.E6.97.BA.E9.99.BD.E6.AD.BB.E5.9B.A0_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E7.94.B0.E5.8C.97.E8.BE.B0.E5.B0.87.E5.8E.BB.E4.BF.A1.E4.BA.BA.E5.A4.A7.E5.B8.B8.E5.A7.94.E3.80.80.E4.BF.83.E6.9F.A5.E6.9D.8E.E6.97.BA.E9.99.BD.E6.AD.BB.E5.9B.A0-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20120608apple_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-20120608apple-24">[24]</a></sup> Notably, <a href="/wiki/Ip_Kwok-him" title="Ip Kwok-him">Ip Kwok-him</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Alliance_for_the_Betterment_and_Progress_of_Hong_Kong" title="Democratic Alliance for the Betterment and Progress of Hong Kong">DAB</a> initially said it was "unnecessary" to write to air his concerns,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hks20120611phila_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-hks20120611phila-25">[25]</a></sup> because Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference (CPPCC) members should not tell the authorities how to conduct their affairs. Ip later made a U-turn owing to public pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HKEJ20120611_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-HKEJ20120611-26">[26]</a></sup> At <i>City Forum,</i> CPPCC member <a href="/wiki/Lew_Mon-hung" title="Lew Mon-hung">Lew Mon-hung</a> said he would immediately write to the CPPCC regarding the death under bizarre circumstances. He opined that for a man to be killed under guise of protecting social stability would be complete denial of human rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lew_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lew-27">[27]</a></sup> Nevertheless, he defended the Central government, saying it was still uncertain whether Li's death was a suicide or a homicide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hks20120611phila_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-hks20120611phila-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to organisers of the march on 10 June, in Hong Kong to demand a thorough investigation into Li's death, 25,000 people attended the largest-scale protest ever at the main entrance of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liaison_Office_of_the_Central_People%27s_Government_in_the_Hong_Kong_Special_Administrative_Region" title="Liaison Office of the Central People's Government in the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region">Central Government Liaison Office</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Police estimated the turnout at 5,400.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hks20120611phila_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-hks20120611phila-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp20120611Lau_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-scmp20120611Lau-30">[30]</a></sup> On 13 June 1500 people attended a candlelight vigil held outside the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Supreme_Court_Building,_Hong_Kong" title="Old Supreme Court Building, Hong Kong">Legislative Council Building</a> commemorating the seventh day of Li's death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hks20120614phila_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-hks20120614phila-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Editorials in the main newspapers all referred to Li's death as "suspicious", and said ongoing publicity about the case is unfavourable to China's image, and that it was in China's interests for the death to be probed transparently by central authorities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> An opinion piece in the <i>Hong Kong Economic Journal</i> said Li's death stoked the anger of the Hong Kong people and brought up to 25,000 people onto the streets. "It not only reopened the wounds of the Tiananmen protests; the ridiculous claims that he committed suicide was a travesty of the rule of law". The writer lamented that despite China's rise, she still languished third from last in world rankings in terms of respect for human rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HKEJ20120611_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-HKEJ20120611-26">[26]</a></sup> In a piece translated and published in the <i>South China Morning Post</i>, Chang Ping wrote that Li's death "exposed the relationship between the June 4 crackdown and the Chinese government's policy to 'maintain stability' ... [which] becomes the priority that overrides all. June 4 became the government's model for dealing with any dissent, using extreme force."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wang Xiangwei, the newly appointed editor-in-Chief of the <i><a href="/wiki/South_China_Morning_Post" title="South China Morning Post">South China Morning Post</a></i> was criticised for his decision to reduce paper's coverage of Li' death on 7 June. Wang reportedly reversed the decision to run a full story, and instead published a two-paragraph report inside the paper; other news media reported it prominently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> A senior staff member who sought to understand the decision received a stern rebuff by return email from Wang.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Self-censorship concerns were raised in the Chinese-language press of the territory because Wang is mainland born, and is a member of the Jilin Provincial CPPCC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hks20120620_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-hks20120620-37">[37]</a></sup> Other than on the first day, Li's death and aftermath was covered in the <i>Post</i> as major news stories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hks20120620_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-hks20120620-37">[37]</a></sup> On 21 June, responding to news of alleged downplaying of Li's case, Wang said he understood the "huge responsibility to deliver news... [and]... the journalistic heritage we have inherited". He said that his decision not to pursue extensive coverage as the story broke was pending "more facts and details surrounding the circumstances of this case".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp20120621Xiangwei_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-scmp20120621Xiangwei-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Outgoing Health Secretary <a href="/wiki/York_Chow" title="York Chow">York Chow</a> was the first minister to express scepticism of the 'suicide' claim; then others followed suit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hks20120613phila_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-hks20120613phila-39">[39]</a></sup> Former Legco president and National People's Congress Standing Committee member <a href="/wiki/Rita_Fan" title="Rita Fan">Rita Fan</a> noted the doubts of the public in Hong Kong over the death of Li Wangyang. She said that "If these doubts can be resolved, and if some people are held responsible, Hong Kong people will have a better impression of the country."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hks20120614phila_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-hks20120614phila-31">[31]</a></sup> She added that "Matters like these happen because the officials do not value the lives of the people, do not respect the rights of the people … do not have an in-depth understanding of accountability to the people."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp20120615stuart_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-scmp20120615stuart-40">[40]</a></sup> Chief Executive-elect, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CY_Leung" title="CY Leung">CY Leung</a> maintained his refusal to directly comment on Li's death, which was universally hailed as "suspicious". Many local pro-establishment politicians said they had escalated the matter to various Communist Party officials or bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hks20120615phila_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-hks20120615phila-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nine days after the death, outgoing Chief Executive Donald Tsang expressed doubts about Li's case, said he understood the views of Hong Kong people. He said: "Under the principle of 'one country, two systems', the most important responsibility for a chief executive is to safeguard the freedoms of speech and expression of Hong Kong people"; Chief executive-elect CY Leung said he shared the feelings of fellow citizens about the issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp20120615stuart_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-scmp20120615stuart-40">[40]</a></sup> On 16 June, Chief Executive-elect, C.Y. Leung, observed a minute's silence in honour of Li at a function, but maintained that it would be "inappropriate" for him to comment on the death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Macao">Macao</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_of_Li_Wangyang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Macao">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pro-democracy Macanese legislators <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chan_Wai_Chi" title="Chan Wai Chi">Chan Wai Chi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Au_Kam_San" title="Au Kam San">Au Kam San</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ng_Kuok_Cheong" title="Ng Kuok Cheong">Ng Kuok Cheong</a> wrote to PRC Premier Wen Jiabao and other deputies to the Chinese People’s National Congress demanding an investigation of Li's mysterious "suicide". They protested the Shaoyang public security office ignoring escalating calls for a transparent investigation Li's death, and accused them of "destroying the evidence" by cremating Li’s body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hunan_investigation">Hunan investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_of_Li_Wangyang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hunan investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the public outcry, Li Gang, deputy director of the Hong Kong–central government liaison office in said that Hunan Public Security Bureau ordered a criminal investigation into the death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hks20120615phila_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-hks20120615phila-41">[41]</a></sup> Li said: "We have noted the concern expressed by the Hong Kong community and media on [Li Wangyang's death]. We have reflected their concerns to the relevant departments in the central government."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp20120615stuart_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-scmp20120615stuart-40">[40]</a></sup> The <i>South China Morning Post</i> noted that announcement about the forensic and criminal investigation by experts from outside Hunan province came through the semi-official Hong Kong China News Agency rather than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xinhua" title="Xinhua">Xinhua</a>, saying it indicated that the announcement was targeted at Hong Kong, designed to calm public anger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp20120616staff_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-scmp20120616staff-44">[44]</a></sup> Pundits say the uproar in Hong Kong has put pressure on mainland authorities to order a criminal investigation so as not to threaten the legacy of outgoing president <a href="/wiki/Hu_Jintao" title="Hu Jintao">Hu Jintao</a> and overshadow his impending visit for the 15th anniversary celebrations of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handover_of_Hong_Kong" title="Handover of Hong Kong">handover of Hong Kong</a>. Political commentator Johnny Lau said that if Hu had not been planning on coming to Hong Kong, "the case could drag on or may be forgotten".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp20120616staff_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-scmp20120616staff-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
