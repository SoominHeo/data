<p><b>Ri Je-gang</b> (1930 – 2 June 2010) was a <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korean</a> politician.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ri_Je-gang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ri studied at <a href="/wiki/Kim_Il-sung_University" title="Kim Il-sung University">Kim Il-sung University</a>. He was elevated to the <a href="/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Workers%27_Party_of_Korea" title="Central Committee of the Workers' Party of Korea">Central Committee</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_Party_of_Korea" title="Workers' Party of Korea">Workers' Party of Korea</a> (WPK) in 1973 as head of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organization_and_Guidance_Department_of_the_Workers%27_Party_of_Korea" title="Organization and Guidance Department of the Workers' Party of Korea">Organization and Guidance Department</a> (OGD); <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-il" title="Kim Jong-il">Kim Jong-il</a>'s takeover of the bureau later that year set the stage for his friendship with Ri. He became a deputy chief of the same bureau as well as Kim's personal secretary in 1982; he was elevated to first deputy chief in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He had a long rivalry with <a href="/wiki/Jang_Sung-taek" title="Jang Sung-taek">Jang Sung-taek</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ri reportedly directed many of the purges of senior government officials which took place during Kim Jong-il's reign. Ri reportedly imposed <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">capital punishment</a> on many of those purged, in spite of recommendations from his subordinates for lesser punishments such as reeducation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ri_Je-gang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ri died due to injuries sustained in a car crash; there were different theories about the cause, including speculation that it may have been foul play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-2">[2]</a></sup> In particular, <a href="/wiki/Andrei_Lankov" title="Andrei Lankov">Andrei Lankov</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kookmin_University" title="Kookmin University">Kookmin University</a> called the death suspicious, pointing to the lack of traffic in North Korea, and described the death as "part of a long tradition" of politicians being killed in mysterious traffic incidents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In contrast, North Korea analyst Lee Sang-hyun of the <a href="/wiki/Sejong_Institute" title="Sejong Institute">Sejong Institute</a> suspected a more innocent explanation, that Ri may have been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Driving_under_the_influence_of_alcohol" title="Driving under the influence of alcohol">driving under the influence of alcohol</a> after returning from a party held by Kim Jong-il, and found himself unable to control his vehicle on poorly lit and poorly maintained roads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
