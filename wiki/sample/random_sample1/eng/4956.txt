<p><b>Yorkshire Carnegie</b> (formerly <b>Leeds Carnegie</b>, <b>Leeds Tykes</b> and <b>Leeds RUFC</b>) are an English <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a> club in <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England, who play in the <a href="/wiki/RFU_Championship" title="RFU Championship">RFU Championship</a>.</p>
<p>The club was founded in 1991 as Leeds RUFC. In 1998, the club merged with <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Rhinos" title="Leeds Rhinos">Leeds Rhinos</a> to form Leeds Rugby Limited, also known as Leeds Tykes. In 2007, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leeds_Metropolitan_University" title="Leeds Metropolitan University">Leeds Metropolitan University</a> bought a 51% stake in the club and changed the name to fit with the university's sport department, Carnegie College.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> At the end of the 2008–09 season, ownership of the club passed back into the hands of Leeds Rugby.</p>
<p>Leeds have bounced between the Premiership and the second-level National Division One, now known as the <a href="/wiki/RFU_Championship" title="RFU Championship">RFU Championship</a>; they were either <a href="/wiki/Promotion_and_relegation" title="Promotion and relegation">promoted or relegated</a> in four consecutive seasons starting in 2006. Leeds were relegated from the Premiership as bottom finishers in <a href="/wiki/2005%E2%80%9306_English_Premiership_(rugby_union)" title="2005–06 English Premiership (rugby union)">2006</a>, promoted as National Division One champions in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Division_One_2006%E2%80%9307" title="National Division One 2006–07">2007</a>, relegated again from the Premiership in <a href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%9308_English_Premiership_(rugby_union)" title="2007–08 English Premiership (rugby union)">2008</a>, and promoted a second time as National Division One champions in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Division_One_2008%E2%80%9309" title="National Division One 2008–09">2009</a>. They managed to stay in the Premiership in the <a href="/wiki/2009%E2%80%9310_English_Premiership_(rugby_union)" title="2009–10 English Premiership (rugby union)">2009–10 season</a>, which helped to secure their financial future; starting with the 2010–11 season, they will become a full shareholder in Premier Rugby, the company behind the Premiership. In 2009–10, they only received 60% of a full share of Premiership revenues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Carnegie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1877-1900s:_Headingley_and_Roundhay">1877-1900s: Headingley and Roundhay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Carnegie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1877-1900s: Headingley and Roundhay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Headingley" title="Headingley">Headingley</a> has a tradition of rugby which started back in 1877, after several youngsters became interested in rugby after watching Leeds St. John's, later to become the <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Rhinos" title="Leeds Rhinos">Leeds</a> <a href="/wiki/Rugby_league" title="Rugby league">rugby league</a> team. Their first game was in November against the Saints second team. Union was centred around a church club. The original rugby union team was Leeds St John's and it played at the Militia Barracks ground before moving to Cardigan Fields. The Headingley name was adopted in 1878 and Cardigan Fields was used for both rugby and cricket. On 5 January 1884, England played Wales there and won 5–3 with a crowd in the region of 2,000 in attendance. The club playing there was then disbanded but was re-formed again in 1885 under the auspices of the Headingley Hill Chapel Sunday Class and played matches on local fields against local teams, including <a href="/wiki/Roundhay" title="Roundhay">Roundhay</a>.</p>
<p>In 1888, the Cardigan Estate was sold at auction and Lot 17a was purchased by a group of Leeds citizens, who intended to form the city's leading sports club. Lot 17a became what is now Headingley Stadium. Leeds St John's played their final season under that name in 1889–90, before becoming the football section of Leeds Cricket, Football and Athletic Company Ltd the following season. With Headingley still being completed, Leeds' first game was staged at Cardigan Fields, the home side defeating Otley. The first game at Headingley was played on 20 September 1890, when Manningham were beaten by one try and one dropped goal to nil. Leeds were founder members of the Northern Union when it broke away from the Rugby Football Union in 1895. Leeds' début in the Northern Union was a 6–3 victory at Leigh on 7 September 1895, the inaugural day of the new competition.</p>
<p>The development of the playing fields into the Headingley ground was down to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Hawke" title="Lord Hawke">Lord Hawke</a>, who was behind the creation of the Leeds Cricket, Football and Athletic Company and the purchase of lot 17A of the Cardigan Estate. (Lord Hawke captained England and Yorkshire at cricket). However, this saw the demise of the Leeds club which split into two. The part that was to become the Rugby League club in 1895-6 stayed at the Headingley ground and Headingley RUFC was reborn in 1891, eventually finishing up in 1902 in Clarence Fields, Kirkstall. Two other internationals were played in Leeds before the split between Union and League, against Ireland and Scotland, both ending in defeat.</p>
<p>In 1889, Headingley was disbanded when Leeds St. John's moved into the area, built <a href="/wiki/Headingley_Stadium" title="Headingley Stadium">Headingley Stadium</a> and dropped the St. John's from their name. However, Headingley bounced back and found fixtures outside <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>, in 1901 their fixture list including a game against the famous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackheath_Rugby_Club" title="Blackheath Rugby Club">Blackheath Rugby Club</a>. Roundhay were formed in 1924 and moved to their ground at Chandos Park in the 1930s. Forty internationals have played for one team or the other perhaps the best known being <a href="/wiki/Peter_Winterbottom" title="Peter Winterbottom">Peter Winterbottom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ian_McGeechan" title="Ian McGeechan">Ian McGeechan</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chris_Rea_(rugby_player)" title="Chris Rea (rugby player)">Chris Rea</a>, who played for Headingley. Former Scotland coach <a href="/wiki/Frank_Hadden" title="Frank Hadden">Frank Hadden</a> also had a spell at Headingley, where his and McGeechan's playing paths crossed, at the tail end of his career. <a href="/wiki/Brian_Moore_(rugby_union)" title="Brian Moore (rugby union)">Brian Moore</a> played for <a href="/wiki/Roundhay" title="Roundhay">Roundhay</a> before his move to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nottingham_RFC" title="Nottingham RFC">Nottingham</a> and selection for England.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1991-1997:_Leeds_RUFC">1991-1997: Leeds RUFC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Carnegie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1991-1997: Leeds RUFC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Leeds RUFC was founded in 1991 after the merger of Roundhay and Headingley; <a href="/wiki/Morley_R.F.C." title="Morley R.F.C.">Morley RFC</a> was also invited to join but declined. The new club played their first match on 1 September 1992 against <a href="/wiki/Hull_Ionians" title="Hull Ionians">Hull Ionians</a>. The first try was scored by Glynn Thompson, previously of Roundhay. Richard Cardus, Bev Dovey, Denis Wilkins and <a href="/wiki/Keith_Smith_(rugby)" title="Keith Smith (rugby)">Keith Smith</a> all won international caps while in the Roundhay ranks. Smith featured in England's first full tour of Australia in 1975, but had to return home injured and Wilkins, who was also in the Royal Navy, won 13 caps, between 1951–53. When they amalgamated in 1991–92, both clubs were in National Division Three.</p>
<p>In the first season in National Three, Leeds finished 6th, but League reorganisation put the club in National Division Four, with finishes of 6th, 6th and 5th in 1995–96. The following season, they finished 3rd, scoring 1,209 points in thirty games, with the former England ‘A’ outside-half <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Ainscough" title="Gerry Ainscough">Gerry Ainscough</a> scoring 307 points, and the ex-Scotland 'A' utility back Mark Appleson scoring sixteen tries. In 1997–98, they were promoted from the newly formed Jewson One to Premiership Two, finishing runners-up to Worcester. The Tongan Sateki Tuipulotu scored a then club record 322 points.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1998-2006:_Leeds_Tykes">1998-2006: Leeds Tykes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Carnegie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1998-2006: Leeds Tykes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:124px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leeds_tykes.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="83" data-file-width="122" height="83" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/cf/Leeds_tykes.PNG" width="122"/></a>

<p>In 1998, the club amalgamated with <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Rhinos" title="Leeds Rhinos">Leeds Rhinos</a> to form <i>Leeds Rugby Limited</i>. Leeds RUFC took on a new name when they entered the 1998 Premiership Two competition, Leeds Tykes.</p>
<p>The Tykes finished 6th in their first season in Premiership Two with the Tongan scoring 250 points and Simon Middleton and Jonathan Scales scoring ten tries apiece. Since 1996, the Tykes have played at Leeds Rhinos' Headingley stadium and <a href="/wiki/Phil_Davies" title="Phil Davies">Phil Davies</a> became their player-coach. In their first season, they finished 8th in a twelve team fourth division. They were promoted to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinness_Premiership" title="Guinness Premiership">Premiership</a> in 2001. In gaining promotion Richard Le Bas scored 337 points and Graham Mackay, a product of Leeds Rhinos, scored 19 tries, both club season records still extant.</p>
<p>In 2001, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tom_Palmer_(rugby_player)" title="Tom Palmer (rugby player)">Tom Palmer</a> became the first Leeds Tykes player to be capped for England when he appeared as a replacement against the United States. The Leeds Tykes team for their first game in the Premiership on 2 September 2001 against <a href="/wiki/Bath_Rugby" title="Bath Rugby">Bath</a> was Shelley, Holt, Wring, C. Murphy, Palmer, Mather, Ponton, Fea'unati, Benton, Bachop, Emmerson, Woof, Mayer, Scarbrough, Benson. The replacements were Hogg for Mather (74), Davies for Fea'unati (75), Kerr for Fea'unati (26), O'Reilly for Mayer (73), Fea'unati for Kerr (33). Not used were Luffman, Clarke, Le Bas.</p>
<p>The Tykes survived their first season after finishing bottom of the league when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_League_One" title="National League One">National League One</a> champions were denied entry into the Premiership in 2002 (Champions <a href="/wiki/Rotherham_R.U.F.C." title="Rotherham R.U.F.C.">Rotherham Titans</a> were refused promotion to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinness_Premiership" title="Guinness Premiership">Zurich Premiership</a> for the 2002–03 season due to facilities failing to meet Premiership criteria). The next season Leeds finished fifth in the table and made their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heineken_Cup" title="Heineken Cup">Heineken Cup</a> début in December 2003. In their four seasons in the Zurich Premiership, they finished an average ninth and reached two domestic cup semi-finals.</p>
<p>In 2005, they were mid-table by early November, but at the turn of the year, following injuries to key players, they were bottom of the Zurich Premiership and some way adrift of their rivals. Despite the threat of relegation, they made it to their first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EDF_Energy_Cup" title="EDF Energy Cup">Powergen Cup</a> final. They faced Bath who were top of the table and had never been beaten in a cup final, winning 20–12 to claim their first trophy.</p>
<p>Following the cup win, they won five straight games and avoided the drop by finishing eighth. Phil Davies relinquished control of some coaching duties to concentrate on his role as Director of Rugby in 2005 following another poor start to the season which saw the Tykes lose their first eight games in three different competitions.</p>
<p>After losing three successive away matches in injury time to <a href="/wiki/Northampton_Saints" title="Northampton Saints">Northampton Saints</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sale_Sharks" title="Sale Sharks">Sale Sharks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leicester_Tigers" title="Leicester Tigers">Leicester Tigers</a>, in January 2006 former Rugby League international <a href="/wiki/Daryl_Powell" title="Daryl Powell">Daryl Powell</a> was promoted from the back room staff to First Team coach. Leeds were finally relegated after Newcastle Falcons beat Sale Sharks. Following relegation, Marshall was transfer listed by request and <a href="/wiki/Mike_Shelley" title="Mike Shelley">Mike Shelley</a> announced his retirement. Other players who left were: <a href="/wiki/Iain_Balshaw" title="Iain Balshaw">Iain Balshaw</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chris_Bell_(rugby_player)" title="Chris Bell (rugby player)">Chris Bell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Bulloch" title="Gordon Bulloch">Gordon Bulloch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Danny_Care" title="Danny Care">Danny Care</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andy_Craig" title="Andy Craig">Andy Craig</a>, David Doherty, Dan Hyde, Mark McMillan, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tom_Palmer_(rugby_player)" title="Tom Palmer (rugby player)">Tom Palmer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Parks" title="Richard Parks">Richard Parks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roland_Reid" title="Roland Reid">Roland Reid</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gordon_Ross_(rugby_player)" title="Gordon Ross (rugby player)">Gordon Ross</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Rees_(rugby_player)" title="David Rees (rugby player)">David Rees</a>, Roland De Marigny, <a href="/wiki/Scott_Morgan_(rugby_player)" title="Scott Morgan (rugby player)">Scott Morgan</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chris_Murphy_(rugby_league)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chris Murphy (rugby league) (page does not exist)">Chris Murphy</a> and Nathan Thomas. Players who remained at club were: <a href="/wiki/Tom_Biggs" title="Tom Biggs">Tom Biggs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lee_Blackett" title="Lee Blackett">Lee Blackett</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michael_Cusack_(rugby_player)" title="Michael Cusack (rugby player)">Michael Cusack</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jon_Dunbar" title="Jon Dunbar">Jon Dunbar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Hooper" title="Stuart Hooper">Stuart Hooper</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Isaacson" title="James Isaacson">James Isaacson</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chris_Jones_(rugby_player_born_1982)" title="Chris Jones (rugby player born 1982)">Chris Jones</a>, Rob Rawlinson and Rob Vickerman. At the end of April 2006, <a href="/wiki/Phil_Davies" title="Phil Davies">Phil Davies</a> resigned as Director Of Rugby to be replaced by academy coach <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stuart_Lancaster_(rugby)" title="Stuart Lancaster (rugby)">Stuart Lancaster</a>.</p>
<p>The new players signed for the new season under Lancaster were <a href="/wiki/Leigh_Hinton" title="Leigh Hinton">Leigh Hinton</a> from <a href="/wiki/Newport_Gwent_Dragons" title="Newport Gwent Dragons">Newport Gwent Dragons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leinster_Rugby" title="Leinster Rugby">Leinster</a> centre <a href="/wiki/Jonny_Hepworth" title="Jonny Hepworth">Jonny Hepworth</a>, wing/full-back <a href="/wiki/Richard_Welding" title="Richard Welding">Richard Welding</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Pirates" title="Cornish Pirates">Cornish Pirates</a>, scrum-half <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Rauluni" title="Jacob Rauluni">Jacob Rauluni</a> ex-Earth Titans and Bristol, scrum-half <a href="/wiki/Darren_Edwards" title="Darren Edwards">Darren Edwards</a> from <a href="/wiki/London_Irish" title="London Irish">London Irish</a>, winger <a href="/wiki/John_Holtby" title="John Holtby">John Holtby</a> from Earth Titans, and centre Anitelia Tuilagi, on loan from <a href="/wiki/Leicester_Tigers" title="Leicester Tigers">Leicester Tigers</a>. New forwards include flanker <a href="/wiki/Mark_Lock" title="Mark Lock">Mark Lock</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Wasps" title="London Wasps">London Wasps</a>, Argentinian 7s and ex-<a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Albion_R.F.C." title="Plymouth Albion R.F.C.">Plymouth Albion</a> flanker <a href="/wiki/Mart%C3%ADn_Schusterman" title="Martín Schusterman">Martín Schusterman</a>, Former <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Rugby" title="Bristol Rugby">Bristol Rugby</a> No 8 <a href="/wiki/Rhys_Oakley" title="Rhys Oakley">Rhys Oakley</a> from Newport Gwent Dragons, hooker <a href="/wiki/James_Parkes_(rugby_player)" title="James Parkes (rugby player)">James Parkes</a> from Gloucester and props <a href="/wiki/United_States_national_rugby_union_team" title="United States national rugby union team">USA international</a> <a href="/wiki/Mike_MacDonald_(rugby_union)" title="Mike MacDonald (rugby union)">Mike MacDonald</a>, ex-Worcester who helped the Eagles to qualify for the <a href="/wiki/2007_Rugby_World_Cup" title="2007 Rugby World Cup">World Cup</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Colin_Noon" title="Colin Noon">Colin Noon</a> from <a href="/wiki/Biarritz_Olympique" title="Biarritz Olympique">Biarritz</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2007-2013:_Leeds_Carnegie_RUFC">2007-2013: Leeds Carnegie RUFC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Carnegie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2007-2013: Leeds Carnegie RUFC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leeds_carnegie_badge.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="100" data-file-width="150" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/9/93/Leeds_carnegie_badge.png" width="150"/></a>

<p>IN 2007, the club was renamed as Leeds Carnegie in a deal with Leeds Metropolitan University, which took a 51% stake. Carnegie College is part of the university's sport department. Leeds director of rugby Stuart Lancaster said: "I am delighted by the announcement. This is another hugely significant step in our evolution."</p>
<p>In 2008, Lancaster left Leeds to take a job at the RFU, replaced by England World Cup winner <a href="/wiki/Neil_Back" title="Neil Back">Neil Back</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Andy_Key&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Andy Key (page does not exist)">Andy Key</a>, both of whom were previously on the coaching staff at <a href="/wiki/Leicester_Tigers" title="Leicester Tigers">Leicester Tigers</a>.</p>
<p>In May 2009, Leeds Carnegie announced that it had restructured for the Premiership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Leeds Met University returned its 51% stake in Leeds Carnegie and Leeds Rugby retook full control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The restructuring saw former Wales and British and Irish Lions international <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gareth_Davies_(rugby_union)" title="Gareth Davies (rugby union)">Gareth Davies</a> join a new board of directors representing the university.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2014-present:_Yorkshire_Carnegie">2014-present: Yorkshire Carnegie</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Carnegie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2014-present: Yorkshire Carnegie">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2014, the club was rebranded as Yorkshire Carnegie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> They also struck a two-year sponsorship deal with Satsuma Loans at a reported £300,000 per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_ground">Home ground</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Carnegie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Home ground">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Headingley_Carnegie.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Headingley_Carnegie.jpg/200px-Headingley_Carnegie.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Headingley_Carnegie.jpg/300px-Headingley_Carnegie.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Headingley_Carnegie.jpg/400px-Headingley_Carnegie.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Headingley_Carnegie.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The new Carnegie Stand at the rugby ground.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Leeds St. Johns, who were later to become Leeds Rugby League Football Club, then <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Rhinos" title="Leeds Rhinos">Leeds Rhinos</a>, moved to Headingley in 1889 and built Headingley stadium. Since then the stadium has staged more than forty international matches and countless domestic finals. Undersoil heating was installed in 1963, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floodlights_(sport)" title="Floodlights (sport)">floodlights</a> in 1966. New changing rooms were added in 1991.</p>
<p>On Saturday 13 August 2005, Headingley hosted back-to-back union and league games. The Tykes played Edinburgh in a friendly followed by a Super League game between the Rhinos and London. 2006 saw the construction of the Carnegie Stand. Built to replace the old eastern terrace, it was opened on 1 September 2006 for the <a href="/wiki/Super_League" title="Super League">Super League</a> match between <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Rhinos" title="Leeds Rhinos">Leeds Rhinos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warrington_Wolves" title="Warrington Wolves">Warrington Wolves</a>. The ground now has a capacity of 22,250.</p>
<p>The record attendance at <a href="/wiki/Headingley_Stadium#Headingley_Carnegie_Stadium" title="Headingley Stadium">Headingley</a> was 40,175 for the <a href="/wiki/Rugby_league" title="Rugby league">rugby league</a> match between Leeds and Bradford Northern on 21 May 1947. The highest attendance for a Tykes match was against Newcastle Falcons on 27 December 2004, with a crowd of 14,293 at Headingley to see the Falcons take home a 15–11 victory. In July 1998 Leeds RUFC became part of the world's first dual-code rugby partnership, <i>Leeds Rugby Limited</i>. In 2006 a new sponsorship deal with Leeds Metropolitan University led to Headingley Stadium been renamed Headingley Carnegie Stadium.</p>
