<p><b>Richard Ewen Borcherds</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɔːr/ 'or' in 'abhor'">ɔːr</span><span title="/tʃ/ 'ch' in 'china'">tʃ</span><span title="/ər/ 'er' in 'finger'">ər</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>; born 29 November 1959)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whoswho_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-whoswho-1">[1]</a></sup> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British-American" title="British-American">British-American</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> currently working in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theory</a>. He is known for his work in <a href="/wiki/Lattice_(group)" title="Lattice (group)">lattices</a>, <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Group_theory" title="Group theory">group theory</a>, and infinite-dimensional algebras,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mathgene_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-mathgene-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mactutor_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-mactutor-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olympiad_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-olympiad-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scopus_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-scopus-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-microsoft_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-microsoft-16">[16]</a></sup> for which he was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Fields_Medal" title="Fields Medal">Fields Medal</a> in 1998.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Borcherds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Borcherds was born in <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>, but the family moved to <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> when he was six months old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-singh_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-singh-17">[17]</a></sup> His father is a physicist and he has three brothers, two of whom are mathematics teachers. He was a promising mathematician and chess player as a child, winning several national mathematics championships and "was in line for becoming a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chess_master" title="Chess master">chess master</a>" before giving up chess after coming to believe that the higher levels of competitive chess are merely about the competition rather than the fun of playing.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Borcherds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was educated at <a href="/wiki/King_Edward%27s_School,_Birmingham" title="King Edward's School, Birmingham">King Edward's School, Birmingham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College, Cambridge</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ub_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ub-18">[18]</a></sup> where he studied under <a href="/wiki/John_Horton_Conway" title="John Horton Conway">John Horton Conway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ams_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-ams-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Borcherds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After receiving his doctorate in 1985 he has held various alternating positions at Cambridge and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>, serving as Morrey Assistant Professor of Mathematics at Berkeley from 1987 to 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ub_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-ub-18">[18]</a></sup> From 1996 he held a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society Research Professorship</a> at Cambridge before returning to Berkeley in 1999 as Professor of mathematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ub_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-ub-18">[18]</a></sup> At Berkeley, he held a <a href="/wiki/Miller_Institute" title="Miller Institute">Miller</a> Research Professorship from 2000–2001.</p>
<p>Borcherds's early work included pioneering results on classification of unimodular lattices, and the introduction of new algebraic objects, most notably <a href="/wiki/Vertex_operator_algebra" title="Vertex operator algebra">vertex algebras</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borcherds-Kac-Moody_algebra" title="Borcherds-Kac-Moody algebra">Borcherds-Kac-Moody algebras</a>. These ideas came together in his vertex-algebraic construction and analysis of the fake monster Lie algebra (called the monster Lie algebra at the time).</p>
<p>Borcherds is best known for his resolution of the Conway-Norton <a href="/wiki/Monstrous_moonshine" title="Monstrous moonshine">monstrous moonshine conjecture</a>, which describes an intricate relation between the <a href="/wiki/Monster_group" title="Monster group">monster group</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modular_function" title="Modular function">modular functions</a> on the complex upper half-plane. To prove this conjecture, he drew upon theories that he had previously introduced, namely those of vertex algebras and Borcherds-Kac-Moody algebras, together with techniques of <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">string theory</a>, and applied them to the "moonshine module", a vertex operator algebra with monster symmetry constructed by <a href="/wiki/Igor_Frenkel" title="Igor Frenkel">Igor Frenkel</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Lepowsky" title="James Lepowsky">James Lepowsky</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arne_Meurman" title="Arne Meurman">Arne Meurman</a>. Additional work in moonshine concerned mod p variants of this conjecture, and were known as modular moonshine.</p>
<p>Later contributions include the theory of Borcherds products, which are holomorphic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automorphic_forms" title="Automorphic forms">automorphic forms</a> on <i>O</i>(<i>n</i>,2) that have well-behaved infinite product expansions at cusps. Borcherds used this theory to resolve some long-standing conjectures concerning quasi-affineness of certain <a href="/wiki/Moduli_space" title="Moduli space">moduli spaces</a> of <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_surface" title="Algebraic surface">algebraic surfaces</a>. More recently, Borcherds has rendered perturbative renormalization, in particular the 't Hooft-Veltman proof of perturbative renormalizability of gauge theory, into rigorous mathematical language.</p>
<p>An interview with <a href="/wiki/Simon_Singh" title="Simon Singh">Simon Singh</a> for <a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">the Guardian</a>, in which Borcherds suggested he might have some traits associated with <a href="/wiki/Asperger_syndrome" title="Asperger syndrome">Asperger syndrome</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-singh_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-singh-17">[17]</a></sup> subsequently led to a chapter about him in a book on autism by <a href="/wiki/Simon_Baron-Cohen" title="Simon Baron-Cohen">Simon Baron-Cohen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sbc_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-sbc-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Baron-Cohen concluded that while Borcherds had many autistic traits, he did not merit a formal diagnosis of Asperger syndrome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sbc_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-sbc-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honours">Awards and honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Borcherds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Awards and honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1992 he was one of the first recipients of the <a href="/wiki/European_Mathematical_Society" title="European Mathematical Society">EMS prizes</a> awarded at the first <a href="/wiki/European_Congress_of_Mathematics" title="European Congress of Mathematics">European Congress of Mathematics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, and in 1994 he was an <a href="/wiki/List_of_International_Congresses_of_Mathematicians_Plenary_and_Invited_Speakers" title="List of International Congresses of Mathematicians Plenary and Invited Speakers">invited speaker at the International Congress of Mathematicians</a> in Zurich.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ams_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-ams-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1994, he was elected to the Royal Society of Fellows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royal_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-royal-22">[22]</a></sup> In 1998 at the 23rd <a href="/wiki/International_Congress_of_Mathematicians" title="International Congress of Mathematicians">International Congress of Mathematicians</a> in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, Germany he received the <a href="/wiki/Fields_Medal" title="Fields Medal">Fields Medal</a> together with <a href="/wiki/Maxim_Kontsevich" title="Maxim Kontsevich">Maxim Kontsevich</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Timothy_Gowers" title="William Timothy Gowers">William Timothy Gowers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Curtis_T._McMullen" title="Curtis T. McMullen">Curtis T. McMullen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ams_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-ams-19">[19]</a></sup> The award cited him "for his contributions to algebra, the theory of automorphic forms, and mathematical physics, including the introduction of vertex algebras and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generalized_Kac-Moody_algebra" title="Generalized Kac-Moody algebra">Borcherds' Lie algebras</a>, the proof of the Conway-Norton moonshine conjecture and the discovery of a new class of automorphic infinite products." In 2012 he became a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Mathematical_Society" title="American Mathematical Society">American Mathematical Society</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and in 2014 he was elected to the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
