<p><b>Richard Wesley Hamming</b> (February 11, 1915 – January 7, 1998) was an American <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> whose work had many implications for <a href="/wiki/Computer_engineering" title="Computer engineering">computer engineering</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telecommunications" title="Telecommunications">telecommunications</a>. His contributions include the <a href="/wiki/Hamming_code" title="Hamming code">Hamming code</a> (which makes use of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hamming_matrix" title="Hamming matrix">Hamming matrix</a>), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hamming_window#Hamming_window" title="Hamming window">Hamming window</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hamming_numbers" title="Hamming numbers">Hamming numbers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sphere-packing" title="Sphere-packing">sphere-packing</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Hamming_bound" title="Hamming bound">Hamming bound</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Hamming_distance" title="Hamming distance">Hamming distance</a>.</p>
<p>Born in Chicago, Hamming attended <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Nebraska" title="University of Nebraska">University of Nebraska</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana-Champaign" title="University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign">University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign</a>, where he wrote his doctoral thesis in mathematics under the supervision of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Waldemar_Trjitzinsky&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Waldemar Trjitzinsky (page does not exist)">Waldemar Trjitzinsky</a> (1901-1973). In April 1945 he joined the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos Laboratory">Los Alamos Laboratory</a>, where he programmed the <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calculating_machines" title="Calculating machines">calculating machines</a> that computed the solution to equations provided by the project's physicists. He left to join the <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Telephone Laboratories</a> in 1946. Over the next fifteen years he was involved in nearly all of the Laboratories' most prominent achievements.</p>
<p>After retiring from the Bell Labs in 1976, Hamming took a position at the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Postgraduate_School" title="Naval Postgraduate School">Naval Postgraduate School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Monterey,_California" title="Monterey, California">Monterey, California</a>, where he worked as an <a href="/wiki/Adjunct_professor" title="Adjunct professor">Adjunct Professor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Senior_lecturer" title="Senior lecturer">senior lecturer</a> in <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, and devoted himself to teaching and writing books. He delivered his last lecture in December 1997, just a few weeks before he died from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_attack" title="Heart attack">heart attack</a> on January 7, 1998.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Hamming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Richard Wesley Hamming was born in Chicago, Illinois, on February 11, 1915,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEEE_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-IEEE-1">[1]</a></sup> the son of Richard J. Hamming, a credit manager,and Mabel G. Redfield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarnes2005220.E2.80.93221_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarnes2005220.E2.80.93221-2">[2]</a></sup> He grew up in Chicago, where he attended <a href="/wiki/Crane_High_School_(Chicago)" title="Crane High School (Chicago)">Crane Technical High School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X_College" title="Malcolm X College">Crane Junior College</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarnes2005220.E2.80.93221_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarnes2005220.E2.80.93221-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hamming initially wanted to study engineering, but money was scarce during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, and the only scholarship offer he received came from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>, which had no engineering school. Instead, he became a science student, majoring in mathematics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACM_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ACM-3">[3]</a></sup> and received his <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> degree in 1937.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEEE_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-IEEE-1">[1]</a></sup> He later considered this a fortunate turn of events. "As an engineer," he said, "I would have been the guy going down manholes instead of having the excitement of frontier research work."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEEE_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-IEEE-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>He went on to earn a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">Master of Arts</a> degree from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Nebraska" title="University of Nebraska">University of Nebraska</a> in 1939, and then entered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana-Champaign" title="University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign">University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign</a>, where he wrote his doctoral thesis on <i>Some Problems in the Boundary Value Theory of Linear Differential Equations</i> under the supervision of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Waldemar_Trjitzinsky&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Waldemar Trjitzinsky (page does not exist)">Waldemar Trjitzinsky</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACM_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ACM-3">[3]</a></sup> His thesis was an extension of Trjitzinsky's work in that area. He looked at <a href="/wiki/Green%27s_function" title="Green's function">Green's function</a> and further developed <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Tamarkin" title="Jacob Tamarkin">Jacob Tamarkin</a>'s methods for obtaining <a href="/wiki/Characteristic_equation_(calculus)" title="Characteristic equation (calculus)">characteristic</a> solutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-St_Andrews_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-St_Andrews-4">[4]</a></sup> While he was a graduate student, he discovered and read <a href="/wiki/George_Boole" title="George Boole">George Boole</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Laws_of_Thought" title="The Laws of Thought">The Laws of Thought</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamming1998643_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamming1998643-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign awarded Hamming his <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Doctor of Philosophy</a> in 1942, and he became an Instructor in Mathematics there. He married Wanda Little, a fellow student, on September 5, 1942,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACM_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-ACM-3">[3]</a></sup> immediately after she was awarded her own Master of Arts in English literature. They would remain married until his death, but had no children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarnes2005220.E2.80.93221_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarnes2005220.E2.80.93221-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1944, he became an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assistant_Professor" title="Assistant Professor">Assistant Professor</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.B._Speed_School_of_Engineering" title="J.B. Speed School of Engineering">J.B. Speed Scientific School</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Louisville" title="University of Louisville">University of Louisville</a> in <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville, Kentucky</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACM_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-ACM-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manhattan_Project">Manhattan Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Hamming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Manhattan Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> still ongoing, Hamming left Louisville in April 1945 to work on the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos Laboratory">Los Alamos Laboratory</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Hans_Bethe" title="Hans Bethe">Hans Bethe</a>'s division, programming the <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calculating_machines" title="Calculating machines">calculating machines</a> that computed the solution to equations provided by the project's physicists. His wife Wanda soon followed, taking a job at Los Alamos as a <a href="/wiki/Human_computer" title="Human computer">human computer</a>, working for Bethe and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Teller" title="Edward Teller">Edward Teller</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACM_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-ACM-3">[3]</a></sup> Hamming later recalled that:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Shortly before the first field test (you realize that no small scale experiment can be done—either you have a critical mass or you do not), a man asked me to check some arithmetic he had done, and I agreed, thinking to fob it off on some subordinate. When I asked what it was, he said, "It is the probability that the test bomb will ignite the whole atmosphere." I decided I would check it myself! The next day when he came for the answers I remarked to him, "The arithmetic was apparently correct but I do not know about the formulas for the capture cross sections for oxygen and nitrogen—after all, there could be no experiments at the needed energy levels." He replied, like a physicist talking to a mathematician, that he wanted me to check the arithmetic not the physics, and left. I said to myself, "What have you done, Hamming, you are involved in risking all of life that is known in the Universe, and you do not know much of an essential part?" I was pacing up and down the corridor when a friend asked me what was bothering me. I told him. His reply was, "Never mind, Hamming, no one will ever blame you."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamming1998643_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamming1998643-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Hamming remained at Los Alamos until 1946, when he accepted a post at the <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Telephone Laboratories</a> (BTL). For the trip to New Jersey, he bought <a href="/wiki/Klaus_Fuchs" title="Klaus Fuchs">Klaus Fuchs</a>'s old car. When he later sold it just weeks before Fuchs was unmasked as a spy, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FBI" title="FBI">FBI</a> regarded the timing as suspicious enough to interrogate Hamming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarnes2005220.E2.80.93221_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarnes2005220.E2.80.93221-2">[2]</a></sup> Although Hamming described his role at Los Alamos as being that of a "computer janitor",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998972_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998972-6">[6]</a></sup> he saw <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">computer simulations</a> of experiments that would have been impossible to perform in a laboratory. "And when I had time to think about it," he later recalled, "I realized that it meant that science was going to be changed".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEEE_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-IEEE-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bell_Laboratories">Bell Laboratories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Hamming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bell Laboratories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>At the Bell Labs Hamming shared an office for a time with <a href="/wiki/Claude_Shannon" title="Claude Shannon">Claude Shannon</a>. The Mathematical Research Department also included <a href="/wiki/John_Tukey" title="John Tukey">John Tukey</a> and Los Alamos veterans Donald Ling and <a href="/wiki/Brockway_McMillan" title="Brockway McMillan">Brockway McMillan</a>. Shannon, Ling, McMillan and Hamming came to call themselves the <a href="/wiki/Young_Turks_(Bell_Labs)" title="Young Turks (Bell Labs)">Young Turks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACM_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-ACM-3">[3]</a></sup> "We were first-class troublemakers," Hamming later recalled. "We did unconventional things in unconventional ways and still got valuable results. Thus management had to tolerate us and let us alone a lot of the time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEEE_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-IEEE-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although Hamming had been hired to work on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elasticity_theory" title="Elasticity theory">elasticity theory</a>, he still spent much of his time with the calculating machines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998972_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998972-6">[6]</a></sup> Before he went home on one Friday in 1947, he set the machines to perform a long and complex series of calculations over the weekend, only to find when he arrived on Monday morning that an error had occurred early in the process and the calculation had errored off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Additional_Materials_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Additional_Materials-7">[7]</a></sup> Digital machines manipulated information as sequences of zeroes and ones, units of information that Tukey would christen "<a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bits</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShannon1948379_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShannon1948379-8">[8]</a></sup> If a single bit in a sequence was wrong, then the whole sequence would be. To detect this, a <a href="/wiki/Parity_bit" title="Parity bit">parity bit</a> was used to verify the correctness of each sequence. "If the computer can tell when an error has occurred," Hamming reasoned, "surely there is a way of telling where the error is so that the computer can correct the error itself."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Additional_Materials_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Additional_Materials-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hamming set himself the task of solving this problem,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarnes2005220.E2.80.93221_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarnes2005220.E2.80.93221-2">[2]</a></sup> which he realised would have an enormous range of applications. Each bit can only be a zero or a one, so if you know which bit is wrong, then it can be corrected. In a landmark paper published in 1950, he introduced a concept of the number of positions in which two code words differ, and therefore how many changes are required to transform one <a href="/wiki/Code_word" title="Code word">code word</a> into another, which is today known as the <a href="/wiki/Hamming_distance" title="Hamming distance">Hamming distance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998973.E2.80.93975_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998973.E2.80.93975-9">[9]</a></sup> Hamming thereby created a family of mathematical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Error-correcting_code" title="Error-correcting code">error-correcting code</a>, which are called <a href="/wiki/Hamming_code" title="Hamming code">Hamming codes</a>. This not only solved an important problem in telecommunications and computer science, it opened up a whole new field of study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998973.E2.80.93975_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998973.E2.80.93975-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamming1950147.E2.80.93160_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamming1950147.E2.80.93160-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hamming_bound" title="Hamming bound">Hamming bound</a>, also known as the sphere-packing or volume bound is a limit on the parameters of an arbitrary <a href="/wiki/Block_code" title="Block code">block code</a>. It is from an interpretation in terms of <a href="/wiki/Sphere_packing" title="Sphere packing">sphere packing</a> in the Hamming distance into the <a href="/wiki/Space_(mathematics)" title="Space (mathematics)">space</a> of all possible words. It gives an important limitation on the <a href="/wiki/Efficiency" title="Efficiency">efficiency</a> with which any error-correcting code can utilize the space in which its code words are embedded. A code which attains the Hamming bound is said to be a perfect code. Hamming codes are perfect codes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELingXing200482.E2.80.9388_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELingXing200482.E2.80.9388-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPless198221.E2.80.9324_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPless198221.E2.80.9324-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Returning to <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equations</a>, Hamming studied means of numerically integrating them. A popular approach at the time was Milne's Method, attributed to <a href="/wiki/Edward_Arthur_Milne" title="Edward Arthur Milne">Arthur Milne</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This had the drawback of being unstable, so that under certain conditions the result could be swamped by roundoff noise. Hamming developed an improved version, the Hamming predictor-corrector. This was in use for many years, but has since been superseded by the <a href="/wiki/Linear_multistep_method" title="Linear multistep method">Adams method</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998975_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998975-14">[14]</a></sup> He did extensive research into digital <a href="/wiki/Filter_(signal_processing)" title="Filter (signal processing)">filters</a>, devising a new filter, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hamming_window" title="Hamming window">Hamming window</a>, and eventually writing an entire book on the subject, <i>Digital Filters</i> (1977).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998976.E2.80.93977_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998976.E2.80.93977-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 1950s, he programmed one of the earliest computers, the <a href="/wiki/IBM_650" title="IBM 650">IBM 650</a>, and with Ruth A. Weiss developed the L2 programming language, one of the earliest computer languages, in 1956. It was widely used within the Bell Labs, and also by external users, who knew it as Bell 2. It was superseded by <a href="/wiki/Fortran" title="Fortran">Fortran</a> when the Bell Labs' IBM 650 were replaced by the <a href="/wiki/IBM_704" title="IBM 704">IBM 704</a> in 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i>A Discipline of Programming</i> (1967), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edsger_Dijkstra" title="Edsger Dijkstra">Edsger Dijkstra</a> attributed to Hamming the problem of efficiently finding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regular_numbers" title="Regular numbers">regular numbers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDijkstra1976129.E2.80.93134_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDijkstra1976129.E2.80.93134-17">[17]</a></sup> The problem became known as "Hamming's problem", and the regular numbers are often referred to as Hamming numbers in Computer Science, although he did not discover them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout his time at Bell Labs, Hamming avoided management responsibilities. He was promoted to management positions several times, but always managed to make these only temporary. "I knew in a sense that by avoiding management," he later recalled, "I was not doing my duty by the organization. That is one of my biggest failures."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEEE_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-IEEE-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Hamming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hamming served as president of the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">Association for Computing Machinery</a> from 1958 to 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998972_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998972-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1960, he predicted that one day half of the Bell Lab's budget would be spent on computing. None of his colleagues thought that it would ever be so high, but his forecast actually proved to be too low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998977_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998977-19">[19]</a></sup> His philosophy on scientific computing appeared as the motto of his <i>Numerical Methods for Scientists and Engineers</i> (1962):</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The purpose of computing is insight, not numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHamming1962vii.2C_276.2C_395_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamming1962vii.2C_276.2C_395-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In later life, Hamming became interested in teaching. Between 1960 and 1976, when he left the Bell labs, he held visiting or adjunct professorships at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>, the <a href="/wiki/City_College_of_New_York" title="City College of New York">City College of New York</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_California_at_Irvine" title="University of California at Irvine">University of California at Irvine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarnes2005220.E2.80.93221_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarnes2005220.E2.80.93221-2">[2]</a></sup> As a Young Turk, Hamming had resented older scientists who had used up space and resources that would have been put to much better use by the young Turks. Looking at a commemorative poster of the Bell Labs' valued achievements, he noted that he had worked on or been associated with nearly all of those listed in the first half of his career at Bell Labs, but none in the second. He therefore resolved to retire in 1976, after thirty years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEEE_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-IEEE-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1976 he moved to the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Postgraduate_School" title="Naval Postgraduate School">Naval Postgraduate School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Monterey,_California" title="Monterey, California">Monterey, California</a>, where he worked as an <a href="/wiki/Adjunct_professor" title="Adjunct professor">Adjunct Professor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Senior_lecturer" title="Senior lecturer">senior lecturer</a> in <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarnes2005220.E2.80.93221_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarnes2005220.E2.80.93221-2">[2]</a></sup> He gave up research, and concentrated on teaching and writing books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACM_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-ACM-3">[3]</a></sup> He noted that:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The way mathematics is currently taught it is exceedingly dull. In the calculus book we are currently using on my campus, I found no single problem whose answer I felt the student would care about! The problems in the text have the dignity of solving a crossword puzzle – hard to be sure, but the result is of no significance in life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACM_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-ACM-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Hamming attempted to rectify the situation with a new text, <i>Methods of Mathematics Applied to Calculus, Probability, and Statistics</i> (1985).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACM_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-ACM-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1993, he remarked that "when I left BTL, I knew that that was the end of my scientific career. When I retire from here, in another sense, it's really the end."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEEE_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-IEEE-1">[1]</a></sup> And so it proved. He became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professor_Emeritus" title="Professor Emeritus">Professor Emeritus</a> in June 1997,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-obit-21">[21]</a></sup> and delivered his last lecture in December 1997, just a few weeks before his death from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_attack" title="Heart attack">heart attack</a> on January 7, 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998972_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998972-6">[6]</a></sup> He was survived by his wife Wanda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-obit-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

Hamming Medal</a>, named after him, is an award given annually by the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Electrical_and_Electronics_Engineers" title="Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers">Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers</a> (IEEE), for "exceptional contributions to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_sciences" title="Information sciences">information sciences</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_systems" title="Information systems">systems</a> and <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">technology</a>", and he was the first recipient of this medal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The reverse side of the medal depicts a Hamming parity check matrix for a Hamming error-correcting code.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998972_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorgan1998972-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
