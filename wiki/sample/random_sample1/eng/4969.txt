<p><b>Rich Moore</b> (born May 10, 1963) is an American film and television <a href="/wiki/Animation_director" title="Animation director">animation director</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voice_actor" title="Voice actor">voice actor</a> and a creative partner at both <a href="/wiki/Rough_Draft_Studios" title="Rough Draft Studios">Rough Draft Studios</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Animation_Studios" title="Walt Disney Animation Studios">Walt Disney Animation Studios</a>. He is best known for his work on the animated television shows <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">The Simpsons</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Critic" title="The Critic">The Critic</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Futurama" title="Futurama">Futurama</a></i>, and for directing the <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Animation_Studios" title="Walt Disney Animation Studios">Disney animated</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Wreck-It_Ralph" title="Wreck-It Ralph">Wreck-It Ralph</a></i> (2012) and co-directing <i><a href="/wiki/Zootopia" title="Zootopia">Zootopia</a></i> (2016). He is a two-time <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Award</a> winner, a two-time <a href="/wiki/Annie_Award" title="Annie Award">Annie Award</a> winner, and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> nominee.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rich_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Moore was born and raised in <a href="/wiki/Oxnard,_California" title="Oxnard, California">Oxnard, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He studied film and video at the <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_the_Arts" title="California Institute of the Arts">California Institute of the Arts</a>, graduating with a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Fine_Arts" title="Bachelor of Fine Arts">Bachelor of Fine Arts</a> degree in 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pfraser_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pfraser-2">[2]</a></sup> While there, he narrated <a href="/wiki/Jim_Reardon" title="Jim Reardon">Jim Reardon</a>'s 1986 student film <i><a href="/wiki/Bring_Me_the_Head_of_Charlie_Brown" title="Bring Me the Head of Charlie Brown">Bring Me the Head of Charlie Brown</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pfraser_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-pfraser-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rich_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rich_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After graduating from CalArts, Moore worked for <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Bakshi" title="Ralph Bakshi">Ralph Bakshi</a> on <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Mighty_Mouse:_The_New_Adventures" title="Mighty Mouse: The New Adventures">Mighty Mouse: The New Adventures</a></i>, co-writing all 13 season 1 episodes in 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Moore was one of the original three directors of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">The Simpsons</a></i>, directing 17 episodes in the first 5 seasons from 1990 to 1993,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jmacquarrie_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-jmacquarrie-5">[5]</a></sup> including some of the show's most famous episodes: "<a href="/wiki/Flaming_Moe%27s" title="Flaming Moe's">Flaming Moe's</a>", "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Itchy_and_Scratchy:_The_Movie" title="Itchy and Scratchy: The Movie">Itchy and Scratchy: The Movie</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Marge_vs._the_Monorail" title="Marge vs. the Monorail">Marge vs. the Monorail</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lgoodsell_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-lgoodsell-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He won a 1991 <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Animated_Program" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Animated Program">Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Animated Program</a> for <i>The Simpsons</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes08261991_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-latimes08261991-8">[8]</a></sup> and was the sequence director on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons_Movie" title="The Simpsons Movie">The Simpsons Movie</a></i> in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1994, Moore became a producer and supervising director for the animated series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Critic" title="The Critic">The Critic</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jmacquarrie_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-jmacquarrie-5">[5]</a></sup> He then oversaw the creative development and production of <i><a href="/wiki/Futurama" title="Futurama">Futurama</a></i> as the show's supervising director. He also directed several episodes of the animated series from 1999 to 2001, including the classic "<a href="/wiki/Roswell_That_Ends_Well" title="Roswell That Ends Well">Roswell That Ends Well</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jmacquarrie_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-jmacquarrie-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lgoodsell_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-lgoodsell-6">[6]</a></sup> for which he won the Emmy Award for Outstanding Animated Program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emmys_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-emmys-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moore's other television animation directing credits include <a href="/wiki/Comedy_Central" title="Comedy Central">Comedy Central</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Drawn_Together" title="Drawn Together">Drawn Together</a></i> and "<a href="/wiki/Spy_vs._Spy" title="Spy vs. Spy">Spy vs. Spy</a>" for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MADtv" title="MADtv">MADtv</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jmacquarrie_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-jmacquarrie-5">[5]</a></sup> He served as supervising director on the 2009 animated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_Broadcasting" title="Fox Broadcasting">Fox</a> television series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sit_Down,_Shut_Up_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Sit Down, Shut Up (U.S. TV series)">Sit Down, Shut Up</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rich_Moore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2004, Moore directed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warner_Brothers" title="Warner Brothers">Warner Brothers</a> animated short film <i>Duck Dodgers in Attack of the Drones</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jmacquarrie_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-jmacquarrie-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2008, he was invited by <a href="/wiki/John_Lasseter" title="John Lasseter">John Lasseter</a> to join Disney Animation as a director, with the suggestion that he develop a story set in the world of video games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This would become the 2012 animated feature <i><a href="/wiki/Wreck-It_Ralph" title="Wreck-It Ralph">Wreck-It Ralph</a></i>, Moore's feature directing debut, and a box office and critical success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jmacquarrie_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-jmacquarrie-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lgoodsell_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-lgoodsell-6">[6]</a></sup> Moore also supplied the voices for the film's characters Sour Bill and <a href="/wiki/Zangief" title="Zangief">Zangief</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <i>Wreck-It Ralph</i> won five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annie_Awards" title="Annie Awards">Annie Awards</a>, including Best Animated Feature and a Best Director award for Moore,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cgiardina_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-cgiardina-14">[14]</a></sup> and was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Animated_Feature" title="Academy Award for Best Animated Feature">Academy Award for Best Animated Feature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-btruitt_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-btruitt-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moore's next animated feature film, Disney's <i><a href="/wiki/Zootopia" title="Zootopia">Zootopia</a></i>, which he co-directed with <a href="/wiki/Byron_Howard" title="Byron Howard">Byron Howard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jared_Bush" title="Jared Bush">Jared Bush</a>, was released in March 2016, having the <a href="/wiki/List_of_highest-grossing_openings_for_animated_films#Biggest_worldwide_openings_for_an_animated_film_since_2002" title="List of highest-grossing openings for animated films">Biggest worldwide opening for an animated film</a> ever and <a href="/wiki/List_of_animated_feature_films_of_2016" title="List of animated feature films of 2016">The second highest grossing animated feature film of 2016</a> to <a href="/wiki/Finding_Dory" title="Finding Dory">Finding Dory</a> with over a billion dollars (one of five animted films to surpass a billion dollars), The film was also critically acclaimed.</p>
