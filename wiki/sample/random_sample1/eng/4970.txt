<p><b>Richard Hugh "Ritchie" Blackmore</b> (born 14 April 1945) is an English guitarist and songwriter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He was one of the founding members of <a href="/wiki/Deep_Purple" title="Deep Purple">Deep Purple</a> in 1968, playing jam-style hard-rock music which mixed guitar riffs and organ sounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Quietus_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Quietus-6">[6]</a></sup> During his solo career, he established a heavy metal band called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainbow_(English_band)" title="Rainbow (English band)">Rainbow</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rainbow_bio_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rainbow_bio-7">[7]</a></sup> which fused <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">baroque music</a> influences and elements of <a href="/wiki/Hard_rock" title="Hard rock">hard rock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RStone_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-RStone-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guitar_International_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guitar_International-9">[9]</a></sup> Rainbow steadily moved to catchy pop-style mainstream rock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rainbow_bio_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rainbow_bio-7">[7]</a></sup> Later in life, he formed the traditional <a href="/wiki/Folk_rock" title="Folk rock">folk rock</a> project <a href="/wiki/Blackmore%27s_Night" title="Blackmore's Night">Blackmore's Night</a> transitioning to vocalist-centred sounds. As a member of Deep Purple, Blackmore was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">Rock and Roll Hall of Fame</a> in April 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ritchie_Blackmore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Blackmore was born at Allendale Nursing Home in <a href="/wiki/Weston-super-Mare" title="Weston-super-Mare">Weston-super-Mare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>, as second son to Lewis J. Blackmore and Violet (née Short). The family moved to <a href="/wiki/Heston" title="Heston">Heston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a>, when Blackmore was two. He was 11 when he was given his first <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a> by his father on certain conditions, including learning how to play properly, so he took classical guitar lessons for one year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an interview with <a href="/wiki/Sounds_(magazine)" title="Sounds (magazine)"><i>Sounds</i> magazine</a> in 1979, Blackmore said that he started the guitar because he wanted to be like <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Steele" title="Tommy Steele">Tommy Steele</a>, who used to just jump around and play. Blackmore loathed school and hated his teachers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sounds.2C_15_December_1979_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sounds.2C_15_December_1979-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>While at school, he participated in sports including the <a href="/wiki/Javelin_throw" title="Javelin throw">javelin</a>. Blackmore left school at age 15 and started work as an apprentice radio mechanic at nearby <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">Heathrow Airport</a>. He took <a href="/wiki/Electric_guitar" title="Electric guitar">electric guitar</a> lessons from session guitarist <a href="/wiki/Big_Jim_Sullivan" title="Big Jim Sullivan">Big Jim Sullivan</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ritchie_Blackmore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1960s">1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ritchie_Blackmore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1960s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1960 he began to work as a session player for <a href="/wiki/Joe_Meek" title="Joe Meek">Joe Meek</a>'s music productions, and performed in several bands. He was initially a member of the instrumental band <a href="/wiki/The_Outlaws_(band)" title="The Outlaws (band)">The Outlaws</a>, who played in both studio recordings and live concerts. Otherwise, in mainly studio recordings, he backed female singer <a href="/wiki/Glenda_Collins" title="Glenda Collins">Glenda Collins</a>, German-born pop singer <a href="/wiki/Heinz_Burt" title="Heinz Burt">Heinz</a> (playing on his top ten hit "<a href="/wiki/Just_Like_Eddie" title="Just Like Eddie">Just Like Eddie</a>", "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Beating_Of_My_Heart&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Beating Of My Heart (page does not exist)">Beating Of My Heart</a>"), and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Thereafter, in mainly live concerts, he backed horror-themed singer <a href="/wiki/Screaming_Lord_Sutch" title="Screaming Lord Sutch">Screaming Lord Sutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beat_(music)" title="Beat (music)">beat</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Neil_Christian" title="Neil Christian">Neil Christian</a>, and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Blackmore joined <a href="/wiki/Deep_Purple" title="Deep Purple">Deep Purple</a> in 1968 after receiving an invitation from <a href="/wiki/Chris_Curtis" title="Chris Curtis">Chris Curtis</a> who originated the concept of the band (though Curtis would be forced out before the band fully formed). Purple's early sound leaned on psychedelic and <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive rock</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-In_Prog_Rock_Heaven_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-In_Prog_Rock_Heaven-15">[15]</a></sup> but also included generic 1960s pop songs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This "Mark One" line-up featuring singer <a href="/wiki/Rod_Evans" title="Rod Evans">Rod Evans</a> lasted until mid-1969 and produced three studio albums. During this period, organist <a href="/wiki/Jon_Lord" title="Jon Lord">Jon Lord</a> appeared to be the leader of the band,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-In_Prog_Rock_Heaven_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-In_Prog_Rock_Heaven-15">[15]</a></sup> and wrote much of their original material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>The first studio album from Purple's second line-up, <i><a href="/wiki/Deep_Purple_in_Rock" title="Deep Purple in Rock">In Rock</a></i> (1970), signalled a transition in the band's sound from progressive rock to <a href="/wiki/Hard_rock" title="Hard rock">hard rock</a>, with Blackmore being impressed at the time by the <a href="/wiki/King_Crimson" title="King Crimson">King Crimson</a>'s <a href="/wiki/In_the_Court_of_the_Crimson_King" title="In the Court of the Crimson King">first album</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="If Blackmore was impressed with Crimson, why would he want to move ''away'' from prog? (October 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guitar_International_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guitar_International-9">[9]</a></sup> This "Mark Two" line-up featuring rock singer <a href="/wiki/Ian_Gillan" title="Ian Gillan">Ian Gillan</a> lasted until mid-1973, producing four studio albums, and two live albums. During this period, the band's songs primarily came out of their jam sessions, so songwriting credits were shared by the five members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Quietus_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Quietus-6">[6]</a></sup> Blackmore later stated, "I didn't give a damn about song construction. I just wanted to make as much noise and play as fast and as loud as possible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-When_There.27s_Smoke.._THERE.27S_FIRE.21_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-When_There.27s_Smoke.._THERE.27S_FIRE.21-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The third line-up featured <a href="/wiki/David_Coverdale" title="David Coverdale">David Coverdale</a> on vocals. This "Mark Three" line-up lasted until mid-1975 and produced two studio albums. Blackmore quit the band to front a new group, <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_(rock_band)" title="Rainbow (rock band)">Rainbow</a>. In 1974, Blackmore took cello lessons from <a href="/wiki/Hugh_McDowell" title="Hugh McDowell">Hugh McDowell</a> of (<a href="/wiki/Electric_Light_Orchestra" title="Electric Light Orchestra">ELO</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Blackmore later stated that when playing a different musical instrument, he found it refreshing because there is a sense of adventure not knowing exactly what chord he's playing or what key he is in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guitar_International_Magazine_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guitar_International_Magazine-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Blackmore originally planned to make a solo album, but instead in 1975 formed his own band, Ritchie Blackmore's Rainbow, later shortened to <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_(rock_band)" title="Rainbow (rock band)">Rainbow</a>. Featuring vocalist <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_James_Dio" title="Ronnie James Dio">Ronnie James Dio</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Blues_rock" title="Blues rock">blues rock</a> backing band <a href="/wiki/Elf_(band)" title="Elf (band)">Elf</a> as studio musicians, this first line-up never performed live. The band's debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Ritchie_Blackmore%27s_Rainbow" title="Ritchie Blackmore's Rainbow">Ritchie Blackmore's Rainbow</a></i>, was released in 1975. Rainbow was originally thought to be a one-off collaboration, but endured as an ongoing band project with a series of album releases and tours. Rainbow's music was partly inspired by elements of <a href="/wiki/Medieval_music" title="Medieval music">medieval</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">baroque music</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guitar_International_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guitar_International-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> since Blackmore started to play cello for musical composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-When_There.27s_Smoke.._THERE.27S_FIRE.21_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-When_There.27s_Smoke.._THERE.27S_FIRE.21-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guitar_International_Magazine_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guitar_International_Magazine-20">[20]</a></sup> During this period, Blackmore wrote a lot of Dio's vocal melodies, particularly on their debut album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burrn.21_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burrn.21-23">[23]</a></sup> Shortly after the first album was recorded, Blackmore recruited new backing musicians to record the second album <i><a href="/wiki/Rising_(Rainbow_album)" title="Rising (Rainbow album)">Rising</a></i> (1976), and the following live album, <i><a href="/wiki/On_Stage_(Rainbow_album)" title="On Stage (Rainbow album)">On Stage</a></i> (1977). <i>Rising</i> was originally billed as "Blackmore's Rainbow" in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discogs_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discogs-24">[24]</a></sup> After the next studio album's release and supporting tour in 1978, Dio left Rainbow due to "creative differences" with Blackmore, who disliked Dio's fantasy oriented lyric style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Blackmore continued with Rainbow, and in 1979 the band released a new album entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Down_to_Earth_(Rainbow_album)" title="Down to Earth (Rainbow album)">Down To Earth</a></i>, which featured R&amp;B singer <a href="/wiki/Graham_Bonnet" title="Graham Bonnet">Graham Bonnet</a>. During song composition, Bonnet made his vocal melodies though it was uncredited contributions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The album marked the commercialisation of the band's sound, and contained Rainbow's first chart successes, as the single "<a href="/wiki/Since_You_Been_Gone" title="Since You Been Gone">Since You Been Gone</a>" (a cover of the <a href="/wiki/Russ_Ballard" title="Russ Ballard">Russ Ballard</a> penned tune) became a smash hit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frame_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-frame-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ritchie_Blackmore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ritchie_Blackmore_(1985).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="472" data-file-width="241" height="353" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Ritchie_Blackmore_%281985%29.jpg/180px-Ritchie_Blackmore_%281985%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a7/Ritchie_Blackmore_%281985%29.jpg 1.5x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ritchie_Blackmore_(1985).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
In San Francisco, 1985</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The next Rainbow album, <i><a href="/wiki/Difficult_to_Cure" title="Difficult to Cure">Difficult to Cure</a></i> (1981), introduced melodic vocalist <a href="/wiki/Joe_Lynn_Turner" title="Joe Lynn Turner">Joe Lynn Turner</a>. The instrumental title track from this album was an arrangement of <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._9_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 9 (Beethoven)">Beethoven's Ninth Symphony</a> with additional music. Blackmore once said, "I found the blues too limiting, and classical was too disciplined. I was always stuck in a musical no man's land."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RStone_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-RStone-8">[8]</a></sup> The album marked the further commercialisation of the band's sound with Blackmore describing at the time a liking for the AOR band, <a href="/wiki/Foreigner_(band)" title="Foreigner (band)">Foreigner</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The music was consciously radio-targeted in a more <a href="/wiki/Album-oriented_rock" title="Album-oriented rock">AOR</a> style,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stranger_in_Us_All_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stranger_in_Us_All-29">[29]</a></sup> resulting in some degree of alienation with many of Rainbow's earlier fans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rainbow_bio_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rainbow_bio-7">[7]</a></sup> Rainbow's next studio album was <i><a href="/wiki/Straight_Between_the_Eyes" title="Straight Between the Eyes">Straight Between the Eyes</a></i> (1982) and included the hit single "Stone Cold." It would be followed by the album <i><a href="/wiki/Bent_Out_of_Shape" title="Bent Out of Shape">Bent Out of Shape</a></i> (1983), which featured the single "<a href="/wiki/Street_of_Dreams_(Rainbow_song)" title="Street of Dreams (Rainbow song)">Street of Dreams</a>". In 1983, Rainbow was also nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> for the Blackmore-penned instrumental ballad track "Anybody There".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Official_Blackmore.27s_Night_website_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Official_Blackmore.27s_Night_website-30">[30]</a></sup> Rainbow disbanded in 1984. A then-final Rainbow album, <i><a href="/wiki/Finyl_Vinyl" title="Finyl Vinyl">Finyl Vinyl</a></i>, was patched together from live tracks and the B-sides of various singles.</p>
<p>In 1984, Blackmore joined a reunion of the former Deep Purple "Mark Two" line-up and recorded new material. This reunion line-up lasted until 1989, producing two studio albums and one live album. However, the reunion's second studio album <i><a href="/wiki/The_House_of_Blue_Light" title="The House of Blue Light">The House of Blue Light</a></i> (1987) displayed a sound that was closer to Rainbow's music. The album's musical style differed from the traditional Purple sound due to Blackmore's Rainbow background, which distinguished him from the other members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_House_of_Blue_Light_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_House_of_Blue_Light-31">[31]</a></sup> During the 1987–1988 tour, Blackmore was reluctant to play "Smoke on the Water",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and singer <a href="/wiki/Ian_Gillan" title="Ian Gillan">Ian Gillan</a> apologised for his vocal range, which had become weaker than audiences expected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ritchie_Blackmore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The next line-up recorded one album entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Slaves_and_Masters" title="Slaves and Masters">Slaves and Masters</a></i> (1990), which featured former Rainbow vocalist Joe Lynn Turner. During song composition, Turner wrote his vocal melodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-When_There.27s_Smoke.._THERE.27S_FIRE.21_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-When_There.27s_Smoke.._THERE.27S_FIRE.21-18">[18]</a></sup> Subsequently, the "Mark Two" line-up reunited for a second time in late 1992 and produced one studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Battle_Rages_On..." title="The Battle Rages On...">The Battle Rages On...</a></i>. Overall, the traditional Purple sound returned, but the guitar riffs sometimes sounded like generic <a href="/wiki/Def_Leppard" title="Def Leppard">Def Leppard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> During the follow-up promotional tour, Blackmore quit the band for good in November 1993. Prominent guitarist <a href="/wiki/Joe_Satriani" title="Joe Satriani">Joe Satriani</a> was brought in to complete the remaining tour dates.</p>
<p>Blackmore reformed Rainbow with new members in 1994. This Rainbow line-up, featuring hard rock singer <a href="/wiki/Doogie_White" title="Doogie White">Doogie White</a>, lasted until 1997 and produced one album entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Stranger_in_Us_All" title="Stranger in Us All">Stranger in Us All</a></i> in 1995. It was originally intended to be a solo album but due to the record company pressures the record was billed as Ritchie Blackmore's Rainbow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackmore.27s_Night_biography_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blackmore.27s_Night_biography-35">[35]</a></sup> Though Doogie White wasn't as distinctive as previous Rainbow singers, the album had a sound dissimilar to any Rainbow of old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stranger_in_Us_All_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stranger_in_Us_All-29">[29]</a></sup> This was Rainbow's eighth studio album, made after a gap of 12 years since <i>Bent Out of Shape</i>, and is regarded as Blackmore's last hard rock album. A world tour including South America followed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Official_Blackmore.27s_Night_website_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Official_Blackmore.27s_Night_website-30">[30]</a></sup> Rainbow was disbanded once again after playing its final concert in 1997. Blackmore later said, "I didn't want to tour very much."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Play_Minstrel_Play_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Play_Minstrel_Play-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over the years Rainbow went through many personnel changes with no two studio albums featuring the same line-up: Blackmore was the sole constant band member.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frame_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-frame-27">[27]</a></sup> Rainbow achieved modest success; the band's worldwide sales are estimated at more than 28 million album copies, including 4 million copies sold in the US<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1997 Blackmore, with his girlfriend <a href="/wiki/Candice_Night" title="Candice Night">Candice Night</a> as vocalist, formed the traditional <a href="/wiki/Folk_rock" title="Folk rock">folk rock</a> duo <a href="/wiki/Blackmore%27s_Night" title="Blackmore's Night">Blackmore's Night</a>. From about 1995, they were already working on their debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Shadow_of_the_Moon" title="Shadow of the Moon">Shadow of the Moon</a></i> (1997).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stranger_in_Us_All_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stranger_in_Us_All-29">[29]</a></sup> Blackmore once portrayed their artistic characteristics as "<a href="/wiki/Mike_Oldfield" title="Mike Oldfield">Mike Oldfield</a> plus <a href="/wiki/Enya" title="Enya">Enya</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackmore.27s_Night_biography_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blackmore.27s_Night_biography-35">[35]</a></sup> Blackmore mostly used acoustic guitar,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackmore.27s_Night_biography_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-Blackmore.27s_Night_biography-35">[35]</a></sup> to back Night's delicate vocal melodies, which he wrote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Night said, "When he sings, he sings only for me, in private".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> As a result, his musical approach shifted to vocalist-centered sounds. They recorded a mixture of original and cover materials. The band's musical style is inspired by <a href="/wiki/Medieval_music" title="Medieval music">medieval music</a> and it blended with Night's lyrics about love's themes. The second release, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Under_a_Violet_Moon" title="Under a Violet Moon">Under a Violet Moon</a></i> (1999) continued in the same folk-rock style, with Night's vocals remaining a prominent feature of the band's style. The title track's lyrics were partly written by Blackmore. "Violet" was his mother's first name and "Moon" was his grandmother's surname.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Play_Minstrel_Play_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Play_Minstrel_Play-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s.E2.80.93present">2000s–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ritchie_Blackmore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2000s–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In subsequent albums, particularly <i><a href="/wiki/Fires_at_Midnight" title="Fires at Midnight">Fires at Midnight</a></i> (2001) which featured the <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a> cover "<a href="/wiki/The_Times_They_Are_a-Changin%27_(song)" title="The Times They Are a-Changin' (song)">The Times They Are a Changin'</a>," there was occasionally an increased incorporation of electric guitar into the music, whilst maintaining a <a href="/wiki/Folk_rock" title="Folk rock">folk rock</a> direction. A live album, <i><a href="/wiki/Past_Times_with_Good_Company" title="Past Times with Good Company">Past Times with Good Company</a></i> was released in 2002. After the next studio album's release, an official compilation album <i><a href="/wiki/Beyond_the_Sunset:_The_Romantic_Collection" title="Beyond the Sunset: The Romantic Collection">Beyond the Sunset: The Romantic Collection</a></i> was released in 2004, featuring music from the four studio albums. A Christmas-themed holiday album, <i><a href="/wiki/Winter_Carols" title="Winter Carols">Winter Carols</a></i> was released in 2006. Through numerous personnel changes, the backing musicians have totalled 26 persons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Blackmore sometimes played drums in recording studio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Play_Minstrel_Play_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-Play_Minstrel_Play-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SpotonLI_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-SpotonLI-41">[41]</a></sup> They choose to avoid typical rock concert tours, instead limiting their appearances to small intimate venues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In 2011, Night said, "We have actually turned down a lot of (touring) opportunities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Blackmore continued to write her vocal melodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guitar_International_Magazine_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guitar_International_Magazine-20">[20]</a></sup> To date they have released eight studio albums.</p>
<p>A reformed Rainbow performed three European concerts in June 2016. The concert setlists included both <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_(rock_band)" title="Rainbow (rock band)">Rainbow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deep_Purple" title="Deep Purple">Deep Purple</a> material. The band featured metal singer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ronnie_Romero&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ronnie Romero (page does not exist)">Ronnie Romero</a>, keyboardist Jens Johansson, drummer David Keith and bassist Bob Nouveau. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equipment">Equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ritchie_Blackmore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the 1960s, Blackmore played a <a href="/wiki/Gibson_ES-335" title="Gibson ES-335">Gibson ES-335</a> but from 1970 he mainly played a <a href="/wiki/Fender_Stratocaster" title="Fender Stratocaster">Fender Stratocaster</a> until he formed Blackmore's Night in 1997. The middle <a href="/wiki/Pickup_(music_technology)" title="Pickup (music technology)">pick-up</a> on his Stratocaster is screwed down and not used. Blackmore occasionally used a <a href="/wiki/Fender_Telecaster_Thinline" title="Fender Telecaster Thinline">Fender Telecaster Thinline</a> during recording sessions. He is also one of the first rock guitarists to use a <a href="/wiki/Fingerboard#Scalloping" title="Fingerboard">"scalloped"</a> fretboard which has a "U" shape between the frets.</p>
<p>In his soloing, Blackmore combines blues scales and phrasing with dominant minor scales and ideas from European classical music. While playing he would often put the pick in his mouth, playing <a href="/wiki/Fingerstyle_guitar" title="Fingerstyle guitar">with his fingers</a>. He occasionally uses the diatonic scale, with rapidly changing tonality.</p>
<p>In the 1970s, Blackmore used a number of different Stratocasters; one of his main guitars was an Olympic white 1974 model with a rosewood fingerboard that was scalloped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-munich_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-munich-45">[45]</a></sup> Blackmore added a strap lock to the headstock of this guitar as a conversation piece to annoy and confuse people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>His amplifiers were originally 200-Watt <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Major" title="Marshall Major">Marshall Major</a> stacks which were modified by Marshall with an additional output stage (generated approximately 27Db) to make them sound more like Blackmore's favourite <a href="/wiki/Vox_AC30" title="Vox AC30">Vox AC30</a> amp cranked to full volume. Since 1994, he has used <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=ENGL&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="ENGL (page does not exist)">ENGL</a> valve amps.</p>
<p>Effects he used from 1970 to 1997, besides his usual tape echo, included a Hornby Skewes <a href="/wiki/Treble_booster" title="Treble booster">treble booster</a> in the early days. Around late-1973, he experimented with an EMS Synthi Hi Fli guitar synthesizer. He sometimes used a <a href="/wiki/Wah-wah_pedal" title="Wah-wah pedal">wah-wah pedal</a> and a variable control treble-booster for sustain, and <a href="/wiki/Moog_Taurus" title="Moog Taurus">Moog Taurus</a> bass pedals were used in solo parts during concerts. He also had a modified Aiwa TP-1011 tape machine built to supply echo and delay effects; the tape deck was also used as a pre-amp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-munich_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-munich-45">[45]</a></sup> Other effects that Blackmore used were a <a href="/wiki/Uni-Vibe" title="Uni-Vibe">Uni-Vibe</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Fuzz_Face" title="Fuzz Face">Dallas Arbiter Fuzz Face</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Octave_effect" title="Octave effect">Octave Divider</a>.</p>
<p>In the mid-1980s he experimented with <a href="/wiki/Roland_Corporation" title="Roland Corporation">Roland</a> guitar synthesizers. A <a href="/wiki/Roland_Corporation" title="Roland Corporation">Roland GR-700</a> was seen on stage as late as 1995–96, later replaced with the GR-50.</p>
<p>Blackmore has experimented with many different pick-ups in his Strats. In the early Rainbow era, they were still stock Fenders, later Dawk installed over wound, dipped, Fender pick-ups. He has also used <a href="/wiki/Schecter_Guitar_Research" title="Schecter Guitar Research">Schecter</a> F-500-Ts, Velvet Hammer "Red Rhodes", <a href="/wiki/DiMarzio" title="DiMarzio">DiMarzio</a> "HS-2", OBL "Black Label", Bill Lawrence L-450, XL-250 (bridge), L-250 (neck). In his signature stratocaster Seymour Duncan Quarter Pound Flat SSL-4's are used to emulate the <a href="/wiki/Schecter_Guitar_Research" title="Schecter Guitar Research">Schecter Guitar Research</a> F500ts and since the early 90s, he has used <a href="/wiki/Lace_Sensor" title="Lace Sensor">Lace Sensor</a> (Gold) "noiseless" pick-ups.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_tastes">Musical tastes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ritchie_Blackmore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Musical tastes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1979,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sounds.2C_15_December_1979_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sounds.2C_15_December_1979-12">[12]</a></sup> Blackmore said: "I like popular music. I like <a href="/wiki/ABBA" title="ABBA">ABBA</a> very much. But there's so much stigma like, 'you can't do this because you're a heavy band', and I think that's rubbish. You should do what you want ... I think classical music is very good for the soul. A lot of people go 'ah well, classical music is for old fogies' but I was exactly the same. At 16 I didn't want to know about classical music: I'd had it rammed down my throat. But now I feel an obligation to tell the kids 'look, just give classical music a chance' ... the guitar frustrates me a lot because I'm not good enough to play it sometimes so I get mad and throw a moody. Sometimes I feel that what I'm doing is not right, in the sense that the whole rock and roll business has become a farce, like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billy_Smart,_Jr." title="Billy Smart, Jr.">Billy Smart, Jr.</a> Circus, and the only music that ever moves me is very disciplined classical music, which I can't play. But there's a reason I've made money. It's because I believe in what I'm doing, in that I do it my way—I play for myself first, then secondly the audience—I try to put as much as I can in it for them. Lastly I play for musicians and the band, and for critics not at all."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ritchie_Blackmore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In May 1964, Blackmore married Margit Volkmar (b. 1945) from Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> They lived in <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> during the late 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Between_Us_June_2004_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Between_Us_June_2004-48">[48]</a></sup> Their son, Jürgen (b. 1964), played guitar in touring tribute band <a href="/wiki/Over_the_Rainbow_(band)" title="Over the Rainbow (band)">Over the Rainbow</a>. Following their divorce, Blackmore married Bärbel, a former dancer from Germany, in September 1969<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> until their divorce in early 1970s. As a result, he is a fluent German speaker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Between_Us_June_2004_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Between_Us_June_2004-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>For tax reasons, he moved to the USA in 1974. Initially he lived in <a href="/wiki/Oxnard,_California" title="Oxnard, California">Oxnard, California</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guitar_International_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Guitar_International-9">[9]</a></sup> with opera singer Shoshana Feinstein for one year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> She provided backing vocals on two songs in Rainbow's first album. During this period, he listened to early European classical music and <a href="/wiki/Light_music" title="Light music">light music</a> a lot, for about three-quarters of his private time. Blackmore once said, "It's hard to relate that to rock. I listen very carefully to the patterns that <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Bach</a> plays. I like direct, dramatic music."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guitar_International_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Guitar_International-9">[9]</a></sup> After having an affair with another woman, Christine, Blackmore met Amy Rothman in 1978,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> and moved to <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> He married Rothman in 1981,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> but they divorced in 1983. Following the marriage's conclusion, he began a relationship with Tammi Williams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> In early 1984 Blackmore met Williams in <a href="/wiki/Chattanooga,_Tennessee" title="Chattanooga, Tennessee">Chattanooga, Tennessee</a>, where she was working as a hotel employee. In the same year, he purchased his first car, having learnt to drive at 39 years of age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Blackmore and then-fashion model <a href="/wiki/Candice_Night" title="Candice Night">Candice Night</a> began living together in 1991. They moved to her native <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a> in 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (August 2015)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> Having been engaged for nearly fifteen years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> the couple married in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Night said, "he's making me younger and I'm aging him rapidly."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Their daughter Autumn was born on 27 May 2010,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russell_A._Trunk_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Russell_A._Trunk-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> and their son Rory on 7 February 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burrn.21_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burrn.21-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SpotonLI_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-SpotonLI-41">[41]</a></sup> They also keep two cats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (November 2015)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> Blackmore is a heavy drinker,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burrn.21_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Burrn.21-23">[23]</a></sup> plays football once a week,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SpotonLI_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-SpotonLI-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (November 2015)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> and watches German language television on his satellite dish when he is at home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Between_Us_June_2004_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-Between_Us_June_2004-48">[48]</a></sup> He has many German friends<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Between_Us_June_2004_48-3"><a href="#cite_note-Between_Us_June_2004-48">[48]</a></sup> and a collection of about 2,000 CDs of <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_music" title="Renaissance music">Renaissance music</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Between_Us_June_2004_48-4"><a href="#cite_note-Between_Us_June_2004-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russell_A._Trunk_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-Russell_A._Trunk-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ritchie_Blackmore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite completely retiring from hard rock, Blackmore was ranked number 16 on <i><a href="/wiki/Guitar_World" title="Guitar World">Guitar World</a>'</i>s "100 Greatest Metal Guitarists of All Time" in 2004,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> and number 50 in <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine's list of the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone%27s_100_greatest_guitarists_of_all_time" title="Rolling Stone's 100 greatest guitarists of all time">100 Greatest Guitarists of All Time</a>" in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RStone_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-RStone-8">[8]</a></sup> Readers of <i>Guitar World</i> also voted two of Blackmore's guitar solos (both recorded with Deep Purple) among the 100 Greatest Guitar Solos of all time. ("Highway Star" ranked 19th, and "Lazy" ranked 74th.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> On 8 April 2016, he was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">Rock and Roll Hall of Fame</a> as one of original members of Deep Purple; however, he did not attend the ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1993, Musicologist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Walser_(musicologist)" title="Robert Walser (musicologist)">Robert Walser</a> defined him "the most important musician of the emerging <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_metal" title="Neoclassical metal">metal/classical fusion</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> He is also credited as a precursor of the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Shred_guitar" title="Shred guitar">guitar shredders</a>" that emerged in the mid-1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>Blackmore has been an influence on various guitarists such as <a href="/wiki/Fredrik_%C3%85kesson" title="Fredrik Åkesson">Fredrik Åkesson</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Brett_Garsed" title="Brett Garsed">Brett Garsed</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Janick_Gers" title="Janick Gers">Janick Gers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gilbert" title="Paul Gilbert">Paul Gilbert</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Craig_Goldy" title="Craig Goldy">Craig Goldy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Scott_Henderson" title="Scott Henderson">Scott Henderson</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dave_Meniketti" title="Dave Meniketti">Dave Meniketti</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Randy_Rhoads" title="Randy Rhoads">Randy Rhoads</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Romeo" title="Michael Romeo">Michael Romeo</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wolf_Hoffmann" title="Wolf Hoffmann">Wolf Hoffmann</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lita_Ford" title="Lita Ford">Lita Ford</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Brian_May" title="Brian May">Brian May</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Yngwie_Malmsteen" title="Yngwie Malmsteen">Yngwie Malmsteen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Mathew_Baynton" title="Mathew Baynton">Mathew Baynton</a> in the 2009 film <i><a href="/wiki/Telstar:_The_Joe_Meek_Story" title="Telstar: The Joe Meek Story">Telstar</a></i>.</p>
