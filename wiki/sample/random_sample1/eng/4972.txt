<p><b>Richmond</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="/tʃ/ 'ch' in 'china'">tʃ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a city in east central <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.A.</a>, bordering on <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>. It is the <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">county seat</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wayne_County,_Indiana" title="Wayne County, Indiana">Wayne County</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR6_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR6-6">[6]</a></sup> and in the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 census</a> had a population of 36,812.</p>
<p>Situated largely within <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Township,_Wayne_County,_Indiana" title="Wayne Township, Wayne County, Indiana">Wayne Township</a>, its area includes a non-contiguous portion in nearby <a href="/wiki/Boston_Township,_Wayne_County,_Indiana" title="Boston Township, Wayne County, Indiana">Boston Township</a> where the Richmond Municipal Airport is located.</p>
<p>The city is sometimes called the "cradle of recorded jazz" because some early <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> records were made there at the studio of <a href="/wiki/Gennett_Records" title="Gennett Records">Gennett Records</a>, a division of the Starr Piano Company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city has twice received the <a href="/wiki/All-America_City_Award" title="All-America City Award">All-America City Award</a>, most recently in 2009.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Indiana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Richmond is located at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Richmond%2C_Indiana&amp;params=39.830189_N_-84.890668_E_type:city_region:US"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">39°49′49″N</span> <span class="longitude">84°53′26″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">39.830189°N 84.890668°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">39.830189; -84.890668</span></span></span></a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the 2010 census, Richmond has a total area of 24.067 square miles (62.33 km<sup>2</sup>), of which 23.91 square miles (61.93 km<sup>2</sup>) (or 99.35%) is land and 0.157 square miles (0.41 km<sup>2</sup>) (or 0.65%) is water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-census-g001_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-census-g001-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_census">2010 census</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Indiana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2010 census">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FactFinder_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FactFinder-3">[3]</a></sup> of 2010, there were 36,812 people, 15,098 households, and 8,909 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 1,539.0 inhabitants per square mile (594.2/km<sup>2</sup>). There were 17,649 housing units at an average density of 737.8 per square mile (284.9/km<sup>2</sup>). The racial makeup of the city was 83.9% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 8.6% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 0.3% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 1.1% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.1% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(U.S._Census)" title="Race (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 1.9% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(U.S._Census)" title="Race (U.S. Census)">other races</a>, and 4.0% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 4.1% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 15,098 households of which 28.8% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 37.5% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 16.3% had a female householder with no husband present, 5.3% had a male householder with no wife present, and 41.0% were non-families. 34.2% of all households were made up of individuals and 13.5% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.29 and the average family size was 2.91.</p>
<p>The median age in the city was 38.4 years. 22.1% of residents were under the age of 18; 11.4% were between the ages of 18 and 24; 24.4% were from 25 to 44; 25.6% were from 45 to 64; and 16.5% were 65 years of age or older. The gender makeup of the city was 47.9% male and 52.1% female.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000_census">2000 census</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Indiana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2000 census">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR2_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-5">[5]</a></sup> of 2000, there were 39,124 people, 16,287 households, and 9,918 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 1,685.3 people per square mile (650.8/km²). There were 17,647 housing units at an average density of 760.2 per square mile (293.6/km²). The racial makeup of the city was 86.78% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 8.87% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 0.27% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 0.80% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.06% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 1.09% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 2.14% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 2.03% of the population.</p>

<p>There were 16,287 households out of which 27.8% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 43.1% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 13.9% had a female householder with no husband present, and 39.1% were non-families. 33.0% of all households were made up of individuals and 13.7% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.29 and the average family size was 2.89.</p>
<p>In the city the population was spread out with 23.4% under the age of 18, 11.0% from 18 to 24, 27.5% from 25 to 44, 21.6% from 45 to 64, and 16.4% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 36 years. For every 100 females there were 88.7 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 84.2 males.</p>
<p>The median income for a household in the city was $30,210, and the median income for a family was $38,346. Males had a median income of $30,849 versus $21,164 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $17,096. About 12.1% of families and 15.7% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 22.8% of those under age 18 and 10.8% of those age 65 or over.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Indiana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1806 the first European Americans in the area, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaker" title="Quaker">Quaker</a> families from <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, settled along the East Fork of the <a href="/wiki/Whitewater_River_(Great_Miami_River)" title="Whitewater River (Great Miami River)">Whitewater River</a>. This was part of a general westward migration in the early decades after the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>. John Smith and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Hoover" title="David Hoover">David Hoover</a> were among the earliest settlers. Richmond is still home to several Quaker institutions, including <a href="/wiki/Friends_United_Meeting" title="Friends United Meeting">Friends United Meeting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earlham_College" title="Earlham College">Earlham College</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Earlham_School_of_Religion" title="Earlham School of Religion">Earlham School of Religion</a>.</p>
<p>The settlement was incorporated as a town on Sept. 1, 1818. At that time, it had 24 adult settlers. The first post office in Richmond was established in 1818.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early cinema and television pioneer <a href="/wiki/Charles_Francis_Jenkins" title="Charles Francis Jenkins">Charles Francis Jenkins</a> grew-up on a farm north of Richmond, where he began inventing useful gadgets. As the Richmond Telegram reported, on June 6, 1894, Jenkins gathered his family, friends and newsmen at Jenkins' cousin's jewelry store in downtown Richmond and projected a filmed motion picture for the first time in front of an audience. The motion picture was of a vaudeville entertainer performing a butterfly dance, which Jenkins had filmed himself. Jenkins filed for a patent for the Phantoscope projector in November 1894 and it was issued in March of '95. A modified version of the Phantoscope was later sold to <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a> who named it <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Edison%27s_Vitascope&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Edison's Vitascope (page does not exist)">Edison's Vitascope</a> and began projecting motion pictures in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> <a href="/wiki/Vaudeville" title="Vaudeville">vaudeville</a> theaters, raising the curtain on American cinema.</p>
<p>Richmond is believed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (July 2011)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> to have been the smallest community in the United States to have supported a professional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opera_company" title="Opera company">opera company</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symphony_orchestra" title="Symphony orchestra">symphony orchestra</a>. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Whitewater_Opera&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Whitewater Opera (page does not exist)">Whitewater Opera</a> has since closed but the Richmond Symphony Orchestra has continued. In 1899 <a href="/wiki/Will_Earhart" title="Will Earhart">Will Earhart</a> formed the first complete high school orchestra in the nation. A later high school orchestra director, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_E._Maddy" title="Joseph E. Maddy">Joseph E. Maddy</a>, went on to found what is now known as the <a href="/wiki/Interlochen_Center_for_the_Arts" title="Interlochen Center for the Arts">Interlochen Center for the Arts</a> in Michigan.</p>
<p>In the 1920s during the national revival of the <a href="/wiki/Ku_Klux_Klan" title="Ku Klux Klan">Ku Klux Klan</a> (KKK), Indiana had the largest Klan organization in the country, led by Grand Dragons <a href="/wiki/D._C._Stephenson" title="D. C. Stephenson">D. C. Stephenson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walter_F._Bossert" title="Walter F. Bossert">Walter F. Bossert</a>. At its height, national membership during the second Klan movement reached 1.5 million, with 300,000 from Indiana. Records show that Richmond (home to Whitewater Klan #60) and Wayne County were Klan strongholds, with up to 45 percent of the county's white males having been Klan members. Forty percent of Richmond's <a href="/wiki/Kiwanis" title="Kiwanis">Kiwanis</a> club members, thirty percent of its doctors, and 27 percent of its lawyers were Klan members, but none of the city's bank executives or most powerful business leaders were members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-state_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-state-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1923 a reported 30,000 people watched a Klan parade through Richmond streets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1922, Robert Lyons introduced the Klan in Richmond, initially by recruiting at Reid Memorial Presbyterian Church where his father had been pastor until his death seven years earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The Klan polished its reputation by making contributions of money and goods to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> churches and organizations, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salvation_Army" title="Salvation Army">Salvation Army</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Thomas Barr, son of <a href="/wiki/Daisy_Douglas_Barr" title="Daisy Douglas Barr">Daisy Douglas Barr</a>, nationally prominent Quaker minister and Klan official,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> attended Earlham College and was a KKK campus recruiter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also notable was the fact that <a href="/wiki/Hoagy_Carmichael" title="Hoagy Carmichael">Hoagy Carmichael</a> recorded "<a href="/wiki/Stardust_(song)" title="Stardust (song)">Stardust</a>" for the first time in Richmond at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gennett" title="Gennett">Gennett</a> recording studio. Famed trumpeter and singer <a href="/wiki/Louis_Armstrong" title="Louis Armstrong">Louis Armstrong</a> was first recorded at Gennett as a member of <a href="/wiki/King_Oliver" title="King Oliver">King Oliver</a> and his Creole Jazz Band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>A group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artists" title="Artists">artists</a> in the area in the late 19th and early 20th centuries came to be known as the <a href="/wiki/Richmond_Group" title="Richmond Group">Richmond Group</a>. They included <a href="/wiki/John_Elwood_Bundy" title="John Elwood Bundy">John Elwood Bundy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Conner" title="Charles Conner">Charles Conner</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Herbert_Baker" title="George Herbert Baker">George Herbert Baker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maude_Kaufman_Eggemeyer" title="Maude Kaufman Eggemeyer">Maude Kaufman Eggemeyer</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Albert_Seaford&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Albert Seaford (page does not exist)">John Albert Seaford</a>, among others. The <a href="/wiki/Richmond_Art_Museum" title="Richmond Art Museum">Richmond Art Museum</a> has a collection of regional and American art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Many consider the most significant painting in the collection to be a self-portrait of Indiana-born <a href="/wiki/William_Merritt_Chase" title="William Merritt Chase">William Merritt Chase</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city was connected to the <a href="/wiki/National_Road" title="National Road">National Road</a>, the first road built by the federal government and a major route west for pioneers of the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> It became part of the system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Auto_Trail" title="National Auto Trail">National Auto Trails</a>. The highway is now known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Route_40_in_Indiana" title="U.S. Route 40 in Indiana">U.S. Route 40</a>. One of the extant <a href="/wiki/Madonna_of_the_Trail" title="Madonna of the Trail">Madonna of the Trail</a> monuments was dedicated at Richmond on October 28, 1928<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The monument sits in a corner of Glen Miller Park adjacent to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Route_40_in_Indiana" title="U.S. Route 40 in Indiana">US 40</a>.</p>
<p>Richmond's cultural resources include two of Indiana's three <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egyptian</a> <a href="/wiki/Mummy" title="Mummy">mummies</a>. One is held by the Wayne County Historical Museum and the second by Earlham College's Joseph Moore Museum, leading to the local nickname of "Mummy capital of Indiana".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The arts were supported by a strong economy increasingly based on manufacturing. Richmond was once known as "the lawn mower capital" because it was a center for manufacturing of <a href="/wiki/Lawn_mower" title="Lawn mower">lawn mowers</a> from the late 19th century through the mid-20th century. Manufacturers included Davis, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Motomower&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Motomower (page does not exist)">Motomower</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dille-McGuire&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dille-McGuire (page does not exist)">Dille-McGuire</a> and F&amp;N. The farm machinery builder <a href="/wiki/Gaar-Scott" title="Gaar-Scott">Gaar-Scott</a> was based in Richmond. The <a href="/wiki/Davis_Aircraft" title="Davis Aircraft">Davis Aircraft Co.</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> builder of a light <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasol_wing" title="Parasol wing">parasol wing</a> monoplane, operated in Richmond beginning in 1929.</p>
<p>After starting out in nearby <a href="/wiki/Union_City,_Indiana" title="Union City, Indiana">Union City</a>, Wayne Agricultural Works moved to Richmond. Wayne was a manufacturer of horse-drawn vehicles, including "<a href="/wiki/Kid_hack" title="Kid hack">kid hacks</a>", a precursor of the motorized <a href="/wiki/School_bus" title="School bus">school bus</a>. From the early 1930s through the 1940s, several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobile</a> designers and manufacturers were located in Richmond. Among the automobiles locally manufactured were the <a href="/wiki/Richmond_(automobile)" title="Richmond (automobile)">Richmond</a>, built by the Wayne Works; the "Rodefeld"; the <a href="/wiki/George_W._Davis_Motor_Car_Company" title="George W. Davis Motor Car Company">Davis</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Pilot_(automobile)" title="Pilot (automobile)">Pilot</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Westcott_(automobile)" title="Westcott (automobile)">Westcott</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Crosley" title="Crosley">Crosley</a>.</p>
<p>In the 1950s, Wayne Works changed its name to <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Corporation" title="Wayne Corporation">Wayne Corporation</a>, by then a well-known <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">bus</a> and school-bus manufacturer. In 1967 it relocated to a site adjacent to <a href="/wiki/Interstate_70" title="Interstate 70">Interstate 70</a>. The company was a leader in school-bus safety innovations, but it closed in 1992 during a period of school-bus manufacturing industry consolidations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Richmond was known as the "<a href="/wiki/Rose" title="Rose">Rose</a> City" because of the many varieties once grown there by Hill's Roses. The company had several sprawling complexes of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouses</a>, with a total of about 34 acres (140,000 m<sup>2</sup>) under glass. The annual Richmond Rose Festival honored the rose industry and was a popular summer attraction.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="20th-century_challenges">20th-century challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Indiana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 20th-century challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On April 6, 1968, a <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Indiana_explosion" title="Richmond, Indiana explosion">natural gas explosion and fire</a> destroyed or damaged several downtown blocks and killed 41 people; more than 150 were injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The book <i>Death in a Sunny Street</i> is about the event.</p>
<p>In the rebuilding effort, the city closed the main street through downtown to traffic and built the Downtown Promenade in 1972 (expanded in 1978). When studies showed that car traffic helped businesses, the city had the five-block pedestrian mall broken up. It reopened the street to traffic in 1997 as part of an urban revitalization effort.</p>
<p>On March 17, 1999, the 155-year-old family run business, <a href="/wiki/Swayne,_Robinson_and_Company" title="Swayne, Robinson and Company">Swayne, Robinson and Company</a> was destroyed in a fire. The site is now the location of the Wayne County Jail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Indiana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Richmond is noted for its rich stock of historic architecture. In 2003, a book entitled <i>Richmond Indiana: Its Physical Development and Aesthetic Heritage to 1920</i> by <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> architectural historians, Michael and Mary Raddant Tomlan, was published by the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Historical_Society" title="Indiana Historical Society">Indiana Historical Society</a>. Particularly notable buildings are the 1902 <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a> Station designed by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Burnham" title="Daniel Burnham">Daniel H. Burnham</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> and the 1893 <a href="/wiki/Wayne_County_Courthouse_(Indiana)" title="Wayne County Courthouse (Indiana)">Wayne County Court House</a> designed by <a href="/wiki/James_W._McLaughlin" title="James W. McLaughlin">James W. McLaughlin</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a>. Local architects of note include <a href="/wiki/John_A._Hasecoster" title="John A. Hasecoster">John A. Hasecoster</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_S._Kaufman" title="William S. Kaufman">William S. Kaufman</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_O._Yates&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stephen O. Yates (page does not exist)">Stephen O. Yates</a>.</p>
<p>The significance of the architecture has been recognized. Five large districts, such as the Depot District, and several individual buildings are listed in the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historic_American_Buildings_Survey" title="Historic American Buildings Survey">Historic American Buildings Survey</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historic_American_Engineering_Record" title="Historic American Engineering Record">Historic American Engineering Record</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Educational_institutions">Educational institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Indiana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Educational institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Richmond has four colleges: <a href="/wiki/Earlham_College" title="Earlham College">Earlham College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana_University_East" title="Indiana University East">Indiana University East</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivy_Tech_Community_College_of_Indiana" title="Ivy Tech Community College of Indiana">Ivy Tech Community College of Indiana</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Purdue_Polytechnic_Institute" title="Purdue Polytechnic Institute">Purdue Polytechnic Institute - Richmond</a>.</li>
<li>Richmond is home to two <a href="/wiki/Seminary" title="Seminary">seminaries</a>: <a href="/wiki/Earlham_School_of_Religion" title="Earlham School of Religion">Earlham School of Religion</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaker" title="Quaker">Quaker</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Bethany_Theological_Seminary" title="Bethany Theological Seminary">Bethany Theological Seminary</a> (<a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Brethren" title="Church of the Brethren">Church of the Brethren</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Richmond_High_School_(Richmond,_Indiana)" title="Richmond High School (Richmond, Indiana)">Richmond High School</a> includes the <a href="/wiki/Richmond_Art_Museum" title="Richmond Art Museum">Richmond Art Museum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civic_Hall_Performing_Arts_Center" title="Civic Hall Performing Arts Center">Civic Hall Performing Arts Center</a> and the Tiernan Center, the 5th-largest high school gym in the United States.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Seton_Catholic_High_School_(Richmond,_Indiana)" title="Seton Catholic High School (Richmond, Indiana)">Seton Catholic High School</a> (founded 2002), a junior and senior high school, is a religious high school. It is based in the former home of St. Andrew High School (1899–1936) and, more recently, St. Andrew Elementary School, adjacent to St. Andrew Church of the Richmond Catholic Community.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=New_Creations_Christian_School&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="New Creations Christian School (page does not exist)">New Creations Christian School</a>, located on the campus of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=New_Creations_Chapel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="New Creations Chapel (page does not exist)">New Creations Chapel</a>, serves elementary, middle school, and high school students.</li>
</ul>
<p>The Richmond Japanese Language School (リッチモンド（IN）補習授業校 <i>Ritchimondo(IN)Hoshū Jugyō Kō</i>) a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hoshuko" title="Hoshuko">part-time Japanese school</a>, holds its classes at the Highland Heights School.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAList_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAList-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air">Air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Indiana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Richmond_Municipal_Airport" title="Richmond Municipal Airport">Richmond Municipal Airport</a> is a public-use airport located five nautical miles (6 mi, 9 km) southeast of the <a href="/wiki/Central_business_district" title="Central business district">central business district</a> of Richmond. It is owned by the Richmond Board of Aviation Commissioners. It is also an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclave" title="Exclave">exclave</a> of Richmond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Road">Road</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Indiana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Road">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Richmond is served by <a href="/wiki/Interstate_70" title="Interstate 70">Interstate 70</a> at exits 149, 151, 153, and 156. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_transit" title="Public transit">Public transit</a> service is provided by city-owned Roseview Transit, operating daily except Sundays and major holidays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roseview_index_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roseview_index-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Indiana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The daily newspaper is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gannett" title="Gannett">Gannett</a>-owned <i><a href="/wiki/Palladium-Item" title="Palladium-Item">Palladium-Item</a></i>.</p>
<p>Full-power radio stations include <a href="/wiki/WKBV" title="WKBV">WKBV</a>, <a href="/wiki/WFMG" title="WFMG">WFMG</a>, <a href="/wiki/WQLK" title="WQLK">WQLK</a>, <a href="/wiki/WHON" title="WHON">WHON</a>, <a href="/wiki/WKRT" title="WKRT">WKRT</a>, and Earlham College's student-run public radio station <a href="/wiki/WECI" title="WECI">WECI</a>. Richmond is also served by <a href="/wiki/WJYW" title="WJYW">WJYW</a> which is repeated on 94.5 and 97.7. Area <a href="/wiki/NPR" title="NPR">NPR</a> radio stations include <a href="/wiki/WBSH" title="WBSH">WBSH</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hagerstown,_Indiana" title="Hagerstown, Indiana">Hagerstown, Indiana</a> and <a href="/wiki/WMUB" title="WMUB">WMUB</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford,_OH" title="Oxford, OH">Oxford, OH</a>.</p>
<p>Richmond is considered to be within the <a href="/wiki/Dayton,_Ohio" title="Dayton, Ohio">Dayton, Ohio</a>, television market and has one full-power television station, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WKOI" title="WKOI">WKOI</a>, which is affiliated with <a href="/wiki/Trinity_Broadcasting_Network" title="Trinity Broadcasting Network">TBN</a>. The city also has one county-wide <a href="/wiki/Public,_educational,_and_government_access" title="Public, educational, and government access">public, educational, and government access</a> (PEG) <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">cable television</a> station, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Whitewater_Community_Television&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Whitewater Community Television (page does not exist)">Whitewater Community Television</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
