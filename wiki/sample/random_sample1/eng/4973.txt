<p><b>Richmond</b> is a city in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> and the seat of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Bend_County,_Texas" title="Fort Bend County, Texas">Fort Bend County</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR6_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR6-4">[4]</a></sup> within the <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Greater_Houston" title="Greater Houston">Houston–The Woodlands–Sugar Land</a></span> metropolitan area. As of the 2010 U.S. census, the city population was 11,679.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_2010_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Census_2010-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Even though it is the <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">county seat</a>, thus containing most of the <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local government</a> offices, and the historic center of the area, it is actually one of the smaller cities in the area. Nearby <a href="/wiki/Sugar_Land,_Texas" title="Sugar Land, Texas">Sugar Land</a> is the largest city in the county.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1822, a group of Austin's colonists went up the <a href="/wiki/Brazos_River" title="Brazos River">Brazos River</a>, stopping near present-day Richmond where they built a fort called "Fort Bend". Named after <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_London" title="Richmond, London">Richmond, England</a>, the town was among the 19 cities first incorporated by the short-lived <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Texas" title="Republic of Texas">Republic of Texas</a>, in 1837. Early residents of the city include many prominent figures in Texas lore such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jane_Long" title="Jane Long">Jane Long</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deaf_Smith" title="Deaf Smith">Deaf Smith</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mirabeau_Lamar" title="Mirabeau Lamar">Mirabeau Lamar</a>, who are all buried in Richmond. On August 16, 1889, the town was the site of the "Battle of Richmond", an armed fight culminating the <a href="/wiki/Jaybird%E2%80%93Woodpecker_War" title="Jaybird–Woodpecker War">Jaybird–Woodpecker War</a>, a violent feud over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-Reconstruction" title="Post-Reconstruction">post-Reconstruction</a> political control of Fort Bend County. The mayor from 1949 until his death in 2012 was <a href="/wiki/Hilmar_Moore" title="Hilmar Moore">Hilmar Moore</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historically Richmond had government agencies and nonprofit organizations, while most of the area private businesses were located in <a href="/wiki/Rosenberg,_Texas" title="Rosenberg, Texas">Rosenberg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kevermemoriesofhome_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kevermemoriesofhome-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RichmondTXMap.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="425" data-file-width="575" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/RichmondTXMap.gif/220px-RichmondTXMap.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/RichmondTXMap.gif/330px-RichmondTXMap.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/RichmondTXMap.gif/440px-RichmondTXMap.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>

Map of Richmond</div>

<p>Richmond is located near the center of Fort Bend County at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Richmond%2C_Texas&amp;params=29.580921_N_-95.763000_E_type:city_region:US"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">29°34′51″N</span> <span class="longitude">95°45′47″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">29.580921°N 95.763000°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">29.580921; -95.763000</span></span></span></a></span> (29.580921, -95.763000).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR1-9">[9]</a></sup> Most of the city is situated on the southwest side of the <a href="/wiki/Brazos_River" title="Brazos River">Brazos River</a>, with a small portion (Richmond Landing) on the northeast side, connected by <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_90_Alternate_(Texas)" title="U.S. Route 90 Alternate (Texas)">US Highway 90A</a>. Richmond is bordered to the southwest by the city of <a href="/wiki/Rosenberg,_Texas" title="Rosenberg, Texas">Rosenberg</a>. US 90A leads east 8 miles (13 km) to <a href="/wiki/Sugar_Land,_Texas" title="Sugar Land, Texas">Sugar Land</a> and west through Rosenberg 19 miles (31 km) to <a href="/wiki/East_Bernard,_Texas" title="East Bernard, Texas">East Bernard</a>. Downtown <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a> is 30 miles (48 km) to the northeast.</p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city of Richmond has a total area of 4.3 square miles (11.1 km<sup>2</sup>), of which 3.9 square miles (10.2 km<sup>2</sup>) is land and 0.35 square miles (0.9 km<sup>2</sup>), or 8.22%, is water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_2010_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Census_2010-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003 Jeannie Kever of the <i><a href="/wiki/Houston_Chronicle" title="Houston Chronicle">Houston Chronicle</a></i> said, "Some of the old buildings have been reincarnated as shops or law offices. But in other ways, life in Richmond isn't so different from that in the big city, with its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wal-Mart" title="Wal-Mart">Wal-Mart</a> and fast-food joints, check-cashing businesses and strip-center sprawl."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kevermemoriesofhome_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kevermemoriesofhome-8">[8]</a></sup> As of 2006<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Texas&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> several <a href="/wiki/Strip_mall" title="Strip mall">strip malls</a> are along <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_59" title="U.S. Route 59">U.S. Route 59</a> south of town. During the same year the community included tack stores, two-lane blacktop roads, and horse ranches. John P. Lopez of the <i>Houston Chronicle</i> said, "Richmond is a city of contradiction and transition. It's as if the place is not sure if it wants to be a part of Houston's bustle or remain a slow-paced farm and ranch town. It tries to be both," and, "It is part <a href="/wiki/Acres_Homes,_Houston" title="Acres Homes, Houston">Acres Homes</a>, part Fort Bend County Fair."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lopez_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lopez-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Communities">Communities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Communities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>John P. Lopez of the <i>Houston Chronicle</i> said in 2006, "There are a lot of simple scenes. And there are places where reality has polluted neighborhoods."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lopez_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lopez-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The wealthiest neighborhood, as of 2003, in Richmond is Hillcrest. Winston Terrace, another neighborhood, had its first houses built in 1940. Construction increased around the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Most of the houses were built between 1940 and 1965. Jeannie Kever of the <i>Houston Chronicle</i> said that Winston Terrace is "a swath of mid-20th-century America, with sweeping oak trees and colorful brick or wood bungalows, named for the descendants of one of the region's most illustrious pioneers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kevermemoriesofhome_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kevermemoriesofhome-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Mud Alley" as of 1985 had older bars and strip clubs. Mud Alley is located in an area which, in 1985, housed most of the <a href="/wiki/African_Americans" title="African Americans">African Americans</a> in Richmond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> As of 1993 many police raids for drugs occurred in "Mud Alley".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> "Mud Alley" was known by several other nicknames, including "Little Boomtown". Historically the area had a lot of recreational drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lopez_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lopez-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-2">[2]</a></sup> of 2000, there were 11,081 people, 3,413 households, and 2,628 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 2,975.4 people per square mile (1,150.1/km<sup>2</sup>). There were 3,595 housing units at an average density of 965.3 per square mile (373.1/km<sup>2</sup>). The racial makeup of the city was 51.20% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 10.55% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 0.63% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 3.53% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.07% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 31.00% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 3.01% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 58.71% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 3,413 households out of which 39.6% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 53.6% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 16.4% had a female householder with no husband present, and 23.0% were non-families. 18.7% of all households were made up of individuals and 6.7% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 3.16 and the average family size was 3.60.</p>
<p>In the city the population was spread out with 31.6% under the age of 18, 11.0% from 18 to 24, 28.5% from 25 to 44, 18.7% from 45 to 64, and 10.1% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 30 years. For every 100 females there were 98.1 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 95.2 males.</p>
<p>The median income for a household in the city was $34,888, and the median income for a family was $35,801. Males had a median income of $27,457 versus $22,723 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $15,195. About 17.0% of families and 20.0% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 30.4% of those under age 18 and 12.9% of those age 65 or over.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_and_infrastructure">Government and infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Government and infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a> Richmond Post Office is located at 5560 <a href="/wiki/Farm_to_Market_Road_1640" title="Farm to Market Road 1640">Farm to Market Road 1640</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city is governed by a mayor and two commissioners elected at large for two-year terms. The commission sets policy and the day-to-day management is done by a city manager hired by the commission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cityelected_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-cityelected-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hilmar_G._Moore" title="Hilmar G. Moore">Hilmar G. Moore</a> served as mayor from 1949 until his death on December 4, 2012 and was believed to be the longest-serving mayor in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cityhilbio_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-cityhilbio-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChronRichmond_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChronRichmond-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Texas_Department_of_Criminal_Justice" title="Texas Department of Criminal Justice">Texas Department of Criminal Justice</a> operates the <a href="/wiki/Jester_State_Prison_Farm" title="Jester State Prison Farm">Jester State Prison Farm</a> units, including the <a href="/wiki/Jester_I_Unit" title="Jester I Unit">Jester I Unit</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Carol_Vance_Unit" title="Carol Vance Unit">Carol Vance Unit</a> (formerly the Jester II Unit), the <a href="/wiki/Jester_III_Unit" title="Jester III Unit">Jester III Unit</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Jester_IV_Unit" title="Jester IV Unit">Jester IV Unit</a>, in an <a href="/wiki/Unincorporated_area" title="Unincorporated area">unincorporated area</a> 4 miles (6.4 km) east of Richmond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TDCJProfile_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-TDCJProfile-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_schools">Public schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Public schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The corporate city limits of Richmond is served by <a href="/wiki/Lamar_Consolidated_Independent_School_District" title="Lamar Consolidated Independent School District">Lamar Consolidated Independent School District</a>.</p>
<p>Elementary schools within the Richmond city limits include Jane Long,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Pink,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and Deaf Smith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wessendorff Middle School, Lamar Junior High School, and <a href="/wiki/Lamar_Consolidated_High_School" title="Lamar Consolidated High School">Lamar Consolidated High School</a> serve the Richmond city limits. The three schools are in Rosenberg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Various schools operated by LCISD and neighboring <a href="/wiki/Fort_Bend_Independent_School_District" title="Fort Bend Independent School District">Fort Bend Independent School District</a> bear Richmond addresses, but do not serve the city of Richmond. <a href="/wiki/George_Ranch_High_School" title="George Ranch High School">George Ranch High School</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foster_High_School_(Fort_Bend_County,_Texas)" title="Foster High School (Fort Bend County, Texas)">Foster High School</a>, Reading Junior High School, and Briscoe Junior High in LCISD and <a href="/wiki/Travis_High_School_(Fort_Bend_County,_Texas)" title="Travis High School (Fort Bend County, Texas)">Travis High School</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Bush_High_School" title="George Bush High School">Bush High School</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Fort_Bend_Independent_School_District" title="Fort Bend Independent School District">Fort Bend Independent School District</a> (FBISD) bear "Richmond, Texas" addresses.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_libraries">Public libraries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richmond,_Texas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Public libraries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fort_Bend_County_Libraries" title="Fort Bend County Libraries">Fort Bend County Libraries</a> operates the George Memorial Library, the central library and the site of the administrative offices of the library system, located along <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farm_to_Market_Road_762" title="Farm to Market Road 762">Farm to Market Road 762</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GMemorial_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-GMemorial-27">[27]</a></sup> Richmond also has the Fort Bend County Law Library.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lawlibrary_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lawlibrary-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Main Library moved from <a href="/wiki/Rosenberg,_Texas" title="Rosenberg, Texas">Rosenberg</a> to its current location in Richmond in 1986. The George Foundation funded the 77,000 square feet (7,200 m<sup>2</sup>) library facility, designed by Ronald Wedemeyer Associates and built on 6 acres (2.4 ha) of land. When the library opened, it had unfinished areas to facilitate future expansion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GMemorial_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-GMemorial-27">[27]</a></sup> In December 1989 the Commissioners Court of Fort Bend County, in accordance with Texas Local Government code §323.021(a), ordered the construction of a law library.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lawlibrary_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lawlibrary-28">[28]</a></sup> The main library expansion and the 1991 installation of an automation system used funds from the 1989 bond election, and expansion occurred on 1995 and 2002. The 2002 expansion included the addition of two conference rooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GMemorial_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-GMemorial-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
