<p><b>Ricardo Alberto Martinelli Berrocal</b> (born March 11, 1951) is a <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panamanian</a> <a href="/wiki/Politician" title="Politician">politician</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Businessman" title="Businessman">businessman</a> who was the 49th <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/President_of_Panama" title="President of Panama">President of Panama</a> from 2009 to 2014.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ricardo_Martinelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Panama_City" title="Panama City">Panama City</a>, Ricardo Martinelli is the son of Ricardo Martinelli Pardini and Gloria Berrocal Fabrega.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EFE_Bio_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EFE_Bio-1">[1]</a></sup> His father is of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_people" title="Italian people">Italian</a> descent, and his mother is of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_people" title="Spanish people">Spanish</a> descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He completed his <a href="/wiki/Secondary_education" title="Secondary education">secondary education</a> at <a href="/wiki/Staunton_Military_Academy" title="Staunton Military Academy">Staunton Military Academy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Staunton,_Virginia" title="Staunton, Virginia">Staunton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. In 1973 he graduated with a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Business_Administration" title="Bachelor of Business Administration">Bachelor of Business Administration</a> degree from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arkansas" title="University of Arkansas">University of Arkansas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB-3">[3]</a></sup> He earned a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Business_Administration" title="Master of Business Administration">Master of Business Administration</a> degree from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/INCAE" title="INCAE">INCAE</a> Business School in 1977, graduating from its campus in Nicaragua.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_career">Business career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ricardo_Martinelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Business career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Martinelli began his career as a credit officer at Citibank in Panama. After several years of banking, he purchased the business of a client, in turn becoming an entrepreneur, buying or starting additional businesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> His net worth was estimated at $400 million or more, according to press reports. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> stated on his winning the 2009 election, that voters "want him to run the country as well as he manages his businesses." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>He is currently the president and director of the board of <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panamanian</a> supermarket chain Super 99<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EFE_Bio_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-EFE_Bio-1">[1]</a></sup> and of two other companies.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ricardo_Martinelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the presidency of <a href="/wiki/Ernesto_P%C3%A9rez_Balladares" title="Ernesto Pérez Balladares">Ernesto Pérez Balladares</a>, Martinelli served as Director of <a href="/wiki/Social_security" title="Social security">Social Security</a> from 1994 to 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB-3">[3]</a></sup> From September 1999 to January 2003, during the presidency of <a href="/wiki/Mireya_Moscoso" title="Mireya Moscoso">Mireya Moscoso</a>, he served as chairman of the board of directors of the <a href="/wiki/Panama_Canal" title="Panama Canal">Panama Canal</a> and as the Minister for Canal Affairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-EB-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Martinelli is the president of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Change_(Panama)" title="Democratic Change (Panama)">Democratic Change</a> party, which was founded in May 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EFE_Bio_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-EFE_Bio-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-EB-3">[3]</a></sup> He led the party and was the presidential candidate during the <a href="/wiki/Panamanian_general_election,_2004" title="Panamanian general election, 2004">2004 general election</a>, when his party came in last; Martinelli received 5.3% of the vote and came in fourth place in the election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Martinelli was the leader of Democratic Change and presidential candidate in the <a href="/wiki/Panamanian_general_election,_2009" title="Panamanian general election, 2009">2009 general election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EFE_Bio_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-EFE_Bio-1">[1]</a></sup> He ran on a pro-business platform, promising to cut political corruption and reduce violent crime and spent an estimated $35 million on promoting his campaign. By Election Day, Martinelli was the favorite to win the election, with opinion polls giving him a double-digit lead over the ruling <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Revolutionary_Party" title="Democratic Revolutionary Party">Democratic Revolutionary Party</a> (PRD)–<a href="/wiki/People%27s_Party_(Panama)" title="People's Party (Panama)">People's Party</a> coalition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-7">[7]</a></sup> He had the support of the Alliance for Change, a group of political parties that includes his own <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Change_(Panama)" title="Democratic Change (Panama)">Democratic Change</a> party, the <a href="/wiki/Paname%C3%B1ista_Party" title="Panameñista Party">Panameñista Party</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nationalist_Republican_Liberal_Movement" title="Nationalist Republican Liberal Movement">Nationalist Republican Liberal Movement</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriotic_Union_Party_(Panama)" title="Patriotic Union Party (Panama)">Patriotic Union Party</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EFE_Bio_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-EFE_Bio-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>His main opponent was PRD candidate <a href="/wiki/Balbina_Herrera" title="Balbina Herrera">Balbina Herrera</a>. Though initially the favorite,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-EB-3">[3]</a></sup> she was damaged in the election by her links to former military ruler <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Noriega" title="Manuel Noriega">Manuel Noriega</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-E59_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-E59-8">[8]</a></sup> and by the perception that she was a "Chavista", a supporter of leftist Venezuelan president <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hugo_Chavez" title="Hugo Chavez">Hugo Chavez</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSM_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSM-9">[9]</a></sup> Martinelli was also helped by strong support from the business community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-EB-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 3, 2009, Martinelli won the national election by a landslide, with over 60% of the votes, compared to Herrera, who received about 36%. Former president <a href="/wiki/Guillermo_Endara" title="Guillermo Endara">Guillermo Endara</a> finished a distant third.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSM_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-CSM-9">[9]</a></sup> This was the second-largest majority in Panamanian history and the largest since 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Martinelli's victory was an exception to a trend of victories for left-leaning Latin American candidates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-E59_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-E59-8">[8]</a></sup> He was sworn in on July 1, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidency">Presidency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ricardo_Martinelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Presidency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Martinelli served as president from 2009 through 2014, during which time the Panamanian economy grew robustly and steadily.</p>
<p>As lauded by <i>The Economist</i>, "Though it lies in Central America, the poorest and most violent region in the West, the country’s 3.6m citizens are now richer than most Latin Americans." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>As reported in <i>the New York Times</i>: <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> "Panama is booming, with an average economic growth of 9 percent in the past five years, the highest in Latin America."</p>
<p>This prosperity widely benefits Panama, with unemployment declining from 6.6% to 4.1%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Income disparity also declined: according to <i>The Economist</i>, "the incomes of the poorest 10% are now 35 times lower than those of the richest 10%, rather than 60 times lower, according to the finance ministry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Gross domestic product grew by nearly half,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> while GDP per capita, according to the World Bank, rose 11%, from $9,982 (2010) to $11,036 (2014).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Martinelli introduced a number of measures designed to alleviate poverty, including a $100 monthly pension for the elderly, an increase in the minimum wage, and subsidies for students to meet the cost of uniforms and supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> He also increased the minimum wage, making it the highest of Latin America. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>He also implemented measures to help <a href="/wiki/Col%C3%B3n,_Panama" title="Colón, Panama">Colón</a>, an impoverished city on the Gulf Coast. This included projects like a new highway connecting Panama and Colón, the Canal expansion, construction of a new hospital and other public works intended to help reduce unemployment and poverty. The government also announced a $9 million project to rehabilitate Colón’s seaside park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pro-business_policies.2C_but_also_raising_taxes">Pro-business policies, but also raising taxes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ricardo_Martinelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pro-business policies, but also raising taxes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Twice during his first year in office, Martinelli proposed and signed into law tax reforms to simplify filings, reduce rates, and improve collection. The number of income brackets was reduced from five to two, the corporate tax rate was cut to 25%, and delinquent collection was outsourced.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The tax reform imposed and collected taxes on a large swath of Panama’s elite, which had largely avoided significant taxation. The program was not without controversy. A spokesperson for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fitch_Group" title="Fitch Group">Fitch Group</a> stated that the tax reform "underpin[ned] the government's commitment to sustainable fiscal policies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pro-Israel_foreign_policy">Pro-Israel foreign policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ricardo_Martinelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pro-Israel foreign policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2012, Panama—along with the U.S.—was one of the few countries who stood by Israel in a key U.N. vote. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrastructure_investment">Infrastructure investment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ricardo_Martinelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Infrastructure investment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of 2010, Martinelli's administration announced plans, ultimately fulfilled during his term, to invest $20 billion over the next four years on infrastructure designed to enhance Panama's role as a global logistics hub and increase foreign direct investment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The plan included greater investment in roads, hospitals, sewers, schools, and a Panama City metro.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-E714_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-E714-24">[24]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fitch_Group" title="Fitch Group">Fitch Group</a> called the "ambitious public investment program" part of "Panama's highly favorable investment cycle."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fitchratings1_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-fitchratings1-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cornerstone of Martinelli's expansion program was the $5.3 billion expansion of the Panama Canal. He oversaw investment in ports, and the construction of a giant causeway around the old part of town, which also features a new <a href="/wiki/Biomuseo" title="Biomuseo">Biomuseo</a> (also known as the Biodiversity Museum: Panama Bridge of Life) designed by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Gehry" title="Frank Gehry">Frank Gehry</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pro-business_policies">Pro-business policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ricardo_Martinelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pro-business policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Martinelli oversaw the final approval of the <a href="/wiki/Panama%E2%80%93United_States_Trade_Promotion_Agreement" title="Panama–United States Trade Promotion Agreement">Panama–United States Trade Promotion Agreement</a>, which was signed more than two years before he took office but had not been finalized. Martinelli had designated the completion of this agreement as his top priority upon taking office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The agreement was ratified by the US Congress on October 13, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>He also oversaw <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> the approval of a comprehensive Association treaty with other regional countries and the European Union.</p>
<p>Through pro-business policies, Martinelli saw through the growth of Panama as an international transport and financial hub. According to <i>The Economist</i>, "Its canal is becoming the backbone of a transoceanic logistics network. Its airline, Copa, connects much of Latin America. Its offshore-banking sector sucks in Latin American money."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rule_of_Law">Rule of Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ricardo_Martinelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rule of Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Martinelli also undertook efforts to increase the "Rule of Law" in Panama and to create greater transparency in its institutions. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> According to the <i>New York Times</i>, "American law enforcement officials give the country credit for improving its police forces and cooperation with international agencies." Furthermore, they report that "In the past two years [Panama] has signed agreements with 12 countries, including the United States, to exchange tax and other information upon request, a tool to investigate financial criminals."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, Martinelli upheld and reinforced drug trafficking laws throughout his presidency. In 2012 alone, the Panamanian government seized over 11 tons of cocaine, as reported by IHS. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Panama_becomes_.22investment_grade.22">Panama becomes "investment grade"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ricardo_Martinelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title='Edit section: Panama becomes "investment grade"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During Martinelli's term, Panama's sovereign debt rating was upgraded to "investment grade" by Fitch, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moody%27s" title="Moody's">Moody's</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Standard_%26_Poor%27s" title="Standard &amp; Poor's">Standard &amp; Poor's</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Fitch had upgraded Panama twice<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fitchratings1_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-fitchratings1-25">[25]</a></sup> since Martinelli took office, and Standard &amp; Poor's followed its upgrade with a revised "positive" outlook.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The Fitch upgrade was described as "a victory for conservative President Ricardo Martinelli, who has pushed two tax reforms through Congress since taking office".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Martinelli's policies contributed to credit upgrades but also robust increases in <a href="/wiki/Foreign_direct_investment" title="Foreign direct investment">foreign direct investment</a>. During his tenure, it rose from $1,259.3 billion (2009) to $4,651.3 billion (2013). <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the <i>New York Times</i>, direct investment in real estate was exemplified by "the tallest building in Latin America, a 70-story Trump hotel and condo tower," further growth in financial services ("banking is booming") with “well-heeled foreign transplants" spending on local services and goods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reputation_while_in_office">Reputation while in office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ricardo_Martinelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reputation while in office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Martinelli experienced high popularity ratings during his term of office, at one time in excess of 90%, the highest in the Americas at the time. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In 2014, when he was preparing to leave office, his approval rating was still high, at 65%. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The party he heads, the Democratic Change party, has continued to see strong legislative support: with Martinelli at its leader it continues to hold the biggest bloc of 30 seats, versus 12 seats for Panameñista Party in the 71-seat National Assembly. Martinelli remains the leader of Panama’s opposition party.</p>
<p>Martinelli has been subject to a whisper campaign in the local and international media.</p>
<p>In 2011, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> described the foreign investment as still hurt by "doubts about the rule of law", citing suspected corruption in the bidding for the metro contract and the flooding of a wealthy Panama City neighborhood with sewage due to a lack of enforcement of planning laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-E714_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-E714-24">[24]</a></sup> Martinelli was criticized during his presidency for authoritarian tactics. He sought to reduce the time period before the president could run for re-election though he withdrew when it proved unfeasible. He was accused of tampering with the Supreme Court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-E714_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-E714-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In August 2009, the US Ambassador to Panama, <a href="/wiki/Barbara_J._Stephenson" title="Barbara J. Stephenson">Barbara J. Stephenson</a>, wrote to the US State Department that Martinelli had asked her for wiretaps on his political opponents, and she complained of his "bullying style" and "autocratic tendencies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP1227_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-WP1227-41">[41]</a></sup> A copy of the cable was released in December 2010 by <a href="/wiki/WikiLeaks" title="WikiLeaks">WikiLeaks</a>. After the leak, Martinelli's administration said that "help in tapping the telephones of politicians was never requested" and that Stephenson was "mistaken" in her interpretation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP1227_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-WP1227-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NC_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-NC-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2011, former military ruler <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Noriega" title="Manuel Noriega">Manuel Noriega</a> was extradited from France to Panama by Martinelli's government. Critics charged that Martinelli had requested the extradition to turn public attention away from administration scandals, an accusation denied by the French and Panamanian governments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors">Honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ricardo_Martinelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On February 20, 2010, the University of Arkansas established the Ricardo A. Martinelli Berrocal Scholarship to provide financial aid to prospective University of Arkansas students from Panama. He was also presented with the Citation of Distinguished Alumnus award and was made an official ambassador of the State of <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a> by Governor <a href="/wiki/Mike_Beebe" title="Mike Beebe">Mike Beebe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 16, 2013, received and acknowledgement from the FAO in Rome, Italy, for helping to reduce the child malnutrition in the Panamenian territory. It took place during the 38th Session of the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO) Conference. Martinelli was awarded besides other thirty seven countries.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_life">Private life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ricardo_Martinelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Private life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1978, Martinelli married <a href="/wiki/Marta_Linares_de_Martinelli" title="Marta Linares de Martinelli">Marta Linares</a>, with whom he has three children: Ricardo Martinelli Linares, Luis Enrique Martinelli Linares, and Carolina Martinelli Linares.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
