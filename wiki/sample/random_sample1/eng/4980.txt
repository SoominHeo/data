<p><b>Li Keqiang</b> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans">李克强</span>; <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin</a>: <span class="IPA" style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Mandarin" title="Help:IPA for Mandarin">[lì kʰɤ̂.tɕʰi̯ǎŋ]</a></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[a]</a></sup> born 1 July 1955) is the current <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Premier of the People's Republic of China">Premier</a> of the <a href="/wiki/State_Council_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="State Council of the People's Republic of China">State Council</a> of the <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">People's Republic of China</a>. An economist by training, Li is China's <a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">head of government</a> as well as one of the leading figures behind <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_China" title="Economy of China">Chinese economic policy</a>. He is also the second-ranked member of the <a href="/wiki/Politburo_Standing_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="Politburo Standing Committee of the Communist Party of China">Politburo Standing Committee</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party of China</a>, the <i>de facto</i> top decision-making body of the country. Li is a major part of the "<a href="/wiki/Generations_of_Chinese_leadership" title="Generations of Chinese leadership">fifth generation of Chinese leadership</a>". Li was named the World's 12th Most Powerful Person by the <a href="/wiki/Forbes_list_of_The_World%27s_Most_Powerful_People" title="Forbes list of The World's Most Powerful People">Forbes list of The World's Most Powerful People</a> in 2015 and 2016.</p>
<p>Li rose through the ranks through his involvement in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist_Youth_League" title="Communist Youth League">Communist Youth League</a>. From 1998 to 2004, Li served as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor_of_Henan" title="Governor of Henan">Governor of Henan</a> and the province's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Party_chief_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="Party chief of the Communist Party of China">party secretary.</a> From 2004 to 2007 he served as the Party Secretary of <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Liaoning" title="Politics of Liaoning">Liaoning</a>, the top political office in the province. From 2008 to 2013, Li served as the first-ranked <a href="/wiki/Vice_Premier_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Vice Premier of the People's Republic of China">Vice-Premier</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[b]</a></sup> under then-Premier <a href="/wiki/Wen_Jiabao" title="Wen Jiabao">Wen Jiabao</a>, overseeing a broad portfolio which included economic development, <a href="/wiki/Price_controls" title="Price controls">price controls</a>, finance, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, and macroeconomic management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-China.org.cn_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-China.org.cn-4">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Given his Youth League experience, Li has generally been considered a political ally of former leader <a href="/wiki/Hu_Jintao" title="Hu Jintao">Hu Jintao</a>. Li assumed the post of Premier in 2013, and has facilitated the Chinese government's shifting of priorities from export-led growth to a greater focus on internal consumption. Li has also been a major force behind the implementation of the "comprehensively deepening reforms"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> announced in the fall of 2013.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Li_Keqiang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Li was born on 1 July 1955 in <a href="/wiki/Hefei" title="Hefei">Hefei</a>, the capital and largest city of <a href="/wiki/Anhui" title="Anhui">Anhui</a>. His father was a local official in Anhui.</p>
<p>Li graduated from Hefei No.8 Senior High School in 1974,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[3]</a></sup> during the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Revolution" title="Cultural Revolution">Cultural Revolution</a>, and was sent for rural labour in <a href="/wiki/Fengyang_County" title="Fengyang County">Fengyang County</a>, Anhui, where he eventually joined the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party of China</a> and made his way in becoming the party head of the <a href="/wiki/Production_team_(China)" title="Production team (China)">local production team</a>. He was awarded the honour of <i>Outstanding Individual in the Study of Mao Zedong Thought</i> during this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Li refused his father's offer of grooming him for the local county's party leadership and entered the School of Law at <a href="/wiki/Peking_University" title="Peking University">Peking University</a>, where he received his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LLB" title="LLB">LLB</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rfe_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-rfe-7">[5]</a></sup> and became the president of the University's student council. He earned a PhD in economics in 1995, and the prominent economist <a href="/wiki/Li_Yining" title="Li Yining">Li Yining</a> (no relation) was his doctoral advisor. His doctoral dissertation was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Sun_Yefang" title="Sun Yefang">Sun Yefang</a> Prize, China's highest prize in economics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sohu_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-sohu-8">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1980, Li became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist_Youth_League" title="Communist Youth League">Communist Youth League</a> secretary at Peking University. He entered the top leadership of the national organization of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist_Youth_League" title="Communist Youth League">Communist Youth League</a> (CYL) in 1982 as a member of its Secretariat, and has worked closely with former Party General Secretary <a href="/wiki/Hu_Jintao" title="Hu Jintao">Hu Jintao</a>, who also rose through the ranks of the CYL, ever since. Li became the organization's First Secretary in 1993 and served until 1998. He is a representative member of the first generation to have risen from the CYL leadership.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rising_through_the_ranks_.281998-2012.29">Rising through the ranks (1998-2012)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Li_Keqiang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rising through the ranks (1998-2012)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Provincial_tenures">Provincial tenures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Li_Keqiang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Provincial tenures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Li became the youngest Chinese provincial governor in June 1998 when he was appointed governor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henan_Province" title="Henan Province">Henan</a> at the age of 43. According to provincial officials working with him at the time, Li refused to participate in any banquets or large fancy events not related to government activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[7]</a></sup> During his time as governor, a public sense of his "bad luck" grew due to the occurrence of three major fires in the province.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-the-diplomat.com_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-the-diplomat.com-10">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Li is known to be outspoken and led <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a> in Henan, transforming the poor inland region into an attractive area for investment. Li did not spend time pursuing superficial projects. He trekked through all regions of the province trying to search for a comprehensive solution to its growing problems. Henan jumped in national GDP rankings from 28th in the early 1990s to 18th in 2004, when Li left Henan. However, his government was relatively ineffective at curbing the <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a> epidemic that was affecting the rural areas of the province.</p>
<p>Li was transferred to work as the Party Secretary in <a href="/wiki/Liaoning" title="Liaoning">Liaoning</a> in December 2004, taking on the top political office of the province. There he is known for the "Five-points to one Line" project, where he linked up <a href="/wiki/Dalian" title="Dalian">Dalian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dandong" title="Dandong">Dandong</a>, as well as a series of other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ports_of_China" title="Ports of China">ports</a> into a comprehensive network to improve trade flow.</p>
<p>With his Youth League experience and his association with then paramount leader <a href="/wiki/Hu_Jintao" title="Hu Jintao">Hu Jintao</a>, Li was seen from early on in Hu's term as a contender to succeed Hu when his term as party leader ends in 2012. Li gained membership to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Politburo_Standing_Committee" title="Politburo Standing Committee">Politburo Standing Committee</a> at <a href="/wiki/17th_National_Congress_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="17th National Congress of the Communist Party of China">17th Party Congress</a> held in October 2007. He was succeeded in his provincial party chief post by Governor <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Wenyue" title="Zhang Wenyue">Zhang Wenyue</a>. While Li's political future seemed promising, he was outranked on the Standing Committee by <a href="/wiki/Xi_Jinping" title="Xi Jinping">Xi Jinping</a>, who had just left his role as party chief of Shanghai to join the central leadership ranks in Beijing. This rank order ostensibly signaled that it would be Xi, not Li, who would eventually succeed Hu as party General Secretary and President. At the <a href="/wiki/2008_National_People%27s_Congress" title="2008 National People's Congress">2008 National People's Congress</a>, Li was elected Vice-Premier, first in rank, reinforcing the speculation that Li would become Premier and was being groomed to succeed then-Premier <a href="/wiki/Wen_Jiabao" title="Wen Jiabao">Wen Jiabao</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-the-diplomat.com_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-the-diplomat.com-10">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vice_Premiership_.282008-2013.29">Vice Premiership (2008-2013)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Li_Keqiang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vice Premiership (2008-2013)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>During his first term in the PSC between 2007 and 2012, Li took on the most important portfolios in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Government of the People's Republic of China">Chinese government</a>, including economic development, government budgets, land and resources, the environment, and health, ostensibly to prepare him for his upcoming premiership. He also became the head of central commissions overseeing the <a href="/wiki/Three_Gorges_Dam" title="Three Gorges Dam">Three Gorges Dam</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South-North_Water_Transfer_Project" title="South-North Water Transfer Project">South-North Water Transfer Project</a>, as well as the leader of steering committees in charge of health care reform, food safety, and AIDS-related work. In addition, Li was the principal lieutenant to Premier Wen Jiabao in the broad portfolios of climate change, energy, information technology, northeastern China revitalization, and developing the Chinese far west.</p>
<p>Li's first major appearance internationally was at the 2010 <a href="/wiki/World_Economic_Forum" title="World Economic Forum">World Economic Forum</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Davos,_Switzerland" title="Davos, Switzerland">Davos, Switzerland</a>. The event was seen as an <a href="/wiki/Litmus_test_(politics)" title="Litmus test (politics)">litmus test</a> for Li. At the forum, Li succinctly presented China's long-term vision for development in front of world business and political leaders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forbes_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-forbes-11">[9]</a></sup> In particular, Li briefed the WEF on China's commitment to <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_energy" title="Green energy">green energy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Income_inequality" title="Income inequality">decreasing the income gap</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_development" title="Industrial development">modernizing key strategic industries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forbes_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-forbes-11">[9]</a></sup> While reiterating Beijing's commitment to peaceful development and its focus in increasing domestic demand in the face of external pressures during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_financial_crisis" title="Global financial crisis">global financial crisis</a>, Li also warned against <a href="/wiki/Protectionism" title="Protectionism">protectionism</a>, saying "opening up can be both bilateral and multilateral... in this sense, one plus one is more often than not bigger than two." He also touched upon the importance of <a href="/wiki/International_development" title="International development">international development</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Global_financial_system" title="Global financial system">international financial reform</a>. He called for a global governance structure that was "more reflective of the changes in the global political and economic landscape."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davos_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-davos-12">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2010, Li gave a speech to ministerial and provincial-level leaders about the importance of changing the economic structure of the country in order to be better poised for future growth. The speech was published with minor omissions in the 1 June issue of <i><a href="/wiki/Qiushi" title="Qiushi">Qiushi</a></i>, the Communist Party's political theory publication. Li said that China had come to a critical historical inflection point where a fundamental shift in the structure of the economy must take place in order for the country to continue its path of growth. Li particularly emphasized the need to boost domestic <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a>, and emphasized the importance of continued urbanization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-qiushi_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-qiushi-13">[11]</a></sup> Li also emphasized that China should be moving towards a more middle class-oriented society with an "olive"-shaped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wealth_distribution" title="Wealth distribution">wealth distribution</a>, with the majority of the country's population and wealth belonging to the middle class.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>He has also reiterated the importance of industrialization, urbanization and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture_in_China" title="Agriculture in China">agricultural</a> modernization in China in order to improve its <a href="/wiki/Competitiveness" title="Competitiveness">competitiveness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy_security" title="Energy security">energy security</a>, <a href="/wiki/Affordable_housing" title="Affordable housing">affordable housing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Health_in_China" title="Health in China">healthcare</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2011, Li went on an official visit to Hong Kong, including a trip to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hong_Kong" title="University of Hong Kong">University of Hong Kong</a>. The political sensitivities and heightened security surrounding the event resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_818_incident" title="Hong Kong 818 incident">Hong Kong 818 incident</a>, an event that caused controversy in the territory.</p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/18th_National_Congress_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="18th National Congress of the Communist Party of China">18th Party Congress</a> held in the fall of 2012, Li was elevated to the number two spot on the <a href="/wiki/Politburo_Standing_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="Politburo Standing Committee of the Communist Party of China">Politburo Standing Committee</a> (PSC) and thereby became one of the top leaders of China. As he was expected to become Premier, this was a shift from previous convention on the PSC set in 1997 whereby the Premier ranked third, after the <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Standing_Committee_of_the_National_People%27s_Congress" title="Chairman of the Standing Committee of the National People's Congress">Chairman of the Standing Committee of the National People's Congress</a>, who ranked second. This change showed that the party intended to project Li as the number two figure in the Chinese political hierarchy, behind only party General Secretary Xi Jinping. Also in that same year, he had personally met with the openly gay Ma Baoli (a policeman turned businessman) to discuss the threat posed by <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[14]</a></sup> In the same conversation, they discussed LGBT issues on preventing discrimination which has been interpreted as a sign that the People's Republic is slowly coming around to the idea even as major Chinese businesses have already begun tapping into the <a href="/wiki/Purchasing_power_parity" title="Purchasing power parity">purchasing power parity</a> of the LGBT community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 15 March 2013, Li Keqiang was, as expected, elected by the 12th <a href="/wiki/National_People%27s_Congress" title="National People's Congress">National People's Congress</a> as <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Premier of the People's Republic of China">Premier</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-18">[16]</a></sup> At the same Congress, Party General Secretary <a href="/wiki/Xi_Jinping" title="Xi Jinping">Xi Jinping</a> was elected <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="President of the People's Republic of China">President</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup> Li replaced <a href="/wiki/Wen_Jiabao" title="Wen Jiabao">Wen Jiabao</a>, who retired after serving two terms as premier. Of the nearly 3,000 legislators assembled at the Congress, 2,940 voted for him, three against, and six abstained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-18">[16]</a></sup> He was elected for a five-year term, but was expected to serve two terms like his predecessor Wen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-18">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 16 March, the Congress selected <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Gaoli" title="Zhang Gaoli">Zhang Gaoli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liu_Yandong" title="Liu Yandong">Liu Yandong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wang_Yang_(politician)" title="Wang Yang (politician)">Wang Yang</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ma_Kai" title="Ma Kai">Ma Kai</a> as <a href="/wiki/Vice_Premier_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Vice Premier of the People's Republic of China">vice premiers</a> following their respective nominations from Li Keqiang.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berg16mar_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-berg16mar-20">[18]</a></sup> He gave his first major speech March 17 at the conclusion of the National Peoples Congress, calling for frugality in government, a fairer distribution of income and continued economic reform. Li has focused his attention on China to move towards a consumption based economy instead of relying on export led growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cpd_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-cpd-21">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gt_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-gt-22">[20]</a></sup> Li was ranked 14th of the 2013 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forbes_Magazine%27s_List_of_The_World%27s_Most_Powerful_People" title="Forbes Magazine's List of The World's Most Powerful People">Forbes Magazine's List of The World's Most Powerful People</a>, after taking the office of Chinese Premier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestic_policy">Domestic policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Li_Keqiang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Domestic policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Li_Keqiang,_Chinese_and_foreign_press_conference.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="759" data-file-width="1349" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Li_Keqiang%2C_Chinese_and_foreign_press_conference.jpg/220px-Li_Keqiang%2C_Chinese_and_foreign_press_conference.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Li_Keqiang%2C_Chinese_and_foreign_press_conference.jpg/330px-Li_Keqiang%2C_Chinese_and_foreign_press_conference.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Li_Keqiang%2C_Chinese_and_foreign_press_conference.jpg/440px-Li_Keqiang%2C_Chinese_and_foreign_press_conference.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

March 2015, Li attends the Chinese and foreign press conference.</div>

<p>Li has an academic background in economics and is therefore a firm believer in the use of robust economic data to aid in government decision making. He is known to study economic statistics and routinely questions the reliability of the data he works with. Li has been described by international media as the pioneer behind the so-called "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keqiang_index" title="Keqiang index">Keqiang index</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[22]</a></sup> an unconventional economic measurement indicator devised by Li that was said to bypass the often unreliable official GDP numbers and thus serves as a better indicator of economic health and a more useful barometer for decision-making. Instead of gathering data on total economic output alone, Li used three other indicators to keep tabs on the economy while working in Liaoning province. These were, the railway cargo volume, electricity consumption and total loans disbursed by banks.</p>
<p>When Li initially entered office, China was facing numerous structural problems inherited from the previous administration. Namely, the large abundance of non-performing loans, that many of the giant infrastructure projects the country embarked on since the global financial crisis was overloaded with crushing debt and lower than expected revenues, and the increasingly large wealth gap. Under these circumstances, Li was said to have responded with what became known as "Likonomics", a term coined by economists at the investment bank <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barclays_Capital" title="Barclays Capital">Barclays Capital</a>. Likonomics consisted of a three-prong approach that included the across-the-board reduction of debt, an end to massive stimulus practices of the Wen Jiabao government, and structural reforms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-econ2_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-econ2-25">[23]</a></sup> However, by 2014, global economic pressures and a decrease in demand of Chinese exports led to lower than expected economic growth rates. Year-on-year GDP growth amounted to less than 7.5% for the first time since 1989. Li's government then responded with tax cuts for small businesses, renovation projects of poor urban areas, and another round of rail construction, particularly focused on the country's interior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gorge_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-gorge-26">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the announcement of comprehensive reforms at the Third Plenum in 2013, Li became a leading figure of the government's reform implementation efforts. The Third Plenum called for market forces to play a "decisive" role in the allocation of resources, ostensibly looking to decrease government regulation on the free market. In early 2014, Li said that local governments still were still ineffective at following the central government's reform directives, and that "[some governments] meddle in affairs they shouldn't be involved in, and some don't pay attention to things they should be doing." Li emphasized that the success of reforms came down to "execution and implementation," and criticized local governments for failing to act in support of reforms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_policy">Foreign policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Li_Keqiang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Foreign policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Li Keqiang made his first foreign visit to India on 18 May 2013 in a bid to resolve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/China-India_relations" title="China-India relations">border disputes</a> and to stimulate economic relations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[26]</a></sup> He said the choice of India as the first international visit highlights Chinese importance in its relations with the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[27]</a></sup> During Prime Minister Narendra Modi's visit to China in 2015, Li and Modi took a <a href="/wiki/Selfie" title="Selfie">selfie</a> together at the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Heaven" title="Temple of Heaven">Temple of Heaven</a>.</p>
<p>During his visit to Pakistan he met with top leadership of country and expressed his views "As Pakistan's closest friend and brother, we would like to provide as much assistance as we can for the Pakistani side".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Li Keqiang also visited <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> on his first EU trip and met with the two countries' leaders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[29]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction_of_Bureaucracy">Reduction of Bureaucracy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Li_Keqiang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reduction of Bureaucracy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Li is especially critical of unnecessary government bureaucracy, particularly at the grassroots level. He believes that many lower-level officials fail to provide services to the public in an effective manner. Regarding his disdain for the matter, Li's many quotable anecdotes have become viral. Li referred to a case in which a citizen filling out a form to travel overseas had to write down an emergency contact (the citizen put down their mother as the contact), and the government official overseeing the matter asked the citizen to provide a notarized document to "prove your mom is your mom."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mom_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-mom-32">[30]</a></sup> Li called this incident "absolutely preposterous." In another case, he referred to a grassroots civil servant who asked for proof that a one-year old does not have a criminal record in order to deliver a government service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnews_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnews-33">[31]</a></sup> In yet another case, Li referred to a senior citizen applying for welfare benefits being forced by government employees to provide proof that "they are still alive." Regarding the latter two incidents, Li said, "this is not a joke, it's all real!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnews_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-cnews-33">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Xi-Li_Administration">Xi-Li Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Li_Keqiang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Xi-Li Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xi-Li_Administration" title="Xi-Li Administration">Xi-Li Administration</a></div>
<p>At the Third Plenum of the 18th Central Committee held in the fall of 2013, the Communist Party announced far reaching economic and social reforms. However, the document outlining the reforms was drafted under the leadership of Xi, <a href="/wiki/Liu_Yunshan" title="Liu Yunshan">Liu Yunshan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Gaoli" title="Zhang Gaoli">Zhang Gaoli</a>, and Li was ostensibly not involved in preparing the document. This departure from convention (Wen Jiabao was the principal drafter of documents behind the reforms announced at the Third Plenum of 2003) led to speculation that Li was becoming marginalized in the new administration, and that the widely touted "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xi-Li_Administration" title="Xi-Li Administration">Xi-Li Administration</a>" in fact did not exist, as power was increasingly being centralized under the hands of Xi as the <a href="/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="General Secretary of the Communist Party of China">General Secretary of the Communist Party of China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the Third Plenum of 2013, Xi amassed a series of leadership roles on four new powerful supra-ministerial bodies overseeing "<a href="/wiki/Central_Leading_Group_for_Comprehensively_Deepening_Reforms" title="Central Leading Group for Comprehensively Deepening Reforms">comprehensively deepening reforms</a>", <a href="/wiki/Central_Leading_Group_for_Internet_Security_and_Informatization" title="Central Leading Group for Internet Security and Informatization">the internet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Leading_Group_for_Military_Reform" title="Central Leading Group for Military Reform">military reform</a>, and also the <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Commission_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="National Security Commission of the Communist Party of China">National Security Commission</a>. The "deepening reforms" leading group was said to be encroaching on the affairs in the economic realm normally handled by the Premier, and was seen as having the effect of reducing Li's institutional power. However, Li appeared in official press releases as Xi's foremost lieutenant, being named Vice Chairman of the National Security Commission,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup> in addition to becoming the deputy leader of leading groups on "deepening reforms", internet security, and <a href="/wiki/Leading_Group_for_Financial_and_Economic_Affairs" title="Leading Group for Financial and Economic Affairs">the economy and finance</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Li_Keqiang&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Li is married to <a href="/wiki/Cheng_Hong" title="Cheng Hong">Cheng Hong</a>, a professor at <a href="/wiki/Capital_University_of_Economics_and_Business" title="Capital University of Economics and Business">Capital University of Economics and Business</a> in Beijing. His father-in-law was once the deputy secretary of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist_Youth_League" title="Communist Youth League">Communist Youth League</a> Central Committee. He speaks conversational English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DW_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-DW-36">[34]</a></sup></p>
