<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ritalin class-action lawsuits</h1>

<p><b>The Ritalin class-action lawsuits</b> were a series of federal lawsuits in 2000, filed in five separate US states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> All five lawsuits were dismissed by the end of 2002. The lawsuits alleged that the makers of <a href="/wiki/Methylphenidate" title="Methylphenidate">methylphenidate</a> (brand name Ritalin) and the <a href="/wiki/American_Psychiatric_Association" title="American Psychiatric Association">American Psychiatric Association</a> had conspired to invent and promote the disorder <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ADHD" title="ADHD">ADHD</a> to create a highly profitable market for the drug. The lawsuit also alleged that <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CHADD&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CHADD (page does not exist)">CHADD</a> (children and adults with attention deficit/hyperactivity disorder) deliberately attempted to increase the supply of Ritalin and ease restrictions on the supply of Ritalin to help increase profits for <a href="/wiki/Novartis" title="Novartis">Novartis</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Previous_lawsuits.2Fhistory_of_class_action">Previous lawsuits/history of class action</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ritalin_class-action_lawsuits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Previous lawsuits/history of class action">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beginning in the 1980s, a series of lawsuits were filed based on the perceived harmful side effects of Ritalin. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Coale&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Coale (page does not exist)">John Coale</a>, who had participated in one of these lawsuits, joined what became an ever larger contingent of lawyers involved in what was then a growing series of Ritalin class action lawsuits. In the late 90's, there was a significant increase in production of Ritalin. A minority but vocal group of critics perceived that a crisis was on hand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Coale also expressed alarm, "They were giving this stuff away like candy”. The <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scientology" title="Church of Scientology">Church of Scientology</a> advocacy organization, <a href="/wiki/Citizens_Commission_on_Human_Rights" title="Citizens Commission on Human Rights">Citizens Commission on Human Rights</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anti-psychiatry" title="Anti-psychiatry">anti-psychiatry</a> critics believed Ritalin to be highly dangerous and completely unnecessary. Coale seemed to share these beliefs as he stated the purpose of the lawsuit to be; “...to put [Ritalin] off the market."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Petersburg_Times" title="St. Petersburg Times">St. Petersburg Times</a></i> wrote at that time that Coale, like his wife <a href="/wiki/Greta_Van_Susteren" title="Greta Van Susteren">Greta Van Susteren</a>, was a practicing Scientologist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Scruggs" title="Richard Scruggs">Richard Scruggs</a>, like John Coale, and a few other lawyers who participated in the Ritalin class action lawsuits, had previously helped win a landmark settlement from the asbestos and tobacco industries, Ritalin was to be the next major battleground. Scruggs would lead and also become a spokesman for the plaintiffs. He asserted the Ritalin defendants, "manufactured a disease"...and "it has been grossly over-prescribed. It is a huge risk." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Breggin" title="Peter Breggin">Peter Breggin</a> who is a noted Anti-Psychiatry critic, was hired as a medical consultant by the firm and was also involved as a consultant in the other lawsuits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first class action was filed in Texas by the law firm Waters &amp; Kraus in 2000. They created a webpage called Ritalinfraud.com which had an online form to seek additional participants in class action lawsuits. According to Breggin, Plaintiff Andy Waters had previously read his book <i>Talking back to Ritalin</i> before filing his lawsuit. The firm believed that the improper conduct of Novartis rivaled the improper conduct of the tobacco and asbestos industries and that the drug company could be liable for billions of dollars. The firm claimed that Novartis specifically took the following steps to dramatically increase the sale of Ritalin.</p>
<ol>
<li>Actively promoting and supporting the concept that a significant percentage of children suffer from a "disease" which required narcotic treatment/therapy;</li>
<li>Actively promoting Ritalin as the "drug of choice" to treat children diagnosed with ADD and ADHD:</li>
<li>Actively supporting groups such as Defendant CHADD, both financially and with other means, so that such organizations would promote and support (as a supposed neutral party) the ever-increasing implementation of ADD/ADHD diagnoses as well as directly increasing Ritalin sales;</li>
<li>Distributing misleading sales and promotional literature to parents, schools and other interested persons in a successful effort to further increase the number of diagnoses and the number of persons prescribed Ritalin.</li>
</ol>

<p>A spokesperson for Novartis responded to the Texas suit, "Ritalin has been used safely and effectively in the treatment of millions of ADHD patients for over 40 years, and is the most studied drug prescribed for the disorder."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The American Psychiatric Association stated, "the allegation that it had conspired with Novartis to create the ADHD diagnosis was "ludicrous and totally false," and said there existed "a mountain of scientific evidence to refute these meritless allegations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outcome">Outcome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ritalin_class-action_lawsuits&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Outcome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first suit to be dismissed occurred in California in 2001. U.S. District Judge Rudi Brewster dismissed the suit under California's anti-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SLAPP" title="SLAPP">SLAPP</a> statute. A SLAPP is a form of litigation filed to intimidate and silence a less powerful critic by so severely burdening them with the cost of a legal defense that they abandon their criticism. The Anti-SLAPP statute is designed to eliminate potential lawsuits early in court procedures, that are in reality political actions. Judge Brewster dismissed the suit stating that the defendants' speech is "protected under both the United States and California Constitutions" and that plaintiffs "failed to state a cause of action." In addition to dismissing the suit, the court also ordered that the plaintiffs pay the legal fees for Novartis, APA and CHADD. In the conclusion to one of the other lawsuits, Judge Tagla stated. "that the allegations were fully without merit. Defendants failed to provide any concrete statements to document their claims." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> By 2002 all five class action lawsuits had been dismissed or had withdrawn their lawsuits. A Novartis spokesperson stated;"...the fact that all five of the class action lawsuits have been dismissed, sends a strong message that the decision of how to treat ADHD is between the parent, patient and physician, and has no place in the courts."</p>
