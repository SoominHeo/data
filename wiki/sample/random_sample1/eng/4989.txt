<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Isotopes of lithium</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>Naturally occurring <b><a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a></b> (chemical symbol <b>Li</b>) (<a href="/wiki/Relative_atomic_mass" title="Relative atomic mass">relative atomic mass</a>: 6.941(2)) is composed of two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stable_isotope" title="Stable isotope">stable isotopes</a>, <b>lithium-6</b> and <b>lithium-7</b>, with the latter being far more abundant: about 92.5 percent of the <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>. Both of the natural <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> have an unexpectedly low <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_binding_energy" title="Nuclear binding energy">nuclear binding energy</a> per <a href="/wiki/Nucleon" title="Nucleon">nucleon</a> (~5.3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MeV" title="MeV">MeV</a>) when compared with the adjacent lighter and heavier elements, <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> (~7.1 MeV) and <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">beryllium</a> (~6.5 MeV). The longest-lived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotope</a> of lithium is lithium-8, which has a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of just 838 <a href="/wiki/Millisecond" title="Millisecond">milliseconds</a>. Lithium-9 has a half-life of 178 milliseconds, and lithium-11 has a half-life of about 8.6 milliseconds. All of the remaining isotopes of lithium have half-lives that are shorter than 10 <a href="/wiki/Nanosecond" title="Nanosecond">nanoseconds</a>. The shortest-lived known isotope of lithium is lithium-4, which decays by <a href="/wiki/Proton_emission" title="Proton emission">proton emission</a> with a half-life of about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6977909999999999999♠</span>9.1<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−23</sup></span> seconds, although the half-life of lithium-3 is yet to be determined, and is likely to be much shorter.</p>
<p>Lithium-7 and lithium-6 are two of the <a href="/wiki/Primordial_nuclide" title="Primordial nuclide">primordial nuclides</a> that were produced in the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis" title="Big Bang nucleosynthesis">Big Bang</a>, with lithium-7 to be 10<sup>−9</sup> of all primordial nuclides and amount of lithium-6 around 10<sup>−13</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Primodal-lithium-problem-2012-arx_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Primodal-lithium-problem-2012-arx-1">[1]</a></sup> A small percentage of lithium-6 is also known to be produced by <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reaction" title="Nuclear reaction">nuclear reactions</a> in certain stars. The isotopes of lithium separate somewhat during a variety of <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geological</a> processes, including mineral formation (chemical precipitation and <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a>). Lithium ions replace <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> in certain <a href="/wiki/Octahedron" title="Octahedron">octahedral locations</a> in <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clays</a>, and lithium-6 is sometimes preferred over lithium-7. This results in some enrichment of lithium-7 in geological processes.</p>
<p>Lithium-6 is an important isotope in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">nuclear physics</a> because when it is bombarded with <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a> is produced.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotope_separation">Isotope separation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_lithium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Isotope separation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colex_separation">Colex separation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_lithium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Colex separation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lithium-6 has a greater affinity than lithium-7 for the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">element</a> <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>. When an amalgam of lithium and mercury is added to solutions containing <a href="/wiki/Lithium_hydroxide" title="Lithium hydroxide">lithium hydroxide</a>, the lithium-6 becomes more concentrated in the amalgam and the lithium-7 more in the hydroxide solution.</p>
<p>The colex (<b>col</b>umn <b>ex</b>change) separation method makes use of this by passing a counter-flow of amalgam and hydroxide through a cascade of stages. The <a href="/wiki/Fraction_(chemistry)" title="Fraction (chemistry)">fraction</a> of lithium-6 is preferentially drained by the mercury, but the lithium-7 flows mostly with the hydroxide. At the bottom of the column, the lithium (enriched with lithium-6) is separated from the amalgam, and the mercury is recovered to be reused with fresh <a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">raw material</a>. At the top, the lithium hydroxide solution is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrolyzed" title="Electrolyzed">electrolyzed</a> to liberate the lithium-7 fraction. The enrichment obtained with this method varies with the column length and the flow speed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vacuum_distillation">Vacuum distillation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_lithium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vacuum distillation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lithium is heated to a temperature of about 550 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C2%B0C" title="°C">°C</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a>. Lithium atoms evaporate from the liquid surface and are collected on a cold surface positioned a few centimetres above the liquid surface. Since lithium-6 atoms have a greater <a href="/wiki/Mean_free_path" title="Mean free path">mean free path</a>, they are collected preferentially.</p>
<p>The theoretical separation efficiency is about 8.0 percent. A multistage process may be used to obtain higher degrees of separation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithium-4">Lithium-4</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_lithium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lithium-4">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Lithium-4</b> contains three <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and one <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a>. It is the shortest-lived known isotope of lithium, with a half-life of about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6977909999999999999♠</span>9.1<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−23</sup></span> seconds and decays by <a href="/wiki/Proton_emission" title="Proton emission">proton emission</a> to <a href="/wiki/Helium-3" title="Helium-3">helium-3</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Isotopes_of_Lithium_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Isotopes_of_Lithium-2">[2]</a></sup> Lithium-4 can be formed as an intermediate in some <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> reactions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithium-6">Lithium-6</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_lithium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lithium-6">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Lithium-6</b> is valuable as the source material for the production of <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a> (hydrogen-3) and as an absorber of neutrons in nuclear fusion reactions. Natural lithium contains about 7.5 percent lithium-6, with the rest being lithium-7. Large amounts of lithium-6 have been separated out for placing into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bomb" title="Hydrogen bomb">hydrogen bombs</a>. The separation of lithium-6 has by now ceased in the large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermonuclear" title="Thermonuclear">thermonuclear</a> powers, but stockpiles of it remain in these countries. Lithium-6 is one of only three stable isotopes with a <a href="/wiki/Electron_magnetic_moment" title="Electron magnetic moment">spin</a> of 1<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[n 1]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> and has the smallest nonzero nuclear electric <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadrupole_moment" title="Quadrupole moment">quadrupole moment</a> of any stable nucleus.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithium-7">Lithium-7</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_lithium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lithium-7">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lithium-7 is by far the most-common isotope, making up about 92.5 percent of all natural lithium. A lithium-7 atom contains three protons, four neutrons, and three electrons. Because of its nuclear properties lithium-7 is less common than <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">beryllium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in the Universe, even though the latter four all have heavier <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nuclei</a>.</p>
<p>The industrial production of lithium-6 results in a waste product which is enriched in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithium-7" title="Lithium-7">lithium-7</a> and depleted in lithium-6. This material has been sold commercially, and some of it has been released into the environment. A relative abundance of lithium-7, as high as 35 percent greater than the natural value, has been measured in the ground water in a carbonate aquifer underneath the West Valley Creek in <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, which is downstream from a lithium processing plant. In the depleted lithium, the relative abundance of lithium-6 can be reduced to as little as 20 percent of its nominal value, giving an <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic mass</a> for the discharged<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> lithium that can range from about 6.94 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dalton_(unit)" title="Dalton (unit)">Da</a> to about 7.00 Da. Hence the isotopic composition of lithium can vary somewhat depending on its source. An accurate atomic mass for samples of lithium cannot be measured for all sources of lithium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lithium-7 is used as a part of the molten <a href="/wiki/Lithium_fluoride" title="Lithium fluoride">lithium fluoride</a> in <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt_reactor" title="Molten salt reactor">molten salt reactors</a>: liquid-<a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a> <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>. The large <a href="/wiki/Neutron_cross_section" title="Neutron cross section">neutron-absorption cross-section</a> of lithium-6 (about 940 <a href="/wiki/Barn_(unit)" title="Barn (unit)">barns</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup>) as compared with the very small neutron cross-section of lithium-7 (about 45 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millibarn" title="Millibarn">millibarns</a>) makes high separation of lithium-7 from natural lithium a strong requirement for the possible use in lithium fluoride reactors.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lithium_hydroxide" title="Lithium hydroxide">Lithium-7 hydroxide</a> is used for <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alkalizing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alkalizing (page does not exist)">alkalizing</a> of the coolant in <a href="/wiki/Pressurized_water_reactor" title="Pressurized water reactor">pressurized water reactors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some lithium-7 has been produced, for a few picoseconds, which contains a <a href="/wiki/Lambda_baryon" title="Lambda baryon">lambda particle</a> in its nucleus, whereas an atomic nucleus is generally thought to contain only neutrons, protons, and <a href="/wiki/Pion" title="Pion">pions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley2001_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley2001-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithium-11">Lithium-11</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_lithium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lithium-11">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Lithium-11</b> is thought to possess a <a href="/wiki/Halo_nucleus" title="Halo nucleus">halo nucleus</a> consisting of a core of three protons and eight neutrons, two of which are in a nuclear halo. It has an exceptionally large cross-section of 3.16 fm<sup>2</sup>, comparable to that of <sup>208</sup>Pb. It decays by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_emission" title="Beta emission">beta emission</a> to <sup>11</sup>Be, which then decays in several ways (see table below).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_lithium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The precision of the abundance of isotopes of lithium and the overall <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_weight" title="Atomic weight">atomic weight</a> is limited through variations. The given ranges should be applicable to any normal terrestrial material.</li>
<li>Exceptional samples of lithium from geology are known in which the isotopic composition lies outside the reported range. The uncertainty in the atomic mass might exceed the stated value for such samples.</li>
<li>Commercially available samples of lithium may have been subjected to the undisclosed or inadvertent separation of the isotopes. Substantial deviations from the given atomic mass and isotopic composition can be found.</li>
<li>In depleted lithium (with the <sup>6</sup>Li removed), the relative abundance of lithium-6 can be reduced to as little as 20 percent of its normal value, giving the measured atomic mass ranging from 6.94 Da to 7.00 Da.</li>
<li>The values marked with # are not purely from experimental data, but they are partly or totally estimated from the general trends. The values of spin with weak assignment arguments are enclosed in parentheses.</li>
<li>Uncertainties are given in concise form in parentheses after the corresponding last digits. Uncertainty values denote one <a href="/wiki/Standard_deviation" title="Standard deviation">standard deviation</a> from the norm, except for the isotopic compositions and standard atomic masses from the IUPAC that use larger uncertainties.</li>
<li>The unusual isotope lithium-11 has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_halo" title="Nuclear halo">nuclear halo</a> of two weakly linked <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> that explains the important difference in its nuclear radius.</li>
<li>Nuclide masses are given by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAP" title="IUPAP">IUPAP</a> Commission on Symbols, Units, Nomenclature, Atomic Masses and Fundamental Constants (SUNAMCO)</li>
<li>Isotope abundances are given by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a> <a href="/wiki/Commission_on_Isotopic_Abundances_and_Atomic_Weights" title="Commission on Isotopic Abundances and Atomic Weights">Commission on Isotopic Abundances and Atomic Weights</a></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decay_chains">Decay chains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isotopes_of_lithium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Decay chains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While β<sup>−</sup> decay into <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_beryllium" title="Isotopes of beryllium">isotopes of beryllium</a> (often combined with single- or multiple-neutron emission) is predominant over heavier isotopes of lithium, <sup>10</sup>Li and <sup>12</sup>Li decay via neutron emission into <sup>9</sup>Li and <sup>11</sup>Li respectively due to their positions above the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutron_drip_line" title="Neutron drip line">neutron drip line</a>. Lithium-11 has also been observed to decay via multiple forms of fission. Lighter isotopes of lithium (&lt;<sup>6</sup>Li) are only known to decay by proton emission. The decay modes of the two isomers of <sup>10</sup>Li are unknown.</p>
