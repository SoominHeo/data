<p><b>Little Spain</b> was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish-American" title="Spanish-American">Spanish-American</a> <a href="/wiki/Neighbourhood" title="Neighbourhood">neighborhood</a> in the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> <a href="/wiki/Borough_(New_York_City)" title="Borough (New York City)">borough</a> of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> during the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-El_Pa.C3.ADs_Cr.C3.B3nica_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-El_Pa.C3.ADs_Cr.C3.B3nica-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-El_Pa.C3.ADs_Edici.C3.B3n_Impresa_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-El_Pa.C3.ADs_Edici.C3.B3n_Impresa-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Little Spain" was on <a href="/wiki/14th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="14th Street (Manhattan)">14th Street</a>, between <a href="/wiki/Seventh_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="Seventh Avenue (Manhattan)">Seventh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eighth_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="Eighth Avenue (Manhattan)">Eighth</a> Avenues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-El_Pa.C3.ADs_USA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-El_Pa.C3.ADs_USA-3">[3]</a></sup> A very different section of <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_Manhattan" title="Chelsea, Manhattan">Chelsea</a> existed on a stretch of 14th Street often referred to by residents as "Calle Catorce," or "Little Spain".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hartford_Courant_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hartford_Courant-4">[4]</a></sup> The Church of Our Lady of Guadelupe (No. 299) was founded in 1902, when Spaniards started to settle in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HERALD_TRIBUNE_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-HERALD_TRIBUNE-5">[5]</a></sup> Although the Spanish business have given way to such nightclubs as Nell's and Oh Johnny on the block between Seventh and Eighth Avenues, the Spanish food and gift emporium known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Casa_Moneo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Casa Moneo (page does not exist)">Casa Moneo</a> has been at 210 West 14th since 1929. In 2010 the documentary <b>Little Spain</b>, directed and written by <a href="/wiki/Artur_Balder" title="Artur Balder">Artur Balder</a>, was filmed in New York City. The documentary pulled together for first time an archive that reveals the untold history of the Spanish-American presence in Manhattan. They present the history of the streets of Little Spain in New York City throughout the 20th Century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hispanic_New_York_Project_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hispanic_New_York_Project-6">[6]</a></sup> The archive contains more than 450 photographs and 150 documents that have never been publicly displayed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EFE_America_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-EFE_America-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Antena_3_TV_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Antena_3_TV-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Informativos_Telecinco_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Informativos_Telecinco-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radio_Television_Espa.C3.B1ola_.28RTVE.29_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Radio_Television_Espa.C3.B1ola_.28RTVE.29-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Onda_Cero_Radio_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Onda_Cero_Radio-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La_Opinion_A_Coruna_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-La_Opinion_A_Coruna-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diario_Informacion_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Diario_Informacion-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other important commerces and Spanish business of Little Spain were restaurants like La Bilbaína, Trocadero Valencia, Bar Coruña, Little Spain Bar, Café Madrid, Mesón Flamenco, or <a href="/wiki/El_Faro_Restaurant" title="El Faro Restaurant">El Faro Restaurant</a>, established 1927, and still today open at 823 Greenwich St. The Iberia was a famous Spanish dress shop.</p>
<p>The heart of the <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> community in that area were the two landmarks: the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Benevolent_Society" title="Spanish Benevolent Society">Spanish Benevolent Society</a> and the Roman Catholic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_of_Our_Lady_of_Guadalupe_(New_York_City)" title="Church of Our Lady of Guadalupe (New York City)">Church of Our Lady of Guadalupe</a>, founded at the turn of the 19th century, being the first parish in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> with mass in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>. Just like with other immigrant neighborhoods of Manhattan, such as <a href="/wiki/Little_Italy" title="Little Italy">Little Italy</a>, Little Spain celebrated a feast day; that of <a href="/wiki/James,_son_of_Zebedee" title="James, son of Zebedee">St. James</a>. It was held in June, and was held until the mid-1990s. During this festival, the saint's image was the symbol of the feeling of the Spanish community and paraded down the street 14, which was cut to traffic for a week. In this time, food festivals were taking place shows of typical Spanish folklore.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Virginia_Admiral" title="Virginia Admiral">Virginia Admiral</a> and her son, <a href="/wiki/Robert_De_Niro" title="Robert De Niro">Robert Mario De Niro Jr.</a>, moved to a top floor studio at the 219 West 14th Street, owned by the Galician entrepreneur José García. According to investigations, Robert De Niro was living during the 1950s at the heart of Little Spain after the divorce of his parents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La_Voz_de_Galicia_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-La_Voz_de_Galicia-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
