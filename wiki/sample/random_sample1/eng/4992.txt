<p><b>Little Italy</b> is a neighborhood in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Manhattan" title="Lower Manhattan">lower</a> <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, once known for its large population of <a href="/wiki/Italian_Americans" title="Italian Americans">Italian Americans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lifeinitaly.com_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-lifeinitaly.com-1">[1]</a></sup> Today the neighborhood consists of only a few Italian stores and restaurants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is bounded on the west by <a href="/wiki/Tribeca" title="Tribeca">Tribeca</a> and <a href="/wiki/SoHo,_Manhattan" title="SoHo, Manhattan">Soho</a>, on the south by <a href="/wiki/Chinatown,_Manhattan" title="Chinatown, Manhattan">Chinatown</a>, on the east by the <a href="/wiki/Bowery" title="Bowery">Bowery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lower_East_Side" title="Lower East Side">Lower East Side</a>, and on the north by <a href="/wiki/Nolita" title="Nolita">Nolita</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Italy,_Manhattan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Little Italy on <a href="/wiki/Mulberry_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Mulberry Street (Manhattan)">Mulberry Street</a> used to extend as far south as <a href="/wiki/Worth_Street" title="Worth Street">Worth Street</a>, as far north as <a href="/wiki/Houston_Street" title="Houston Street">Houston Street</a>, as far west as <a href="/wiki/Lafayette_Street" title="Lafayette Street">Lafayette Street</a>, and as far east as <a href="/wiki/Bowery" title="Bowery">Bowery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lifeinitaly.com_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-lifeinitaly.com-1">[1]</a></sup> It is now only three blocks on Mulberry Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nypost.com_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-nypost.com-3">[3]</a></sup> Little Italy originated as Mulberry Bend. <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Riis" title="Jacob Riis">Jacob Riis</a> described Mulberry Bend as "the foul core of New York’s slums."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonelli1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli1-4">[4]</a></sup> During this time period "Immigrants of the late 19th century usually settled in ethnic neighborhood".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Therefore, the “mass immigration from italy during the 1880’s”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup> led to the large settlement of Italian immigrants in lower manhattan. The results of such migration had created an "influx of Italian immigrants" which had "led to the commercial gathering of their dwelling and business".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bill Tonelli from <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_(magazine)" title="New York (magazine)">New York</a></i> magazine said, "Once, Little Italy was like an insular Neapolitan village re-created on these shores, with its own language, customs, and financial and cultural institutions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonelli1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli1-4">[4]</a></sup> Little Italy was not the largest Italian neighborhood in New York City, as <a href="/wiki/East_Harlem" title="East Harlem">East Harlem</a> (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_Harlem" title="Italian Harlem">Italian Harlem</a>) had a larger Italian population. Tonelli said that Little Italy "was perhaps the city’s poorest Italian neighborhood".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonelli1_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli1-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1910 Little Italy had almost 10,000 Italians; that was the peak of the community's Italian population. At the turn of the 20th century over 90% of the residents of the Fourteenth Ward were of Italian birth or origins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonelli1_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli1-4">[4]</a></sup> Tonnelli said that it meant "that residents began moving out to more spacious digs almost as soon as they arrived."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonelli1_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli1-4">[4]</a></sup> Such a vastly gowing community impacted the "U.S. labor movement in the 20th century” by making up much of the labor population in the garment industry".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, many residents of the <a href="/wiki/Lower_East_Side" title="Lower East Side">Lower East Side</a> began moving to <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Staten_Island" title="Staten Island">Staten Island</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>. Chinese immigrants became an increased presence after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Immigration_Act_of_1965" title="U.S. Immigration Act of 1965">U.S. Immigration Act of 1965</a> removed immigration restrictions, and the <a href="/wiki/Chinatown,_Manhattan" title="Chinatown, Manhattan">Manhattan Chinatown</a> to Little Italy's south expanded. In 2004, Tonelli said, "You can go back 30 years and find newspaper clips chronicling the expansion of Chinatown and mourning the loss of Little Italy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonelli1_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prior to 2004, several upscale businesses entered the northern portion of the area between Houston and Kenmare Street. Tonelli said "Real-estate prices zoomed, making it even tougher for the old-timers—residents and businesspeople alike—to hang on."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonelli1_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli1-4">[4]</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> in 2001, areas below Houston Street were cut off for the rest of the fall of 2001. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Gennaro_feast" title="San Gennaro feast">San Gennaro feast</a>, scheduled for September 13, was postponed. Business from the <a href="/wiki/Financial_District,_Manhattan" title="Financial District, Manhattan">Financial District</a> dropped severely, due to the closure of <a href="/wiki/Park_Row_(Manhattan)" title="Park Row (Manhattan)">Park Row</a>, which connected Chinatown and the <a href="/wiki/Civic_Center,_Manhattan" title="Civic Center, Manhattan">Civic Center</a>; as a result, residents in Little Italy and Chinatown suffered. Tonelli said the post-9/11 events "strangely enough, ended up motivating all these newfangled efforts to save what’s left of the old neighborhood."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonelli1_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004 Tonelli said "Today, Little Italy is a veneer—50 or so restaurants and cafés catering to tourists, covering a dense neighborhood of tenements shared by recent Chinese immigrants, young Americans who can’t afford <a href="/wiki/SoHo,_Manhattan" title="SoHo, Manhattan">Soho</a>, and a few remaining real live Italians."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonelli1_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli1-4">[4]</a></sup> This sentiment has also been echoed by Italian culture and heritage website <i>ItalianAware</i>. The site has called the dominance of Italians in the area, "relatively short lived." It attributes this to the quick financial prosperity many Italians achieved, which afforded them the opportunity to leave the cramped neighborhood for areas in Brooklyn and Queens. The site also goes on to state that the area is currently referred to as Little Italy more out of nostalgia than as a reflection of a true ethnic population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ItalianAware_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ItalianAware-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, Little Italy and <a href="/wiki/Chinatown,_Manhattan" title="Chinatown, Manhattan">Chinatown</a> were listed in a single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historic_district_(United_States)" title="Historic district (United States)">historic district</a> on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRHP_listing_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NRHP_listing-9">[9]</a></sup> Little Italy, by this point, was shrinking rapidly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nypost.com_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-nypost.com-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_and_current_demographics"><span id="Demographics"></span> Historic and current demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Italy,_Manhattan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historic and current demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> sent its reporters to characterize the Little Italy/Mulberry neighborhood in May 1896:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>They are laborers; toilers in all grades of manual work; they are artisans, they are junkman, and here, too, dwell the rag pickers....There is a monster colony of Italians who might be termed the commercial or shop keeping community of the Latins. Here are all sorts of stores, pensions, groceries, fruit emporiums, tailors, shoemakers, wine merchants, importers, musical instrument makers....There are notaries, lawyers, doctors, apothecaries, undertakers.... There are more bankers among the Italians than among any other foreigners except the Germans in the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>As of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2000_U.S._Census" title="2000 U.S. Census">2000 U.S. Census</a>, 1,211 residents claiming Italian ancestry lived in three census tracts that make up Little Italy. Those residents comprise 8.25% of the population in the community, which is similar to the proportion of those of Italian ancestry throughout New York City. Bill Tonelli of <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_(magazine)" title="New York (magazine)">New York</a></i> magazine contrasted Little Italy with the <a href="/wiki/Chinatown,_Manhattan" title="Chinatown, Manhattan">Manhattan Chinatown</a>; in 2000, of the residents of the portions of Chinatown south of Grand Street, 81% were of Chinese origins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonelli1_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, Tonelli revisited the issue, saying, "Little Italy may always endure as an open-air theme park of nineteenth- and twentieth-century European immigration to the Lower East Side... But you’ll spend a long time in the neighborhood before you hear anyone speak Italian, and then the speaker will be a tourist from Milan."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonelli2_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli2-11">[11]</a></sup> Tonelli added, "You have to slow your gaze to find the neighbors in this neighborhood, because they’re so overwhelmed and outnumbered by the tourists. But once you focus, you can see them, standing (or sitting) in the interstices, taking in the scene, like the group of men, mostly senior citizens, loitering contentedly under an awning on Mulberry Street."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonelli2_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli2-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_attractions">Cultural attractions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Italy,_Manhattan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cultural attractions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Little_Italy.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Little_Italy.JPG/250px-Little_Italy.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Little_Italy.JPG/375px-Little_Italy.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Little_Italy.JPG/500px-Little_Italy.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>

<a href="/wiki/Mulberry_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Mulberry Street (Manhattan)">Mulberry Street</a> in Little Italy</div>

<p>Little Italy was home to dozens of restaurants that serve authentic Italian cuisine, but between March 2013 and March 2014, eight eateries closed down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 2004, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sorrento_Lactalis" title="Sorrento Lactalis">Sorrento Lactalis</a> funds neighborhood cultural events in Little Italy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonelli1_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Feast of San Gennaro originally was once only a one-day religious commemoration. It began in September 1926 with the new arrival of immigrants from Naples. The Italian immigrants congregated along Mulberry Street in Manhattan's <i>Little Italy</i> to celebrate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Gennaro" title="San Gennaro">San Gennaro</a> as the Patron Saint of <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>. The <i>Feast of San Gennaro</i> is a large street fair, lasting 11 days, that takes place every September along Mulberry Street between Houston and Canal Streets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The festival is an annual celebration of <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Italy" title="Culture of Italy">Italian culture</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian-American" title="Italian-American">Italian-American</a> community. In 1995 Mort Berkowitz became the professional manager of a community group that had been formed to take over management of the San Gennaro feast. Since then, Berkowitz became involved in other recreational activities in Little Italy, including the summer, <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">Carnevale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Day" title="Columbus Day">Columbus Day</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonelli1_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Richard Alba, a sociologist and professor at <a href="/wiki/University_at_Albany,_SUNY" title="University at Albany, SUNY">University at Albany, SUNY</a>, said "The fascinating part here is the way in which ethnic tourism—not only by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian-Americans" title="Italian-Americans">Italian-Americans</a> but by people who want to see an authentic urban village—keeps these neighborhoods going."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonelli2_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli2-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organized_crime_and_the_Mafia">Organized crime and the Mafia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Italy,_Manhattan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Organized crime and the Mafia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Little Italy residents have seen organized crime since the early 1900s. Powerful members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian-American_Mafia" title="Italian-American Mafia">Italian Mafia</a> have operated in <i>Little Italy</i>.</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ignazio_Saietta" title="Ignazio Saietta">Ignazio "The Wolf" Lupo</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Morello_crime_family" title="Morello crime family">Morello crime family</a> boss operated in Little Italy from late 1890s-1920s)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Michele_Miranda" title="Michele Miranda">Michele "Big Mike" Miranda</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Caporegime" title="Caporegime">Capo</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Genovese_crime_family" title="Genovese crime family">Genovese crime family</a> operated in the neighborhood from the 1950s into the late 1960s)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Peter_DeFeo" title="Peter DeFeo">Peter DeFeo</a> (a <i>Genovese crime family</i> capo who operated an illegal <i>Italian lottery</i> in the 1960s into the 1970s)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Matthew_Ianniello" title="Matthew Ianniello">Matthew "Matty the Horse" Ianniello</a> (a <i>Genovese crime family</i> capo operated from his restaurant <i><a href="/wiki/Umberto%27s_Clam_House" title="Umberto's Clam House">Umberto's Clam House</a></i> in the 1970s)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Gotti" title="John Gotti">John Gotti</a> (boss of the <a href="/wiki/Gambino_crime_family" title="Gambino crime family">Gambino crime family</a> operated from the <a href="/wiki/Ravenite_Social_Club" title="Ravenite Social Club">Ravenite Social Club</a> in the late 1980s into the early 1990s)</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Italy,_Manhattan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Little Italy was the locale of the fictional <a href="/wiki/Corleone_family" title="Corleone family">Corleone crime family</a> depicted in the novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Godfather_(novel)" title="The Godfather (novel)">The Godfather</a></i> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Godfather_Trilogy" title="Godfather Trilogy">three movies</a> based on it. It is the setting for the 1973 film <i><a href="/wiki/Mean_Streets" title="Mean Streets">Mean Streets</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Robert_De_Niro" title="Robert De Niro">Robert De Niro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Keitel" title="Harvey Keitel">Harvey Keitel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Italian_American_neighborhoods_in_New_York_City">Other Italian American neighborhoods in New York City</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Italy,_Manhattan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other Italian American neighborhoods in New York City">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Italian_American_neighborhoods" title="List of Italian American neighborhoods">List of Italian American neighborhoods</a></div>
<p>Other Italian American neighborhoods in New York City include:</p>
<ul>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> - East Harlem (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_Harlem" title="Italian Harlem">Italian Harlem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pleasant_Avenue" title="Pleasant Avenue">Pleasant Avenue</a>)</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/The_Bronx" title="The Bronx">the Bronx</a> - <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Avenue" title="Arthur Avenue">Arthur Avenue</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fordham,_Bronx" title="Fordham, Bronx">Fordham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morris_Park,_Bronx" title="Morris Park, Bronx">Morris Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Country_Club,_Bronx" title="Country Club, Bronx">Country Club</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelham_Bay,_Bronx" title="Pelham Bay, Bronx">Pelham Bay</a></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a> - <a href="/wiki/Bensonhurst,_Brooklyn" title="Bensonhurst, Brooklyn">Bensonhurst</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bay_Ridge" title="Bay Ridge">Bay Ridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dyker_Heights,_Brooklyn" title="Dyker Heights, Brooklyn">Dyker Heights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bath_Beach,_Brooklyn" title="Bath Beach, Brooklyn">Bath Beach</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Brooklyn" title="South Brooklyn">South Brooklyn</a>, and other various neighborhoods in Brooklyn</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Queens" title="Queens">Queens</a> - <a href="/wiki/Howard_Beach,_Queens" title="Howard Beach, Queens">Howard Beach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ozone_Park,_Queens" title="Ozone Park, Queens">Ozone Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middle_Village,_Queens" title="Middle Village, Queens">Middle Village</a> and other various neighborhoods in Queens</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Staten_Island" title="Staten Island">Staten Island</a> - the borough has the highest proportion of Italian Americans of any county in the United States. Over 200,000 residents claim Italian heritage (over 40%), with <a href="/wiki/Rosebank,_Staten_Island" title="Rosebank, Staten Island">Rosebank</a> being the first Italian enclave.</li>
</ul>
