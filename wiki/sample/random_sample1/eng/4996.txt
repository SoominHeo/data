<p><b>Richard Martin Willstätter</b>, <span style="font-size:85%;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreign_Member_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Foreign Member of the Royal Society">ForMemRS</a></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-frs-1">[1]</a></sup> (13 August 1872 – 3 August 1942) was a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_chemist" title="Organic chemist">organic chemist</a> whose study of the structure of plant pigments, <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">chlorophyll</a> included, won him the 1915 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_for_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize for Chemistry">Nobel Prize for Chemistry</a>. Willstätter invented <a href="/wiki/Paper_chromatography" title="Paper chromatography">paper chromatography</a> independently of <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Tsvet" title="Mikhail Tsvet">Mikhail Tsvet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Willst%C3%A4tter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Willstätter was born into a Jewish family in <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He was the son of Sophie (Ulmann) and Maxwell Willstätter, a textile merchant. He went to school there and, when his family moved, he attended the Technical School in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>. At age 18 he entered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Munich" title="University of Munich">University of Munich</a> to study science and stayed for the next fifteen years. He was in the Department of Chemistry, first as a student of <a href="/wiki/Adolf_von_Baeyer" title="Adolf von Baeyer">Adolf von Baeyer</a>—he received his doctorate in 1894 - then as a faculty member. His doctoral thesis was on the structure of <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a>. Willstätter continued his research into other <a href="/wiki/Alkaloid" title="Alkaloid">alkaloids</a> and synthesized several of them. In 1896 he was named <a href="/wiki/Lecturer" title="Lecturer">Lecturer</a> and in 1902 <i>Professor extraordinarius</i> (professor without a chair).</p>
<p>In 1905 he left Munich to become professor at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ETH_Z%C3%BCrich" title="ETH Zürich">ETH Zürich</a> and there he worked on the plant pigment <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">chlorophyll</a>. He first determined its empirical formula.</p>
<p>In 1912 he became professor of chemistry at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Berlin" title="University of Berlin">University of Berlin</a> and director of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaiser_Wilhelm_Institute_for_Chemistry" title="Kaiser Wilhelm Institute for Chemistry">Kaiser Wilhelm Institute for Chemistry</a>, studying the structure of pigments of flowers and fruits. It was here that Willstätter showed that chlorophyll was a mixture of two compounds, <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll_a" title="Chlorophyll a">chlorophyll a</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll_b" title="Chlorophyll b">chlorophyll b</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1915 his friend <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Haber" title="Fritz Haber">Fritz Haber</a> asked him to join in the development of poison gasses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Willstätter would not work on poisons but agreed to work on protection. He and his coworkers developed a three layer filter that absorbed all of the enemy’s gasses. Thirty million were manufactured by 1917 and Willstätter was awarded the Iron Cross Second Class.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1916 he returned to Munich as the successor to his mentor Baeyer. During the 1920s Willstätter investigated the mechanisms of <a href="/wiki/Enzyme_kinetics" title="Enzyme kinetics">enzyme reactions</a> and did much to establish that <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> are chemical substances, not biological organisms.</p>
<p>In 1924 Willstätter's career came to "a tragic end when, as a gesture against increasing antisemitism, he announced his retirement." According to his Nobel biography:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> "Expressions of confidence by the Faculty, by his students and by the Minister failed to shake the fifty-three year old scientist in his decision to resign. He lived on in retirement in Munich....Dazzling offers both at home and abroad were alike rejected by him." His only research was with assistants who telephoned their results.</p>
<p>In 1939 Willstätter emigrated to <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>. He spent the last three years of his life there in <a href="/wiki/Muralto" title="Muralto">Muralto</a> near <a href="/wiki/Locarno" title="Locarno">Locarno</a> writing his autobiography. He died of a heart attack in 1942.</p>
<p>Willstätter's autobiography, <i>Aus meinem Leben</i>, was not published in German until 1949. It was translated into English as <i>From My Life</i> in 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willst.C3.A4tter_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Willst.C3.A4tter-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anecdote">Anecdote</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Willst%C3%A4tter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Anecdote">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1911 the fledgling American chemist <a href="/wiki/Michael_Heidelberger" title="Michael Heidelberger">Michael Heidelberger</a> went to work for a year with Willstätter in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zurich" title="Zurich">Zurich</a>. Willstätter helped his somewhat impecunious American student by sharing the cost of laboratory supplies with him, arranging that when expensive materials, such as <a href="/wiki/Silver_nitrate" title="Silver nitrate">silver nitrate</a>, were to be bought, it was his turn to pay, while Heidelberger took turns buying cheaper materials like <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>. "Better training than that you couldn't have," Heidelberger summed up his experience with Willstätter. They remained friends for life.</p>
