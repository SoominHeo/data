<p><b>Richard Sorge</b> (October 4, 1895 – November 7, 1944) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main_Intelligence_Directorate_(Russia)" title="Main Intelligence Directorate (Russia)">Soviet military intelligence</a> <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_agency" title="Intelligence agency">officer</a>, active before and during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, working as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undercover" title="Undercover">undercover</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_people" title="German people">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Journalist" title="Journalist">journalist</a> in both <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Empire of Japan</a>. His codename was "Ramsay" (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Рамза́й</span>).</p>
<p>Sorge is most famous for his service in Japan in 1940 and 1941, when he provided information about <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Operation_Barbarossa" title="Operation Barbarossa">plan</a> to attack the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, although he did not succeed in finding out the exact date of the attack.</p>
<p>In mid-September 1941, he informed the Soviet command that Japan was not going to attack the Soviet Union in the near future, which allowed the command to transfer 18 divisions, 1,700 tanks, and over 1,500 aircraft from <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">Far East</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(Soviet_Union)" title="Western Front (Soviet Union)">Western Front</a> against Nazi Germany during the most critical months of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_for_Moscow" title="Battle for Moscow">Battle for Moscow</a>; one of the turning points of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.</p>
<p>A month later Sorge was arrested in Japan on the counts of espionage. The German <i><a href="/wiki/Abwehr" title="Abwehr">Abwehr</a></i> legitimately denied he was an agent; USSR repudiated him and refused three offers to spare him through a prisoner exchange. He was tortured, forced to confess, tried, and then hanged in November 1944. Two decades passed before he was posthumously awarded the title of <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Hero of the Soviet Union">Hero of the Soviet Union</a> in 1964.</p>

<p>Sorge was born in the settlement of <a href="/wiki/Sabun%C3%A7u,_Baku" title="Sabunçu, Baku">Sabunchi</a>, a suburb of <a href="/wiki/Baku" title="Baku">Baku</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baku_Governorate" title="Baku Governorate">Baku Governorate</a> of the Russian Empire (modern <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He was the youngest of nine children of Wilhelm Richard Sorge (d. 1907), a German mining engineer employed by the Caucasian Oil Company, and his Russian wife Nina Semionovna Kobieleva.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> His father's lucrative contract expired a few years later, and the family moved back to <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">Germany</a>. In Sorge's own words,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The one thing that made my life a little different from the average was a strong awareness of the fact that I had been born in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Caucasus" title="Southern Caucasus">southern Caucasus</a> and that we had moved to <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> when I was very small.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The cosmopolitan Sorge household was "very different from the average <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bourgeois" title="Bourgeois">bourgeois</a> home in Berlin."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although Sorge considered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friedrich_Adolf_Sorge" title="Friedrich Adolf Sorge">Friedrich Adolf Sorge</a>, an associate of <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Friedrich Engels</a>, to be his grandfather, he was in fact his great-uncle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sorge enlisted in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_German_Army" title="Imperial German Army">German Army</a> in October 1914; shortly after the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. At age 18 he was posted to a <a href="/wiki/Field_artillery" title="Field artillery">field artillery</a> battalion with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3rd_Guards_Division_(German_Empire)" title="3rd Guards Division (German Empire)">3rd Guards Division</a>. He served on the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a>, and was severely wounded there in March 1916. <a href="/wiki/Shrapnel_shell" title="Shrapnel shell">Shrapnel</a> cut off three of his fingers and broke both his legs, causing a lifelong limp. He was promoted to the rank of <a href="/wiki/Corporal" title="Corporal">corporal</a>, received the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Cross" title="Iron Cross">Iron Cross</a> and was later medically discharged.</p>
<p>During his convalescence he read Marx and became a <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">Communist</a>, mainly due to the influence of the father of a nurse with whom he had developed a relationship. He spent the rest of the war studying <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> at the universities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Berlin" title="University of Berlin">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kiel" title="University of Kiel">Kiel</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hamburg" title="University of Hamburg">Hamburg</a>. Sorge received his <a href="/wiki/Doctorate#Germany" title="Doctorate">doctorate</a> in <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a> (Dr. rer. pol.) from Hamburg in August 1919.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibid1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ibid1-7">[7]</a></sup> He also joined the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Germany" title="Communist Party of Germany">Communist Party of Germany</a>. His political views, however, got him fired from both a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teaching" title="Teaching">teaching</a> job and <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">coal mining</a> work. He emigrated to the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, where he became a junior agent for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comintern" title="Comintern">Comintern</a> in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_military_intelligence_agent">Soviet military intelligence agent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Sorge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Soviet military intelligence agent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sorge was recruited as an agent for Soviet intelligence. With the cover of a journalist, he was sent to various European countries to assess the possibility of communist revolutions.</p>
<p>From 1920 to 1922, Sorge lived in <a href="/wiki/Solingen" title="Solingen">Solingen</a>, in present-day <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>, Germany. He was joined there by Christiane Gerlach, ex-wife of Dr <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Albert_Gerlach" title="Kurt Albert Gerlach">Kurt Albert Gerlach</a>, a wealthy communist and professor of political science in Kiel, who had taught Sorge. Sorge and Christiane married in May 1921. In 1922, he was relocated to <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>, where he gathered intelligence about the business community. In the summer of 1923, he took part in the <i>Erste Marxistische Arbeitswoche</i> ("First Marxist Work Week" conference) in <a href="/wiki/Ilmenau" title="Ilmenau">Ilmenau</a>. Sorge continued his work as a journalist, and also helped organized the library of the <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Social_Research" title="Institute for Social Research">Institute for Social Research</a>, a new Marxist think-tank in Frankfurt.</p>
<p>In 1924, he and Christiane moved to Moscow, where he officially joined the International Liaison Department of the Comintern, which was also an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OGPU" title="OGPU">OGPU</a> intelligence-gathering body. Apparently, Sorge's dedication to duty led to his divorce. In 1929, Sorge became part of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a>'s Fourth Department (the later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glavnoye_Razvedyvatel%27noye_Upravleniye" title="Glavnoye Razvedyvatel'noye Upravleniye">GRU</a>, or military intelligence).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibid1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ibid1-7">[7]</a></sup> He remained with the Department for the rest of his life.</p>
<p>In 1929 Sorge went to the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> to study the labor movement there, the status of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Great_Britain" title="Communist Party of Great Britain">Communist Party of Great Britain</a>, and the country's political and economic conditions. He was instructed to remain undercover and stay out of politics.</p>
<p>In November 1929, Sorge was sent to <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Germany</a>. He was instructed to join the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> and not associate with any left-wing activists. As cover, he got a job with the agricultural newspaper <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Deutsche_Getreide-Zeitung&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Deutsche Getreide-Zeitung (page does not exist)">Deutsche Getreide-Zeitung</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China_1930">China 1930</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Sorge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: China 1930">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1930, Sorge was sent to <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a>. For cover he worked as the editor of a German news service and for the <i><a href="/wiki/Frankfurter_Zeitung" title="Frankfurter Zeitung">Frankfurter Zeitung</a></i>. He contacted another agent, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Clausen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Max Clausen (page does not exist)">Max Clausen</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Christiansen-Clausen" title="de:Max Christiansen-Clausen">de</a>)</span>. Sorge also met the German Soviet agent <a href="/wiki/Ursula_Kuczynski" title="Ursula Kuczynski">Ursula Kuczynski</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and American journalist <a href="/wiki/Agnes_Smedley" title="Agnes Smedley">Agnes Smedley</a>. Smedley, a well-known left-wing journalist, also worked for the <i>Frankfurter Zeitung</i>. She introduced Sorge to <a href="/wiki/Hotsumi_Ozaki" title="Hotsumi Ozaki">Hotsumi Ozaki</a> of the Japanese newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Asahi_Shimbun" title="Asahi Shimbun">Asahi Shimbun</a></i> (a future Sorge recruit), and to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hanako_Ishii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hanako Ishii (page does not exist)">Hanako Ishii</a>, with whom he would become romantically involved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a journalist, Sorge established himself as an expert on Chinese agriculture. In this role, he travelled around the country, contacting members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party" title="Chinese Communist Party">Chinese Communist Party</a>. In January 1932, Sorge reported on <a href="/wiki/January_28_Incident" title="January 28 Incident">fighting between Chinese and Japanese troops</a> in the streets of <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a>. In December he was recalled to Moscow.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moscow_1933">Moscow 1933</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Sorge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Moscow 1933">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sorge returned to Moscow, where he wrote a book about Chinese agriculture. He also married <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yekaterina_Maximova&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yekaterina Maximova (page does not exist)">Yekaterina Maximova</a> ("Katya"), a woman he had met in China and brought back with him to Russia.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan_1933">Japan 1933</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Sorge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Japan 1933">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stamps_of_Germany_(DDR)_1976,_MiNr_Block_044.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1568" data-file-width="1986" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Stamps_of_Germany_%28DDR%29_1976%2C_MiNr_Block_044.jpg/220px-Stamps_of_Germany_%28DDR%29_1976%2C_MiNr_Block_044.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Stamps_of_Germany_%28DDR%29_1976%2C_MiNr_Block_044.jpg/330px-Stamps_of_Germany_%28DDR%29_1976%2C_MiNr_Block_044.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Stamps_of_Germany_%28DDR%29_1976%2C_MiNr_Block_044.jpg/440px-Stamps_of_Germany_%28DDR%29_1976%2C_MiNr_Block_044.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In May 1933, the GRU decided to have Sorge organize an <a href="/wiki/Espionage" title="Espionage">intelligence network</a> in Japan. He was given the code name "Ramsay" ("Рамзай" (Ramzai, Ramzay). He first went to Berlin, to renew contacts in <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Germany</a>, and obtain a new newspaper assignment in Japan as cover.</p>
<p>In Berlin, he insinuated himself into the Nazi Party and read <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi_propaganda" title="Nazi propaganda">Nazi propaganda</a>, in particular <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Mein_Kampf" title="Mein Kampf">Mein Kampf</a></i>. Sorge attended so many beer halls with his new acquaintances that he gave up drinking so as not to say anything inappropriate. His abstinence from drinking did not make his Nazi companions suspicious. It was an example of his devotion to and absorption in his mission, as he was a heavy drinker. He later explained to <a href="/wiki/Hede_Massing" title="Hede Massing">Hede Massing</a>, "That was the bravest thing I ever did. Never will I be able to drink enough to make up for this time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Later, his drinking came to undermine his work.</p>
<p>While in <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, he received commissions from two newspapers, the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Berliner_B%C3%B6rsen_Zeitung&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Berliner Börsen Zeitung (page does not exist)">Berliner Börsen Zeitung</a></i> and the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=T%C3%A4gliche_Rundschau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tägliche Rundschau (page does not exist)">Tägliche Rundschau</a></i>, to report from Japan; also the Nazi theoretical journal <i><a href="/wiki/Geopolitik" title="Geopolitik">Geopolitik</a></i>. Later he was able to obtain work from the <i>Frankfurter Zeitung</i>. He went to Japan via the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, passing through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> in August 1933.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sorge arrived in <a href="/wiki/Yokohama" title="Yokohama">Yokohama</a> on September 6, 1933. He was warned by his commanders not to have contact with the underground <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Communist_Party#History" title="Japanese Communist Party">Japanese Communist Party</a> or with the Soviet Embassy in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>. His intelligence network in Japan included Red Army officer and radio operator Max Clausen,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hotsumi_Ozaki" title="Hotsumi Ozaki">Hotsumi Ozaki</a>, and two other Comintern agents, <a href="/wiki/Branko_Vukeli%C4%87_(spy)" title="Branko Vukelić (spy)">Branko Vukelić</a>, a journalist working for the French magazine, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=VU_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="VU (magazine) (page does not exist)">Vu</a></i> and a Japanese journalist, <a href="/wiki/Yotoku_Miyagi" title="Yotoku Miyagi">Miyagi Yotoku</a>, who was employed by the English-language newspaper, the <i>Japan Advertiser</i>. Max Clausen's wife Anna acted as ring courier from time to time. From summer 1937, Clausen operated under cover of his business, M Clausen Shokai, suppliers of blueprint machinery and reproduction services. The business had been set up with Soviet funds but in time became a commercial success.</p>
<p>Between 1933 and 1934 Sorge formed a network of informants. His agents had contacts with senior politicians and picked up information on Japanese foreign policy. His agent Ozaki developed a close contact with the prime minister <a href="/wiki/Fumimaro_Konoe" title="Fumimaro Konoe">Fumimaro Konoe</a>. Ozaki copied secret documents for Sorge.</p>
<p>As he appeared to be an ardent Nazi, Sorge was welcome at the German Embassy. Ironically, Sorge may have been much safer spying for the Soviets in Japan during the late 1930s than if he had been in Moscow. Claiming too many pressing responsibilities, he disobeyed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Josef_Stalin" title="Josef Stalin">Stalin's</a> orders to return to the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> in 1937 during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Purge" title="Great Purge">Great Purge</a>, as he realized the risk of arrest and execution, given Stalin's general paranoia (especially towards the intelligence community) and Sorge's German ancestry. In fact, two of Sorge's earliest GRU handlers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yan_Karlovich_Berzin" title="Yan Karlovich Berzin">Yan Karlovich Berzin</a> and his successor, Artur Khristianovich Artuzov, were shot during the purges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wartime_intelligence_supplied_by_the_Sorge_Ring">Wartime intelligence supplied by the Sorge Ring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Sorge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wartime intelligence supplied by the Sorge Ring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German-Japanese_relations" title="German-Japanese relations">German-Japanese relations</a></div>
<p>Sorge supplied Soviet intelligence with information about the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Comintern_Pact" title="Anti-Comintern Pact">Anti-Comintern Pact</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German-Japanese_Pact" title="German-Japanese Pact">German-Japanese Pact</a>. In 1941, through his Embassy contacts, he learned of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Barbarossa" title="Operation Barbarossa">Operation Barbarossa</a>, the imminent <a href="/wiki/Axis_powers" title="Axis powers">Axis</a> invasion of the USSR, and even the approximate date. Moscow received the report, but ultimately <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a> and other top leaders ignored Sorge's warnings, as well as those of other sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has been rumored that Sorge provided the exact date of "Barbarossa", but historian <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Prange" title="Gordon Prange">Gordon Prange</a> in 1984 concluded that the closest Sorge came was June 20, 1941 and that Sorge himself never claimed to have discovered the correct date (June 22) in advance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The date of June 20 was given to Sorge by <i><a href="/wiki/Oberstleutnant" title="Oberstleutnant">Oberstleutnant</a></i> (lieutenant-colonel) Erwin Scholl, the deputy military attaché at the German embassy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Soviet press reported in 1964 that on June 15, 1941, Sorge had sent a radio dispatch saying that "The war will begin on June 22."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Prange, who did not have access to material released by the Russian authorities in the 1990s, did not accept the veracity of this report. Stalin was quoted as having ridiculed Sorge and his intelligence before "Barbarossa":</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>There's this bastard who's set up factories and brothels in Japan and even deigned to report the date of the German attack as 22 June. Are you suggesting I should believe him too?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Sorge advised the Red Army on September 14, 1941, that Japan would not attack the Soviet Union until:</p>
<ol>
<li>Moscow was captured</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Kwantung_Army" title="Kwantung Army">Kwantung Army</a> was three times the size of Soviet Far Eastern forces</li>
<li>A civil war had started in Siberia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>This information made possible the transfer of Soviet divisions from the Far East, although the presence of the <a href="/wiki/Kwantung_Army" title="Kwantung Army">Kwantung Army</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a> necessitated the Soviet Union's keeping a large number of troops on the eastern borders...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Various writers have speculated that this information allowed the release of Siberian divisions for the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Moscow" title="Battle of Moscow">Battle of Moscow</a>, where the German Army suffered its first tactical defeat in the war. To this end, Sorge's information might have been the most important military intelligence work in World War II.</p>
<p>Another important item allegedly reported by Sorge may have affected the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stalingrad" title="Battle of Stalingrad">Battle of Stalingrad</a>. Sorge reported that Japan would attack the Soviet Union from the East as soon as the German army captured any city on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volga" title="Volga">Volga</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sorge's rival and opponent in Japan and east Asia was <a href="/wiki/Ivar_Lissner" title="Ivar Lissner">Ivar Lissner</a>, an agent of the German <i><a href="/wiki/Abwehr" title="Abwehr">Abwehr</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arrests_and_trials">Arrests and trials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Sorge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Arrests and trials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the war progressed, Sorge was in increasing danger, but he continued his service. His radio messages were enciphered with unbreakable <a href="/wiki/One-time_pad" title="One-time pad">one-time pads</a> (always used by the Soviet intelligence agencies), and appeared as gibberish. However, due to the increasing number of these mystery messages, the Japanese began to suspect that an intelligence ring was operating. Sorge was also coming under increasing suspicion in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>. By 1941 the Nazis had instructed <a href="/wiki/Josef_Albert_Meisinger" title="Josef Albert Meisinger">Josef Albert Meisinger</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Gestapo" title="Gestapo">Gestapo</a> <a href="/wiki/Resident_spy" title="Resident spy">resident</a> at the German Embassy in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>, to begin monitoring Sorge and his activities. The <i><a href="/wiki/Kempeitai" title="Kempeitai">Kempeitai</a></i> (Japanese secret police) intercepted many messages and began to close in. Ozaki was arrested on October 14, 1941 and immediately interrogated.</p>
<p>Sorge was arrested shortly thereafter on October 18, 1941, in Tokyo. The next day, a brief memo notified German ambassador <a href="/wiki/Eugen_Ott_(ambassador)" title="Eugen Ott (ambassador)">Eugen Ott</a> that Sorge had been arrested "on suspicion of espionage" together with Max Clausen. Ott was both surprised and outraged, and assumed it was a case of "Japanese espionage hysteria". He thought that Sorge had been discovered passing secret information on the Japan-US negotiations to the German embassy, and also that the arrest could be due to anti-German elements in the Japanese government. Nonetheless, he immediately sided with Japanese authorities to "investigate the incident fully".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> It was not until a few months later that Japanese authorities announced that Sorge had in fact been indicted as a Soviet agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was incarcerated in <a href="/wiki/Sugamo_Prison" title="Sugamo Prison">Sugamo Prison</a>. Initially, the Japanese believed that, due to his Nazi Party membership and German ties, Sorge was an <i>Abwehr</i> agent. However, the <i>Abwehr</i> denied that he was one of their agents. Under torture, Sorge confessed, but the Soviet Union denied he was a Soviet agent. The Japanese made three overtures to the Soviet Union, offering to trade Sorge for one of their own spies. However, the Soviet Union declined all the offers, maintaining that Sorge was unknown to them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sakaida_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-sakaida-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Execution">Execution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Sorge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Execution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Richard Sorge was hanged on November 7, 1944, at 10:20 a.m. Tokyo time in Sugamo Prison; Hotsumi Ozaki was hanged earlier in the same day. The Soviet Union did not officially acknowledge Sorge until 1964. It was argued that Sorge's biggest coup led to his undoing, because Stalin could not afford to let it become known that he had rejected Sorge's warning about the German attack in June 1941. However, nations seldom officially recognize their own undercover agents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sorge was survived by his mother, then living in Germany, and he left his estate to Anna Clausen, the wife of his radio operator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibid2_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-ibid2-28">[28]</a></sup> He was buried in the Sugamo Prison (Zhogaya) graveyard,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibid2_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-ibid2-28">[28]</a></sup> but his remains were later relocated to <a href="/wiki/Tama_Cemetery" title="Tama Cemetery">Tama Cemetery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fuch%C5%AB,_Tokyo" title="Fuchū, Tokyo">Fuchū, Tokyo</a>.</p>

<p>In 1954, <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West German</a> film director <a href="/wiki/Veit_Harlan" title="Veit Harlan">Veit Harlan</a> wrote and directed the film <i>Betrayal of Germany</i> (<i>Verrat an Deutschland</i>) about Sorge's espionage in Japan. Harlan had been the favorite filmmaker of Nazi propaganda minister <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Goebbels" title="Joseph Goebbels">Joseph Goebbels</a>, and directed many propaganda films, including <a href="/wiki/Jud_S%C3%BC%C3%9F_(1940_film)" title="Jud Süß (1940 film)">Jud Süss</a>. Harlan's film is a romantic drama, starring Harlan's wife <a href="/wiki/Kristina_S%C3%B6derbaum" title="Kristina Söderbaum">Kristina Söderbaum</a>, as Sorge's love interest. The film was prohibited in West Germany only two days after its release in 1955 and only released again after re-editing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1961 a movie called <i>Qui êtes-vous, Monsieur Sorge?</i> (<i>Who Are You, Mr. Sorge?</i>) was produced in France in collaboration with West Germany, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and Japan. This movie was very popular in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> as well. The part of Sorge was played by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Holtzmann&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thomas Holtzmann (page does not exist)">Thomas Holtzmann</a>.</p>
<p>On November 5, 1964, 20 years after his death, the Soviet government awarded Sorge with the title of <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Hero of the Soviet Union">Hero of the Soviet Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Sorge's widow Hanako Ishii received a Soviet and Russian pension until her death in July 2000 in Tokyo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sakaida_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-sakaida-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1965, three <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East German</a> journalists published <i>Dr. Sorge funkt aus Tokyo</i> in celebration of Sorge and his actions. In the lead up to the award, Sorge's claim that Friedrich Adolf Sorge was his grandfather was repeated in the Soviet press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In a strange Cold War oddity, these authors stirred up a free speech scandal with patriotic letters to former Nazis in West Germany, causing the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verfassungsschutz" title="Verfassungsschutz">Verfassungsschutz</a></i> to issue a stern warning in early 1967: "If you receive mail from a certain <a href="/wiki/Julius_Mader" title="Julius Mader">Julius Mader</a>, do not reply to him and pass on the letter to the respective security authorities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1971, a comic book based on Sorge's life, titled <i>Wywiadowca XX wieku</i> ("20th Century intelligence officer"), was published in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_Poland" title="People's Republic of Poland">People's Republic of Poland</a> to familiarize younger readers with Sorge.</p>
<p>Sorge also appears in <a href="/wiki/Osamu_Tezuka" title="Osamu Tezuka">Osamu Tezuka</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adolf_(manga)" title="Adolf (manga)">Adolf</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a>.</p>
<p>In his 1981 book, <i>Their Trade is Treachery</i>, author <a href="/wiki/Chapman_Pincher" title="Chapman Pincher">Chapman Pincher</a> asserted that Sorge, a GRU agent himself, recruited Englishman <a href="/wiki/Roger_Hollis" title="Roger Hollis">Roger Hollis</a> in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> in the early 1930s to provide information. Hollis later returned to England, joined <a href="/wiki/MI5" title="MI5">MI5</a> just before <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> began, and eventually became Director-General of MI5 from 1956 to 1965. As detailed by former MI5 staffer <a href="/wiki/Peter_Wright" title="Peter Wright">Peter Wright</a> in his 1988 book <i><a href="/wiki/Spycatcher" title="Spycatcher">Spycatcher</a></i>, Hollis was accused of being a Soviet agent, but despite several lengthy and seemingly thorough investigations, no conclusive proof was ever obtained.</p>
<p>One of <a href="/wiki/Aleksandar_Hemon" title="Aleksandar Hemon">Aleksandar Hemon</a>'s first stories in English is "The Sorge Spy Ring" (<i><a href="/wiki/TriQuarterly" title="TriQuarterly">TriQuarterly</a></i>, 1997).</p>
<p>The 2003 Japanese film <i><a href="/wiki/Spy_Sorge" title="Spy Sorge">Spy Sorge</a></i>, directed by Masahiro Shinoda, details his exploits in Shanghai and Japan. In the film he is portrayed by Scottish actor <a href="/wiki/Iain_Glen" title="Iain Glen">Iain Glen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
