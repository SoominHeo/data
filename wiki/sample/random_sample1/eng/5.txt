<p>In <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>, <b>Valhalla</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <i><b>Valhöll</b></i> "hall of the slain"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ORCHARD171-172_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ORCHARD171-172-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a majestic, enormous hall located in <a href="/wiki/Asgard" title="Asgard">Asgard</a>, ruled over by the god <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a>. Chosen by Odin, half of those who die in combat travel to Valhalla upon death, led by <a href="/wiki/Valkyrie" title="Valkyrie">valkyries</a>, while the other half go to the goddess <a href="/wiki/Freyja" title="Freyja">Freyja</a>'s field <a href="/wiki/F%C3%B3lkvangr" title="Fólkvangr">Fólkvangr</a>. In Valhalla, the dead join the masses of those who have died in combat known as <a href="/wiki/Einherjar" title="Einherjar">Einherjar</a>, as well as women who died during childbirth,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and various legendary <a href="/wiki/Germanic_mythology" title="Germanic mythology">Germanic heroes</a> and kings, as they prepare to aid Odin during the events of <a href="/wiki/Ragnar%C3%B6k" title="Ragnarök">Ragnarök</a>. Before the hall stands the golden tree <a href="/wiki/Glasir" title="Glasir">Glasir</a>, and the hall's ceiling is thatched with golden shields. Various creatures live around Valhalla, such as the stag <a href="/wiki/Eik%C3%BEyrnir" title="Eikþyrnir">Eikþyrnir</a> and the goat <a href="/wiki/Hei%C3%B0r%C3%BAn" title="Heiðrún">Heiðrún</a>, both described as standing atop Valhalla and consuming the foliage of the tree <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A6ra%C3%B0r" title="Læraðr">Læraðr</a>.</p>
<p>Valhalla is attested in the <i><a href="/wiki/Poetic_Edda" title="Poetic Edda">Poetic Edda</a></i>, compiled in the 13th century from earlier traditional sources, the <i><a href="/wiki/Prose_Edda" title="Prose Edda">Prose Edda</a></i>, written in the 13th century by <a href="/wiki/Snorri_Sturluson" title="Snorri Sturluson">Snorri Sturluson</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Heimskringla" title="Heimskringla">Heimskringla</a></i>, also written in the 13th century by Snorri Sturluson, and in stanzas of an anonymous 10th century poem commemorating the death of <a href="/wiki/Eric_Bloodaxe" title="Eric Bloodaxe">Eric Bloodaxe</a> known as <i><a href="/wiki/Eir%C3%ADksm%C3%A1l" title="Eiríksmál">Eiríksmál</a></i> as compiled in <i><a href="/wiki/Fagrskinna" title="Fagrskinna">Fagrskinna</a></i>. Valhalla has inspired various works of art, publication titles, popular culture references, and has become a term synonymous with a martial (or otherwise) hall of the chosen dead.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valhalla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Modern English noun <i>Valhalla</i> derives from Old Norse <i>Valhöll</i>, a compound noun composed of two elements: the masculine noun <i>valr</i> 'the slain' and the feminine noun <i>höll</i> 'hall'. <i>Valr</i> has <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognates</a> in other Germanic languages such as Old English <i>wæl</i> 'the slain, slaughter, carnage', <a href="/wiki/Old_Saxon" title="Old Saxon">Old Saxon</a> <i>wal-dād</i> 'murder', <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> 'battlefield, blood bath'. All of these forms descend from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> masculine noun *<i>walaz</i>. Among related Old Norse concepts, <i>valr</i> also appears as the first element of the nouns <i>valkyrja</i> 'chooser of the slain, <a href="/wiki/Valkyrie" title="Valkyrie">valkyrie</a>' and <a href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Freyja" title="List of names of Freyja"><i>Valfreyja</i>, one of the goddess Freyja's several names</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VALHALLA-ETYMOLOGY_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-VALHALLA-ETYMOLOGY-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second element, <i>höll</i>, is an extremely common Old Norse noun. It is cognate to Modern English <i>hall</i> and has the same meaning. Both developed from Proto-Germanic *<i>xallō</i> or *<i>hallō</i>, meaning 'covered place, hall', itself from the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_root" title="Proto-Indo-European root">Proto-Indo-European root</a> *<i>kol-</i>. As <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">philologists</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Calvert_Watkins" title="Calvert Watkins">Calvert Watkins</a> have noted, the same Indo-European root produced Old Norse <i>hel</i>, a proper noun employed for both the <a href="/wiki/Hel_(location)" title="Hel (location)">name of another afterlife location</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Hel_(being)" title="Hel (being)">supernatural female entity</a> who oversees it, as well as the modern English noun <i>hell</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VALHALLA-ETYMOLOGY_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-VALHALLA-ETYMOLOGY-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attestations">Attestations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valhalla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Attestations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poetic_Edda"><i>Poetic Edda</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valhalla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Poetic Edda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Valhalla is referenced at length in the <i>Poetic Edda</i> poem <i><a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%ADmnism%C3%A1l" title="Grímnismál">Grímnismál</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Helgakvi%C3%B0a_Hundingsbana_II" title="Helgakviða Hundingsbana II">Helgakviða Hundingsbana II</a></i>, while Valhalla receives lesser direct references in stanza 33 of the <i><a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lusp%C3%A1" title="Völuspá">Völuspá</a></i>, where the god <a href="/wiki/Baldr" title="Baldr">Baldr</a>'s death is referred to as the "woe of Valhalla",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LARRINGTON8_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-LARRINGTON8-4">[4]</a></sup> and in stanzas 1 to 3 of <i><a href="/wiki/Hyndlulj%C3%B3%C3%B0" title="Hyndluljóð">Hyndluljóð</a></i>, where the goddess <a href="/wiki/Freyja" title="Freyja">Freyja</a> states her intention of riding to Valhalla with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyndla" title="Hyndla">Hyndla</a>, in an effort to help <a href="/wiki/%C3%93ttar_(mythology)" title="Óttar (mythology)">Óttar</a>, as well as in stanzas 6 through 7, where Valhalla is mentioned again during a dispute between the two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LARRINGTON253-254_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-LARRINGTON253-254-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gr.C3.ADmnism.C3.A1l"><i>Grímnismál</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valhalla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Grímnismál">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In stanzas 8 to 10 of <i><a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%ADmnism%C3%A1l" title="Grímnismál">Grímnismál</a></i>, the god Odin (in the guise of <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Odin" title="List of names of Odin">Grímnir</a></i>) states that Valhalla is located in the realm of <a href="/wiki/Gla%C3%B0sheimr" title="Glaðsheimr">Glaðsheimr</a>. Odin describes Valhalla as shining and golden, and that it "rises peacefully" when seen from afar. From Valhalla, every day Odin chooses from those who have died in combat. Valhalla has <a href="/wiki/Spear" title="Spear">spear</a>-shafts for rafters, a roof thatched with shields, <a href="/wiki/Mail_(armour)" title="Mail (armour)">coats of mail</a> are strewn over its benches, a wolf hangs in front of its west doors, and an eagle hovers above it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LARRINGTON53_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-LARRINGTON53-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>From stanzas 22 to 24, more details are given by Odin about Valhalla: the holy doors of the ancient gate Valgrind stand before Valhalla, Valhalla has five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hundred_(word)" title="Hundred (word)">hundred</a> and forty doors that eight hundred men can exit from at once (from which the <a href="/wiki/Einherjar" title="Einherjar">einherjar</a> will flow forth to engage the wolf <a href="/wiki/Fenrir" title="Fenrir">Fenrir</a> at <a href="/wiki/Ragnar%C3%B6k" title="Ragnarök">Ragnarök</a>). Within Valhalla exists <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">Thor</a>'s hall <a href="/wiki/Bilskirnir" title="Bilskirnir">Bilskirnir</a>, and within it exist five hundred and forty rooms, and of all the halls within Valhalla, Odin states that he thinks his son's may be greatest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LARRINGTON55_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-LARRINGTON55-7">[7]</a></sup> In stanzas 25 through 26, Odin states that the goat Heiðrún and the <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">hart</a> Eikþyrnir stand on top of Valhalla and graze on the branches of the tree Læraðr. Heiðrún produces vats of <a href="/wiki/Mead" title="Mead">mead</a> that liquor cannot be compared to, and from Eikþyrnir's <a href="/wiki/Antler" title="Antler">antlers</a> drip liquid into the spring <a href="/wiki/Hvergelmir" title="Hvergelmir">Hvergelmir</a> from which flows forth all waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LARRINGTON55_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-LARRINGTON55-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Helgakvi.C3.B0a_Hundingsbana_II"><i>Helgakviða Hundingsbana II</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valhalla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Helgakviða Hundingsbana II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In stanza 38 of the poem <i><a href="/wiki/Helgakvi%C3%B0a_Hundingsbana_II" title="Helgakviða Hundingsbana II">Helgakviða Hundingsbana II</a></i>, the hero <a href="/wiki/Helgi_Hundingsbane" title="Helgi Hundingsbane">Helgi Hundingsbane</a> dies and goes to Valhalla. In stanza 38, Helgi's glory there is described:</p>
<blockquote>
<dl>
<dd>So was Helgi beside the chieftains</dd>
<dd>like the bright-growing ash beside the thorn-bush</dd>
<dd>and the young stag, drenched in dew,</dd>
<dd>who surpasses all other animals</dd>
<dd>and whose horns glow against the sky itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LARRINGTON139_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-LARRINGTON139-8">[8]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</blockquote>
<p>Prose follows after this stanza, stating that a <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">burial-mound</a> was made for Helgi, and that when Helgi arrived in Valhalla, he was asked by Odin to manage things with him. In stanza 39, Helgi, now in Valhalla, has his former enemy Hunding—also in Valhalla—do menial tasks; fetching foot-baths for all of the men there, kindling fire, tying dogs, keeping watch of horses, and feeding the pigs before he can get any sleep. In stanzas 40 to 42, Helgi has returned to Midgard from Valhalla with a host of men. An unnamed maid of Sigrún, Helgi's valkyrie wife, sees Helgi and his large host of men riding into the mound. The maid asks if she is experiencing a delusion, if Ragnarök has begun, or if Helgi and his men have been allowed to return.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LARRINGTON139_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-LARRINGTON139-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the stanzas that follow, Helgi responds that none of these things have occurred, and so Sigrún's maid goes home to Sigrún. The maid tells Sigrún that the burial mound has opened up, and that Sigrún should go to Helgi there, as Helgi has asked her to come and tend his wounds, which have opened up and are bleeding. Sigrún goes into the mound, and finds that Helgi is drenched in gore, his hair is thick with frost. Filled with joy at the reunion, Sigrún kisses him before he can remove his coat of mail, and asks how she can heal him. Sigrún makes a bed there, and the two sleep together in the enclosed burial mound. Helgi awakens, stating that he must "ride along the blood-red roads, to set the pale horse to tread the path of the sky," and return before the rooster Salgófnir crows. Helgi and the host of men ride away, and Sigrún and her servant go back to their house. Sigrún has her maid wait for him by the mound the next night, but when she arrives at dawn, she finds that he has not returned. The prose narrative at the end of the poem relates that Sigrún dies of sadness, but that the two are thought to have been reborn as <a href="/wiki/Helgi_Haddingjaskati" title="Helgi Haddingjaskati">Helgi Haddingjaskati</a> and the valkyrie <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1ra" title="Kára">Kára</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LARRINGTON139-141_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-LARRINGTON139-141-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prose_Edda"><i>Prose Edda</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valhalla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Prose Edda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Valhalla is referenced in the <i>Prose Edda</i> books <i><a href="/wiki/Gylfaginning" title="Gylfaginning">Gylfaginning</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%A1ldskaparm%C3%A1l" title="Skáldskaparmál">Skáldskaparmál</a></i>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gylfaginning"><i>Gylfaginning</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valhalla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Gylfaginning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Valhalla is first mentioned in chapter 2 of the <i>Prose Edda</i> book <i>Gylfaginning</i>, where it is described partially in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euhemerized" title="Euhemerized">euhemerized</a> form. In the chapter, <a href="/wiki/Gylfi" title="Gylfi">King Gylfi</a> sets out to <a href="/wiki/Asgard" title="Asgard">Asgard</a> in the guise of an old man going by the name of <i>Gangleri</i> to find the source of the power of the gods. The narrative states that the <a href="/wiki/%C3%86sir" title="Æsir">Æsir</a> foresaw his arrival and had prepared grand illusions for him, so that when Gangerli enters the fortress, he sees a hall of such a height that he has trouble seeing over it, and notices that the roof of the hall is covered in golden shields, as if they were shingles. Snorri then quotes a stanza by the skald <a href="/wiki/%C3%9Ej%C3%B3%C3%B0%C3%B3lfr_of_Hvinir" title="Þjóðólfr of Hvinir">Þjóðólfr of Hvinir</a> (c. 900). As he continues, Gangleri sees a man in the doorway of the hall juggling short swords, and keeping seven in the air at once. Among other things, the man says that the hall belongs to his king, and adds that he can take Gangleri to the king. Gangleri follows him, and the door closes behind him. All around him he sees many living areas, and throngs of people, some of which are playing games, some are drinking, and others are fighting with weapons. Gangleri sees three thrones, and three figures sitting upon them: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High,_Just-As-High,_and_Third" title="High, Just-As-High, and Third">High</a> sitting on the lowest throne, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High,_Just-As-High,_and_Third" title="High, Just-As-High, and Third">Just-As-High</a> sitting on the next highest throne, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High,_Just-As-High,_and_Third" title="High, Just-As-High, and Third">Third</a> sitting on the highest. The man guiding Gangleri tells him that High is the king of the hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BYOCK10-11_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-BYOCK10-11-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In chapter 20, Third states that Odin mans Valhalla with the Einherjar: the dead who fall in battle and become Odin's adopted sons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BYOCK31_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-BYOCK31-11">[11]</a></sup> In chapter 36, High states that valkyries serve drinks and see to the tables in Valhalla, and <i>Grímnismál</i> stanzas 40 to 41 are then quoted in reference to this. High continues that the valkyries are sent by Odin to every battle, where they choose who is to die, and determine victory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BYOCK44-45_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-BYOCK44-45-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In chapter 38, Gangleri says: "You say that all men who have fallen in battle from the beginning of the world are now with Odin in Valhalla. With what does he feed them? I should think the crowd there is large." High responds that this is indeed true, that a huge amount are already in Valhalla, but yet this amount will seem to be too few when "the wolf comes." High describes that there are never too many to feed in Valhalla, for they feast from <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A6hr%C3%ADmnir" title="Sæhrímnir">Sæhrímnir</a> (here described as a boar), and that this beast is cooked every day and is again whole every night. <i>Grímnismál</i> stanza 18 is then recounted. Gangleri asks if Odin himself eats the same food as the Einherjar, and High responds that Odin needs nothing to eat—Odin only consumes wine—and he gives his food to his wolves <a href="/wiki/Geri_and_Freki" title="Geri and Freki">Geri and Freki</a>. <i>Grímnismál</i> stanza 19 is then recounted. High additionally states that at sunrise, Odin sends his ravens <a href="/wiki/Huginn_and_Muninn" title="Huginn and Muninn">Huginn and Muninn</a> from Valhalla to fly throughout the entire world, and they return in time for the first meal there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BYOCK46-47_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-BYOCK46-47-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In chapter 39, Gangleri asks about the food and drinks the Einherjar consume, and asks if only water is available there. High replies that, of course, Valhalla has food and drinks fit for kings and <a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">jarls</a>, for the mead consumed in Valhalla is produced from the udders of the goat Heiðrún, who in turn feeds on the leaves of the "famous tree" Læraðr. The goat produces so much mead in a day that it fills a massive vat large enough for all of the Einherjar in Valhalla to satisfy their thirst from it. High further states that the stag Eikþyrnir stands atop Valhalla and chews on the branches of Læraðr. So much moisture drips from his horns that it falls down to the well Hvelgelmir, resulting in numerous rivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BYOCK48_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-BYOCK48-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In chapter 40, Gangleri muses that Valhalla must be quite crowded, to which High responds by stating that Valhalla is massive and remains roomy despite the large amount of inhabitants, and then quotes <i>Grímnismál</i> stanza 23. In chapter 41, Gangleri says that Odin seems to be quite a powerful lord, as he controls quite a big army, but he yet wonders how the Einherjar keep themselves busy when they are not drinking. High replies that daily, after they've dressed and put on their war gear, they go out to the courtyard and battle one another in one-on-one combat for sport. Then, when mealtime comes, they ride home to Valhalla and drink. High then quotes <i><a href="/wiki/Vaf%C3%BEr%C3%BA%C3%B0nism%C3%A1l" title="Vafþrúðnismál">Vafþrúðnismál</a></i> stanza 41. In chapter 42, High describes that, "right at the beginning, when the gods were settling" they had established Asgard and then built Valhalla.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BYOCK49-50_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-BYOCK49-50-15">[15]</a></sup> The death of the god Baldr is recounted in chapter 49, where the <a href="/wiki/Mistletoe" title="Mistletoe">mistletoe</a> that is used to kill Baldr is described as growing west of Valhalla.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BYOCK66_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-BYOCK66-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sk.C3.A1ldskaparm.C3.A1l"><i>Skáldskaparmál</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valhalla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Skáldskaparmál">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>At the beginning of <i>Skáldskaparmál</i>, a partially euhemerized account is given of <a href="/wiki/%C3%86gir" title="Ægir">Ægir</a> visiting the gods in Asgard and shimmering swords are brought out and used as their sole source of light as they drink. There, numerous gods feast, they have plenty of strong <a href="/wiki/Mead" title="Mead">mead</a>, and the hall has wall-panels covered with attractive shields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAULKES59_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAULKES59-17">[17]</a></sup> This location is confirmed as Valhalla in chapter 33.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAULKES95_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAULKES95-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In chapter 2, a quote from the anonymous 10th century poem <a href="/wiki/Eir%C3%ADksm%C3%A1l" title="Eiríksmál">Eiríksmál</a> is provided (see the <i>Fagrskinna</i> section below for more detail and another translation from another source):</p>
<blockquote>
<p>What sort of dream is that, Odin? I dreamed I rose up before dawn to clear up Val-hall for slain people. I aroused the Einheriar, bade them get up to strew the benches, clean the beer-cups, the valkyries to serve wine for the arrival of a prince.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAULKES69_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAULKES69-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In chapter 17 of <i>Skáldskaparmál</i>, the <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6tunn" title="Jötunn">jötunn</a> <a href="/wiki/Hrungnir" title="Hrungnir">Hrungnir</a> is in a rage and, while attempting to catch up and attack Odin on his steed <a href="/wiki/Sleipnir" title="Sleipnir">Sleipnir</a>, ends up at the doors to Valhalla. There, the Æsir invite him in for a drink. Hrungnir goes in, demands a drink, and becomes drunk and belligerent, stating that he will remove Valhalla and take it to the land of the jötunn, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6tunheimr" title="Jötunheimr">Jötunheimr</a>, among various other things. Eventually, the gods tire of his boasting and invoke Thor, who arrives. Hrungnir states that Thor is under their protection, and subsequently he can't be harmed while in Valhalla. After an exchange of words, Hrungnir challenges Thor to a duel at the location of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Griotunagardar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Griotunagardar (page does not exist)">Griotunagardar</a>, resulting in Hrungnir's death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAULKES77-78_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAULKES77-78-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In chapter 34, the tree <a href="/wiki/Glasir" title="Glasir">Glasir</a> is stated as located in front of the doors of Valhalla. The tree is described as having foliage of red gold and being the most beautiful tree among both gods and men. A quote from a work by the 9th century skald <a href="/wiki/Bragi_Boddason" title="Bragi Boddason">Bragi Boddason</a> is presented that confirms the description.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAULKES96_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAULKES96-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heimskringla"><i>Heimskringla</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valhalla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Heimskringla">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Valhalla is mentioned in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euhemerized" title="Euhemerized">euhemerized</a> form and as an element of remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_paganism" title="Norse paganism">Norse pagan</a> belief in <i><a href="/wiki/Heimskringla" title="Heimskringla">Heimskringla</a></i>. In chapter 8 of <i><a href="/wiki/Ynglinga_saga" title="Ynglinga saga">Ynglinga saga</a></i>, the "historical" Odin is described as ordaining burial laws over his country. These laws include that all the dead are to be burned on a pyre on a burial mound with their possessions, and their ashes are to be brought out to sea or buried in the earth. The dead would then arrive in Valhalla with everything that one had on their pyre, and whatever one had hidden in the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HOLLANDER12_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-HOLLANDER12-22">[22]</a></sup> Valhalla is additionally referenced in the phrase "visiting Odin" in a work by the 10th century skald <a href="/wiki/%C3%9Ej%C3%B3%C3%B0%C3%B3lfr_of_Hvinir" title="Þjóðólfr of Hvinir">Þjóðólfr of Hvinir</a> describing that, upon his death, <a href="/wiki/Vanlandi" title="Vanlandi">King Vanlandi</a> went to Valhalla.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HOLLANDER17_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-HOLLANDER17-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In chapter 32 of <i>Hákonar saga Góða</i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haakon_I_of_Norway" title="Haakon I of Norway">Haakon I of Norway</a> is given a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_paganism" title="Norse paganism">pagan</a> burial, which is described as sending him on his way to Valhalla. Verses from <i><a href="/wiki/H%C3%A1konarm%C3%A1l" title="Hákonarmál">Hákonarmál</a></i> are then quoted in support, themselves containing references to Valhalla.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HOLLANDER125_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-HOLLANDER125-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fagrskinna"><i>Fagrskinna</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valhalla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Fagrskinna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In chapter 8 of <i><a href="/wiki/Fagrskinna" title="Fagrskinna">Fagrskinna</a></i>, a prose narrative states that, after the death of her husband <a href="/wiki/Eric_Bloodaxe" title="Eric Bloodaxe">Eric Bloodaxe</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gunnhild_Mother_of_Kings" title="Gunnhild Mother of Kings">Gunnhild Mother of Kings</a> had a poem composed about him. The composition is by an anonymous author from the 10th century and is referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Eir%C3%ADksm%C3%A1l" title="Eiríksmál">Eiríksmál</a></i>, and describes Eric Bloodaxe and five other kings arriving in Valhalla after their death. The poem begins with comments by Odin (as Old Norse <i>Óðinn</i>):</p>
<blockquote>
<dl>
<dd>'What kind of a dream is it,' said Óðinn,</dd>
<dd>in which just before daybreak,</dd>
<dd>I thought I cleared Valhǫll,</dd>
<dd>for coming of slain men?</dd>
<dd>I waked the Einherjar,</dd>
<dd>bade valkyries rise up,</dd>
<dd>to strew the bench,</dd>
<dd>and scour the beakers,</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>wine to carry,</dd>
<dd>as for a king's coming,</dd>
<dd>here to me I expect</dd>
<dd>heroes' coming from the world,</dd>
<dd>certain great ones,</dd>
<dd>so glad is my heart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FINLAY58_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FINLAY58-25">[25]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</blockquote>
<p>The god <a href="/wiki/Bragi" title="Bragi">Bragi</a> asks where a thundering sound is coming from, and says that the benches of Valhalla are creaking—as if the god Baldr had returned to Valhalla—and that it sounds like the movement of a thousand. Odin responds that Bragi knows well that the sounds are for Eric Bloodaxe, who will soon arrive in Valhalla. Odin tells the heroes <a href="/wiki/Sigmund" title="Sigmund">Sigmund</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sinfj%C3%B6tli" title="Sinfjötli">Sinfjötli</a> to rise to greet Eric and invite him into the hall, if it is indeed he.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FINLAY59_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FINLAY59-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sigmund asks Odin why he would expect Eric more than any other king, to which Odin responds that Eric has reddened his gore-drenched sword with many other lands. Eric arrives, and Sigmund greets him, tells him that he is welcome to come into the hall, and asks him what other lords he has brought with him to Valhalla. Eric says that with him are five kings, that he will tell them the name of them all, and that he, himself, is the sixth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FINLAY59_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-FINLAY59-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional">
<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 235px">

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_influence">Modern influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valhalla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Modern influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of Valhalla continues to have influence in modern popular culture. Examples include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walhalla_temple" title="Walhalla temple">Walhalla temple</a> built by <a href="/wiki/Leo_von_Klenze" title="Leo von Klenze">Leo von Klenze</a> for <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_I_of_Bavaria" title="Ludwig I of Bavaria">Ludwig I of Bavaria</a> between 1830–1847 near <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, and the Tresco Abbey Gardens Valhalla museum built by August Smith around 1830 to house <a href="/wiki/Figurehead_(object)" title="Figurehead (object)">ship figureheads</a> from shipwrecks that occurred at the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, where the museum is located.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SIMEK348TRESCO_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-SIMEK348TRESCO-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>References to Valhalla appear in literature, art, and other forms of media. Examples include K. Ehrenberg's charcoal illustration <i>Gastmahl in Walhalla (mit einziehenden Einheriern)</i> (1880), <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>'s depiction of Valhalla in his opera cycle <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Ring_des_Nibelungen" title="Der Ring des Nibelungen">Der Ring des Nibelungen</a></i> (1848–1874), the <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, Germany-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_Neopaganism" title="Germanic Neopaganism">Germanic Neopagan</a> magazine <i>Walhalla</i> (1905–1913), and the comic series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valhalla_(comic)" title="Valhalla (comic)">Valhalla</a></i> (1978-2009) by Peter Madsen, and its subsequent animated film of the <a href="/wiki/Valhalla_(film)" title="Valhalla (film)">same name</a> (1986).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SIMEK348TRESCO_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-SIMEK348TRESCO-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
