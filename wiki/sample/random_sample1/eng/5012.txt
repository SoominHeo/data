<p><b>Lindsay Gordon Anderson</b> (17 April 1923 – 30 August 1994)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Who_Was_Who_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Who_Was_Who-1">[1]</a></sup> was a British <a href="/wiki/Feature_film" title="Feature film">feature film</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theatre" title="Theatre">theatre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentary</a> <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">director</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_critic" title="Film critic">film critic</a>, and leading light of the <a href="/wiki/Free_Cinema" title="Free Cinema">Free Cinema</a> movement and the <a href="/wiki/British_New_Wave" title="British New Wave">British New Wave</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He is most widely remembered for his 1968 film <i><a href="/wiki/If...." title="If....">if....</a></i>, which won the <i><a href="/wiki/Palme_d%27Or" title="Palme d'Or">Palme d'Or</a></i> at <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a> and was <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_McDowell" title="Malcolm McDowell">Malcolm McDowell</a>'s cinematic debut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He is also notable, though not a professional actor, for playing a minor role in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> winning film <i><a href="/wiki/Chariots_of_Fire" title="Chariots of Fire">Chariots of Fire</a></i>. Malcolm McDowell produced a 2007 documentary about his experiences with Lindsay Anderson, <i><a href="/wiki/Never_Apologize" title="Never Apologize">Never Apologize</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lindsay_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> parentage, Anderson was the son of a British Army officer. He was born in <a href="/wiki/Bangalore" title="Bangalore">Bangalore</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">South India</a>, and educated at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Ronan%27s_School" title="St. Ronan's School">Saint Ronan's School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Worthing" title="Worthing">Worthing</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Sussex" title="West Sussex">West Sussex</a>, and at <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_College" title="Cheltenham College">Cheltenham College</a>, where he met his lifelong friend and biographer, the screenwriter and novelist <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Lambert" title="Gavin Lambert">Gavin Lambert</a>; and at <a href="/wiki/Wadham_College,_Oxford" title="Wadham College, Oxford">Wadham College, Oxford</a>, where he read <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>.</p>
<p>After graduating, Anderson worked for the final year of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">cryptographer</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="Intelligence Corps (United Kingdom)">Intelligence Corps</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/Wireless_Experimental_Centre" title="Wireless Experimental Centre">Wireless Experimental Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Delhi" title="Delhi">Delhi</a>. Anderson assisted in nailing the <a href="/wiki/Red_flag_(politics)" title="Red flag (politics)">Red flag</a> to the roof of the Junior Officers' mess in Annan Parbat, in August 1945, after the victory of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1945" title="United Kingdom general election, 1945">general election</a> was confirmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StandUp_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-StandUp-6">[6]</a></sup> The colonel did not approve, he recalled a decade later, but no disciplinary action was taken against them.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lindsay_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_criticism">Film criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lindsay_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Film criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before going into film-making, Anderson was a prominent film critic writing for the influential <i><a href="/wiki/Sequence_(journal)" title="Sequence (journal)">Sequence</a></i> magazine (1947–52), which he co-founded with <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Lambert" title="Gavin Lambert">Gavin Lambert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karel_Reisz" title="Karel Reisz">Karel Reisz</a>; later writing for the <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Institute" title="British Film Institute">British Film Institute</a>'s journal <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sight_and_Sound" title="Sight and Sound">Sight and Sound</a></i> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left-wing" title="Left-wing">left-wing</a> political weekly the <i><a href="/wiki/New_Statesman" title="New Statesman">New Statesman</a></i>. In a 1956 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polemics" title="Polemics">polemical</a> article, "Stand Up, Stand Up" for <i>Sight and Sound</i>, he attacked contemporary critical practices, in particular the pursuit of <a href="/wiki/Objectivity_(science)" title="Objectivity (science)">objectivity</a>. Taking as an example some comments made by <a href="/wiki/Alistair_Cooke" title="Alistair Cooke">Alistair Cooke</a> in 1935, where Cooke claimed to be without politics as a critic, Anderson responded:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The problems of commitment are directly stated, but only apparently faced. …The denial of the critics moral responsibility is specific; but only at the cost of sacrificing his dignity. … [These assumptions:] the holding of liberal, or humane, values; the proviso that these must not be taken too far; the adoption of a tone which enables the writer to evade through humour [mean] the fundamental issues are balked."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StandUp_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-StandUp-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Following a series of screenings which he and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Film_Theatre" title="National Film Theatre">National Film Theatre</a> programmer <a href="/wiki/Karel_Reisz" title="Karel Reisz">Karel Reisz</a> organized for the venue of independently-produced short films by himself and others, he developed a philosophy of cinema which found expression in what became known, by the late-1950s, as the <a href="/wiki/Free_Cinema" title="Free Cinema">Free Cinema</a> movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This was the belief that the British cinema must break away from its class-bound attitudes and that non-metropolitan Britain ought to be shown on the nation's screens. He had already begun to make films himself, starting in 1948 with <i>Meet the Pioneers</i>, a documentary about a conveyor-bet factory. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filmmaking">Filmmaking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lindsay_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Filmmaking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Along with <a href="/wiki/Karel_Reisz" title="Karel Reisz">Karel Reisz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Richardson" title="Tony Richardson">Tony Richardson</a>, and others, he secured funding from a variety of sources (including <a href="/wiki/Ford_of_Britain" title="Ford of Britain">Ford of Britain</a>) and they each made a series of short documentaries on a variety of subjects. One of Anderson's early short films, <i><a href="/wiki/Thursday%27s_Children" title="Thursday's Children">Thursday's Children</a></i> (1954), concerning the education of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deaf" title="Deaf">deaf</a> children, made in collaboration with Guy Brenton, a friend from his Oxford days, won an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Oscar</a> for Best Documentary Short in 1954.</p>
<p>These films, influenced by one of Anderson' heroes, the <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Jean_Vigo" title="Jean Vigo">Jean Vigo</a>, and made in the tradition of the British documentaries of <a href="/wiki/Humphrey_Jennings" title="Humphrey Jennings">Humphrey Jennings</a>, foreshadowed much of the social realism of British cinema that emerged in the next decade, with Reisz's <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_and_Sunday_Morning_(film)" title="Saturday Night and Sunday Morning (film)">Saturday Night and Sunday Morning</a></i> (1960), Richardson's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Loneliness_of_the_Long_Distance_Runner_(film)" title="The Loneliness of the Long Distance Runner (film)">The Loneliness of the Long Distance Runner</a></i> (1962) and Anderson's own <i><a href="/wiki/This_Sporting_Life" title="This Sporting Life">This Sporting Life</a></i> (1963), produced by Reisz. Anderson's film met with mixed reviews at the time, and was not a commercial success.</p>
<p>Anderson is perhaps best remembered as a filmmaker for his "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mick_Travis_trilogy" title="Mick Travis trilogy">Mick Travis trilogy</a>", all of which star <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_McDowell" title="Malcolm McDowell">Malcolm McDowell</a> as the title character: <i><a href="/wiki/If...." title="If....">if....</a></i> (1968), a satire on public schools; <i><a href="/wiki/O_Lucky_Man!" title="O Lucky Man!">O Lucky Man!</a></i> (1973) a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilgrim%27s_Progress" title="Pilgrim's Progress">Pilgrim's Progress</a></i> inspired road movie; and <i><a href="/wiki/Britannia_Hospital" title="Britannia Hospital">Britannia Hospital</a></i> (1982), a fantasia taking stylistic influence from the populist wing of British cinema represented by <a href="/wiki/Hammer_Film_Productions" title="Hammer Film Productions">Hammer</a> horror films and Carry On comedies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1981, Anderson played the role of the Master of <a href="/wiki/Gonville_and_Caius_College,_Cambridge" title="Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge">Caius College</a> at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge University</a> in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Chariots_of_Fire" title="Chariots of Fire">Chariots of Fire</a></i>.</p>
<p>Anderson developed an acquaintance from 1950 with <a href="/wiki/John_Ford" title="John Ford">John Ford</a>, which led to what has come to be regarded as one of the standard books on that director, Anderson's <i>About John Ford</i> (1983). Based on half a dozen meetings over more than two decades, and a lifetime's study of the man's work, the book has been described as "One of the best books published by a film-maker on a film-maker".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1985, producer <a href="/wiki/Martin_Lewis_(humorist)" title="Martin Lewis (humorist)">Martin Lewis</a> invited Anderson to chronicle <a href="/wiki/Wham!" title="Wham!">Wham!</a>'s visit to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">China</a>, the first-ever visit by Western pop artists, which resulted in Anderson's film <i>Foreign Skies: Wham! In China</i>. He admitted in his diary on 31 March 1985, to having "no interest in Wham!", or China, and he was simply "'doing this for the money'".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1986, he was a member of the jury at the <a href="/wiki/36th_Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="36th Berlin International Film Festival">36th Berlin International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berlinale_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berlinale-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anderson was also a significant British theatre director. He was long associated with London's <a href="/wiki/Royal_Court_Theatre" title="Royal Court Theatre">Royal Court Theatre</a>, where he was Co-Artistic Director 1969–70, and Associate Artistic Director 1971–75, directing premiere productions of plays by <a href="/wiki/David_Storey" title="David Storey">David Storey</a>, among others.</p>
<p>In 1992, as a close friend of actresses <a href="/wiki/Jill_Bennett_(British_actress)" title="Jill Bennett (British actress)">Jill Bennett</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rachel_Roberts_(British_actress)" title="Rachel Roberts (British actress)">Rachel Roberts</a>, Anderson included a touching episode in his autobiographical <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> film <i>Is That All There Is?</i>, with a boat trip down the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> (several of their professional colleagues and friends aboard) to scatter their ashes on the waters while musician <a href="/wiki/Alan_Price" title="Alan Price">Alan Price</a> sang the song "<a href="/wiki/Is_That_All_There_Is%3F" title="Is That All There Is?">Is That All There Is?</a>".</p>
<p>Every year, the International Documentary Festival in Amsterdam (<a href="/wiki/International_Documentary_Film_Festival_Amsterdam" title="International Documentary Film Festival Amsterdam">IDFA</a>) gives an acclaimed filmmaker the chance to screen his or her personal Top 10 favorite films. In 2007, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iranian_people" title="Iranian people">Iranian</a> filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Maziar_Bahari" title="Maziar Bahari">Maziar Bahari</a> selected <i><a href="/wiki/O_Dreamland" title="O Dreamland">O Dreamland</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Every_Day_Except_Christmas" title="Every Day Except Christmas">Every Day Except Christmas</a></i> (1957), a record of a day in the old <a href="/wiki/Covent_Garden" title="Covent Garden">Covent Garden</a> market, for his top 10 classics from the history of documentary.[3]</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lindsay_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gavin_Lambert" title="Gavin Lambert">Gavin Lambert</a>'s memoir, <i>Mainly About Lindsay Anderson</i>, in which he claimed that Anderson repressed his <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">homosexuality</a>, was seen as a betrayal by his other friends. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In November 2006 <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_McDowell" title="Malcolm McDowell">Malcolm McDowell</a> told <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I know that he was in love with <a href="/wiki/Richard_Harris" title="Richard Harris">Richard Harris</a> the star of Anderson's first feature, <i>This Sporting Life</i>. I am sure that it was the same with me and <a href="/wiki/Albert_Finney" title="Albert Finney">Albert Finney</a> and the rest. It wasn't a physical thing. But I suppose he always fell in love with his leading men. He would always pick someone who was unattainable because he was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterosexual" title="Heterosexual">heterosexual</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
