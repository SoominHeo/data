<p><b>Lili Damita</b> (10 July 1904 – 21 March 1994) was a French-American actress and singer who appeared in 33 films between 1922 and 1937.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lili_Damita&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born <b>Liliane Marie-Madeleine Carré</b> in <a href="/wiki/Blaye" title="Blaye">Blaye</a>, France, she was educated in convents and ballet schools in several European countries, including France, Spain and Portugal. At 14, she was enrolled as a dancer at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opera_de_Paris" title="Opera de Paris">Opera de Paris</a>.</p>
<p>As a teenager, she was performing in popular <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">music halls</a>, eventually appearing in the Revue at the <a href="/wiki/Casino_de_Paris" title="Casino de Paris">Casino de Paris</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> She worked as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photographic_model" title="Photographic model">photographic model</a>. Offered a role in film as a prize for winning a magazine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beauty_competition" title="Beauty competition">beauty competition</a> in 1921, she appeared in several <a href="/wiki/Silent_film" title="Silent film">silent films</a> before being offered her first leading role in <i><a href="/wiki/Das_Spielzeug_von_Paris" title="Das Spielzeug von Paris">Das Spielzeug von Paris</a></i> (1925) by Hungarian-born director <a href="/wiki/Michael_Curtiz" title="Michael Curtiz">Michael Curtiz</a>. She was an instant success, and Curtiz directed her in two more films: <i>Fiaker Nr 13</i> (1926) and <i>Der Goldene Schmetterling</i> (1926). Damita continued appearing in German productions directed by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wiene" title="Robert Wiene">Robert Wiene</a> (<i>Die Grosse Abenteuerin</i>; 1927), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G.W._Pabst" title="G.W. Pabst">G.W. Pabst</a> (<i>Man Spielt nicht mit der Liebe</i>; 1926) and British <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">director</a> <a href="/wiki/Graham_Cutts" title="Graham Cutts">Graham Cutts</a> (<a href="/wiki/The_Queen_Was_in_the_Parlour_(film)" title="The Queen Was in the Parlour (film)"><i>The Queen Was in the Parlour</i></a>; 1927).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>In 1928, at the invitation of <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Goldwyn" title="Samuel Goldwyn">Samuel Goldwyn</a> she went to <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a>, making her American debut in a film titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rescue_(1929_film)" title="The Rescue (1929 film)">The Rescue</a></i>. Leased out to various studios, she appeared with stars such as <a href="/wiki/Gary_Cooper" title="Gary Cooper">Gary Cooper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Chevalier" title="Maurice Chevalier">Maurice Chevalier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Olivier" title="Laurence Olivier">Laurence Olivier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cary_Grant" title="Cary Grant">Cary Grant</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Cagney" title="James Cagney">James Cagney</a>. Her films included the <a href="/wiki/Box_office" title="Box office">box office</a> successes <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cock-Eyed_World" title="The Cock-Eyed World">The Cock-Eyed World</a></i> (1929),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the semi-silent <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bridge_of_San_Luis_Rey_(1929_film)" title="The Bridge of San Luis Rey (1929 film)">The Bridge of San Luis Rey</a></i> (1929) and <i><a href="/wiki/This_Is_the_Night_(film)" title="This Is the Night (film)">This Is the Night</a></i> (1932).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lili_Damita&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Damita_%26_Flynn.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="515" data-file-width="475" height="239" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Damita_%26_Flynn.jpg/220px-Damita_%26_Flynn.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Damita_%26_Flynn.jpg/330px-Damita_%26_Flynn.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Damita_%26_Flynn.jpg/440px-Damita_%26_Flynn.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Damita_%26_Flynn.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Lili Damita and husband <a href="/wiki/Errol_Flynn" title="Errol Flynn">Errol Flynn</a> at Los Angeles airport, 1941</div>
</div>

<p>In 1935, she married a virtual unknown who would become Hollywood's biggest box office attraction, <a href="/wiki/Errol_Flynn" title="Errol Flynn">Errol Flynn</a>, with whom she had a son, <a href="/wiki/Sean_Flynn_(photojournalist)" title="Sean Flynn (photojournalist)">Sean Flynn</a> (born 1941). Following the marriage, she retired from the screen. The couple divorced in 1942. (<a href="/wiki/Barbara_Hershey" title="Barbara Hershey">Barbara Hershey</a> portrayed Damita in the TV film <i><a href="/wiki/My_Wicked,_Wicked_Ways" title="My Wicked, Wicked Ways">My Wicked, Wicked Ways</a></i> [1985] based on <a href="/wiki/Errol_Flynn" title="Errol Flynn">Errol Flynn</a>'s autobiography.) While living in <a href="/wiki/Palm_Beach,_Florida" title="Palm Beach, Florida">Palm Beach, Florida</a>, Damita married Allen Loomis, a retired <a href="/wiki/Fort_Dodge,_Iowa" title="Fort Dodge, Iowa">Fort Dodge, Iowa</a> dairy owner, and spent part of each year living there.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Cambodian_Civil_War" title="Cambodian Civil War">Cambodian Civil War</a> (<a href="/wiki/Khmer_Rouge" title="Khmer Rouge">Khmer Rouge</a> reign), her son Sean Flynn was working as a freelance <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photo_journalist" title="Photo journalist">photo journalist</a> under contract to <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine when he and fellow journalist <a href="/wiki/Dana_Stone" title="Dana Stone">Dana Stone</a> went missing on the road south of <a href="/wiki/Phnom_Penh" title="Phnom Penh">Phnom Penh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, on 6 April 1970. Although Damita spent an enormous amount of money searching for her son, he was never found, and in 1984 he was declared legally dead. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_testing" title="DNA testing">DNA testing</a> was conducted on remains found in Cambodia and turned over to the U.S. authorities there in March 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, the results, released 30 June 2010 by JPAC, showed the remains were not those of Sean Flynn or Dana Stone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lili_Damita&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lili Damita died of <a href="/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Alzheimer's disease">Alzheimer's disease</a> on 21 March 1994, in <a href="/wiki/Palm_Beach,_Florida" title="Palm Beach, Florida">Palm Beach, Florida</a>, aged 89. She was interred in the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Cemetery_(Fort_Dodge,_Iowa)" title="Oakland Cemetery (Fort Dodge, Iowa)">Oakland Cemetery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Dodge,_Iowa" title="Fort Dodge, Iowa">Fort Dodge, Iowa</a>, her husband's hometown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
