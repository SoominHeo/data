<p><b>Ligdan Khutugtu Khan</b> (from Mongolian "Ligden Khutugt Khan"; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongolian_Cyrillic_script" title="Mongolian Cyrillic script">Mongolian Cyrillic</a>: Лигдэн Хутугт хаан; or from Chinese, <b>Lindan Han</b>; <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">林丹汗</span>; 1588–1634) was the last khan of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Yuan_dynasty" title="Northern Yuan dynasty">Northern Yuan dynasty</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> as well as the last in the <a href="/wiki/Borjigin" title="Borjigin">Borjigin</a> clan of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Mongol_Khans" title="List of Mongol Khans">Mongol Khans</a> who ruled the <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongols</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chahar_(Mongols)" title="Chahar (Mongols)">Chakhar</a>. His unpopular reign generated violent opposition due to his harsh restrictions over the Mongols. His alliance with <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> of China, sponsorship of <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism" title="Tibetan Buddhism">Tibetan Buddhism</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chahar_Mongols" title="Chahar Mongols">Chakhar</a> and reorganization of Mongolian political divisions were ineffective when the <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a> became the major power in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_reign">Life and reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ligdan_Khan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ligden (b.1588) was a son of Mangghus Mergen Taiji and grandson of <a href="/wiki/Buyan_Sechen_Khan" title="Buyan Sechen Khan">Buyan Sechen Khan</a> (r. 1593–1603).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Because his father died early, Ligden was chosen to succeed his grandfather Buyan as <a href="/wiki/Khagan" title="Khagan">khan</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Yuan_dynasty" title="Northern Yuan dynasty">Northern Yuan dynasty</a> with the reign title Khutugtu in 1603. At the time the Khagan's <a href="/wiki/Appanage" title="Appanage">appanage</a>, Chakhar people, occupied Sira Mören valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Ligden divided the Chakhar into right and left wings and built Chaghan city near Abaga Khara Mountain.</p>
<p>During his early reign, Ligden had the respect and loyalty of other Mongol tümens. Boshigo <a href="/wiki/Jinong" title="Jinong">jinong</a> of the Three Right Wing Tumens expressed his allegiance to Ligden Khan. Allied with princes of the Southern Khalkha (Baarin and Jarud), Ligden raided <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_China" title="Ming China">Ming China</a>. However, from 1612 on, leaders of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khorchin" title="Khorchin">Khorchin</a> and the Jarud became in-laws with the rising <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manchus" title="Manchus">Manchus</a>. By the early 17th century, the Khan's court had lost most of its power and was under pressure from the Manchus in the east. Hoping that he could consolidate his power over the Mongol tümens, Ligden moved the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> religious center of the Mongols to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chahar_(province)" title="Chahar (province)">Chakhar</a> and had himself declared both religious and political leader of the Mongols by a <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibetan</a> religious leader.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Ligden revived the old Saskyapa order of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kublai" title="Kublai">Kublai</a>'s time (r. 1260–94), inviting the order's Sharba pandita, who was appointed his preceptor in 1617.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Sharba installed Mahakala image in Ligden's capital Chagan. Ligden Khan also built temples at Küriye.</p>
<p>In 1618, Ligden signed a <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaty</a> with the Ming Dynasty to protect their northern border from the Manchus in exchange for thousands of <a href="/wiki/Tael" title="Tael">taels</a> of silver. He received an annual subsidy of 40,000 taels of silver in 1620. Sog Zaisai, a Southern Khalkha nobleman, prince Sanasarai of the Khorchin, and Paghwa of the Jarud attacked the Manchus with more than 10,000–50,000 men to assist the Ming in August 1619, but they were crushed. Because the Manchus wanted to use the Mongols against the Ming, they persuaded their leaders, including Ligden, to ally with them. Since a conflict with the Manchu in 1619, the relations between his clan federation and the Manchu federation had continuously deteriorated. In 1620, after an exchange of contemptuous letters, Ligden and the Manchu ruler <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nurhachi" title="Nurhachi">Nurhachi</a> (r. 1616–26) broke off relations and most of the Eastern Tümens deserted to Nurhachi in 1622–24.</p>
<p>Ligden, on the other hand, by trying to assume this former power, ruled in a progressively aggressive fashion, so several Mongolian tribes opted to form a coalition with the Manchus. When the Khorchin and some formerly allied tribes bonded with the Manchus in 1624, Ligden undertook a <a href="/wiki/Punitive_expedition" title="Punitive expedition">punitive expedition</a> and besieged the Khorchin nobleman Oba for 3 days, but retreated when Manchu relief troops arrived. When Ligden Khan called the northern Khalkhas to join him against the Manchus, only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsogt_Taij" title="Tsogt Taij">Tsogt Taij</a> accepted his appeal.</p>
<p>Ligden aimed at centralizing <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolian</a> rule. He appointed officials to rule the left and right wing tümens, and organized a special court nobility and a corps of 300 baaturs (warriors). In 1627, the other tümens were in full <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revolt" title="Revolt">revolt</a>. Princes ruling the Sunid, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uzemchin" title="Uzemchin">Uzemchin</a>, and Abaga moved northwest and allied with the Three Right Wing Tumens against the Khaghan. They attacked Ligden at Zhaocheng. Ligden defeated the allies but lost 10,000 Chakhars. When the Ming court refused to pay <a href="/wiki/Subsidy" title="Subsidy">subsidy</a>, he raided China, forcing them to renew the treaty. The Ming increased his annual subsidy to 81,000 taels of silver. In 1631 Ligden passed the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Khingan" title="Greater Khingan">Khinggan Range</a> and successfully attacked the Khorchin and the Tümed. A powerful alliance of Khorchin, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%C3%BCmed" title="Tümed">Tümed</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yungshiyebu" title="Yungshiyebu">Yungshiyebu</a>, Ordos and Abaga was formed against Ligden. They destroyed 4,000 Chahar militias in <a href="/wiki/Hohhot" title="Hohhot">Hohhot</a> and 3,000 soldiers who were going to take Ligden's subsidy from the Ming. In 1632, the Manchu Emperor <a href="/wiki/Hong_Taiji" title="Hong Taiji">Hong Taiji</a> and his Mongolian allies undertook a campaign against Ligden who avoided a confrontation and with maybe 100,000 Chakhar fled to <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">Kokenuur</a>. Ligden made himself yet more unpopular by seizing the wife of Erinchin jinong and taking refuge in the Eight White Yurts of Genghis Khan in Kokenuur. Allied with the Tibetan monarchs, he opposed Dalai Lama V and Banchin Erdene IV. He died of <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> at Sira Tala (in modern <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a>) in 1634 while marching to attack <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DGe-lugs-pa" title="DGe-lugs-pa">dGe-lugs-pa</a> order (Yellow Hat sect) in <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>.</p>
<p>After Ligden Khan's death, his son <a href="/wiki/Ejei_Khan" title="Ejei Khan">Ejei Khan</a> (Erke qongγor eje) returned and was handed over to the Manchus <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" title="Qing Dynasty">Qing Dynasty</a> who soon after assumed power in <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ligdan_Khan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name is borrowed from the <a href="/wiki/Classical_Tibetan" title="Classical Tibetan">Classical Tibetan</a> term <i>legs-ldan</i>. There, the letters <i>s</i> and <i>l</i> had already become silent, <i>g</i> before <i>d</i> could be realized as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[n]</span> and <i>a</i> before <i>n</i> got palatalized. In Mongolian sources, the most frequent ways to write the name are <i>Ligda/en</i> and <i>Linda/en</i>, but the intermediate <i>Lingda/en</i> (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ŋ]</span>) does appear as well. <i>a</i> and <i>e</i> are not differentiated in normal <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_script" title="Mongolian script">Mongolian writing</a> in this position, but <i>a</i> is attested in a strict transcription from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tibetan_script" title="Tibetan script">Tibetan letters</a> in the chronicle <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bolur_Erdeni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bolur Erdeni (page does not exist)">Bolur Erdeni</a>. However, for any Mongolian reader who does not immediately perceive the name to be a loan, the letter <i>g</i> would (by means of <a href="/wiki/Consonant_harmony" title="Consonant harmony">consonant harmony</a> and its interaction with <a href="/wiki/Vowel_harmony" title="Vowel harmony">vowel harmony</a>) indicate that the word only contains <a href="/wiki/Front_vowel" title="Front vowel">front vowels</a>. This must have been perceived in this fashion at the time of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breaking_(linguistics)" title="Breaking (linguistics)">i-breaking</a> as well, as this <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonological process</a> took place in <a href="/wiki/Back_vowel" title="Back vowel">back-vocalic</a> words only and would have resulted in <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Tibetan" title="Help:IPA for Tibetan">[ɮʲaɢtan]</a></span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (July 2009)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Ligdan_Khan#Dubious" title="Talk:Ligdan Khan">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> had it been <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/liɡdan/</span>. Today, western scholars tend to cling to the written form of the Tibetan word and write <i>Ligdan</i>, while Mongolian scholars will usually write <i>Ligden</i>, both pointing to a possible alternative with <i>n</i>. In <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, the name is written as 林丹, the standard <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">Pinyin</a> transcription being <i>Líndān</i>.</p>
