<p><b>Lincoln Center for the Performing Arts</b> is a 16.3-acre (6.6-hectare) complex of buildings in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lincoln_Square_(Manhattan)" title="Lincoln Square (Manhattan)">Lincoln Square</a> neighborhood of the <a href="/wiki/Borough_(New_York_City)" title="Borough (New York City)">borough</a> of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. It hosts many notable <a href="/wiki/Performing_arts" title="Performing arts">performing arts</a> organizations, which are nationally and internationally renowned, including the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Philharmonic" title="New York Philharmonic">New York Philharmonic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Opera" title="Metropolitan Opera">Metropolitan Opera</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Ballet" title="New York City Ballet">New York City Ballet</a>.</p>

<p>A consortium of civic leaders and others led by, and under the initiative of, <a href="/wiki/John_D._Rockefeller_III" title="John D. Rockefeller III">John D. Rockefeller III</a> built Lincoln Center as part of the "Lincoln Square Renewal Project" during <a href="/wiki/Robert_Moses" title="Robert Moses">Robert Moses</a>'s program of <a href="/wiki/Urban_renewal" title="Urban renewal">urban renewal</a> in the 1950s and 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rockefeller_Web_Reference_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rockefeller_Web_Reference-1">[1]</a></sup> Respected architects were contracted to design the major buildings on the site, and over the next thirty years the previously blighted area around Lincoln Center became a new cultural hub.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roth_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roth-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rockefeller was Lincoln Center's inaugural president from 1956 and became its chairman in 1961. He is credited with raising more than half of the $184.5 million in private funds needed to build the complex, including drawing on his own funds; the <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Brothers_Fund" title="Rockefeller Brothers Fund">Rockefeller Brothers Fund</a> also contributed to the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rockefeller_Web_Reference_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rockefeller_Web_Reference-1">[1]</a></sup> The center's three buildings, <a href="/wiki/David_Geffen_Hall" title="David Geffen Hall">David Geffen Hall</a> (formerly Avery Fisher Hall), <a href="/wiki/David_H._Koch_Theater" title="David H. Koch Theater">David H. Koch Theater</a> (formerly the New York State Theater) and the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Opera_House_(Lincoln_Center)" title="Metropolitan Opera House (Lincoln Center)">Metropolitan Opera House</a> were opened in 1962, 1964 and 1966, respectively.</p>
<p>While the center may have been named because it was located in the Lincoln Square neighborhood, it is unclear whether the area was named as a tribute to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._President" title="U.S. President">U.S. President</a> <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>. The name was bestowed on the area in 1906 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_City_Board_of_Aldermen" title="New York City Board of Aldermen">New York City Board of Aldermen</a>, but records give no reason for choosing that name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> There has long been speculation that the name came from a local landowner, because the square was previously named Lincoln Square. City records from the time show only the names Johannes van Bruch, Thomas Hall, Stephan de Lancey, James de Lancey, James de Lancey Jr. and John Somerindyck as area property owners. One speculation is that references to President Lincoln were omitted from the records because the mayor in 1906 was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Brinton_McClellan,_Jr." title="George Brinton McClellan, Jr.">George B. McClellan, Jr.</a>, son of General <a href="/wiki/George_B._McClellan" title="George B. McClellan">George B. McClellan</a> who was <a href="/wiki/General-in-chief" title="General-in-chief">general-in-chief</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union Army</a> early in the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> and a bitter rival of Lincoln.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mystery_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-mystery-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Architects who designed buildings at the center include:</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facility_history">Facility history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lincoln_Center_for_the_Performing_Arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Facility history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lincoln_Center_for_the_Performing_Arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first structure to be completed and occupied as part of this renewal was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fordham_Law_School" title="Fordham Law School">Fordham Law School</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fordham_University" title="Fordham University">Fordham University</a> in 1962. Located between <a href="/wiki/Ninth_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="Ninth Avenue (Manhattan)">Columbus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tenth_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="Tenth Avenue (Manhattan)">Amsterdam Avenues</a>, from West <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/60th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="60th Street (Manhattan)">60th</a> to <a href="/wiki/66th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="66th Street (Manhattan)">66th Streets</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Square,_Manhattan" title="Lincoln Square, Manhattan">Lincoln Square</a>, on <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Upper_West_Side" title="Upper West Side">Upper West Side</a>, the complex was the first gathering of major cultural institutions into a centralized location in an American city.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The development of the condominium at 3 Lincoln Center,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldberger1991_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldberger1991-5">[5]</a></sup> completed in 1991, designed by <a href="/wiki/Lee_Jablin" title="Lee Jablin">Lee Jablin</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Harman_Jablin_Architects&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Harman Jablin Architects (page does not exist)">Harman Jablin Architects</a>, made possible the expansion of <a href="/wiki/Juilliard_School" title="Juilliard School">The Juilliard School</a> and the <a href="/wiki/School_of_American_Ballet" title="School of American Ballet">School of American Ballet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldberger1991_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-goldberger1991-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The center's cultural institutions also make use of facilities located away from the main campus. In 2004, the center was expanded through the addition of <a href="/wiki/Jazz_at_Lincoln_Center" title="Jazz at Lincoln Center">Jazz at Lincoln Center</a>'s newly built facilities, the <a href="/wiki/Frederick_P._Rose" title="Frederick P. Rose">Frederick P. Rose</a> Hall, at the new <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner_Center" title="Time Warner Center">Time Warner Center</a>, located a few blocks to the south. In March 2006, the center launched construction on a major redevelopment plan that modernized, renovated, and opened up its campus. Redevelopment was completed in 2012 with the completion of the President's Bridge over West 65th Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-President.27s_Bridge_opens.2C_completes_redevelopment_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-President.27s_Bridge_opens.2C_completes_redevelopment-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renovations">Renovations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lincoln_Center_for_the_Performing_Arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Renovations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When it was first announced in 1999, Lincoln Center's campuswide redevelopment was to cost $1.5 billion over 10 years and radically transform the campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> There was an architectural competition, won by the British architect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_Foster" title="Norman Foster">Norman Foster</a> in 2005, but a full scale redesign was not approved until 2012, in part because of the need to raise $300 million in construction costs and the New York Philharmonic's fear that it might lose audiences and revenue while it was displaced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Among the architects that have been involved were <a href="/wiki/Frank_Gehry" title="Frank Gehry">Frank Gehry</a>; <a href="/wiki/Cooper,_Robertson_%26_Partners" title="Cooper, Robertson &amp; Partners">Cooper, Robertson &amp; Partners</a>; <a href="/wiki/Skidmore,_Owings_%26_Merrill" title="Skidmore, Owings &amp; Merrill">Skidmore, Owings &amp; Merrill</a>; <a href="/wiki/Beyer_Blinder_Belle" title="Beyer Blinder Belle">Beyer Blinder Belle</a>; <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Fowle" title="Bruce Fowle">Fox &amp; Fowle</a>; <a href="/wiki/Laurie_Olin" title="Laurie Olin">Olin Partnership</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Diller_Scofidio_%2B_Renfro" title="Diller Scofidio + Renfro">Diller &amp; Scofidio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2006, the center launched the 65th Street Project  –  part of a major redevelopment plan continuing through the fall of 2012  –  to create a new pedestrian promenade designed to improve accessibility and the aesthetics of that area of the campus. Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Alice_Tully_Hall" title="Alice Tully Hall">Alice Tully Hall</a> was modernized and reopened to critical and popular acclaim in 2009 and the <a href="/wiki/Film_Society_of_Lincoln_Center" title="Film Society of Lincoln Center">Film Society of Lincoln Center</a> expanded with the new Elinor Bunin Munroe Film Center. Topped by a sloping lawn roof, the film center is part of a new pavilion that also houses a destination restaurant named Lincoln, as well as offices. Subsequent projects were added which addressed improvements to the main plazas and Columbus Avenue Grand Stairs. Under the direction of the Lincoln Center Development Project, Diller Scofidio + Renfro in association with FXFOWLE Architects and <a href="/wiki/Beyer_Blinder_Belle" title="Beyer Blinder Belle">Beyer Blinder Belle</a> Architects provided the design services. Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Turner_Construction" title="Turner Construction">Turner Construction</a> Company and RCDolner, LLC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> were the construction managers for the projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Another component to redevelopment was the addition of the David Rubenstein Atrium designed by Tod Williams Billie Tsien Architects, a visitors' center and a gateway to the center that offers free performances, day-of-discount tickets, food, and free <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Timeline">Timeline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lincoln_Center_for_the_Performing_Arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Timeline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<li>May 22, 1969: Damrosch Park and the Guggenheim Band Shell opened.</li>

<li>October 26, 1969: Juilliard School opened.</li>
<li>October 19, 1976: Avery Fisher Hall re-opened after renovation to improve acoustics.</li>

<li>November 19, 1990: The Samuel B. and David Rose Building opened; houses the Walter Reade Theater, the Stanley H. Kaplan Penthouse, the Daniel and Joanna S. Rose Rehearsal Studio, the Clark Studio Theater, the School of American Ballet, and Juilliard School student residences, as well as office space for a number of the member organizations.</li>
<li>December 3, 1991: The Walter Reade Theater opened within the previously completed Samuel B. and David Rose Building.</li>
<li>July 12, 1997: The Paul Milstein Plaza dedicated.</li>
<li>October 18, 2004: Jazz at Lincoln Center opened.</li>
<li>March 2006: Preliminary construction on the West 65th Street Project begins</li>
<li>June 8, 2006: Plans for Lincoln Center to transform the nearby Harmony Atrium into a public space for the arts open to the public, neighbors, students, and Lincoln Center patrons are announced.</li>
<li>June 12, 2006: The Lincoln Center Promenade initiative to revitalize Lincoln Center's Columbus Avenue frontage and the iconic Josie Robertson Plaza is unveiled.</li>
<li>August 20, 2006: Paul Milstein Plaza dismantled as part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/65th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="65th Street (Manhattan)">65th Street</a> Redevelopment project.</li>
<li>February 22, 2009: Alice Tully Hall reopens after redevelopment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alice_Tully_Hall_reopens_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alice_Tully_Hall_reopens-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>September 30, 2009: Opening of the Charles H. Revson Fountain</li>
<li>May 21, 2010: Renovation of central and north plazas unveiled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>June 4, 2012: Claire Tow Theater opened</li>
<li>October 1, 2012: The President's Bridge opened over West 65th Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-President.27s_Bridge_opens.2C_completes_redevelopment_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-President.27s_Bridge_opens.2C_completes_redevelopment-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>May 15, 2013: <a href="/wiki/Jed_Bernstein" title="Jed Bernstein">Jed Bernstein</a> begins his term as president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jed_bernstein_prez_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-jed_bernstein_prez-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>September 24, 2015: Avery Fisher Hall was renamed David Geffen Hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lincoln_Center_Concert_Hall_Renamed_for_David_Geffen_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lincoln_Center_Concert_Hall_Renamed_for_David_Geffen-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p>The center has 30 indoor and outdoor performance facilities that include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alice_Tully_Hall" title="Alice Tully Hall">Alice Tully Hall</a>: a 1,095-seat concert hall located within the <a href="/wiki/Juilliard_School" title="Juilliard School">Juilliard School</a> building; the home stage of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Chamber_Music_Society_of_Lincoln_Center" title="The Chamber Music Society of Lincoln Center">The Chamber Music Society of Lincoln Center</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/David_Geffen_Hall" title="David Geffen Hall">David Geffen Hall</a> (formerly Philharmonic Hall and Avery Fisher Hall): a 2,738-seat symphony hall; the home stage of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Philharmonic" title="New York Philharmonic">New York Philharmonic</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Opera_House_(Lincoln_Center)" title="Metropolitan Opera House (Lincoln Center)">Metropolitan Opera House</a>: a 3,900-seat opera house; the home stage of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Opera" title="Metropolitan Opera">Metropolitan Opera</a>; as well as List Hall.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/David_H._Koch_Theater" title="David H. Koch Theater">David H. Koch Theater</a> (formerly New York State Theater): a 2,586-seat theater; constructed to be the home of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Ballet" title="New York City Ballet">New York City Ballet</a>, it is also the former home of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Opera" title="New York City Opera">New York City Opera</a> and the Music Theater of Lincoln Center companies.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vivian_Beaumont_Theater" title="Vivian Beaumont Theater">Vivian Beaumont Theater</a>: a 1,080-seat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadway_theater" title="Broadway theater">Broadway</a> theater; operated since 1985 as the main stage of Lincoln Center Theater; previously occupied by The Repertory Theater of Lincoln Center (1965–1973) and The New York Shakespeare Festival (1973–1977).</li>
<li>Mitzi E. Newhouse Theater (originally known as the Forum): a 299-seat theater; operated by Lincoln Center Theater for its <a href="/wiki/Off-Broadway" title="Off-Broadway">Off-Broadway</a>-style productions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>Claire Tow Theater: a 131-seat theater operated by Lincoln Center Theater to house its more experimental productions.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walter_Reade_Theater" title="Walter Reade Theater">Walter Reade Theater</a>: a 268-seat movie theater; used by the <a href="/wiki/Film_Society_of_Lincoln_Center" title="Film Society of Lincoln Center">Film Society of Lincoln Center</a>; features a raised dais used for post-screening filmmaker discussions.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Elinor_Bunin_Munroe_Film_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Elinor Bunin Munroe Film Center (page does not exist)">Elinor Bunin Munroe Film Center</a>: home to the Francesca Beale Theater, Howard Gilman Theater, and the Amphitheater.</li>
<li>Bruno Walter Auditorium at the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Public_Library_for_the_Performing_Arts" title="New York Public Library for the Performing Arts">New York Public Library for the Performing Arts</a></li>
<li>The David Rubenstein Atrium: a facility on <a href="/wiki/Broadway_(Manhattan)" title="Broadway (Manhattan)">Broadway</a> between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/62nd_Street_(Manhattan)" title="62nd Street (Manhattan)">62nd</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/63rd_Street_(Manhattan)" title="63rd Street (Manhattan)">63rd Streets</a>; includes a public visitors' and discount-ticketing facility with amenities that include free performances and a café</li>
<li>The Clark Studio Theater: a 120-seat dance theater; a part of the facilities of <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Center_Education" title="Lincoln Center Education">Lincoln Center Education</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lincoln_Center_Institute_rebranded_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lincoln_Center_Institute_rebranded-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Damrosch_Park" title="Damrosch Park">Damrosch Park</a>: an outdoor amphitheater with a bowl-style stage known as the Guggenheim Band Shell; used for free Lincoln Center Out of Doors presentations and with a special dance floor for Midsummer Night Swing.</li>
<li>Daniel and Joanna S. Rose Rehearsal Studio</li>
<li>Josie Robertson Plaza: the center's central plaza, featuring its iconic fountain; the three main buildings (Metropolitan Opera House, David Geffen Hall, and David H. Koch Theater) face onto this plaza; used as an outdoor venue during Lincoln Center Out of Doors presentations</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Juilliard_School" title="Juilliard School">Juilliard School</a>: a facility housing the school of the same name: building also incorporates <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Morse_Recital_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Morse Recital Hall (page does not exist)">Morse Recital Hall</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Recital_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paul Recital Hall (page does not exist)">Paul Recital Hall</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Juilliard_Drama_Theater&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Juilliard Drama Theater (page does not exist)">Juilliard Drama Theater</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Jay_Sharp_Theater_(Julliard)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peter Jay Sharp Theater (Julliard) (page does not exist)">Peter Jay Sharp Theater</a></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stanley_H._Kaplan_Penthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stanley H. Kaplan Penthouse (page does not exist)">Stanley H. Kaplan Penthouse</a>: a nightclub-style venue; used for intimate concerts, "Meet the Artist" and Great Performers events, lectures, and other events where a small, intimate space is preferred; was also used for jazz performances prior to the construction of the new Jazz at Lincoln Center facilities</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jazz_at_Lincoln_Center" title="Jazz at Lincoln Center">Jazz at Lincoln Center</a>: while a part of the center, it is located separately in the Frederick P. Rose Hall complex within the <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner_Center" title="Time Warner Center">Time Warner Center</a> at <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Circle" title="Columbus Circle">Columbus Circle</a>. It consists of the following performance and related facilities:
<ul>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Appel_Room&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Appel Room (page does not exist)">The Appel Room</a>: a 508-seat amphitheater with 50-foot (15-metre) glass wall overlooking <a href="/wiki/Central_Park" title="Central Park">Central Park</a>; from 2011 to 2013, it was used as the studio for <i><a href="/wiki/Anderson_Live" title="Anderson Live">Anderson Live</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Daytime_television" title="Daytime television">daytime-television</a> <a href="/wiki/Talk_show" title="Talk show">talk show</a> hosted by <a href="/wiki/Anderson_Cooper" title="Anderson Cooper">Anderson Cooper</a></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dizzy%27s_Club_Coca-Cola&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dizzy's Club Coca-Cola (page does not exist)">Dizzy's Club Coca-Cola</a>: a nightclub-style venue that allows jazz to be performed in its traditional venue</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Theater_(New_York_City)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rose Theater (New York City) (page does not exist)">Rose Theater</a>: a 1,094-seat concert hall designed for jazz performances</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Irene_Diamond" title="Irene Diamond">Irene Diamond</a> Education Center: a rehearsal, recording and classroom facility</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Other outdoor venues include Hearst Plaza, Barclay's Capital Grove, and Broadway Plaza.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-venuemap_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-venuemap-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lincoln_Center_for_the_Performing_Arts.2C_Inc.">Lincoln Center for the Performing Arts, Inc.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lincoln_Center_for_the_Performing_Arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Lincoln Center for the Performing Arts, Inc.">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lincoln Center for the Performing Arts, Inc. (LCPA) is one of the eleven resident organizations, and serves three primary roles: presenter of artistic programming, national leader in arts and education and community relations, and manager of the center's campus. As the world's largest presenter of performing arts offering some 5,000 programs, initiatives and events annually, its programs include American Songbook, Great Performers, Lincoln Center Festival, Lincoln Center Out of Doors, Midsummer Night Swing, the <a href="/wiki/Mostly_Mozart_Festival" title="Mostly Mozart Festival">Mostly Mozart Festival</a>, Target Free Thursdays, the White Light Festival and the <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Award</a>–winning <i><a href="/wiki/Live_from_Lincoln_Center" title="Live from Lincoln Center">Live from Lincoln Center</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-about-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2006, the LCPA announced it would join with publishing company <a href="/wiki/John_Wiley_%26_Sons" title="John Wiley &amp; Sons">John Wiley &amp; Sons</a> to publish at least 15 books on performing arts, and would draw on the Lincoln Center Institute's educational background and archives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lincoln_Center_for_the_Performing_Arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lincoln Center is featured in the 1968 films <i><a href="/wiki/No_Way_to_Treat_a_Lady_(film)" title="No Way to Treat a Lady (film)">No Way to Treat a Lady</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Producers_(1968_film)" title="The Producers (1968 film)">The Producers</a></i>. In the former, the character played by <a href="/wiki/Lee_Remick" title="Lee Remick">Lee Remick</a> works there as a tour guide, and in the latter, the theatrical producers Max Bialystock and Leo Bloom, played by <a href="/wiki/Zero_Mostel" title="Zero Mostel">Zero Mostel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gene_Wilder" title="Gene Wilder">Gene Wilder</a>, meet at the Revson Fountain to discuss their scheme to defraud their investors; the climax of the scene is provided by the eruption of the plaza's fountain while Bloom dances around it. Lincoln Center is also where the final competition takes place in <i><a href="/wiki/Pitch_Perfect" title="Pitch Perfect">Pitch Perfect</a></i>.</p>
