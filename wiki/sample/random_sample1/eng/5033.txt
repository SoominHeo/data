<p>The <b>Link-Belt Construction Equipment Company</b> is an American<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> industrial company that develops and manufactures heavy construction equipment, specializing in cranes and excavators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Kentucky" title="Lexington, Kentucky">Lexington, Kentucky</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and has been a part of <a href="/wiki/Sumitomo_Heavy_Industries" title="Sumitomo Heavy Industries">Sumitomo Heavy Industries</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Link-Belt_Construction_Equipment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Link-Belt Construction Equipment Company was founded as Link-Belt Machinery Company in 1880 by <a href="/wiki/William_Dana_Ewart" title="William Dana Ewart">William Dana Ewart</a>. Ewart was a farm implement dealer in <a href="/wiki/Belle_Plaine,_Iowa" title="Belle Plaine, Iowa">Belle Plaine, Iowa</a>. Ewart conceived the idea of a square detachable chain belt system for use in the harvester equipment, for which he obtained a patent on an "improvement in <a href="/wiki/Chain_drive" title="Chain drive">drive-chain</a>", on September 1, 1874.</p>
<p>In 1939, Link-Belt Co. purchased the Speeder Machinery Corporation, a crane-shovel manufacturer in the 3/8 yard to 3/4 yard capacity range. Speeder had introduced the first wheel mounted excavator, in 1922. Link-Belt's Crane and Shovel Division merged with Speeder Machinery to form the Link-Belt Speeder Corporation, a wholly owned subsidiary of Link-Belt Co., eventually locating in <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Rapids,_Iowa" title="Cedar Rapids, Iowa">Cedar Rapids, Iowa</a>.</p>
<p>In 1967, the <a href="/wiki/FMC_Corporation" title="FMC Corporation">FMC Corporation</a> merged with the Link-Belt Company. The company produced <b>FMC Link-Belt</b> branded cranes and excavators.</p>
<p>FMC also produced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_apparatus" title="Fire apparatus">fire truck</a> fire pumps and pumper bodies, and had an OEM arrangement with LTI (<a href="/wiki/American_LaFrance" title="American LaFrance">Ladder Towers Inc.</a>) to market aerial ladders. In the early 1980s the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_apparatus" title="Fire apparatus">fire apparatus</a> division of FMC tried to expand its role in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_apparatus#Aerial_apparatus" title="Fire apparatus">aerial ladders</a> on fire trucks, leveraging the Link-Belt crane division. FMC was ultimately unsuccessful in its expansion into production of aerial ladders. The FMC Fire Apparatus division was also ultimately shut down in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Then in 1986, the Link-Belt Construction Equipment Company was formed as a joint venture between FMC Corporation and <a href="/wiki/Sumitomo_Heavy_Industries" title="Sumitomo Heavy Industries">Sumitomo Heavy Industries</a>.</p>
<p>In 1998, the <a href="/wiki/Excavator" title="Excavator">excavator</a> products were spun off from the Link-Belt Construction Equipment Company to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=LBX_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="LBX Company (page does not exist)">LBX Company</a>, a stand-alone, joint-venture company formed between Sumitomo Construction Machinery Co. and <a href="/wiki/Case_Corporation" title="Case Corporation">Case Corporation</a> to market and sell Link-Belt excavators.</p>
