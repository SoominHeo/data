<p>The <b>Maghreb</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OD_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-OD-1">[1]</a></sup> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ʌ/ short 'u' in 'bud'">ʌ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span></span>/</a></span></span>; literally "sunset";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OD_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-OD-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">المغرب</span>‎‎ <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Arabic transliteration">al-Maghrib</span></i>, "the <b>Arab West</b>"; <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">Berber</a>: <span lang="ber" xml:lang="ber">ⵜⴰⵎⴰⵣⵖⴰ</span> <i><b><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Berber transliteration"><a href="/wiki/Tamazgha" title="Tamazgha">Tamazgha</a></span></b></i>; previously known as <b><a href="/wiki/Barbary_Coast" title="Barbary Coast">Barbary Coast</a>, derived from <a href="/wiki/Berbers" title="Berbers">Berber</a></b>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> or the <b>Greater Maghreb</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">المغرب الكبير</span> ‎‎ <i>al-Maghrib al-Kabīr</i>), is usually defined as much or most of the <a href="/wiki/Region" title="Region">region</a> of western <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> or <b>Northwest Africa</b>, west of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>. The traditional definition as the region including the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas Mountains</a> and the coastal plains of <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>, was later superseded, especially following the 1989 formation of the <a href="/wiki/Arab_Maghreb_Union" title="Arab Maghreb Union">Arab Maghreb Union</a> (اتحاد المغرب العربي), by the inclusion of <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a> and of the disputed territory of <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">Western Sahara</a> (mostly controlled by Morocco). During the <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Al-Andalus</a> era in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> (711–1492), the Maghreb's inhabitants, <a href="/wiki/Maghrebis" title="Maghrebis">Maghrebis</a>, were known as "<a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a>";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> areas of Spain in those times were usually included in contemporary definitions of the Maghreb—hence the use of "Moorish" or "Moors" to describe the Muslim inhabitants of Spain in Western sources.</p>
<p>Historical terms for the region or various portions of the Maghreb include <i><a href="/wiki/Mauretania" title="Mauretania">Mauretania</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Numidia" title="Numidia">Numidia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Libya" title="Ancient Libya">Libya</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Africa_(Roman_province)" title="Africa (Roman province)">Africa</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a>. The term <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D9%85%D8%BA%D8%B1%D8%A8" title="wikt:مغرب"><i>maghrib</i></a> is Arabic for "west", from the verb <i>gharaba</i> (<span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">غرب</span>, "to depart, withdraw"). In the strict sense, the definite form <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D8%A7%D9%84%D9%85%D8%BA%D8%B1%D8%A8" title="wikt:المغرب">al-maghrib</a></i> denotes the country of <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> in particular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It identified the westernmost territories that fell to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_conquests" title="Islamic conquests">Islamic conquests</a> of the 7th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reclus_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reclus-7">[7]</a></sup> Today, it is a proper noun for the present region of the Maghreb, also known politically as <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Arabic transliteration"><i>al-maghrib al-ʻarabīy</i></span> (<span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">المغرب العربي</span> ‘the Arab Maghreb’) or <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Arabic transliteration"><i>al-maghrib al-kabīr</i></span> (<span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">المغرب الكبير</span> "the great Maghreb"). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_language" title="Berber language">Berber language</a>'s alternative term for the region, <i><a href="/wiki/Tamazgha" title="Tamazgha">Tamazgha</a></i> (‘land of the Berbers’),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> has been popularized by <a href="/wiki/Berberism" title="Berberism">Berber activists</a> since the second half of the 20th century.</p>
<p>Before the establishment of modern nation states in the region during the mid-20th century, <i>Maghreb</i> most commonly referred to a smaller area between the Atlas Mountains in the south and the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>, often also including eastern Libya, but not modern Mauritania. As recently as the late 19th century it was used to refer to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Mediterranean" title="Western Mediterranean">Western Mediterranean</a> region of coastal North Africa in general, and to Algeria, Morocco and Tunisia in particular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reclus_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reclus-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Partially isolated from the rest of the continent by the Atlas Mountains and the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> desert, inhabitants of the northern parts of the Maghreb have long had commercial and cultural ties to the inhabitants of the Mediterranean countries of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">Southern Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">Western Asia</a>, going back at least to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenicians" title="Phoenicians">Phoenicians</a> in the 1st millennium BC (the Phoenician colony of <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a> having been founded, according to tradition, in what is now Tunisia circa 800 BC).</p>
<p>The region was somewhat unified as an independent political entity during the rule of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_people" title="Berber people">Berber</a> kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Numidia" title="Numidia">Numidia</a>, which was followed by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>'s rule or influence. That was followed by the brief invasion of the Germanic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vandal" title="Vandal">Vandals</a>, the equally brief re-establishment of a weak Byzantine rule by the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>, the rule of the Islamic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caliphates" title="Caliphates">Caliphates</a> under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umayyad" title="Umayyad">Umayyads</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbasid" title="Abbasid">Abbasids</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatimid" title="Fatimid">Fatimids</a>. The most enduring rule was that of the local Berber empires of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almoravid" title="Almoravid">Almoravids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almohad" title="Almohad">Almohads</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hammadid" title="Hammadid">Hammadids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zirid" title="Zirid">Zirids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marinid" title="Marinid">Marinids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saadi_dynasty" title="Saadi dynasty">Saadi</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wattasid" title="Wattasid">Wattasids</a> (to name some of those among the most prominent) from the 8th to 13th centuries. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turks" title="Ottoman Turks">Ottoman Turks</a> ruled the region as well.</p>
<p>Mauritania, Morocco, Tunisia, Algeria, and Libya established the Maghreb Union in 1989 to promote cooperation and economic integration in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_market" title="Common market">common market</a>. It was envisioned initially by <a href="/wiki/Muammar_Gaddafi" title="Muammar Gaddafi">Muammar Gaddafi</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Superstate" title="Superstate">superstate</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The union included <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">Western Sahara</a> implicitly under Morocco's membership,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> putting Morocco's long cold war with Algeria to a rest. However, this progress was short-lived, and the union is now frozen. Tensions between Algeria and Morocco over <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">Western Sahara</a> re-emerged strongly, reinforced by the unsolved borderline issue between the two countries. These two main conflicts have hindered progress on the union's joint goals and practically made it inactive as a whole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, the instability in the region and growing cross-border security threats revived the calls for regional cooperation – foreign ministers of the Arab Maghreb Union declared a need for coordinated security policy in May 2015 during the 33rd session of the follow-up committee meeting, which brings back the hope of some form of cooperation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">Prehistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maghreb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_North_Africa" title="Prehistoric North Africa">Prehistoric North Africa</a></div>
<p>Around 3,500 BC a tilt in the Earth's orbit created a rapid desertification of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> and formed a natural barrier that severely limited contact between the Maghreb and <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The Maghreb or western North Africa is believed to have been inhabited by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_people" title="Berber people">Berbers</a> since from at least 10,000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity">Antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maghreb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/North_Africa_during_Antiquity" title="North Africa during Antiquity">North Africa during Antiquity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Carthage" title="Ancient Carthage">Ancient Carthage</a></div>
<p>Maghreb coast ports were predominantly occupied or constructed by the Phoenicians, who were then followed by the Carthaginians. The main Phoenician settlements centered in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Tunis" title="Gulf of Tunis">Gulf of Tunis</a> (<a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utica,_Tunisia" title="Utica, Tunisia">Utica, Tunisia</a>) along the North African <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Littoral" title="Littoral">littoral</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Pillars_of_Hercules" title="Pillars of Hercules">Pillars of Hercules</a> and the Libyan coast east of ancient <a href="/wiki/Cyrenaica" title="Cyrenaica">Cyrenaica</a>. They dominated the trade and intercourse of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Mediterranean" title="Western Mediterranean">Western Mediterranean</a> for centuries. The Carthage defeat in the <a href="/wiki/Punic_Wars" title="Punic Wars">Punic Wars</a> during 206 BC allowed Rome to establish the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Province_of_Africa" title="Province of Africa">Province of Africa</a> and control many of these ports and eventually control the entire Maghreb north of the Atlas Mountains. Rome was greatly helped by the defection of King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Massinissa" title="Massinissa">Massinissa</a> and Carthaginian's eastern Numidian <a href="/wiki/Massylii" title="Massylii">Massylii</a> client-allies. Some of the most mountainous regions such as the Moroccan <a href="/wiki/Rif" title="Rif">Rif</a> remained outside Rome's control and the pressures put on the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a> by the invading forces of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbarian_invasions" title="Barbarian invasions">Barbarian invasions</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">Vandals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Spain</a>) in the 5th-century reduced Roman control and establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Vandal_Kingdom" title="Vandal Kingdom">Vandal Kingdom</a> with its capital at Carthage in 430 AD. A century later, the Byzantine emperor <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a> sent a force under General <a href="/wiki/Belisarius" title="Belisarius">Belisarius</a> that <a href="/wiki/Vandalic_War" title="Vandalic War">succeeded in destroying</a> the Vandal kingdom; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exarchate_of_Carthage" title="Exarchate of Carthage">Byzantine rule</a> lasted for 150 years. The <a href="/wiki/Berbers" title="Berbers">Berbers</a> contested outside-the-area control although after the 640s-700 AD period the Arabs controlled the entire region.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maghreb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate" title="Umayyad Caliphate">Umayyad Caliphate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate" title="Abbasid Caliphate">Abbasid Caliphate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Idrisid_dynasty" title="Idrisid dynasty">Idrisid dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Almoravid_dynasty" title="Almoravid dynasty">Almoravid dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Almohad_Caliphate" title="Almohad Caliphate">Almohad Caliphate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hafsid_dynasty" title="Hafsid dynasty">Hafsid dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marinid_dynasty" title="Marinid dynasty">Marinid dynasty</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ziyyanid_dynasty" title="Ziyyanid dynasty">Ziyyanid dynasty</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wattasid_dynasty" title="Wattasid dynasty">Wattasid dynasty</a></div>
<p>The Arabs reached the Maghreb in early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umayyad" title="Umayyad">Umayyad</a> times. Arab expansion and the spread of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> pushed the development of <a href="/wiki/Trans-Saharan_trade" title="Trans-Saharan trade">trans-Saharan trade</a>. While restricted due to the cost and dangers, the trade was highly profitable. Commodities traded included such goods as salt, gold, ivory, and <a href="/wiki/Arab_slave_trade" title="Arab slave trade">slaves</a>. Arab control over the Maghreb was quite weak. Various Islamic variations, such as the <a href="/wiki/Ibadi" title="Ibadi">Ibadis</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shia" title="Shia">Shia</a>, were adopted by some Berbers, often leading to scorning of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caliph" title="Caliph">Caliphal</a> control in favour of their own interpretation of Islam.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic language</a> became widespread only later, as a result of the invasion of the <a href="/wiki/Banu_Hilal" title="Banu Hilal">Banu Hilal</a>, unleashed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatimid" title="Fatimid">Fatimids</a> in punishment for their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zirid" title="Zirid">Zirid</a> clients' political defection and abandonment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiism" title="Shiism">Shiism</a> in the 12th century. Throughout this period, the Maghreb most often was divided into three states roughly corresponding to modern Morocco, western Algeria, and eastern Algeria and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>. The region was occasionally briefly unified, as under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almohad" title="Almohad">Almohads</a>, and briefly under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marinid" title="Marinid">Marinids</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">Modern history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maghreb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modern history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>After the 19th century, areas of the Maghreb were colonized by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>.</p>
<p>Today, more than two and a half million Maghrebi immigrants live in France, many from Algeria and Morocco. In addition, there are 3 million French of Maghrebi origin (in 1999) (with at least one grandparent from Algeria, Morocco or Tunisia).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Another estimation gives a number of six million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maghreb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Maghreb is home to 1 percent of the global population as of 2010. Maghrebi people include Algerians, Libyans, Mauritanians, Moroccans and Tunisians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Maghrebis are largely composed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_people" title="Berber people">Berber</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab_people" title="Arab people">Arab</a> descent with significantly smaller <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Europe" title="Ethnic groups in Europe">European</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan African</a> elements.</p>
<p>Various other influences are also prominent throughout the Maghreb. In northern coastal towns, in particular, several waves of European immigrants influenced the population in the Medieval era. Most notable were the <a href="/wiki/Morisco" title="Morisco">moriscos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muladi" title="Muladi">muladies</a>, that is, the indigenous <a href="/wiki/Spaniards" title="Spaniards">Spaniards</a> (Moors) who forcibly converted to Catholicism and later to be expelled, together with ethnic Arab and Berber Muslims, from the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Spanish Catholic Reconquista</a>. Other European contributions included French, Italians, and others captured by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbary_corsairs" title="Barbary corsairs">corsairs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historically, the Maghreb was home to significant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jewish</a> communities called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maghrebim" title="Maghrebim">Maghrebim</a> who predated the 7th-century introduction and conversion of the region to Islam. These were later augmented by Jews from Spain who, fleeing the Spanish Catholic Inquisition, established a presence in North Africa, chiefly in the urban trading centers. Many Jews from Spain emigrated to North America in the early 19th century or to France and Israel later in the 20th century.</p>
<p>Another significant group are Turks who came over with the expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>. A large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kouloughli" title="Kouloughli">Turkish descended</a> population exists, particularly in Tunisia and Algeria.</p>
<p>Sub-Saharan Africans joined the population mix during centuries of <a href="/wiki/Trans-Saharan_trade" title="Trans-Saharan trade">trans-Saharan trade</a>. Traders and slaves went to the Maghreb from the <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a> region. On the Saharan southern edge of the Maghreb are small communities of black populations, sometimes called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haratine" title="Haratine">Haratine</a>, who are apparently descended from black populations who inhabited the Sahara during its last wet period and then migrated north.</p>
<p>In Algeria especially, a large European minority, the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pied_noir" title="Pied noir">pied noirs</a>", immigrated and settled under French colonial rule in late 19th century. The overwhelming majority of these, however, left Algeria during and following the war for independence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maghreb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original religions of the peoples of the Maghreb seem<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> to have been based and related with fertility cults of a strong <a href="/wiki/Matriarchal_religion" title="Matriarchal religion">matriarchal pantheon</a>, given the social and linguistic structures of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazigh" title="Amazigh">Amazigh</a> cultures antedating all Egyptian and eastern, Asian, northern Mediterranean, and European influences.</p>
<p>Historic records of religion in the Maghreb region show its gradual inclusion in the Classical World, with coastal colonies established first by Phoenicians, some Greeks, and later extensive conquest and colonization by the Romans. By the 2nd century of the common era, the area had become a center of Phoenician-speaking Christianity, where bishops spoke and wrote in Punic, and even Emperor <a href="/wiki/Septimius_Severus" title="Septimius Severus">Septimius Severus</a> was noted by his local accent. Roman settlers and Romanized populations converted to Christianity. The region produced figures such as Christian Church writer <a href="/wiki/Tertullian" title="Tertullian">Tertullian</a> (c. 155 – c. 202); and Christian Church martyrs or leading figures such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perpetua" title="Perpetua">Perpetua and Felicity</a> (martyrs, c. 200 CE); <a href="/wiki/Cyprian" title="Cyprian">St. Cyprian of Carthage</a> (+ 258); <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monica_(Christian_saint)" title="Monica (Christian saint)">St. Monica</a>; her son the philosopher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Augustine" title="St. Augustine">St. Augustine</a>, Bishop of Hippo I (+ 430) (1); and <a href="/wiki/Julia_of_Corsica" title="Julia of Corsica">St. Julia of Carthage</a> (5th century).</p>
<p>The domination of Christianity ended when Arab invasions brought Islam in 647. Carthage fell in 698 and the remainder of the region followed in subsequent decades. Gradual Islamization proceeded, although surviving letters showed correspondence from regional Christians to Rome up until the 12th century. Christianity was still a living faith. Although there were a fair number of conversions after the conquest Muslims did not become a majority until some time late in the 9th century and became vast majority during the 10th (Staying Roman, Jonathan Conant, pp. 362–368, 2012). Christian bishoprics and dioceses continued to be active, with relations continuing with Rome. As late as Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedict_VII" title="Benedict VII">Benedict VII</a> (974-983) reign, a new <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Archbishop_of_Carthage&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Archbishop of Carthage (page does not exist)">Archbishop of Carthage</a> was consecrated. Evidence of Christianity in the region then faded through the 10th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> However, by the end of the 11th century only two bishops were left in Carthage and Hippo Regius. Pope Gregory VII, 1073–85, consecrated a new bishop for Hippo. Christianty seems to have suffered several shocks that lead to its demise. First many upper-class urban-swelling Latin-speaking Christians left for Europe after the Muslim conquest. The second were large scale conversions to Islam in the 9th century. Many Christians of a much reduced community left in the mid-11th century. Finally the small remnant were evacuated to Sicily in the 12th by the Normans. The Latin-African language lingered on a while longer.</p>
<p>From the end of the 7th century the region's peoples began their total conversion to Islam which took more than 400 years. There is a small but thriving Jewish community, as well as a small Christian community. Most Muslims follow the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunni" title="Sunni">Sunni</a> <a href="/wiki/Maliki" title="Maliki">Maliki</a> school. Small <a href="/wiki/Ibadi" title="Ibadi">Ibadi</a> communities remain in some areas. A strong tradition of venerating <a href="/wiki/Marabout" title="Marabout">marabouts</a> and saints' tombs is found throughout regions inhabited by Berbers. Any map of the region demonstrates the tradition by the proliferation of "<a href="/wiki/Sidi" title="Sidi">Sidi</a>"s, showing places named after the marabouts. Like some other religious traditions, this has substantially decreased over the 20th century. A network of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zaouia" title="Zaouia">zaouias</a> traditionally helped proliferate basic literacy and knowledge of Islam in rural regions.</p>
<p>There are communities of Christians mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholics" title="Roman Catholics">Roman Catholics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> (100,000-380,000),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-multiple_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-multiple-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a>, (6,500) <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> (~380,000),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> (170,000) and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> (25,000).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Most of the Roman Catholics in Greater Maghreb are of <a href="/wiki/Pied-Noir" title="Pied-Noir">French</a>, Spanish, and Italian descent who immigrated during the colonial era, while some are foreign missionaries or immigrant worker. There is also a Christian communities of Berber or Arab descent in Greater Maghreb countries, mostly converted during the modern era or under and after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_colonialism" title="French colonialism">French colonialism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-worldreview.info_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-worldreview.info-27">[27]</a></sup> Prior to independence, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> was home to 1.4 million <a href="/wiki/Pied-Noir" title="Pied-Noir">Pied-Noir</a> (mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cook_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cook-28">[28]</a></sup> and Morocco was home to half a million <a href="/wiki/European_Moroccans" title="European Moroccans">Europeans</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> was home to 255,000 <a href="/wiki/European_Tunisians" title="European Tunisians">Europeans</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> was home to 145,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europeans" title="Europeans">Europeans</a>. In religion, most of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pieds-noirs" title="Pieds-noirs">pieds-noirs</a></i> in Maghreb are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christians</a>. Due to the exodus of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pieds-noirs" title="Pieds-noirs">pieds-noirs</a></i> in the 1960s there are more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_African" title="North African">North African</a> Christians of Berber or Arab descent live in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> than in Greater Maghreb.</p>
<p>Recently, the Protestant community of Berber or Arab descent has experienced significant growth, and conversions to Christianity, especially to <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">Evangelicalism</a>, is common in <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> especially in the <a href="/wiki/Kabylie" title="Kabylie">Kabylie</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-report-34">[34]</a></sup> A 2015 study estimates 380,000 Muslims converted to <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_Algeria" title="Christianity in Algeria">Christianity in Algeria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Believers_in_Christ_from_a_Muslim_Background_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Believers_in_Christ_from_a_Muslim_Background-35">[35]</a></sup> The number of the <a href="/wiki/Moroccans" title="Moroccans">Moroccans</a> who <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Converted_to_Christianity" title="Converted to Christianity">converted to Christianity</a> (most of them secret worshippers) are estimated between 40,000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>-150,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> International Religious Freedom Report for 2007 estimates thousands of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tunisian_people" title="Tunisian people">Tunisian</a> Muslims who <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convert_to_Christianity" title="Convert to Christianity">convert to Christianity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-report-34">[34]</a></sup> A 2015 study estimate some 1,500 believers in Christ from a Muslim background living in the Libya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maghrebi_traders_in_Jewish_history">Maghrebi traders in Jewish history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maghreb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Maghrebi traders in Jewish history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 10th century, as the social and political environment in <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a> became increasingly hostile to Jews, some Jewish traders emigrated to the Maghreb, especially <a href="/wiki/Kairouan" title="Kairouan">Kairouan</a> in Tunisia. Over the following two or three centuries, such Jewish traders became known as the Maghribis, a distinctive social group who traveled throughout the Mediterranean world. They passed this identification on from father to son. Their tight-knit pan-Maghreb community had the ability to use social sanctions as a credible alternative to legal recourse, which was weak at the time anyway. This unique institutional alternative permitted the Maghribis to very successfully participate in Mediterranean trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maghreb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecoregions">Ecoregions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maghreb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ecoregions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Maghreb is divided into a <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">Mediterranean climate</a> region in the north, and the <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> in the south. The Maghreb's variations in elevation, rainfall, temperature, and soils give rise to distinct communities of plants and animals. The <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">World Wide Fund for Nature</a> (WWF) identifies several distinct <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregions</a> in the Maghreb.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mediterranean_Maghreb">Mediterranean Maghreb</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maghreb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mediterranean Maghreb">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The portions of the Maghreb between the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas Mountains</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>, along with coastal <a href="/wiki/Tripolitania" title="Tripolitania">Tripolitania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyrenaica" title="Cyrenaica">Cyrenaica</a> in Libya, are home to <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_forests,_woodlands,_and_scrub" title="Mediterranean forests, woodlands, and scrub">Mediterranean forests, woodlands, and scrub</a>. These ecoregions share many species of plants and animals with other portions of <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean Basin</a>. The southern extent of the Mediterranean Maghreb corresponds with the 100 mm <a href="/wiki/Contour_line#Precipitation_and_air_moisture" title="Contour line">isohyet</a>, or the southern range of the <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">European Olive</a> <i>(Olea europea)</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Esparto" title="Esparto">Esparto Grass</a> <i>(Stipa tenacissima)</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wickens_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wickens-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean_acacia-argania_dry_woodlands_and_succulent_thickets" title="Mediterranean acacia-argania dry woodlands and succulent thickets">Mediterranean acacia-argania dry woodlands and succulent thickets</a> (Morocco, Canary Islands (Spain), Western Sahara)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_dry_woodlands_and_steppe" title="Mediterranean dry woodlands and steppe">Mediterranean dry woodlands and steppe</a> (Algeria, Egypt, Libya, Morocco, Tunisia)</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saharan_Maghreb">Saharan Maghreb</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maghreb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Saharan Maghreb">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Sahara extends across northern Africa from the Atlantic Ocean to the Red Sea. Its central part is hyper-arid and supports little plant or animal life, but the northern portion of the desert receives occasional winter rains, while the strip along the Atlantic coast receives moisture from marine fog, which nourishes a greater variety of plants and animals. The northern edge of the Sahara corresponds to the 100 mm isohyet, which is also the northern range of the <a href="/wiki/Date_palm" title="Date palm">date palm</a> <i>(Phoenix dactylifera)</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wickens_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wickens-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/North_Saharan_steppe_and_woodlands" title="North Saharan steppe and woodlands">North Saharan steppe and woodlands</a>: This ecoregion lies along the northern edge of the Sahara, next to the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_forests,_woodlands,_and_scrub" title="Mediterranean forests, woodlands, and scrub">Mediterranean forests, woodlands, and scrub</a> ecoregions of the Mediterranean Maghreb and Cyrenaica. Winter rains sustain shrublands and dry woodlands that form a transition between the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">Mediterranean climate</a> regions to the north and the hyper-arid Sahara proper to the south. It covers 1,675,300 square km (646,800 square miles) in <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>, Mauritania, <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>, and Western Sahara.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Atlantic_coastal_desert" title="Atlantic coastal desert">Atlantic coastal desert</a>: The Atlantic coastal desert occupies a narrow strip along the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> coast, where fog generated offshore by the cool <a href="/wiki/Canary_Current" title="Canary Current">Canary Current</a> provides sufficient moisture to sustain a variety of <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Succulent_plant" title="Succulent plant">succulents</a>, and shrubs. It covers 39,900 square kilometres (15,400 sq mi) in <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">Western Sahara</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert_(ecoregion)" title="Sahara Desert (ecoregion)">Sahara desert</a>: This ecoregion covers the hyper-arid central portion of the Sahara where rainfall is minimal and sporadic. Vegetation is rare, and this ecoregion consists mostly of sand dunes <i>(<a href="/wiki/Erg_(landform)" title="Erg (landform)">erg</a>)</i>, stone plateaus <i>(<a href="/wiki/Hamada" title="Hamada">hamada</a>)</i>, gravel plains <i>(<a href="/wiki/Desert_pavement" title="Desert pavement">reg</a>)</i>, dry valleys <i>(<a href="/wiki/Wadi" title="Wadi">wadi</a>)</i>, and salt flats. It covers 4,639,900 square km (1,791,500 square miles) of Algeria, <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>, Egypt, Libya, <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a>, Mauritania, <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saharan_halophytics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saharan halophytics (page does not exist)">Saharan halophytics</a>: Seasonally flooded saline depressions in the Maghreb are home to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halophytic" title="Halophytic">halophytic</a>, or salt-adapted, plant communities. The Saharan halophytics cover 54,000 square km (20,800 square miles), including <a href="/wiki/Tunisian_salt_lakes" title="Tunisian salt lakes">Tunisian salt lakes</a> of central Tunisia, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chott_Melghir" title="Chott Melghir">Chott Melghir</a> in Algeria, and other areas of Egypt, Algeria, Mauritania, and Western Sahara.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maghreb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The countries of the Maghreb share many cultural traditions. Among these is a culinary tradition that <a href="/wiki/Habib_Bourguiba" title="Habib Bourguiba">Habib Bourguiba</a> defined as western Arab, where <a href="/wiki/Couscous" title="Couscous">couscous</a> is the staple food, as opposed to Eastern Arab where white rice is the staple food. In terms of food, similarities beyond the starches are found throughout the Arab world.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetics_of_the_Maghreb_population">Genetics of the Maghreb population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maghreb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Genetics of the Maghreb population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Y-chromosome genetic structure of the Maghreb population seems to be mainly modulated by geography, The Y-DNA Haplogroups <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_E1b1b_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup E1b1b (Y-DNA)">E1b1b</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_J_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup J (Y-DNA)">J</a> make up the vast majority of the genetic markers of the populations of the Maghreb. Haplogroup <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_E1b1b_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup E1b1b (Y-DNA)">E1b1b</a> is the most widespread among Maghrebi groups, especially the downstream lineage of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-M81" title="E-M81">E1b1b1b1a</a>, which is typical of the indigenous Berbers of North-West Africa. Haplogroup J is more indicative of Middle East origins, and has its highest distribution among populations in Arabia and the Levant. Due to the distribution of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-M81" title="E-M81">E-M81</a>(E1b1b1b1a), which has reached its highest documented levels in the world at 95-100% in some populations of the Maghreb, it has often been termed the "Berber marker" in the scientific literature. The second most common marker, <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_J_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup J (Y-DNA)">Haplogroup J</a> especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_J1_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup J1 (Y-DNA)">J1</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> which is typically Middle Eastern and originates in the Arabian peninsula can reach frequencies of up to 35% in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-49">[49]</a></sup> Its highest density is founded in the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-49">[49]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_R1" title="Haplogroup R1">Haplogroup R1</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> which is a Eurasian marker has also been observed in the Maghreb, though with lower frequency. The Y-DNA Haplogroups shown above are observed in both Arabs and Berber-speakers.</p>
