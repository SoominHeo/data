<p>The <b>Madeira River</b> (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><i>Rio Madeira</i></span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Portuguese" title="Help:IPA for Portuguese">[ˈʁi.u mɐˈdejɾɐ]</a></span>) is a major waterway in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, approximately 3,250 km (2,020 mi) long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Madeira is one of the biggest tributaries of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon</a>, accounting for about 15% of the water in the basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> A map from <a href="/wiki/Emanuel_Bowen" title="Emanuel Bowen">Emanuel Bowen</a> in 1747, held by the <a href="/wiki/David_Rumsey_Historical_Map_Collection" title="David Rumsey Historical Map Collection">David Rumsey Map Collection</a>, refers to the Madeira by the pre-colonial, indigenous name Cuyari:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>The River of Cuyari, called by the Portuguese Madeira or the Wood River, is formed by two great rivers, which join near its mouth. It was by this River, that the Nation of Topinambes passed into the River Amazon.</p>
</blockquote>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madeira_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The mean inter-annual precipitations on the great basins vary from 75 to 300 centimetres (2.46–9.84 ft), the entire upper Madeira basin receiving 170.5 centimetres (5.59 ft). The greatest extremes of rainfall are between 49 to 700 centimetres (1.61–22.97 ft). At its head, the Madeira on its own is still one of the largest rivers of the world, with a mean inter-annual discharge of 18,000 cubic metres per second (640,000 cu ft/s), i.e. 536 cubic kilometres (129 cu mi) per year, approximately half the discharge of the <a href="/wiki/Congo_River" title="Congo River">Congo River</a>. The mean inter-annual contribution of the Bolivian <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> is 4,170 cubic metres per second (147,000 cu ft/s), i.e. 132 cubic kilometres (32 cu mi) per year, representing 25% of the discharge of the entire upper Madeira basin. On the further course towards the Amazon, the mean discharge of the Madeira increases up to 31,200 cubic metres per second (1,100,000 cu ft/s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Course">Course</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madeira_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Course">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rio_Madeira-_Cachoeira_do_Teot%C3%B4nio.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1902" data-file-width="2789" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Rio_Madeira-_Cachoeira_do_Teot%C3%B4nio.jpg/250px-Rio_Madeira-_Cachoeira_do_Teot%C3%B4nio.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Rio_Madeira-_Cachoeira_do_Teot%C3%B4nio.jpg/375px-Rio_Madeira-_Cachoeira_do_Teot%C3%B4nio.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Rio_Madeira-_Cachoeira_do_Teot%C3%B4nio.jpg/500px-Rio_Madeira-_Cachoeira_do_Teot%C3%B4nio.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

<p>Between <a href="/wiki/Guajar%C3%A1-Mirim" title="Guajará-Mirim">Guajará-Mirim</a> and the falls of Teotônio, the Madeira receives the drainage of the north-eastern slopes of the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> from <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_de_la_Sierra" title="Santa Cruz de la Sierra">Santa Cruz de la Sierra</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cusco_Region" title="Cusco Region">Cuzco</a>, the whole of the south-western slope of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazilian</a> <a href="/wiki/Mato_Grosso" title="Mato Grosso">Mato Grosso</a> and the northern slope of the <a href="/wiki/Chiquitania" title="Chiquitania">Chiquitos</a> sierras. In total, the catchment area is 850,000 km<sup>2</sup>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNESCO_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-6">[6]</a></sup> almost equal in area to <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> combined. The waters flow into the Madeira from many large rivers, the principal of which, (from east to west), are the <a href="/wiki/Guapor%C3%A9_River" title="Guaporé River">Guaporé</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Itenez" title="Itenez">Itenez</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Baures" title="Baures">Baures</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blanco_River_(Bolivia)" title="Blanco River (Bolivia)">Blanco</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Itonama" title="Itonama">Itonama</a> or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=San_Miguel_River_(South_America)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="San Miguel River (South America) (page does not exist)">San Miguel</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mamor%C3%A9" title="Mamoré">Mamoré</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beni_River" title="Beni River">Beni</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mayutata" title="Mayutata">Mayutata</a> or <a href="/wiki/Madre_de_Dios_River" title="Madre de Dios River">Madre de Dios</a>, all of which are reinforced by numerous secondary but powerful affluents. The climate of the upper catchment area varies from humid in the western edge with the origin of the river's main stem by volume (Río Madre de Dios, Río Beni) to semi arid in the southernmost part with the andine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Headwaters" title="Headwaters">headwaters</a> of the main stem by length (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_Caine" title="Río Caine">Río Caine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rocha_River" title="Rocha River">Río Rocha</a>, <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_Grande_(Bolivia)" title="Río Grande (Bolivia)">Río Grande</a>, Mamoré).</p>
<p>All of the upper branches of the river Madeira find their way to the falls across the open, almost level <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mojos,_Bolivia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mojos, Bolivia (page does not exist)">Mojos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beni_Department" title="Beni Department">Beni</a> plains, 90,000 km<sup>2</sup> (35,000 sq mi) of which are yearly flooded to an average depth of about 3 feet (0.91 m) for a period of from three to four months.</p>
<p>From its source in the confluence of <a href="/wiki/Beni_River" title="Beni River">Beni</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mamor%C3%A9_River" title="Mamoré River">Mamoré</a> rivers and downstream to <a href="/wiki/Abuna_River" title="Abuna River">Abuna</a> river the Madeira flows northward forming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolivia-Brazil_border" title="Bolivia-Brazil border">border between Bolivia and Brazil</a>. Below its confluence with the latter tributary the flow of river changes to north-eastward direction, inland of <a href="/wiki/Rond%C3%B4nia" title="Rondônia">Rondônia</a> state of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. The section of the river from the border to <a href="/wiki/Porto_Velho" title="Porto Velho">Porto Velho</a> has notable drop of bed and was not navigable. Before 2012 the falls of Teotônio and of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Falls_of_San_Antonio" title="Falls of San Antonio">San Antonio</a> existed here, they had higher flow rate and bigger level drop than more famous <a href="/wiki/Boyoma_Falls" title="Boyoma Falls">Boyoma Falls</a> in Africa. Currently these rapids are submerged by the reservoir of <a href="/wiki/Santo_Ant%C3%B4nio_Dam" title="Santo Antônio Dam">Santo Antônio Dam</a>. Below <a href="/wiki/Porto_Velho" title="Porto Velho">Porto Velho</a> the Madeira meanders north-eastward through the <a href="/wiki/Rond%C3%B4nia" title="Rondônia">Rondônia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazonas_State,_Brazil" title="Amazonas State, Brazil">Amazonas</a> states of north west Brazil to its junction with the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon</a>.</p>
<p>The 283,117 hectares (699,600 acres) <a href="/wiki/Rio_Madeira_Sustainable_Development_Reserve" title="Rio Madeira Sustainable Development Reserve">Rio Madeira Sustainable Development Reserve</a>, created in 2006, extends along the north bank of the river opposite the town of <a href="/wiki/Novo_Aripuan%C3%A3" title="Novo Aripuanã">Novo Aripuanã</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> At its mouth is <a href="/wiki/Tupinambarana" title="Tupinambarana">Ilha Tupinambaranas</a>, an extensive marshy region formed by the Madeira’s distributaries.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Navigation">Navigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madeira_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Navigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Madeira river rises more than 15 m (50 ft) during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainy_season" title="Rainy season">rainy season</a>, and ocean vessels may ascend it to the Falls of San Antonio, near <a href="/wiki/Porto_Velho" title="Porto Velho">Porto Velho</a>, Brazil, 1,070 km (660 mi) above its mouth; but in the dry months, from June to November, it is only navigable for the same distance for craft drawing about 2 m (from 5 to 6 feet) of water. The <a href="/wiki/Madeira-Mamor%C3%A9_Railroad" title="Madeira-Mamoré Railroad">Madeira-Mamoré Railroad</a> runs in a 365 km (227 mi) loop around the unnavigable section to <a href="/wiki/Guajar%C3%A1-Mirim" title="Guajará-Mirim">Guajará-Mirim</a> on the Mamoré River, but is not functional, limiting shipping from the Atlantic at <a href="/wiki/Porto_Velho" title="Porto Velho">Porto Velho</a>.</p>
<p>Today, it is also one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_Basin" title="Amazon Basin">Amazon Basin</a>'s most active waterways, and helps export close to 4 million tons of grains, which are loaded onto barges in <a href="/wiki/Porto_Velho" title="Porto Velho">Porto Velho</a>, where both <a href="/wiki/Cargill" title="Cargill">Cargill</a> and Amaggi have loading facilities, and then shipped down the Madeira to the ports of <a href="/wiki/Itacoatiara" title="Itacoatiara">Itacoatiara</a>, near the mouth of the Madeira, just upstream on the left bank of the Amazon, or further down the Amazon, to the port of <a href="/wiki/Santar%C3%A9m,_Par%C3%A1" title="Santarém, Pará">Santarem</a>, at the mouth of the Tapajos River. From these two ports, Panamax type ships then export the grains - mainly soy and corn - to Europe and Asia. The Madeira waterway is also used to take fuel from the REMAN refinery (<a href="/wiki/Petrobras" title="Petrobras">Petrobras</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Manaus" title="Manaus">Manaus</a>, state capital of <a href="/wiki/Amazonas_(Brazilian_state)" title="Amazonas (Brazilian state)">Amazonas</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Porto_Velho" title="Porto Velho">Porto Velho</a>, from where the states of <a href="/wiki/Acre_(state)" title="Acre (state)">Acre</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rondonia" title="Rondonia">Rondonia</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Mato_Grosso" title="Mato Grosso">Mato Grosso</a> are supplied mainly with gasoline (petrol) refined in <a href="/wiki/Manaus" title="Manaus">Manaus</a>. Cargo barges also use the Madeira on the route between <a href="/wiki/Manaus" title="Manaus">Manaus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Porto_Velho" title="Porto Velho">Porto Velho</a>, which is 1,225 km<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hidrovias_Brasileiras_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hidrovias_Brasileiras-8">[8]</a></sup> along the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Negro_(Amazon)" title="Rio Negro (Amazon)">Rio Negro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon</a> and Madeira, connecting Manaus' industrial district with the rest of Brazil, as Manaus is land-locked as far as logistics with the rest of the country are concerned, to bring in part of its raw materials, and export its produce to the major consumer centres of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sao_Paulo" title="Sao Paulo">Sao Paulo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a>. In 2012, the cargo amounted to 287,835 tons<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hidrovias_Brasileiras_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hidrovias_Brasileiras-8">[8]</a></sup> (both directions). The total tonnage shipped in 2012 on the Madeira accounted to 5,076,014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hidrovias_Brasileiras_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hidrovias_Brasileiras-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two large dams (see below) are under construction as part of the <a href="/wiki/Initiative_for_the_Integration_of_the_Regional_Infrastructure_of_South_America" title="Initiative for the Integration of the Regional Infrastructure of South America">IIRSA</a> regional integration project. The dam projects include large ship-locks capable of moving oceangoing vessels between the impounded reservoir and the downstream river. If the project is completed, "more than 4,000 km of waterways upstream from the dams in Brazil, Bolivia, and Peru would become navigable.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madeira_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>As typical of Amazonian rivers with the primary headwaters in the Andes, the Madeira River is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turbid" title="Turbid">turbid</a> because of high sediment levels and it is <a href="/wiki/Whitewater_river_(river_type)" title="Whitewater river (river type)">whitewater</a>, but some of its tributaries are <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Clearwater_river_(river_type)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Clearwater river (river type) (page does not exist)">clearwater</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Aripuan%C3%A3_River" title="Aripuanã River">Aripuanã</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ji-Paran%C3%A1_River" title="Ji-Paraná River">Ji-Paraná</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Blackwater_river" title="Blackwater river">blackwater</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Manicor%C3%A9_River" title="Manicoré River">Manicoré</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>A subspecies of <a href="/wiki/Boto" title="Boto">boto</a> (<a href="/wiki/Amazon_river_dolphin" title="Amazon river dolphin">Amazon river dolphin</a>) inhabits the Madeira River system. It has been estimated that there are more than 900 fish species in the Madeira River Basin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> making it one of the freshwater systems in the world with the highest <a href="/wiki/Species_richness" title="Species richness">species richness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madeira_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The river is the fifth title of the album <a href="/wiki/Aguas_da_Amazonia" title="Aguas da Amazonia">Aguas da Amazonia</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dams">Dams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madeira_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In July 2007, plans have been approved by the Brazilian Government to construct two <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydroelectric dams</a> on the Madeira River, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santo_Antonio_Dam" title="Santo Antonio Dam">Santo Antonio Dam</a> near Porto Velho and the <a href="/wiki/Jirau_Dam" title="Jirau Dam">Jirau Dam</a> about 100 km upstream. Both the Jirau and Santo Antonio dams are run-of-the-river projects that do not impound a large reservoir. Both dams also feature some environmental re-mediation efforts (such as fish ladders). As a consequence, it has been suggested that there has not been strong environmental opposition to the implementation of the Madeira river complex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Yet, if the fish ladders fail, "several valuable migratory fish species could suffer near-extinction as a result of the Madeira dams."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwashing_Hydropower_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwashing_Hydropower-14">[14]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> There are also concerns with deforestation and pressure on conservation areas and indigenous peoples' territories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The Worldwatch institute has also criticized the fast-track approval process for "kindler, gentler dams with smaller reservoirs, designed to lessen social and environmental impacts", claiming that no project should "fast-track the licensing of new dams in Amazonia and allow projects to circumvent Brazil's tough environmental laws".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwashing_Hydropower_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwashing_Hydropower-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
