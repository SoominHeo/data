<p>The <b>Royal Palace of Madrid</b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><i>Palacio Real de Madrid</i></span>) is the official residence of the Spanish Royal Family at the city of <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, but is only used for state ceremonies. King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Felipe_VI" title="Felipe VI">Felipe VI</a> and the Royal Family do not reside in the palace, choosing instead the more modest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palacio_de_la_Zarzuela" title="Palacio de la Zarzuela">Palacio de la Zarzuela</a> on the outskirts of Madrid.</p>
<p>The palace is owned by the Spanish State and administered by the <a href="/wiki/Patrimonio_Nacional" title="Patrimonio Nacional">Patrimonio Nacional</a>, a public agency of the Ministry of the Presidency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sancho_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sancho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:7</sup> The palace is located on Calle de Bailén (Bailén Street), in the Western part of downtown Madrid, East of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manzanares_River" title="Manzanares River">Manzanares River</a>, and is accessible from the Ópera <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madrid_metro" title="Madrid metro">metro</a> station. Several rooms in the palace are regularly open to the public except during state functions. An admission fee of €11 is required.</p>
<p>The palace is located on the site of a 9th-century <a href="/wiki/Alc%C3%A1zar" title="Alcázar">Alcázar</a> ("Muslim-era fortress"), near the town of <i>Magerit</i>, constructed as an outpost by <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_I_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Muhammad I of Córdoba">Muhammad I of Córdoba</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:7</sup> and inherited after 1036 by the independent <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moorish</a> <a href="/wiki/Taifa_of_Toledo" title="Taifa of Toledo">Taifa of Toledo</a>. After Madrid fell to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfonso_VI_of_Castile" title="Alfonso VI of Castile">Alfonso VI of Castile</a> in 1083, the edifice was only rarely used by the kings of Castile. In 1329, King <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_XI_of_Castile" title="Alfonso XI of Castile">Alfonso XI of Castile</a> convened the <i>cortes</i> of Madrid for the first time. <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II</a> moved his court to Madrid in 1561.</p>
<p>The old <i><a href="/wiki/Royal_Alcazar_of_Madrid" title="Royal Alcazar of Madrid">Alcázar</a></i> was built on the location in the 16th century. It burned 24 December 1734 and King <a href="/wiki/Philip_V_of_Spain" title="Philip V of Spain">Philip V</a> ordered a new palace built on the same site. Construction spanned the years 1738 to 1755<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patromonio_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-patromonio-3">[3]</a></sup> and followed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernini" title="Bernini">Berniniesque</a> design by <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Juvarra" title="Filippo Juvarra">Filippo Juvarra</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Giovanni_Battista_Sacchetti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Giovanni Battista Sacchetti (page does not exist)">Giovanni Battista Sacchetti</a> in cooperation with <a href="/wiki/Ventura_Rodr%C3%ADguez" title="Ventura Rodríguez">Ventura Rodríguez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Sabatini" title="Francesco Sabatini">Francesco Sabatini</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mart%C3%ADn_Sarmiento" title="Martín Sarmiento">Martín Sarmiento</a>. <a href="/wiki/Charles_III_of_Spain" title="Charles III of Spain">Charles III</a> first occupied the new palace in 1764.</p>
<p>The last monarch who lived continuously in the palace was <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_XIII_of_Spain" title="Alfonso XIII of Spain">Alfonso XIII</a>, although <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Aza%C3%B1a" title="Manuel Azaña">Manuel Azaña</a>, president of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Second_Republic" title="Spanish Second Republic">Second Republic</a>, also inhabited it, making him the last head of state to do so. During that period the palace was known as "Palacio Nacional". There is still a room next to the Real Capilla, which is known by the name "Office of Azaña".</p>
<p>The palace has 135,000 square metres (1,450,000 sq ft) of floorspace and contains 3,418 rooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rooms_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-rooms-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biggestpalace_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-biggestpalace-5">[5]</a></sup> It is the largest royal palace in Europe by floor area. The interior of the palace is notable for its wealth of art and the use of many types of fine materials in the construction and the decoration of its rooms. These include paintings by artists such as <a href="/wiki/Caravaggio" title="Caravaggio">Caravaggio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez" title="Diego Velázquez">Velázquez</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Goya" title="Francisco de Goya">Francisco de Goya</a> and frescoes by <a href="/wiki/Corrado_Giaquinto" title="Corrado Giaquinto">Corrado Giaquinto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Flandes" title="Juan de Flandes">Juan de Flandes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Tiepolo" title="Giovanni Battista Tiepolo">Giovanni Battista Tiepolo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anton_Raphael_Mengs" title="Anton Raphael Mengs">Anton Raphael Mengs</a>. Other collections of great historical and artistic importance preserved in the building include the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Armoury_of_Madrid" title="Royal Armoury of Madrid">Royal Armoury of Madrid</a>, Porcelain, Watches, Furniture, Silverware and the world's only complete <a href="/wiki/Stradivarius" title="Stradivarius">Stradivarius</a> string quintet.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_building">History of the building</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of the building">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Royal_Alcazar_of_Madrid" title="Royal Alcazar of Madrid">Royal Alcazar of Madrid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Project_of_Filippo_Juvarra_for_the_Royal_Palace_of_Madrid" title="Project of Filippo Juvarra for the Royal Palace of Madrid">Project of Filippo Juvarra for the Royal Palace of Madrid</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Muhammad I, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umayyad" title="Umayyad">Umayyad</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Cordoba" title="Emirate of Cordoba">Emir</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caliphate_of_Cordoba" title="Caliphate of Cordoba">Cordoba</a>, between 860 and 880. After the Moors were driven out of Toledo in the 11th century, the castle retained its defensive function. <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_Castile" title="Henry III of Castile">Henry III of Castile</a> added several towers. His son <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_Castile" title="John II of Castile">John II</a> used it as a royal residence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:7</sup></p>
<p>However, during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Castilian_Succession" title="War of the Castilian Succession">War of the Castilian Succession</a> (1476) the troops of <a href="/wiki/Joanna_la_Beltraneja" title="Joanna la Beltraneja">Joanna la Beltraneja</a> were besieged in the Alcázar, causing severe damage to the royal building.</p>
<p>The only drawing of the castle from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> is one made in 1534 by Cornelius Vermeyen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:7</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Spain" title="Habsburg Spain">Habsburg</a> Emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a> extended and renovated the castle in 1537, using the architects <a href="/wiki/Alonso_de_Covarrubias" title="Alonso de Covarrubias">Alonso de Covarrubias</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luis_de_Vega" title="Luis de Vega">Luis de Vega</a>. <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II</a> made Madrid his capital in 1561 and added a continued the renovations. <a href="/wiki/Philip_III_of_Spain" title="Philip III of Spain">Philip III</a> added a long southern facade between 1610 and 1636. <a href="/wiki/Philip_V_of_Spain" title="Philip V of Spain">Philip V of Bourbon</a> renovated the royal apartments in 1700.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:7–8</sup></p>
<p>The Alcázar of the Habsburgs was austere in comparison to the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Versailles" title="Palace of Versailles">Palace of Versailles</a> where the new king spent his childhood and he began a series redesigns led by <a href="/wiki/Teodoro_Ardemans" title="Teodoro Ardemans">Teodoro Ardemans</a> and René Carlier. On the other hand, the main rooms were redecorated in the style of French palaces by the Queen <a href="/wiki/Maria_Luisa_of_Savoy" title="Maria Luisa of Savoy">Maria Luisa of Savoy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Marie_Anne_de_La_Tr%C3%A9moille,_princesse_des_Ursins" title="Marie Anne de La Trémoille, princesse des Ursins">Princess of Ursins</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_baroque_palace">The baroque palace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The baroque palace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vista_de_parte_del_Real_Palacio_desde_la_Cuesta_de_la_Vega.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="289" data-file-width="477" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Vista_de_parte_del_Real_Palacio_desde_la_Cuesta_de_la_Vega.jpg/220px-Vista_de_parte_del_Real_Palacio_desde_la_Cuesta_de_la_Vega.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Vista_de_parte_del_Real_Palacio_desde_la_Cuesta_de_la_Vega.jpg/330px-Vista_de_parte_del_Real_Palacio_desde_la_Cuesta_de_la_Vega.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Vista_de_parte_del_Real_Palacio_desde_la_Cuesta_de_la_Vega.jpg/440px-Vista_de_parte_del_Real_Palacio_desde_la_Cuesta_de_la_Vega.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>On Christmas Eve 1734, the Alcázar was destroyed by a fire originating in the rooms of the French painter <a href="/wiki/Jean_Ranc" title="Jean Ranc">Jean Ranc</a>. It was not detected quickly, due to the warning bells being confused with the call to mass. For fear of looting, the doors of the building remained closed, hampering rescue efforts. Many works of art were lost, such as the <i>Expulsion of the Moors</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez" title="Diego Velázquez">Diego Velázquez</a>. Others, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Las_Meninas" title="Las Meninas">Las Meninas</a></i>, were rescued by tossing them out the windows. Fortunately, many pieces were saved because the king ordered that much of his collection be moved to the <a href="/wiki/Buen_Retiro_Palace" title="Buen Retiro Palace">Buen Retiro Palace</a> shortly before the blaze. This fire lasted four days and completely destroyed the old Alcázar, whose last walls were finally demolished in 1738.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Filippo_Juvarra" title="Filippo Juvarra">Filippo Juvarra</a> oversaw work on the new palace. The Italian architect devised a lavish project of enormous proportions inspired by <a href="/wiki/Gian_Lorenzo_Bernini" title="Gian Lorenzo Bernini">Bernini's</a> plans for Versailles. This plan was not realized due to Juvarra's untimely death in March 1736.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:8</sup> His disciple, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Giambattista_Sacchetti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Giambattista Sacchetti (page does not exist)">Giambattista Sacchetti</a> also known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_Bautista_Sacchetti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Juan Bautista Sacchetti (page does not exist)">Juan Bautista Sacchetti</a> or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Giovanni_Battista_Sacchetti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Giovanni Battista Sacchetti (page does not exist)">Giovanni Battista Sacchetti</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uam_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-uam-7">[7]</a></sup> was chosen to continue the work of his mentor. He designed the structure around a large square courtyard and resolved the sightline problems by creating projecting wings.</p>
<p>In 1760, <a href="/wiki/Charles_III_of_Spain" title="Charles III of Spain">Charles III</a> called upon Sicilian <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Sabatini" title="Francesco Sabatini">Francesco Sabatini</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:9</sup> a <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">Neoclassical</a> architect to enlarge the building. The original idea was to frame the Plaza de la Armería with a series of galleries and arcades which would accommodate the various dependencies and the construction of two wings around the same square. Only the extension of the southeast tower known as <i>la de San Gil</i> was completed. Sabatini also planned to extend the north side with a large facade that echoed the style of the building and included three square courtyards in size somewhat smaller than the large central courtyard. Work on this expansion started quickly but was soon interrupted, leaving the foundations buried under a platform on which the royal stables were later built. These were demolished in the 20th century and replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Sabatini_Gardens" title="Sabatini Gardens">Sabatini Gardens</a>. Charles III first occupied the palace in 1764.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_VII_of_Spain" title="Ferdinand VII of Spain">Ferdinand VII</a>, who spent many years imprisoned in the <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Valen%C3%A7ay" title="Château de Valençay">Château de Valençay</a>, began the most thorough renovation of the palace in the 19th century. The aim of this redesign was to turn the old-fashioned Italian style building into a modern French-style palace. However, his grandson <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_XII_of_Spain" title="Alfonso XII of Spain">Alfonso XII</a> proposed to turn the palace into a <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian style</a> residence. The plans were designed by the architect <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jos%C3%A9_Segundo_de_Lema&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="José Segundo de Lema (page does not exist)">José Segundo de Lema</a> and consisted of remodeling several rooms, replacing marble floors with parquet and the adding of period furniture.</p>
<p>The restorations made during the twentieth century repaired damage suffered during the Civil Wars in Spain by repairing or reinstalling decoration and decorative trim, replacing damaged walls with faithful reproductions of the original.</p>

<p>The main facade of the Palace, the one facing the Plaza de la Armeria, consists of a two-story rusticated stone base, from which rise <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_columns" title="Ionic columns">Ionic columns</a> on <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_order" title="Tuscan order">Tuscan</a> pilasters framing the windows of the three main floors. The upper story is hidden behind a cornice which encircles the building and is capped with a large ballustrade. The ballustrade was adorned with a series of statues of saints and kings, but these were relocated elsewhere under the reign of Charles III to give the building a more classical appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sancho_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sancho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:18</sup></p>
<p>The restoration of facade in 1973, which includes Sabitini's balcony of four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doric_columns" title="Doric columns">Doric columns</a>, returned some of Sachetti's sculptures. These include statues of the Aztec ruler <a href="/wiki/Moctezuma_II" title="Moctezuma II">Moctezuma II</a> and the Inca emperor <a href="/wiki/Atahualpa" title="Atahualpa">Atahualpa</a>, works by Juan Pascual de Mena and Domingo Martínez, respectively. Representations of the Roman emperors <a href="/wiki/Honorius_(emperor)" title="Honorius (emperor)">Honorius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arcadius" title="Arcadius">Arcadius</a> by G.D. Olivieri, and <a href="/wiki/Trajan" title="Trajan">Trajan</a> by Felipe de Castro were placed in the Prince's courtyard. Flanking Sabatini's clock the Statues of Philip V, <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_VI_of_Spain" title="Ferdinand VI of Spain">Ferdinand VI</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbara_of_Braganza" title="Barbara of Braganza">Barbara of Braganza</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maria_Luisa_of_Savoy" title="Maria Luisa of Savoy">Maria Luisa of Savoy</a> interspersed with <i>The Rising Sun Following the Zodiac</i>. Above the clock is the royal coat of arms flanked by angels, and above, bells dating from 1637 and 1761.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sancho_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sancho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:18–20</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:14–15</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plaza_de_la_Armer.C3.ADa">Plaza de la Armería</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Plaza de la Armería">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Madrid_May_2014-34a.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5435" data-file-width="16039" height="85" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Madrid_May_2014-34a.jpg/250px-Madrid_May_2014-34a.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Madrid_May_2014-34a.jpg/375px-Madrid_May_2014-34a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Madrid_May_2014-34a.jpg/500px-Madrid_May_2014-34a.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Madrid_May_2014-34a.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
View from the Plaza de la Armeria</div>

<p>The square as it exists now was laid-out in 1892, according to a plan by the architect Enrique María Repullés. However, the history of this square dates back to 1553, the year in which Philip II ordered a building to house the royal stables.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Almudena_Cathedral" title="Almudena Cathedral">Almudena Cathedral</a> faces the palace across the plaza. Its exterior is neo-classical to match its surroundings while its interior is neo-gothic. Construction was funded by King Alfonso XII to house the remains of his wife <a href="/wiki/Mercedes_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Mercedes of Orléans">Mercedes of Orléans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gomadrid_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-gomadrid-8">[8]</a></sup> Construction of the church began in 1878 and concluded in 1992.</p>
<p>Narciso Pascual Colomer, the same architect who crafted the <a href="/wiki/Plaza_de_Oriente" title="Plaza de Oriente">Plaza de Oriente</a>, designed the layout of the plaza in 1879, but failed to materialize. The site now occupied by the Plaza de la Armería was used for many decades as anteplaza de armas. Sachetti tried to build a cathedral to finish the cornice of the <a href="/wiki/Manzanares_(river)" title="Manzanares (river)">Manzanares</a>, and Sabatini proposed to unite this building with the royal palace, to form a single block. Both projects were ignored by Charles III.</p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%81ngel_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_los_R%C3%ADos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ángel Fernández de los Ríos (page does not exist)">Ángel Fernández de los Ríos</a> in 1868 proposed the creation of a large wooded area that would travel all around the Plaza de Oriente, in order to give a better view of the Royal Palace. A decade later Segundo de Lema added a staircase to the original design of Fernández, which led to the idea of Francisco de Cubas to give more importance to the emerging church of Almudena.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plaza_de_Oriente">Plaza de Oriente</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Plaza de Oriente">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Plaza de Oriente is a rectangular park that connects the east facade of Palacio Real to the <a href="/wiki/Teatro_Real" title="Teatro Real">Teatro Real</a>. The eastern side of plaza is curved and bordered by several cafes in the adjoining buildings. Although the plaza was part of Sacchetti's plan for the palace, construction did not begin until 1808 when King <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Bonaparte" title="Joseph Bonaparte">Joseph Bonaparte</a>, who ordered the demolition of approximately 60 medieval structures, that included a church, monastery and royal library, located on the site. Joseph died before construction was completed, it was finished by Queen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isabella_II_of_Spain" title="Isabella II of Spain">Isabella II</a> who charged architect <a href="/wiki/Narciso_Pascual_Colomer" title="Narciso Pascual Colomer">Narciso Pascual Colomer</a> with creating the final design in 1844.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goplaza_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-goplaza-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tourist_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-tourist-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plaza_de_Oriente_(Madrid)_11.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2205" data-file-width="2915" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Plaza_de_Oriente_%28Madrid%29_11.jpg/250px-Plaza_de_Oriente_%28Madrid%29_11.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Plaza_de_Oriente_%28Madrid%29_11.jpg/375px-Plaza_de_Oriente_%28Madrid%29_11.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Plaza_de_Oriente_%28Madrid%29_11.jpg/500px-Plaza_de_Oriente_%28Madrid%29_11.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plaza_de_Oriente_(Madrid)_11.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Statues of the Gothic kings in the Plaza de Oriente.</div>

<p>Pathways divide the Plaza into three main plots: the Central Gardens, the Cabo Noval Gardens and the Lepanto Gardens. The Central Gardens are arranged in a grid around the central monument to <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">Philip IV</a>, following the <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> model garden. They consist of seven flowerbeds, each bordered with box hedges and holding small cypress, yew and <a href="/wiki/Magnolia" title="Magnolia">magnolias</a> and annual flowers. The north and south boundaries of the Central Gardens are marked by a row of statues, popularly known as the <i>Gothic kings</i>— sculptures representing five Visigoth rulers and fifteen rulers of the early Christian kingdoms in the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a>. They are carved from <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, and are part of a series dedicated to all <a href="/wiki/List_of_heads_of_state_of_Spain" title="List of heads of state of Spain">monarchs of Spain</a>. These were ordered for the decoration of the Palacio Real and were executed between 1750 and 1753. Engineers felt the statues were too heavy for the palace ballustrade, so they were left on ground level where their lack of fine detail is readily apparent. The remainder of the statues are in the Sabatini Gardens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alvarez_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-alvarez-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prieto_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-prieto-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Isabel II laid out the grounds so that <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Tacca" title="Pietro Tacca">Pietro Tacca</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Equestrian_statue" title="Equestrian statue">equestrian statue</a> of Philip IV was placed in the center, opposite the Prince's Gate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sancho_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sancho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:82</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Campo_del_Moro_Gardens">Campo del Moro Gardens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Campo del Moro Gardens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Espa%C3%B1a_-_Madrid_-_Campo_del_Moro_-_Entrada.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1996" data-file-width="2683" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Espa%C3%B1a_-_Madrid_-_Campo_del_Moro_-_Entrada.JPG/250px-Espa%C3%B1a_-_Madrid_-_Campo_del_Moro_-_Entrada.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Espa%C3%B1a_-_Madrid_-_Campo_del_Moro_-_Entrada.JPG/375px-Espa%C3%B1a_-_Madrid_-_Campo_del_Moro_-_Entrada.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Espa%C3%B1a_-_Madrid_-_Campo_del_Moro_-_Entrada.JPG/500px-Espa%C3%B1a_-_Madrid_-_Campo_del_Moro_-_Entrada.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Espa%C3%B1a_-_Madrid_-_Campo_del_Moro_-_Entrada.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
View of Paseo Principal, part of Campo del Moro Gardens.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>These gardens are so named because the Muslim leader Ali ben Yusuf allegedly camped here with his troops in 1109 during an attempted reconquest of Madrid. The first improvements to the area occurred under King Philip IV, who built fountains and planted various types of vegetation, but its overall look remained largely neglected. During the construction of the palace various landscaping projects were put forth based on the gardens of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Palace_of_La_Granja_de_San_Ildefonso" title="Royal Palace of La Granja de San Ildefonso">Royal Palace of La Granja de San Ildefonso</a>, but lack of funds hampered further improvement until the reign of Isabel II who began work in earnest. Following the taste of the times, the park was designed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanticist" title="Romanticist">Romanticist</a> style.</p>
<p>The Triton fountain from the Islet Garden of Aranjuez and the Fountain of the Shells from the Palace of the Infante Luis at Boadilla del Monte were aligned in the center of the right angled pathways by Isabel II, according to plans by <a href="/wiki/Narciso_Pascual_Colomer" title="Narciso Pascual Colomer">Narciso Pascual Colomer</a>. Under the regency of <a href="/wiki/Maria_Christina_of_Austria" title="Maria Christina of Austria">Maria Christina of Austria</a>, the park was reformed according to Ramon Oliva's <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">romanticism</a> plans. Between the Fountain of Tritons and the palace is The Large Cavern or Grotto (Camellia House), built by <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Villanueva" title="Juan de Villanueva">Juan de Villanueva</a> during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Bonaparte" title="Joseph Bonaparte">Joseph Bonaparte</a>. Sacchetti's 1757-1758 Little Cavern or Grotto (Potato Room) is in front of the Parade Ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sancho_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Sancho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:82–83</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:60–63</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sabatini_Gardens">Sabatini Gardens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sabatini Gardens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sabatini_Gardens" title="Sabatini Gardens">Sabatini Gardens</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Palacio_Real,_Madrid,_Espa%C3%B1a,_2014-12-27,_DD_09.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3688" data-file-width="5321" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Palacio_Real%2C_Madrid%2C_Espa%C3%B1a%2C_2014-12-27%2C_DD_09.JPG/250px-Palacio_Real%2C_Madrid%2C_Espa%C3%B1a%2C_2014-12-27%2C_DD_09.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Palacio_Real%2C_Madrid%2C_Espa%C3%B1a%2C_2014-12-27%2C_DD_09.JPG/375px-Palacio_Real%2C_Madrid%2C_Espa%C3%B1a%2C_2014-12-27%2C_DD_09.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Palacio_Real%2C_Madrid%2C_Espa%C3%B1a%2C_2014-12-27%2C_DD_09.JPG/500px-Palacio_Real%2C_Madrid%2C_Espa%C3%B1a%2C_2014-12-27%2C_DD_09.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Palacio_Real,_Madrid,_Espa%C3%B1a,_2014-12-27,_DD_09.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
View from the Sabatini Gardens.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Sabatini Gardens adjoin the north side of the Palacio real and extend to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Calle_de_Bail%C3%A9n_(Madrid)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Calle de Bailén (Madrid) (page does not exist)">calle de Bailén</a> and the cuesta de San Vicente. The garden follows the symmetrical French design and work began in 1933, under the Republican government. Although they were designed by Zaragozan architect <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_Garc%C3%ADa_Mercadal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fernando García Mercadal (page does not exist)">Fernando García Mercadal</a>, they were named for Francesco Sabatini who designed the royal stables that previously occupied this site. These gardens feature a large rectangular pond which is surrounded by four fountains and statues of Spanish kings which were originally intended to crown the Royal Palace. Geometrically sited between its rides, there are several fountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sidebits_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-sidebits-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Republican government under Franco constructed the gardens to return the area from control of the royal family to the people, the public was not allowed in the gardens until 1978 when they were opened by King Juan Carlos I.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sabatini_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-sabatini-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interior_of_the_palace">Interior of the palace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Interior of the palace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ground_Floor">Ground Floor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ground Floor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Grand_Staircase">Grand Staircase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Grand Staircase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Royal_Palace_of_Madrid_Frescoe.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5312" data-file-width="2988" height="391" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Royal_Palace_of_Madrid_Frescoe.jpg/220px-Royal_Palace_of_Madrid_Frescoe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Royal_Palace_of_Madrid_Frescoe.jpg/330px-Royal_Palace_of_Madrid_Frescoe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Royal_Palace_of_Madrid_Frescoe.jpg/440px-Royal_Palace_of_Madrid_Frescoe.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Royal_Palace_of_Madrid_Frescoe.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Corrado_Giaquinto" title="Corrado Giaquinto">Giaquinto</a>'s fresco above the staircase</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Built by Sabatini in 1789 when Charles IV wanted it moved to the opposite side of where Sabatini placed it in 1760, it is composed of a single piece of San Agustin marble. Two lions grace the landing, one by Felipe de Castro and another by Robert Michel. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frescoes" title="Frescoes">frescoes</a> on the ceiling is by <a href="/wiki/Corrado_Giaquinto" title="Corrado Giaquinto">Corrado Giaquinto</a> and depicts <i>Religion Protected by Spain</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sancho_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Sancho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:24</sup> On the ground floor is a statue of Charles III in Roman <a href="/wiki/Toga" title="Toga">toga</a>, with a similar statue on the first floor depicting Charles IV. The four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartouches" title="Cartouches">cartouches</a> at the corners depict the elements of water, earth, air and fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:30</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_Library">Royal Library</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Royal Library">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Royal Library was moved to the lower floor during the regency of <a href="/wiki/Maria_Christina_of_the_Two_Sicilies" title="Maria Christina of the Two Sicilies">Maria Christina</a>. The bookshelves date from the period of Charles III, Isabel II and Alfonso XII.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sancho_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Sancho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:77</sup></p>
<p>Highlights of the collection include the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_hours" title="Book of hours">Book of hours</a> of <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Castile" title="Isabella I of Castile">Isabella I of Castile</a>, a codex of the time of <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_XI_of_Castile" title="Alfonso XI of Castile">Alfonso XI of Castile</a>, a Bible of Doña <a href="/wiki/Mar%C3%ADa_de_Molina" title="María de Molina">María de Molina</a> and the <i>Fiestas reales</i>, dedicated to Ferdinand VI by <a href="/wiki/Farinelli" title="Farinelli">Farinelli</a>. Also important are the maps kept in the library, which analyze the extent of the kingdoms under the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a>. Also on display a selection of the best medals from the Royal Collection.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RealFarmacia.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/RealFarmacia.JPG/220px-RealFarmacia.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/RealFarmacia.JPG/330px-RealFarmacia.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/RealFarmacia.JPG/440px-RealFarmacia.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RealFarmacia.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Royal Pharmacy.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The bookcovers demonstrate evolution of binding styles by era. Examples in the holdings include <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">Rococo</a> in gold with iron lace, <a href="/wiki/Neoclassicism" title="Neoclassicism">Neoclassical</a> in polychrome and <a href="/wiki/Romanticism_in_Spanish_literature" title="Romanticism in Spanish literature">Romantic</a> with <a href="/wiki/Gothic_art" title="Gothic art">Gothic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Renaissance" title="Spanish Renaissance">Renaissance</a> motifs.</p>
<p>The Archives of the Royal Palace contains approximately twenty thousand articles ranging from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_disastrous_decade&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Spanish disastrous decade (page does not exist)">Disastrous decade</a> (1823-1833) to the proclamation of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Spanish_Republic" title="Second Spanish Republic">Second Spanish Republic</a> in 1931. In addition, it holds some scores of musicians of the Royal Chapel, privileges of various kings, the founding order of the <a href="/wiki/El_Escorial" title="El Escorial">Royal Monastery of San Lorenzo de El Escorial</a>, the testament of Philip II and correspondence of most of the kings of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">House of Bourbon</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_Pharmacy">Royal Pharmacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Royal Pharmacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the reign of <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II</a> the Royal Pharmacy became an appendage of the royal household and ordered the supply of medicines, a role that continues today.</p>
<p>The collection includes jars made by La Granja de San Ildefonso, 19th century, and <a href="/wiki/Talavera_de_la_Reina_pottery" title="Talavera de la Reina pottery">Talavera de la Reina pottery</a>, 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sancho_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Sancho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:78</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_Armory">Royal Armory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Royal Armory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ArmeriaPalacioRealMadrid.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="394" data-file-width="525" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/ArmeriaPalacioRealMadrid.JPG/250px-ArmeriaPalacioRealMadrid.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/ArmeriaPalacioRealMadrid.JPG/375px-ArmeriaPalacioRealMadrid.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/ArmeriaPalacioRealMadrid.JPG/500px-ArmeriaPalacioRealMadrid.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ArmeriaPalacioRealMadrid.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Royal Armory</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Royal_Armoury_of_Madrid" title="Royal Armoury of Madrid">Royal Armoury of Madrid</a></div>
<p>Along with the Imperial Armoury of <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, the armory is considered one of the best in the world and consists of pieces as early as the 13th century. The building, designed by J.S. de Lema and <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Mar%C3%ADa_Repull%C3%A9s" title="Enrique María Repullés">E. Repulles</a>, was opened in 1897.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:54</sup></p>
<p>The collection highlights the tournament pieces made for Charles V and Philip II by the leading armourers of <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a>. Among the most remarkable works are full armour and weapons that Emperor Charles V used in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_M%C3%BChlberg" title="Battle of Mühlberg">Battle of Mühlberg</a>, and which was portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a> in his <a href="/wiki/Equestrian_Portrait_of_Charles_V" title="Equestrian Portrait of Charles V">famous equestrian portrait</a> housed at the <a href="/wiki/Museo_del_Prado" title="Museo del Prado">Museo del Prado</a>. Unfortunately, parts of the collection were lost during the <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">Peninsular War</a> and during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a>.</p>
<p>Still, the armoury retains some of the most important pieces of this art in Europe and the world, including a shield and <a href="/wiki/Burgonet" title="Burgonet">burgonet</a> by Francesco and <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Negroli" title="Filippo Negroli">Filippo Negroli</a>, one of the most famous designers in the armourers' guild.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:56–57</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_floor">First floor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: First floor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="King_Charles_III.27s_Apartments">King Charles III's Apartments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: King Charles III's Apartments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Medici_lions" title="Medici lions">Medici lions</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Halberd" title="Halberd">Halberdier's Room</a>, or Guard Room, was designed by Sabatini, and includes the fresco by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Tiepolo" title="Giovanni Battista Tiepolo">Tiepolo</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Venus_(mythology)" title="Venus (mythology)">Venus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_(mythology)" title="Vulcan (mythology)">Vulcan</a></i>. Two paintings by <a href="/wiki/Luca_Giordano" title="Luca Giordano">Luca Giordano</a> depict scenes from the life of <a href="/wiki/Solomon" title="Solomon">Solomon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sancho_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Sancho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:26–28</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:32–33</sup></p>
<p>The Hall of Columns has a ceiling fresco by Giaquinto, representing <i>The Sun before Which All the Forces of Nature Awaken and Rejoice</i>, an <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegory</a> of the king as <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a>. A 1878 bronze statue of <i>Charles V Vanquishing Fury</i> is by <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Barbedienne" title="Ferdinand Barbedienne">Ferdinand Barbedienne</a>. The bronze chandeliers were made in Paris in 1846, and installed by Isbella II for her balls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sancho_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Sancho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:28–31</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:34–35</sup></p>
<p>The Throne Room dates from Charles III in 1772, and features Tiepolo's ceiling fresco, <i>The <a href="/wiki/Apotheosis" title="Apotheosis">Apotheosis</a> of the Spanish Monarchy</i>. Bronze sculptures include the Four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_Virtues" title="Cardinal Virtues">Cardinal Virtues</a>, four of the Seven Planets, <a href="/wiki/Satyr" title="Satyr">Satyr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germanicus" title="Germanicus">Germanicus</a>, and four <a href="/wiki/Medici_lions" title="Medici lions">Medici lions</a> flanking the dual throne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sancho_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-Sancho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:34–40</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:50–53</sup></p>
<p>Charles III's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anteroom" title="Anteroom">Anteroom</a> (Saleta) contains a 1774 ceiling fresco <i>Apotheosis of <a href="/wiki/Trajan" title="Trajan">Trajan</a></i> by A.R. Mengs. The Antechamber of Charles III (The Conversation Room) also contains a ceiling fresco by Mengs, <i>The Apotheosis of <a href="/wiki/Hercules" title="Hercules">Hercules</a></i>. This room has four royal family portraits by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goya" title="Goya">Goya</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sancho_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-Sancho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:40–47</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-14"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:36–37</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Queen.27s_apartments_and_banqueting_hall">The Queen's apartments and banqueting hall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: The Queen's apartments and banqueting hall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Formerly the queen's apartments under Charles III, the three rooms were converted into a banquet hall by Alfonso XII in 1879, and completed in 1885. The three ceiling frescoes remained though, <i>Dawn in Her Chariot</i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raphael_Mengs" title="Raphael Mengs">Raphael Mengs</a>, <i>Christopher Columbus Offering the New World to the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Alejandro_Gonz%C3%A1lez_Vel%C3%A1zquez" title="Alejandro González Velázquez">Alejandro González Velázquez</a>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boabdil" title="Boabdil">Boabdil</a> Giving the Keys to Granada to the Catholic Monarchs</i> by <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Bayeu_y_Sub%C3%ADas" title="Francisco Bayeu y Subías">Francisco Bayeu y Subías</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sancho_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-Sancho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:56–59</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-15"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:44–45</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Apartments_of_Infante_Luis">Apartments of Infante Luis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Apartments of Infante Luis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>These rooms were formerly occupied by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infante_Luis,_Count_of_Chinch%C3%B3n" title="Infante Luis, Count of Chinchón">Infante Luis, Count of Chinchón</a> before his exile. The Stradivarius Room now contains a <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">viola</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">viloncello</a>, and two <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violins</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stradivari" title="Stradivari">Stradivari</a>. The ceiling fresco by A. G. Velazquez, depicts <i>Gentleness accompanied by the Four Cardinal Virtues</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sancho_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-Sancho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:62–63</sup></p>
<p>The Chamber of the Infante Luis, Musical Instruments Room, has a ceiling fresco by Francisco Bayeu depicting <i>Providence Presiding over the Virtues and Faculties of Man</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sancho_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-Sancho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:62–64</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_Chapel">Royal Chapel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Royal Chapel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Designed in 1748 by Sacchetti and <a href="/wiki/Ventura_Rodr%C3%ADguez" title="Ventura Rodríguez">Ventura Rodríguez</a>, the chapel features ceiling frescoes by Giaquinto, including <i>The Trinity</i>, <i>Allegory of Religion</i>, <i>Glory and the Holy Trinity Crowning the Virgin</i>. Above the High Altar is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ramon_Bayeu" title="Ramon Bayeu">Ramon Bayeu</a>'s <i>St. Michael</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Reliquary" title="Reliquary">reliquary</a> altar has <a href="/wiki/Ercole_Ferrata" title="Ercole Ferrata">Ercole Ferrata</a>'s 1659 silver relief <i><a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_I" title="Pope Leo I">Pope Leo I</a> Stopping <a href="/wiki/Attila" title="Attila">Attila</a> at the Gates of Rome</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sancho_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-Sancho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:67–70</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-16"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:46–47</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Crown_Room">The Crown Room</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Palace_of_Madrid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: The Crown Room">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spanish_Royal_Crown_1crop.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1896" data-file-width="2258" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Spanish_Royal_Crown_1crop.jpg/220px-Spanish_Royal_Crown_1crop.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Spanish_Royal_Crown_1crop.jpg/330px-Spanish_Royal_Crown_1crop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Spanish_Royal_Crown_1crop.jpg/440px-Spanish_Royal_Crown_1crop.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Spanish Royal Crown and Scepter</div>

<p>Formerly the apartment of Alfonso XIII's mother, <a href="/wiki/Maria_Christina_of_Austria" title="Maria Christina of Austria">Maria Christina of Austria</a>, the room contains Charles III's throne, scepter and crown. Tapestries from <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Amigoni" title="Jacopo Amigoni">Jacopo Amigoni</a>'s <i>Four Seasons</i> adorn the walls. Also of note are the abdication speech of <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_I_of_Spain" title="Juan Carlos I of Spain">Juan Carlos I</a> and the proclamation speech of <a href="/wiki/Felipe_VI_of_Spain" title="Felipe VI of Spain">Felipe VI</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Viso_2-17"><a href="#cite_note-Viso-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:48–49</sup></p>
