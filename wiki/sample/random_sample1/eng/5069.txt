<p><b>Mahdia</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">المهدية</span>‎‎ <i><span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Mahdia.wav" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5a/Mahdia.wav" title="Mahdia.wav">al-Mahdīya</a></span></span></i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisian</a> coastal city with 79,545 inhabitants, south of <a href="/wiki/Monastir,_Tunisia" title="Monastir, Tunisia">Monastir</a> and southeast of <a href="/wiki/Sousse" title="Sousse">Sousse</a>.</p>
<p>Mahdia is a provincial centre north of <a href="/wiki/Sfax" title="Sfax">Sfax</a>. It is important for the associated fish-processing industry, as well as weaving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lexic_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lexic-1">[1]</a></sup> It is the capital of <a href="/wiki/Mahdia_Governorate" title="Mahdia Governorate">Mahdia Governorate</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mahdia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mahdia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="1200" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Mahdia.jpg/220px-Mahdia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Mahdia.jpg/330px-Mahdia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Mahdia.jpg/440px-Mahdia.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mahdia.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Marine cemetery in Mahdia</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The old part of Mahdia corresponds to the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> city called <b>Aphrodisium</b> and, later, called <b>Africa</b> (a name perhaps derived from the older name),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> or <b>Cape Africa</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tissot_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tissot-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>'s list of <a href="/wiki/Titular_see" title="Titular see">titular sees</a> includes a no longer residential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishopric" title="Bishopric">bishopric</a> called Africa<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and, since there is no record of an episcopal see in <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> times called by either of these names (nor by that of Alipota, another Roman town that Charles Tissot suggested tentatively might be represented by present-day Mehdia),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tissot_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tissot-7">[7]</a></sup> it is supposed that the <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_see" title="Episcopal see">episcopal see</a> of Africa was established when the city was held by the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sicily" title="Kingdom of Sicily">Kingdom of Sicily</a>, as a part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Africa" title="Kingdom of Africa">Kingdom of Africa</a> (1147–1160) and when <a href="/wiki/Pope_Eugene_III" title="Pope Eugene III">Pope Eugene III</a> consecrated a bishop for it in 1148. An inventory of movable property of the church of Africa (<i>inventarium thesauri Africani</i>) exists in an archive of the <a href="/wiki/Cappella_Palatina" title="Cappella Palatina">Cappella Palatina</a> of <a href="/wiki/Palermo" title="Palermo">Palermo</a> in Sicily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Robert Favreau identified Mahdia instead with ancient <i>Ruspae</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Ruspe" title="Ruspe">Ruspe</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (October 2014)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> which is more commonly taken to have been at Henchir Sbia (or just Sbia), north of Mahdia, or at the ruins known as Ksour Siad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The most illustrious bishop of this see was <a href="/wiki/Fulgentius_of_Ruspe" title="Fulgentius of Ruspe">Fulgentius of Ruspe</a>. The Catholic Church's list of titular sees, which identifies the see of Africa as Mahdia, identifies Ruspe/Ruspae as Henchir Sbia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mahdia_shipwreck" title="Mahdia shipwreck">Mahdia shipwreck</a> – a sunken ship found off Mahdia's shore, containing Greek art treasures – is dated to about 80 BC, the early part of Roman rule in this region.</p>
<p>Muslim Mahdia was founded by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatimids" title="Fatimids">Fatimids</a> under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caliph" title="Caliph">Caliph</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abdallah_al-Mahdi" title="Abdallah al-Mahdi">Abdallah al-Mahdi</a> in 921 and made the capital of <a href="/wiki/Ifriqiya" title="Ifriqiya">Ifriqiya</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TN_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-TN-13">[13]</a></sup> It was chosen as the capital because of its proximity to the sea, and the promontory on which an important military settlement had been since the time of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenicians" title="Phoenicians">Phoenicians</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lexic_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lexic-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1087 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahdia_campaign" title="Mahdia campaign">town was attacked</a> by raiding ships from <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a> who burned the Muslim fleet in the harbor. The attack played a critical role in Christians' seizure of control of the Western Mediterranean, which allowed the <a href="/wiki/First_Crusade" title="First Crusade">First Crusade</a> to be supplied by sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zirid" title="Zirid">Zirid</a> dynasty had its residence here in the 11th century, but was brought to an end by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normans_of_Sicily" title="Normans of Sicily">Norman conquest</a> of the city in 1148. In 1160 the city came under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almohad" title="Almohad">Almohad</a> rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Timeline_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Timeline-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The role of the capital was taken over by <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a> in the 12th century during the Almohad era, which it remained during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hafsid_Dynasty" title="Hafsid Dynasty">Hafsid Dynasty</a>. Some buildings still exist from the 10th and 11th centuries, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_mosque_of_Mahdia" title="Great mosque of Mahdia">Great Mosque</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casbah" title="Casbah">Casbah</a>, which have helped make the city an important tourist attraction. Later the city was subject to many raids. In 1390 it was the target of the <a href="/wiki/Barbary_Crusade" title="Barbary Crusade">Barbary Crusade</a>, when a French army laid siege to the city but failed to take it.</p>
<p>The city was <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_Mahdiye_(1550)" title="Capture of Mahdiye (1550)">captured</a> by the Spaniards in 1550. A Spanish garrison remained there until 1553. <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a> then offered the charge of the town to the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Hospitaller" title="Knights Hospitaller">Order of Saint John</a> who <a href="/wiki/History_of_Malta_under_the_Order_of_Saint_John" title="History of Malta under the Order of Saint John">ruled Malta</a> but they refused it deeming it too expensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The emperor ordered the Viceroy of Sicily, <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Vega" title="Juan de Vega">Juan de Vega</a>, to dismantle Mahdia despite it being a strategically important stronghold. The demolition tasks were carried out by Hernando de Acuña. Shortly after Mahdia was reoccupied by the Ottomans, but only to live by fishing and oil-works, and the town lost its logistic and commercial importance. It remained under Turkish rule until the <a href="/wiki/French_conquest_of_Tunisia" title="French conquest of Tunisia">19th century</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi_Occupation_of_Tunisia" title="Nazi Occupation of Tunisia">Nazi Occupation of Tunisia</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Mahdia was the site where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khaled_Abdelwahhab" title="Khaled Abdelwahhab">Khaled Abdelwahhab</a> hid approximately two dozen persecuted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tunisian_Jews" title="Tunisian Jews">Jews</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mahdia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gare_Mahdia" title="Gare Mahdia">Gare Mahdia</a> forms the southern terminus of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metre-gauge" title="Metre-gauge">metre-gauge</a> <a href="/wiki/Sahel_Metro" title="Sahel Metro">Sahel Metro</a> railway line, which runs from <a href="/wiki/Sousse" title="Sousse">Sousse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monastir,_Tunisia" title="Monastir, Tunisia">Monastir</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SNCFT_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-SNCFT-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
