<p><b>Madhya Pradesh</b> (<b>MP</b>) (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="/ʃ/ 'sh' in 'shy'">ʃ</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Madhyapradesh.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e5/Madhyapradesh.ogg" title="Madhyapradesh.ogg"></a></span> <small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:Madhyapradesh.ogg" title="File:Madhyapradesh.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span>, meaning Central Province) is a state in central <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital</a> is <a href="/wiki/Bhopal" title="Bhopal">Bhopal</a> and the largest city is <a href="/wiki/Indore" title="Indore">Indore</a>. Nicknamed the "heart of India" due to its geographical location in India, Madhya Pradesh is the second-largest state in the country by area. With over 75 million inhabitants, it is the fifth-largest state in India by population. It borders the states of <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a> to the northeast, <a href="/wiki/Chhattisgarh" title="Chhattisgarh">Chhattisgarh</a> to the southeast, <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a> to the south, <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a> to the west, and <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a> to the northwest. Its total area is 308,245 km². Before 2000, When <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chattisgarh" title="Chattisgarh">Chattisgarh</a> was a part of Madhya Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh was the largest state in India and the distance between the two furthest points inside the state, <a href="/wiki/Singoli" title="Singoli">Singoli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Konta" title="Konta">Konta</a>, was 1500 km.</p>
<p>The area covered by the present-day Madhya Pradesh includes the area of the ancient <a href="/wiki/Avanti_(India)" title="Avanti (India)">Avanti</a> <a href="/wiki/Mahajanapada" title="Mahajanapada">mahajanapada</a>, whose capital <a href="/wiki/Ujjain" title="Ujjain">Ujjain</a> (also known as Avantika) arose as a major city during the second wave of Indian urbanisation in the sixth century BCE. Subsequently, the region was ruled by the major dynasties of India. By the early 18th century, the region was divided into several small kingdoms which were captured by the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British</a> and incorporated into <a href="/wiki/Central_Provinces_and_Berar" title="Central Provinces and Berar">Central Provinces and Berar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Central_India_Agency" title="Central India Agency">Central India Agency</a>. After India's independence, Madhya Pradesh state was created with <a href="/wiki/Nagpur" title="Nagpur">Nagpur</a> as its capital: this state included the southern parts of the present-day Madhya Pradesh and northeastern portion of today's Maharashtra. In 1956, this state was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/States_Reorganisation_Act" title="States Reorganisation Act">reorganised</a> and its parts were combined with the states of <a href="/wiki/Madhya_Bharat" title="Madhya Bharat">Madhya Bharat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vindhya_Pradesh" title="Vindhya Pradesh">Vindhya Pradesh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bhopal_State_(1949%E2%80%9356)" title="Bhopal State (1949–56)">Bhopal</a> to form the new Madhya Pradesh state with Bhopal as its capital; the Marathi-speaking <a href="/wiki/Vidarbha" title="Vidarbha">Vidarbha</a> region was removed and merged with the then <a href="/wiki/Bombay_State" title="Bombay State">Bombay State</a>. This state was the largest in India by area until 2000, when its southeastern <a href="/wiki/Chhattisgarh" title="Chhattisgarh">Chhattisgarh</a> region was made a separate state.</p>
<p>In recent years, the state's GDP growth has been above the national average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rich in mineral resources, MP has the largest reserves of diamond and copper in India. More than 30% of its area is under forest cover. Its tourism industry has seen considerable growth, with the state topping the National Tourism Awards in 2010–11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>The city of <a href="/wiki/Ujjain" title="Ujjain">Ujjain</a> arose as a major centre in the region, during the second wave of Indian urbanisation in the sixth century BCE. It served as the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Avanti_(India)" title="Avanti (India)">Avanti kingdom</a>. Other kingdoms mentioned in ancient epics — <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malava_Kingdom" title="Malava Kingdom">Malava</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karusha_Kingdom" title="Karusha Kingdom">Karusha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dasarna_Kingdom" title="Dasarna Kingdom">Dasarna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nishada_Kingdom" title="Nishada Kingdom">Nishada</a> — have also been identified with parts of Madhya Pradesh.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chandragupta_Maurya" title="Chandragupta Maurya">Chandragupta Maurya</a> united northern India around 320 BCE, establishing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mauryan_Empire" title="Mauryan Empire">Mauryan Empire</a>, which included all of modern-day Madhya Pradesh. <a href="/wiki/Ashoka" title="Ashoka">Ashoka</a> the greatest of Mauryan rulers brought the region under firmer control. After the decline of the Maurya empire, the region was contested among the <a href="/wiki/Saka" title="Saka">Sakas</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kushana" title="Kushana">Kushanas</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Satavahana_dynasty" title="Satavahana dynasty">Satavahanas</a>, and several local dynasties during the 1st to 3rd centuries CE. Heliodorus, the Greek Ambassador to the court of the Shunga king Bhagabhadra erected the <a href="/wiki/Heliodorus_pillar" title="Heliodorus pillar">Heliodorus pillar</a> near Vidisha.</p>
<p>Ujjain emerged as the predominant commercial centre of western India from the first century BCE, located on the trade routes between the <a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">Ganges</a> plain and India's <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a> ports. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satavahana" title="Satavahana">Satavahana</a> dynasty of the northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deccan" title="Deccan">Deccan</a> and the Saka dynasty of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Satraps" title="Western Satraps">Western Satraps</a> fought for the control of Madhya Pradesh during the 1st to 3rd centuries CE.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Satavahana_dynasty" title="Satavahana dynasty">Satavahana</a> king <a href="/wiki/Gautamiputra_Satakarni" title="Gautamiputra Satakarni">Gautamiputra Satakarni</a> inflicted a crushing defeat upon the <a href="/wiki/Saka" title="Saka">Saka</a> rulers and conquered parts of Malwa and Gujarat in the 2nd century CE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Subsequently, the region came under the control of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gupta_empire" title="Gupta empire">Gupta empire</a> in the 4th and 5th centuries, and their southern neighbours, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vakataka" title="Vakataka">Vakataka</a>'s. The rock-cut temples at <a href="/wiki/Bagh_Caves" title="Bagh Caves">Bagh Caves</a> in the Kukshi tehsil of the Dhar district attest to the presence of the Gupta dynasty in the region, supported by the testimony of a Badwani inscription dated to the year of 487 CE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The attacks of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hephthalite" title="Hephthalite">Hephthalites</a> or White Huns brought about the collapse of the Gupta empire, which broke up into smaller states. The king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yasodharman" title="Yasodharman">Yasodharman</a> of <a href="/wiki/Malwa" title="Malwa">Malwa</a> defeated the Huns in 528, ending their expansion. Later, <a href="/wiki/Harsha" title="Harsha">Harsha</a> (c. 590—647) ruled the northern parts of the <a href="/wiki/States_and_union_territories_of_India" title="States and union territories of India">state</a>. Malwa was ruled by the south Indian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rashtrakuta_Dynasty" title="Rashtrakuta Dynasty">Rashtrakuta Dynasty</a> from the late 8th century to the 10th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> When the south Indian Emperor <a href="/wiki/Govinda_III" title="Govinda III">Govinda III</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Rashtrakuta_dynasty" title="Rashtrakuta dynasty">Rashtrakuta dynasty</a> annexed Malwa, he set up the family of one of his subordinates there, who took the name of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramara" title="Paramara">Paramara</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Medieval period saw the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Gurjar" title="Gurjar">Gurjar</a> clans, including the <a href="/wiki/Paramara_dynasty" title="Paramara dynasty">Paramaras</a> of Malwa and the <a href="/wiki/Chandela" title="Chandela">Chandelas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bundelkhand" title="Bundelkhand">Bundelkhand</a>. The Chandellas built the majestic Hindu-Jain temples at <a href="/wiki/Khajuraho_Group_of_Monuments" title="Khajuraho Group of Monuments">Khajuraho</a>, which represent the culmination of Hindu temple architecture in Central India. The Gurjara-Pratihara dynasty also held sway in northern and western Madhya Pradesh at this time. It also left some monuments of architectural value in Gwalior. Southern parts of Madhya Pradesh like Malwa were several times invaded by the south Indian <a href="/wiki/Western_Chalukya_Empire" title="Western Chalukya Empire">Western Chalukya Empire</a> which imposed its rule on the Paramara kingdom of Malwa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The Paramara king <a href="/wiki/Bhoja" title="Bhoja">Bhoja</a> (c. 1010–1060) was a renowned <a href="/wiki/Polymath" title="Polymath">polymath</a>. The small <a href="/wiki/Gondi_people" title="Gondi people">Gond</a> kingdoms emerged in the <a href="/wiki/Gondwana_(India)" title="Gondwana (India)">Gondwana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mahakoshal" title="Mahakoshal">Mahakoshal</a> regions of the state. Northern Madhya Pradesh was conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Turkic_peoples" title="Turkic peoples">Turkic</a> <a href="/wiki/Delhi_Sultanate" title="Delhi Sultanate">Delhi Sultanate</a> in the 13th century. After the collapse of the Delhi Sultanate at the end of the 14th century, independent regional kingdoms re-emerged, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tomara" title="Tomara">Tomara</a> Gurjar kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Gwalior" title="Gwalior">Gwalior</a> and the Muslim Sultanate of <a href="/wiki/Malwa_Sultanate" title="Malwa Sultanate">Malwa</a>, with its capital at <a href="/wiki/Mandu,_Madhya_Pradesh" title="Mandu, Madhya Pradesh">Mandu</a>.</p>
<p>The Malwa Sultanate was conquered by the Sultanate of <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a> in 1531. In the 1540s, most parts of the state fell to <a href="/wiki/Sher_Shah_Suri" title="Sher Shah Suri">Sher Shah Suri</a>, and subsequently to the Hindu king <a href="/wiki/Hemu" title="Hemu">Hemu</a>. Hemu, who had earlier served as the General of the Suri dynasty, operated from the <a href="/wiki/Gwalior_Fort" title="Gwalior Fort">Gwalior Fort</a> during 1553–56 and became the rural of Delhi as a Vikramaditya king winning 22 battles continuously from Bengal to Gujrat and defeating Akbar's forces in <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tughlaqabad" title="Battle of Tughlaqabad">Battle of Delhi</a> 1556 on 7th Oct. 1556. However, he chose Delhi as his capital after his formal Coronation and left Gwalior. After Hemu's defeat in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_Panipat" title="Second Battle of Panipat">Second Battle of Panipat</a> in 1556 to <a href="/wiki/Akbar" title="Akbar">Akbar</a>, most of Madhya Pradesh came under the <a href="/wiki/Mughal_Empire" title="Mughal Empire">Mughal</a> rule. Gondwana and Mahakoshal remained under the control of Gond kings, who acknowledged Mughal supremacy but enjoyed virtual autonomy.</p>
<p>The Mughal control weakened considerably after the death of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Aurangzeb" title="Aurangzeb">Aurangzeb</a> in 1707. Between 1720 and 1760, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marathas" title="Marathas">Marathas</a> took control of most of Madhya Pradesh, resulting in the establishment of semi-autonomous states under the nominal control of the <a href="/wiki/Peshwa" title="Peshwa">Peshwa</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pune" title="Pune">Pune</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Holkar" title="Holkar">Holkars</a> of <a href="/wiki/Indore" title="Indore">Indore</a> ruled much of Malwa, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puars" title="Puars">Puars</a> ruled <a href="/wiki/Dewas_State" title="Dewas State">Dewas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dhar_State" title="Dhar State">Dhar</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bhonsle" title="Bhonsle">Bhonsles</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nagpur_kingdom" title="Nagpur kingdom">Nagpur</a> dominated <a href="/wiki/Mahakoshal" title="Mahakoshal">Mahakoshal</a>-<a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> area, while the <a href="/wiki/Scindia" title="Scindia">Scindias</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gwalior_State" title="Gwalior State">Gwalior</a> controlled the northern parts of the state. The most notable Maratha rulers of the region were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahadji_Shinde" title="Mahadji Shinde">Mahadji Shinde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ahilyabai_Holkar" title="Ahilyabai Holkar">Ahilyabai Holkar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yashwantrao_Holkar" title="Yashwantrao Holkar">Yashwantrao Holkar</a>. Besides these, there were several other <a href="/wiki/Category:Princely_states_of_Madhya_Pradesh" title="Category:Princely states of Madhya Pradesh">small states</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Bhopal_State" title="Bhopal State">Bhopal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orchha_State" title="Orchha State">Orchha</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rewa_(princely_state)" title="Rewa (princely state)">Rewa</a>. The Bhopal state, which paid tribute to both the Marathas and the <a href="/wiki/Nizam_of_Hyderabad" title="Nizam of Hyderabad">Nizam of Hyderabad</a>, was founded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dost_Mohammad_Khan,_Nawab_of_Bhopal" title="Dost Mohammad Khan, Nawab of Bhopal">Dost Mohammed Khan</a>, a former General in the Mughal army.</p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Third_Anglo-Maratha_War" title="Third Anglo-Maratha War">Third Anglo-Maratha War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British</a> took control of the entire region. All the sovereign states in the region became <a href="/wiki/Princely_state" title="Princely state">princely states</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_India" title="British India">British India</a>, governed by the <a href="/wiki/Central_India_Agency" title="Central India Agency">Central India Agency</a>. The Mahakoshal region became a British province: the <a href="/wiki/Saugor_and_Nerbudda_Territories" title="Saugor and Nerbudda Territories">Saugor and Nerbudda Territories</a>. In 1861, the British merged the <a href="/wiki/Nagpur_Province" title="Nagpur Province">Nagpur Province</a> with the Saugor and Nerbudda Territories to form the <a href="/wiki/Central_Provinces" title="Central Provinces">Central Provinces</a>.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Rebellion_of_1857" title="Indian Rebellion of 1857">1857 uprising</a>, rebellions happened in the northern parts of the state, led by leaders like <a href="/wiki/Tatya_Tope" title="Tatya Tope">Tatya Tope</a>. However, these were crushed by the British and the princes loyal to them. The state witnessed a number of anti-British activities and protests during the <a href="/wiki/Indian_independence_movement" title="Indian independence movement">Indian independence movement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DwarkaPrasad1956_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-DwarkaPrasad1956-15">[15]</a></sup> Several notable leaders such as <a href="/wiki/Chandra_Shekhar_Azad" title="Chandra Shekhar Azad">Chandra Shekhar Azad</a>, <a href="/wiki/B._R._Ambedkar" title="B. R. Ambedkar">B. R. Ambedkar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shankar_Dayal_Sharma" title="Shankar Dayal Sharma">Shankar Dayal Sharma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atal_Bihari_Vajpayee" title="Atal Bihari Vajpayee">Atal Bihari Vajpayee</a> were born in what is now Madhya Pradesh.</p>
<p>After the independence of India, Madhya Pradesh was created in 1950 from the former British <a href="/wiki/Central_Provinces_and_Berar" title="Central Provinces and Berar">Central Provinces and Berar</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princely_states" title="Princely states">princely states</a> of <a href="/wiki/Makrai" title="Makrai">Makrai</a> and Chhattisgarh, with <a href="/wiki/Nagpur" title="Nagpur">Nagpur</a> as the capital of the state. The new states of <a href="/wiki/Madhya_Bharat" title="Madhya Bharat">Madhya Bharat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vindhya_Pradesh" title="Vindhya Pradesh">Vindhya Pradesh</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bhopal_State_(1949%E2%80%9356)" title="Bhopal State (1949–56)">Bhopal</a> were formed out of the Central India Agency. In 1956, the states of Madhya Bharat, Vindhya Pradesh, and Bhopal were merged into Madhya Pradesh, and the <a href="/wiki/Marathi_language" title="Marathi language">Marathi</a>-speaking southern region <a href="/wiki/Vidarbha" title="Vidarbha">Vidarbha</a>, which included Nagpur, was ceded to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombay_state" title="Bombay state">Bombay state</a>. Bhopal became the new capital of the state. In November 2000, as part of the Madhya Pradesh Reorganization Act, the southeastern portion of the state split off to form the new state of <a href="/wiki/Chhattisgarh" title="Chhattisgarh">Chhattisgarh</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madhya_Pradesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_in_India">Location in India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madhya_Pradesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Location in India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Madhya Pradesh literally means "Central Province", and is located in the geographic heart of India, between latitude 21.2°N-26.87°N and longitude 74°02'-82°49' E. The state straddles the <a href="/wiki/Narmada_River" title="Narmada River">Narmada River</a>, which runs east and west between the <a href="/wiki/Vindhya_Range" title="Vindhya Range">Vindhya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Satpura_Range" title="Satpura Range">Satpura</a> ranges; these ranges and the Narmada are the traditional boundary between the north and south of India. The highest point in Madhya Pradesh is <a href="/wiki/Dhupgarh" title="Dhupgarh">Dhupgarh</a>, with an elevation of 1,350 m (4,429 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The state is bordered on the west by <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a>, on the northwest by <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a>, on the northeast by <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a>, on the east by Chhattisgarh, and on the south by <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madhya_Pradesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Madhya Pradesh has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">subtropical</a> <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>. Like most of north India, it has a hot dry summer (April–June), followed by <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> rains (July–September) and a cool and relatively dry winter. The average rainfall is about 1,371 mm (54.0 in). The southeastern districts have the heaviest rainfall, some places receiving as much as 2,150 mm (84.6 in), while the western and northwestern districts receive 1,000 mm (39.4 in) or less.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madhya_Pradesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the 2011 figures, the recorded forest area of the state is 94,689 km<sup>2</sup> (36,560 sq mi) constituting 30.72% of the geographical area of the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FSI_2011_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FSI_2011-17">[17]</a></sup> It constitutes 12.30% of the forest area of India. Legally this area has been classified into "Reserved Forest" (65.3%), "Protected Forest" (32.84%) and "Unclassified Forest" (0.18%). Per capita forest area is 2,400 m<sup>2</sup> (0.59 acres) as against the national average of 700 m<sup>2</sup> (0.17 acres). The forest cover is less dense in the northern and western parts of the state, which contain the major urban centres. Variability in climatic and <a href="/wiki/Edaphic" title="Edaphic">edaphic</a> conditions brings about significant difference in the forest types of the state.</p>
<p>The major types of soils found in the state are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Black soil, most predominantly in <a href="/wiki/Malwa" title="Malwa">Malwa</a> region, <a href="/wiki/Mahakoshal" title="Mahakoshal">Mahakoshal</a> and in southern <a href="/wiki/Bundelkhand" title="Bundelkhand">Bundelkhand</a></li>
<li>Red and yellow soil, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baghelkhand" title="Baghelkhand">Baghelkhand</a> region</li>
<li>Alluvial soil, in Northern Madhya Pradesh</li>
<li>Laterite soil, in highland areas</li>
<li>Mixed soil, in parts of <a href="/wiki/Gwalior" title="Gwalior">Gwalior</a> and Chambal division</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_and_fauna">Flora and fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madhya_Pradesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Flora and fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flora_and_fauna_of_Madhya_Pradesh" title="Flora and fauna of Madhya Pradesh">Flora and fauna of Madhya Pradesh</a></div>
<p>Madhya Pradesh is home to 09 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_parks_of_India" title="National parks of India">National Parks</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Bandhavgarh_National_Park" title="Bandhavgarh National Park">Bandhavgarh National Park</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kanha_National_Park" title="Kanha National Park">Kanha National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Satpura_National_Park" title="Satpura National Park">Satpura National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sanjay_National_Park" title="Sanjay National Park">Sanjay National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madhav_National_Park" title="Madhav National Park">Madhav National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Van_Vihar_National_Park" title="Van Vihar National Park">Van Vihar National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mandla_Plant_Fossils_National_Park" title="Mandla Plant Fossils National Park">Mandla Plant Fossils National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Panna_National_Park" title="Panna National Park">Panna National Park</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pench_National_Park" title="Pench National Park">Pench National Park</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> There are also a number of natural preserves, including <a href="/wiki/Amarkantak" title="Amarkantak">Amarkantak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bagh_Caves" title="Bagh Caves">Bagh Caves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Balaghat" <p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kanha_National_Park" title="Kanha National Park">Kanha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bandhavgarh_National_Park" title="Bandhavgarh National Park">Bandhavgarh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pench_National_Park" title="Pench National Park">Pench</a>, <a href="/wiki/Panna_National_Park" title="Panna National Park">Panna</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Satpura_National_Park" title="Satpura National Park">Satpura National Park</a> are managed as <a href="/wiki/Project_Tiger" title="Project Tiger">Project Tiger</a> areas. Sardarpur sanctuary in <a href="/wiki/Dhar" title="Dhar">Dhar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sailana" title="Sailana">Sailana</a> are managed for conservation of kharmor or <a href="/wiki/Lesser_florican" title="Lesser florican">lesser florican</a>. Ghatigaon sanctuary is managed for <a href="/wiki/Great_Indian_bustard" title="Great Indian bustard">great Indian bustard</a> or Son Chiriya. The <a href="/wiki/National_Chambal_Sanctuary" title="National Chambal Sanctuary">National Chambal Sanctuary</a> is managed for conservation of <a href="/wiki/Gharial" title="Gharial">gharial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mugger_crocodile" title="Mugger crocodile">mugger</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_dolphin" title="River dolphin">river dolphin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smooth-coated_otter" title="Smooth-coated otter">smooth-coated otter</a> and a number of turtle species. Ken-gharial and Son-gharial sanctuaries are managed for conservation of <a href="/wiki/Gharial" title="Gharial">gharial</a> and mugger. <a href="/wiki/Barasingha" title="Barasingha">Barasingha</a> is the state animal and <a href="/wiki/Asian_paradise_flycatcher" title="Asian paradise flycatcher">dudhraj</a> is the state bird of Madhya Pradesh.</p>
<p>Based on composition, the <a href="/wiki/Teak" title="Teak">teak</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sal_tree" title="Sal tree">sal</a> forests are the important forest formations in the state. Bamboo-bearing areas are widely distributed.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Narmada_River" title="Narmada River">Narmada</a> is the longest river in Madhya Pradesh. It flows westward through a rift valley, with the Vindhya ranges sprawling along its northern bank and the Satpura range of mountains along the southern. Its tributaries include the Banjar, the <a href="/wiki/Tawa_River" title="Tawa River">Tawa</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Machna&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Machna (page does not exist)">Machna</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Shakkar_River" title="Shakkar River">Shakkar</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Denwa" title="Denwa">Denwa</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sonbhardra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sonbhardra (page does not exist)">Sonbhardra</a> rivers. The <a href="/wiki/Tapti_River" title="Tapti River">Tapti River</a> runs parallel to Narmada, and also flows through a rift valley. The Narmada–Tapti systems carry and enormous volume of water and provide drainage for almost a quarter of the land area of Madhya Pradesh.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vindhyas" title="Vindhyas">Vindhyas</a> form the southern boundary of the Ganges basin, with the western part of the Ganges basin draining into the <a href="/wiki/Yamuna" title="Yamuna">Yamuna</a> and the eastern part directly into the Ganges itself. All the rivers, which drain into the Ganges, flow from south to north, with the <a href="/wiki/Chambal_River" title="Chambal River">Chambal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shipra_River" title="Shipra River">Shipra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kali_Sindh_River" title="Kali Sindh River">Kali Sindh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parbati_River_(Madhya_Pradesh)" title="Parbati River (Madhya Pradesh)">Parbati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuno_River" title="Kuno River">Kuno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sind_River" title="Sind River">Sind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Betwa_River" title="Betwa River">Betwa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dhasan_River" title="Dhasan River">Dhasan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ken_River" title="Ken River">Ken</a> rivers being the main tributaries of the Yamuna. Shipra River is one of the most sacred rivers of Hinduism. It is the site of the <a href="/wiki/Ujjain_Simhastha" title="Ujjain Simhastha">Simhastha Kumbh Mela</a>, which is held every 12 years. The land drained by these rivers is agriculturally rich, with the natural vegetation largely consisting of grass and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dry_deciduous_forest" title="Dry deciduous forest">dry deciduous forest</a> types, largely thorny. The eastern part of the Ganges basin consists of the <a href="/wiki/Son_River" title="Son River">Son</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tons_River" title="Tons River">Tons</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rihand" title="Rihand">Rihand</a> Rivers. Son, which arises in the Maikal hills around <a href="/wiki/Amarkantak" title="Amarkantak">Amarkantak</a>, is the largest tributary that goes into the Ganges on the south bank and that does not arise from the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>. Son and its tributaries contribute the bulk of the monsoon flow into the Ganges, because the north bank tributaries are all snow fed. The forests in their basins are much richer than the thorn forests of the northwestern part of Madhya Pradesh.</p>
<p>After the formation of Chhattisgarh State, the major portion of <a href="/wiki/Mahanadi" title="Mahanadi">Mahanadi</a> basin now lies in Chhattisgarh. Presently, only 154 km<sup>2</sup> basin area of Hasdeo River in Anuppur District lies in Madhya Pradesh.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satpura" title="Satpura">Satpuras</a>, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gawilgarh" title="Gawilgarh">Gawilgarh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mahadeo_Hills" title="Mahadeo Hills">Mahadeo Hills</a>, also contain a watershed, which is south facing. The <a href="/wiki/Wainganga_River" title="Wainganga River">Wainganga</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Wardha_River" title="Wardha River">Wardha</a>, the Pench, the <a href="/wiki/Kanhan_River" title="Kanhan River">Kanhan</a> rivers, discharge an enormous volume of water into the <a href="/wiki/Godavari_River" title="Godavari River">Godavari</a> river system. The Godavari basin consists of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-tropical" title="Sub-tropical">sub-tropical</a>, semi-moist forests, mainly in the valley of the Indrawati. There are many important multi-state irrigation projects in development, including the <a href="/wiki/Godavari_River_Basin_Irrigation_Projects" title="Godavari River Basin Irrigation Projects">Godavari River Basin Irrigation Projects</a>.</p>

<p>The population of Madhya Pradesh consists of a number of <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic groups</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tribe" title="Tribe">tribes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caste" title="Caste">castes</a> and communities, including the indigenous tribals and relatively more recent migrants from other states. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scheduled_castes" title="Scheduled castes">scheduled castes</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scheduled_tribes" title="Scheduled tribes">scheduled tribes</a> constitute a significant portion of the <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a> of the State. The main tribal groups in Madhya Pradesh are <a href="/wiki/Gondi_people" title="Gondi people">Gond</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bhil" title="Bhil">Bhil</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baiga_(tribe)" title="Baiga (tribe)">Baiga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korku_people" title="Korku people">Korku</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bharia_people" title="Bharia people">Bhadia</a> (or Bhariya), Halba, <a href="/wiki/Kaul" title="Kaul">Kaul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madia_Gond" title="Madia Gond">Mariya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malto_people" title="Malto people">Malto</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahariya" title="Sahariya">Sahariya</a>. <a href="/wiki/Dhar" title="Dhar">Dhar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jhabua" title="Jhabua">Jhabua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mandla" title="Mandla">Mandla</a> districts have more than 50 percent tribal population. In <a href="/wiki/Khargone" title="Khargone">Khargone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chhindwara" title="Chhindwara">Chhindwara</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seoni" title="Seoni">Seoni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sidhi" title="Sidhi">Sidhi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singrauli" title="Singrauli">Singrauli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shahdol" title="Shahdol">Shahdol</a> districts 30–50 percent population is of tribes. According to the 2001 census, the population of the tribals in Madhya Pradesh was 12,233,000, constituting 20.27% of the total population. There were 46 recognised Scheduled Tribes and three of them have been identified as "Special Primitive Tribal Groups" in the State.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to the different linguistic, cultural and geographical environment, and its peculiar complications, the diverse tribal world of Madhya Pradesh has been largely cut off from the mainstream of development. Madhya Pradesh ranks very low on the <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> value of 0.375 (2011), which is below the national average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNDP_HDI_2011_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNDP_HDI_2011-21">[21]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/India_State_Hunger_Index" title="India State Hunger Index">India State Hunger Index</a> (2008) compiled by the <a href="/wiki/International_Food_Policy_Research_Institute" title="International Food Policy Research Institute">International Food Policy Research Institute</a>, the malnutrition situation in Madhya Pradesh was "extremely alarming", receiving a severity rating between <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbchunger_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbchunger-22">[22]</a></sup> The state ranks is also the worst performer in India, when it comes to <a href="/wiki/Female_foeticide_in_India" title="Female foeticide in India">female foeticides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Madhya Pradesh is home to a large <a href="/wiki/Tribals_in_Madhya_Pradesh" title="Tribals in Madhya Pradesh">tribal population</a>, who have been largely cut off from the mainstream development. This makes Madhya Pradesh one of the least developed states in India, with an <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">HDI</a> (Human Development Index) value of 0.375 (2011), which is well below the national average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNDP_HDI_2011_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-UNDP_HDI_2011-21">[21]</a></sup> The state's per-capita <a href="/wiki/Gross_state_product" title="Gross state product">gross state domestic product</a> (nominal GDP) is the fourth lowest in the country (2010–11).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PlanningCom_GSDP_2012_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-PlanningCom_GSDP_2012-24">[24]</a></sup> MP is also the lowest-ranked state on the <a href="/wiki/India_State_Hunger_Index" title="India State Hunger Index">India State Hunger Index</a>.</p>
<p>Madhya Pradesh is one of the worst-affected states as far as malnutrition is concerned. The recent National Family Health Survey 2015-16 points out that Panna has 43.1 per cent stunted children, 24.7 per cent wasted and 40.3 per cent underweight children. Similar was the case in rural Chhatarpur where 44.4 per cent children are stunted, 17.8 per cent wasted and 41.2 per cent underweight, as per the report.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages">Languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madhya_Pradesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The official language of the state is <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a>. In addition <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marathi_language" title="Marathi language">Marathi</a> are spoken by a substantial number of the population since the state was home to several important and prestigious <a href="/wiki/Maratha" title="Maratha">Maratha</a> states. The state in fact has the highest concentration of <a href="/wiki/Marathi_people" title="Marathi people">Marathi people</a> outside <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a>. Several regional variants are spoken, which are considered by some to be dialects of Hindi, and by others to be distinct but related languages. Among these dialects are <a href="/wiki/Malvi_language" title="Malvi language">Malvi</a> in <a href="/wiki/Malwa" title="Malwa">Malwa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nimadi_language" title="Nimadi language">Nimadi</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nimar" title="Nimar">Nimar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bundeli_language" title="Bundeli language">Bundeli</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bundelkhand" title="Bundelkhand">Bundelkhand</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bagheli" title="Bagheli">Bagheli</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bagelkhand" title="Bagelkhand">Bagelkhand</a> and the southeast. Each of these languages has dialects of its own. Other languages include <a href="/wiki/Telugu_language" title="Telugu language">Telugu</a>, Bhilodi (<a href="/wiki/Bhili_language" title="Bhili language">Bhili</a>), <a href="/wiki/Gondi_language" title="Gondi language">Gondi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korku_language" title="Korku language">Korku</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kalto_language" title="Kalto language">Kalto</a> (Nahali), and <a href="/wiki/Nihali_language" title="Nihali language">Nihali</a> (Nahali), all spoken by tribal groups.</p>

<p>Three sites in Madhya Pradesh have been declared <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Sites</a> by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khajuraho" title="Khajuraho">Khajuraho Group of Monuments</a> (1986) including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Devi_Jagadambi_temple" title="Devi Jagadambi temple">Devi Jagadambi temple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rewa,_Madhya_Pradesh" title="Rewa, Madhya Pradesh">Rewa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sanchi" title="Sanchi">Buddhist Monuments at Sanchi</a> (1989) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_Shelters_of_Bhimbetka" title="Rock Shelters of Bhimbetka">Rock Shelters of Bhimbetka</a> (2003). Other architecturally significant or scenic sites include <a href="/wiki/Ajaigarh" title="Ajaigarh">Ajaigarh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amarkantak" title="Amarkantak">Amarkantak</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asirgarh" title="Asirgarh">Asirgarh</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bandhavgarh" title="Bandhavgarh">Bandhavgarh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bawangaja" title="Bawangaja">Bawangaja</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhopal" title="Bhopal">Bhopal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vidisha" title="Vidisha">Vidisha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chanderi" title="Chanderi">Chanderi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chitrakoot,_Madhya_Pradesh" title="Chitrakoot, Madhya Pradesh">Chitrakuta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dhar" title="Dhar">Dhar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gwalior" title="Gwalior">Gwalior</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indore" title="Indore">Indore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jabalpur" title="Jabalpur">Jabalpur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burhanpur" title="Burhanpur">Burhanpur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maheshwar" title="Maheshwar">Maheshwar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mandleshwar" title="Mandleshwar">Mandleshwar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mandu,_Madhya_Pradesh" title="Mandu, Madhya Pradesh">Mandu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Omkareshwar" title="Omkareshwar">Omkareshwar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orchha" title="Orchha">Orchha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pachmarhi" title="Pachmarhi">Pachmarhi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shivpuri" title="Shivpuri">Shivpuri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sonagiri" title="Sonagiri">Sonagiri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mandla" title="Mandla">Mandla</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ujjain" title="Ujjain">Ujjain</a>.</p>
<p>Madhya Pradesh is noted for its classical and folk music. Some of the noted <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_classical_music" title="Hindustani classical music">Hindustani classical music</a> <a href="/wiki/Gharana" title="Gharana">gharanas</a> in Madhya Pradesh include the <a href="/wiki/Maihar_gharana" title="Maihar gharana">Maihar gharana</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Gwalior_gharana" title="Gwalior gharana">Gwalior gharana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aashish_Khan" title="Aashish Khan">Senia gharana</a>. Two of the medieval India's most noted singers, <a href="/wiki/Tansen" title="Tansen">Tansen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baiju_Bawra" title="Baiju Bawra">Baiju Bawra</a>, were born near Gwalior in present-day Madhya Pradesh. Noted <a href="/wiki/Dhrupad" title="Dhrupad">Dhrupad</a> exponents <a href="/wiki/Aminuddin_Dagar" title="Aminuddin Dagar">Aminuddin Dagar</a> (Indore), <a href="/wiki/Gundecha_Brothers" title="Gundecha Brothers">Gundecha Brothers</a> (Ujjain) and <a href="/wiki/Uday_Bhawalkar" title="Uday Bhawalkar">Uday Bhawalkar</a> (Ujjain) were also born in present-day Madhya Pradesh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SimonMark2000_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-SimonMark2000-25">[25]</a></sup> The birthplaces of noted <a href="/wiki/Playback_singer" title="Playback singer">playback singers</a> <a href="/wiki/Kishore_Kumar" title="Kishore Kumar">Kishore Kumar</a> (Khandwa) and <a href="/wiki/Lata_Mangeshkar" title="Lata Mangeshkar">Lata Mangeshkar</a> (Indore) are also located in MP. The local styles of folk singing include Faga, Bhartahari, Sanja geet, Bhopa, <a href="/wiki/Kalbelia" title="Kalbelia">Kalbelia</a>, Bhat/Bhand/Charan, Vasdeva, Videsia, Kalgi Turra, Nirgunia, Alha, Pandwani Gayan and Garba Garbi Govalan.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The major folk dances of MP are Badhai, Rai, Saira, Jawara, Sher, Akhara, Shaitan, baredi, karma, kathi, Sua, Saila, Mauni, Dhimrai, kanara, Bhagoria, Dashera, dadariya, Duldul Ghodi, Lehgi ghodi, Fefriya, Mandlya, danda, Ada-khada, dadel, Matki, birha, Ahirai, pardhauni, Vilma, Dadar and Kalsa.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madhya_Pradesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Woman_harvesting_wheat,_Raisen_district,_Madhya_Pradesh,_India_ggia_version.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2472" data-file-width="3532" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Woman_harvesting_wheat%2C_Raisen_district%2C_Madhya_Pradesh%2C_India_ggia_version.jpg/220px-Woman_harvesting_wheat%2C_Raisen_district%2C_Madhya_Pradesh%2C_India_ggia_version.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Woman_harvesting_wheat%2C_Raisen_district%2C_Madhya_Pradesh%2C_India_ggia_version.jpg/330px-Woman_harvesting_wheat%2C_Raisen_district%2C_Madhya_Pradesh%2C_India_ggia_version.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Woman_harvesting_wheat%2C_Raisen_district%2C_Madhya_Pradesh%2C_India_ggia_version.jpg/440px-Woman_harvesting_wheat%2C_Raisen_district%2C_Madhya_Pradesh%2C_India_ggia_version.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Madhya_Pradesh" title="Economy of Madhya Pradesh">Economy of Madhya Pradesh</a></div>
<p>Madhya Pradesh's <a href="/wiki/Gross_state_product" title="Gross state product">gross state domestic product</a> (nominal GDP) for 2013-14 was <span style="white-space: nowrap">₹</span> 4,509 billion (approximately <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$</span> 72,726,000,000). The per-capita figure was <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$</span> 871.45 in 2013-14, the sixth-lowest in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Between 1999 and 2008, the annualised growth rate of the state was very low: 3.5%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lakshmi_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-lakshmi-28">[28]</a></sup> Subsequently, the state's GDP growth rate has improved significantly, rising to 8% during 2010–11 and 12% during 2011–12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TOI_2012_GDP_12pc_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-TOI_2012_GDP_12pc-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Madhya pradesh is also famous for honey production in district Morena.</p>
<p>The state has an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agrarian_economy" title="Agrarian economy">agrarian economy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TOI_2012_GDP_12pc_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-TOI_2012_GDP_12pc-29">[29]</a></sup> The major crops of Madhya Pradesh are wheat, soybean, gram, sugarcane, rice, maize, cotton, rapeseed, mustard and arhar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEF_2011_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-IBEF_2011-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Non-timber_forest_product" title="Non-timber forest product">Minor Forest Produce</a> (MFP), such as <a href="/wiki/Diospyros_melanoxylon" title="Diospyros melanoxylon">tendu</a> leaves used to roll <a href="/wiki/Beedi" title="Beedi">beedi</a>, sal seed, teak seed, and lak also contribute to state's rural economy.</p>
<p>Madhya Pradesh has 5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_Economic_Zones" title="Special Economic Zones">Special Economic Zones</a> (SEZs): 3 <a href="/wiki/Information_technology_in_India" title="Information technology in India">IT/ITeS</a> (Indore, <a href="/wiki/Gwalior" title="Gwalior">Gwalior</a>), 1 mineral-based (Jabalpur) and 1 agro-based (Jabalpur). In October 2011, approval was given to 14 proposed SEZs, out of which 10 were IT/ITeS-based.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEF_2011_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-IBEF_2011-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Indore" title="Indore">Indore</a> is the major commercial centre of the state. Because of the state's central location, a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumer_goods" title="Consumer goods">consumer goods</a> companies have established manufacturing bases in MP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEF_2011_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-IBEF_2011-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The state has the largest reserves of diamond and copper in India. Other major mineral reserves include those of coal, <a href="/wiki/Coalbed_methane" title="Coalbed methane">coalbed methane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEF_2011_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-IBEF_2011-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Madhya Pradesh has 6 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordnance_Factories" title="Ordnance Factories">Ordnance Factories</a>, 4 of which are located at Jabalpur (<a href="/wiki/Vehicle_Factory_Jabalpur" title="Vehicle Factory Jabalpur">Vehicle Factory</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_Iron_Foundry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Grey Iron Foundry (page does not exist)">Grey Iron Foundry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gun_Carriage_Factory_Jabalpur" title="Gun Carriage Factory Jabalpur">Gun Carriage Factory</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordnance_Factory_Khamaria" title="Ordnance Factory Khamaria">Ordnance Factory Khamaria</a>) and one each at Katni and Itarsi. The factories are run by the <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Factories_Board" title="Ordnance Factories Board">Ordnance Factories Board</a>, and manufacture a variety of products for the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Armed_Forces" title="Indian Armed Forces">Indian Armed Forces</a>.</p>
<p>Madhya Pradesh won the 10th National Award for excellent work in Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act, 2005.</p>
<p>The state's tourism industry is growing, fuelled by wildlife tourism and a number of places of historical and religious significance. <a href="/wiki/Sanchi" title="Sanchi">Sanchi</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khajuraho" title="Khajuraho">Khajuraho</a> are frequented by external tourists. Besides the major cities, <a href="/wiki/Bhedaghat" title="Bhedaghat">Bhedaghat</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bhimbetka" title="Bhimbetka">Bhimbetka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhojpur,_Madhya_Pradesh" title="Bhojpur, Madhya Pradesh">Bhojpur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maheshwar" title="Maheshwar">Maheshwar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mandu,_Madhya_Pradesh" title="Mandu, Madhya Pradesh">Mandu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orchha" title="Orchha">Orchha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pachmarhi" title="Pachmarhi">Pachmarhi</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kanha_National_Park" title="Kanha National Park">Kanha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ujjain" title="Ujjain">Ujjain</a> are the other popular tourist destinations.</p>

<p>Bus and train services cover most of Madhya Pradesh. The 99,043 km long road network of the state includes 20 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_National_Highways_in_India" title="List of National Highways in India">national highways</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEF_2011_30-5"><a href="#cite_note-IBEF_2011-30">[30]</a></sup> A 4,948 km long rail network criss-crosses the state, with <a href="/wiki/Jabalpur" title="Jabalpur">Jabalpur</a> serving as headquarters for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Central_Railway" title="West Central Railway">West Central Railway</a> Zone of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Railways" title="Indian Railways">Indian Railways</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Central_Railway_zone" title="Central Railway zone">Central Railway</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Western_Railway_zone" title="Western Railway zone">Western Railway</a> also cover parts of the state. Most of the western Madhya Pradesh comes under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ratlam_Rail_Division" title="Ratlam Rail Division">Ratlam Rail Division</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Railways" title="Western Railways">Western Railways</a>, including cities like Indore, Ujjain, Mandsaur, Khandwa, Neemuch and Bairagarh in Bhopal. The state has a total of 20 major railway junctions. The major <a href="/wiki/Inter_State_Bus_Terminals" title="Inter State Bus Terminals">inter-state bus terminals</a> are located in <a href="/wiki/Bhopal" title="Bhopal">Bhopal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indore" title="Indore">Indore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gwalior" title="Gwalior">Gwalior</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jabalpur" title="Jabalpur">Jabalpur</a>. More than 2000 buses are conducted daily from these four cities. The intra-city transit systems mostly consist of buses, private <a href="/wiki/Auto_rickshaw" title="Auto rickshaw">autos</a> and taxis.</p>
<p>The state does not have a coastline. Most of the sea trade happens through the <a href="/wiki/Kandla" title="Kandla">Kandla</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jawaharlal_Nehru_Port" title="Jawaharlal Nehru Port">Jawaharlal Nehru Port</a> (Nhava Sheva) in the neighbouring states, which are well-connected to MP by road and rail networks.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Aviation">Aviation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madhya_Pradesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Aviation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Devi_Ahilyabai_Holkar_Airport" title="Devi Ahilyabai Holkar Airport">Devi Ahilyabai Holkar Airport</a> at <a href="/wiki/Indore" title="Indore">Indore</a> is the busiest airport in Madhya Pradesh. <a href="/wiki/Raja_Bhoj_Airport" title="Raja Bhoj Airport">Raja Bhoj Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bhopal" title="Bhopal">Bhopal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jabalpur_Airport" title="Jabalpur Airport">Jabalpur Airport</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gwalior_Airport" title="Gwalior Airport">Gwalior Airport</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khajuraho_Airport" title="Khajuraho Airport">Khajuraho Airport</a> also have scheduled commercial passenger services. Besides these, minor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airstrip" title="Airstrip">airstrips</a> are located at <a href="/wiki/Ratlam" title="Ratlam">Ratlam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ujjain" title="Ujjain">Ujjain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Khandwa_Airport" title="Khandwa Airport">Khandwa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rewa_district" title="Rewa district">Rewa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shivpuri" title="Shivpuri">Shivpuri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Satna_Airport" title="Satna Airport">Satna</a> .</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madhya_Pradesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The state has 51 district hospitals, 333 community health centres, 1,155 primary health centres and 8,860 sub-centres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEF_2014_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-IBEF_2014-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The urban infrastructure has improved considerably in the past decade. 22 projects costing above $500 million have been sanctioned under the <a href="/wiki/Jawaharlal_Nehru_National_Urban_Renewal_Mission" title="Jawaharlal Nehru National Urban Renewal Mission">Jawaharlal Nehru National Urban Renewal Mission</a> for the development of Bhopal, Indore, <a href="/wiki/Jabalpur" title="Jabalpur">Jabalpur</a> and Ujjain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEF_2011_30-6"><a href="#cite_note-IBEF_2011-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madhya_Pradesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dainik_Bhaskar" title="Dainik Bhaskar">Dainik Bhaskar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dainik_Jagran" title="Dainik Jagran">Dainik Jagran</a>, Nava Bharat, Nai Duniya, Rajasthan Patrika, raj express, are the leading <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a> newspapers. Other local newspapers are published in the cities. In English Times of India, <a href="/wiki/Hindustan_Times" title="Hindustan Times">Hindustan Times</a>, The Hitavada, Central Chronicle and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Free_Press_Journal" title="The Free Press Journal">Free Press</a></i> have editions from Bhopal with The Hitavada also being in Jabalpur. Urdu journals are common in Bhopal. <i>Nadeem</i>, the oldest <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a> newspaper of the state, is published from Bhopal. <i>Urdu Action</i> and <i>Haq-o-Insaf</i> are also published. <i>Farz</i>, a Sindhi daily, is published from Bhopal is the only Sindhi newspaper in state.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_and_politics">Government and politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madhya_Pradesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Government and politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Madhya_Pradesh" title="Government of Madhya Pradesh">Government of Madhya Pradesh</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_of_Madhya_Pradesh" title="Legislative Assembly of Madhya Pradesh">Legislative Assembly of Madhya Pradesh</a></div>

<p>Madhya Pradesh has a 230-seat state legislative assembly. The state also sends 40 members to the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_India" title="Parliament of India">Parliament of India</a>: 29 are elected to the <a href="/wiki/Lok_Sabha" title="Lok Sabha">Lok Sabha</a> (Lower House) and 11 to the <a href="/wiki/Rajya_Sabha" title="Rajya Sabha">Rajya Sabha</a> (Upper House). The constitutional head of the state is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_Madhya_Pradesh" title="List of Governors of Madhya Pradesh">Governor</a>, appointed by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_India" title="President of India">President of India</a>. The executionary powers lie with the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Minister_(India)" title="Chief Minister (India)">Chief Minister</a>, who is the elected leader of the state legislature. As of 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Madhya_Pradesh&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the current governor is Om Prakash Kohli (additional charge), and the chief minister is <a href="/wiki/Shivraj_Singh_Chouhan" title="Shivraj Singh Chouhan">Shivraj Singh Chouhan</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Bharatiya_Janata_Party" title="Bharatiya Janata Party">Bharatiya Janata Party</a> (BJP).</p>
<p>The dominant political parties in the state are the <a href="/wiki/Bharatiya_Janata_Party" title="Bharatiya Janata Party">Bharatiya Janata Party</a> (BJP) and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_National_Congress" title="Indian National Congress">Indian National Congress</a>. Unlike in many of the neighbouring states, the small or regional parties have not had much success in the state elections. In the November 2013 state elections, the BJP won an absolute majority of 165 seats, defeating Congress which won 58 seats. <a href="/wiki/Bahujan_Samaj_Party" title="Bahujan Samaj Party">Bahujan Samaj Party</a> is the third major party in the state legislature, with 4 seats while others won 3 seats.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration">Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madhya_Pradesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_Madhya_Pradesh_by_population" title="List of cities in Madhya Pradesh by population">List of cities in Madhya Pradesh by population</a></div>
<p>Madhya Pradesh state is made up of 51 <a href="/wiki/List_of_districts_of_Madhya_Pradesh" title="List of districts of Madhya Pradesh">Districts</a>, which are grouped into 10 divisions. As of 2013, the state has 51 jila (district) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panchayat" title="Panchayat">panchayats</a>, 313 janpad panchayats/<a href="/wiki/Community_development_block_in_India" title="Community development block in India">blocks</a>, and 23043 gram (village) panchayats. The municipalities in the state include 16 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nagar_Nigam" title="Nagar Nigam">Nagar Nigams</a>, 100 <a href="/wiki/Nagar_Palika" title="Nagar Palika">Nagar Palikas</a> and 238 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nagar_Panchayat" title="Nagar Panchayat">Nagar Panchayats</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>According to the 2011 census, Madhya Pradesh had a literacy rate of 70.60%. According to the 2009–10 figures, the state had 105,592 primary schools, 6,352 high schools and 5,161 higher secondary schools. The state has 208 engineering &amp; architecture colleges, 208 management institutes and 12 medical colleges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEF_2011_30-7"><a href="#cite_note-IBEF_2011-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The state is home to some of the premier educational and research institutions of India including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Institute_of_Science_Education_and_Research" title="Indian Institute of Science Education and Research">Indian Institute of Science Education and Research (IISER)</a> Bhopal, <a href="/wiki/Indian_Institute_of_Management_Indore" title="Indian Institute of Management Indore">IIM Indore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_Institute_of_Technology_Indore" title="Indian Institute of Technology Indore">IIT Indore</a>, NITTTR (Bhopal), <a href="/wiki/Maulana_Azad_National_Institute_of_Technology" title="Maulana Azad National Institute of Technology">Maulana Azad National Institute of Technology</a>(Bhopal), <a href="/wiki/Indian_Institute_of_Information_Technology,_Design_and_Manufacturing,_Jabalpur" title="Indian Institute of Information Technology, Design and Manufacturing, Jabalpur">IIITDM Jabalpur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indian_Institute_of_Information_Technology_and_Management,_Gwalior" title="Indian Institute of Information Technology and Management, Gwalior">IIITM Gwalior</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://iittm.net/" rel="nofollow">Indian institute of Tourism and Travel Management</a>(IITTM, Gwalior), <a href="/wiki/School_of_Planning_and_Architecture" title="School of Planning and Architecture">SPA Bhopal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_Institute_of_Forest_Management" title="Indian Institute of Forest Management">IIFM</a> (Bhopal), <a href="/wiki/National_Law_Institute_University" title="National Law Institute University">National Law Institute University</a> (Bhopal), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Institute_Of_Engineering_%26_Science_IPS_Academy" title="Institute Of Engineering &amp; These universities include Jawaharlal Nehru Agriculture University, Madhya Pradesh Veterinary Sciences University, Madhya Pradesh Medical University, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rajiv_Gandhi_Technical_University" title="Rajiv Gandhi Technical University">Rajiv Gandhi Technical University</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bhopal" title="Bhopal">Bhopal</a>), <a href="/wiki/Awadhesh_Pratap_Singh_University" title="Awadhesh Pratap Singh University">Awadhesh Pratap Singh University</a> (<a href="/wiki/Rewa,_Madhya_Pradesh" title="Rewa, Madhya Pradesh">Rewa</a>), <a href="/wiki/Barkatullah_University" title="Barkatullah University">Barkatullah University</a> (Bhopal University), <a href="/wiki/Devi_Ahilya_Vishwavidyalaya" title="Devi Ahilya Vishwavidyalaya">Devi Ahilya Vishwavidyalaya</a> (<a href="/wiki/Indore" title="Indore">Indore</a>), <a href="/wiki/Rani_Durgavati_University" title="Rani Durgavati University">Rani Durgavati University</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jabalpur" title="Jabalpur">Jabalpur</a>), <a href="/wiki/Vikram_University" title="Vikram University">Vikram University</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ujjain" title="Ujjain">Ujjain</a>), <a href="/wiki/Jiwaji_University" title="Jiwaji University">Jiwaji University</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gwalior" title="Gwalior">Gwalior</a>), Dr Hari Singh Gaur University (<a href="/wiki/Sagar,_Madhya_Pradesh" title="Sagar, Madhya Pradesh">Sagar</a> University), Indira Gandhi National Tribal University (<a href="/wiki/Amarkantak" title="Amarkantak">Amarkantak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anuppur_district" title="Anuppur district">Anuppur</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Makhanlal_Chaturvedi_National_University_of_Journalism_and_Communication" title="Makhanlal Chaturvedi National University of Journalism and Communication">Makhanlal Chaturvedi National University of Journalism and Communication</a> (Bhopal).</p>
<p>The Professional Examination Board was initialised as Pre Medical Test Board by Government of Madhya Pradesh in the year 1970. After some year in 1981, Pre Engineering Board was constituted. Then after, in the year 1982 both these Boards were amalgamated and named as Madhya Pradesh Professional Examination Board (M.P.P.E.B.) also known as <a class="external text" href="http://truealerts.in" rel="nofollow">vyapam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madhya_Pradesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2013, state govt declared Malkhamb as the state sport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">Cricket</a>, <a href="/wiki/Field_hockey" title="Field hockey">hockey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Football" title="Football">football</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volleyball" title="Volleyball">volleyball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cycling" title="Cycling">cycling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swimming_(sport)" title="Swimming (sport)">swimming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Badminton" title="Badminton">badminton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Table_tennis" title="Table tennis">table tennis</a> are the popular sports in the state. Traditional games like <a href="/wiki/Kho_kho" title="Kho kho">kho kho</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gilli_danda" title="Gilli danda">gilli danda</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sitoliya" title="Sitoliya">sitoliya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kanche" title="Kanche">kanche</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hopscotch" title="Hopscotch">langdi</a> are popular in the rural areas.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Snooker" title="Snooker">Snooker</a>, a cue sport, generally regarded as having been invented in <a href="/wiki/Jabalpur" title="Jabalpur">Jabalpur</a> by <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> officers, is popular in many of the English-speaking and Commonwealth countries, with top professional players attaining multimillion-pound career earnings from the game.</p>
<p>Cricket is the most popular sport in Madhya Pradesh. There are three international <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cricket_(sport)" title="Cricket (sport)">cricket</a> stadiums in the state – <a href="/wiki/Nehru_Stadium,_Indore" title="Nehru Stadium, Indore">Nehru Stadium</a> (Indore), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roop_Singh_Stadium" title="Roop Singh Stadium">Roop Singh Stadium</a> (Gwalior) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holkar_Cricket_Stadium" title="Holkar Cricket Stadium">Holkar Cricket Stadium</a> (Indore). <a href="/wiki/Madhya_Pradesh_cricket_team" title="Madhya Pradesh cricket team">Madhya Pradesh cricket team</a>'s best performances in <a href="/wiki/Ranji_Trophy" title="Ranji Trophy">Ranji Trophy</a> was in 1998–99, when the <a href="/wiki/Chandrakant_Pandit" title="Chandrakant Pandit">Chandrakant Pandit</a>-led team ended as the runner-up. Its predecessor, the Indore-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holkar_cricket_team" title="Holkar cricket team">Holkar cricket team</a>, had won the Ranji Trophy four times.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aishbagh_Stadium" title="Aishbagh Stadium">Aishbagh Stadium</a> in Bhopal is the home ground for <a href="/wiki/World_Series_Hockey" title="World Series Hockey">World Series Hockey</a> team <a href="/wiki/Bhopal_Badshahs" title="Bhopal Badshahs">Bhopal Badshahs</a>. The state also has a <a href="/wiki/Madhya_Pradesh_football_team" title="Madhya Pradesh football team">football team</a> that participates in the <a href="/wiki/Santosh_Trophy" title="Santosh Trophy">Santosh Trophy</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/MP_United_FC_(India)" title="MP United FC (India)">MP United FC</a> is an Indian football that played in the <a href="/wiki/I-League_2nd_Division" title="I-League 2nd Division">2nd Division I-League</a>.</p>
