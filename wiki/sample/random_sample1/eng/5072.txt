<p><b>Maracas</b> (<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Br-Maraca.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/23/Br-Maraca.ogg" title="Br-Maraca.ogg">pronunciation</a></span> <small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:Br-Maraca.ogg" title="File:Br-Maraca.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span>), sometimes called <b>Rumba shakers</b>, <b>shac-shacs</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and various other names, are <a href="/wiki/Rattle_(percussion_instrument)" title="Rattle (percussion instrument)">rattles</a> which appear in many genres of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caribbean_music" title="Caribbean music">Caribbean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latin_music_(genre)" title="Latin music (genre)">Latin music</a>. Players hold them by their handles, usually in pairs, and shake them. Maracas can't be tuned.</p>
<p>Maracas (Mbaracás<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>), also known as Tamaracas, were rattles of divination, an oracle of the Brazilian <a href="/wiki/Tupinamb%C3%A1_people" title="Tupinambá people">Tupinamba</a> Indians, found also with other Indian tribes (<a href="/wiki/Garifuna_people" title="Garifuna people">Garifuna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guaran%C3%AD_people" title="Guaraní people">Guarani</a>), and on the <a href="/wiki/Orinoco" title="Orinoco">Orinoco</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. Rattles made from gourds (<a href="/wiki/Lagenaria" title="Lagenaria">Lagenaria</a>) are being shaken by the natural grip, while the round calabash (<a href="/wiki/Crescentia" title="Crescentia">Crescentia</a>) fruits are fitted to a handle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Human hair is sometimes fastened on the top, and a slit is cut in it to represent a mouth, through which their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shaman" title="Shaman">shamans</a> (payes) made it utter its responses. A few pebbles are inserted to make it rattle, and it is crowned with the red feathers of the <a href="/wiki/Scarlet_ibis" title="Scarlet ibis">Goaraz</a>. Every man had his maraca. It was used at their dances, and to heal the sick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Andean <a href="/wiki/Curandero" title="Curandero">curanderos</a> (healers) use maracas in their healing rites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern maraca balls are also made of leather, wood, or plastic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
