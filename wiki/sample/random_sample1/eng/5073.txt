<p><b>Lake Maracaibo</b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><i>Lago de Maracaibo</i></span>) is a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brackish" title="Brackish">brackish</a> tidal bay (or tidal <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> and an "inlet of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It is sometimes considered a <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a> rather than a <a href="/wiki/Bay" title="Bay">bay</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Limnol_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Limnol-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Publishing2010_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Publishing2010-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QuinnWoodward2015_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-QuinnWoodward2015-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It is connected to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Venezuela" title="Gulf of Venezuela">Gulf of Venezuela</a> by Tablazo Strait which is 5.5 kilometres (3.4 mi) wide at the northern end. It is fed by numerous rivers, the largest being the <a href="/wiki/Catatumbo_River" title="Catatumbo River">Catatumbo</a>. At 13,210 square kilometres (5,100 sq mi) it was once the largest lake in South America; the geological record shows that it has been a true lake in the past, and as such is one of the oldest lakes on Earth at 20–36 million years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LakeNet_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-LakeNet-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Columbia_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Columbia-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lake Maracaibo acts as a major shipping route to the ports of <a href="/wiki/Maracaibo" title="Maracaibo">Maracaibo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cabimas" title="Cabimas">Cabimas</a>. The surrounding <a href="/wiki/Maracaibo_Basin" title="Maracaibo Basin">Maracaibo Basin</a> contains large reserves of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a>, making the lake a major profit center for Venezuela.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Columbia_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Columbia-15">[15]</a></sup> It also holds almost a quarter of Venezuela's population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> A dredged channel gives oceangoing vessels access to the bay. The 8.7-kilometre (5.4 mi) long <a href="/wiki/General_Rafael_Urdaneta_Bridge" title="General Rafael Urdaneta Bridge">General Rafael Urdaneta Bridge</a>, which was completed in 1962 and spans the bay's outlet, is one of the longest bridges in the world.</p>
<p>The weather phenomenon known as the <a href="/wiki/Catatumbo_lightning" title="Catatumbo lightning">Catatumbo lightning</a> at Lake Maracaibo regularly produces more lightning than any other place on the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>The first known settlements on the bay were those of the <a href="/wiki/Wayuu_people" title="Wayuu people">Guajiros</a>, who still are present in large numbers, but were re-settled in the western boundary area with Colombia. The first <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> to discover the bay was <a href="/wiki/Alonso_de_Ojeda" title="Alonso de Ojeda">Alonso de Ojeda</a> on August 24, 1499, on a voyage with <a href="/wiki/Amerigo_Vespucci" title="Amerigo Vespucci">Amerigo Vespucci</a> (the same explorer for whom the American continents were named).</p>
<p>Legend has it that upon entering the lake, Ojeda's expedition found groups of indigenous huts, built over stilts on water (Spanish: <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palafitos" title="Palafitos">palafitos</a></i>), and interconnected by boardwalks on stilts, with each other and with the lake shore. The stilt houses reminded Vespucci of the city of <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><i>Venezia</i></span>), so he named the region "<i>Venezuela</i>,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dydynski_Beech_2004_177_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dydynski_Beech_2004_177-19">[19]</a></sup> meaning "little Venice" in Italian. The word has the same meaning in Spanish, where the <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a> <i>-uela</i> is used as a <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">diminutive</a> term (e.g., <i>plaza / plazuela</i>, <i>cazo / cazuela</i>); thus, the term's original sense would have been that of a "little Venice".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas_2005_189_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas_2005_189-20">[20]</a></sup> (Examples of palafitos can still be found in "Santa Rosa", an area in the city of Maracaibo.)</p>
<p>Although the Vespucci story remains the most popular and accepted version of the origin of the country's name, a different reason for the name comes up in the account of <a href="/wiki/Mart%C3%ADn_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_Enciso" title="Martín Fernández de Enciso">Martín Fernández de Enciso</a>, a member of the Vespucci and Ojeda crew. In his work <i>Summa de Geografía</i>, he states that they found an indigenous population who called themselves the "<i>Veneciuela</i>," which suggests that the name "Venezuela" may have evolved from the native word.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICH_1958_386_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ICH_1958_386-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The port town of <a href="/wiki/Maracaibo" title="Maracaibo">Maracaibo</a> was founded in 1529 on the western side. In July 1821, the bay was the site of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lake_Maracaibo" title="Battle of Lake Maracaibo">Battle of Lake Maracaibo</a>, an important battle in the <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_War_of_Independence" title="Venezuelan War of Independence">Venezuelan War of Independence</a>. Oil production began in the surrounding basin in 1914, with wells drilled by Bataafsche Petroleum Maatschappij, a predecessor of <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell" title="Royal Dutch Shell">Royal Dutch Shell</a>.</p>
<p>On April 6, 1964, at 11:45 pm, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supertanker" title="Supertanker">supertanker</a> <i>Esso Maracaibo</i>, loaded with 236,000 barrels (37,500 m<sup>3</sup>) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a>, suffered a major electrical failure, so that control of steering was lost. Thus it collided with pier #31 of the two-year-old General Rafael Urdaneta Bridge across the mouth of the lake. A 259 metres (850 ft) section of the bridge roadway fell into the water with a portion coming to rest across the tanker just a few feet from the ship's superstructure. No <a href="/wiki/Oil_spill" title="Oil spill">oil spill</a> occurred, and there were no deaths or serious injuries on the tanker. However seven motorists and passengers in vehicles crossing the bridge were killed.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fishing">Fishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Maracaibo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As recently as 2000, Lake Maracaibo supported 20,000 fishermen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Settlements">Settlements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Maracaibo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Settlements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several settlements built out on stilts over the lake – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palafitos" title="Palafitos">palafitos</a> – still exist in the south and south-west, notably at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lagunetas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lagunetas (page does not exist)">Lagunetas</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsiding_ground">Subsiding ground</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Maracaibo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Subsiding ground">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to the massive volume of oil removed in the Maracaibo Basin, some oil-producing areas adjacent to Lake Maracaibo have sunk, changing the geography of the region. The original concessions to oil companies purposefully assigned swamps and wetlands along the East border of the lakes for facilities. This required the oil companies to build dikes and drain the land in order to build their facilities, Dutch Shell takes credit for some of the most enduring dike systems. Since the nationalization of the oil industry, maintenance of the dike systems has fallen upon the Venezuelan government to sub-sea-level areas like Tía Juana, Lagunillas, and Bachaquero from encroachment by the waters. Cumulative subsidence is as much as 5 metres (16 ft), and it continues at a rate of up to 20 centimetres per year (7.9 in/year) at some locations inland and typically 5 cm/year (2.0 in/year) along the coast. Due to negligence of maintenance to the dike, many consider it to be a disaster in waiting, with the potential of an earthquake causing <a href="/wiki/Soil_liquefaction" title="Soil liquefaction">soil liquefaction</a> and submerging a large population. A program of mitigative measures to address the seismic risk was begun in 1988. Ongoing maintenance and improvements to the dike will be needed, as it continues to subside by as much as 7 cm/year (2.8 in/year).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Duckweed_infestation">Duckweed infestation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Maracaibo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Duckweed infestation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>As of June 18, 2004, a large portion (18%) of the surface of Lake Maracaibo is covered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duckweed" title="Duckweed">duckweed</a>, specifically <i><a href="/wiki/Lemna" title="Lemna">Lemna</a></i>. Although efforts to remove the plant have been underway, the plant – which can double its size every 48 hours – occupies over 130 million cubic metres of the lake. The only way to remove the weed is to pull it out of the lake physically – no chemical or biological method has been found to treat the weed. The government has been spending $2 million monthly to clean the lake, and the state-run oil company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petroleos_de_Venezuela_S.A." title="Petroleos de Venezuela S.A.">Petroleos de Venezuela S.A.</a> has created a $750 million cleanup fund. Current efforts are barely keeping up with the growth of the plant. The removal process has proven to be particularly difficult in the center of the lake, where a specially equipped ship may be needed to pull the weed off the lake.</p>
<p>There is some mystery as to how the plant came to reside in the waters of Lake Maracaibo. According to scientists from the Institute for the Conservation of Lake Maracaibo (ICLAM), one of the government organizations charged with the care of Lake Maracaibo, the weed is probably native to the lake, but few studies have been conducted to confirm that suspicion. The prodigious growth of the freshwater marine plant is likely a self-purification mechanism. Others<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2012)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> disagree, believing the type of duckweed to be native to Florida and Texas and thus the infestation is a result of its having been transported by ship.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Another point of uncertainty is why the scale of the outbreak is so great. Maracaibo is fed by both salt water from the Caribbean and fresh water from numerous rivers. The lighter fresh water floats on top of the heavier salt water, which forms a dense layer on the bottom. This set-up traps nutrients that have settled on the floor of the lake. In the spring of 2004, heavy rains disrupted the usual pattern. The sudden influx of fresh water stirred the layers, allowing nutrients to float to the top, where duckweed and other plants reside. These nutrients may have triggered the duckweed's rapid expansion. Additional sources of nutrients include untreated <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a> discharge and <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Industrial_waste" title="Industrial waste">industrial waste</a> flowing into the lake through rivers (97 percent of the country's raw sewage is discharged without treatment into the environment). Furthermore, chemicals used to clean up <a href="/wiki/Oil_spill" title="Oil spill">oil spills</a> may have contributed to the duckweed problem. The lake basin hosts Venezuela's largest <a href="/wiki/Oil_field" title="Oil field">oil fields</a>, and high concentrations of biodegradable <a href="/wiki/Dispersant" title="Dispersant">dispersants</a> that contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphates" title="Phosphates">phosphates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polyaspartic_acid" title="Polyaspartic acid">polyaspartic acid</a> – a chemical used to increase nutrient uptake in crops – have been found, a veritable feast for the plants. Scientists at ICLAM disagree, saying that dispersants have been banned from the lake for years and, even if they were present, could not contain enough nutrients to support the current duckweed population.</p>
<p>Duckweed is not toxic to fish, but some scientists are concerned that it could suck <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> out of the lake as it decays, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asphyxiation" title="Asphyxiation">asphyxiating</a> large numbers of fish. Though officials say the weed hasn't harmed fish yet, it is putting a dent in the local <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a> industry. The plant clogs the <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">motors</a> of small boats, making it impossible for fishers to launch their vessels. Duckweed further threatens the local <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> by choking out other plants as it shades large portions of the lake. In certain conditions, the weed may concentrate <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Salmonella" title="Salmonella">Salmonella</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Vibrio_cholerae" title="Vibrio cholerae">Vibrio cholerae</a></i>, the bacterium that causes <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>. Despite these problems, the weed may yet have some positive use; duckweed can be treated to be fed to <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a> or to make <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>.</p>
<p>As of 2007, the duckweed problem continues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
