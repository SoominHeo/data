<p>The <b>Holy Lavra of Saint <a href="/wiki/Sabbas_the_Sanctified" title="Sabbas the Sanctified">Sabbas the Sanctified</a></b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> known in Arabic as <b>Mar Saba</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">دير مار سابا</span>‎‎; <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he" xml:lang="he">מנזר מר סבא</span>‎‎; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Ἱερὰ Λαύρα τοῦ Ὁσίου Σάββα τοῦ Ἡγιασμένου</span>), is a <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Greek Orthodox</a> <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a> overlooking the <a href="/wiki/Kidron_Valley" title="Kidron Valley">Kidron Valley</a> at a point halfway between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_City_of_Jerusalem" title="Old City of Jerusalem">Old City of Jerusalem</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> within the <a href="/wiki/Bethlehem_Governorate" title="Bethlehem Governorate">Bethlehem Governorate</a> of the <a href="/wiki/West_Bank" title="West Bank">West Bank</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNESCO_consideration_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO_consideration-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The traditional date for the founding of the monastery by Sabbas the Sanctified of Mutalaska, <a href="/wiki/Cappadocia" title="Cappadocia">Cappadocia</a> is the year 483.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Today, the complex houses around 20 <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monks</a>. It is considered to be one of the oldest inhabited monasteries in the world, and still maintains many of its ancient traditions. One in particular is the restriction on <a href="/wiki/Women_in_Christianity" title="Women in Christianity">women</a> entering the main compound. The only building that women can enter is the Women's Tower, near the main entrance.</p>
<p>The monastery holds the <a href="/wiki/Relic" title="Relic">relics</a> of Saint Sabbas. Mar Saba is occasionally referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Convent" title="Convent">Convent</a> or <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">Monastery</a> of Santa Sabba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mar Saba was also the home of <a href="/wiki/John_of_Damascus" title="John of Damascus">St. John of Damascus</a> (676 - 749), a key religious figure in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iconoclastic_Controversy" title="Iconoclastic Controversy">Iconoclastic Controversy</a>, who, around 726, wrote letters to the Byzantine emperor <a href="/wiki/Leo_III_the_Isaurian" title="Leo III the Isaurian">Leo III the Isaurian</a> refuting his edicts prohibiting the veneration of <a href="/wiki/Icon" title="Icon">icons</a> (images of Christ or other Christian religious figures). John worked as a high financial officer to the Muslim <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caliph" title="Caliph">Caliph</a> <a href="/wiki/Abd_al-Malik_ibn_Marwan" title="Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan">Abd al-Malik</a>; he eventually felt a higher calling and migrated to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judaean_desert" title="Judaean desert">Judaean desert</a>, where he was <a href="/wiki/Tonsure" title="Tonsure">tonsured</a> a monk and was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordained" title="Ordained">ordained</a> a <a href="/wiki/Hieromonk" title="Hieromonk">hieromonk</a> (monastic priest) at the Monastery of Mar Saba. St. John's tomb lies in a cave under the monastery.</p>
<p>The monastery is important in the historical development of the <a href="/wiki/Liturgy" title="Liturgy">liturgy</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Church" title="Orthodox Church">Orthodox Church</a> in that the monastic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typicon" title="Typicon">Typicon</a> (manner of celebrating worship services) of Saint Sabbas became the standard throughout the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a> and those <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic Churches</a> which follow the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Rite" title="Byzantine Rite">Byzantine Rite</a>. The Typicon took the standard form of services which were celebrated in the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Patriarch_of_Jerusalem" title="Greek Orthodox Patriarch of Jerusalem">Patriarchate of Jerusalem</a> and added some specifically monastic usages which were local traditions at Saint Sabbas. From there it spread to <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, and thence throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">Byzantine</a> world. Although this Typicon has undergone further evolution, particularly at the Monastery of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoudion" title="Stoudion">Stoudion</a> in Constantinople, it is still referred to as the <i>Typicon of Saint Sabas</i>.</p>
<p>Mar Saba is where <a href="/wiki/Morton_Smith" title="Morton Smith">Morton Smith</a> found a copy of <a href="/wiki/Mar_Saba_letter" title="Mar Saba letter">a letter</a> ascribed to <a href="/wiki/Clement_of_Alexandria" title="Clement of Alexandria">Clement of Alexandria</a> containing excerpts of a so-called <i><a href="/wiki/Secret_Gospel_of_Mark" title="Secret Gospel of Mark">Secret Gospel of Mark</a>,</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and was for several centuries home to the <a href="/wiki/Archimedes_Palimpsest" title="Archimedes Palimpsest">Archimedes Palimpsest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thearchimedespalimpsestproject_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-thearchimedespalimpsestproject-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sabbas' relics were taken by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crusaders" title="Crusaders">Crusaders</a> in the 12th century and remained in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> until <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_VI" title="Pope Paul VI">Pope Paul VI</a> returned them to the monastery in 1965 as a gesture of good will towards the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Orthodox</a>.</p>
