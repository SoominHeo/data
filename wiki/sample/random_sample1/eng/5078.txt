<p><b>Muhammad Marmaduke Pickthall</b> (born <b>Marmaduke William Pickthall</b>, 7 April 1875 – 19 May 1936) was a Western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_scholar" title="Islamic scholar">Islamic scholar</a> noted for his English translation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a> (1930). A convert from Christianity, Pickthall was a novelist, esteemed by <a href="/wiki/D._H._Lawrence" title="D. H. Lawrence">D. H. Lawrence</a>, <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">H. G. Wells</a>, and <a href="/wiki/E._M._Forster" title="E. M. Forster">E. M. Forster</a>, as well as a journalist, headmaster, and political and religious <a href="/wiki/Leadership" title="Leadership">leader</a>. He declared his conversion to Islam in dramatic fashion after delivering a talk on 'Islam and Progress' on 29 November 1917, to the <a href="/wiki/Muslim_Literary_Society" title="Muslim Literary Society">Muslim Literary Society</a> in <a href="/wiki/Notting_Hill" title="Notting Hill">Notting Hill</a>, West London.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marmaduke_Pickthall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Marmaduke William Pickthall was born in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Terrace" title="Cambridge Terrace">Cambridge Terrace</a>, London on 7 April 1875, the eldest of the two sons of the Reverend Charles Grayson Pickthall (1822–1881) and his second wife, Mary Hale, <i>née</i> O'Brien (1836–1904).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shaheen_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-shaheen-1">[1]</a></sup> Charles was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> clergyman, the <a href="/wiki/Rector_(ecclesiastical)" title="Rector (ecclesiastical)">rector</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chillesford" title="Chillesford">Chillesford</a>, a village near <a href="/wiki/Woodbridge,_Suffolk" title="Woodbridge, Suffolk">Woodbridge, Suffolk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shaheen_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-shaheen-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murad_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-murad-2">[2]</a></sup> The Pickthalls traced their ancestry to a knight of <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a>, Sir Roger de Poictu, from whom their surname derives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murad_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-murad-2">[2]</a></sup> Mary, of the Irish <a href="/wiki/Baron_Inchiquin" title="Baron Inchiquin">Inchiquin</a> clan, was the widow of William Hale and the daughter of Admiral Donat Henchy O'Brien, who served in the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murad_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-murad-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fremantle_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-fremantle-3">[3]</a></sup> Pickthall spent the first few years of his life in the countryside, living with several older half-siblings and a younger brother in his father's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rectory" title="Rectory">rectory</a> in rural Suffolk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-muriel_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-muriel-4">[4]</a></sup> He was a sickly child. When about six months old, he fell very ill of measles complicated by bronchitis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fremantle_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-fremantle-3">[3]</a></sup> On the death of his father in 1881 the family moved to London. He attended <a href="/wiki/Harrow_School" title="Harrow School">Harrow School</a> but left after six terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rentfrow_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-rentfrow-5">[5]</a></sup> As a schoolboy at Harrow Pickthall was a classmate and friend of <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Pickthall travelled across many Eastern countries, gaining a reputation as a Middle-Eastern scholar.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Before declaring his faith as a Muslim, Pickthall was a strong ally of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>. He studied the <a href="/wiki/Orient" title="Orient">Orient</a>, and published articles and novels on the subject. While in the service of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osman_Ali_Khan,_Asif_Jah_VII" title="Osman Ali Khan, Asif Jah VII">Nizam of Hyderabad</a>, Pickthall published his English translation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a> with the title <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Meaning_of_the_Glorious_Koran_(book)" title="The Meaning of the Glorious Koran (book)">The Meaning of the Glorious Koran</a></i>. The translation was authorized by the <a href="/wiki/Al-Azhar_University" title="Al-Azhar University">Al-Azhar University</a> and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Times_Literary_Supplement" title="Times Literary Supplement">Times Literary Supplement</a></i> praised his efforts by writing "noted translator of the glorious Quran into English language, a great literary achievement."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hurst_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hurst-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>When a propaganda campaign was launched in the United Kingdom in 1915 over the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Genocide" title="Armenian Genocide">massacres of Armenians</a>, Pickthall rose to challenge it and argued that the blame could not be placed on the Turkish government entirely. At a time when Muslims in London had been co-opted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreign_Office" title="Foreign Office">Foreign Office</a> to provide propaganda services in support of Britain's war against Turkey, Pickthall's stand was considered<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> courageous given the wartime climate. When British Muslims were asked to decide whether they were loyal to the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I" title="Allies of World War I">Allies</a> (Britain and France) or the <a href="/wiki/Central_Powers" title="Central Powers">Central Powers</a> (Germany and Turkey), Pickthall said he was ready to be a combatant for his country so long as he did not have to fight the Turks. He was conscripted in the last months of the war and became corporal in charge of an influenza isolation hospital.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1920 he went to India with his wife to serve as editor of the <i>Bombay Chronicle</i>, returning to England only in 1935, a year before his death at St Ives, Cornwall. It was in India that he completed his famous translation, <i>The Meaning of the Glorious Koran</i>.</p>
<p>Pickthall was buried in the Muslim section at <a href="/wiki/Brookwood_Cemetery" title="Brookwood Cemetery">Brookwood Cemetery</a> in Surrey, England,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup> where <a href="/wiki/Abdullah_Yusuf_Ali" title="Abdullah Yusuf Ali">Abdullah Yusuf Ali</a> was later buried.</p>
