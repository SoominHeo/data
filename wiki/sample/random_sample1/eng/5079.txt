<p><b>Marcel Reich-Ranicki</b> (<small>German:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[maɐ̯ˈsɛl ˈʀaɪç ʀaˈnɪtski]</a></span>; 2 June 1920 – 18 September 2013) was a <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a>-born German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literary_critic" title="Literary critic">literary critic</a> and member of the literary group <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gruppe_47" title="Gruppe 47">Gruppe 47</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spiegel_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel-1">[1]</a></sup> He was regarded as one of the most influential contemporary literary critics in the field of <a href="/wiki/German_literature" title="German literature">German literature</a> and has often been called <i>Literaturpapst</i> ("Pope of Literature") in Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Who.27s_who_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Who.27s_who-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Reich-Ranicki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Reich-Ranicki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Marceli Reich</b> was born on 2 June 1920 in <a href="/wiki/W%C5%82oc%C5%82awek" title="Włocławek">Włocławek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Second_Polish_Republic" title="Second Polish Republic">Poland</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_obit_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP_obit-3">[3]</a></sup> to David Reich, a Polish Jewish merchant, and his wife, Helene (née Auerbach) Reich, who came from a German Jewish family. Reich moved with his family to <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> in 1929.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_obit_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-AP_obit-3">[3]</a></sup> As a Polish Jew he was banished to Poland in 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_obit_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_obit-4">[4]</a></sup> In November 1940, Reich and his parents found themselves in the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Ghetto" title="Warsaw Ghetto">Warsaw Ghetto</a>, during which time he worked for the <a href="/wiki/Judenrat" title="Judenrat">Judenrat</a> as a chief translator,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_obit_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-AP_obit-3">[3]</a></sup> and contributed to the collaborative newspaper <i>Gazeta Żydowska</i> (<i>The Jewish Newspaper</i>) as a music critic.</p>
<p>He married his wife, Teofila, on 22 July 1942, the first day of the mass transports to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treblinka" title="Treblinka">Treblinka</a> extermination camp (Judenrat employees and their wives were excluded from the first round of deportations).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_obit_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-AP_obit-3">[3]</a></sup> Reich's translator work meant that he was an eyewitness to meetings between the Jewish and Nazi authorities. In 1943 Reich and his wife managed to escape the Ghetto. His parents and brother were killed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holocaust" title="Holocaust">Holocaust</a>. His sister survived, having escaped to England shortly before the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_obit_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-AP_obit-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1944 he joined the <a href="/wiki/Armia_Ludowa" title="Armia Ludowa">Polish People's Army</a>, and became an officer in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urz%C4%85d_Bezpiecze%C5%84stwa" title="Urząd Bezpieczeństwa">Urząd Bezpieczeństwa</a>, Poland's intelligence service, where he worked in the <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">censorship</a> department.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He joined the <a href="/wiki/Polish_Workers%27_Party" title="Polish Workers' Party">Polish Workers' Party</a> after the war.</p>
<p>From 1948–49 he was a Polish diplomat and intelligence worker (operating under the pseudonym "Ranicki") in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_obit_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_obit-4">[4]</a></sup> He was recalled from London in 1949, sacked from the intelligence service and expelled from the Party on charges of "ideological estrangement", for which he was jailed for a short time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_obit_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_obit-4">[4]</a></sup> Subsequently, he developed a career as an editor, publisher of East German authors, and freelance writer for newspapers and radio with a focus on German literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SZ_obit_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-SZ_obit-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloom_obit_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bloom_obit-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_in_Germany">Life in Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Reich-Ranicki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Life in Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Frustrated by the curtailment of his liberty in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_Poland" title="People's Republic of Poland">People's Republic of Poland</a> he emigrated in 1958 with his wife and son to the <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_obit_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-AP_obit-3">[3]</a></sup> living in the city of Hamburg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_obit_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_obit-4">[4]</a></sup> Here he began writing for leading German periodicals, including <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Welt" title="Die Welt">Die Welt</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Frankfurter_Allgemeine_Zeitung" title="Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung">Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung</a></i>. In Poland, he had published under the pseudonym Ranicki,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_obit_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-AP_obit-3">[3]</a></sup> his intelligence codename. On the advice of the arts editor of the <i>Frankfurter Allgemeine</i> he adopted the name <b>Marcel Reich-Ranicki</b> professionally. From 1963 to 1973 he was literary critic for the German weekly <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Zeit" title="Die Zeit">Die Zeit</a></i>, published in Hamburg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zeit_obit_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zeit_obit-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1973 he moved to <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>, where, from 1973 to 1988, he was head of the literature staff at the daily <i><a href="/wiki/Frankfurter_Allgemeine_Zeitung" title="Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung">Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_obit_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_obit-4">[4]</a></sup> Reich-Ranicki would go on to write and edit for the <i>Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung</i> for the rest of his life. In 1969 he taught at an American university.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> From 1971 to 1975 he held visiting professorships at <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uppsala" title="Uppsala">Uppsala</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLK_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-BLK-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1974 he was awarded an honorary professorship at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_T%C3%BCbingen" title="University of Tübingen">University of Tübingen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLK_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-BLK-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1990 and 1991 he received the Heinrich-Hertz visiting professorship of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Karlsruhe" title="University of Karlsruhe">University of Karlsruhe</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and in 1991 and 1992 he received the Heinrich-Heine visiting professorship at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heinrich_Heine_University" title="Heinrich Heine University">University of Düsseldorf</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1988 to 2001, Reich-Ranicki hosted the literary talk show <i>Literarisches Quartett</i> on German public television.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spiegel_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel-1">[1]</a></sup> Through the show he became a household name in Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_obit_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_obit-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2002 the show was followed by a similar but short-lived programme, <i>Reich-Ranicki Solo</i>, which consisted of him talking about old and new books in front of a studio audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Having written about German literature for most of his life, he published books on American and Polish literature, after cutting down on his television appearances. Reich-Ranicki's wife and son encouraged him to write an autobiography "before it was too late". Published in 1999, <i>Mein Leben</i> was a bestseller in Germany, cementing his status. Mainly dealing with life and survival during the war, the book was adapted for television and broadcast in April 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_obit_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_obit-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2006 he received the <a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">honorary degree</a> of <i>Doctor Philosophiae Honoris Causa</i> from <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv_University" title="Tel Aviv University">Tel Aviv University</a>, which later that year established an endowed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chair_(academic)" title="Chair (academic)">chair</a> for German literature named after him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2007 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humboldt_University" title="Humboldt University">Humboldt University</a> in Berlin awarded him an honorary degree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ja_20070222_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-ja_20070222-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nch_20070215_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-nch_20070215-20">[20]</a></sup> This is the same university that Reich-Ranicki applied to in 1938, when his application was turned down because of his Jewish ancestry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ja_20070222_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-ja_20070222-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nch_20070215_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-nch_20070215-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2008, he was awarded a lifetime achievement award during a German television awards telecast for <i>Literarisches Quartett</i>. He made headlines with his acceptance speech, in which he spurned the prize and criticized the state of German television.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Marcel's son, Andrew Ranicki, is a professor of mathematics at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edinburgh_University" title="Edinburgh University">Edinburgh University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_obit_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_obit-4">[4]</a></sup> Marcel's wife, Teofila Reich-Ranicki, predeceased her husband by two years, dying in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> "He appreciated Jewish culture, especially its way with words, but found religion pointless and, after Warsaw, God inconceivable."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Reich-Ranicki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reich-Ranicki died on 18 September 2013 in <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>, having previously been diagnosed with prostate cancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spiegel_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guad_obit_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guad_obit-24">[24]</a></sup> German Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Angela_Merkel" title="Angela Merkel">Angela Merkel</a> paid tribute: "We lose in him a peerless friend of literature, but also of freedom and democracy. I will miss this passionate and brilliant man."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_obit_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_obit-4">[4]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/S%C3%BCddeutsche_Zeitung" title="Süddeutsche Zeitung">Süddeutsche Zeitung</a></i> described Reich-Ranicki as "the man who taught us how to read."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guad_obit_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guad_obit-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships_with_authors">Relationships with authors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Reich-Ranicki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relationships with authors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a tough critic Reich-Ranicki had a difficult relationship with other authors. Following the publication of <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Too_Far_Afield&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Too Far Afield (page does not exist)">Too Far Afield</a></i> by his fellow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gruppe_47" title="Gruppe 47">Gruppe 47</a> member <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCnter_Grass" title="Günter Grass">Günter Grass</a>, Reich-Ranicki appeared on the cover of the magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Spiegel" title="Der Spiegel">Der Spiegel</a></i>, tearing the novel apart. The magazine included his unfavorable review of the book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guad_obit_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guad_obit-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Reich-Ranicki praised Grass' next book, <i><a href="/wiki/Crabwalk" title="Crabwalk">Crabwalk</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Australian writer <a href="/wiki/Clive_James" title="Clive James">Clive James</a> stated "Every living German writer wants his praise but it has always been hard to get: the reason, of course, why they would like to have it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
