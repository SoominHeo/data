<p><b>Valentin Louis Georges Eugène Marcel Proust</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uː</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[maʁsɛl pʁust]</a></span>; 10 July 1871 – 18 November 1922), better known as <b>Marcel Proust</b>, was a French novelist, critic, and essayist best known for his monumental novel <i>À la recherche du temps perdu</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/In_Search_of_Lost_Time" title="In Search of Lost Time">In Search of Lost Time</a></i>; earlier rendered as <i>Remembrance of Things Past</i>), published in seven parts between 1913 and 1927: <i>The Walk by Swann's Place, In the Shade of Blooming Young Girls, The Guermantes Walk, Sodom and Gomorrah, The Captive Girl, Vanished Albertine, Time Found Again</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He is considered by English critics and writers to be one of the most influential authors of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Proust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Proust was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auteuil-Neuilly-Passy" title="Auteuil-Neuilly-Passy">Auteuil</a> (the south-western sector of Paris's then-rustic <a href="/wiki/16th_arrondissement_of_Paris" title="16th arrondissement of Paris">16th arrondissement</a>) at the home of his great-uncle on 10 July 1871, two months after the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Frankfurt_(1871)" title="Treaty of Frankfurt (1871)">Treaty of Frankfurt</a> formally ended the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a>. His birth took place during the violence that surrounded the suppression of the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Commune" title="Paris Commune">Paris Commune</a>, and his childhood corresponded with the consolidation of the <a href="/wiki/French_Third_Republic" title="French Third Republic">French Third Republic</a>. Much of <i><a href="/wiki/In_Search_of_Lost_Time" title="In Search of Lost Time">In Search of Lost Time</a></i> concerns the vast changes, most particularly the decline of the aristocracy and the rise of the middle classes that occurred in France during the Third Republic and the <i><a href="/wiki/Fin_de_si%C3%A8cle" title="Fin de siècle">fin de siècle</a>.</i></p>
<p>Proust's father, <a href="/wiki/Adrien_Proust" title="Adrien Proust">Adrien Proust</a>, was a prominent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathologist" title="Pathologist">pathologist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology" title="Epidemiology">epidemiologist</a>, studying <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> in Europe and Asia. He was the author of numerous articles and books on medicine and hygiene. Proust's mother, Jeanne Clémence Weil, was the daughter of a wealthy Jewish family from <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">Alsace</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Literate and well-read, she demonstrates a well-developed sense of humour in her letters, and her command of English was sufficient to help with her son's translations of <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tadi.C3.A9_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tadi.C3.A9-6">[6]</a></sup> Proust was raised in his father's Catholic faith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He was baptized (on 5 August 1871, at the church of <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint-Louis_d%27Antin" title="fr:Saint-Louis d'Antin">Saint-Louis d'Antin</a>) and later confirmed as a Catholic but he never formally practiced that faith. He later became an atheist and was somewhat of a mystic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the age of nine, Proust had had his first serious <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a> attack, and thereafter he was considered a sickly child. Proust spent long holidays in the village of <a href="/wiki/Illiers-Combray" title="Illiers-Combray">Illiers</a>. This village, combined with recollections of his great-uncle's house in Auteuil, became the model for the fictional town of Combray, where some of the most important scenes of <i>In Search of Lost Time</i> take place. (Illiers was renamed Illiers-Combray in 1971 on the occasion of the Proust centenary celebrations.)</p>
<p>In 1882, at the age of eleven, Proust became a pupil at the <a href="/wiki/Lyc%C3%A9e_Condorcet" title="Lycée Condorcet">Lycée Condorcet</a>, but his education was disrupted by his illness. Despite this he excelled in literature, receiving an award in his final year. Thanks to his classmates, he was able to gain access to some of the salons of the upper bourgeoisie, providing him with copious material for <i><a href="/wiki/In_Search_of_Lost_Time" title="In Search of Lost Time">In Search of Lost Time</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>Despite his poor health, Proust served a year (1889–90) enlisted in the French army, stationed at Coligny Barracks in <a href="/wiki/Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Orléans">Orléans</a>, an experience that provided a lengthy episode in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Guermantes%27_Way" title="The Guermantes' Way">The Guermantes' Way</a></i>, part three of his novel. As a young man, Proust was a dilettante and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_climber" title="Social climber">social climber</a> whose aspirations as a writer were hampered by his lack of self-discipline. His reputation from this period, as a snob and an amateur, contributed to his later troubles with getting <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swann%27s_Way" title="Swann's Way">Swann's Way</a></i>, the first part of his large-scale novel, published in 1913. At this time, he attended the <i>salons</i> of <a href="/wiki/Genevi%C3%A8ve_Hal%C3%A9vy" title="Geneviève Halévy">Mme Straus</a>, widow of <a href="/wiki/Georges_Bizet" title="Georges Bizet">Georges Bizet</a> and mother of Proust's childhood friend Jacques Bizet, of <a href="/wiki/Madeleine_Lemaire" title="Madeleine Lemaire">Madeleine Lemaire</a> and of <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9ontine_Lippmann" title="Léontine Lippmann">Mme Arman de Caillavet</a>, one of the models for Madame Verdurin, and mother of his friend <a href="/wiki/Gaston_Arman_de_Caillavet" title="Gaston Arman de Caillavet">Gaston Arman de Caillavet</a>, with whose fiancée (Jeanne Pouquet) he was in love. It is through Mme Arman de Caillavet that he made the acquaintance of <a href="/wiki/Anatole_France" title="Anatole France">Anatole France</a>, her lover.</p>
<p>In an 1892 article published in <i>Le Banquet</i> entitled "L'Irréligion d'État" and again in a 1904 <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Figaro" title="Le Figaro">Le Figaro</a></i> article entitled "La mort des cathédrales", Proust argued against the <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_church_and_state" title="Separation of church and state">separation of church and state</a>, declaring that socialism posed a greater threat to society than the Church and emphasizing the latter's role in sustaining a cultural and educational tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Proust had a close relationship with his mother. To appease his father, who insisted that he pursue a career, Proust obtained a volunteer position at <a href="/wiki/Biblioth%C3%A8que_Mazarine" title="Bibliothèque Mazarine">Bibliothèque Mazarine</a> in the summer of 1896. After exerting considerable effort, he obtained a sick leave that extended for several years until he was considered to have resigned. He never worked at his job, and he did not move from his parents' apartment until after both were dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tadi.C3.A9_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tadi.C3.A9-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>His life and family circle changed markedly between 1900 and 1905. In February 1903, Proust's brother, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Proust&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Robert Proust (page does not exist)">Robert Proust</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Proust" title="fr:Robert Proust">fr</a>)</span>, married and left the family home. His father died in November of the same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Finally, and most crushingly, Proust's beloved mother died in September 1905. She left him a considerable inheritance. His health throughout this period continued to deteriorate.</p>
<p>Proust spent the last three years of his life mostly confined to his bedroom, sleeping during the day and working at night to complete his novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> He died of <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Lung_abscess" title="Lung abscess">pulmonary abscess</a> in 1922. He was buried in the <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A8re_Lachaise_Cemetery" title="Père Lachaise Cemetery">Père Lachaise Cemetery</a> in Paris.</p>

<p>Proust was involved in writing and publishing from an early age. In addition to the literary magazines with which he was associated, and in which he published, while at school, <i>La Revue verte</i> and <i>La Revue lilas</i>, from 1890 to 1891 Proust published a regular society column in the journal <i>Le Mensuel</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tadi.C3.A9_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tadi.C3.A9-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1892 he was involved in founding a literary review called <i>Le Banquet</i> (also the French title of <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Symposium_(Plato)" title="Symposium (Plato)">Symposium</a></i>), and throughout the next several years Proust published small pieces regularly in this journal and in the prestigious <i>La Revue Blanche</i>.</p>
<p>In 1896 <i>Les Plaisirs et les Jours</i>, a compendium of many of these early pieces, was published. The book included a foreword by <a href="/wiki/Anatole_France" title="Anatole France">Anatole France</a>, drawings by madame <a href="/wiki/Madeleine_Lemaire" title="Madeleine Lemaire">Lemaire</a> in whose <i>salon</i> Proust would be a frequent guest, and who was a model for Mme Verdurin. She invited him and <a href="/wiki/Reynaldo_Hahn" title="Reynaldo Hahn">Reynaldo Hahn</a> to her <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9veillon,_Marne" title="Réveillon, Marne">château de Réveillon</a> (the model for Mme Verdurin's La Raspelière) in summer 1894 and three weeks in 1895. This book was so sumptuously produced that it cost twice the normal price of a book its size.</p>
<p>That year Proust also began working on a novel, which was eventually published in 1952 and titled <i><a href="/wiki/Jean_Santeuil" title="Jean Santeuil">Jean Santeuil</a></i> by his posthumous editors. Many of the themes later developed in <i>In Search of Lost Time</i> find their first articulation in this unfinished work, including the enigma of memory and the necessity of reflection; several sections of <i>In Search of Lost Time</i> can be read in the first draft in <i>Jean Santeuil</i>. The portrait of the parents in <i>Jean Santeuil</i> is quite harsh, in marked contrast to the adoration with which the parents are painted in Proust's masterpiece. Following the poor reception of <i>Les Plaisirs et les Jours</i>, and internal troubles with resolving the plot, Proust gradually abandoned <i>Jean Santeuil</i> in 1897 and stopped work on it entirely by 1899.</p>
<p>Beginning in 1895 Proust spent several years reading <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Carlyle" title="Thomas Carlyle">Carlyle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Emerson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a>. Through this reading Proust began to refine his own theories of art and the role of the artist in society. Also, in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_Temps_retrouv%C3%A9" title="Le Temps retrouvé">Time Regained</a></i> Proust's universal protagonist recalls having translated Ruskin's <i>Sesame and Lilies</i>. The artist's responsibility is to confront the appearance of nature, deduce its essence and retell or explain that essence in the work of art. Ruskin's view of artistic production was central to this conception, and Ruskin's work was so important to Proust that he claimed to know "by heart" several of Ruskin's books, including <i>The Seven Lamps of Architecture</i>, <i>The Bible of Amiens</i>, and <i>Praeterita</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tadi.C3.A9_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Tadi.C3.A9-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Proust set out to translate two of Ruskin's works into French, but was hampered by an imperfect command of English. To compensate for this he made his translations a group affair: sketched out by his mother, the drafts were first revised by Proust, then by Marie Nordlinger, the English cousin of his friend and sometime lover<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carter_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-carter-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Reynaldo_Hahn" title="Reynaldo Hahn">Reynaldo Hahn</a>, then again finally polished by Proust. Confronted about his method by an editor, Proust responded, "I don't claim to know English; I claim to know Ruskin".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tadi.C3.A9_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Tadi.C3.A9-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <i>The Bible of Amiens</i>, with Proust's extended introduction, was published in French in 1904. Both the translation and the introduction were very well reviewed; <a href="/wiki/Henri_Bergson" title="Henri Bergson">Henri Bergson</a> called Proust's introduction "an important contribution to the psychology of Ruskin" and had similar praise for the translation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tadi.C3.A9_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Tadi.C3.A9-6">[6]</a></sup> At the time of this publication, Proust was already at work on translating Ruskin's <i>Sesame and Lilies</i>, which he completed in June 1905, just before his mother's death, and published in 1906. Literary historians and critics have ascertained that, apart from Ruskin, Proust's chief literary influences included <a href="/wiki/Louis_de_Rouvroy,_duc_de_Saint-Simon" title="Louis de Rouvroy, duc de Saint-Simon">Saint-Simon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montaigne" title="Montaigne">Montaigne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stendhal" title="Stendhal">Stendhal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Flaubert" title="Gustave Flaubert">Flaubert</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Eliot" title="George Eliot">George Eliot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fyodor_Dostoyevsky" title="Fyodor Dostoyevsky">Fyodor Dostoyevsky</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy" title="Leo Tolstoy">Leo Tolstoy</a>.</p>
<p>1908 was an important year for Proust's development as a writer. During the first part of the year he published in various journals <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pastiches" title="Pastiches">pastiches</a> of other writers. These exercises in imitation may have allowed Proust to solidify his own style. In addition, in the spring and summer of the year Proust began work on several different fragments of writing that would later coalesce under the working title of <i>Contre Sainte-Beuve</i>. Proust described what he was working on in a letter to a friend: "I have in progress: a study on the nobility, a Parisian novel, an essay on <a href="/wiki/Charles_Augustin_Sainte-Beuve" title="Charles Augustin Sainte-Beuve">Sainte-Beuve</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Flaubert" title="Gustave Flaubert">Flaubert</a>, an essay on women, an essay on pederasty (not easy to publish), a study on stained-glass windows, a study on tombstones, a study on the novel".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tadi.C3.A9_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Tadi.C3.A9-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>From these disparate fragments Proust began to shape a novel on which he worked continually during this period. The rough outline of the work centered on a first-person narrator, unable to sleep, who during the night remembers waiting as a child for his mother to come to him in the morning. The novel was to have ended with a critical examination of Sainte-Beuve and a refutation of his theory that biography was the most important tool for understanding an artist's work. Present in the unfinished manuscript notebooks are many elements that correspond to parts of the <i>Recherche</i>, in particular, to the "Combray" and "Swann in Love" sections of Volume 1, and to the final section of Volume 7. Trouble with finding a publisher, as well as a gradually changing conception of his novel, led Proust to shift work to a substantially different project that still contained many of the same themes and elements. By 1910 he was at work on <i>À la recherche du temps perdu</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Search_of_Lost_Time"><i>In Search of Lost Time</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Proust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In Search of Lost Time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/In_Search_of_Lost_Time" title="In Search of Lost Time">In Search of Lost Time</a></div>
<p>Begun in 1909, when Proust was 38 years old, <i>À la recherche du temps perdu</i> consists of seven volumes totaling around 3,200 pages (about 4,300 in The Modern Library's translation) and featuring more than 2,000 characters. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graham_Greene_(writer)" title="Graham Greene (writer)">Graham Greene</a> called Proust the "greatest novelist of the 20th century",<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a href="/wiki/W._Somerset_Maugham" title="W. Somerset Maugham">W. Somerset Maugham</a> called the novel the "greatest fiction to date".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Proust died before he was able to complete his revision of the drafts and proofs of the final volumes, the last three of which were published posthumously and edited by his brother, Robert.</p>
<p>The book was translated into English by <a href="/wiki/C._K._Scott_Moncrieff" title="C. K. Scott Moncrieff">C. K. Scott Moncrieff</a>, appearing under the title <i>Remembrance of Things Past</i> between 1922 and 1931. Scott Moncrieff translated volumes one through six of the seven volumes, dying before completing the last. This last volume was rendered by other translators at different times. When Scott Moncrieff's translation was later revised (first by <a href="/wiki/Terence_Kilmartin" title="Terence Kilmartin">Terence Kilmartin</a>, then by <a href="/wiki/D._J._Enright" title="D. J. Enright">D. J. Enright</a>) the title of the novel was changed to the more literal <i>In Search of Lost Time</i>.</p>
<p>In 1995 Penguin undertook a fresh translation of the book by editor Christopher Prendergast and seven translators in three countries, based on the latest, most complete and authoritative French text. Its six volumes, comprising Proust's seven, were published in Britain under the Allen Lane imprint in 2002.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Proust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Proust was homosexual, and his sexuality and relationships with men are often discussed by his biographers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Although his housekeeper, Céleste Albaret, denies this aspect of Proust's sexuality in her memoirs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> her denial runs contrary to the statements of many of Proust's friends and contemporaries, including his fellow writer <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Gide" title="André Gide">André Gide</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> as well as his valet Ernest A. Forssgren.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>During his life, Proust never openly admitted to his homosexuality, though his family and close friends either knew or suspected it. In 1897, he even fought a duel with writer <a href="/wiki/Jean_Lorrain#Duel" title="Jean Lorrain">Jean Lorrain</a>, who publicly questioned the nature of Proust's relationship with Lucien Daudet. (Both duelists survived.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Despite Proust's own public denial, his romantic relationship with composer <a href="/wiki/Reynaldo_Hahn#Jean_Santeuil" title="Reynaldo Hahn">Reynaldo Hahn</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carter_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-carter-14">[14]</a></sup> and his infatuation with his chauffeur and secretary, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Agostinelli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alfred Agostinelli (page does not exist)">Alfred Agostinelli</a>, are well documented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The exact influence of Proust's sexuality on his writing is a topic of debate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> However, <i>In Search of Lost Time</i> discusses homosexuality at length and features several principal characters, both men and women, who are either homosexual or bisexual: the <a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">Baron</a> de Charlus, Robert de Saint-Loup, and Albertine Simonet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Homosexuality also appears as a theme in <i>Les plaisirs et les jours</i> and his unfinished novel, <i>Jean Santeuil</i>.</p>
