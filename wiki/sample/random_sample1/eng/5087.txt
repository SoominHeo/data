<p><b>Marco Bode</b> (born 23 July 1969 in <a href="/wiki/Osterode_am_Harz" title="Osterode am Harz">Osterode am Harz</a>) is a former German <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">footballer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A true <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/One_club_man" title="One club man">one club man</a>, Bode spent his entire professional career at <a href="/wiki/SV_Werder_Bremen" title="SV Werder Bremen">Werder Bremen</a>. He played as a left winger and forward.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Club_career">Club career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marco_Bode&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Club career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He played his first football with home club VfR Osterode, before moving to the amateur team of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Werder_Bremen" title="Werder Bremen">Werder Bremen</a> in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Otto_Rehhagel" title="Otto Rehhagel">Otto Rehhagel</a> and soon moved to the professional team.</p>
<p>Between 1989 and 2002 he was active in 379 games for Werder, in which he scored 101 goals, making him the record Bremen goal-scorer. Despite some offers by major European clubs such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayern_Munich" title="Bayern Munich">Bayern Munich</a>, he remained loyal to Werder Bremen, retiring somewhat early after the 2002 World Cup. Bode also won renown because he was a particularly fair and decent player who only was booked ten times in his entire Bundesliga career and never got sent off. He was also known for his smart, sundry TV interviews.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_career">International career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marco_Bode&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: International career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the seven years following 1995, he also played in 40 games for the <a href="/wiki/Germany_national_football_team" title="Germany national football team">German national football team</a>, scoring 9 goals. He took part in the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Euro_1996" title="UEFA Euro 1996">UEFA Euro 1996</a> final and in the final of the 2002 World Championships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/2002_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2002 FIFA World Cup">2002 FIFA World Cup</a> Bode was a surprise substitution from manager <a href="/wiki/Rudi_V%C3%B6ller" title="Rudi Völler">Rudi Völler</a> in the crucial third group match against Cameroon with the match still at 0–0.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Being close to retirement the German fans felt Bode's selection was dubious, however Bode silenced the doubter by scoring the opening goal of an eventual 2–0 win for Germany propelling them into the knockout stages as group winners. It would be Bode's final goal<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and he made his last appearance in the World Cup final as Germany fell to Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
