<p><b><a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a></b> competed at the <b><a href="/wiki/2016_Summer_Olympics" title="2016 Summer Olympics">2016 Summer Olympics</a></b> in <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a>, Brazil, from 5 to 21 August 2016. This was the nation's tenth consecutive appearance at the Olympics, after gaining its independence from the former <i>Rhodesia</i>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe_Olympic_Committee" title="Zimbabwe Olympic Committee">Zimbabwe Olympic Committee</a> (ZOC) fielded a team of 31 athletes, 9 men and 22 women, to compete in seven different sports at the Games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZIM-Olympics_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZIM-Olympics-3">[3]</a></sup> It was the nation's largest ever delegation sent to the Olympics in a non-boycotting edition and the second-largest overall in history, a stark contrast to the seven athletes who attended the London Games four years earlier. Among the sports represented by the nation's athletes, Zimbabwe marked its Olympic debut in equestrian eventing and women's football, as well as its return to archery after nearly three decades.</p>
<p>Topping the list of athletes to make the Zimbabwean roster was swimmer <a href="/wiki/Kirsty_Coventry" title="Kirsty Coventry">Kirsty Coventry</a>. At 32 years old and headed to her fifth Olympic Games, Coventry emerged herself as Zimbabwe's most decorated Olympian of all time, and Africa's most successful female swimmer in history, with a total of seven medals, including two golds in the women's 200 m backstroke. Because of her outstanding achievements in swimming, Coventry was chosen again to carry the Zimbabwean flag at the opening ceremony for the second consecutive Games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flag-bearer_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-flag-bearer-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Apart from Coventry, the Zimbabwean team also saw four more athletes competing in more than one edition. Among the returnees were single sculls rower <a href="/wiki/Micheen_Thornycroft" title="Micheen Thornycroft">Micheen Thornycroft</a>, marathon runner <a href="/wiki/Wirimai_Juwawo" title="Wirimai Juwawo">Wirimai Juwawo</a>, along with his teammate and three-time Olympian <a href="/wiki/Cuthbert_Nyasango" title="Cuthbert Nyasango">Cuthbert Nyasango</a>, and double trap shooter <a href="/wiki/Sean_Nicholson" title="Sean Nicholson">Sean Nicholson</a>, the oldest member of the team (aged 42).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZIM-Olympics_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ZIM-Olympics-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the second Olympics in a row, Zimbabwe left Rio de Janeiro without a single medal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Before retiring from the sport, Coventry capped off her illustrious Olympic career with a sixth-place finish in the <a href="/wiki/Swimming_at_the_2016_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Women%27s_200_metre_backstroke" title="Swimming at the 2016 Summer Olympics â Women's 200 metre backstroke">women's 200 m backstroke</a>, the most successful outcome for Zimbabwe at the Games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
