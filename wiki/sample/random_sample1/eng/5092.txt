<p><b>Marxist sociology</b> is the study of <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a> from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist" title="Marxist">Marxist</a> perspective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-black_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-black-1">[1]</a></sup> Marxism itself can be recognized as both a <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, particularly so far as it attempts to remain <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific, systematic</a> and objective rather than purely <a href="/wiki/Normative" title="Normative">normative</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/prescriptive" title="wikt:prescriptive">prescriptive</a>. Marxist sociology is "a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conflict_theory" title="Conflict theory">conflict theory</a> associated with ... Marxism's objective of developing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positivism_(sociology)" title="Positivism (sociology)">positive</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empirical" title="Empirical">empirical</a>) science of <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a> as part of the mobilization of a revolutionary <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">working class</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encofsoc_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-encofsoc-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/American_Sociological_Association" title="American Sociological Association">American Sociological Association</a> has a section dedicated to the issues of Marxist sociology that is "interested in examining how insights from Marxist <a href="/wiki/Methodology" title="Methodology">methodology</a> and Marxist analysis can help explain the complex dynamics of modern society".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-section_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-section-3">[3]</a></sup> Marxist sociology would come to facilitate the developments of <a href="/wiki/Critical_theory" title="Critical theory">critical theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cultural_studies" title="Cultural studies">cultural studies</a> as loosely distinct disciplines.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concepts_and_issues">Concepts and issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marxist_sociology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Concepts and issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Key concepts of Marxist sociology include <a href="/wiki/Historical_materialism" title="Historical materialism">historical materialism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mode_of_production" title="Mode of production">modes of production</a> and the relation between <a href="/wiki/Capital_(economics)" title="Capital (economics)">capital</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labour_(economics)" title="Labour (economics)">labour</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encofsoc_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-encofsoc-2">[2]</a></sup> Marxist sociology is primarily concerned with, but not limited to, the relations between society and economics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-section_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-section-3">[3]</a></sup> Key questions asked by Marxist sociology include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-black_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-black-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>How does capital control workers?</li>
<li>How does a mode of production influence the <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">social class</a>?</li>
<li>What is the relation between workers, capital, <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">the state</a> and <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>?</li>
<li>How do economic factors influence inequalities such as those relating to <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">gender</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(classification_of_human_beings)" title="Race (classification of human beings)">race</a>?</li>
</ul>
<p>Within the field of <a href="/wiki/Sociological_theory" title="Sociological theory">sociological theory</a>, Marxist sociology is recognized as one of the major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sociological_paradigm" title="Sociological paradigm">sociological paradigms</a> and is associated with conflict theory and critical theory. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marxist_philosophy" title="Marxist philosophy">Marxist philosophy</a>, Marxist sociology has put relatively little weight on creating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Class_revolution" title="Class revolution">class revolution</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-black_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-black-1">[1]</a></sup> pursuing instead the development of an objective, <a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">politico-economic</a> study of society rather than a critical philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Praxis_(process)" title="Praxis (process)">praxis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encofsoc_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-encofsoc-2">[2]</a></sup> It may be seen as a field of <a href="/wiki/Economic_sociology" title="Economic sociology">economic sociology</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_development">Historical development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marxist_sociology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">References for this section:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-black_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-black-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encofsoc_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-encofsoc-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dictbot_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-dictbot-4">[4]</a></sup></div>
<p>Influenced by the thought of <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a>, Marxist sociology emerged during the end of the 19th and beginning of the 20th century. As well as Marx, <a href="/wiki/Max_Weber" title="Max Weber">Max Weber</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Durkheim" title="Émile Durkheim">Émile Durkheim</a> are considered seminal influences in <a href="/wiki/History_of_sociology" title="History of sociology">early sociology</a>. The first Marxist school of sociology was known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austro-Marxism" title="Austro-Marxism">Austro-Marxism</a>, of which <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gr%C3%BCnberg" title="Carl Grünberg">Carl Grünberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Labriola" title="Antonio Labriola">Antonio Labriola</a> were among its most notable members. Much of the development in the field of Marxist sociology occurred on the outskirts of academia, pitting Marxist against "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bourgeois" title="Bourgeois">bourgeois</a>" sociology.</p>
<p>For a while, this division was reinforced by the Marxism-inspired <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">Russian Revolution</a>, which led to the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>; soon, however, sociology found itself a victim of the <a href="/wiki/Suppressed_research_in_the_Soviet_Union#Sociology" title="Suppressed research in the Soviet Union">suppression of "bourgeois" science</a> in the communist states. While, after several decades, sociology was reestablished in communist states, two separate currents of thought evolved within Marxist sociology: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_Marxism" title="Soviet Marxism">Soviet Marxism</a>, developed in the communist states (primarily the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>) of the 20th century, serving the state's interests and significantly crippled by forced adherence to the <a href="/wiki/Dogma" title="Dogma">dogma</a> of <a href="/wiki/Historical_materialism" title="Historical materialism">historical materialism</a>; and the more independent school centered on the studies of Marxism in <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">the West</a>. During the 1940s, the <a href="/wiki/Western_Marxism" title="Western Marxism">Western Marxist</a> school became accepted within Western academia, subsequently fracturing into several different perspectives such as the <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_School" title="Frankfurt School">Frankfurt School</a> or <a href="/wiki/Critical_theory" title="Critical theory">critical theory</a>.</p>
<p>Due to its former state-supported position, there has been a backlash against Marxist thought in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-communist" title="Post-communist">post-communist</a> states (see, for example, <a href="/wiki/Sociology_in_Poland" title="Sociology in Poland">Sociology in Poland</a>) but it remains dominant in the sociological research sanctioned and supported by those communist states that remain (see, for example, <a href="/wiki/Sociology_in_China" title="Sociology in China">Sociology in China</a>).</p>
