<p><b>Martensite</b>, named after the German <a href="/wiki/Category:Metallurgists" title="Category:Metallurgists">metallurgist</a> <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Martens" title="Adolf Martens">Adolf Martens</a> (1850–1914), most commonly refers to a very <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">hard</a> form of steel crystalline structure, but it can also refer to any crystal structure that is formed by <a href="/wiki/Diffusionless_transformation" title="Diffusionless transformation">diffusionless transformation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A.Q._Khan.2C_University_of_Leuven.2C_Belgium_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-A.Q._Khan.2C_University_of_Leuven.2C_Belgium-1">[1]</a></sup> It includes a class of hard minerals occurring as lath- or plate-shaped <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystal</a> grains. When viewed in cross section, the <a href="/wiki/Lens_(geology)" title="Lens (geology)">lenticular</a> (lens-shaped) crystal grains may be incorrectly described as <i><a href="/wiki/Crystal_habit" title="Crystal habit">acicular</a></i> (needle-shaped)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martensite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Martensite is formed in carbon steels by the rapid cooling (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quench" title="Quench">quenching</a>) of the <a href="/wiki/Austenite" title="Austenite">austenite</a> form of iron at such a high rate that carbon atoms do not have time to diffuse out of the crystal structure in large enough quantities to form <a href="/wiki/Cementite" title="Cementite">cementite</a> (Fe<sub>3</sub>C). Austenite is γ-Fe, (gamma-phase iron), a solid solution of iron and alloying elements. As a result of the quenching, the face-centered cubic austenite transforms to a highly strained body-centered tetragonal form called martensite that is supersaturated with carbon. The shear deformations that result produce a large number of dislocations, which is a primary strengthening mechanism of steels. The highest hardness of a pearlitic steel is 400 <a href="/wiki/Brinell_scale" title="Brinell scale">Brinell</a> whereas martensite can achieve 700 Brinell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marks.27_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marks.27-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The martensitic reaction begins during cooling when the austenite reaches the martensite start temperature (M<sub>s</sub>) and the parent austenite becomes mechanically unstable. As the sample is quenched, an increasingly large percentage of the austenite transforms to martensite until the lower transformation temperature M<sub>f</sub> is reached, at which time the transformation is completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A.Q._Khan.2C_University_of_Leuven.2C_Belgium_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-A.Q._Khan.2C_University_of_Leuven.2C_Belgium-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>For a eutectoid steel (0.78% C), between 6 and 10% of austenite, called retained austenite, will remain. The percentage of retained austenite increases from insignificant for less than 0.6% C steel, to 13% retained austenite at 0.95% C and 30–47% retained austenite for a 1.4% carbon steels. A very rapid quench is essential to create martensite.</p>
<p>For a eutectoid carbon steel of thin section, if the quench starting at 750 °C and ending at 450 °C takes place in 0.7 seconds (a rate of 430 °C/s) no pearlite will form and the steel will be martensitic with small amounts of retained austenite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marks.27_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Marks.27-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>For steel 0-0.6% carbon the martensite has the appearance of <a href="/wiki/Lath" title="Lath">lath</a>, and is called lath martensite. For steel greater than 1% carbon it will form a plate like structure called plate martensite. Between those two percentages, the physical appearance of the grains is a mix of the two. The strength of the martensite is reduced as the amount of retained austenite grows. If the cooling rate is slower than the critical cooling rate, some amount of pearlite will form, starting at the grain boundaries where it will grow into the grains until the M<sub>s</sub> temperature is reached then the remaining austenite transforms into martensite at about half the speed of sound in steel.</p>
<p>In certain alloy steels, martensite can also be formed by the working and hence deformation of the steel at temperature, while it is in its austenitic form, by quenching to below M<sub>s</sub> and then working by plastic deformations to reductions of cross section area between 20% to 40% of the original. The process produces dislocation densities up to 10<sup>13</sup>/cm<sup>2</sup>. The great number of dislocations, combined with precipitates that originate and pin the dislocations in place, produces a very hard steel (this property is frequently used in toughened ceramics like <a href="/wiki/Yttria-stabilized_zirconia" title="Yttria-stabilized zirconia">yttria-stabilized zirconia</a> and in special steels like <a href="/wiki/TRIP_steel" title="TRIP steel">TRIP steels</a>). Thus, martensite can be thermally induced or stress induced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A.Q._Khan.2C_University_of_Leuven.2C_Belgium_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-A.Q._Khan.2C_University_of_Leuven.2C_Belgium-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Verhoeven_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Verhoeven-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the differences between the two phases is that martensite has a body-centered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetragonal" title="Tetragonal">tetragonal</a> (BCT) crystal structure, whereas austenite has a <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">face-centered cubic</a> (FCC) structure. The transition between these two structures requires very little <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_activation" title="Thermal activation">thermal activation</a> energy because it is a <a href="/wiki/Diffusionless_transformation" title="Diffusionless transformation">diffusionless transformation</a>, which results in the subtle but rapid rearrangement of atomic positions, and has been known to occur even at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">cryogenic</a> temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A.Q._Khan.2C_University_of_Leuven.2C_Belgium_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-A.Q._Khan.2C_University_of_Leuven.2C_Belgium-1">[1]</a></sup> Martensite has a lower density than austenite, so that the martensitic transformation results in a relative change of volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EM2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-EM2-4">[4]</a></sup> Of considerably greater importance than the volume change is the shear strain which has a magnitude of about 0.26 and which determines the shape of the plates of martensite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EM3_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-EM3-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Martensite is not shown in the equilibrium <a href="/wiki/Phase_diagram" title="Phase diagram">phase diagram</a> of the iron-carbon system because it is not an equilibrium phase. Equilibrium phases form by slow cooling rates that allow sufficient time for diffusion, whereas martensite is usually formed by very high cooling rates. Since chemical processes (the attainment of equilibrium) <a href="/wiki/Activation_energy" title="Activation energy">accelerate</a> at higher temperature, martensite is easily destroyed by the application of heat. This process is called <a href="/wiki/Tempering_(metallurgy)" title="Tempering (metallurgy)">tempering</a>. In some alloys, the effect is reduced by adding elements such as <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> that interfere with <a href="/wiki/Cementite" title="Cementite">cementite</a> nucleation, but, more often than not, the nucleation is allowed to proceed to relieve stresses. Since quenching can be difficult to control, many steels are quenched to produce an overabundance of martensite, then tempered to gradually reduce its concentration until the preferred structure for the intended application is achieved. The needle-like microstructure of martensite leads to brittle behavior of the material. Too much martensite leaves steel <a href="/wiki/Brittleness" title="Brittleness">brittle</a>, too little leaves it <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">soft</a>.</p>
