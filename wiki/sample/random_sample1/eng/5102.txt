<p><b>Martin Jong-bum Hyun</b> (born May 4, 1979, in <a href="/wiki/Krefeld" title="Krefeld">Krefeld</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>) is a short story writer, columnist, author and retired <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> professional <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a> player who played in Germany's <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Eishockey_Liga" title="Deutsche Eishockey Liga">Deutsche Eishockey Liga</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He was appointed technical coordinator and manager for the <a href="/wiki/2018_Winter_Olympics" title="2018 Winter Olympics">2018 Winter Olympics</a> by the <a href="/wiki/PyeongChang_Organizing_Committee_for_the_2018_Olympic_%26_Paralympic_Winter_Games" title="PyeongChang Organizing Committee for the 2018 Olympic &amp; Paralympic Winter Games">PyeongChang Organizing Committee for the Olympic Games</a> (POCOG) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> in the field of ice hockey and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_sledge_hockey" title="Ice sledge hockey">ice sledge hockey</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hockey_career">Hockey career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Hyun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hockey career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The son of <a href="/wiki/Koreans_in_Germany" title="Koreans in Germany">Korean immigrants in Germany</a>, he began playing ice hockey at the age of 5, progressing his way through local youth leagues and into the <a href="/wiki/Krefeld_Pinguine" title="Krefeld Pinguine">Krefeld Pinguine</a>'s youth development system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHL_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHL-3">[3]</a></sup> He became a regular player for the German Junior National Team. In 1997 he was summoned by Coach Miroslav Berek and General Manager Rüdiger Noack to begin the season with the Elite league team at the age of 17, where he scored two goals in five preseason games playing alongside former NHL player Peter Ihnacak (Toronto Maple Leafs). Hyun played most of the season with the second-league development team <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.eurohockey.net/players/show_player.cgi?serial=25580" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>. In 1998 Hyun decided to go to the United States to further his education and pursue his hockey career at the same time. He first studied at Benilde-St. Margaret's High School, a <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholic</a> school in <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Park,_Minnesota" title="St. Louis Park, Minnesota">St. Louis Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, later transferring to <a href="/wiki/Northwood_School_(Lake_Placid,_New_York)" title="Northwood School (Lake Placid, New York)">Northwood Prep School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Placid,_New_York" title="Lake Placid, New York">Lake Placid</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHL_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHL-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Soon after arriving in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, Hyun became a fan of <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">National Hockey League</a> player <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kariya" title="Paul Kariya">Paul Kariya</a> who is of partial Japanese ancestry. After failing to be picked in the NHL Draft during the World U-17 Championships in Moncton, Canada in 1997, Hyun put aside his pursuit of a professional NHL hockey career, and enrolled in <a href="/wiki/Colchester,_Vermont" title="Colchester, Vermont">Colchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Saint_Michael%27s_College" title="Saint Michael's College">Saint Michael's College</a>, where he played <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a> Division II hockey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHL_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-NHL-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2002 Hyun won the ECAC Division II Northeast Championship with the Purple Knights.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Upon graduation in 2003, he entered a masters' program at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kent" title="University of Kent">University of Kent</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> campus; while a student, he also played for a <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Hockey_League" title="Belgian Hockey League">Belgian Hockey League</a> team in <a href="/wiki/Leuven" title="Leuven">Leuven</a>. He returned to Germany in 2004 to sign with the Krefeld Pinguine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHL_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-NHL-3">[3]</a></sup> With his signing Hyun became the first Asian to ever make it to the highest professional league in Germany. He made his league debut on September 17, 2004 against the Cologne Sharks. Hyun chose to wear Number 71 to honour the accomplishments of Korean guestworkers in Germany. In April 2005 Hyun was invited by German President Horst Köhler to attend the state banquet in honour of the state visit of <a href="/wiki/President_of_South_Korea" title="President of South Korea">South Korean President</a> <a href="/wiki/Roh_Moo-hyun" title="Roh Moo-hyun">Roh Moo-hyun</a>. Hyun is only the third player of Korean heritage to play in the highest league, the previous two being Jim Paek and Richard Park of the USA.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Hyun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After just one season, Hyun retired from professional ice hockey to move to <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, where he worked with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_the_Republic_of_Korea" title="National Assembly of the Republic of Korea">National Assembly</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_for_Health,_Welfare_and_Family_Affairs_(South_Korea)" title="Ministry for Health, Welfare and Family Affairs (South Korea)">Ministry of Health and Welfare</a> to promote Korean-German friendship. Though Hyun sometimes faced racism and exclusion in Germany, he stated that he felt even more like a stranger in South Korea; he returned to Germany to enroll in a doctoral programme at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bonn" title="University of Bonn">University of Bonn</a>, where he is writing his thesis on the experiences of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_generation_immigrant" title="Second generation immigrant">second-generation</a> Korean youth in Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHL_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-NHL-3">[3]</a></sup> He also gained more experience in the political world with the German and European Parliament and the Ministry of Generations, Family, Women and Integration.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_as_author">Career as author</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Hyun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career as author">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He has published a book entitled <i>Lautlos-JA Sprachlos-NEIN</i> (Silent-YES Speechless-NO) on the social integration experiences of second-generation Koreans in Germany. The book was published in 2008 by EB-Verlag Hamburg <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9783936912845">ISBN 978-3-936912-84-5</a>. In his highly acclaimed debut book Hyun was supported by his friend and bestselling author Wladimir Kaminer. They both performed together on stage at the Goethe-Institut in Seoul. Five years later Hyun published his second book with publishing power house Random House. The book was titled "Ohne Fleiß kein Reis - wie ich ein guter Deutscher wurde <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>". The author explores issues central to the Asian German experience: the encounter of diverse cultures; the challenges of racism, discrimination, and exclusion. The author above all raises the question of becoming and being German with "slanted" eyes. His special views on minorities and social integration has landed him a job with Germany's most influential political news broadcasting radio station "Deutschlandradio Kultur <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>" as columnist.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_in_politics">Career in politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Hyun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Career in politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hyun has become a vivid fighter for minority rights in Germany. In 2007 Hyun was selected among thirty young leaders with a foreign background to participate in the Bertelsmann-Foundation Leadership-Program in order to promote social integration of minorities in Germany. As a professional ice hockey player Hyun initiated a mentoring-project for children from low income families and helped children with cancer. On October 10, 2008 Hyun received a second invitation from German President Horst Köhler to talk about the demographic change and increasing diversity in Germany. Numerous visits from different Presidents followed. In 2008 Hyun was appointed Goodwill Ambassador for the European Year of the intercultural dialogue, sponsored by the European Commission and European Parliament. In 2010 Hyun founded "Hockey is Diversity <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>" an initiative to promote diversity outside the boundaries of sports.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Winter_Olympics_2018">Winter Olympics 2018</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Hyun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Winter Olympics 2018">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In January 2015 Hyun with the recommendation of the International Ice Hockey Federation (IIHF) was appointed by the PyeongChang Organizing Committee of the Winter Olympics 2018 (POCOG) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> as their new technical coordinator for their men's and women's ice hockey and ice sledge hockey games.</p>
