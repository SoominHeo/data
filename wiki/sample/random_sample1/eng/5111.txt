<p>The <b>Marinid dynasty</b> (<a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">Berber</a>: <i>Imrinen</i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <i>Marīniyūn</i>) or <b>Banu abd al-Haqq</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunni Muslim</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> dynasty of <a href="/wiki/Zenata" title="Zenata">Zenata</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_people" title="Berber people">Berber</a> descent that ruled <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> from the 13th to the 15th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1244, the Marinids overthrew the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almohad_dynasty" title="Almohad dynasty">Almohad dynasty</a>, which controlled Morocco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It briefly held sway over all the <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">Maghreb</a> in the mid-14th century. The Marinid dynasty supported the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Granada" title="Kingdom of Granada">Kingdom of Granada</a> in <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Al-Andalus</a> in the 13th and 14th centuries; an attempt to gain a direct foothold on the European side of the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar" title="Strait of Gibraltar">Strait of Gibraltar</a> was however defeated at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_R%C3%ADo_Salado" title="Battle of Río Salado">Battle of Río Salado</a> in 1340 and finished after the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Castilian</a> conquest of <a href="/wiki/Algeciras" title="Algeciras">Algeciras</a> from the Marinids in 1344.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Marinids were overthrown after the <a href="/wiki/1465_Moroccan_revolt" title="1465 Moroccan revolt">1465 revolt</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Wattasid_dynasty" title="Wattasid dynasty">Wattasids</a>, a related dynasty, came to power in 1472.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marinid_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marinid_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Marinids were a branch of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wassin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wassin (page does not exist)">Wassin</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wassin" title="fr:Wassin">fr</a>)</span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khald.C3.BBn1856_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Khald.C3.BBn1856-7">[7]</a></sup> a nomadic <a href="/wiki/Zenata" title="Zenata">Zenata</a> Berber tribe that lived in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zibans&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zibans (page does not exist)">Zibans</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zibans" title="fr:Zibans">fr</a>)</span> (present-day <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>) before being driven towards <a href="/wiki/Tlemcen" title="Tlemcen">Tlemcen</a> by the Arab invasion in the 11th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The tribe had first frequented the area between <a href="/wiki/Sijilmasa" title="Sijilmasa">Sijilmasa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Figuig" title="Figuig">Figuig</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EI571_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-EI571-9">[9]</a></sup> Following the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Banu_Hilal" title="Banu Hilal">Arab</a> tribes in the area in the 11th-12th centuries, Marinids moved to the north-west of present-day <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EI571_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-EI571-9">[9]</a></sup> before settling into northern Morocco by the beginning of the 13th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Marinids took their name from their ancestor, Marin ibn Wartajan al-Zenati.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise">Rise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marinid_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After arriving in Morocco, they initially submitted to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almohad_dynasty" title="Almohad dynasty">Almohads</a>, the ruling dynasty at the time. After successfully contributing to the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Alarcos" title="Battle of Alarcos">Battle of Alarcos</a>, in central Spain, the tribe started to assert itself as a political power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-google1-12">[12]</a></sup> Starting in 1213, they began to tax farming communities of north-eastern Morocco (the area between <a href="/wiki/Nador" title="Nador">Nador</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berkane" title="Berkane">Berkane</a>). The relationship between them and the Almohads became strained and starting in 1215, there were regular outbreaks of fighting between the two parties. In 1217 they tried to occupy eastern Morocco, but they were expelled, after which they pulled back and settled in the eastern Rif mountains. Here they remain for nearly 30 years. During their stay in the <a href="/wiki/Rif" title="Rif">Rif</a>, the Almohad state suffered huge blows, losing large territories to the Christians in Spain, while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hafsids" title="Hafsids">Hafsids</a> of Ifriqia broke away in 1229, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Zayyanid_dynasty" title="Zayyanid dynasty">Zayyanids</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tlemcen" title="Tlemcen">Tlemcen</a> in 1235.</p>
<p>Between 1244 and 1248 the Marinids were able to take <a href="/wiki/Taza" title="Taza">Taza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rabat" title="Rabat">Rabat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sal%C3%A9" title="Salé">Salé</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meknes" title="Meknes">Meknes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fes,_Morocco" title="Fes, Morocco">Fes</a> from the weakened Almohads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The Marinid leadership installed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fes,_Morocco" title="Fes, Morocco">Fes</a> declared war on the Almohads, fighting with the aid of Christian <a href="/wiki/Mercenary" title="Mercenary">mercenaries</a>. <a href="/wiki/Abu_Yusuf_Yaqub_ibn_Abd_Al-Haqq" title="Abu Yusuf Yaqub ibn Abd Al-Haqq">Abu Yusuf Yaqub</a> (1259–1286) captured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marrakech" title="Marrakech">Marrakech</a> in 1269.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Height">Height</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marinid_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Height">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Nasrid_dynasty" title="Nasrid dynasty">Nasrids</a> ceded <a href="/wiki/Algeciras" title="Algeciras">Algeciras</a> to the Marinids, Abu Yusuf went to <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Al-Andalus</a> to support the ongoing struggle against the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">Kingdom of Castile</a>. The Marinid dynasty then tried to extend its control to include the commercial traffic of the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar" title="Strait of Gibraltar">Strait of Gibraltar</a>.</p>
<p>It was in this period that the Spanish Christians were first able to take the fighting to Morocco: in 1260 and 1267 they attempted an invasion of Morocco, but both attempts were defeated. After gaining a foothold in Spain, the Marinids became active in the conflict between Muslims and Christians in Iberia. To gain absolute control of the trade in the Strait of Gibraltar, from their base at Algeciras they started the conquest of several Spanish towns: by the year 1294 they had occupied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rota,_Spain" title="Rota, Spain">Rota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tarifa" title="Tarifa">Tarifa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>.</p>
<p>In 1276 they founded <a href="/wiki/Fes_Jdid" title="Fes Jdid">Fes Jdid</a>, which they made their administrative and military centre. While Fes had been a prosperous city throughout the Almohad period, even becoming the largest city in the world during that time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> it was in the Marinid period that Fes reached its golden age, a period which marked the beginning of an official, historical narrative for the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It is from the Marinid period that Fes' reputation as an important intellectual centre largely dates, they established the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madrassa" title="Madrassa">madrassas</a> in the city and country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The principal monuments in the medina, the residences and public buildings, date from the Marinid period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despte internal infighting, Abu Said Uthman II (1310–1331) initiated huge construction projects across Morocco. Several madrassas were built, the <a href="/wiki/Al-Attarine_Madrasa" title="Al-Attarine Madrasa">Al-Attarine Madrasa</a> being the most famous. The building of these madrassas were necessary to create a dependent bureaucratic class, in order to undermine the marabouts and Sharifian elements in Morocco.</p>
<p>The Marinids also strongly influenced the policy of the <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Granada" title="Emirate of Granada">Emirate of Granada</a>, from which they enlarged their army in 1275. In the 13th century, the Kingdom of Castile made several incursions into Morocco. In 1260, <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Castilian</a> forces raided <a href="/wiki/Sal%C3%A9" title="Salé">Salé</a> and, in 1267, initiated a full-scale invasion of Morocco, but the Marinids repelled them.</p>
<p>At the height of their power, during the rule of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_al-Hasan_%27Ali" title="Abu al-Hasan 'Ali">Abu al-Hasan 'Ali</a> (1331–1348), the Marinid army was large and disciplined. It consisted of 40.000 Zenata cavalry, while Arab nomads contributed to the cavalry and Andalusians were included as archers. The personal bodyguard of the sultan consisted of 7.000 men, and included Christian, Kurdish and Black African elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-google1-12">[12]</a></sup> Under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_al-Hasan_%27Ali" title="Abu al-Hasan 'Ali">Abu al-Hasan</a> another attempt was made to reunite the <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">Maghreb</a>. In 1337 the <a href="/wiki/Zayyanid_dynasty" title="Zayyanid dynasty">Abdalwadid</a> <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Tlemcen" title="Kingdom of Tlemcen">kingdom of Tlemcen</a> was conquered, followed in 1347 by the defeat of the <a href="/wiki/Hafsid_dynasty" title="Hafsid dynasty">Hafsid</a> empire in <a href="/wiki/Ifriqiya" title="Ifriqiya">Ifriqiya</a>, which made him master of a huge territory, which spanned from southern Morocco to <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">Tripoli</a>. However, in 1340 the Marinids suffered a crushing defeat at the hands of a Portuguese-Castilian coalition in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_R%C3%ADo_Salado" title="Battle of Río Salado">Battle of Río Salado</a>, and finally had to withdraw from Andalusia, only holding on to Algeciras. In that same year a revolt of Arab tribes in southern Tunisia, made them lose their eastern territories.</p>
<p>In 1348 Abu al-Hasan was deposed by his son <a href="/wiki/Abu_Inan_Faris" title="Abu Inan Faris">Abu Inan Faris</a>, who tried to reconquer Algeria and Tunisia. Despite several successes, he was strangled by his own vizir in 1358, after which the dynasty began to decline.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline">Decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marinid_dynasty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the death of Abu Inan Faris in 1358, the real power lay with the viziers, while the Marinid sultans were paraded and forced to succeed each other in quick succession. The county was divided and political anarchy set in, with different viziers and foreign powers supporting different factions. In 1359 Hintata tribesmen from the High Atlas came down and occupied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marakesh" title="Marakesh">Marakesh</a>, capital of their Almohad ancestors, which they would govern independently until 1526. To the south of Marakesh, Sufi mystics claimed autonomy, and in the 1370s <a href="/wiki/Azemmour" title="Azemmour">Azemmour</a> broke off under a coalition of merchants and Arab clan leaders of the Banu Sabih. To the east, the Zianid and Hafsid families reemerged and to the north, the Europeans were taking advantage of the Moroccan instability by attacking the Moroccan coast. Meanwhile, unruly wandering Arab <a href="/wiki/Bedouin" title="Bedouin">Bedouin</a> tribes increasingly spread anarchy in Morocco, which accelerated the decline of the empire.</p>
<p>In the 15th century Morocco was hit by a financial crisis, after which the state had to stop financing the different marabouts and Sharifian families, which had previously been useful instruments in controlling the country. The political support of these marabouts and Sharifians halted, and Morocco splintered into different entities. In 1399 Tetouan was taken and its population was massacred and in 1415 the Portuguese captured Ceuta. After the sultan Abdalhaqq II (1421–1465) tried to break the power of the Wattasids, he was executed.</p>
<p>Marinid rulers after 1420 came under the control of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wattasid" title="Wattasid">Wattasids</a>, who exercised a regency as <a href="/wiki/Abd_al-Haqq_II" title="Abd al-Haqq II">Abd al-Haqq II</a> became Sultan one year after his birth. The Wattasids however refused to give up the Regency after Abd al-Haqq came to age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1459, Abd al-Haqq II managed a massacre of the Wattasid family, breaking their power. His reign, however, brutally ended as he was murdered during the <a href="/wiki/1465_Moroccan_revolt" title="1465 Moroccan revolt">1465 revolt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> This event saw the end of the Marinid dynasty as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Muhammad_ibn_Ali_Amrani-Joutey&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Muhammad ibn Ali Amrani-Joutey (page does not exist)">Muhammad ibn Ali Amrani-Joutey</a>, leader of the <a href="/wiki/Sharif" title="Sharif">Sharifs</a>, was proclaimed Sultan in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fes" title="Fes">Fes</a>. He was in turn overthrown in 1471 by <a href="/wiki/Abu_Abd_Allah_al-Sheikh_Muhammad_ibn_Yahya" title="Abu Abd Allah al-Sheikh Muhammad ibn Yahya">Abu Abd Allah al-Sheikh Muhammad ibn Yahya</a>, one of the two the surviving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wattasids" title="Wattasids">Wattasids</a> from the 1459 massacre, who instigated the <a href="/wiki/Wattasid_dynasty" title="Wattasid dynasty">Wattasid dynasty</a>.</p>

<ul>
<li>1215: The Banu Marin (Marinids) attacks the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almohads" title="Almohads">Almohads</a> when the 16-year-old Almohad caliph <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yusuf_II,_Almohad_Caliph" title="Yusuf II, Almohad Caliph">Yusuf II Al-Mustansir</a> comes to power in 1213. The battle takes place on the coast of the <a href="/wiki/Rif" title="Rif">Rif</a>. In the reign of Yusuf II Al-Mustansir a great tower is erected to protect the royal palace in Seville.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>1217: <a href="/wiki/Abd_al-Haqq_I" title="Abd al-Haqq I">Abd al-Haqq I</a> dies during victorious combat against the Almohads. His son <a href="/wiki/Uthman_ibn_Abd_al-Haqq" title="Uthman ibn Abd al-Haqq">Uthman ibn Abd al-Haqq</a> (Uthman I) succeeds to the throne. Marinids take possession of the Rif and seem to want to remain there. The Almohades counterattack in vain.</li>
<li>1240: <a href="/wiki/Uthman_ibn_Abd_al-Haqq" title="Uthman ibn Abd al-Haqq">Uthman I</a> is assassinated by one of his Christian slaves. His brother <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Abd_Al-Haqq" title="Muhammad ibn Abd Al-Haqq">Muhammad ibn Abd Al-Haqq</a> (Muhammad I) succeeds him.</li>
<li>1244: Muhammad I is killed by an officer of his own Christian mercenary militia. <a href="/wiki/Abu_Yahya_ibn_Abd_al-Haqq" title="Abu Yahya ibn Abd al-Haqq">Abu Yahya ibn Abd al-Haqq</a>, the third son of Abd Al-Haqq, succeeds him.</li>
<li>1249: Severe repression of anti-Marinid forces in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fes" title="Fes">Fes</a>.</li>
<li>1258: Abu Yahya ibn Abd al-Haqq dies of disease. His uncle, <a href="/wiki/Abu_Yusuf_Yaqub_ibn_Abd_Al-Haqq" title="Abu Yusuf Yaqub ibn Abd Al-Haqq">Abu Yusuf Yaqub ibn Abd Al-Haqq</a>, fourth son of Abd Al-Haqq, succeeds to the throne.</li>
<li>1260: The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">Castilians</a> raid <a href="/wiki/Sal%C3%A9" title="Salé">Salé</a>.</li>
<li>1269: Seizure of <a href="/wiki/Marrakesh" title="Marrakesh">Marrakesh</a> and the end of Almohad domination of the western <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">Maghreb</a>.</li>
<li>1274: The Marinids seize <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sijilmassa" title="Sijilmassa">Sijilmassa</a>.</li>
<li>1276: Founding of <a href="/wiki/Fes_Jdid" title="Fes Jdid">Fes Jdid</a> ("New Fes"), a new city near Fes, which comes to be considered a new district of Fes, in contrast to <a href="/wiki/Fes_el_Bali" title="Fes el Bali">Fes el Bali</a> ("Old Fes").</li>
<li>1286: Abu Yusuf Yaqub ibn Abd Al-Haqq dies of disease in Algeciras after a fourth expedition to the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>. His son <a href="/wiki/Abu_Yaqub_Yusuf_an-Nasr" title="Abu Yaqub Yusuf an-Nasr">Abu Yaqub Yusuf an-Nasr</a> replaces him.</li>
<li>1286: Abu Yaqub Yusuf an-Nasr combats revolts in and around the <a href="/wiki/Draa_River" title="Draa River">Draa River</a> and the province of Marrakesh.</li>
<li>1288: Abu Yaqub Yusuf an-Nasr receives in Fes the envoys of the <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus#The_Emirate_of_Granada" title="Al-Andalus">king of Granada</a>, to whom the town of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cadiz" title="Cadiz">Cadiz</a> is returned.</li>
<li>1291: Construction of the mosque of <a href="/wiki/Taza" title="Taza">Taza</a>, the earliest preserved Marinid building.</li>
<li>1296: Construction of Sidi Boumediene mosque, or Sidi Belhasan, in Tlemcen.</li>
<li>1299: Beginning of Tlemcen's siege by the Marinids, which will last nine years.</li>
<li>1306: Conquest and destruction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taroudannt" title="Taroudannt">Taroudannt</a>.</li>
<li>1307: Abu Yaqub Yusuf an-Nasr is assassinated by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eunuch_(court_official)" title="Eunuch (court official)">eunuch</a> in connection with some obscure matter related to the harem. His son <a href="/wiki/Abu_Thabit_Amir" title="Abu Thabit Amir">Abu Thabit Amir</a> succeeds to the throne.</li>
<li>1308: Abu Thabit dies of disease after only one year in power in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetouan" title="Tetouan">Tetouan</a>, a city which he has just founded. His brother, <a href="/wiki/Abu_al-Rabi_Sulayman" title="Abu al-Rabi Sulayman">Abu al-Rabi Sulayman</a> succeeds him.</li>
<li>1309: Abu al-Rabi Sulayman enters Ceuta.</li>
<li>1310: Abu al-Rabi dies of disease after having repressed a revolt of army officials in Taza. Among them is Gonzalve, chief of the Christian militia. His brother Abu Said Uthman succeeds him to the throne.</li>
<li>1323: Construction of the Attarin's madrassa in Fes.</li>
<li>1325: <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Battuta" title="Ibn Battuta">Ibn Battuta</a> begins his 29-year journey across Africa and Eurasia.</li>
<li>1329: The Marinids defeat the Castilians in Algeciras, establishing a foothold in the south of the Iberian peninsula with the hope of reversing the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a>.</li>
<li>1331: Abu Said Uthman dies. His son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_al-Hasan_%27Ali" title="Abu al-Hasan 'Ali">Abu al-Hasan ibn Uthman</a> succeeds him.</li>
<li>1337: First occupation of Tlemcen.</li>
<li>1340: A combined Portuguese–Castilian army defeats the Marinids in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Rio_Salado" title="Battle of Rio Salado">Battle of Rio Salado</a>, close to <a href="/wiki/Tarifa" title="Tarifa">Tarifa</a>, the southernmost town of the Iberian peninsula. The Marinids return to Africa.</li>
<li>1344: The Castilians take over Algeciras. The Marinids are ejected from Iberia.</li>
<li>1347: Abu al-Hasan ibn Uthman destroys the <a href="/wiki/Hafsid_dynasty" title="Hafsid dynasty">Hafsid dynasty</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a> and restores his authority over all the Maghreb.</li>
<li>1348: Abu al-Hasan dies, his son <a href="/wiki/Abu_Inan_Faris" title="Abu Inan Faris">Abu Inan Faris</a> succeeds him as Marinid ruler.</li>
<li>1348: The <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> and the rebellions of Tlemcen and Tunis mark the beginning of the decline of the Marinids, who are unable to drive back the Portuguese and the Castilians.</li>
<li>1350: Construction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bou_Inania" title="Bou Inania">Bou Inania</a>'s madrassa in <a href="/wiki/Meknes" title="Meknes">Meknes</a>.</li>
<li>1351: Second seizure of Tlemcen.</li>
<li>1357: Defeat of Abu Inan Faris in front of Tlemcen. Construction of another of Bou Inania's madrassas in Fes.</li>
<li>1358 Abu Inan is assassinated by his vizir. A time of confusion starts. Each vizir tries to install weak candidates on the throne.</li>
<li>1358: Abu Zian as-Said Muhammad ibn Faris is named sultan by the vizirs, just after the assassination of Abu Inan. His reign lasts only a few months. Abu Yahya abu Bakr ibn Faris comes to power, but also reigns only a few months.</li>
<li>1359: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_Salim_Ibrahim" title="Abu Salim Ibrahim">Abu Salim Ibrahim</a> is nominated sultan by the vizirs. He is one of the sons of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_al-Hasan_ibn_Uthman" title="Abu al-Hasan ibn Uthman">Abu al-Hasan ibn Uthman</a> and is supported by the king of Castille, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedro_of_Castile" title="Pedro of Castile">Pedro</a>.</li>
<li>1359: Resurgence of the Zianids of Tlemcen.</li>
<li>1361: Abu Umar Tachfin is named the successor to Abu Salim Ibrahim by the vizirs, with the support of the Christian militia. He reigns only a few months.</li>
<li>1361: The period called the "reign of the vizirs" ends.</li>
<li>1362: Muhammad ibn Yaqub assumes power. He is a young son of Abu al-Hasan ibn Uthman, who had taken refuge in Castile.</li>
<li>1366: Muhammad ibn Yaqub is assassinated by his vizir. He is replaced by Abu Faris Abd al-Aziz ibn Ali, one of the sons of Abu al-Hasan ibn Uthman who until this time had been held locked up in the palace of Fes.</li>
<li>1370: Third seizure of Tlemcen.</li>
<li>1372: Abu Faris Abd al-Aziz ibn Ali dies of disease leaving the throne to his very young son Muhammad as-Said, beginning a new period of instability. The vizirs try on several occasions to install a <a href="/wiki/Puppet_state" title="Puppet state">puppet sovereign</a>.</li>
<li>1373: Muhammad as-Said is presented as the heir to his father, Abu Faris Abd al-Aziz ibn Ali, but being only five years old cannot reign, and dies in the same year.</li>
<li>1374: Abu al-Abbas Ahmad, supported by the <a href="/wiki/Nasrid_dynasty" title="Nasrid dynasty">Nasrid</a> princes of Granada, takes power.</li>
<li>1374: Partition of the empire into two kingdoms: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Fes" title="Kingdom of Fes">Kingdom of Fes</a> and the Kingdom of Marrakech.</li>
<li>1384: Abu al-Abbas is temporarily removed by the Nasrids. The Nasrids replace him with Abu Faris Musa ibn Faris, a disabled son of Abu Inan Faris. This ensures a kind of interim during the reign of Abu al-Abbas Ahmad from 1384 to 1386.</li>
<li>1384: Abu Zayd Abd ar-Rahman reigns over the Kingdom of Marrakech from 1384 to 1387 while the Marinid throne is still based in Fes.</li>
<li>1386: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Wathiq,_Marinid_Caliph" title="Al-Wathiq, Marinid Caliph">Al-Wathiq</a> ensures the second part of the interim in the reign of Abu al-Abbas from 1386 to 1387.</li>
<li>1387: Abu Al-Abbas begins to give vizirs more power. Morocco knows six years of peace again, although Abu Al-Abbas benefits from this period to reconquer Tlemcen and <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">Algiers</a>.</li>
<li>1393: Abu Al-Abbas dies. Abu Faris Abd al-Aziz ibn Ahmad is designated as the new sultan. The troubles which follow the sudden death of Abu Al-Abbas in <a href="/wiki/Taza" title="Taza">Taza</a> make it possible for the Christian sovereigns to carry the war into Morocco.</li>
<li>1396: Abu Amir Abdallah succeeds to the throne.</li>
<li>1398: Abu Amir dies. His brother, Abu Said Uthman ibn Ahmad, takes power.</li>
<li>1399: Benefitting from the anarchy within the Marinid kingdom, king <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_Castile" title="Henry III of Castile">Henry III of Castile</a> arrives in Morocco, seizes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetouan" title="Tetouan">Tetouan</a>, massacres half of the population and reduces the rest to slavery.</li>
<li>1415: King <a href="/wiki/John_I_of_Portugal" title="John I of Portugal">John I of Portugal</a> seizes Ceuta. This conquest marks the beginning of overseas European expansion.</li>
<li>1418: Abu Said Uthman <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_Ceuta_(1418)" title="Siege of Ceuta (1418)">besieges Ceuta</a> but is defeated.</li>
<li>1420: Abu Said Uthman dies. He is replaced by his son, Abu Muhammad Abd al-Haqq, who is only one year old.</li>
<li>1437: Failure of a <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tangier_(1437)" title="Battle of Tangier (1437)">Portuguese expedition</a> to <a href="/wiki/Tangier" title="Tangier">Tangier</a>. Many prisoners are taken and the infant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fernando,_the_Saint_Prince" title="Fernando, the Saint Prince">Fernando, the Saint Prince</a> is kept as a hostage. A treaty is made with the Portuguese enabling them to embark if they return Ceuta. Fernando is kept as a hostage to guarantee the execution of this pact. Influenced by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Eugene_IV" title="Pope Eugene IV">Pope Eugene IV</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_of_Portugal" title="Edward of Portugal">Edward of Portugal</a> sacrifices his brother for national trade interests.</li>
<li>1458: King <a href="/wiki/Afonso_V_of_Portugal" title="Afonso V of Portugal">Afonso V of Portugal</a> prepares an army for a crusade against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottomans" title="Ottomans">Ottomans</a> in response to the call of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_II" title="Pope Pius II">Pope Pius II</a>, but he instead uses the army to attack a small port located between <a href="/wiki/Tangier" title="Tangier">Tangier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">Ceuta</a>.</li>
<li>1459: Abu Muhammad Abd Al-Haqq revolts against his own Wattasid vizirs. Only two brothers survive, who will become the first Wattasid sultans in 1472.</li>
<li>1462: Ferdinand IV of Castile takes over <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>.</li>
<li>1465: Abu Muhammad Abd Al-Haqq appoints a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> vizir, Aaron ben Batash, provoking a <a href="/wiki/1465_Moroccan_revolt" title="1465 Moroccan revolt">popular revolt</a>. The sultan dies in the revolt when his throat is cut. The Portuguese king <a href="/wiki/Afonso_V_of_Portugal" title="Afonso V of Portugal">Afonso V</a> finally manages to take Tangier, benefitting from the troubles in Fes.</li>
<li>1472: Abu Abd Allah al-Sheikh Muhammad ibn Yahya, one of the two Wattasid vizirs surviving the 1459 massacre, installs himself in Fes, where he founds the Wattasid dynasty.</li>
</ul>
