<p><b>Infanta Maria Luisa of Spain</b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i>María Luisa</i>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i>Maria Ludovika</i>) (24 November 1745 – 15 May 1792) was Holy Roman Empress, German Queen, Queen of Hungary and Bohemia, Grand Duchess of Tuscany as the spouse of <a href="/wiki/Leopold_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Leopold II, Holy Roman Emperor">Leopold II, Holy Roman Emperor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_Luisa_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Maria Luisa was born a Princess of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples_and_Sicily" title="Kingdom of Naples and Sicily">Naples and Sicily</a>. Her father, the future <a href="/wiki/Charles_III_of_Spain" title="Charles III of Spain">Charles III of Spain</a>, had become King of Naples and Sicily in 1735 after its occupation by the Spanish in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_of_Polish_Succession" title="War of Polish Succession">War of Polish Succession</a>. After her father became King of Spain at the death of her half-uncle, <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_VI_of_Spain" title="Ferdinand VI of Spain">Ferdinand VI of Spain</a>, in 1759, she became known as <i>Infanta Maria Luisa of Spain</i>. She still had the use of the style of <a href="/wiki/Royal_Highness" title="Royal Highness">Royal Highness</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_Luisa_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Maria Luisa was born in <a href="/wiki/Portici" title="Portici">Portici</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Campania" title="Campania">Campania</a>, the site of the summer palace (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reggia_di_Portici" title="Reggia di Portici">Reggia di Portici</a></i>) of her parents, King <a href="/wiki/Charles_III_of_Spain" title="Charles III of Spain">Charles, King of Naples and Sicily</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maria_Amalia_of_Saxony" title="Maria Amalia of Saxony">Maria Amalia of Saxony</a>. She was the fifth daughter, and second surviving child, of her parents. Her father became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Spain" title="King of Spain">King of Spain</a> as Charles III in 1759, and she moved with her family to Spain. Her first cousins included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_XVI" title="Louis XVI">Louis XVI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maria_I_of_Portugal" title="Maria I of Portugal">Maria I of Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Emmanuel_IV_of_Sardinia" title="Charles Emmanuel IV of Sardinia">Charles Emmanuel IV of Sardinia</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grand_Duchess_of_Tuscany">Grand Duchess of Tuscany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_Luisa_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Grand Duchess of Tuscany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Maria Luisa was originally intended to marry the future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Joseph_II" title="Emperor Joseph II">Emperor Joseph II</a>, but this was stopped by the discontent of Louis XV of France, who instead wished for Joseph to marry his granddaughter, Isabella of Parma.</p>
<p>On 16 February 1764 she was married by proxy at <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leopold_I,_Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" title="Leopold I, Grand Duke of Tuscany">Leopold I, Grand Duke of Tuscany</a>, the second son of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Francis_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Francis I, Holy Roman Emperor">Francis I</a> and Empress <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maria_Theresa_of_Austria" title="Maria Theresa of Austria">Maria Theresa of Austria</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heir_Apparent" title="Heir Apparent">heir apparent</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Tuscany" title="Grand Duchy of Tuscany">Grand Duchy of Tuscany</a>. Before her marriage, she was made to renounce her rights to the throne of Spain upon the wish of her father. After her wedding by proxy, she traveled to Austria by way of Barcelona, Genova and Bolzano. The next year, on 5 August, she married him in person at <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck" title="Innsbruck">Innsbruck</a>. Only a few days later, the death of Emperor Francis made Maria Luisa's husband the new Grand Duke of Tuscany, and the newly married couple moved to <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, where they would live for the next twenty-five years. The couple arrived to Florence 13 September 1765. They were settled in the <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Pitti" title="Palazzo Pitti">Palazzo Pitti</a>.</p>
<p>By the time of her wedding, Maria Luisa was described as a blue-eyed beauty with a vivid charm, unpretentious and simple and with a disposition to be generous and kind, and her natural warm friendliness was said to have contrasted to the somewhat cold nature of Leopold. By her strict Catholic upbringing, Maria Luisa was raised to endure any hardship of pregnancy and marriage without complaint, a role she also fulfilled during her marriage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The relationship between Maria Luisa and Leopold has been described as happy, and Maria Luisa as a supporting and loyal wife. She accepted the infidelities of her spouse without complaints: among his most known lovers were <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lady_Anna_Gore_Cowper&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lady Anna Gore Cowper (page does not exist)">Lady Anna Gore Cowper</a>, and another was the ballerina <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Livia_Raimondi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Livia Raimondi (page does not exist)">Livia Raimondi</a>, with whom he had a son, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Luigi_von_Gr%C3%BCn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Luigi von Grün (page does not exist)">Luigi von Grün</a> (1788–1814), and gave her own palace at <a href="/wiki/Piazza_San_Marco" title="Piazza San Marco">Piazza San Marco</a>.</p>
<p>As Grand Duchess of Tuscany, Maria Luisa made herself appreciated already the first year in Florence, during the famine of 1765, when she provided the poor and needing with food and medical aid, and she was referred to as an ideal "model of feminine virtue".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> She was never crowned as Grand Duchess, though she was present at the coronation of Leopold in July 1768. She accompanied her consort and her sister-in-law, Maria Carolina of Austria, at the later's marriage to her brother, the King of Naples: the couple remained there for the summer of 1768. In 1770, she accompanied Leopold on his visit to Vienna. Maria Luisa and Leopold neither enjoyed formal occasions and rarely participated in representation or indeed upheld much of a ceremonial court life at all; while Leopold spent his time with politics and his personal pleasure life, Maria Luisa isolated herself almost completely from high society and devoted herself completely to the upbringing of her children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Maria Luisa and her spouse gave their children a very free upbringing, away from any formal court life, and occasionally took them on trips to the country side and the coast. She remained mostly unknown in the local aristocracy, and restricted her private social life to a very small circle of friends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Holy_Roman_Empress">Holy Roman Empress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_Luisa_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Holy Roman Empress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1790, on the death of Peter Leopold's childless brother, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Joseph II, Holy Roman Emperor">Joseph II</a>, Maria Luisa's husband inherited the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburg" title="Habsburg">Habsburg</a> lands in Central Europe, and was shortly thereafter elected Holy Roman Emperor. Taking the name of Leopold II, the new Emperor moved his family to <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, where Maria Luisa took on the role of imperial consort. Leopold died scarcely two years later, on 1 March 1792. Maria Luisa followed her husband to the grave in less than three months, not living long enough to see her eldest son Francis elected as the last Holy Roman Emperor.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozart" title="Mozart">Mozart</a>'s opera <i><a href="/wiki/La_clemenza_di_Tito" title="La clemenza di Tito">La clemenza di Tito</a></i> was commissioned by the Estates of <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> as part of the festivities that accompanied the <a href="/wiki/Coronation" title="Coronation">coronation</a> of Maria Luisa and her husband Leopold as king and queen of <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> on 6 September 1791. In musical circles, Maria Luisa is famous for her putative denigration of Mozart's opera, which she supposedly dismissed as "<i>una porcheria tedesca</i>" (Italian for "German swinishness"), however no claim that she made this remark pre-dates the publication in 1871 of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Meissner" title="Alfred Meissner">Alfred Meissner</a>'s <i>Rococo-Bilder: nach Aufzeichnungen meines Grossvaters</i>, a collection of stories about cultural and political life in Prague in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Titles_and_Styles">Titles and Styles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_Luisa_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Titles and Styles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b>24 November 1745 – 10 August 1759</b> <i>Her Royal Highness</i> Princess Maria Luisa of Sicily and Naples</li>
<li><b>10 August 1759 – 16 February 1764</b> <i>Her Royal Highness</i> Infanta Maria Luisa of Spain</li>
<li><b>16 February 1764 – 18 August 1765</b> <i>Her Royal Highness</i> Archduchess Maria Luisa of Austria, Princess Royal of Hungary and Bohemia</li>
<li><b>18 August 1765 – 20 February 1790</b> <i>Her Royal Highness</i> The Grand Duchess of Tuscany</li>
<li><b>20 February 1790 – 1 March 1792</b> <i>Her Imperial Majesty</i> The Holy Roman Empress, Archduchess of Austria, Queen of Hungary and Bohemia</li>
<li><b>1 March 1792 – 15 May 1792</b> <i>Her Imperial Majesty</i> The Holy Roman Empress Dowager, Dowager Archduchess of Austria, Queen Dowager of Hungary and Bohemia</li>
</ul>
