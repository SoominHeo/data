<p><b>Marien Ngouabi</b> (or <b>N'Gouabi</b>) (December 31, 1938 – March 18, 1977) was the military President of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Republic of the Congo">Republic of the Congo</a> from January 1, 1969, to March 18, 1977.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marien_Ngouabi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marien_Ngouabi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Marien Ngouabi was born in 1938 at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ombell%C3%A9&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ombellé (page does not exist)">Ombellé</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuvette_Department" title="Cuvette Department">Cuvette Department</a>, in kuyu territory. His family was of humble origins. From 1947 to 1953, he went to primary school in <a href="/wiki/Owando" title="Owando">Owando</a>. In 1953, he went to study to the <i>Ecole des enfants de troupes Général Leclerc</i> in <a href="/wiki/Brazzaville" title="Brazzaville">Brazzaville</a> and in 1957 he was sent to <a href="/wiki/Bouar" title="Bouar">Bouar</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oubangui-Chari" title="Oubangui-Chari">Oubangui-Chari</a> (now the <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a>).</p>
<p>After serving in Cameroun (1958–1960), Marien went to the <i>Ecole Militaire Préparatoire</i> in <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and then to the <i>Ecole Inter-armes</i> at <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_sp%C3%A9ciale_militaire_de_Saint-Cyr" title="École spéciale militaire de Saint-Cyr">Coëtquidan Saint-Cyr</a> in 1961. He returned to Congo in 1962 as Second Lieutenant and was stationed at the <a href="/wiki/Pointe-Noire" title="Pointe-Noire">Pointe-Noire</a> garrison. In 1963 Marien Ngouabi was promoted to Lieutenant.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_to_power">Rise to power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marien_Ngouabi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rise to power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1965 he created the first battalion of <a href="/wiki/Paratrooper" title="Paratrooper">paratroopers</a> in the Congo Republic. Known for his leftist views, in April 1966 Ngouabi was demoted to the rank of soldier second class when he refused to be posted again at Pointe-Noire. President <a href="/wiki/Alphonse_Massamba-D%C3%A9bat" title="Alphonse Massamba-Débat">Alphonse Massamba-Débat</a> had Ngouabi and Second Lieutenant Eyabo arrested on July 29, 1968.</p>
<p>Ngouabi's arrest provoked discontent among the military, and on July 31 Ngouabi was freed by soldiers. The National Revolutionary Council (CNR), headed by Ngouabi, was created on August 5, 1968. Massamba-Débat, whose powers had been curtailed by the CNR, resigned on September 4, and Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Raoul" title="Alfred Raoul">Alfred Raoul</a> served as acting head of state until December 31, 1968, when the CNR formally became the country's supreme authority and Ngouabi, as head of the CNR, assumed the presidency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Head_of_state">Head of state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marien_Ngouabi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Head of state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Once in power, President Ngouabi changed the country's name to the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="People's Republic of the Congo">People's Republic of the Congo</a>, declaring it to be <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>'s first Marxist–Leninist state, and founded the <a href="/wiki/Congolese_Party_of_Labour" title="Congolese Party of Labour">Congolese Workers' Party</a> (<i>Parti Congolais du Travail</i>, PCT) as the country's sole legal political party.</p>
<p>Ngouabi was a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kouyou&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kouyou (page does not exist)">Kouyou</a> from the north and his regime shifted control of the country away from the south. Such moves created opposition among the population in the highly politicized environment of <a href="/wiki/Brazzaville" title="Brazzaville">Brazzaville</a>. There was an attempted coup in February 1972 that triggered a series of 'purges' of the opposition. It is claimed that Ngouabi was under <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> pressure to annex the <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a>-rich <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabinda_(province)" title="Cabinda (province)">Cabinda</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enclave" title="Enclave">enclave</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, a part of <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Angola" title="Portuguese Angola">Portuguese Angola</a>, and his refusal to act cost him French support. There is some speculation that the French financed some of the following attempts to remove Ngouabi. He visited the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> in July 1973.</p>
<p>Ngouabi was re-elected to his post as Chairman of the PCT Central Committee on December 30, 1974; he was additionally elected as Permanent Secretary of the PCT. He was then sworn in as President for another term on January 9, 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Also in 1975, he signed an economic aid pact with the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Assassination">Assassination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marien_Ngouabi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Assassination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On March 18, 1977, President Ngouabi was assassinated by an alleged suicide commando. The persons accused of taking part in the plot were tried and some of them executed including Massamba-Débat.</p>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_Committee_of_the_Congolese_Labour_Party" title="Military Committee of the Congolese Labour Party">Military Committee of the Party</a> (CMP) was named to head an interim government with the conservative Colonel <a href="/wiki/Joachim_Yhombi-Opango" title="Joachim Yhombi-Opango">Joachim Yhombi-Opango</a> to serve as Head of State.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commemoration">Commemoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marien_Ngouabi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Commemoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>March 18 is <b>Marien Ngouabi Day</b> in the Republic of Congo.</p>
<p>The country's only university is the <a href="/wiki/Marien_Ngouabi_University" title="Marien Ngouabi University">Marien Ngouabi University</a> in Brazzaville. Ngouabi is interred at the Marien Ngouabi Mausoleum in Brazzaville.</p>
