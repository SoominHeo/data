<p><i><b>Mario Golf: Toadstool Tour</b></i>, known in Japan as <i><b>Mario Golf: Family Tour</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">マリオゴルフ ファミリーツアー</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Mario Gorufu Famirī Tsuā</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, is a 2003 <a href="/wiki/Sports_game" title="Sports game">sports game</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Camelot_Software_Planning" title="Camelot Software Planning">Camelot Software Planning</a> and published by <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nintendo_GameCube" title="Nintendo GameCube">Nintendo GameCube</a>. It is the sequel to the 1999 <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_64" title="Nintendo 64">Nintendo 64</a> title <i><a href="/wiki/Mario_Golf_(video_game)" title="Mario Golf (video game)">Mario Golf</a></i>, and is the second game in the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Mario_sports_games#Mario_Golf_games" title="List of Mario sports games"><i>Mario Golf</i> series</a>. It was released in North America on July 29, 2003, in Japan on September 5, 2003, and in the PAL region on June 18, 2004.</p>
<p><i>Toadstool Tour</i> is a golf game featuring characters and elements from the <a href="/wiki/Mario_(franchise)" title="Mario (franchise)"><i>Mario</i> series</a>. There are 16 playable characters in total, each with a set of golfing statistics defining their style of play. The game's main mode involves the player competing in tournaments to obtain new features, although there are alternative modes consisting of training session and variations to the golf format. This includes "Ring Attack", requiring the player to hit the ball through rings of varying sizes while remaining on or under par.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurogamer_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eurogamer-1">[1]</a></sup> Players can transfer characters between <i>Mario Golf: Toadstool Tour</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mario_Golf:_Advance_Tour" title="Mario Golf: Advance Tour">Mario Golf: Advance Tour</a></i> using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nintendo_GameCube_Game_Boy_Advance_Cable" title="Nintendo GameCube Game Boy Advance Cable">Nintendo GameCube Game Boy Advance Cable</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameSpy_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpy-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Toadstool Tour</i> received a positive reception from the media. In general, reviewers praised the game's visuals, sound, and variety of courses, although a perceived lack of advancement from its predecessor was criticised. The game became part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Player%27s_Choice" title="Player's Choice">Player's Choice</a> label in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Player.27s_Choice_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Player.27s_Choice-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">Gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mario_Golf:_Toadstool_Tour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mario_Golf_Toadstool_Tour.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="182" data-file-width="260" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f1/Mario_Golf_Toadstool_Tour.jpg/220px-Mario_Golf_Toadstool_Tour.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/f/f1/Mario_Golf_Toadstool_Tour.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mario_Golf_Toadstool_Tour.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mario about to hit the golf ball</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Toadstool Tour</i> is a golf game incorporating characters, enemies, and themes from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mario_series" title="Mario series"><i>Mario</i> series</a>. As a typical <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a> game, the player's objective is to hit the ball into the hole using as few strokes as possible. Prior to the swing, the player chooses a club, a general direction and range for the ball to travel. During the swing, the player determines power by timing a button press for a marker to stop at the desired point on a power meter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-4">[4]</a></sup> At this point, the player can choose to influence the direction of the ball by applying spin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameSpot_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpot-5">[5]</a></sup> Players can alternate between auto and manual shots, with the latter providing the player with more control, albeit at a higher risk of a poor shot. Many of these gameplay aspects, such as spin, are affected by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mario_Golf_(series)#characters" title="Mario Golf (series)">16 characters'</a> individual statistics. These relate to features such as control of the ball and the general height of the character's shot, which determines how much the character's play is affected by the wind. At varying stages in the "Character match" mode, characters have a chance to be upgraded into "Star characters", which grants statistical enhancements. To achieve this, the player must defeat a computer-designated <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> (AI) opponent with a character of their choice, awarding the AI character with the upgrade for use later by the player.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurogamer_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eurogamer-1">[1]</a></sup> The opportunities are only available once an envelope appears beside a character portrait in the selection screen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are seven courses in the game, with the later versions featuring more complex terrain and exotic features. The more advanced courses offer a higher frequency of difficult terrain and elevation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-4">[4]</a></sup> as in <a href="/wiki/Golf_course#Hazards" title="Golf course">bunkers</a>, which limit the accuracy and range of shots. There are also hazards such as lava pits and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Mario_series_enemies#Thwomp" title="List of Mario series enemies">thwomps</a>, which will incur a one-shot penalty if landed on. Each course takes its name from a particular <i>Mario</i> feature, such as "Peach's Castle Grounds", which is themed in particular on the <a href="/wiki/Mushroom_Kingdom" title="Mushroom Kingdom">Mushroom Kingdom</a>. This course includes themed hazards such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Mario_series_enemies#Thwomp" title="List of Mario series enemies">chain chomps</a> in bunkers, as well as warp pipes to change the location of the ball.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurogamer_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Eurogamer-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Toadstool Tour</i> contains several gameplay modes and variants of golf, as well as the traditional <a href="/wiki/Stroke_play" title="Stroke play">stroke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Match_play" title="Match play">match play</a>. The player can choose to play the "Doubles" option in several modes, which allows two players to play alternate strokes as a team. "Coin attack" entails collecting several coins scattered on the course, while "Ring attack" requires the player to direct the ball through rings of varying locations, angles, and diameter while keeping on or under par.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurogamer_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Eurogamer-1">[1]</a></sup> Some side games incorporate personal training for the player, such as the side games, which develops approaching, putting, shots, and birdie skills (see <a href="/wiki/Golf#Types_of_shot" title="Golf">types of shot</a>). The main mode is "Tournament mode", which involves the player competing against <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> opponents on each course to win trophies. The game features interactivity with the <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Advance" title="Game Boy Advance">Game Boy Advance</a>. Players can transfer characters between <i>Mario Golf: Toadstool Tour</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mario_Golf:_Advance_Tour" title="Mario Golf: Advance Tour">Mario Golf: Advance Tour</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameSpy_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpy-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mario_Golf:_Toadstool_Tour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Toadstool Tour</i> was developed by <a href="/wiki/Camelot_Software_Planning" title="Camelot Software Planning">Camelot Software Planning</a>, the same team responsible for the game's predecessor, <i><a href="/wiki/Mario_Golf_(video_game)" title="Mario Golf (video game)">Mario Golf</a></i>. During an interview with two Camelot developers, Hiroyuki and Shugo Takahashi, it was revealed that the game was developed simultaneously with the GameCube <i>Mario Tennis</i> title, <i><a href="/wiki/Mario_Power_Tennis" title="Mario Power Tennis">Mario Power Tennis</a></i>. According to the brothers, ideas and technology used for the development of <i>Toadstool Tour</i> was also used for <i>Power Tennis</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The game was displayed as a playable demonstration in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E3_(Electronic_Entertainment_Expo)" title="E3 (Electronic Entertainment Expo)">E3</a> convention of 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <i>Toadstool Tour</i> became part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Player%27s_Choice" title="Player's Choice">Player's Choice</a> label in 2004, which offers a reduced price to games that have sold more than one million copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Player.27s_Choice_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Player.27s_Choice-3">[3]</a></sup> The music in <i>Toadstool Tour</i> was composed by <a href="/wiki/Motoi_Sakuraba" title="Motoi Sakuraba">Motoi Sakuraba</a>, who has scored several other <i>Mario Golf</i> titles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mario_Golf:_Toadstool_Tour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><i>Toadstool Tour</i> received a positive reaction from critics, although it was criticised for being too similar to its predecessor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurogamer_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Eurogamer-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameSpot_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpot-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eurogamer" title="Eurogamer">Eurogamer</a>'s Tom Bramwell commented that "Mario Golf hasn't really grown much since its time on the N64", despite enjoying the game's course design and "sense of fun".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurogamer_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Eurogamer-1">[1]</a></sup> Both <a href="/wiki/GameSpot" title="GameSpot">GameSpot</a> and <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> praised the game's courses, although the later stages were preferred to the more basic initial courses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameSpot_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpot-5">[5]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Electronic_Gaming_Monthly" title="Electronic Gaming Monthly">Electronic Gaming Monthly</a></i> lauded the game's physics evidenced by the use of wind, weather, and surface conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-egm_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-egm-12">[12]</a></sup> The game was often likened to the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiger_Woods_PGA_Tour" title="Tiger Woods PGA Tour">Tiger Woods PGA Tour</a></i> games, which served as a criticism when Eurogamer noted the absence of events and player progression in the game's single-player mode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurogamer_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Eurogamer-1">[1]</a></sup> This point was shared by Jennifer Tsao of <i>EGM</i>, who wanted "a more compelling single-player mode" that would offer a "golf pro who coaches me based on my swings".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-egm_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-egm-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The game's controls were generally well received, despite specific issues such as difficulties executing very short putts due to the game's power meter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameSpy_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpy-2">[2]</a></sup> The accessibility of the controls in particular were lauded, although Camelot's choice not to use the analogue swing present in many golf games was a common complaint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurogamer_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Eurogamer-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-4">[4]</a></sup> This specifically was compared to the analogue system present in <i>Tiger Woods</i> games, prompting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matt_Casamassina" title="Matt Casamassina">Matt Casamassina</a> to comment "going from Tiger Woods back to the mechanics of Mario Golf feels like going from car to horse".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-4">[4]</a></sup> The variety of modes available in <i>Toadstool Tour</i> was rated as "amazing" by GameSpot, who proceeded to commend the clarification provided by the game's manual booklet and in-game tutorial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameSpot_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpot-5">[5]</a></sup> Eurogamer also noted this by offering praise to "Ring attack", but conversely rated "Coin attack" as "a bit shallow".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurogamer_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Eurogamer-1">[1]</a></sup> The game's multiplayer offerings were highly regarded by most reviewers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameSpy_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpy-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many reviewers criticised <i>Toadstool Tour<span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span></i>s use of camera, especially when the ball's presented route would ignore obstacles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameSpy_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpy-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-4">[4]</a></sup> GameSpot otherwise welcomed the game's visuals, however, arguing that the recurring <i>Mario</i> characters "never looked better".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameSpot_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpot-5">[5]</a></sup> IGN also lauded the graphics present in the <a href="/wiki/Full_motion_video" title="Full motion video">full motion video</a> and the rest of the game, as well as <i>Toadstool Tour</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s audio. Matt Casamassina acknowledged the characters' voiced taunts, saying "the muttering, complaining Waluigi, never fails to bring a smile to our faces".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alex Trickett of <a href="/wiki/BBC_Sport" title="BBC Sport">BBC Sport</a> gave it 85% and stated, "If you like pure simulation stick with world number one <a href="/wiki/Tiger_Woods" title="Tiger Woods">Tiger Woods</a>, but if you are ready for a wackier round of golf, let your favourite Italian plumber come to the fore."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-19">[19]</a></sup> Likewise, Marc Saltzman of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cincinnati_Enquirer" title="The Cincinnati Enquirer">The Cincinnati Enquirer</a></i> gave it four stars out of five and stated that, "One of the game's greatest assets is its intuitive control scheme. Novice and seasoned players will be able to pick up and play with little trouble. There are customizable options for manual or automatic swing modes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cincinnati_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cincinnati-20">[20]</a></sup> However, the only negative review came from Alex Porter of <i><a href="/wiki/Maxim_(magazine)" title="Maxim (magazine)">Maxim</a></i>, who gave it a score of four out of ten, saying, "Golfing game standards like club selection, power meter, and control of backspins and topspins satisfy, but the sometimes-clunky controls and confusing camera perspectives will leave you teed-off."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Toadstool Tour</i> sold 1.03 million units in North America as of December 27, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
