<p><b>Marillion</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> are a British <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band, formed in <a href="/wiki/Aylesbury" title="Aylesbury">Aylesbury</a>, Buckinghamshire, England, in 1979. They emerged from the <a href="/wiki/Post-punk" title="Post-punk">post-punk</a> music scene in Britain and existed as a bridge between the styles of <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a> and classic <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive rock</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-romano_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-romano-4">[4]</a></sup> becoming the most commercially successful <a href="/wiki/Neo-progressive_rock" title="Neo-progressive rock">neo-progressive rock</a> band of the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Marillion's recorded studio output since 1982 is composed of eighteen albums, generally regarded in two distinct eras, delineated by the departure of original frontman <a href="/wiki/Fish_(singer)" title="Fish (singer)">Fish</a> in late 1988 and the subsequent arrival of replacement <a href="/wiki/Steve_Hogarth" title="Steve Hogarth">Steve Hogarth</a> in early 1989. The band achieved eight Top Ten UK albums between 1983 and 1994, including a <a href="/wiki/List_of_UK_Albums_Chart_number_ones_of_the_1980s" title="List of UK Albums Chart number ones of the 1980s">number one</a> album in 1985 with <i><a href="/wiki/Misplaced_Childhood" title="Misplaced Childhood">Misplaced Childhood</a></i>, and during the period the band were fronted by Fish they scored eleven Top 40 hits on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>. They are best known for the 1985 singles "<a href="/wiki/Kayleigh" title="Kayleigh">Kayleigh</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Lavender_(Marillion_song)" title="Lavender (Marillion song)">Lavender</a>", which reached number two and number five respectively.</p>
<p>Marillion's first album released with Hogarth, 1989's <i><a href="/wiki/Seasons_End" title="Seasons End">Seasons End</a></i>, was another Top Ten hit, and albums continued to chart well until their departure from <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a> following the release of their 1996 live album <i><a href="/wiki/Made_Again" title="Made Again">Made Again</a></i> and the dissipation of the band's mainstream popularity in the late 1990s; save for a resurgence in the mid- to late-2000s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> they have essentially been a <a href="/wiki/Cult_following" title="Cult following">cult act</a> since then.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Marillion have achieved a further twelve Top 40 hit singles in the UK with Hogarth, including 2004's "<a href="/wiki/You%27re_Gone_(Marillion_song)" title="You're Gone (Marillion song)">You're Gone</a>", which charted at No. 7 and is the biggest hit of his tenure. The band continue to tour internationally, and were ranked 38th in <a href="/wiki/Classic_Rock_(magazine)" title="Classic Rock (magazine)"><i>Classic Rock</i></a>'s "50 Best Live Acts of All Time" in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2016, they returned to the UK Albums Chart Top Ten for the first time in 22 years with their highest chart placing since 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite much <a href="#Marillion_in_the_media">antipathy towards them in the mainstream media</a> and a consistently unfashionable status within the British music industry, Marillion have maintained an extremely loyal international fanbase, as well as being widely acknowledged as playing a pioneering role in the development of <a href="/wiki/Crowdfunding" title="Crowdfunding">crowdfunding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fan-funded_music" title="Fan-funded music">fan-funded music</a>. They have sold more than 15 million albums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Fish_era">The Fish era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Fish era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_early_years_.281979.E2.80.931982.29">Formation and early years (1979–1982)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formation and early years (1979–1982)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Marillion was formed in 1979 as <i>Silmarillion</i>, after <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J.R.R. Tolkien</a>'s book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Silmarillion" title="The Silmarillion">The Silmarillion</a></i>, by Mick Pointer, Steve Rothery, and others. They played their first gig at <a href="/wiki/Berkhamsted" title="Berkhamsted">Berkhamsted</a> Civic Centre, <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a>, on 1 March 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The band name was shortened to <i>Marillion</i> in 1981 to avoid potential copyright conflicts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> at the same time as Fish and bassist William 'Diz' Minnitt<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> replaced original bassist/vocalist Doug 'Rastus' Irvine following an audition at Leyland Farm Studios in Buckinghamshire on 2 January 1981. Rothery, drummer Mick Pointer, and keyboardist Brian Jelliman completed this line-up; the first gig with this line-up was at the Red Lion Pub in <a href="/wiki/Bicester" title="Bicester">Bicester</a>, Oxfordshire, on 14 March 1981. By the end of 1981, Kelly had replaced Jelliman, with Trewavas replacing Minnitt in 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Minnitt later formed Pride of Passion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The early works of Marillion contained Fish's poetic, fantastical and introspective lyrics melded with music to create a sound that reflected the band's influences. Marillion's first recordings were two demos recorded in March and the summer of 1980, prior to Fish and Minnitt joining the band. Two versions of the Spring demo circulate amongst collectors; the first has four tracks; "The Haunting of Gill House", "Herne the Hunter", an untitled track known as "Scott's Porridge", and "Alice". The second version has an instrumental version of "Alice" in place of "Scott's Porridge". All tracks are instrumental apart from "Alice", with vocals by Doug Irvine. The summer demo has three tracks; "Close" (parts of which were later rewritten into "The Web", "He Knows You Know" and "Chelsea Monday"), "Lady Fantasy" (an original based on an earlier Electric Gypsy song), and another version of "Alice". Both were recorded at The Enid's studio in Hertfordshire. Following Irvine's departure and replacement by Fish and Minnitt, the band recorded another demo tape, produced by Les Payne, in July 1981 that included early versions of "He Knows You Know", "Garden Party", and "Charting the Single".</p>
<p>The group attracted attention with a three-track session for the <i><a href="/wiki/Friday_Rock_Show" title="Friday Rock Show">Friday Rock Show</a></i> (early versions of "The Web", "Three Boats Down from The Candy", and "Forgotten Sons") and were subsequently signed by <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a>. They released their first single, "<a href="/wiki/Market_Square_Heroes" title="Market Square Heroes">Market Square Heroes</a>", in 1982, with the epic song "Grendel" on the B-side of the 12" version. Following the single, the band released their first full-length album in 1983.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Script_for_a_Jester.27s_Tear_and_Fugazi_.281983.E2.80.931984.29"><i>Script for a Jester's Tear</i> and <i>Fugazi</i> (1983–1984)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Script for a Jester's Tear and Fugazi (1983–1984)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The music on their debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Script_for_a_Jester%27s_Tear" title="Script for a Jester's Tear">Script for a Jester's Tear</a></i>, was born out of the intensive gigging of the previous years. Although it had some progressive rock stylings, it also had a darker edge, suggested by the bedsit squalour on the album's cover. The album was a commercial success, peaking at number seven on the UK album chart and producing the Top 40 singles "<a href="/wiki/He_Knows_You_Know" title="He Knows You Know">He Knows You Know</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Garden_Party_(Marillion_song)" title="Garden Party (Marillion song)">Garden Party</a>". Although they were accused of being <a href="/wiki/Genesis_(band)" title="Genesis (band)">Genesis</a> soundalikes, the album reached the <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">Platinum certification</a> and has been credited with giving a second life to progressive rock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the UK tour to promote <i>Script for a Jester's Tear</i>, Mick Pointer was dismissed due to Fish's dissatisfaction with what he later described as the drummer's "awful" timing and failure to develop as a musician with the rest of the band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Former <a href="/wiki/Steve_Hackett" title="Steve Hackett">Steve Hackett</a> drummer <a href="/wiki/Ian_Mosley" title="Ian Mosley">Ian Mosley</a> was eventually secured as Pointer's replacement after a series of other drummers, including <a href="/wiki/Andy_Ward_(musician)" title="Andy Ward (musician)">Andy Ward</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Mover" title="Jonathan Mover">Jonathan Mover</a>, proved short-lived and less than satisfactory. Despite the numerous production problems encountered during this period, the second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Fugazi_(album)" title="Fugazi (album)">Fugazi</a></i>, built upon the success of the first album with a more streamlined <a href="/wiki/Hard_rock" title="Hard rock">hard rock</a> sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It improved on the chart placing of its predecessor by reaching the top five and produced the top 30 singles "<a href="/wiki/Punch_and_Judy_(song)" title="Punch and Judy (song)">Punch and Judy</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Assassing" title="Assassing">Assassing</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-website_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-website-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 1984, Marillion then released their first live album, <i><a href="/wiki/Real_to_Reel_(Marillion_album)" title="Real to Reel (Marillion album)">Real to Reel</a></i>, featuring songs from <i>Fugazi</i> and <i>Script for a Jester's Tear</i>, as well as "Cinderella Search" (B-side to 'Assassing') and the debut single "Market Square Heroes", which had not been available on album until that point. The album entered the UK album charts at No. 8.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Misplaced_Childhood_and_international_success_.281985-1986.29"><i>Misplaced Childhood</i> and international success (1985-1986)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Misplaced Childhood and international success (1985-1986)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Their third and commercially most successful studio album was <i><a href="/wiki/Misplaced_Childhood" title="Misplaced Childhood">Misplaced Childhood</a></i>. With the blessing of their record company, the band was free to depart stylistically from their previous albums, in the process developing a more mainstream sound. The lead single from the album, "<a href="/wiki/Kayleigh" title="Kayleigh">Kayleigh</a>", received major promotion by EMI and gained heavy rotation on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">BBC Radio 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Independent_Local_Radio" title="Independent Local Radio">Independent Local Radio</a> stations as well as television appearances, bringing the band to the attention of a much wider audience. The band were able to showcase their ability to juxtapose easily accessible rock <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballads" title="Ballads">ballads</a> ("Kayleigh", charting at No. 2 in the United Kingdom, behind charity fundraiser "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/You%27ll_Never_Walk_Alone_(song)" title="You'll Never Walk Alone (song)">You'll Never Walk Alone</a>" by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Crowd_(music)" title="The Crowd (music)">The Crowd</a>, and "<a href="/wiki/Lavender_(Marillion_song)" title="Lavender (Marillion song)">Lavender</a>", which charted at No. 5) with darker subject matter on the album such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drug_abuse" title="Drug abuse">drug abuse</a> ("Blind Curve"), alcoholism ("Waterhole") and prostitution ("Bitter Suite"), ultimately leading to rebirth and redemption, all inspired by Fish's life experiences. Following the exposure given to "Kayleigh" and its subsequent chart success, the album went to <a href="/wiki/List_of_UK_Albums_Chart_number_ones_of_the_1980s" title="List of UK Albums Chart number ones of the 1980s">No. 1</a> in the United Kingdom, knocking <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Ferry" title="Bryan Ferry">Bryan Ferry</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Boys_and_Girls_(album)" title="Boys and Girls (album)">Boys and Girls</a></i> off the top spot and holding off a challenge from <a href="/wiki/Sting_(musician)" title="Sting (musician)">Sting</a>, who released his first solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dream_of_the_Blue_Turtles" title="The Dream of the Blue Turtles">The Dream of the Blue Turtles</a></i>, in the same week. The third single from the album, "<a href="/wiki/Heart_of_Lothian" title="Heart of Lothian">Heart of Lothian</a>", became another top-thirty hit for the band, reaching No. 29. The album came sixth in <i><a href="/wiki/Kerrang!" title="Kerrang!">Kerrang!</a></i> magazine's "Albums of the Year" in 1985. "Kayleigh" also gave Marillion its sole entry on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>, reaching No. 74. In the summer of 1986, the band played to their biggest ever audience as special guests to <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">Queen</a> at a festival in Germany attended by a crowd of over 150,000 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The band were also offered the <i><a href="/wiki/Highlander_(film)" title="Highlander (film)">Highlander</a></i> soundtrack but turned it down because of their world tour, a missed opportunity which Rothery later claimed to regret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Clutching_at_Straws_and_the_departure_of_Fish_.281987.E2.80.931988.29"><i>Clutching at Straws</i> and the departure of Fish (1987–1988)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Clutching at Straws and the departure of Fish (1987–1988)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The fourth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Clutching_at_Straws" title="Clutching at Straws">Clutching at Straws</a></i>, shed some of its predecessor's pop stylings and retreated into a darker exploration of excess, alcoholism, and life on the road, representing the strains of constant touring that would result in the departure of Fish to pursue a solo career. It did continue the group's commercial success, however; lead single "<a href="/wiki/Incommunicado_(song)" title="Incommunicado (song)">Incommunicado</a>" charted at No. 6 in the UK charts gaining the band an appearance on <i><a href="/wiki/Top_of_the_Pops" title="Top of the Pops">Top of the Pops</a></i>, and the album entered the UK album chart at No. 2, Marillion's second highest placing. "<a href="/wiki/Sugar_Mice" title="Sugar Mice">Sugar Mice</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Warm_Wet_Circles" title="Warm Wet Circles">Warm Wet Circles</a>" also became hit singles, both reaching No. 22. Fish has also stated in interviews since that he believes this was the best album he made with the band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The album came sixth in <i><a href="/wiki/Kerrang!" title="Kerrang!">Kerrang!</a></i> magazine's "Albums of the Year" in 1987, equalling the ranking given to <i>Misplaced Childhood</i>. It was also included in <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(magazine)" title="Q (magazine)">Q</a></i> magazine's "50 Best Recordings of the Year".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Fish explained his reasons for leaving in an interview in 2003:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"By 1987 we were over-playing live because the manager was on 20 per cent of the gross. He was making a fantastic amount of money while we were working our asses off. Then I found a bit of paper proposing an American tour. At the end of the day the band would have needed a £14,000 loan from EMI as tour support to do it. That was when I knew that, if I stayed with the band, I'd probably end up a raging alcoholic and be found overdosed and dying in a big house in Oxford with Irish wolfhounds at the bottom of my bed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Fish gave the band a choice to continue with either him or the manager. The band sided with the manager and Fish left for a solo career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> His last live performance with the band was at <a href="/wiki/Craigtoun_Country_Park" title="Craigtoun Country Park">Craigtoun Country Park</a> on 23 July 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Due to lengthy legal battles, informal contact between Fish and the other four band members apparently did not resume until 1999. Fish would later disclose in the liner notes to the 2-CD reissue of <i>Clutching at Straws</i> that he and his former bandmates had met up and discussed the demise of the band and renewed their friendship, and had come to the consensus that an excessive touring schedule and too much pressure from the band's management led to the rift.</p>
<p>Although reportedly now on good personal terms, both camps had always made it very clear that the oft-speculated-upon reunion would never happen. However, when Fish headlined the 'Hobble on the Cobbles' free concert in Aylesbury's Market Square on 26 August 2007, the attraction of playing their debut single in its spiritual home proved strong enough to overcome any lingering bad feeling between the former band members, and Kelly, Mosley, Rothery, and Trewavas replaced Fish's backing band for an emotional encore of "Market Square Heroes".</p>
<p>In a press interview following the event, Fish denied this would lead to a full reunion, saying that: "<a href="/wiki/Steve_Hogarth" title="Steve Hogarth">Hogarth</a> does a great job with the band. We forged different paths over the 19 years."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Steve_Hogarth_era">The Steve Hogarth era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Steve Hogarth era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Seasons_End_and_Holidays_in_Eden_.281989.E2.80.931991.29"><i>Seasons End</i> and <i>Holidays in Eden</i> (1989–1991)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Seasons End and Holidays in Eden (1989–1991)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>After the split, the band found Steve Hogarth, the former keyboardist and sometime vocalist of <a href="/wiki/The_Europeans_(band)" title="The Europeans (band)">The Europeans</a>. Hogarth stepped into a difficult situation, as the band had already recorded some demos of the next studio album, which eventually would have become <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seasons_End_(album)" title="Seasons End (album)">Seasons End</a></i>. Hogarth was a significant contrast with Fish, coming from a <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a> musical background instead of progressive rock. He had also never owned a Marillion album before joining the band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ling2001_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ling2001-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Fish left the group (taking his lyrics with him), Hogarth set to work crafting new lyrics to existing songs with lyricist and author <a href="/wiki/John_Helmer_(musician)" title="John Helmer (musician)">John Helmer</a>. The demo sessions of the songs from <i>Seasons End</i> with Fish vocals and lyrics can be found on the bonus disc of the remastered version of <i>Clutching at Straws</i>, while the lyrics found their way into various Fish solo albums such as his first solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Vigil_in_a_Wilderness_of_Mirrors" title="Vigil in a Wilderness of Mirrors">Vigil in a Wilderness of Mirrors</a></i>, some snippets on his second, <i><a href="/wiki/Internal_Exile_(Fish_album)" title="Internal Exile (Fish album)">Internal Exile</a></i> and even a line or two found its way to his third album, <i><a href="/wiki/Suits_(album)" title="Suits (album)">Suits</a></i>.</p>
<p>Hogarth's second album with the band, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holidays_in_Eden_(Album)" title="Holidays in Eden (Album)">Holidays in Eden</a></i>, was the first he wrote in partnership with the band, and includes the song "Dry Land" which Hogarth had written and recorded in a previous project with the band <a href="/wiki/How_We_Live" title="How We Live">How We Live</a>. As quoted from Steve Hogarth, "<i>Holidays in Eden</i> was to become Marillion's "pop"est album ever, and was greeted with delight by many, and dismay by some of the hardcore fans".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Despite its pop stylings, the album failed to crossover beyond the band's existing fanbase and produced no major hit singles.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Brave.2C_Afraid_of_Sunlight_and_split_with_EMI_.281992.E2.80.931995.29"><i>Brave</i>, <i>Afraid of Sunlight</i> and split with EMI (1992–1995)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Brave, Afraid of Sunlight and split with EMI (1992–1995)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i>Holidays in Eden</i> was followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Brave_(Marillion_album)" title="Brave (Marillion album)">Brave</a></i>, a dark and richly complex <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a> that took the band 18 months to release. The album also marked the start of the band's longtime relationship with producer <a href="/wiki/Dave_Meegan" title="Dave Meegan">Dave Meegan</a>. An independent film based on the album, which featured the band, was also released.</p>
<p>The next album, <i><a href="/wiki/Afraid_of_Sunlight" title="Afraid of Sunlight">Afraid of Sunlight</a></i>, would be the band's last album with record label EMI. Once again, it received little promotion, no mainstream radio airplay and its sales were disappointing for the band. Despite this, it was one of their most critically acclaimed albums and was included in <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(magazine)" title="Q (magazine)">Q</a>'</i>s 50 Best Albums of 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> One track of note on the album is <i>Out of This World</i>, a song about <a href="/wiki/Donald_Campbell" title="Donald Campbell">Donald Campbell</a>, who died while trying to set a speed record on water. The song inspired an effort to recover both Campbell's body and the "Bluebird K7," the boat which Campbell crashed in, from the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The recovery was finally undertaken in 2001, and both Steve Hogarth and Steve Rothery were invited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In 1998, Steve Hogarth claimed this was the best album he had made with the band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="This_Strange_Engine.2C_Radiation_and_marillion.com_.281996.E2.80.931999.29"><i>This Strange Engine</i>, <i>Radiation</i> and <i>marillion.com</i> (1996–1999)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: This Strange Engine, Radiation and marillion.com (1996–1999)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>What followed was a string of albums and events that saw Marillion struggling to find their place in the music business. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/This_Strange_Engine_(Album)" title="This Strange Engine (Album)">This Strange Engine</a></i> was released in 1997 with little promotion from their new label <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castle_Records" title="Castle Records">Castle Records</a>, and the band could not afford to make tour stops in the United States. Luckily, their dedicated US fan base decided to solve the problem by raising some $60,000 themselves online to give to the band to come to the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The band's loyal fanbase (combined with the Internet) would eventually become vital to the band's existence.</p>
<p>The band's tenth album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_(Marillion_album)" title="Radiation (Marillion album)">Radiation</a></i> saw the band taking a different approach and was received by fans with mixed reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Marillion.com" title="Marillion.com">marillion.com</a></i> was released the following year and showed some progression in the new direction. The band were still unhappy with their record label situation.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Anoraknophobia_and_Marbles_.282000.E2.80.932006.29"><i>Anoraknophobia</i> and <i>Marbles</i> (2000–2006)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Anoraknophobia and Marbles (2000–2006)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The band decided that they would try a radical experiment by asking their fans if they would help fund the recording of the next album by pre-ordering it before recording even started. The result was over 12,000 pre-orders which raised enough money to record and release <i><a href="/wiki/Anoraknophobia" title="Anoraknophobia">Anoraknophobia</a></i> in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The band was able to strike a deal with EMI to also help distribute the album. This allowed Marillion to retain all the rights to their music while enjoying commercial distribution. By this time the band had also parted company with their long-time manager, saving 20 per cent of the band's income.</p>
<p>The success of <i>Anoraknophobia</i> allowed the band to start recording their next album, but they decided to leverage their fanbase once again to help raise money towards marketing and promotion of a new album. The band put up the album for pre-order in mid-production. This time fans responded by pre-ordering 18,000 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Marbles_(album)" title="Marbles (album)">Marbles</a></i> was released in 2004 with a 2-CD version that is only available at Marillion's website – kind of a 'thank-you' gesture to the over 18,000 fans who pre-ordered it, and as even a further thanks to the fans, their names were credited in the sleeve notes (this 'thank you' to the fans also occurred with the previous album, Anoraknophobia).</p>

<p>The band's management organised the biggest promotional schedule since they had left EMI and Steve Hogarth secured interviews with prominent broadcasters on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio" title="BBC Radio">BBC Radio</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Wright_(television_presenter)" title="Matthew Wright (television presenter)">Matthew Wright</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Harris_(radio)" title="Bob Harris (radio)">Bob Harris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Maconie" title="Stuart Maconie">Stuart Maconie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Simon_Mayo" title="Simon Mayo">Simon Mayo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Lawson" title="Mark Lawson">Mark Lawson</a>. <i>Marbles</i> also became the band's most critically acclaimed album since <i>Afraid of Sunlight</i>, prompting many positive reviews in the press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jon_Hotten_2004_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jon_Hotten_2004-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The band released "<a href="/wiki/You%27re_Gone_(Marillion_song)" title="You're Gone (Marillion song)">You're Gone</a>" as the lead single from the album. Aware that it was unlikely to gain much mainstream radio airplay, the band released the single in three separate formats and encouraged fans to buy a copy of each to get the single into the UK Top Ten. The single reached No. 7, making it the first Marillion song to reach the UK Top Ten since "<a href="/wiki/Incommunicado_(song)" title="Incommunicado (song)">Incommunicado</a>" in 1987 and the band's first Top 40 entry since "Beautiful" in 1995. The second single from the album, "Don't Hurt Yourself", reached No. 16. Following this, they released a download-only single, "The Damage (live)", recorded at the band's sell-out gig at the London Astoria. All of this succeeded in putting the band back in the public consciousness, making the campaign a success. Marillion continued to tour throughout 2005 playing several summer festivals and embarking on acoustic tours of both Europe and the United States, followed up by the "Not Quite Christmas Tour" of Europe throughout the end of 2005.</p>
<p>A new DVD, <i>Colours and Sound</i>, was released in Feb 2006, documenting the creation, promotion, release, and subsequent European tour in support of the album <i>Marbles</i>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Somewhere_Else_and_Happiness_is_the_Road_.282007.E2.80.932008.29"><i>Somewhere Else</i> and <i>Happiness is the Road</i> (2007–2008)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Somewhere Else and Happiness is the Road (2007–2008)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>April 2007 saw Marillion release their fourteenth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Somewhere_Else_(Marillion_album)" title="Somewhere Else (Marillion album)">Somewhere Else</a></i>, their first album in 10 years to make the UK Top No. 30. The success of the album was further underscored by that of the download-only single "See it Like a Baby", making UK No. 45 (March 2007) and the traditional CD release of "<a href="/wiki/Thankyou_Whoever_You_Are" title="Thankyou Whoever You Are">Thankyou Whoever You Are</a> / Most Toys", which made UK No. 15 and No. 6 in the Netherlands during June 2007.</p>
<p>In July 2008 the band posted a contest for fans to create a music video for the soon-to-be released single "Whatever is Wrong with You", and post it on YouTube. The winner would win £5,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Happiness_Is_the_Road" title="Happiness Is the Road">Happiness Is the Road</a></i>, released in October 2008, again featured a pre-order "deluxe edition" with a list of the fans who bought in advance, and a more straightforward regular release. It is another double album, with one disc based on a concept and the second containing the other songs that aren't a part of the theme. Before the album's release, on 9 September 2008, Marillion pre-released their album via p2p networks themselves. Upon attempting to play the downloaded files, users were shown a video from the band explaining why they had taken this route. Downloaders were then able to opt to purchase the album at a user-defined price or select to receive DRM-free files for free, in exchange for an email address. The band explained that although they do not support piracy, they realised their music would inevitably be distributed online anyway, and wanted to attempt to engage with p2p users and make the best of the situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Less_is_More_and_Sounds_That_Can.27t_Be_Made_.282009.E2.80.932014.29"><i>Less is More</i> and <i>Sounds That Can't Be Made</i> (2009–2014)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Less is More and Sounds That Can't Be Made (2009–2014)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The band's sixteenth studio album (released 2 October 2009) was an acoustic album featuring new arrangements of previously released tracks (except one, the new track: 'It's Not Your Fault') entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Less_Is_More_(album)" title="Less Is More (album)">Less Is More</a></i>.</p>
<p>Their seventeenth studio album is titled <i><a href="/wiki/Sounds_That_Can%27t_Be_Made" title="Sounds That Can't Be Made">Sounds That Can't Be Made</a></i> and was released in September 2012. Two versions of the album were released: A 2-disc 'deluxe' version that included a DVD with 'making-of' features and sound-check recordings and a single CD jewel case version. The 'deluxe' version also included a 128-page book that incorporated lyrics, artwork and, as was the case with <i><a href="/wiki/Anoraknophobia" title="Anoraknophobia">Anoraknophobia</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marbles" title="Marbles">Marbles</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Happiness_is_the_Road" title="Happiness is the Road">Happiness is the Road</a></i>, the names of people who pre-ordered the album. Parts of the album were recorded at Peter Gabriel's <a href="/wiki/Real_World_Studios" title="Real World Studios">Real World Studios</a> in 2011.</p>
<p>Marillion were awarded "Band of the Year" at the annual <a href="/wiki/Prog_(magazine)" title="Prog (magazine)">Progressive Music Awards</a> in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuck_Everyone_and_Run_.28F_E_A_R.29_.282015.E2.80.93present.29"><i>Fuck Everyone and Run (F E A R)</i> (2015–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Fuck Everyone and Run (F E A R) (2015–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The band announced in September 2015 that they were working on a new album, provisionally titled <i>M18</i> and later confirmed as <i><a href="/wiki/Fuck_Everyone_and_Run_(F_E_A_R)" title="Fuck Everyone and Run (F E A R)">Fuck Everyone and Run (F E A R)</a></i>. As with several of their previous releases, the recording of the album was to be funded by fan pre-orders, this time through <a href="/wiki/Direct-to-fan" title="Direct-to-fan">direct-to-fan</a> website <a href="/wiki/PledgeMusic" title="PledgeMusic">PledgeMusic</a>. The album was released on 23 September 2016 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-album18_release_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-album18_release-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pledgemusic_campaign_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-pledgemusic_campaign-50">[50]</a></sup> debuting at number 4 in the official UK charts of 30 September 2016, their highest placing since <i><a href="/wiki/Clutching_at_Straws" title="Clutching at Straws">Clutching at Straws</a></i> nearly three decades earlier. In November 2016, they announced their first ever show at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Albert_Hall" title="Royal Albert Hall">Royal Albert Hall</a> in London, scheduled for October 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Line-up.2C_influences_and_sound_changes">Line-up, influences and sound changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Line-up, influences and sound changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Marillion's music has changed stylistically throughout their career. The band themselves stated that each new album tends to represent a reaction to the preceding one, and for this reason their output is difficult to 'pigeonhole'. Although the band has featured two very distinct and different vocalists, the core instrumental line-up<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Steve_Rothery" title="Steve Rothery">Steve Rothery</a> (lead guitar, and the sole 'pre-Fish' original member), <a href="/wiki/Pete_Trewavas" title="Pete Trewavas">Pete Trewavas</a> (bass), <a href="/wiki/Mark_Kelly_(keyboardist)" title="Mark Kelly (keyboardist)">Mark Kelly</a> (keyboards) and <a href="/wiki/Ian_Mosley" title="Ian Mosley">Ian Mosley</a> (drums) has been unchanged since 1984.</p>
<p>Their 1980s sound (with <a href="/wiki/Fish_(singer)" title="Fish (singer)">Fish</a> on vocals) was guitar and keyboard led neo-progressive rock. They have been described as "a bridge between <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk</a> and classic <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive rock</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-romano_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-romano-4">[4]</a></sup> They were often compared unfavourably by critics during this period with the <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gabriel" title="Peter Gabriel">Peter Gabriel</a> era of <a href="/wiki/Genesis_(band)" title="Genesis (band)">Genesis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> although the band had many other influences. Fish was influenced by a wide range of artists and his favourite albums were by artists such as <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Graaf_Generator" title="Van der Graaf Generator">Van der Graaf Generator</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joni_Mitchell" title="Joni Mitchell">Joni Mitchell</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">the Who</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Martyn" title="John Martyn">John Martyn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yes_(band)" title="Yes (band)">Yes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lowell_George" title="Lowell George">Lowell George</a>, <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">Led Zeppelin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roy_Harper" title="Roy Harper">Roy Harper</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Faces_(band)" title="Faces (band)">Faces</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Supertramp" title="Supertramp">Supertramp</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Rothery's main influences were <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Santana" title="Carlos Santana">Carlos Santana</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Gilmour" title="David Gilmour">David Gilmour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Latimer" title="Andrew Latimer">Andrew Latimer</a> of <a href="/wiki/Camel_(band)" title="Camel (band)">Camel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Hackett" title="Steve Hackett">Steve Hackett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Beck" title="Jeff Beck">Jeff Beck</a> and Joni Mitchell, with <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Giltrap" title="Gordon Giltrap">Gordon Giltrap</a> also an early influence on the development of his playing style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steve_Rothery_Interview_All_Access_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steve_Rothery_Interview_All_Access-56">[56]</a></sup> Kelly's biggest inspiration was Yes keyboardist <a href="/wiki/Rick_Wakeman" title="Rick Wakeman">Rick Wakeman</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> and Trewavas' favourite bass player was <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Original drummer <a href="/wiki/Mick_Pointer" title="Mick Pointer">Mick Pointer</a> was a huge fan of <a href="/wiki/Neil_Peart" title="Neil Peart">Neil Peart</a>'s drumming in his favourite band, <a href="/wiki/Rush_(band)" title="Rush (band)">Rush</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the Steve Hogarth era, their sound has been compared, on various albums, to more contemporary acts such as <a href="/wiki/U2" title="U2">U2</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Radiohead" title="Radiohead">Radiohead</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalt2007_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dalt2007-61">[61]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Coldplay" title="Coldplay">Coldplay</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalt2007_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dalt2007-61">[61]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Muse_(band)" title="Muse (band)">Muse</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalt2007_61-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dalt2007-61">[61]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Talk_Talk" title="Talk Talk">Talk Talk</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirkley_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-kirkley-62">[62]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Elbow_(band)" title="Elbow (band)">Elbow</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirkley_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-kirkley-62">[62]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Massive_Attack" title="Massive Attack">Massive Attack</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirkley_62-2"><a href="#cite_note-kirkley-62">[62]</a></sup> In 2016, Hogarth himself was quoted as describing the band: "If Pink Floyd and Radiohead had a love child that was in touch with their feminine side, they would be us."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marillion_in_the_media">Marillion in the media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Marillion in the media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:25%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9; padding:10px;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"We have got a bit of a reputation. I had a conversation with <a href="/wiki/Noel_Gallagher" title="Noel Gallagher">Noel Gallagher</a> at a party once and said to him, 'I'm in a band but it's the most uncool band in the world - Marillion'. He went, 'Yeah, you're right.' Deadpan! Not even smiling."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">—Keyboardist Mark Kelly<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalt2007_61-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dalt2007-61">[61]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>The chief music critic of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Alexis_Petridis" title="Alexis Petridis">Alexis Petridis</a>, has described Marillion as "perennially unfashionable prog-rockers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-petridis_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-petridis-64">[64]</a></sup> By their own admission, the band have never been fashionable in the eyes of the media. On the subject of joining the band, Steve Hogarth said in a 2001 interview: "At about the same time, <a href="/wiki/Matt_Johnson_(singer)" title="Matt Johnson (singer)">Matt Johnson</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_The" title="The The">The The</a> asked me to play piano on his tour. I always say I had to make a choice between the most hip band in the world, and the least." In the same conversation, he claimed: "We're just tired of the opinions of people who haven't heard anything we've done in ten years. A lot of what's spread about this band is laughable."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ling2001_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ling2001-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Much of the band's enduring and unfashionable reputation stems from their emergence in the early 1980s as the most commercially successful band of the <a href="/wiki/Neo-progressive_rock" title="Neo-progressive rock">neo-progressive rock</a> movement, an unexpected revival of the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive rock</a> musical style that had fallen out of critical favour in the mid-1970s. Some early critics were quick to dismiss the band as clones of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gabriel" title="Peter Gabriel">Peter Gabriel</a>-era <a href="/wiki/Genesis_(band)" title="Genesis (band)">Genesis</a> due to musical similarities, such as their extended songs, a prominent and <a href="/wiki/Mellotron" title="Mellotron">Mellotron</a>-influenced keyboard sound, vivid and fantastical lyrics and the equally vivid and fantastical artwork by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Wilkinson" title="Mark Wilkinson">Mark Wilkinson</a> used for the sleeves of their albums and singles. Lead singer Fish was also often compared with Gabriel due to his early vocal style and theatrical stage performances, which in the early years included wearing face paint.</p>
<p>As Jonh Wilde summarised in <i><a href="/wiki/Melody_Maker" title="Melody Maker">Melody Maker</a></i> in 1989:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>At the end of a strange year for pop music, Marillion appeared in November 1982 with "<a href="/wiki/Market_Square_Heroes" title="Market Square Heroes">Market Square Heroes</a>". There were many strange things about 1982, but Marillion were the strangest of them all. For six years, they stood out of time. Marillion were the unhippest group going. As punk was becoming a distant echo, they appeared with a sound and an attitude that gazed back longingly to the age of Seventies pomp. When compared to <a href="/wiki/Yes_(band)" title="Yes (band)">Yes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genesis_(band)" title="Genesis (band)">Genesis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emerson,_Lake_%26_Palmer" title="Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer">ELP</a>, they would take it as a compliment. The Eighties have seen some odd phenomena. But none quite as odd as Marillion. Along the way, as if by glorious fluke, they turned out some singles that everybody quietly liked – "<a href="/wiki/Garden_Party_(Marillion_song)" title="Garden Party (Marillion song)">Garden Party</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Punch_and_Judy_(song)" title="Punch and Judy (song)">Punch and Judy</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Incommunicado_(song)" title="Incommunicado (song)">Incommunicado</a>". By this time, Marillion did not need the support of the hip-conscious. They were massive. Perhaps the oddest thing about Marillion was that they became one of the biggest groups of the decade. They might have been an anomaly but they were monstrously effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The band's unfashionable reputation and image has often been mentioned in the media, even in otherwise positive reviews. In <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(magazine)" title="Q (magazine)">Q</a></i> in 1987, <a href="/wiki/David_Hepworth" title="David Hepworth">David Hepworth</a> claimed: "Marillion may represent the inelegant, unglamorous, public bar end of the current Rock Renaissance but they are no less part of it for that. <i><a href="/wiki/Clutching_at_Straws" title="Clutching at Straws">Clutching at Straws</a></i> suggests that they may be finally coming in from the cold."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> In the same magazine in 1995, Dave Henderson wrote: "It's not yet possible to be sacked for showing an affinity for Marillion, but has there ever been a band with a larger stigma attached?" He also claimed that if the album <i><a href="/wiki/Afraid_of_Sunlight" title="Afraid of Sunlight">Afraid of Sunlight</a></i> "had been made by a new, no baggage-of-the-past combo, it would be greeted with open arms, hailed as virtual genius."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Record_Collector" title="Record Collector">Record Collector</a></i> in 2002, Tim Jones claimed they were "one of the most unfairly berated bands in Britain" and "one of its best live rock acts."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> In 2004, <i><a href="/wiki/Classic_Rock_(magazine)" title="Classic Rock (magazine)">Classic Rock</a>'</i>s <a href="/wiki/Jon_Hotten" title="Jon Hotten">Jon Hotten</a> wrote: "That genre thing has been a bugbear of Marillion's, but it no longer seems relevant. What are <a href="/wiki/Radiohead" title="Radiohead">Radiohead</a> if not a progressive band?" and claimed Marillion were "making strong, singular music with the courage of their convictions, and we should treasure them more than we do."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jon_Hotten_2004_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jon_Hotten_2004-40">[40]</a></sup> In the <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(magazine)" title="Q (magazine)">Q</a></i> &amp; <i><a href="/wiki/Mojo_(magazine)" title="Mojo (magazine)">Mojo</a></i> Classic Special Edition <i><a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a> &amp; The Story of Prog Rock</i>, an article on Marillion written by <a href="/wiki/Mick_Wall" title="Mick Wall">Mick Wall</a> described them as "probably the most misunderstood band in the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, Stephen Dalton of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> stated:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The band have just released their 14th album, <i><a href="/wiki/Somewhere_Else_(Marillion_album)" title="Somewhere Else (Marillion album)">Somewhere Else</a></i>, which is really rather good. Containing tracks that shimmer like <a href="/wiki/Coldplay" title="Coldplay">Coldplay</a>, ache like <a href="/wiki/Radiohead" title="Radiohead">Radiohead</a> and thunder like <a href="/wiki/Muse_(band)" title="Muse (band)">Muse</a>, it is better than 80 per cent of this month's releases. But you are unlikely to hear Marillion on British radio, read about them in the music press or see them play a major festival. This is largely because Marillion have – how can we put this kindly? – an image problem. Their music is still perceived as bloated, bombastic mullet-haired prog-rock, even by people who have never heard it. In fairness, they did once release an album called <i><a href="/wiki/Script_for_a_Jester%27s_Tear" title="Script for a Jester's Tear">Script for a Jester's Tear</a></i>. But, come on, we all had bad hair days in the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalt2007_61-4"><a href="#cite_note-Dalt2007-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Despite publishing a very good review for their 1995 album <i><a href="/wiki/Afraid_of_Sunlight" title="Afraid of Sunlight">Afraid of Sunlight</a></i> and including it in their 50 Best Albums of 1995, <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(magazine)" title="Q (magazine)">Q</a></i> refused to interview the band or write a feature on them. Steve Hogarth later said: "How can they say, this is an amazing record... no, we don't want to talk to you? It's hard to take when they say, here's a very average record... we'll put you on the front cover."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ling2001_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ling2001-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1999, DJ <a href="/wiki/Simon_Mayo" title="Simon Mayo">Simon Mayo</a> commented on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">BBC Radio 1</a>: "Marillion ... where are they now? And who cares anyway?" Fans objecting to the comment brought the station's computer system to a standstill with thousands of emails of complaint. Mayo subsequently apologised for his comment to keyboard player Mark Kelly when he phoned the show to take part in a quiz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnorPR_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-AnorPR-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>To accompany the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Anoraknophobia" title="Anoraknophobia">Anoraknophobia</a></i> in 2001, the band issued a press release asking critics to review the album in "a manner that is both accurate and fair. So, our challenge to you is to firstly listen to the album. Then write a review without using any of the following words: "Progressive rock", "<a href="/wiki/Genesis_(band)" title="Genesis (band)">Genesis</a>", "Fish", "heavy metal", "dinosaurs", "predictable", "concept album". Because if you do, we'll know that you haven't listened to it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnorPR_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-AnorPR-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, <i>The Guardian</i> television critic <a href="/wiki/Gareth_McLean" title="Gareth McLean">Gareth McLean</a> used his review of the <a href="/wiki/Michael_Lewis" title="Michael Lewis">Michael Lewis</a> <a href="/wiki/BBC_Two" title="BBC Two">BBC Two</a> documentary, <i><a href="/wiki/Next:_The_Future_Just_Happened" title="Next: The Future Just Happened">Next: The Future Just Happened</a></i>, to concentrate on launching a scathing attack on the band, whose appearance only constituted one segment of the programme. He described them as "once dodgy and now completely rubbish" and he characterised their fans as "slightly simple folks". He also dismissed the band's efforts to continue their career without a label by dealing directly with their fans on the Internet, claiming: "One suspects that their decision occurred round about the time that the record industry decided to shun Marillion."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rachel_Cooke" title="Rachel Cooke">Rachel Cooke</a>, a writer for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/New_Statesman" title="New Statesman">New Statesman</a></i>, has repeatedly referred negatively to the band and insulted their fans in her articles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>The band have been prepared to send up their unfashionable status, naming their 2001 album "Anoraknophobia" and printing T-shirts with the logo "Marillion: Uncool as F*ck."</p>
<p>In an interview in 2000, Hogarth expressed regret about the band retaining their name after he joined:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>If we had known when I joined Marillion what we know now, we'd have changed the name and been a new band. It was a mistake to keep the name, because what it represented in the mid-Eighties is a millstone we now carry. If we'd changed it, I think we would have been better off. We would have been judged for our music. It's such a grave injustice that the media constantly calls us a 'dinosaur prog band'. They only say that out of ignorance because they haven't listened to anything we've done for the last 15 bloody years. If you hear anything we've done in the last five or six years, that description is totally irrelevant... It's a massive frustration that no-one will play our stuff. If we send our single to Radio 1 they say: 'Sorry, we don't play music by bands who are over so-many years old... and here's the new <a href="/wiki/U2" title="U2">U2</a> single.' I suppose it's something everyone has to cope with – every band are remembered for their big hit single, irrespective of how much they change over the years. But you can only transcend that by continuing to have hits. It's Catch 22. You know, at some stage, someone has to notice that we're doing interesting things. Someday someone will take a retrospective look at us and be surprised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The 2013 film <i><a href="/wiki/Alan_Partridge:_Alpha_Papa" title="Alan Partridge: Alpha Papa">Alan Partridge: Alpha Papa</a></i> includes a fictional portrayal of the band’s drummer as a running joke. The band granted special permission after being shown clips.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crowdfunding_pioneers">Crowdfunding pioneers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marillion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Crowdfunding pioneers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Marillion are widely considered to have been one of the first mainstream acts to have fully recognised and tapped the potential for commercial musicians to interact with their fans via the internet, starting in around 1996, and are nowadays often characterised as a rock &amp; roll 'Web Cottage Industry'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> The history of the band's use of the Internet is described by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Lewis" title="Michael Lewis">Michael Lewis</a> in the book <i><a href="/wiki/Next:_The_Future_Just_Happened" title="Next: The Future Just Happened">Next: The Future Just Happened</a></i> as an example of how the Internet is shifting power away from established elites, such as multinational <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Record_labels" title="Record labels">record labels</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Record_producers" title="Record producers">record producers</a>. The band are renowned for having an extremely dedicated following (often self-termed 'Freaks'),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-78">[78]</a></sup> with some fans regularly travelling significant distances to attend single gigs, driven in large part by the close fan base involvement which the band cultivate via their website, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Podcasts" title="Podcasts">podcasts</a>, biennial conventions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> and regular fanclub<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> publications. The release of their 2001 album <i><a href="/wiki/Anoraknophobia" title="Anoraknophobia">Anoraknophobia</a></i>, which was funded by their fans through advance orders instead of by the band signing to a record company, gained significant attention and was called "a unique funding campaign" by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Alexis_Petridis" title="Alexis Petridis">Alexis Petridis</a> described Marillion as "the undisputed pioneers" of <a href="/wiki/Fan-funded_music" title="Fan-funded music">fan-funded music</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-petridis_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-petridis-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
