<p><b>Marvell Technology Group</b>, Limited, is a producer of storage, <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">communications</a> and consumer <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> products. The company was founded in 1995 and has approximately 7,000 employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-website_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-website-3">[3]</a></sup> Marvell's U.S. operating headquarters is located in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California" title="Santa Clara, California">Santa Clara, California</a>, and the company operates design centers in places including Canada, Europe, Israel, India, Singapore and China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Marvell is a "<a href="/wiki/Fabless_manufacturing" title="Fabless manufacturing">fabless</a>" manufacturer of semiconductors (meaning that it is active entirely in <a href="/wiki/Core_competency" title="Core competency">core competencies</a> of engineering and design, with the actual manufacturing <a href="/wiki/Outsourcing" title="Outsourcing">outsourced</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contract_manufacturing" title="Contract manufacturing">contract manufacturers</a> in lower-cost <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emerging_market" title="Emerging market">emerging markets</a>) that ships more than one billion <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a> (known as "chips") per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-website_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-website-3">[3]</a></sup> Its market segments include the enterprise, cloud, automotive, industrial and consumer markets.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marvell_Technology_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Marvell was founded in 1995 by <a href="/wiki/Sehat_Sutardja" title="Sehat Sutardja">Sehat Sutardja</a>, his wife <a href="/wiki/Weili_Dai" title="Weili Dai">Weili Dai</a>, and brother <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pantas_Sutardja&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pantas Sutardja (page does not exist)">Pantas Sutardja</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> on June 27, 2000 (near the end of the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a>) raised $90 million, with the stock listed on <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> with the symbol MRVL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipo_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipo-6">[6]</a></sup> After quickly raising from $19 to over $63 per share, three days later it was $55.25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> At the time, the five largest customers, <a href="/wiki/Samsung_Electronics" title="Samsung Electronics">Samsung Electronics</a>, <a href="/wiki/HGST" title="HGST">Hitachi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seagate_Technology" title="Seagate Technology">Seagate Technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fujitsu" title="Fujitsu">Fujitsu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toshiba" title="Toshiba">Toshiba</a>, accounted for 97% of sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pro_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-pro-8">[8]</a></sup> The shares dropped sharply in December when insiders were allowed to sell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company is officially headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_Bermuda" title="Hamilton, Bermuda">Hamilton, Bermuda</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipo_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ipo-6">[6]</a></sup> The US operations known as Marvell Semiconductor, Incorporated, are located in <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pro_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-pro-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethernet">Ethernet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marvell_Technology_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ethernet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Marvell's first products were sold for <a href="/wiki/Computer_data_storage" title="Computer data storage">computer data storage</a> devices. In March 2000, <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">computer networking</a> products for the <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> family were first shipped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pro_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-pro-8">[8]</a></sup> In October 2002, the Yukon brand <a href="/wiki/Gigabit_Ethernet" title="Gigabit Ethernet">Gigabit Ethernet</a> controller was announced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="XScale">XScale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marvell_Technology_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: XScale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On June 27, 2006, the sale of <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>'s <a href="/wiki/XScale" title="XScale">XScale</a> assets was announced. Intel agreed to sell the XScale business to Marvell for an estimated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USD" title="USD">USD</a> 600 million in cash and the assumption of unspecified liabilities. The acquisition was completed on November 9, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-XScale_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-XScale-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SheevaPlug">SheevaPlug</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marvell_Technology_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: SheevaPlug">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2009, Marvell announced that the <a href="/wiki/SheevaPlug" title="SheevaPlug">SheevaPlug</a>, a small, low-power, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System-on-a-chip" title="System-on-a-chip">SoC</a>-based <a href="/wiki/ARM_architecture" title="ARM architecture">ARM architecture</a> computer, would be released with full schematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="iPhone">iPhone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marvell_Technology_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: iPhone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Marvell supplied the <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> chip for the <a href="/wiki/IPhone_(1st_generation)" title="IPhone (1st generation)">original (first-generation) Apple iPhone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MMH">MMH</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marvell_Technology_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: MMH">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Marvell Mobile Hotspot (MMH) is an in-car Wi-Fi connectivity. The 2010 <a href="/wiki/Audi_A8" title="Audi A8">Audi A8</a> was the first automobile in the market to feature a factory-installed MMH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chromecast">Chromecast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marvell_Technology_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Chromecast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Google's <a href="/wiki/Chromecast" title="Chromecast">Chromecast</a> products are powered by Marvell SoCs. Namely the Marvell ARMADA 1500 Mini SoC (88DE3005) for the Chromecast 1st gen and Marvell ARMADA 1500 Mini Plus SoC (88DE3006) for the Chromecast 2nd gen &amp; Chromecast audio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, Marvell was named one of <a href="/wiki/Thomson_Reuters" title="Thomson Reuters">Thomson Reuters</a> top 100 global innovators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_cases">Legal cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marvell_Technology_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legal cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stock_options">Stock options</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marvell_Technology_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Stock options">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2006, the US <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a> (SEC) started an inquiry on the company's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stock_option" title="Stock option">stock option</a> grant practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> An investigation determined "grant dates were chosen with the benefit of hindsight" to make the options more valuable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-backed_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-backed-30">[30]</a></sup> The press estimated that the founders and other executives had made $760 million in gains from the options, which were awarded by the founding couple, Sehat Sutardja and Weili Dai.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The SEC asked to interview the company general counsel Matthew Gloss, but Marvell claimed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attorney-client_privilege" title="Attorney-client privilege">attorney-client privilege</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Gloss was fired just before the investigation results were announced in May 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Abraham_David_Sofaer" title="Abraham David Sofaer">Abraham David Sofaer</a> was hired to investigate the investigation after Gloss alleged it was not independent. In announcing the results of its own inquiry, the SEC did not give Marvell the credit granted other companies in the options scandal for cooperating [with the SEC’s investigation] or [for] cleaning up. &gt;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> At the time of the announcement, the co-acting regional director of the SEC’s San Francisco office stated, among other things, that [the SEC] did not believe that the [lack of] cooperation and remediation [shown by Marvell] merited a whole lot of credit in terms of giving [Marvell] a break. &gt;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In announcing its results, the SEC found that Gloss was not a participant in [Dai and Sutardja’s] backdating scheme.&gt;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Marvell restated its financial results, and stated that Dai will no longer be executive vice president, chief operating officer, and a director but continue with the company in a non-management position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The company agreed to pay a $10 million fine in 2008, but did not fire Dai nor replace Sutardja as chairman as stated by the investigating committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-backed_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-backed-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Patent_infringement">Patent infringement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marvell_Technology_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Patent infringement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 2012, a Pittsburgh jury ruled that Marvell had infringed two patents (co-inventors Alek Kavcic and Jose Moura) by incorporating hard disk technology developed and owned by <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a> without a license.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-39">[39]</a></sup> The technology, relating to improving hard disk data read accuracy at high speeds, was reported to have been used in 2.3 billion chips sold by Marvell between 2003 and 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-40">[40]</a></sup> The jury awarded damages of $1.17 billion, the third largest ever in a patent case at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The jury also found that the breach had been "willful", giving the judge discretion to award up to three times the original damage amount.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-40">[40]</a></sup> In December 2012, the company lost its mistrial bid in this dispute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Post-trial hearings were scheduled for May 2013 and Marvell reported to be considering an appeal in the interim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-39">[39]</a></sup> In August, US District Judge <a href="/wiki/Nora_Barry_Fischer" title="Nora Barry Fischer">Nora Barry Fischer</a> upheld the award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> On February 17, 2016, Marvell agreed to a settlement in which Marvell will pay Carnegie Mellon University $750,000,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CMU2016_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-CMU2016-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
