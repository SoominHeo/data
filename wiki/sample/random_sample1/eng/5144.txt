<div class="hatnote" role="note">In this <a href="/wiki/Japanese_name" title="Japanese name">Japanese name</a>, the family name is Masaki.</div>
<p><b>Jinzaburō Masaki</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">真崎 甚三郎</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Masaki Jinzaburō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, 27 November 1876 – 31 August 1956)</span> was a general in the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army" title="Imperial Japanese Army">Imperial Japanese Army</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. He was regarded as a leader of the radical political faction within the Japanese military.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jinzabur%C5%8D_Masaki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Saga_Prefecture" title="Saga Prefecture">Saga Prefecture</a> in 1876, Masaki graduated from the 9th class of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army_Academy" title="Imperial Japanese Army Academy">Imperial Japanese Army Academy</a> in 1897 and was commissioned as a second lieutenant in the IJA 46th Infantry Regiment] in June 1898. He was assigned to <a href="/wiki/Tsushima_Island" title="Tsushima Island">Tsushima</a> Guard Battalion from May 1899 until November 1900, when he was promoted to lieutenant in the IJA 46th Infantry Regiment. He was sent to the front during the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a> in February 1904, and served in <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a> until December 1905. During this period, he was promoted to captain in June 1904; however, he found the war a highly traumatizing experience, and after his return to Japan, Masaki wrote that he had considered resigning from the Army and entering the Buddhist priesthood.</p>
<p>Instead, Masaki entered the 19th class of the <a href="/wiki/Army_War_College_(Japan)" title="Army War College (Japan)">Army Staff College</a> and graduated with honors at the top of his class in December 1905. An <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">infantry</a> officer, he was promoted to the rank of <a href="/wiki/Major" title="Major">major</a> in 1909 before being sent abroad as a <a href="/wiki/Military_attach%C3%A9" title="Military attaché">military attaché</a> to <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> from 1911 to 1914. He was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_colonel" title="Lieutenant colonel">lieutenant colonel</a> in November 1914.</p>
<p>After his return to Japan, Masaki was appointed as a staff officer in the <a href="/wiki/Inspectorate_General_of_Military_Training" title="Inspectorate General of Military Training">Inspectorate General of Military Training</a> where he served from 1916 to 1920, becoming a <a href="/wiki/Colonel" title="Colonel">colonel</a> in 1918. Appointed chief of Military Administration Bureau of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_War_of_Japan" title="Ministry of War of Japan">War Ministry</a> in 1920, he was given command of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Guard_of_Japan" title="Imperial Guard of Japan">1st Imperial Guards Regiment</a> the following year. Upon his promotion to <a href="/wiki/Major_general" title="Major general">major general</a> in 1922, Masaki served as a Brigade commander until becoming director of curriculum at the Japanese Army Academy eventually becoming commandant by 1925.</p>
<p>Promoted to <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_general" title="Lieutenant general">lieutenant general</a> in 1927, Masaki was placed in command of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IJA_8th_Division" title="IJA 8th Division">IJA 8th Division</a> posted at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hirosaki,_Aomori" title="Hirosaki, Aomori">Hirosaki, Aomori</a> for two years before being transferred to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IJA_1st_Division" title="IJA 1st Division">IJA 1st Division</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> until 1931. Serving as vice chief of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army_General_Staff" title="Imperial Japanese Army General Staff">Imperial Japanese Army General Staff</a>, Masaki won promotion to full <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General" title="General">general</a> in 1933 and appointed Inspector General of Military Education between 1934 and 1936. He retired from active military service in 1936.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Masaki was active in the internal political factions within the Japanese Army. He was an early member of the radical <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Way_Faction" title="Imperial Way Faction">Imperial Way Faction</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Sadao_Araki" title="Sadao Araki">Sadao Araki</a>. With <a href="/wiki/Heisuke_Yanagawa" title="Heisuke Yanagawa">Heisuke Yanagawa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hideyoshi_Obata" title="Hideyoshi Obata">Hideyoshi Obata</a>, the group merged with the rival <i><a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dseiha" title="Tōseiha">Tōseiha</a></i> faction under <a href="/wiki/Kazushige_Ugaki" title="Kazushige Ugaki">Kazushige Ugaki</a> to dominate the Japanese army throughout the 1930s until <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. A supporter of the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, Masaki continued his involvement with the Imperial Way Faction until his forced retirement though political maneuvers by General <a href="/wiki/Tetsuzan_Nagata" title="Tetsuzan Nagata">Tetsuzan Nagata</a>. Dissatisfaction with Masaki's forced retirement resulted in the assassination of Nagata the following year which, in turn, led to the <a href="/wiki/February_26_Incident" title="February 26 Incident">February 26 Incident</a> of 1936. He returned to his native Saga Prefecture to serve on the Prefectural Board of Education in 1941.</p>
