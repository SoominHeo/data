<p><b>Herbert Marshall McLuhan</b>, <span style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Order_of_Canada" title="Order of Canada">CC</a></span> (July 21, 1911 – December 31, 1980) was a Canadian professor, philosopher, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_intellectual" title="Public intellectual">public intellectual</a>. His work is viewed as one of the cornerstones of the study of <a href="/wiki/Media_studies" title="Media studies">media theory</a>, as well as having practical applications in the advertising and television industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time101367_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-time101367-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time052570_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-time052570-2">[2]</a></sup> He was educated at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Manitoba" title="University of Manitoba">University of Manitoba</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> and began his teaching career as a Professor of English at several universities in the U.S. and Canada, before moving to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">University of Toronto</a> where he would remain for the rest of his life.</p>
<p>McLuhan is known for coining the expressions "<a href="/wiki/The_medium_is_the_message" title="The medium is the message">the medium is the message</a>" and the <a href="/wiki/Global_village_(term)" title="Global village (term)">global village</a>, and for predicting the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> almost thirty years before it was invented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levinson_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levinson-3">[3]</a></sup> Although he was a fixture in media discourse in the late 1960s, his influence began to wane in the early 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In the years after his death, he continued to be a controversial figure in academic circles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stille_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-stille-5">[5]</a></sup> With the arrival of the internet, however, interest in his work and perspective has renewed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guard08_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-guard08-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_saint_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-wired_saint-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boxer_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-boxer-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Herbert Marshall McLuhan was born in <a href="/wiki/Edmonton" title="Edmonton">Edmonton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, to Elsie Naomi (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Married_and_maiden_names" title="Married and maiden names">née</a> Hall) and Herbert Ernest McLuhan. His brother, Maurice, was born two years later. "Marshall" was a family name: his maternal grandmother's surname. Both of his parents were born in Canada. His mother was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baptist" title="Baptist">Baptist</a> schoolteacher who later became an actress. His father was a <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodist</a> and had a real estate business in Edmonton. When <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> broke out, the business failed, and McLuhan's father enlisted in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Forces" title="Canadian Forces">Canadian army</a>. After a year of service he contracted influenza and remained in Canada, away from the front. After Herbert's discharge from the army in 1915, the McLuhan family moved to <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg" title="Winnipeg">Winnipeg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a>, where Marshall grew up and went to school, attending <a href="/wiki/Kelvin_High_School" title="Kelvin High School">Kelvin Technical School</a> before enrolling in the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Manitoba" title="University of Manitoba">University of Manitoba</a> in 1928.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>At Manitoba, McLuhan explored his conflicted relationship with religion and turned to literature to "gratify his soul's hunger for truth and beauty,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> later referring to this stage as <a href="/wiki/Agnosticism" title="Agnosticism">agnosticism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> After studying for one year as an engineering student, McLuhan changed majors and earned a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">BA</a> (1933)—winning a University Gold Medal in Arts and Sciences<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>—and later, in 1934, an <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">MA</a> (1934) in <a href="/wiki/English_studies" title="English studies">English</a> from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Manitoba" title="University of Manitoba">University of Manitoba</a>. He had long desired to pursue graduate studies in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and, having failed to secure a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhodes_scholarship" title="Rhodes scholarship">Rhodes scholarship</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_University" title="Oxford University">Oxford</a>, he was accepted to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a>.</p>
<p>Although he had already earned a BA and an MA degree at Manitoba, Cambridge required him to enroll as an undergraduate "affiliated" student, with one year's credit towards a three-year bachelor's degree, before entering any doctoral studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He entered <a href="/wiki/Trinity_Hall,_Cambridge" title="Trinity Hall, Cambridge">Trinity Hall, Cambridge</a> in the autumn of 1934, where he studied under <a href="/wiki/I._A._Richards" title="I. A. Richards">I. A. Richards</a> and <a href="/wiki/F._R._Leavis" title="F. R. Leavis">F. R. Leavis</a>, and was influenced by <a href="/wiki/New_Criticism" title="New Criticism">New Criticism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Upon reflection years afterward, he credited the faculty there with influencing the direction of his later work because of their emphasis on the <i>training of perception</i> and such concepts as Richards's notion of <i><a href="/wiki/I._A._Richards#Feedforward" title="I. A. Richards">feedforward</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> These studies formed an important precursor to his later ideas on technological forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAC_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAC-18">[18]</a></sup> He received the required bachelor's degree from Cambridge in 1936 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon.2C_p._94_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon.2C_p._94-19">[19]</a></sup> and entered their graduate program. Later, he returned from England to take a job as a teaching assistant at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison" title="University of Wisconsin–Madison">University of Wisconsin–Madison</a> that he held for the 1936–37 academic year, being unable to find a suitable job in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>While studying the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trivium_(education)" title="Trivium (education)">trivium</a> at Cambridge he took the first steps toward his eventual conversion to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholicism</a> in 1937,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> founded on his reading of <a href="/wiki/G._K._Chesterton" title="G. K. Chesterton">G. K. Chesterton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 1935 he wrote to his mother: "[H]ad I not encountered Chesterton, I would have remained agnostic for many years at least".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> At the end of March 1937,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> McLuhan completed what was a slow, but total conversion process, when he was formally received into the Roman Catholic Church. After consulting a minister, his father accepted the decision to convert. His mother, however, felt that his conversion would hurt his career and was inconsolable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> McLuhan was devout throughout his life, but his religion remained a private matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> He had a lifelong interest in the number three <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> —the trivium, the <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinity</a>—and sometimes said that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blessed_Virgin_Mary" title="Blessed Virgin Mary">Virgin Mary</a> provided intellectual guidance for him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> For the rest of his career he taught in Roman Catholic institutions of higher education. From 1937 to 1944 he taught English at <a href="/wiki/Saint_Louis_University" title="Saint Louis University">Saint Louis University</a> (with an interruption from 1939 to 1940, when he returned to Cambridge). There he taught courses on <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and tutored and befriended <a href="/wiki/Walter_J._Ong" title="Walter J. Ong">Walter J. Ong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">S.J.</a> (1912–2003), who would go on to write his Ph.D. dissertation on a topic McLuhan had called to his attention, and who also would later become a well-known authority on communication and technology.</p>
<p>While in St. Louis, he also met his future wife. On August 4, 1939, McLuhan married Corinne Lewis (1912–2008),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> a teacher and aspiring actress from <a href="/wiki/Fort_Worth,_Texas" title="Fort Worth, Texas">Fort Worth, Texas</a>. Together, they spent 1939–40 in Cambridge, where he completed his master's degree (awarded in January 1940)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon.2C_p._94_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon.2C_p._94-19">[19]</a></sup> and began to work on his doctoral dissertation on <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Nashe" title="Thomas Nashe">Thomas Nashe</a> and the verbal arts. While the McLuhans were in England, <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">war</a> had broken out in Europe. For this reason, he obtained permission to complete and submit his dissertation from the United States, without having to return to Cambridge for an oral defence. In 1940, the McLuhans returned to Saint Louis University, where he continued teaching and they started a family. He was awarded a Ph.D. in December 1943.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Returning to Canada, from 1944 to 1946, McLuhan taught at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assumption_University_(Windsor)" title="Assumption University (Windsor)">Assumption College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Windsor,_Ontario" title="Windsor, Ontario">Windsor, Ontario</a>. Moving to <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a> in 1946, McLuhan joined the faculty of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_St._Michael%27s_College" title="University of St. Michael's College">St. Michael's College</a>, a Catholic college of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">University of Toronto</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Kenner" title="Hugh Kenner">Hugh Kenner</a> was one of his students and Canadian economist and communications scholar <a href="/wiki/Harold_Innis" title="Harold Innis">Harold Innis</a> was a university colleague who had a strong influence on McLuhan's work. McLuhan wrote in 1964: "I am pleased to think of my own book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Gutenberg_Galaxy" title="The Gutenberg Galaxy">The Gutenberg Galaxy</a></i> as a footnote to the observations of Innis on the subject of the psychic and social consequences, first of <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">writing</a> then of <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">printing</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 1950s, McLuhan began the Communication and Culture seminars, funded by the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Foundation" title="Ford Foundation">Ford Foundation</a>, at the University of Toronto. As his reputation grew, he received a growing number of offers from other universities and, to keep him, the university created the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centre_for_Culture_and_Technology" title="Centre for Culture and Technology">Centre for Culture and Technology</a> in 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAC_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-LAC-18">[18]</a></sup> He published his first major work during this period: <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Mechanical_Bride:_Folklore_of_Industrial_Man" title="The Mechanical Bride: Folklore of Industrial Man">The Mechanical Bride</a></i> (1951). The work was an examination of the effect of advertising on society and culture. He also produced an important journal, <i>Explorations</i>, with <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Snow_Carpenter" title="Edmund Snow Carpenter">Edmund Carpenter</a>, throughout the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Together with <a href="/wiki/Harold_Innis" title="Harold Innis">Harold Innis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_A._Havelock" title="Eric A. Havelock">Eric A. Havelock</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Frye" title="Northrop Frye">Northrop Frye</a>, McLuhan and Carpenter have been characterized as the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_School_of_communication_theory" title="Toronto School of communication theory">Toronto School of communication theory</a>. During this time McLuhan supervised the doctoral thesis of modernist writer <a href="/wiki/Sheila_Watson_(writer)" title="Sheila Watson (writer)">Sheila Watson</a>, on the subject of <a href="/wiki/Wyndham_Lewis" title="Wyndham Lewis">Wyndham Lewis</a>. McLuhan remained at the University of Toronto through 1979, spending much of this time as head of his Centre for Culture and Technology.</p>
<p>McLuhan was named to the <a href="/wiki/Albert_Schweitzer" title="Albert Schweitzer">Albert Schweitzer</a> Chair in Humanities at <a href="/wiki/Fordham_University" title="Fordham University">Fordham University</a> in the Bronx, New York, for one year (1967–68).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> While at Fordham, McLuhan was diagnosed with a benign brain tumor; it was treated successfully. He returned to Toronto, where, for the rest of his life, he taught at the University of Toronto and lived in <a href="/wiki/Wychwood_Park" title="Wychwood Park">Wychwood Park</a>, a bucolic enclave on a hill overlooking the downtown where <a href="/wiki/Anatol_Rapoport" title="Anatol Rapoport">Anatol Rapoport</a> was his neighbour. In 1970, McLuhan was made a Companion of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Canada" title="Order of Canada">Order of Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In 1975, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Dallas" title="University of Dallas">University of Dallas</a> hosted him from April to May, appointing him to the McDermott Chair.</p>
<p>Marshall and Corinne McLuhan had six children: <a href="/wiki/Eric_McLuhan" title="Eric McLuhan">Eric</a>, twins Mary and Teresa, Stephanie, Elizabeth and Michael. The associated costs of a large family eventually drove McLuhan to advertising work and accepting frequent consulting and speaking engagements for large corporations, IBM and AT&amp;T among them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAC_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-LAC-18">[18]</a></sup> In September 1979, he suffered a stroke, which affected his ability to speak. The University of Toronto's School of Graduate Studies tried to close his research centre shortly thereafter, but was deterred by substantial protests, most notably by <a href="/wiki/Woody_Allen" title="Woody Allen">Woody Allen</a>. Allen's Oscar-winning motion picture <i><a href="/wiki/Annie_Hall" title="Annie Hall">Annie Hall</a></i> (1977) had McLuhan in a cameo as himself: a pompous academic arguing with Allen in a cinema queue is silenced by McLuhan suddenly appearing and saying, "You know nothing of my work." This was one of McLuhan's most frequent statements to and about those who would disagree with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>He never fully recovered from the stroke and died in his sleep on December 31, 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimesobituary_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimesobituary-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_works">Major works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Major works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During his years at <a href="/wiki/Saint_Louis_University" title="Saint Louis University">Saint Louis University</a> (1937–1944), McLuhan worked concurrently on two projects: his doctoral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doctoral_thesis" title="Doctoral thesis">dissertation</a> and the manuscript that was eventually published in 1951 as the book <i>The Mechanical Bride: Folklore of Industrial Man</i>, which included only a representative selection of the materials that McLuhan had prepared for it.</p>
<p>McLuhan's 1942 Cambridge University doctoral dissertation surveys the history of the verbal arts (<a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetoric</a>—collectively known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trivium_(education)" title="Trivium (education)">trivium</a>) from the time of <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a> down to the time of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Nashe" title="Thomas Nashe">Thomas Nashe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In his later publications, McLuhan at times uses the Latin concept of the <i>trivium</i> to outline an orderly and systematic picture of certain periods in the history of Western culture. McLuhan suggests that the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, for instance, was characterized by the heavy emphasis on the formal study of logic. The key development that led to the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> was not the rediscovery of ancient texts but a shift in emphasis from the formal study of logic to rhetoric and language. Modern life is characterized by the reemergence of grammar as its most salient feature—a trend McLuhan felt was exemplified by the <a href="/wiki/New_Criticism" title="New Criticism">New Criticism</a> of Richards and Leavis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i>The Mechanical Bride</i>, McLuhan turned his attention to analysing and commenting on numerous examples of persuasion in contemporary <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a>. This followed naturally from his earlier work as both dialectic and rhetoric in the classical trivium aimed at persuasion. At this point his focus shifted dramatically, turning inward to study the influence of <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">communication media</a> independent of their content. His famous <a href="/wiki/Aphorism" title="Aphorism">aphorism</a> "<a href="/wiki/The_medium_is_the_message" title="The medium is the message">the medium is the message</a>" (elaborated in his 1964 book, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Understanding_Media:_The_Extensions_of_Man" title="Understanding Media: The Extensions of Man">Understanding Media: The Extensions of Man</a></i>) calls attention to this intrinsic effect of communications media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>McLuhan also started the journal <i>Explorations</i> with anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Snow_Carpenter" title="Edmund Snow Carpenter">Edmund "Ted" Carpenter</a>. In a letter to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walter_Ong" title="Walter Ong">Walter Ong</a> dated May 31, 1953, McLuhan reported that he had received a two-year grant of $43,000 from the Ford Foundation to carry out a communication project at the University of Toronto involving faculty from different disciplines, which led to the creation of the journal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tom_Wolfe" title="Tom Wolfe">Tom Wolfe</a> suggests that a major under-acknowledged influence on McLuhan's work is the Catholic philosopher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teilhard_de_Chardin" title="Teilhard de Chardin">Teilhard de Chardin</a> whose ideas anticipated those of McLuhan, especially the evolution of the human mind into the "<a href="/wiki/Noosphere" title="Noosphere">noosphere</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWolfe_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-TWolfe-41">[41]</a></sup> McLuhan references de Chardin early on in <i>The Mechanical Bride</i> (p. 32): "This externalization of our senses creates what de Chardin calls the 'noosphere' or a technological brain for the world. Instead of tending towards a vast Alexandrian library the world has become a computer, an electronic brain, exactly as in an infantile piece of science fiction."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Mechanical_Bride_.281951.29"><i>The Mechanical Bride</i> (1951)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Mechanical Bride (1951)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>McLuhan's first book, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Mechanical_Bride:_Folklore_of_Industrial_Man" title="The Mechanical Bride: Folklore of Industrial Man">The Mechanical Bride: Folklore of Industrial Man</a></i> (1951), is a pioneering study in the field now known as <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a>. His interest in the critical study of popular culture was influenced by the 1933 book <i>Culture and Environment</i> by <a href="/wiki/F._R._Leavis" title="F. R. Leavis">F. R. Leavis</a> and Denys Thompson, and the title <i>The Mechanical Bride</i> is derived from a piece by the <a href="/wiki/Dada" title="Dada">Dadaist</a> artist, <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Duchamp" title="Marcel Duchamp">Marcel Duchamp</a>.</p>
<p>Like his 1962 book <i>The Gutenberg Galaxy</i>, <i>The Mechanical Bride</i> is composed of a number of short essays that can be read in any order—what he styled the "mosaic approach" to writing a book. Each essay begins with a newspaper or magazine article or an advertisement, followed by McLuhan's analysis thereof. The analyses bear on aesthetic considerations as well as on the implications behind the imagery and text. McLuhan chose the ads and articles included in his book not only to draw attention to their symbolism and their implications for the corporate entities that created and disseminated them, but also to mull over what such advertising implies about the wider society at which it is aimed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Gutenberg_Galaxy_.281962.29"><i>The Gutenberg Galaxy</i> (1962)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Gutenberg Galaxy (1962)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>McLuhan's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Gutenberg_Galaxy" title="The Gutenberg Galaxy">The Gutenberg Galaxy: The Making of Typographic Man</a></i> (written in 1961, first published in Canada by <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto_Press" title="University of Toronto Press">University of Toronto Press</a> in 1962) is a pioneering study in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Orality" title="Orality">oral culture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Print_culture" title="Print culture">print culture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cultural_studies" title="Cultural studies">cultural studies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Media_ecology" title="Media ecology">media ecology</a>.</p>
<p>Throughout the book, McLuhan takes pains to reveal how <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communication_technology" title="Communication technology">communication technology</a> (<a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabetic</a> writing, the <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_media" title="Electronic media">electronic media</a>) affects <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognitive" title="Cognitive">cognitive</a> organization, which in turn has profound ramifications for social organization:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>...[I]f a new technology extends one or more of our senses outside us into the social world, then new ratios among all of our senses will occur in that particular culture. It is comparable to what happens when a new note is added to a melody. And when the sense ratios alter in any culture then what had appeared lucid before may suddenly become opaque, and what had been vague or opaque will become translucent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Movable_type">Movable type</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Movable type">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>His episodic history takes the reader from pre-alphabetic tribal humankind to the <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronic age</a>. According to McLuhan, the invention of <a href="/wiki/Movable_type" title="Movable type">movable type</a> greatly accelerated, intensified, and ultimately enabled cultural and cognitive changes that had already been taking place since the invention and implementation of the alphabet, by which McLuhan means <a href="/wiki/Phonemic_orthography" title="Phonemic orthography">phonemic orthography</a>. (McLuhan is careful to distinguish the phonetic <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabet</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logographic" title="Logographic">logographic</a>/logogramic writing systems, like <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">hieroglyphics</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ideograms" title="Ideograms">ideograms</a>.)</p>
<p>Print culture, ushered in by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gutenberg_press" title="Gutenberg press">Gutenberg press</a> in the middle of the fifteenth century, brought about the cultural predominance of the visual over the aural/oral. Quoting with approval an observation on the nature of the printed word from <i>Prints and Visual Communication</i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Ivins" title="William Ivins">William Ivins</a>, McLuhan remarks:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>In this passage [Ivins] not only notes the ingraining of lineal, sequential habits, but, even more important, points out the visual homogenizing of experience of print culture, and the relegation of auditory and other sensuous complexity to the background. [...] The technology and social effects of typography incline us to abstain from noting interplay and, as it were, "formal" causality, both in our inner and external lives. Print exists by virtue of the static separation of functions and fosters a mentality that gradually resists any but a separative and compartmentalizing or specialist outlook.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The main concept of McLuhan's argument (later elaborated upon in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Medium_is_the_Massage" title="The Medium is the Massage">The Medium is the Massage</a></i>) is that new technologies (like alphabets, printing presses, and even speech itself) exert a gravitational effect on cognition, which in turn affects social organization: print technology changes our perceptual habits ("visual homogenizing of experience"), which in turn affects social interactions ("fosters a mentality that gradually resists all but a... specialist outlook"). According to McLuhan, the advent of print technology contributed to and made possible most of the salient trends in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_age" title="Modern age">Modern</a> period in the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a>: <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">individualism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a>. For McLuhan, these trends all reverberate with print technology's principle of "segmentation of actions and functions and principle of visual quantification."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Global_Village">The Global Village</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Global Village">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the early 1960s, McLuhan wrote that the visual, individualistic print culture would soon be brought to an end by what he called "electronic interdependence": when <a href="/wiki/Electronic_media" title="Electronic media">electronic media</a> replace <a href="/wiki/Visual_culture" title="Visual culture">visual culture</a> with aural/oral culture. In this new age, humankind will move from individualism and fragmentation to a collective identity, with a "tribal base." McLuhan's coinage for this new social organization is the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Village_(term)" title="Global Village (term)">global village</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term is sometimes described as having negative connotations in <i>The Gutenberg Galaxy</i>, but McLuhan himself was interested in exploring effects, not making value judgments:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Instead of tending towards a vast <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexandrian_library" title="Alexandrian library">Alexandrian library</a> the world has become a <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a>, an electronic brain, exactly as an infantile piece of <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a>. And as our senses have gone outside us, <a href="/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">Big Brother</a> goes inside. So, unless aware of this dynamic, we shall at once move into a phase of panic terrors, exactly befitting a small world of tribal drums, total interdependence, and superimposed co-existence. [...] Terror is the normal state of any oral society, for in it everything affects everything all the time. [...] In our long striving to recover for the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a> a unity of sensibility and of thought and feeling we have no more been prepared to accept the tribal consequences of such unity than we were ready for the fragmentation of the human psyche by print culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Key to McLuhan's argument is the idea that technology has no <i>per se</i> moral bent—it is a tool that profoundly shapes an individual's and, by extension, a society's self-conception and realization:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Is it not obvious that there are always enough moral problems without also taking a moral stand on technological grounds? [...] Print is the extreme phase of alphabet culture that detribalizes or decollectivizes man in the first instance. Print raises the visual features of alphabet to highest intensity of definition. Thus print carries the individuating power of the phonetic alphabet much further than <a href="/wiki/Manuscript" title="Manuscript">manuscript</a> culture could ever do. Print is the technology of <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">individualism</a>. If men decided to modify this visual technology by an electric technology, individualism would also be modified. To raise a moral complaint about this is like cussing a buzz-saw for lopping off fingers. "But", someone says, "we didn't know it would happen." Yet even witlessness is not a moral issue. It is a problem, but not a moral problem; and it would be nice to clear away some of the moral fogs that surround our technologies. It would be good for <a href="/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">morality</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The moral <a href="/wiki/Valence_(psychology)" title="Valence (psychology)">valence</a> of technology's effects on cognition is, for McLuhan, a matter of perspective. For instance, McLuhan contrasts the considerable alarm and revulsion that the growing quantity of books aroused in the latter seventeenth century with the modern concern for the "end of the book". If there can be no universal moral sentence passed on technology, McLuhan believes that "there can only be disaster arising from unawareness of the causalities and effects inherent in our technologies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> was invented almost thirty years after <i>The Gutenberg Galaxy</i>, and ten years after his death, McLuhan prophesied the web technology seen today as early as 1962:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The next medium, whatever it is—it may be the extension of consciousness—will include television as its content, not as its environment, and will transform television into an art form. A computer as a research and communication instrument could enhance retrieval, obsolesce mass library organization, retrieve the individual's encyclopedic function and flip into a private line to speedily tailored data of a saleable kind. (1962)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Furthermore, McLuhan coined and certainly popularized the usage of the term "<a href="/wiki/Surfing" title="Surfing">surfing</a>" to refer to rapid, irregular and multidirectional movement through a heterogeneous body of documents or knowledge, e.g., statements like "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heidegger" title="Heidegger">Heidegger</a> surf-boards along on the electronic wave as triumphantly as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descartes" title="Descartes">Descartes</a> rode the mechanical wave." <a href="/wiki/Paul_Levinson" title="Paul Levinson">Paul Levinson</a>'s 1999 book <i>Digital McLuhan</i> explores the ways that McLuhan's work can be better understood through the lens of the digital revolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levinson_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Levinson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>McLuhan frequently quoted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walter_Ong" title="Walter Ong">Walter Ong</a>'s <i>Ramus, Method, and the Decay of Dialogue</i> (1958), which evidently had prompted McLuhan to write <i>The Gutenberg Galaxy</i>. Ong wrote a highly favorable review of this new book in <i>America</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> However, Ong later tempered his praise, by describing McLuhan's <i>The Gutenberg Galaxy</i> as "a racy survey, indifferent to some scholarly detail, but uniquely valuable in suggesting the sweep and depth of the cultural and psychological changes entailed in the passage from illiteracy to print and beyond."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> McLuhan himself said of the book, "I'm not concerned to get any kudos out of [<i>The Gutenberg Galaxy</i>]. It seems to me a book that somebody should have written a century ago. I wish somebody else had written it. It will be a useful prelude to the rewrite of <i>Understanding Media</i> [the 1960 NAEB report] that I'm doing now."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>McLuhan's <i>The Gutenberg Galaxy</i> won Canada's highest literary award, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor_General%27s_Award_for_English_language_non-fiction" title="Governor General's Award for English language non-fiction">Governor-General's Award for Non-Fiction</a>, in 1962. The chairman of the selection committee was McLuhan's colleague at the University of Toronto and oftentime intellectual sparring partner, <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Frye" title="Northrop Frye">Northrop Frye</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Understanding_Media_.281964.29"><i>Understanding Media</i> (1964)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Understanding Media (1964)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>McLuhan's most widely known work, <i><a href="/wiki/Understanding_Media" title="Understanding Media">Understanding Media: The Extensions of Man</a></i> (1964), is a pioneering study in media theory. Dismayed by the way people approached and used new media such as television, McLuhan famously argued that in the modern world "we live mythically and integrally ... but continue to think in the old, fragmented space and time patterns of the pre-electric age."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>McLuhan proposed that <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">media</a> themselves, not the content they carry, should be the focus of study—popularly quoted as "the medium is the message". McLuhan's insight was that a medium affects the society in which it plays a role not by the content delivered over the medium, but by the characteristics of the medium itself. McLuhan pointed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_bulb" title="Light bulb">light bulb</a> as a clear demonstration of this concept. A light bulb does not have content in the way that a newspaper has articles or a television has programs, yet it is a medium that has a social effect; that is, a light bulb enables people to create spaces during nighttime that would otherwise be enveloped by darkness. He describes the light bulb as a medium without any content. McLuhan states that "a light bulb creates an environment by its mere presence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> More controversially, he postulated that content had little effect on society—in other words, it did not matter if television broadcasts children's shows or violent programming, to illustrate one example—the effect of television on society would be identical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> He noted that all media have characteristics that engage the viewer in different ways; for instance, a passage in a book could be reread at will, but a movie had to be screened again in its entirety to study any individual part of it.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Hot.22_and_.22cool.22_media"><span id="Hot_and_cool_media"></span>"Hot" and "cool" media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title='Edit section: "Hot" and "cool" media'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the first part of <i>Understanding Media,</i> McLuhan also stated that different media invite different degrees of participation on the part of a person who chooses to consume a medium. Some media, like the movies, were "hot"—that is, they enhance one single <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">sense</a>, in this case <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">vision</a>, in such a manner that a person does not need to exert much effort in filling in the details of a movie image. McLuhan contrasted this with "cool" TV, which he claimed requires more effort on the part of the viewer to determine meaning, and <a href="/wiki/Comics" title="Comics">comics</a>, which due to their minimal presentation of visual detail require a high degree of effort to fill in details that the cartoonist may have intended to portray. A movie is thus said by McLuhan to be "hot", intensifying one single sense "high definition", demanding a viewer's attention, and a comic book to be "cool" and "low definition", requiring much more conscious participation by the reader to extract value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Any hot medium allows of less participation than a cool one, as a lecture makes for less participation than a seminar, and a book for less than a dialogue."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hot media usually, but not always, provide complete involvement without considerable stimulus. For example, print occupies visual space, uses visual senses, but can immerse its reader. Hot media favour analytical precision, quantitative analysis and sequential ordering, as they are usually sequential, linear and logical. They emphasize one sense (for example, of sight or sound) over the others. For this reason, hot media also include <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">film</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lecture" title="Lecture">lecture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photography</a>.</p>
<p>Cool media, on the other hand, are usually, but not always, those that provide little involvement with substantial stimulus. They require more active participation on the part of the user, including the perception of abstract patterning and simultaneous comprehension of all parts. Therefore, according to McLuhan cool media include <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Seminar" title="Seminar">seminar</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartoons" title="Cartoons">cartoons</a>. McLuhan describes the term "cool media" as emerging from jazz and popular music and, in this context, is used to mean "detached."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>This concept appears to force media into binary categories. However, McLuhan's hot and cool exist on a continuum: they are more correctly measured on a scale than as dichotomous terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAC_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-LAC-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Critiques_of_Understanding_Media">Critiques of <i>Understanding Media</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Critiques of Understanding Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some theorists have attacked McLuhan’s definition and treatment of the word "medium" for being too simplistic. <a href="/wiki/Umberto_Eco" title="Umberto Eco">Umberto Eco</a>, for instance, contends that McLuhan’s medium conflates channels, codes, and messages under the overarching term of the medium, confusing the vehicle, internal code, and content of a given message in his framework.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Debray_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Debray-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i>Media Manifestos</i>, <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9gis_Debray" title="Régis Debray">Régis Debray</a> also takes issue with McLuhan’s envisioning of the medium. Like Eco, he too is ill at ease with this reductionist approach, summarizing its ramifications as follows:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The list of objections could be and has been lengthened indefinitely: confusing technology itself with its use of the media makes of the media an abstract, undifferentiated force and produces its image in an imaginary "public" for mass consumption; the magical naivete of supposed causalities turns the media into a catch-all and contagious "mana"; apocalyptic millenarianism invents the figure of a homo mass-mediaticus without ties to historical and social context, and so on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Debray_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-Debray-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Furthermore, when <i>Wired</i> interviewed him in 1995, Debray stated that he views McLuhan "more as a poet than a historian, a master of intellectual collage rather than a systematic analyst ... McLuhan overemphasizes the technology behind cultural change at the expense of the usage that the messages and codes make of that technology."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dwight_Macdonald" title="Dwight Macdonald">Dwight Macdonald</a>, in turn, reproached McLuhan for his focus on television and for his "aphoristic" style of prose, which he believes left <i>Understanding Media</i> filled with "contradictions, non-sequiturs, facts that are distorted and facts that are not facts, exaggerations, and chronic rhetorical vagueness." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mullen_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mullen-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Winston" title="Brian Winston">Brian Winston</a>’s <i>Misunderstanding Media</i>, published in 1986, chides McLuhan for what he sees as his <a href="/wiki/Technological_determinism" title="Technological determinism">technologically deterministic</a> stances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mullen_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mullen-61">[61]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Williams" title="Raymond Williams">Raymond Williams</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_W._Carey" title="James W. Carey">James W. Carey</a> further this point of contention, claiming:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The work of McLuhan was a particular culmination of an aesthetic theory which became, negatively, a social theory [...] It is an apparently sophisticated technological determinism which has the significant effect of indicating a social and cultural determinism [...] If the medium - whether print or television – is the cause, of all other causes, all that men ordinarily see as history is at once reduced to effects. (Williams 1990, 126/7)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mullen_61-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mullen-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>David Carr states that there has been a long line of "academics who have made a career out of deconstructing McLuhan’s effort to define the modern media ecosystem," whether it be due to what they see as McLuhan’s ignorance toward sociohistorical context or the style of his argument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>While some critics have taken issue with McLuhan’s writing style and mode of argument, McLuhan himself urged readers to think of his work as "probes" or "mosaics" offering a toolkit approach to thinking about the media. His eclectic writing style has also been praised for its postmodern sensibilities<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> and suitability for virtual space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Medium_Is_the_Massage:_An_Inventory_of_Effects_.281967.29"><i>The Medium Is the Massage: An Inventory of Effects</i> (1967)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Medium Is the Massage: An Inventory of Effects (1967)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Medium_Is_the_Massage" title="The Medium Is the Massage">The Medium Is the Massage</a></i>, published in 1967, was McLuhan's best seller,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_saint_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-wired_saint-7">[7]</a></sup> "eventually selling nearly a million copies worldwide."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Initiated by <a href="/wiki/Quentin_Fiore" title="Quentin Fiore">Quentin Fiore</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> McLuhan adopted the term "massage" to denote the effect each medium has on the human <a href="/wiki/Sensorium" title="Sensorium">sensorium</a>, taking inventory of the "effects" of numerous media in terms of how they "massage" the sensorium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fiore, at the time a prominent graphic designer and communications consultant, set about composing the visual illustration of these effects which were compiled by Jerome Agel. Near the beginning of the book, Fiore adopted a pattern in which an image demonstrating a media effect was presented with a textual synopsis on the facing page. The reader experiences a repeated shifting of analytic registers—from "reading" typographic print to "scanning" photographic facsimiles—reinforcing McLuhan's overarching argument in this book: namely, that each medium produces a different "massage" or "effect" on the human sensorium.</p>
<p>In <i>The Medium is the Massage</i>, McLuhan also rehashed the argument—which first appeared in the Prologue to 1962's <i>The Gutenberg Galaxy</i>—that all media are "extensions" of our human senses, bodies and minds.</p>
<p>Finally, McLuhan described key points of change in how man has viewed the world and how these views were changed by the adoption of new media. "The technique of invention was the discovery of the nineteenth [century]", brought on by the adoption of fixed points of view and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perspective_(cognitive)" title="Perspective (cognitive)">perspective</a> by typography, while "[t]he technique of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suspension_of_judgement" title="Suspension of judgement">suspended judgment</a> is the discovery of the twentieth century", brought on by the <a href="/wiki/Bard" title="Bard">bard</a> abilities of radio, movies and television.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>The past went that-a-way. When faced with a totally new situation we tend always to attach ourselves to the objects, to the flavor of the most recent past. We look at the present through a <a href="/wiki/Rear-view_mirror" title="Rear-view mirror">rear-view mirror</a>. We march backward into the future. Suburbia lives imaginatively in <a href="/wiki/Bonanza" title="Bonanza">Bonanza</a>-land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>An audio recording version of McLuhan's famous work was made by Columbia Records. The recording consists of a pastiche of statements made by McLuhan <i>interrupted</i> by other speakers, including people speaking in various <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">phonations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Falsetto" title="Falsetto">falsettos</a>, discordant sounds and 1960s incidental music in what could be considered a deliberate attempt to translate the disconnected images seen on TV into an audio format, resulting in the prevention of a connected stream of conscious thought. Various audio recording techniques and statements are used to illustrate the relationship between spoken, literary speech and the characteristics of electronic audio media. McLuhan biographer Philip Marchand called the recording "the 1967 equivalent of a McLuhan video."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>"I wouldn't be seen dead with a living work of art."—'Old man' speaking</dd>
<dd>"Drop this jiggery-pokery and talk straight turkey."—'Middle aged man' speaking</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="War_and_Peace_in_the_Global_Village_.281968.29"><i>War and Peace in the Global Village</i> (1968)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: War and Peace in the Global Village (1968)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>McLuhan used <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">James Joyce</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Finnegans_Wake" title="Finnegans Wake">Finnegans Wake</a></i>, an inspiration for this study of war throughout history, as an indicator as to how war may be conducted in the future.</p>
<p>Joyce's <i>Wake</i> is claimed to be a gigantic cryptogram which reveals a cyclic pattern for the whole history of man through its <b>Ten Thunders</b>. Each "thunder" below is a 100-character portmanteau of other words to create a statement he likens to an effect that each technology has on the society into which it is introduced. In order to glean the most understanding out of each, the reader must break the portmanteau into separate words (and many of these are themselves <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteaus</a> of words taken from multiple languages other than English) and speak them aloud for the spoken effect of each word. There is much dispute over what each portmanteau truly denotes.</p>
<p>McLuhan claims that the ten thunders in <i>Wake</i> represent different stages in the history of man:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Thunder 1: Paleolithic to Neolithic.</i> Speech. Split of East/West. From herding to harnessing animals.</li>
<li><i>Thunder 2: Clothing as weaponry.</i> Enclosure of private parts. First social aggression.</li>
<li><i>Thunder 3: Specialism.</i> Centralism via wheel, transport, cities: civil life.</li>
<li><i>Thunder 4: Markets and truck gardens.</i> Patterns of nature submitted to greed and power.</li>
<li><i>Thunder 5: Printing.</i> Distortion and translation of human patterns and postures and pastors.</li>
<li><i>Thunder 6: Industrial Revolution.</i> Extreme development of print process and individualism.</li>
<li><i>Thunder 7: Tribal man again.</i> All choractors end up separate, private man. Return of choric.</li>
<li><i>Thunder 8: Movies.</i> Pop art, pop Kulch via tribal radio. Wedding of sight and sound.</li>
<li><i>Thunder 9: Car and Plane.</i> Both centralizing and decentralizing at once create cities in crisis. Speed and death.</li>
<li><i>Thunder 10: Television.</i> Back to tribal involvement in tribal mood-mud. The last thunder is a turbulent, muddy wake, and murk of non-visual, tactile man.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="From_Clich.C3.A9_to_Archetype_.281970.29"><i>From Cliché to Archetype</i> (1970)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: From Cliché to Archetype (1970)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In his 1970 book, <i>From Cliché to Archetype</i>, McLuhan, collaborating with <a href="/wiki/Canadian_poetry" title="Canadian poetry">Canadian poet</a> <a href="/wiki/Wilfred_Watson" title="Wilfred Watson">Wilfred Watson</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watson_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-watson-72">[72]</a></sup> approached the various implications of the verbal <a href="/wiki/Clich%C3%A9" title="Cliché">cliché</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Archetype" title="Archetype">archetype</a>. One major facet in McLuhan's overall framework introduced in this book that is seldom noticed is the provision of a new term that actually succeeds the global village; the <i>global theater</i>.</p>
<p>In McLuhan's terms, a cliché is a "normal" action, phrase, etc. which becomes so often used that we are "anesthetized" to its effects.</p>
<p>An example of this given by McLuhan is <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Ionesco" title="Eugène Ionesco">Eugène Ionesco</a>'s play <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bald_Soprano" title="The Bald Soprano">The Bald Soprano</a></i>, whose dialogue consists entirely of phrases Ionesco pulled from an <a href="/wiki/Assimil" title="Assimil">Assimil</a> language book. "Ionesco originally put all these idiomatic English clichés into literary French which presented the English in the most absurd aspect possible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>McLuhan's <i>archetype</i> "is a quoted extension, medium, technology or environment." "Environment" would also include the kinds of "awareness" and cognitive shifts brought upon people by it, not totally unlike the psychological context <a href="/wiki/Carl_Jung" title="Carl Jung">Carl Jung</a> described.</p>
<p>McLuhan also posits that there is a factor of interplay between the cliché and the archetype, or a "doubleness":</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Another theme of the Wake [<i><a href="/wiki/Finnegans_Wake" title="Finnegans Wake">Finnegans Wake</a></i>] that helps in the understanding of the paradoxical shift from cliché to archetype is 'past time are pastimes.' The dominant technologies of one age become the games and pastimes of a later age. In the 20th century, the number of 'past times' that are simultaneously available is so vast as to create cultural anarchy. When all the cultures of the world are simultaneously present, the work of the artist in the elucidation of form takes on new scope and new urgency. Most men are pushed into the artist's role. The artist cannot dispense with the principle of 'doubleness' or 'interplay' because this type of <a href="/wiki/Hendiadys" title="Hendiadys">hendiadys</a> dialogue is essential to the very structure of consciousness, awareness, and autonomy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>McLuhan relates the cliché-to-archetype process to the <a href="/wiki/Theatre_of_the_Absurd" title="Theatre of the Absurd">Theater of the Absurd</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Pascal, in the seventeenth century, tells us that the heart has many reasons of which the head knows nothing. The Theater of the Absurd is essentially a communicating to the head of some of the silent languages of the heart which in two or three hundred years it has tried to forget all about. In the seventeenth century world the languages of the heart were pushed down into the unconscious by the dominant print cliché.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The "languages of the heart", or what McLuhan would otherwise define as oral culture, were thus made archetype by means of the printing press, and turned into cliché.</p>
<p>The satellite medium, McLuhan states, encloses the Earth in a man-made environment, which "ends 'Nature' and turns the globe into a repertory theater to be programmed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> All previous environments (book, newspaper, radio, etc.) and their artifacts are retrieved under these conditions ("past times are pastimes"). McLuhan thereby meshes this into the term <i>global theater</i>. It serves as an update to his older concept of the global village, which, in its own definitions, can be said to be subsumed into the overall condition described by that of the global theater.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Global_Village:_Transformations_in_World_Life_and_Media_in_the_21st_Century_.281989.29"><i>The Global Village: Transformations in World Life and Media in the 21st Century</i> (1989)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The Global Village: Transformations in World Life and Media in the 21st Century (1989)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In his 1989 posthumous book, <i>The Global Village</i>, McLuhan, collaborating with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bruce_R._Powers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bruce R. Powers (page does not exist)">Bruce R. Powers</a>, provided a strong conceptual framework for understanding the cultural implications of the technological advances associated with the rise of a worldwide electronic network. This is a major work of McLuhan's because it contains the most extensive elaboration of his concept of Acoustic Space, and it provides a critique of standard 20th century communication models like the <a href="/wiki/Shannon%E2%80%93Weaver_model" title="Shannon–Weaver model">Shannon–Weaver model</a>. McLuhan distinguishes between the existing worldview of Visual Space - a linear, quantitative, classically geometric model - and that of Acoustic Space - a holistic, qualitative order with a complex intricate paradoxical topology. "Acoustic Space has the basic character of a sphere whose focus or center is simultaneously everywhere and whose margin is nowhere."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> The transition from Visual to Acoustic Space was not automatic with the advent of the global network, but would have to be a conscious project. The "universal environment of simultaneous electronic flow"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> inherently favors right-brain Acoustic Space, yet we are held back by habits of adhering to a fixed point of view. There are no boundaries to sound. We hear from all directions at once. Yet Acoustic and Visual Space are in fact inseparable. The resonant interval is the invisible borderline between Visual and Acoustic Space. This is like the television camera that the Apollo 8 astronauts focused on the Earth after they had orbited the moon.</p>
<p>Reading, writing, and hierarchical ordering are associated with the left brain, as are the linear concept of time and phonetic literacy. The left brain is the locus of analysis, classification, and rationality. The right brain is the locus of the spatial, tactile, and musical. "Comprehensive awareness" results when the two sides of the brain are in true balance. Visual Space is associated with the simplified worldview of Euclidean geometry, the intuitive three dimensions useful for the architecture of buildings and the surveying of land. It is too rational and has no grasp of the acoustic. Acoustic Space is multisensory.</p>
<p>McLuhan writes about robotism in the context of Japanese Zen Buddhism and how it can offer us new ways of thinking about technology. The Western way of thinking about technology is too much related to the left hemisphere of our brain, which has a rational and linear focus. What he called robotism might better be called androidism in the wake of <i>Blade Runner</i> and the novels of Philip K. Dick. Robotism-androidism emerges from the further development of the right hemisphere of the brain, creativity and a new relationship to spacetime (most humans are still living in 17th century classical Newtonian physics spacetime). Robots-androids will have much greater flexibility than humans have had until now, in both mind and body. Robots-androids will teach humanity this new flexibility. And this flexibility of androids (what McLuhan calls robotism) has a strong affinity with Japanese culture and life. McLuhan quotes from Ruth Benedict, <i>The Chrysanthemum and the Sword</i>, an anthropological study of Japanese culture published in 1946: “Occidentals cannot easily credit the ability of the Japanese to swing from one behavior to another without psychic cost. Such extreme possibilities are not included in our experience. Yet in Japanese life the contradictions, as they seem to us, are as deeply based in their view of life as our uniformities are in ours.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> The ability to live in the present and instantly readjust.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Beyond_existing_communication_models">Beyond existing communication models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Beyond existing communication models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>"All Western scientific models of communication are—like the Shannon–Weaver model—linear, sequential, and logical as a reflection of the late medieval emphasis on the Greek notion of efficient causality."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> McLuhan and Powers criticize the Shannon-Weaver model of communication as emblematic of left-hemisphere bias and linearity, descended from Aristotelean causality.</p>
<p>A third term of <i>The Global Village</i> that McLuhan and Powers develop at length is The Tetrad. The tetrad is something like threads in a complexly interwoven flowing superspace, a four-fold pattern of transformation. "At full maturity the tetrad reveals the metaphoric structure of the artifact as having two figures and two grounds in dynamic and analogical relationship to each other." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> Like the camera focused on the Earth by the Apollo 8 astronauts, the tetrad reveals figure (Moon) and ground (Earth) simultaneously. The right-brain hemisphere thinking is the capability of being in many places at the same time. Electricity is acoustic. It is simultaneously everywhere. The Tetrad, with its fourfold Möbius topological structure of enhancement, reversal, retrieval and obsolescence, is mobilized by McLuhan and Powers to illuminate the media or technological inventions of cash money, the compass, the computer, the database, the satellite, and the global media network.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Key_concepts">Key concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Key concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tetrad">Tetrad</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Tetrad">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tetrad_of_media_effects" title="Tetrad of media effects">Tetrad of media effects</a></div>
<p>In <i>Laws of Media</i> (1988), published posthumously by his son <a href="/wiki/Eric_McLuhan" title="Eric McLuhan">Eric</a>, McLuhan summarized his ideas about <a href="/wiki/Media_studies" title="Media studies">media</a> in a concise tetrad of media effects. The tetrad is a means of examining the effects on society of any technology (i.e., any medium) by dividing its effects into four categories and displaying them simultaneously. McLuhan designed the tetrad as a pedagogical tool, phrasing his laws as questions with which to consider any medium:</p>
<ul>
<li>What does the medium enhance?</li>
<li>What does the medium make obsolete?</li>
<li>What does the medium retrieve that had been obsolesced earlier?</li>
<li>What does the medium flip into when pushed to extremes?</li>
</ul>
<p>The laws of the tetrad exist simultaneously, not successively or chronologically, and allow the questioner to explore the "grammar and syntax" of the "language" of media. McLuhan departs from his mentor <a href="/wiki/Harold_Innis" title="Harold Innis">Harold Innis</a> in suggesting that a medium "overheats", or reverses into an opposing form, when taken to its extreme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAC_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-LAC-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Visually, a tetrad can be depicted as four diamonds forming an X, with the name of a medium in the centre. The two diamonds on the left of a tetrad are the <i>Enhancement</i> and <i>Retrieval</i> qualities of the medium, both <i>Figure</i> qualities. The two diamonds on the right of a tetrad are the <i>Obsolescence</i> and <i>Reversal</i> qualities, both <i>Ground</i> qualities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EL_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-EL-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MediaTetrad.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="425" data-file-width="429" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/04/MediaTetrad.svg/250px-MediaTetrad.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/04/MediaTetrad.svg/375px-MediaTetrad.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/04/MediaTetrad.svg/500px-MediaTetrad.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MediaTetrad.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A blank tetrad diagram</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Using the example of radio:</p>
<ul>
<li>Enhancement (figure): What the medium amplifies or intensifies. <i>Radio amplifies news and music via sound.</i></li>
<li>Obsolescence (ground): What the medium drives out of prominence. <i>Radio reduces the importance of print and the visual.</i></li>
<li>Retrieval (figure): What the medium recovers which was previously lost. <i>Radio returns the spoken word to the forefront.</i></li>
<li>Reversal (ground): What the medium does when pushed to its limits. <i>Acoustic radio flips into audio-visual TV.</i></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Figure_and_ground">Figure and ground</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Figure and ground">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Figure_and_ground_(media)" title="Figure and ground (media)">Figure and ground (media)</a></div>
<p>McLuhan adapted the <a href="/wiki/Gestalt_psychology" title="Gestalt psychology">Gestalt psychology</a> idea of a <i>figure and a ground</i>, which underpins the meaning of "The medium is the message". He used this concept to explain how a form of communications technology, the medium or <i>figure</i>, necessarily operates through its context, or <i>ground</i>.</p>
<p>McLuhan believed that in order to grasp fully the effect of a new technology, one must examine figure (medium) and ground (context) together, since neither is completely intelligible without the other. McLuhan argued that we must study media in their historical context, particularly in relation to the technologies that preceded them. The present environment, itself made up of the effects of previous technologies, gives rise to new technologies, which, in their turn, further affect society and individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAC_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-LAC-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>All technologies have embedded within them their own assumptions about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time-_and_space-bias" title="Time- and space-bias">time and space</a>. The message which the medium conveys can only be understood if the medium and the environment in which the medium is used—and which, simultaneously, it effectively creates—are analysed together. He believed that an examination of the figure-ground relationship can offer a critical commentary on culture and society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAC_18-6"><a href="#cite_note-LAC-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_McLuhan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Marshall_McLuhan_Way_Toronto.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="144" data-file-width="270" height="117" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/ae/Marshall_McLuhan_Way_Toronto.jpg/220px-Marshall_McLuhan_Way_Toronto.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/ae/Marshall_McLuhan_Way_Toronto.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Marshall_McLuhan_Way_Toronto.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A portion of Toronto's St. Joseph Street is co-named Marshall McLuhan Way</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After the publication of <i>Understanding Media</i>, McLuhan received an astonishing amount of publicity, making him perhaps the most publicized English teacher in the twentieth century and arguably the most controversial.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2013)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> This publicity had much to do with the work of two California advertising executives, Gerald Feigen and <a href="/wiki/Howard_Gossage" title="Howard Gossage">Howard Gossage</a>, who used personal funds to fund their practice of "genius scouting."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Much enamoured with McLuhan's work, Feigen and Gossage arranged for McLuhan to meet with editors of several major New York magazines in May 1965 at the Lombardy Hotel in New York. Philip Marchand reports that, as a direct consequence of these meetings, McLuhan was offered the use of an office in the headquarters of both <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i>, any time he needed it.</p>
<p>In August 1965, Feigen and Gossage held what they called a "McLuhan festival" in the offices of Gossage's advertising agency in San Francisco. During this "festival", McLuhan met with advertising executives, members of the mayor's office, and editors from the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ramparts_(magazine)" title="Ramparts (magazine)">Ramparts</a> magazine. Perhaps more significant, however, was <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wolfe" title="Tom Wolfe">Tom Wolfe</a>'s presence at the festival, which he would later write about in his article, "What If He Is Right?", published in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Magazine" title="New York Magazine">New York Magazine</a></i> and Wolfe's own <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pump_House_Gang" title="The Pump House Gang">The Pump House Gang</a></i>. According to Feigen and Gossage, however, their work had only a moderate effect on McLuhan's eventual celebrity: they later claimed that their work only "probably speeded up the recognition of [McLuhan's] genius by about six months."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> In any case, McLuhan soon became a fixture of media discourse. <i>Newsweek</i> magazine did a cover story on him; articles appeared in <i>Life Magazine</i>, <i>Harper's</i>, <i>Fortune</i>, <i>Esquire</i>, and others. Cartoons about him appeared in <i>The New Yorker</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_saint_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-wired_saint-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1969, <i><a href="/wiki/Playboy" title="Playboy">Playboy</a></i> magazine published a lengthy interview with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>McLuhan was credited with coining the phrase <i><a href="/wiki/Turn_on,_tune_in,_drop_out" title="Turn on, tune in, drop out">Turn on, tune in, drop out</a></i> by its popularizer, <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Leary" title="Timothy Leary">Timothy Leary</a>, in the 1960s. In a 1988 interview with <a href="/wiki/Neil_Strauss" title="Neil Strauss">Neil Strauss</a>, Leary stated that slogan was "given to him" by McLuhan during a lunch in New York City. Leary said McLuhan "was very much interested in ideas and marketing, and he started singing something like, 'Psychedelics hit the spot / Five hundred micrograms, that’s a lot,' to the tune of a Pepsi commercial. Then he started going, 'Tune in, turn on, and drop out.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p>During his lifetime and afterward, McLuhan heavily influenced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cultural_criticism" title="Cultural criticism">cultural critics</a>, thinkers, and media theorists such as <a href="/wiki/Neil_Postman" title="Neil Postman">Neil Postman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Baudrillard" title="Jean Baudrillard">Jean Baudrillard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Leary" title="Timothy Leary">Timothy Leary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terence_McKenna" title="Terence McKenna">Terence McKenna</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Irwin_Thompson" title="William Irwin Thompson">William Irwin Thompson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Levinson" title="Paul Levinson">Paul Levinson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Rushkoff" title="Douglas Rushkoff">Douglas Rushkoff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jaron_Lanier" title="Jaron Lanier">Jaron Lanier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Kenner" title="Hugh Kenner">Hugh Kenner</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_David_Ebert&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John David Ebert (page does not exist)">John David Ebert</a>, as well as political leaders such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pierre_Elliott_Trudeau" title="Pierre Elliott Trudeau">Pierre Elliott Trudeau</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Brown" title="Jerry Brown">Jerry Brown</a>. <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Andy Warhol</a> was paraphrasing McLuhan with his now famous <a href="/wiki/15_minutes_of_fame" title="15 minutes of fame">15 minutes of fame</a> quote. When asked in the 70s for a way to sedate violences in <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>, he suggested a massive spread of TV devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> The character "Brian O'Blivion" in <a href="/wiki/David_Cronenberg" title="David Cronenberg">David Cronenberg</a>'s 1983 film <a href="/wiki/Videodrome" title="Videodrome">Videodrome</a> is a "media oracle" based on McLuhan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> In 1991, McLuhan was named as the "patron saint" of <a href="/wiki/Wired_(magazine)" title="Wired (magazine)"><i>Wired Magazine</i></a> and a quote of his appeared on the masthead<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> for the first ten years of its publication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_channel_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-wired_channel-89">[89]</a></sup> He is mentioned by name in a <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gabriel" title="Peter Gabriel">Peter Gabriel</a>-penned lyric in the song "Broadway Melody of 1974". This song appears on the <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lamb_Lies_Down_on_Broadway" title="The Lamb Lies Down on Broadway">The Lamb Lies Down on Broadway</a></i>, from <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Genesis_(band)" title="Genesis (band)">Genesis</a>. The lyric is: "Marshall McLuhan, casual viewin' head buried in the sand." McLuhan is also jokingly referred to during an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sopranos" title="The Sopranos">The Sopranos</a></i> entitled "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_Arrest_(The_Sopranos_episode)" title="House Arrest (The Sopranos episode)">House Arrest</a>". Despite his death in 1980, someone claiming to be McLuhan was posting on a <i>Wired</i> mailing list in 1996. The information this individual provided convinced one writer for <i>Wired</i> that "if the poster was not McLuhan himself, it was a bot programmed with an eerie command of McLuhan's life and inimitable perspective."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_channel_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-wired_channel-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>A new centre known as the <a href="/wiki/McLuhan_Program_in_Culture_and_Technology" title="McLuhan Program in Culture and Technology">McLuhan Program in Culture and Technology</a>, formed soon after his death in 1980, was the successor to McLuhan's Centre for Culture and Technology at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">University of Toronto</a>. Since 1994, it has been part of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto_Faculty_of_Information" title="University of Toronto Faculty of Information">University of Toronto Faculty of Information</a> and in 2008 the McLuhan Program in Culture and Technology incorporated in the Coach House Institute. The first director was literacy scholar and <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Institute_for_Studies_in_Education" title="Ontario Institute for Studies in Education">OISE</a> Professor David R. Olsen. From 1983 until 2008, the McLuhan Program was under the direction of Dr. <a href="/wiki/Derrick_de_Kerckhove" title="Derrick de Kerckhove">Derrick de Kerckhove</a> who was McLuhan's student and translator. From 2008 through 2015 Professor Dominique Scheffel-Dunand of <a href="/wiki/York_University" title="York University">York University</a> served Director of the Program. In 2011 at the time of his centenary the Coach House Institute established a Marshall McLuhan Centenary Fellowship program in his honor, and each year appoints up to four fellows for a maximum of two years. In May 2016 the Coach House Institute was renamed the McLuhan Centre for Culture and Technology; its Interim Director is <a href="/wiki/Seamus_Ross" title="Seamus Ross">Seamus Ross</a> (2015-16).</p>
<p>In Toronto, <a href="/wiki/Marshall_McLuhan_Catholic_Secondary_School" title="Marshall McLuhan Catholic Secondary School">Marshall McLuhan Catholic Secondary School</a> is named after him.</p>

<li id="cite_note-40"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-40">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The phrase "the medium is the message" may be better understood in light of <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Lonergan" title="Bernard Lonergan">Bernard Lonergan</a>'s further articulation of related ideas: at the empirical level of <a href="/wiki/Consciousness" title="Consciousness">consciousness</a>, the medium is the message, whereas at the intelligent and rational levels of consciousness, the content is the message. This sentence uses Lonergan's terminology from <i>Insight: A Study of Human Understanding</i> to clarify the meaning of McLuhan's statement that "the medium is the message"; McLuhan read this when it was first published in 1957 and found "much sense" in it—in his letter of September 21, 1957, to his former student and friend, Walter J. Ong, S.J., McLuhan says, "Find much sense in Bern. Lonergan's <i>Insight</i>" (<i>Letters of Marshall McLuhan</i>, 1987: 251). Lonergan's <i>Insight</i> is an extended guide to "making the inward turn": attending ever more carefully to one's own consciousness, reflecting on it ever more carefully, and monitoring one's articulations ever more carefully. When McLuhan declares that he is more interested in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Percept" title="Percept">percepts</a> than <a href="/wiki/Concept" title="Concept">concepts</a>, he is declaring in effect that he is more interested in what Lonergan refers to as the empirical level of consciousness than in what Lonergan refers to as the intelligent level of consciousness in which concepts are formed, which Lonergan distinguishes from the rational level of consciousness in which the adequacy of concepts and of predications is adjudicated. This inward turn to attending to percepts and to the cultural conditioning of the empirical level of consciousness through the effect of communication media sets him apart from more outward-oriented studies of sociological influences and the outward presentation of self carried out by <a href="/wiki/George_Herbert_Mead" title="George Herbert Mead">George Herbert Mead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erving_Goffman" title="Erving Goffman">Erving Goffman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_L._Berger" title="Peter L. Berger">Berger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Luckmann" title="Thomas Luckmann">Luckmann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Burke" title="Kenneth Burke">Kenneth Burke</a>, Hugh Duncan, and others.</span></li>
