<p><b>The Marshall Tucker Band</b> is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Southern_rock" title="Southern rock">Southern rock</a>/<a href="/wiki/Country_rock" title="Country rock">country rock</a> band originally from <a href="/wiki/Spartanburg,_South_Carolina" title="Spartanburg, South Carolina">Spartanburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>. The band's blend of <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">rhythm and blues</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup> helped establish the Southern rock genre in the early 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-larkin_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-larkin-2">[2]</a></sup> While the band had reached the height of its commercial success by the end of the decade, the band has recorded and performed continuously under various lineups for nearly 40 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-larkin_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-larkin-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The original lineup of the Marshall Tucker Band, formed in 1972, included lead guitarist, vocalist, and primary songwriter <a href="/wiki/Toy_Caldwell" title="Toy Caldwell">Toy Caldwell</a> (1947–1993), lead vocalist <a href="/wiki/Doug_Gray" title="Doug Gray">Doug Gray</a> (born 1948), keyboard player, saxophone player, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flautist" title="Flautist">flautist</a> <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Eubanks" title="Jerry Eubanks">Jerry Eubanks</a> (born 1950), rhythm guitarist <a href="/wiki/George_McCorkle" title="George McCorkle">George McCorkle</a> (1946–2007), drummer Paul Riddle (born 1953), and bassist <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Caldwell_(musician)" title="Tommy Caldwell (musician)">Tommy Caldwell</a> (1949–1980). They signed with <a href="/wiki/Capricorn_Records" title="Capricorn Records">Capricorn Records</a> and in 1973 released their first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_play" title="Long play">LP</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Marshall_Tucker_Band_(album)" title="The Marshall Tucker Band (album)">The Marshall Tucker Band</a></i>.</p>
<p>After Tommy Caldwell was killed in an automobile accident in 1980, he was replaced by bassist Franklin Wilkie. Most of the original band members had left by the mid-1980s to pursue other projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olson_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-olson-3">[3]</a></sup> The band's current lineup consists of Gray on vocals, keyboard player and flautist Marcus James Henderson, guitarist Rick Willis, bassist Pat Elwood, and drummer B.B. Borden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_the_name">Origin of the name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Marshall_Tucker_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin of the name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The "Marshall Tucker" in the band's name does not refer to a band member, but rather a Spartanburg-area piano tuner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> While the band was discussing possible band names one evening in an old warehouse they had rented for rehearsal space, someone noticed that the warehouse's door key had the name "Marshall Tucker" inscribed on it, and suggested they call themselves the "The Marshall Tucker Band," not realizing it referred to an actual person. It later came to light that Marshall Tucker, the blind piano tuner, had rented the space before the band, and his name was inscribed on the key.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup> In his book, <i>Top Pop Singles, 1955-2002</i>, music historian <a href="/wiki/Joel_Whitburn" title="Joel Whitburn">Joel Whitburn</a> attributes "Marshall Tucker" to the owner of the band's rehearsal hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Marshall_Tucker_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Marshall_Tucker_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The original members (and some later members) of the Marshall Tucker Band had been playing in various lineups under different band names around the Spartanburg area since the early 1960s. In 1966, members of several such bands merged to form the Toy Factory, named after guitarist Toy Caldwell. The Toy Factory's constantly shifting lineup included, at times, Caldwell, his younger brother Tommy, Doug Gray, Jerry Eubanks, George McCorkle, and Franklin Wilkie. In the late 1960s, four of the bandmembers served in the U.S. Armed Forces;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Toy Caldwell was wounded in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the 1970s, Toy Caldwell and George McCorkle had returned to Spartanburg, and the Toy Factory had resumed playing in area clubs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-smith-11">[11]</a></sup> In fact, in 1970 the Toy Factory was the opening act for the Allman Brothers when the Allmans played at the "Sitar" music lounge in Spartanburg.</p>
<p>In 1972, Caldwell and McCorkle once again revamped the band's lineup, eventually settling on Tommy Caldwell on bass, George McCorkle rhythm guitar, vocalist Doug Gray, and Jerry Eubanks, flute/tenor sax, while adding Paul Riddle on drums; the new lineup adopted the name "Marshall Tucker Band." Wet Willie lead singer Jimmy Hall told Toy Caldwell to book the band at Grant's Lounge in Macon which he did. After hearing the band play at Grant's Buddy Thornton and Paul Hornsby recorded the band's <a href="/wiki/Demo_(music)" title="Demo (music)">demo</a> at <a href="/wiki/Capricorn_Records" title="Capricorn Records">Capricorn Studios</a> in Macon, Ga. <a href="/wiki/Frank_Fenter" title="Frank Fenter">Frank Fenter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phil_Walden" title="Phil Walden">Phil Walden</a> signed The Marshall Tucker Band based on those demos.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s">1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Marshall_Tucker_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1970s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Marshall Tucker Band's <a href="/wiki/The_Marshall_Tucker_Band_(album)" title="The Marshall Tucker Band (album)">self-titled debut</a>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hornsby" title="Paul Hornsby">Paul Hornsby</a>, was released in 1973, and certified gold in 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elliott_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-elliott-12">[12]</a></sup> All of the tracks were written by Toy Caldwell, including "<a href="/wiki/Can%27t_You_See_(The_Marshall_Tucker_Band_song)" title="Can't You See (The Marshall Tucker Band song)">Can't You See</a>", which was released in 1973 on Capricorn 0023 ("Bubbled Under" at No. 108 on 1 September 1973) and re-released in 1977 on Capricorn 0278 (peaked at No. 75 on 24 September 1977). After the album's release, the band began touring, playing upwards of 300 shows per year throughout the decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elliott_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-elliott-12">[12]</a></sup> Southern rock fiddler <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Daniels" title="Charlie Daniels">Charlie Daniels</a> later recalled that the Marshall Tucker Band "came onstage and just blew it out from start to finish."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alfonso_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-alfonso-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Daniels' first of many collaborations with the Marshall Tucker Band came on the band's second album, <i><a href="/wiki/A_New_Life_(album)" title="A New Life (album)">A New Life</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alfonso_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-alfonso-13">[13]</a></sup> which was released in 1974, and certified gold in 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elliott_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-elliott-12">[12]</a></sup> Daniels and blues guitarist <a href="/wiki/Elvin_Bishop" title="Elvin Bishop">Elvin Bishop</a> were among several musicians that joined the band for <i><a href="/wiki/Where_We_All_Belong" title="Where We All Belong">Where We All Belong</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alfonso_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-alfonso-13">[13]</a></sup> a double-album (one studio album and one live album) released by the band in 1974 and certified gold that same year. The following year the band's <i><a href="/wiki/Searchin%27_for_a_Rainbow" title="Searchin' for a Rainbow">Searchin' for a Rainbow</a></i> was also certified gold the year of its release, and contained the track "Fire on the Mountain," which peaked at No. 38 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_charts" title="Billboard charts">Billboard charts</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Long_Hard_Ride" title="Long Hard Ride">Long Hard Ride</a></i>, the band's fifth consecutive gold album, was released in 1976,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alfonso_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-alfonso-13">[13]</a></sup> and its instrumental title track (which again features Charlie Daniels on fiddle) was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cortner_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-cortner-14">[14]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Carolina_Dreams" title="Carolina Dreams">Carolina Dreams</a></i>, released in 1977 and certified platinum that same year, proved to be the band's most commercially successful album, and included the track "<a href="/wiki/Heard_It_in_a_Love_Song" title="Heard It in a Love Song">Heard It in a Love Song</a>," which reached No. 14 on the Billboard charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elliott_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-elliott-12">[12]</a></sup> The band's final Capricorn release came with 1978's <i><a href="/wiki/Together_Forever_(The_Marshall_Tucker_Band_album)" title="Together Forever (The Marshall Tucker Band album)">Together Forever</a></i>, which was produced by <a href="/wiki/Stewart_Levine" title="Stewart Levine">Stewart Levine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alfonso_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-alfonso-13">[13]</a></sup> Following the bankruptcy of Capricorn, The Marshall Tucker Band moved to <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Records" title="Warner Bros. Records">Warner Bros. Records</a> for their ninth album, <i><a href="/wiki/Running_Like_the_Wind" title="Running Like the Wind">Running Like the Wind</a></i> (the band's eighth release was a compilation album entitled <i>Greatest Hits</i>), and they retained Levine as the album's producer.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Marshall_Tucker_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the completion of the band's tenth album, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Tenth_(The_Marshall_Tucker_Band_album)" title="Tenth (The Marshall Tucker Band album)">Tenth</a></i>, tragedy struck The Marshall Tucker Band. On April 22, 1980, the band's bassist and co-founder, Tommy Caldwell, suffered massive head trauma in a car wreck, and died six days later. Former Toy Factory bassist Franklin Wilkie replaced Caldwell, but the band was never able to recapture its commercial success of the 1970s. On 1982's <i>Tuckerized</i>, only two songs were written by band members; 'Sea, Dreams &amp; Fairy Tales' by Toy Caldwell and 'Sweet Elaine' by George McCorkle. Main songwriter Toy Caldwell only contributed three songs to each of the two albums released in 1983; <i>Just Us</i> and <i>Greetings from South Carolina</i>. After 1983's <i>Greetings from South Carolina</i>, all the rest of the original band members split, sans Doug Gray and Jerry Eubanks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-larkin_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-larkin-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1988 Gray and Eubanks recorded the album <i>Still Holdin' On</i>, their one and only release on the Mercury Records label. Although Gray and Eubanks added new members Rusty Milner, Stuart Swanlund, and Tim Lawter, <i>Still Holdin' On</i> was primarily recorded with studio musicians. The newer members (including newly added keyboardist Don Cameron) had a much greater role, however, on the band's 1990 album, <i>Southern Spirit</i>, released on the Sisaspa label. The album marked a return to the band's country and blues roots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tuckerhead_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-tuckerhead-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Marshall_Tucker_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1992, the Marshall Tucker Band produced its first album for the Cabin Fever label, <i>Still Smokin'</i>, which managed to crack the top 70 on the Billboard charts. The band's 1993 release, <i>Walk Outside the Lines</i>, marked a transition to a more country sound, relying less on long improvised jams that were the trademark of the band's early career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The album's title track was co-written by country music star <a href="/wiki/Garth_Brooks" title="Garth Brooks">Garth Brooks</a>, a long-time fan of the band who considered writing a track for them a "milestone" in his career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elliott_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-elliott-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>For 1998's <i>Face Down In the Blues</i>, the band added Spartanburg-area guitarist Ronald Radford and <a href="/wiki/Firefall" title="Firefall">Firefall</a>'s multi-instrumentalist David Muse, the latter replacing Jerry Eubanks, who had retired in 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alfonso_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-alfonso-13">[13]</a></sup> <i>Gospel</i>, the band's 1999 album, featured the band's rendition of traditional songs such as <a href="/wiki/The_Wayfaring_Stranger_(song)" title="The Wayfaring Stranger (song)">The Wayfaring Stranger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Can_the_Circle_Be_Unbroken_(By_and_By)" title="Can the Circle Be Unbroken (By and By)">Will the Circle Be Unbroken</a>, and several original tracks.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history">Recent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Marshall_Tucker_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Recent history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Marshall Tucker Band continued recording and performing into the 21st century, playing between 150 and 200 shows per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup> The band reissued many of its albums from the 1970s on its new Ramblin' Records label, as well as two two-disc compilations, the first (<i>Anthology</i>) being a 30-year retrospective and the second (<i>Where a Country Boy Belongs</i>) being a collection of the band's country songs. In 2004, they released another studio album, <i>Beyond the Horizon</i>, and the following year released a Christmas album, <i>Carolina Christmas</i>.</p>
<p><i>Can't You See</i> was used for the opening and closing credits of the <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Costner" title="Kevin Costner">Kevin Costner</a> 2008 motion picture <i><a href="/wiki/Swing_Vote_(2008_film)" title="Swing Vote (2008 film)">Swing Vote</a></i>. <i>Take the Highway</i> was also used in the movie.</p>
<p>"Can't You See" is also used in the 2001 film <i><a href="/wiki/Blow_(film)" title="Blow (film)">Blow</a></i>.</p>
<p>The band was mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Georgia_Line" title="Florida Georgia Line">Florida Georgia Line</a> song "<a href="/wiki/Cruise_(song)" title="Cruise (song)">Cruise</a>", which peaked at #16 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>, and spawned a remix featuring rapper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nelly_(rapper)" title="Nelly (rapper)">Nelly</a> which peaked at #6 on the Hot 100. "Cruise" was certified 6x-platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>, becoming the second-highest selling song in country music history, behind <a href="/wiki/Lady_Antebellum" title="Lady Antebellum">Lady Antebellum</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Need_You_Now_(Lady_Antebellum_song)" title="Need You Now (Lady Antebellum song)">Need You Now</a>".</p>
