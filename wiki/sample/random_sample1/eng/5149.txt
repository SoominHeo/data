<p><i><b>The Magic Flute</b></i> (German: <span lang="de"><i><b>Die Zauberflöte</b></i></span>), <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6chel_catalogue" title="Köchel catalogue">K.</a> 620, is an <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a> in two acts by <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart</a> to a German <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">libretto</a> by <a href="/wiki/Emanuel_Schikaneder" title="Emanuel Schikaneder">Emanuel Schikaneder</a>. The work is in the form of a <i><a href="/wiki/Singspiel" title="Singspiel">Singspiel</a></i>, a popular form that included both singing and spoken dialogue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[a]</a></sup> The work premiered on 30 September 1791 at Schikaneder's theatre, the <a href="/wiki/Theater_auf_der_Wieden" title="Theater auf der Wieden">Freihaus-Theater auf der Wieden</a> in Vienna, just two months before the composer's premature death.</p>
<p>In the opera the Queen of the Night persuades Prince Tamino to rescue her daughter Pamina from captivity under the high priest Sarastro; instead, he learns the high ideals of Sarastro's community and seeks to join it. Separately, then together, Tamino and Pamina undergo severe trials of initiation, which end in triumph, with the Queen and her cohorts vanquished. The earthy Papageno, who accompanies Tamino on his quest, fails the trials completely but is rewarded anyway with the hand of his ideal female companion Papagena.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Magic_Flute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The opera was the culmination of a period of increasing involvement by Mozart with Schikaneder's theatrical troupe, which since 1789 had been the resident company at the Theater auf der Wieden. Mozart was a close friend of one of the singer-composers of the troupe, tenor <a href="/wiki/Benedikt_Schack" title="Benedikt Schack">Benedikt Schack</a> (the first Tamino), and had contributed to the compositions of the troupe, which were often collaboratively written. Mozart's participation increased with his contributions to the 1790 collaborative opera <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Stein_der_Weisen" title="Der Stein der Weisen">Der Stein der Weisen</a></i> (<i>The Philosopher's Stone</i>), including the duet ("Nun liebes Weibchen", K. 625/592a) among other passages. Like <i>The Magic Flute</i>, <i>Der Stein der Weisen</i> was a fairy-tale opera and can be considered a kind of precursor; it employed much the same cast in similar roles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBuch1997_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuch1997-3">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The libretto for <i>The Magic Flute</i>, written by Schikaneder, is thought by scholars to be based on many sources. Some works of literature current in Vienna in Schikaneder's day that may have served as sources include the medieval romance <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yvain" title="Yvain">Yvain</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Chr%C3%A9tien_de_Troyes" title="Chrétien de Troyes">Chrétien de Troyes</a>, the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Sethos" title="Sethos">Sethos</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Terrasson" title="Jean Terrasson">Jean Terrasson</a>, and the essay "On the mysteries of the Egyptians" by <a href="/wiki/Ignaz_von_Born" title="Ignaz von Born">Ignaz von Born</a>. The libretto is also a natural continuation of a series of fairy tale operas produced at the time by Schikaneder's troupe, including an adaptation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sophie_Seyler" title="Sophie Seyler">Sophie Seyler</a>'s Singspiel <i><a href="/wiki/Oberon_(Seyler)" title="Oberon (Seyler)">Oberon</a></i> as well as <i>Der Stein der Weisen</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> Especially for the role of Papageno, the libretto draws on the <a href="/wiki/Hanswurst" title="Hanswurst">Hanswurst</a> tradition of the Viennese popular theater. Many scholars also acknowledge an influence of <a href="/wiki/Freemasonry" title="Freemasonry">Freemasonry</a>. For detailed discussion of sources see Branscombe (1991), as well as <i><a href="/wiki/Libretto_of_The_Magic_Flute" title="Libretto of The Magic Flute">Libretto of The Magic Flute</a></i>.</p>
<p>In composing the opera, Mozart evidently kept in mind the skills of the singers intended for the premiere, which included both virtuosi and ordinary comic actors asked to sing for the occasion. Thus, the vocal lines for Papageno—sung by Schikaneder himself—and Monostatos (<a href="/wiki/Johann_Joseph_Nouseul" title="Johann Joseph Nouseul">Johann Joseph Nouseul</a>) are often stated first in the strings so the singer can find his pitch, and are frequently doubled by instruments. In contrast, Mozart's sister-in-law <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Josepha_Hofer" title="Josepha Hofer">Josepha Hofer</a>, who premiered the role of the Queen of the Night, evidently needed little such help: this role is famous for its difficulty. In ensembles, Mozart skillfully combined voices of different ability levels.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Vocal_range" title="Vocal range">vocal ranges</a> of two of the original singers for whom Mozart tailored his music have posed challenges for many singers who have since recreated their roles. Both arias of the Queen of the Night, "<a href="/wiki/O_zittre_nicht,_mein_lieber_Sohn" title="O zittre nicht, mein lieber Sohn">O zittre nicht, mein lieber Sohn</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Der_H%C3%B6lle_Rache_kocht_in_meinem_Herzen" title="Der Hölle Rache kocht in meinem Herzen">Der Hölle Rache kocht in meinem Herzen</a>" require <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In_alt" title="In alt">high</a> <a href="/wiki/Scientific_pitch_notation" title="Scientific pitch notation">F<sub>6</sub></a>, rare in opera. At the low end, the part of Sarastro, premiered by <a href="/wiki/Franz_Xaver_Gerl" title="Franz Xaver Gerl">Franz Xaver Gerl</a>, includes a conspicuous F<sub>2</sub> in a few locations.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Premiere_and_reception">Premiere and reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Magic_Flute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Premiere and reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The opera was premiered in Vienna on 30 September 1791 at the suburban <a href="/wiki/Theater_auf_der_Wieden" title="Theater auf der Wieden">Freihaus-Theater auf der Wieden</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetropolitan_Opera2014_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetropolitan_Opera2014-5">[4]</a></sup> Mozart conducted the orchestra,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[b]</a></sup> Schikaneder himself played Papageno, while the role of the Queen of the Night was sung by Mozart's sister-in-law <a href="/wiki/Josepha_Weber" title="Josepha Weber">Josepha Hofer</a>.</p>
<p>On the reception of the opera, Mozart scholar <a href="/wiki/Maynard_Solomon" title="Maynard Solomon">Maynard Solomon</a> writes:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Although there were no reviews of the first performances,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[6]</a></sup> it was immediately evident that Mozart and Schikaneder had achieved a great success, the opera drawing immense crowds and reaching hundreds of performances during the 1790s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESolomon1995p..C2.A0487_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESolomon1995p..C2.A0487-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>As Mozart's letters show, he was very pleased to have achieved such a success. Solomon continues:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Mozart's delight is reflected in his last three letters, written to <a href="/wiki/Constanze_Mozart" title="Constanze Mozart">Constanze</a>, who with her sister Sophie was spending the second week of October in <a href="/wiki/Baden_bei_Wien" title="Baden bei Wien">Baden</a>. "I have this moment returned from the opera, which was as full as ever", he wrote on 7 October, listing the numbers that had to be encored. "But what always gives me the most pleasure is the <i>silent approval</i>! You can see how this opera is becoming more and more esteemed." … He went to hear his opera almost every night, taking along [friends and] relatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESolomon1995p..C2.A0487_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESolomon1995p..C2.A0487-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The opera celebrated its 100th performance in November 1792, though Mozart did not have the pleasure of witnessing this milestone, as he had died 5 December 1791. The opera was first performed outside Vienna (21 September 1792) in <a href="/wiki/Lviv" title="Lviv">Lemberg</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup> then in <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart_and_Prague" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart and Prague">Prague</a>. It then made (Branscombe) "triumphal progress through Germany's opera houses great and small",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[9]</a></sup> and with the early 19th century spread to essentially all the countries of Europe—and eventually, everywhere in the world—where opera is cultivated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>As Branscombe documents, the earlier performances were often of highly altered, sometimes even mutilated, versions of the opera (see <i><a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Wenzel_Lachnith" title="Ludwig Wenzel Lachnith">Ludwig Wenzel Lachnith</a></i>). Productions of the past century have tended to be more faithful to Mozart's music, though faithful rendering of Mozart and Schikaneder's original (quite explicit) stage directions and dramatic vision continues to be rare; with isolated exceptions modern productions strongly reflect the creative preferences of the stage director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Magic Flute</i> is presently among the top 10 most frequently performed operas worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_publication">First publication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Magic_Flute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First publication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 28 December 1791, three and a half weeks after Mozart's death, his widow Constanze offered to send a manuscript score of <i>The Magic Flute</i> to the electoral court in <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nikolaus_Simrock" title="Nikolaus Simrock">Nikolaus Simrock</a> published this text in the first full-score edition (Bonn, 1814), claiming that it was "in accordance with Mozart's own wishes" (<i><a href="/wiki/Allgemeine_musikalische_Zeitung" title="Allgemeine musikalische Zeitung">Allgemeine musikalische Zeitung</a></i>, 13 September 1815).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFreyhan2009_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFreyhan2009-16">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Magic_Flute_and_Freemasonry"><i>The Magic Flute</i> and Freemasonry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Magic_Flute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Magic Flute and Freemasonry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mozart_and_Freemasonry" title="Mozart and Freemasonry">Mozart and Freemasonry</a></div>
<p><i>The Magic Flute</i> is noted for its prominent <a href="/wiki/Freemasonry" title="Freemasonry">Masonic</a> elements,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[15]</a></sup> although some scholars claim that the Masonic influence is exaggerated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup> Schikaneder and Mozart were Masons as was <a href="/wiki/Ignaz_Alberti" title="Ignaz Alberti">Ignaz Alberti</a>, engraver and printer of the first libretto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup> The opera is also influenced by <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> philosophy, and can be regarded as an allegory advocating <a href="/wiki/Enlightened_absolutism" title="Enlightened absolutism">enlightened absolutism</a>. The Queen of the Night represents a dangerous form of <a href="/wiki/Obscurantism" title="Obscurantism">obscurantism</a> or, according to some, the anti-Masonic Roman Catholic <a href="/wiki/Maria_Theresa" title="Maria Theresa">Empress Maria Theresa</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup> or, according to others, the contemporary Roman Catholic Church itself, which was also <a href="/wiki/Papal_ban_of_Freemasonry" title="Papal ban of Freemasonry">strongly anti-Masonic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RefWordsworth_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-RefWordsworth-21">[19]</a></sup> Her antagonist Sarastro symbolises the enlightened sovereign who rules according to principles based on reason, wisdom, and nature. The story itself portrays the education of mankind, progressing from chaos (the serpent) through religious superstition (the Queen and Ladies) to rationalistic enlightenment (Sarastro and Priests), by means of trial (Tamino) and error (Papageno), ultimately to make "the Earth a heavenly kingdom, and mortals like the gods" ("<span lang="de">Dann ist die Erd' ein Himmelreich, und Sterbliche den Göttern gleich</span>"); this couplet is sung in the finales to both acts.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instrumentation">Instrumentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Magic_Flute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Instrumentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The work is scored for two <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flutes</a> (one doubling on <a href="/wiki/Piccolo" title="Piccolo">piccolo</a>), two <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboes</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinets</a> (doubling <a href="/wiki/Basset_horn" title="Basset horn">basset horns</a>), two <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoons</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Natural_horn" title="Natural horn">horns</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Trumpet" title="Trumpet">trumpets</a>, three <a href="/wiki/Trombone" title="Trombone">trombones</a> (alto, tenor, and bass), <a href="/wiki/Timpani" title="Timpani">timpani</a> and <a href="/wiki/String_section" title="String section">strings</a>. It also requires a four-part <a href="/wiki/Choir" title="Choir">chorus</a> for several numbers (notably the finales of each act). Mozart also called for a <i><span lang="it">stromento d'acciaio</span></i> (instrument of steel) to perform Papageno's magic bells; an instrument which has since been lost to history, though modern day scholars believe it to be a <a href="/wiki/Keyboard_glockenspiel" title="Keyboard glockenspiel">keyed glockenspiel</a>, which is usually substituted with a <a href="/wiki/Celesta" title="Celesta">celesta</a> in modern-day performances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Charles_Rosen" title="Charles Rosen">Charles Rosen</a> has remarked on the character of Mozart's orchestration:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>Die Zauberflöte</i> has the greatest variety of orchestral color that the eighteenth century was to know; the very lavishness, however, is paradoxically also an economy as each effect is a concentrated one, each one – Papageno's whistle, the Queen of the Night's coloratura, the bells, Sarastro's trombones, even the farewell in Scene I for clarinets and pizzicato strings – a single dramatic stroke."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[25]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>The opera begins with the overture, which Mozart composed last.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy-31">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Act_1">Act 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Magic_Flute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Act 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Scene 1: A rough, rocky landscape</i></p>
<p>Tamino, a handsome prince lost in a distant land, is pursued by a serpent and asks the gods to save him (aria: "<span lang="de">Zu Hilfe! Zu Hilfe!</span>" segued into trio: "<span lang="de">Stirb, Ungeheuer, durch uns’re Macht!</span>"). He faints, and three ladies, attendants of the Queen of the Night, appear and kill the serpent. They find the unconscious prince extremely attractive, and each of them tries to convince the other two to leave. After arguing, they reluctantly decide to leave together.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Salzburger_Festspiele_2012_-_Die_Zauberfl%C3%B6te.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2485" data-file-width="1654" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Salzburger_Festspiele_2012_-_Die_Zauberfl%C3%B6te.jpg/220px-Salzburger_Festspiele_2012_-_Die_Zauberfl%C3%B6te.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Salzburger_Festspiele_2012_-_Die_Zauberfl%C3%B6te.jpg/330px-Salzburger_Festspiele_2012_-_Die_Zauberfl%C3%B6te.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Salzburger_Festspiele_2012_-_Die_Zauberfl%C3%B6te.jpg/440px-Salzburger_Festspiele_2012_-_Die_Zauberfl%C3%B6te.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Salzburger_Festspiele_2012_-_Die_Zauberfl%C3%B6te.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Baritone <a href="/wiki/Markus_Werba" title="Markus Werba">Markus Werba</a> appearing as Papageno. He wears his pipes and carries his magic bells; both instruments are essential to the plot.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Tamino wakes up, and is surprised to find himself still alive. Papageno enters dressed as a bird. He describes his life as a bird-catcher, complaining he has no wife or girlfriend (aria: "<span lang="de">Der Vogelfänger bin ich ja</span>"). Tamino introduces himself to Papageno, thinking Papageno killed the serpent. Papageno happily takes the credit – claiming he strangled it with his bare hands. The three ladies suddenly reappear and instead of giving him wine, cake and figs, they give him water, a stone and place a padlock over his mouth as a warning not to lie. They give Tamino a portrait of the Queen of the Night's daughter Pamina, with whom Tamino falls instantly in love (aria: "<a href="/wiki/Dies_Bildnis_ist_bezaubernd_sch%C3%B6n" title="Dies Bildnis ist bezaubernd schön">Dies Bildnis ist bezaubernd schön</a>" / This image is enchantingly beautiful).</p>
<p>The ladies return and tell Tamino that Pamina has been captured by Sarastro, a supposedly evil sorcerer. Tamino vows to rescue Pamina. The Queen of the Night appears and promises Tamino that Pamina will be his if he rescues her from Sarastro (Recitative and aria: "<a href="/wiki/O_zittre_nicht,_mein_lieber_Sohn" title="O zittre nicht, mein lieber Sohn">O zittre nicht, mein lieber Sohn</a>" / Oh, tremble not, my dear son!). The Queen leaves and the ladies remove the padlock from Papageno's mouth with a warning not to lie any more. They give Tamino a magic flute which has the power to change sorrow into joy. They tell Papageno to go with Tamino, and give him (Papageno) magic bells for protection. The ladies introduce three child-spirits, who will guide Tamino and Papageno to Sarastro's temple. Together Tamino and Papageno set forth (Quintet: "Hm! Hm! Hm! Hm!").</p>
<p><i>Scene 2: A room in Sarastro's palace</i></p>
<p>Pamina is dragged in by Sarastro's slaves, apparently having tried to escape. Monostatos, a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blackamoor" title="wikt:blackamoor">blackamoor</a> and chief of the slaves, orders the slaves to chain her and leave him alone with her. Papageno, sent ahead by Tamino to help find Pamina, enters (Trio: "<span lang="de">Du feines Täubchen, nur herein!</span>"). Monostatos and Papageno are each terrified by the other's strange appearance and both flee. Papageno returns and announces to Pamina that her mother has sent Tamino to save her. Pamina rejoices to hear that Tamino is in love with her. She offers sympathy and hope to Papageno, who longs for a wife. Together they reflect on the joys and sacred duties of marital love (duet: "<span lang="de">Bei Männern welche Liebe fühlen</span>").</p>
<p><i>Finale. Scene 3: A grove in front of a temple</i></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SchafferBrothersDesignForMagicFlute1793ArrivalOfSarastro.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="880" data-file-width="1126" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/SchafferBrothersDesignForMagicFlute1793ArrivalOfSarastro.png/220px-SchafferBrothersDesignForMagicFlute1793ArrivalOfSarastro.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/SchafferBrothersDesignForMagicFlute1793ArrivalOfSarastro.png/330px-SchafferBrothersDesignForMagicFlute1793ArrivalOfSarastro.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/SchafferBrothersDesignForMagicFlute1793ArrivalOfSarastro.png/440px-SchafferBrothersDesignForMagicFlute1793ArrivalOfSarastro.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SchafferBrothersDesignForMagicFlute1793ArrivalOfSarastro.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The arrival of Sarastro on a chariot pulled by lions, from a 1793 production in <a href="/wiki/Brno" title="Brno">Brno</a>. Pamina appears at left, Papageno at right. In the background are the temples of Wisdom, Reason, and Nature.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The three child-spirits lead Tamino to Sarastro's temple, promising that if he remains patient, wise and steadfast, he will succeed in rescuing Pamina. Tamino approaches the left-hand entrance and is denied access by voices from within. The same happens when he goes to the entrance on the right. But from the entrance in the middle, an old priest appears and lets Tamino in. (The old priest is referred to as "The Speaker" in the libretto, but his role is a singing role.) He tells Tamino that Sarastro is benevolent, not evil, and that he should not trust the Queen of the Night. He promises that Tamino's confusion will be lifted when Tamino approaches the temple as a friend. Tamino plays his magic flute. Animals appear and dance, enraptured, to his music. Tamino hears Papageno's pipes sounding offstage, and hurries off to find him.</p>
<p>Papageno and Pamina enter, searching for Tamino. They are recaptured by Monostatos and his slaves. Papageno plays his magic bells, and Monostatos and his slaves begin to dance, and exit the stage, still dancing, mesmerised by the beauty of the music (aria: "<span lang="de">Das klinget so herrlich</span>"). Papageno and Pamina hear the sound of Sarastro's retinue approaching. Papageno is frightened and asks Pamina what they should say. She answers that they must tell the truth. Sarastro enters, with a crowd of followers.</p>
<p>Pamina falls at Sarastro's feet and confesses that she tried to escape because Monostatos had forced his attentions on her. Sarastro receives her kindly and assures her that he wishes only for her happiness. But he refuses to return her to her mother, whom he describes as a proud, headstrong woman, and a bad influence on those around her. Pamina, he says, must be guided by a man.</p>
<p>Monostatos brings in Tamino. The two lovers see one another for the first time and embrace, causing indignation among Sarastro's followers. Monostatos tells Sarastro that he caught Papageno and Pamina trying to escape, and demands a reward. Sarastro, however, punishes Monostatos for his lustful behaviour toward Pamina, and sends him away. He announces that Tamino must undergo trials of wisdom in order to become worthy as Pamina's husband. The priests declare that virtue and righteousness will sanctify life and make mortals like gods ("<span lang="de">Wenn Tugend und Gerechtigkeit</span>").</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Act_2">Act 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Magic_Flute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Act 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Scene 1: A grove of palms</i></p>
<p>The council of priests of <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Osiris" title="Osiris">Osiris</a>, headed by Sarastro, enters to the sound of a solemn march. Sarastro tells the priests that Tamino is ready to undergo the ordeals that will lead to enlightenment. He invokes the gods Isis and Osiris, asking them to protect Tamino and Pamina (Aria and chorus: "<span lang="de">O Isis und Osiris</span>").</p>
<p><i>Scene 2: The courtyard of the Temple of Ordeal</i></p>
<p>Tamino and Papageno are led in by two priests for the first trial. The two priests advise Tamino and Papageno of the dangers ahead of them, warn them of women's wiles and swear them to silence (Duet: "<span lang="de">Bewahret euch von Weibertücken</span>"). The three ladies appear and try to frighten Tamino and Papageno into speaking. (Quintet: "<span lang="de">Wie, wie, wie</span>") Papageno cannot resist answering the ladies, but Tamino remains aloof, angrily instructing Papageno not to listen to the ladies' threats and to keep quiet. Seeing that Tamino will not speak to them, the ladies withdraw in confusion.</p>
<p><i>Scene 3: A garden</i></p>
<p>Pamina is asleep. Monostatos approaches and gazes upon her with rapture. (Aria: "<span lang="de">Alles fühlt der Liebe Freuden</span>") He is about to kiss the sleeping Pamina, when the Queen of the Night appears. She gives Pamina a dagger, ordering her to kill Sarastro with it and threatening to disown her if she does not. (Aria: "<a href="/wiki/Der_H%C3%B6lle_Rache_kocht_in_meinem_Herzen" title="Der Hölle Rache kocht in meinem Herzen">Der Hölle Rache kocht in meinem Herzen</a>"). She leaves. Monostatos returns and tries to force Pamina's love by threatening to reveal the Queen's plot, but Sarastro enters and drives him off. Pamina begs Sarastro to forgive her mother and he reassures her that revenge and cruelty have no place in his domain (Aria: "<span lang="de">In diesen heil'gen Hallen</span>").</p>
<p><i>Scene 4: A hall in the Temple of Ordeal</i></p>
<p>Tamino and Papageno are led in by priests, who remind them that they must remain silent. Papageno complains of thirst. An old woman enters and offers Papageno a cup of water. He drinks and teasingly asks whether she has a boyfriend. She replies that she does and that his name is Papageno. She disappears as Papageno asks for her name, and the three child-spirits bring in food, the magic flute, and the bells, sent from Sarastro. Tamino begins to play the flute, which summons Pamina. She tries to speak with him, but Tamino, bound by his vow of silence, cannot answer her, and Pamina begins to believe that he no longer loves her. (Aria: "<span lang="de">Ach, ich fühl's, es ist verschwunden</span>") She leaves in despair.</p>
<p><i>Scene 5: The pyramids</i></p>
<p>The priests celebrate Tamino's successes so far, and pray that he will succeed and become worthy of their order (Chorus: "<span lang="de">O Isis und Osiris</span>"). Pamina is brought in and Sarastro instructs Pamina and Tamino to bid each other farewell before the greater trials ahead, alarming them by describing it as their "final farewell." (Trio: Sarastro, Pamina, Tamino – "<span lang="de">Soll ich dich, Teurer, nicht mehr sehn?</span>" Note: In order to preserve the continuity of Pamina's suicidal feelings, this trio is sometimes performed earlier in act 2, preceding or immediately following Sarastro's aria "<span lang="de">O Isis und Osiris</span>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[f]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[27]</a></sup>) They exit and Papageno enters. The priests grant his request for a glass of wine and he expresses his desire for a wife. (Aria: "<span lang="de">Ein Mädchen oder Weibchen</span>"). The elderly woman reappears and warns him that unless he immediately promises to marry her, he will be imprisoned forever. When Papageno promises to love her faithfully (muttering that he will only do this until something better comes along), she is transformed into the young and pretty Papagena. Papageno rushes to embrace her, but the priests drive him back, telling him that he is not yet worthy of her.</p>
<p><i>Finale. Scene 6: A garden</i></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Max_Slevogt_Zauberfl%C3%B6te_Szene.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="278" data-file-width="350" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Max_Slevogt_Zauberfl%C3%B6te_Szene.jpg/300px-Max_Slevogt_Zauberfl%C3%B6te_Szene.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f0/Max_Slevogt_Zauberfl%C3%B6te_Szene.jpg 1.5x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Max_Slevogt_Zauberfl%C3%B6te_Szene.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tamino and Pamina undergo their final trial; watercolor by <a href="/wiki/Max_Slevogt" title="Max Slevogt">Max Slevogt</a> (1868–1932)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The three child-spirits hail the dawn. They observe Pamina, who is contemplating suicide because she believes Tamino has abandoned her. The child-spirits restrain her and reassure her of Tamino's love. (Quartet: "<span lang="de">Bald prangt, den Morgen zu verkünden</span>").</p>
<p><i>Scene change without interrupting the music, to Scene 7: Outside the Temple of Ordeal</i></p>
<p>Two men in armor lead in Tamino. They recite one of the formal creeds of Isis and Osiris, promising enlightenment to those who successfully overcome the fear of death ("<span lang="de">Der, welcher wandert diese Strasse voll Beschwerden</span>"). This recitation takes the musical form of a <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">Baroque</a> <a href="/wiki/Chorale_prelude" title="Chorale prelude">chorale prelude</a>, to the tune of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Hymn" title="Hymn">hymn</a> "<a href="/wiki/Ach_Gott,_vom_Himmel_sieh_darein" title="Ach Gott, vom Himmel sieh darein">Ach Gott, vom Himmel sieh darein</a>" (Oh God, look down from heaven).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[g]</a></sup> Tamino declares that he is ready to be tested. Pamina calls to him from offstage. The men in armour assure him that the trial by silence is over and he is free to speak with her. Pamina enters and declares her intention to undergo the remaining trials with him. She hands him the magic flute to help them through the trials ("<span lang="de">Tamino mein, o welch ein Glück!</span>"). Protected by the music of the magic flute, they pass unscathed through chambers of fire and water. Offstage, the priests hail their triumph and invite the couple to enter the temple.</p>
<p><i>Scene change without interrupting the music, to Scene 8: A garden with a tree</i></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:15031-Magic_Flute_Production-0413_(15830972013).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2515" data-file-width="3780" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/15031-Magic_Flute_Production-0413_%2815830972013%29.jpg/300px-15031-Magic_Flute_Production-0413_%2815830972013%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/15031-Magic_Flute_Production-0413_%2815830972013%29.jpg/450px-15031-Magic_Flute_Production-0413_%2815830972013%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/15031-Magic_Flute_Production-0413_%2815830972013%29.jpg/600px-15031-Magic_Flute_Production-0413_%2815830972013%29.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:15031-Magic_Flute_Production-0413_(15830972013).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Papageno and Papagena. From a production at Texas A&amp;M University-Commerce.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Papageno despairs at having lost Papagena and decides to hang himself (Aria/Quartet: "Papagena! Papagena! Papagena! Weibchen, Täubchen, meine Schöne") The three child-spirits appear and stop him. They advise him to play his magic bells to summon Papagena. She appears and, united, the happy couple stutter in astonishment and make bird-like courting sounds at each other. They plan their future and dream of the many children they will have together (Duet: "Pa … pa … pa ...").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[h]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Scene change without interrupting the music, to Scene 9: A rocky landscape outside the temple; night</i></p>
<p>The traitorous Monostatos appears with the Queen of the Night and her three ladies. They plot to destroy the temple ("<span lang="de">Nur stille, stille</span>") and the Queen confirms that she has promised her daughter Pamina to Monostatos. But before the conspirators can enter the temple, they are magically cast out into eternal night.</p>
<p><i>Scene change without interrupting the music, to Scene 10: The Temple of the Sun</i></p>
<p>Sarastro announces the sun's triumph over the night. Everyone praises the courage of Tamino and Pamina, gives thanks to Isis and Osiris and hails the dawn of a new era of wisdom and brotherhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMelitz1913_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMelitz1913-37">[29]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recordings">Recordings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Magic_Flute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Recordings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Magic_Flute_discography" title="The Magic Flute discography">The Magic Flute discography</a></div>
<p>The first recording of <i>The Magic Flute</i> was of a live performance at the 1937 <a href="/wiki/Salzburg_Festival" title="Salzburg Festival">Salzburg Festival</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Toscanini" title="Arturo Toscanini">Arturo Toscanini</a> conducting the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Philharmonic" title="Vienna Philharmonic">Vienna Philharmonic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vienna_State_Opera" title="Vienna State Opera">Vienna State Opera</a>. The first studio recording of the work, with Sir <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Beecham" title="Thomas Beecham">Thomas Beecham</a> conducting the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Philharmonic" title="Berlin Philharmonic">Berlin Philharmonic</a>, was completed in 1938. Both of these historic recordings have been reissued on modern recording media. Since then there have been many recordings, in both audio and video formats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[31]</a></sup></p>
