<p><b>Mars 1</b>, also known as <b>1962 Beta Nu 1</b>, <b>Mars 2MV-4</b> and <b>Sputnik 23</b>, was an automatic interplanetary station launched in the direction of <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> on November 1, 1962,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sputnik_23_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sputnik_23-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mihos_2006_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mihos_2006-2">[2]</a></sup> the first of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_probe_program" title="Mars probe program">Mars probe program</a>, with the intent of flying by the planet at a distance of about 11,000 km (6,800 mi). It was designed to image the surface and send back data on cosmic radiation, <a href="/wiki/Micrometeoroid" title="Micrometeoroid">micrometeoroid</a> impacts and Mars' magnetic field, radiation environment, atmospheric structure, and possible organic compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sputnik_23_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sputnik_23-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mihos_2006_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mihos_2006-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After leaving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_orbit" title="Earth orbit">Earth orbit</a>, the spacecraft and the <a href="/wiki/Molniya_(rocket)" title="Molniya (rocket)">Molniya (rocket)</a> booster fourth stage separated and the solar panels were deployed. Early <a href="/wiki/Telemetry" title="Telemetry">telemetry</a> indicated that there was a leak in one of the gas valves in the orientation system so the spacecraft was transferred to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyroscopic" title="Gyroscopic">gyroscopic</a> stabilization. Sixty-one radio transmissions were held, initially at two-day intervals and later at five days in which a large amount of interplanetary data were collected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sputnik_23_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sputnik_23-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 21 March 1963, when the spacecraft was at a distance of 106,760,000 km (66,340,000 mi) from Earth on its way to Mars, communications ceased, probably due to failure of the spacecraft's antenna orientation system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sputnik_23_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sputnik_23-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mihos_2006_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mihos_2006-2">[2]</a></sup> Mars 1 closest approach to Mars probably occurred on June 19, 1963 at a distance of approximately 193,000 km (120,000 mi), after which the spacecraft entered an <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">orbit around the Sun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mihos_2006_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Mihos_2006-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft_design">Spacecraft design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spacecraft design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mars 1 was a modified <a href="/wiki/Venera" title="Venera">Venera</a>-type spacecraft in the shape of a cylinder 3.3 m (11 ft) long and 1 m (3.3 ft) in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sputnik_23_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Sputnik_23-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mihos_2006_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Mihos_2006-2">[2]</a></sup> The spacecraft measured 4 m (13 ft) across with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_module" title="Photovoltaic module">solar panels</a> and radiators deployed. The cylinder was divided into two compartments. The upper 2.7 m (8.9 ft), the orbital module, contained guidance and on-board propulsion systems. The experiment module, containing the scientific instrumentation, comprised the bottom 0.6 m (2.0 ft) of the cylinder. A 1.7 m (5.6 ft) parabolic high gain antenna was used for communication, along with an omnidirectional antenna and a semi-directional antenna. Power was supplied by two solar panel wings with a total area of 2.6 m<sup>2</sup> (28 sq ft) affixed to opposite sides of the spacecraft. Power was stored in a 42 ampere-hour cadmium-nickel battery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mihos_2006_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Mihos_2006-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>Communications were via a decimeter wavelength radio transmitter mounted in the orbital module which used the high-gain antenna. This was supplemented by a metre wavelength range transmitter through the omnidirectional antenna. An 8 centimetre wavelength transmitter mounted in the experiment module was designed to transmit the TV images.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sputnik_23_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Sputnik_23-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mihos_2006_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Mihos_2006-2">[2]</a></sup> Also mounted in the experiment module was a 5-centimeter range impulse transmitter. Temperature control was achieved using a binary gas–liquid system and hemispherical radiators mounted on the ends of the solar panels. The craft carried various scientific instruments including a <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometer</a> probe, television photographic equipment, a spectroreflexometer, radiation sensors (gas-discharge and scintillation counters), a spectrograph to study ozone absorption bands, and a micrometeoroid instrument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sputnik_23_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Sputnik_23-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mihos_2006_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Mihos_2006-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_results">Scientific results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scientific results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The probe recorded one micrometeorite strike every two minutes at altitudes ranging from 6,000 to 40,000 km (3,700 to 24,900 mi) from Earth's surface due to the <a href="/wiki/Taurids" title="Taurids">Taurids</a> meteor shower, and also recorded similar densities at distances from 20 to 40 million kilometres (12,000,000 to 25,000,000 mi) from Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sputnik_23_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Sputnik_23-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mihos_2006_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Mihos_2006-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">Magnetic field</a> intensities of 3–4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanotesla" title="Nanotesla">nanoteslas</a> (nT, also known as gammas) with peaks as high as 6–9 nT were measured in interplanetary space.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> was detected.</li>
<li>The radiation zones around Earth were detected,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mars12mv_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-mars12mv-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bookmars_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-bookmars-5">[5]</a></sup> and their magnitude confirmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mihos_2006_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-Mihos_2006-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Designation">Designation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Designation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This spacecraft is also referenced as <i>Sputnik 23</i> and <i>Mars 2MV-4</i>. It was originally designated <i>Sputnik 30</i> in the U.S. Naval Space Command Satellite Situation Summary.</p>
<p>Although it was called Mars 1, there was at least three other probes prior to this <a href="/wiki/Mars_2MV-4_No.1" title="Mars 2MV-4 No.1">Mars 2MV-4 No.1</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mars_1M_No.2" title="Mars 1M No.2">Mars 1M No.2</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mars_1M_No.1" title="Mars 1M No.1">Mars 1M No.1</a></p>
