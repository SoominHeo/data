<p><i><b>Mars Pathfinder</b></i> (<i><b>MESUR Pathfinder</b></i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-Sojourner_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-Sojourner-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> is an American <a href="/wiki/Robotic_spacecraft" title="Robotic spacecraft">robotic spacecraft</a> that landed a base station with a <a href="/wiki/Rover_(space_exploration)" title="Rover (space exploration)">roving probe</a> on <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> in 1997. It consisted of a <a href="/wiki/Lander_(spacecraft)" title="Lander (spacecraft)">lander</a>, renamed the <b>Carl Sagan Memorial Station</b>, and a lightweight (10.6 kg/23 lb) wheeled <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">robotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Mars_rover" title="Mars rover">Mars rover</a> named <i><a href="/wiki/Sojourner_(rover)" title="Sojourner (rover)">Sojourner</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> which became the first rover to operate outside the Earth-Moon system.</p>
<p>Launched on December 4, 1996 by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> aboard a <a href="/wiki/Delta_II" title="Delta II">Delta II</a> booster a month after the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Global_Surveyor" title="Mars Global Surveyor">Mars Global Surveyor</a> was launched, it landed on July 4, 1997 on <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ares_Vallis" title="Ares Vallis">Ares Vallis</a>, in a region called <a href="/wiki/Chryse_Planitia" title="Chryse Planitia">Chryse Planitia</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Oxia_Palus_quadrangle" title="Oxia Palus quadrangle">Oxia Palus quadrangle</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Lander_(spacecraft)" title="Lander (spacecraft)">lander</a> then opened, exposing the rover which conducted many experiments on the Martian surface. The mission carried a series of scientific instruments to analyze the Martian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body_atmosphere" title="Celestial body atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>, geology and the composition of its <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a> and soil. It was the second project from NASA's <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Program" title="Discovery Program">Discovery Program</a>, which promotes the use of low-cost spacecraft and frequent launches under the motto "cheaper, faster and better" promoted by the then administrator, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Goldin" title="Daniel Goldin">Daniel Goldin</a>. The mission was directed by the <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a> (JPL), a division of the <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a>, responsible for NASA's <a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_Mars" title="Exploration of Mars">Mars Exploration Program</a>. The project manager was JPL's <a href="/wiki/Tony_Spear" title="Tony Spear">Tony Spear</a>.</p>
<p>This mission was the first of a series of missions to Mars that included rovers, and was the first successful lander since the two <i><a href="/wiki/Viking_program" title="Viking program">Vikings</a></i> landed on the red planet in 1976. Although the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> successfully sent rovers to the Moon as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunokhod_program" title="Lunokhod program">Lunokhod program</a> in the 1970s, its attempts to use rovers in its <a href="/wiki/Mars_program" title="Mars program">Mars program</a> failed.</p>
<p>In addition to scientific objectives, the <i>Mars Pathfinder</i> mission was also a "proof-of-concept" for various technologies, such as <a href="/wiki/Airbag" title="Airbag">airbag</a>-mediated touchdown and automated obstacle avoidance, both later exploited by the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Exploration_Rover" title="Mars Exploration Rover">Mars Exploration Rover</a> mission. The <i>Mars Pathfinder</i> was also remarkable for its extremely low cost relative to other unmanned space missions to Mars. Originally, the mission was conceived as the first of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_Environmental_Survey" title="Mars Environmental Survey">Mars Environmental Survey</a> (MESUR) program.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_objectives">Mission objectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_Pathfinder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mission objectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>To prove that the development of "faster, better and cheaper" spacecraft was possible (with three years for development and a cost under $150 million).</li>
<li>To show that it was possible to send a load of scientific instruments to another planet with a simple system and at one fifteenth the cost of a <a href="/wiki/Viking_program" title="Viking program">Viking</a> mission. (For comparison, the Viking missions cost $935 million in 1974<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> or $3.5 billion in 1997 dollars.)</li>
<li>To demonstrate NASA's commitment to low-cost planetary exploration by finishing the mission with a total expenditure of $280 million, including the launch vehicle and mission operations.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_experiments">Science experiments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_Pathfinder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Science experiments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sojourner_on_Mars_PIA01122.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1170" data-file-width="1276" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Sojourner_on_Mars_PIA01122.jpg/200px-Sojourner_on_Mars_PIA01122.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Sojourner_on_Mars_PIA01122.jpg/300px-Sojourner_on_Mars_PIA01122.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Sojourner_on_Mars_PIA01122.jpg/400px-Sojourner_on_Mars_PIA01122.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sojourner_on_Mars_PIA01122.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Sojourner</i> rover on Mars on sol 22</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <i>Mars Pathfinder</i> conducted different investigations on the Martian soil using three scientific instruments. The lander contained a <a href="/wiki/Stereo_camera" title="Stereo camera">stereoscopic camera</a> with spatial filters on an expandable pole called Imager for Mars Pathfinder (IMP),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and the Atmospheric Structure Instrument/Meteorology Package (ASI/MET)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> which acts as a Mars meteorological station, collecting data about pressure, temperature, and winds. The MET structure included three <a href="/wiki/Windsock" title="Windsock">windsocks</a> mounted at three heights on a pole, the topmost at about one meter (yard) and generally registered winds from the West.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Sojourner_(rover)" title="Sojourner (rover)">Sojourner</a></i> rover had an Alpha Proton X-ray Spectrometer (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/APXS" title="APXS">APXS</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> which was used to analyze the components of the rocks and soil. The rover also had two black-and-white cameras and a color one. These instruments could investigate the geology of the Martian surface from just a few millimeters to many hundreds of meters, the <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">geochemistry</a> and evolutionary history of the rocks and surface, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic" title="Magnetic">magnetic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mechanics" title="Mechanics">mechanical</a> properties of the land, as well as the magnetic properties of the dust, atmosphere and the <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotational</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbital</a> dynamics of the planet.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:H_rover-comp_wheels_02.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="670" data-file-width="1024" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/H_rover-comp_wheels_02.jpg/200px-H_rover-comp_wheels_02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/H_rover-comp_wheels_02.jpg/300px-H_rover-comp_wheels_02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/H_rover-comp_wheels_02.jpg/400px-H_rover-comp_wheels_02.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:H_rover-comp_wheels_02.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Wheel size comparison: <i>Sojourner</i>, <a href="/wiki/Mars_Exploration_Rover" title="Mars Exploration Rover">Mars Exploration Rover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mars_Science_Laboratory" title="Mars Science Laboratory">Mars Science Laboratory</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Three <a href="/wiki/Navcam" title="Navcam">navigation cameras</a> were on board the rover: Two black and white 0.3-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megapixel" title="Megapixel">megapixel</a> cameras were located on the front (768 horizontal <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">pixels</a> × 484 vertical pixels configured in 4×4+100 pixel blocks), coupled with five <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> stripe projectors, which enabled <a href="/wiki/Stereoscopy" title="Stereoscopy">stereoscopic</a> images to be taken along with measurements for hazard detection on the rover's path. A third camera with the same resolution but taking color images was located on the back, near the APXS, and rotated by 90°. It provided images of the APXS's target area and the rover's tracks on the ground. The pixels of this colour camera were arranged in such a way, that out of the 16 pixel of a 4×4 pixel block, 12 pixel were sensitive to green, 2 pixel to red and 2 pixel were sensitive to <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> as well as blue. As all cameras had <a href="/wiki/Camera_lens" title="Camera lens">lenses</a> made out of <a href="/wiki/Zinc_selenide" title="Zinc selenide">zinc selenide</a>, which blocks light below a <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> of 500 nm, no blue light actually reached these "blue/infrared" pixels, which therefore recorded only infrared.</p>
<p>All three cameras were <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">CCDs</a> manufactured by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastman_Kodak_Company" title="Eastman Kodak Company">Eastman Kodak Company</a>, and were controlled by the rover's <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">CPU</a>. They all had auto-exposure and capabilities for handling bad pixels, and the image parameters (exposure time, compression used, etc.) were included in the transmitted images as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Image_header" title="Image header">image header</a>. The rover could <a href="/wiki/Data_compression" title="Data compression">compress</a> the front cameras' images using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Block_truncation_coding" title="Block truncation coding">block truncation coding</a> (BTC) algorithm, but it could only do the same for the back camera's images if the colour information was discarded. The cameras' <a href="/wiki/Optical_resolution" title="Optical resolution">optical resolution</a> was sufficient to resolve 0.6 cm details across a 0.65 m range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sojcam_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-sojcam-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landing_site">Landing site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_Pathfinder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Landing site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The landing site was an ancient flood plain in Mars's northern hemisphere called "<a href="/wiki/Ares_Vallis" title="Ares Vallis">Ares Vallis</a>" ("the valley of Ares", the ancient Greek equivalent of the ancient Roman deity Mars) and is among the rockiest parts of Mars. Scientists chose it because they found it to be a relatively safe surface to land on and one that contained a wide variety of rocks deposited during a catastrophic flood. After the landing, at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Mars_Pathfinder&amp;params=19.13_N_33.22_W_globe:Mars"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">19°08′N</span> <span class="longitude">33°13′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">19.13°N 33.22°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">19.13; -33.22</span></span></span></a></span><span style="font-size: small;"><span id="coordinates"><a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">Coordinates</a>: <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Mars_Pathfinder&amp;params=19.13_N_33.22_W_globe:Mars"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">19°08′N</span> <span class="longitude">33°13′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">19.13°N 33.22°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">19.13; -33.22</span></span></span></a></span></span></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> succeeded, the landing site received the name <i>The <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a> Memorial Station</i> in honor of the <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Entry.2C_descent_and_landing">Entry, descent and landing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_Pathfinder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Entry, descent and landing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_entry.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="473" height="254" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Atmospheric_entry.png/200px-Atmospheric_entry.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Atmospheric_entry.png/300px-Atmospheric_entry.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Atmospheric_entry.png/400px-Atmospheric_entry.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_entry.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1990s illustration of Mars atmospheric entry</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Mars Pathfinder</i> entered the Martian atmosphere and landed using an innovative system involving an entry capsule, a supersonic <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">parachute</a>, followed by solid rockets and large airbags to cushion the impact.</p>
<p><i>Mars Pathfinder</i> directly entered Mars atmosphere in a retrograde direction from a hyperbolic trajectory at 6.1 km/s using an atmospheric entry aeroshell (capsule) that was derived from the original Viking Mars lander design. The aeroshell consisted of a back shell and a specially designed ablative heatshield to slow to 370 m/s (830 mph) where a supersonic disk-gap-band parachute was inflated to slow its descent through the thin Martian atmosphere to 68 m/s (about 160 mph). The lander's on-board computer used redundant on-board accelerometers to determine the timing of the parachute inflation. Twenty seconds later the heatshield was pyrotechnically released. Another twenty seconds later the lander was separated and lowered from the backshell on a 20 m bridle (tether). When the lander reached 1.6 km above the surface, a radar was used by the on-board computer to determine altitude and descent velocity. This information was used by the computer to determine the precise timing of the landing events that followed.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pathfinder_Air_Bags_-_GPN-2000-000484.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2036" data-file-width="3060" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Pathfinder_Air_Bags_-_GPN-2000-000484.jpg/200px-Pathfinder_Air_Bags_-_GPN-2000-000484.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Pathfinder_Air_Bags_-_GPN-2000-000484.jpg/300px-Pathfinder_Air_Bags_-_GPN-2000-000484.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Pathfinder_Air_Bags_-_GPN-2000-000484.jpg/400px-Pathfinder_Air_Bags_-_GPN-2000-000484.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pathfinder_Air_Bags_-_GPN-2000-000484.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <i>Pathfinder</i> air bags are tested in June 1995</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Once the lander was 355 m above the ground, airbags were inflated in less than a second using three catalytically cooled solid rocket motors that served as gas generators. The airbags were made of 4 inter-connected multi-layer <a href="/wiki/Vectran" title="Vectran">vectran</a> bags that surrounded the tetrahedron lander. They were designed and tested to accommodate grazing angle impacts as high as 28 m/s. However, as the airbags were designed for no more than about 15 m/s vertical impacts, three solid retrorockets were mounted above the lander in the backshell. These were fired at 98 m above the ground. The lander's on-board computer estimated the best time to fire the rockets and cut the bridle so that the lander velocity would be reduced to about 0 m/s between 15 and 25 m above the ground. After 2.3 seconds, while the rockets were still firing, the lander cut the bridle loose about 21.5 m above the ground and fell to the ground. The rockets flew up and away with the backshell and parachute (they have since been sighted by orbital images). The lander impacted at 14 m/s and limited the impact to only 18 G of deceleration. The first bounce was 15.7 m high and continued bouncing for at least 15 additional bounces (accelerometer data recording did not continue through all of the bounces).</p>
<p>The entire entry, descent and landing (EDL) process was completed in four minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once the lander stopped rolling, the airbags deflated and retracted toward the lander using four winches mounted on the lander "petals". Designed to right itself from any initial orientation, the lander happened to roll right side up onto its base petal. 74 minutes after landing, the petals were deployed with <i>Sojourner</i> rover and the solar panels attached on the inside.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pan_segment1.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1090" data-file-width="1726" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Pan_segment1.gif/200px-Pan_segment1.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Pan_segment1.gif/300px-Pan_segment1.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Pan_segment1.gif/400px-Pan_segment1.gif 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pan_segment1.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
IMP image of landing site in 1997</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The lander arrived at night at 2:56:55 Mars local solar time (16:56:55 UTC) on July 4, 1997. The lander had to wait until sunrise to send its first digital signals and images to Earth. The landing site was located at 19.30° north latitude and 33.52° west longitude in Ares Vallis, only 19 kilometres southwest of the center of the 200 km wide landing site ellipse. During <a href="/wiki/Timekeeping_on_Mars#Sols" title="Timekeeping on Mars">Sol</a> 1, the first Martian solar day the lander spent on the planet, the lander took pictures and made some meteorologic measurements. Once the data was received, the engineers realized that one of the airbags hadn't fully deflated and could be a problem for the forthcoming traverse of <i>Sojourner</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s descent ramp. To solve the problem, they sent commands to the lander to raise one of its petals and perform additional retraction to flatten the airbag. The procedure was a success and on Sol 2, <i>Sojourner</i> was released, stood up and backed down one of two ramps.</p>
<p>The <i>Mars Pathfinder</i> entry descent and landing system design was used (with some modification) on the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Exploration_Rover" title="Mars Exploration Rover">Mars Exploration Rover</a> mission. Likewise, many design aspects of the <i>Sojourner</i> rover (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Rocker-bogie" title="Rocker-bogie">rocker-bogie</a> mobility architecture and the navigation algorithms) were also successfully used on the Mars Exploration Rover mission.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rover_operations">Rover operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_Pathfinder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rover operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sojourner_deployment"><i>Sojourner</i> deployment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_Pathfinder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sojourner deployment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Sojourner</i> rover exit from the lander occurred on Sol 2, after its landing on July 4, 1997. As the next sols progressed it approached some rocks, which the scientists named "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barnacle_Bill_(Mars)" title="Barnacle Bill (Mars)">Barnacle Bill</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Yogi_Rock" title="Yogi Rock">Yogi</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/Scooby-Doo" title="Scooby-Doo">Scooby-Doo</a>", after famous <a href="/wiki/Cartoon" title="Cartoon">cartoon</a> characters. The rover made measurements of the elements found in those rocks and in the martian soil, while the lander took pictures of the <i>Sojourner</i> and the surrounding terrain, in addition to making climate observations.</p>
<p>The <i>Sojourner</i> is a six-wheeled 65 cm long vehicle, 48 cm wide, 30 cm tall and weighing 10.5 kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Its maximum speed reached one centimeter per second. <i>Sojourner</i> travelled approximately 100 metres in total, never more than 12 m from the <i>Pathfinder</i> station. During its 83 <a href="/wiki/Timekeeping_on_Mars#Sols" title="Timekeeping on Mars">sols</a> of operation, it sent 550 photographs to Earth and analyzed the <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemical</a> properties of 16 locations near the lander. (See also <a href="/wiki/Rover_(space_exploration)" title="Rover (space exploration)">Space exploration rovers</a>)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sojourner.27s_rock_analysis"><i>Sojourner</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s rock analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_Pathfinder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sojourner's rock analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sojourner_and_Barnacle_Bill.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Sojourner_and_Barnacle_Bill.jpg/220px-Sojourner_and_Barnacle_Bill.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Sojourner_and_Barnacle_Bill.jpg/330px-Sojourner_and_Barnacle_Bill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Sojourner_and_Barnacle_Bill.jpg/440px-Sojourner_and_Barnacle_Bill.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sojourner_and_Barnacle_Bill.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Sojourner</i> next to the rock <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barnacle_Bill_(Mars)" title="Barnacle Bill (Mars)">Barnacle Bill</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first analysis on a rock started on Sol 3 with Barnacle Bill. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/APXS" title="APXS">Alpha Particle X-ray Spectrometer</a> (APXS) was used to determine its composition, the spectrometer taking ten hours to make a full scan of the sample. It found all the elements except <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, which constitutes just 0.1 percent of the rock's or soil's mass.</p>
<p>The APXS works by irradiating rocks and soil samples with <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha particles</a> (<a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nuclei</a>, which consist of two <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and two <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>). The results indicated that "Barnacle Bill" is much like Earth's <a href="/wiki/Andesite" title="Andesite">andesites</a>, confirming past <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> activity. The discovery of andesites shows that some Martian rocks have been remelted and reprocessed. On Earth, Andesite forms when magma sits in pockets of rock while some of the iron and magnesium settle out. Consequently, the final rock contains less iron and magnesiums and more silica. Volcanic rocks are usually classified by comparing the relative amount of alkalis (Na<sub>2</sub>O and K<sub>2</sub>O) with the amount of silica (SiO<sub>2</sub>). Andesite is different from the rocks found in meteorites that have come from Mars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golombek.2C_M_1997_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Golombek.2C_M_1997-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nssdc.gsfc.nasa.gov_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-nssdc.gsfc.nasa.gov-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruckner.2C_J._2001_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bruckner.2C_J._2001-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Analysis of the Yogi rock again using the APXS showed that it was a <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basaltic</a> rock, more primitive than Barnacle Bill. Yogi's shape and texture show that it was probably deposited there by a <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flood</a>.</p>
<p>Another rock, named Moe, was found to have certain marks on its surface, demonstrating erosion caused by the wind. Most rocks analyzed showed a high content of <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>. In another region known as Rock Garden, <i>Sojourner</i> encountered crescent moon-shaped dunes, which are similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dunes" title="Dunes">crescentic dunes</a> on Earth.</p>
<p>By the time that final results of the mission were described in a series of articles in the journal <i>Science</i> (December 5, 1997), it was believed that the rock Yogi contained a coating of dust, but was similar to the rock Barnacle Bill. Calculations suggest that the two rocks contain mostly the minerals orthopyroxene (magnesium-iron silicate), feldspars (aluminum silicates of potassium, sodium, and calcium), quartz (silicon dioxide), with smaller amounts of magnetite, ilmenite, iron sulfide, and calcium phosphate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golombek.2C_M_1997_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Golombek.2C_M_1997-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nssdc.gsfc.nasa.gov_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-nssdc.gsfc.nasa.gov-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruckner.2C_J._2001_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bruckner.2C_J._2001-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On-board_computer">On-board computer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_Pathfinder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: On-board computer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Comparison_of_embedded_computer_systems_on_board_the_Mars_rovers" title="Comparison of embedded computer systems on board the Mars rovers">Comparison of embedded computer systems on board the Mars rovers</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Embedded_system" title="Embedded system">embedded</a> computer on board the <i>Sojourner</i> rover was based around the 2 MHz<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intel_80C85" title="Intel 80C85">Intel 80C85</a> <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">CPU</a> with 512 <a href="/wiki/Kilobyte" title="Kilobyte">KB</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Random_access_memory" title="Random access memory">RAM</a> and 176 KB of <a href="/wiki/Flash_memory" title="Flash memory">flash memory</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_state_disk" title="Solid state disk">solid-state</a> <a href="/wiki/Data_storage_device" title="Data storage device">storage</a>, running a <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_executive" title="Cyclic executive">cyclic executive</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieeecomputer_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-ieeecomputer-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The computer of the <i>Pathfinder</i> lander was a <a href="/wiki/IBM_RAD6000" title="IBM RAD6000">Radiation Hardened IBM Risc 6000</a> Single Chip (Rad6000 SC) <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">CPU</a> with 128 MB of RAM and 6 MB of <a href="/wiki/EEPROM" title="EEPROM">EEPROM</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and its <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a> was <a href="/wiki/VxWorks" title="VxWorks">VxWorks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wind_River_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wind_River-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The mission was jeopardised by a <a href="/wiki/Concurrent_computing" title="Concurrent computing">concurrent</a> software bug in the lander,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> which had been found in preflight testing but was deemed a glitch and therefore given a low priority as it only occurred in certain unanticipated heavy-load conditions, and the focus was on verifying the entry and landing code. The problem, which was reproduced and corrected from Earth using a laboratory duplicate thanks to the logging and debugging functionality enabled in the flight software, was due to <a href="/wiki/Reset_(computing)" title="Reset (computing)">computer resets</a> caused by <a href="/wiki/Priority_inversion" title="Priority inversion">priority inversion</a>. No scientific or engineering data was lost after a computer reset, but all the following operations were interrupted until the next day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Four resets occurred (on July 5, 10, 11 and 14) during the mission,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> before patching the software on July 21 to enable <a href="/wiki/Priority_inheritance" title="Priority inheritance">priority inheritance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Results_from_Pathfinder">Results from <i>Pathfinder</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_Pathfinder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Results from Pathfinder">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mars_sunset_PIA00920.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="240" data-file-width="248" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Mars_sunset_PIA00920.jpg/220px-Mars_sunset_PIA00920.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/50/Mars_sunset_PIA00920.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mars_sunset_PIA00920.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Close-up of Mars sky at sunset, by <i>Mars Pathfinder</i> (1997)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The lander sent more than 2.3 billion bits of information including 16,500 pictures and made 8.5 million measurements of the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>, temperature and wind speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jplpathfinder_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-jplpathfinder-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>By taking multiple images of the sky at different distances from the Sun, scientists were able to determine that the size of the particles in the pink haze was about one <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometre</a> in radius. The color of some soils was similar to that of an iron oxyhydroxide phase which would support the theory of a warmer and wetter climate in the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <i>Pathfinder</i> carried a series of magnets to examine the magnetic component of the dust. Eventually, all but one of the magnets developed a coating of dust. Since the weakest magnet did not attract any soil, it was concluded that the airborne dust did not contain pure <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a> or just one type of <a href="/wiki/Maghemite" title="Maghemite">maghemite</a>. The dust probably was an aggregate possibly cemented with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferric_oxide" title="Ferric oxide">ferric oxide</a> (Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Using much more sophisticated instruments, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spirit_Rover" title="Spirit Rover">Mars <i>Spirit</i> rover</a> found that magnetite could explain the magnetic nature of the dust and soil on Mars. Magnetite was found in the soil and that the most magnetic part of the soil was dark. Magnetite is very dark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Using <a href="/wiki/Doppler_tracking" title="Doppler tracking">Doppler tracking</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two-way_ranging" title="Two-way ranging">two-way ranging</a>, scientists added earlier measurements from the <i>Viking</i> landers to determine that the non-hydrostatic component of the polar <a href="/wiki/Moment_of_inertia" title="Moment of inertia">moment of inertia</a> is due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tharsis_bulge" title="Tharsis bulge">Tharsis bulge</a> and that the interior is not melted. The central metallic core is between 1300 km and 2000 km in radius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golombek.2C_M_1997_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Golombek.2C_M_1997-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_mission">End of mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_Pathfinder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: End of mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MPT_Hardware_on_the_Surface_MRO_picture.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="4000" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/MPT_Hardware_on_the_Surface_MRO_picture.jpg/220px-MPT_Hardware_on_the_Surface_MRO_picture.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/MPT_Hardware_on_the_Surface_MRO_picture.jpg/330px-MPT_Hardware_on_the_Surface_MRO_picture.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/MPT_Hardware_on_the_Surface_MRO_picture.jpg/440px-MPT_Hardware_on_the_Surface_MRO_picture.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MPT_Hardware_on_the_Surface_MRO_picture.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Mars Pathfinder</i> seen from space by the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter">MRO</a> <a href="/wiki/HiRISE" title="HiRISE">HiRISE</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Although the mission was planned to last from a week to a month, the rover operated successfully for almost three months. Communication failed after 7 October,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-batteryfailure_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-batteryfailure-33">[33]</a></sup> with a final data transmission received from Pathfinder at 10:23 UTC on September 27, 1997. Mission managers tried to restore full communications during the following five months, but the mission was terminated on March 10, 1998. During the extended operation a high-resolution stereo panorama of the surrounding terrain was being made, and the Sojourner rover was to visit a distant ridge, but the panorama was only about one-third completed and the ridge visit had not begun when communication failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-batteryfailure_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-batteryfailure-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The on-board battery—designed to operate for one month—may have failed after repeated charging and discharging. The battery was used to heat the probe's electronics to slightly above the expected nighttime temperatures on Mars. With the failure of the battery, colder-than-normal temperatures may have caused vital parts to break, leading to loss of communications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-batteryfailure_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-batteryfailure-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasafacts_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasafacts-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the landing, <i>Pathfinder</i> was renamed as the <i>Sagan Memorial Station</i> in honor of <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomer</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetology" title="Planetology">planetologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a>. The mission had exceeded its goals in the first month.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mars_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter">Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter</a> spotted <i>Pathfinder</i> lander in January 2007 (left).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_the_rover">Naming the rover</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_Pathfinder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Naming the rover">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pathfinder01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="358" data-file-width="649" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Pathfinder01.jpg/300px-Pathfinder01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Pathfinder01.jpg/450px-Pathfinder01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Pathfinder01.jpg/600px-Pathfinder01.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pathfinder01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Sojourner</i> takes its Alpha Particle X-ray Spectrometer measurement of the <a href="/wiki/Yogi_Rock" title="Yogi Rock">Yogi Rock</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The name <i>Sojourner</i> was chosen for the <i>Mars Pathfinder</i> rover after a year-long, worldwide competition in which students up to 18 years old were invited to select a heroine and submit an essay about her historical accomplishments. The students were asked to address in their essays how a planetary rover named for their heroine would translate these accomplishments to the Martian environment.</p>
<p>Initiated in March 1994 by <a href="/wiki/The_Planetary_Society" title="The Planetary Society">The Planetary Society</a> of Pasadena, California, in cooperation with NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), the contest got under way with an announcement in the January 1995 issue of the National Science Teachers Association's magazine <i>Science and Children</i>, circulated to 20,000 teachers and schools across the nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The winning essay, which suggested naming the rover for <a href="/wiki/Sojourner_Truth" title="Sojourner Truth">Sojourner Truth</a>, was selected from among 3,500 essays and was submitted by 12-year-old Valerie Ambroise of Bridgeport, CT. First runner-up was Deepti Rohatgi, 18, of Rockville, MD, who suggested <a href="/wiki/Marie_Curie" title="Marie Curie">Marie Curie</a>. Second runner-up was Adam Sheedy, 15, of Round Rock, TX, who submitted the name of the late astronaut <a href="/wiki/Judith_Resnik" title="Judith Resnik">Judith Resnik</a>, who perished in the 1986 <i>Challenger</i> space shuttle explosion. Other popular suggestions included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacajewea" title="Sacajewea">Sacajewea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amelia_Earhart" title="Amelia Earhart">Amelia Earhart</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors">Honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_Pathfinder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In 1997, the Sojourner Team was awarded a JPL Award for Technical Excellence</li>
<li>On October 21, 1997, at the <a href="/wiki/Geological_Society_of_America" title="Geological Society of America">Geological Society of America</a>'s annual meeting in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City,_Utah" title="Salt Lake City, Utah">Salt Lake City, Utah</a>, <i>Sojourner</i> was awarded honorary membership in the Planetary Geology Division of the society<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
