<p><i><b>Master of Orion</b></i> (<i>MoO</i> or <i>MOO</i>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turn-based" title="Turn-based">turn-based</a>, <a href="/wiki/4X" title="4X">4X</a> <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_strategy_game" title="Computer strategy game">computer strategy game</a> released in <a href="/wiki/1993_in_video_gaming" title="1993 in video gaming">1993</a> by <a href="/wiki/MicroProse" title="MicroProse">MicroProse</a> on the <a href="/wiki/MS-DOS" title="MS-DOS">MS-DOS</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mac_OS" title="Mac OS">Mac OS</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> operating systems. The game is the first in its franchise, and the rights are now held by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wargaming.net" title="Wargaming.net">Wargaming.net</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spacesector.com_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-spacesector.com-2">[2]</a></sup> The player leads one of ten races to dominate the galaxy through a combination of <a href="/wiki/Diplomacy" title="Diplomacy">diplomacy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Right_of_conquest" title="Right of conquest">conquest</a> while developing technology, exploring and <a href="/wiki/Space_colonization" title="Space colonization">colonizing</a> star systems.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">Gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Orion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Master of Orion</i> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turn-based_game" title="Turn-based game">turn-based game</a>. In the first iteration of the franchise, one can only play against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AI" title="AI">AI</a> (the computer). Human and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AI" title="AI">AI</a> players control the management of colonies, technology development, ship construction, inter-species diplomacy, and combat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOO_Manual_PC_iii_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOO_Manual_PC_iii-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The software generates a map randomly at the start of each game; the player can only choose the size of the galaxy, and the number and difficulty of AI opponents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOO_Manual_PC_2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOO_Manual_PC_2-4">[4]</a></sup> In the first game, star systems have at most one colonizable planet and a few have none. Later games have more planets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOO_Manual_PC_4_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOO_Manual_PC_4-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Master of Orion</i> has 10 playable races, each with a specialty. For instance, the Humans have advantages in trade and diplomacy; the Bulrathi are the best at ground combat; the Silicoids ignore pollution and can colonize even the most hostile planets, but have slow population growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualAlienRaces_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ManualAlienRaces-6">[6]</a></sup> Each race is predisposed to like or dislike some of the other races,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualDiplomaticRelations_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ManualDiplomaticRelations-7">[7]</a></sup> and is advantaged or disadvantaged in different research fields.</p>
<p>The game begins with a single homeworld, one colony ship, and two scout ships that can be used to explore nearby stars. The game will sometimes produce random events which can be harmful or advantageous. One planet is Orion, "throne-world of the Ancients" and most valuable research site in the galaxy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOO_Manual_PC_56_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOO_Manual_PC_56-8">[8]</a></sup> protected by a powerful warship, the Guardian. Victory is gained either by eliminating all opponents or by winning a vote on peaceful unification.</p>
<p>There are seven normal and six hostile planet types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualEnvironments_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ManualEnvironments-9">[9]</a></sup> The various hostile types require increasingly advanced technology to colonize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualQuickNoRefit_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ManualQuickNoRefit-10">[10]</a></sup> Size determines the planet's initial population capacity. Mineral wealth dramatically influences a colony's industrial productivity while Habitability influences population growth rates. Hostile planets are the most likely to be rich or ultra-rich in minerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualEnvironments_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ManualEnvironments-9">[9]</a></sup> Artifact worlds contain relics of a now-vanished advanced civilization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualEnvironments_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-ManualEnvironments-9">[9]</a></sup> All planets can be upgraded to Gaia class with the appropriate technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualGrowingEmpire_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ManualGrowingEmpire-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MooTerraforming_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-MooTerraforming-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>Sliders are used to allocate a colony's output between ship construction, planetary defenses, factory construction, ecology, and research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualQuickNoRefit_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ManualQuickNoRefit-10">[10]</a></sup> Planetary population generates <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production</a>, especially when assisted by factories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualPlanetsScreen_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ManualPlanetsScreen-13">[13]</a></sup> There is a limit on the number of factories a unit of population can operate, but building upgrades can increase this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualPlanetProd_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ManualPlanetProd-14">[14]</a></sup> Defence spending is used to build additional missile bases, upgrade missile bases or planetary shields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualPlanetProd_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-ManualPlanetProd-14">[14]</a></sup> Military and spy maintenance is deducted from every colony's production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOO_Manual_PC_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOO_Manual_PC-15">[15]</a></sup> A planet's output can also be transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Treasury" title="Treasury">treasury</a> at a loss.</p>
<p>Ships can travel to any star system within their range and combat always occurs in <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> over a planet - it is impossible to intercept enemy ships in deep space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualQuickStarts_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ManualQuickStarts-16">[16]</a></sup> Players can control space combat manually or ask the software to resolve combat automatically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualShipCombat_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ManualShipCombat-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Orion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The designers regard technology as the most important contribution to a player's success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualTechScreen_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ManualTechScreen-18">[18]</a></sup> Funding can be put into one or all of the game's six independent <a href="/wiki/Technology_tree" title="Technology tree">tech tree</a> fields, including Computers, Construction, Force Fields, Planetary Science, Vehicle Propulsion, and Weapons.</p>
<p>If a ship uses a component from a particular technology area, further advances in that area reduce the cost and size of the component; this effect is called "miniaturization". When one has researched all of the technologies in an area of the tech tree, further research can discover "advanced technologies" in that area, which do not provide specific new capabilities but increase the miniaturization of ship components.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualTechScreen_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-ManualTechScreen-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Battles are almost always decided by numbers and technology rather than by clever tactics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SirianMOOPlayer_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-SirianMOOPlayer-19">[19]</a></sup> Players can design and use their own ships. There are four hull sizes; smaller sizes are harder to hit while larger ships can survive more damage and hold more components. There are eight types of components, each with different effects. Only six ship designs can be used at a time.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diplomacy">Diplomacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Orion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diplomacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Master of Orion</i> provides a wide range of diplomatic negotiations: <a href="/wiki/Tribute" title="Tribute">gifts</a> of money or technology; one-time technology trades; trade pacts that boost industrial output; <a href="/wiki/Non-aggression_pact" title="Non-aggression pact">non-aggression</a> and <a href="/wiki/Military_alliance" title="Military alliance">alliance</a> treaties. Players can also threaten each other, declare war and arrange cease-fires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Each AI player remembers others' actions, both positive and negative, and will be unwilling to form alliances with a player who has broken previous treaties with it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualDiplomaticRelations_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ManualDiplomaticRelations-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under AI control, each race has a ruler personality and an objective, such as Xenophobic Expansionist or Pacifistic Technologist. These traits guide their politics and economic management; for example militarists maintain large fleets and prioritize technologies which have military benefits, while ecologists put a lot of effort into pollution control and terraforming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualAlienLeaders_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ManualAlienLeaders-21">[21]</a></sup> Traits vary from game to game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualAlienLeaders_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-ManualAlienLeaders-21">[21]</a></sup> Each race has most probable traits and avoids their opposites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualDiplomaticRelations_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-ManualDiplomaticRelations-7">[7]</a></sup> Races may occasionally revolt and change traits.</p>
<p>Hostile actions do not automatically cause war. Clashes are even expected at the opening of the game, when all sides are sending probes out into the unknown. On the other extreme, a ground assault must be knowingly targeted at an inhabited planet, and is a massive provocation.</p>
<p>Colonies can be bombed from space, or taken in ground invasions. Ground invasions can be conducted through enemy defenses. Present enemy ships or missile bases will fire on the approaching transports, possibly destroying some or all of them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The invasion itself is fully automatic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualCombatResolution_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ManualCombatResolution-23">[23]</a></sup> Results depend on numbers, technology and (if one of the races involved is Bulrathi) racial ground combat bonus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualColonyTransps_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ManualColonyTransps-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Invasion is expensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emrich1993_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emrich1993-25">[25]</a></sup> In the first game, there are no special soldier units; colonial population itself is sent to fight, exterminate the existing inhabitants, and form a new planetary population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualColonyTransps_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-ManualColonyTransps-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The production capacity of any remaining factories can be gleaned, and <a href="/wiki/Looting" title="Looting">plundering</a> of technologies if enough factories survived the attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManualColonyTransps_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-ManualColonyTransps-24">[24]</a></sup> Controlling a new system extends the range of the invader's ships.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Orion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Master of Orion</i> is a significantly expanded and refined version of the <a href="/wiki/Prototype" title="Prototype">prototype</a>/predecessor game <i>Star Lords</i> (not to be confused with <i><a href="/wiki/Starlord_(video_game)" title="Starlord (video game)">Starlord</a></i>, also released by MicroProse in 1993). <a href="/wiki/Steve_Barcia" title="Steve Barcia">Steve Barcia</a>'s game development company <a href="/wiki/Simtex" title="Simtex">Simtex</a> demonstrated <i>Star Lords</i> to <a href="/wiki/MicroProse" title="MicroProse">MicroProse</a> and gaming journalist <a href="/wiki/Alan_Emrich" title="Alan Emrich">Alan Emrich</a> who, along with Tom Hughes, assisted Barcia in refining the design to produce <i>Master of Orion</i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and the game's manual thanks them for their contributions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Emrich and Hughes later wrote the strategy guide for the finished product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-consimworld2001EmrichAward_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-consimworld2001EmrichAward-30">[30]</a></sup> MicroProse published the final version of the game in <a href="/wiki/1994_in_video_gaming" title="1994 in video gaming">1994</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Star_Lords"><i>Star Lords</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Orion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Star Lords">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Star Lords</i>, often called <i>Master of Orion 0</i> by fans,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QS_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-QS-32">[32]</a></sup> was a prototype and never commercially released (its intro opens with "SimTex Software and Your Company present"). The crude but fully playable prototype was made available as <a href="/wiki/Freeware" title="Freeware">freeware</a> in 2001, stripped of all documentation and <a href="/wiki/Copy_protection" title="Copy protection">copy protection</a>, in anticipation of the launch of <i><a href="/wiki/Master_of_Orion_III" title="Master of Orion III">Master of Orion III</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QS_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-QS-32">[32]</a></sup> Major differences between <i>Star Lords</i> and <i>Master of Orion</i> include inferior graphics and interface, simpler trade and diplomacy, undirected research, a lack of safeguards to prevent players from building more factories than are usable and the use of transports rather than colony ships to colonize new planets. One feature of <i>Star Lords</i> that <i>Master of Orion</i> lacks is a table of relations between the computer-controlled races. The game was eventually made available for download on FilePlanet<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and the home page for <i>Master of Orion III</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QS_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-QS-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<p>Emrich in a 1993 <i><a href="/wiki/Computer_Gaming_World" title="Computer Gaming World">Computer Gaming World</a></i> preview described <i>Master of Orion</i> as "the best that galactic conquest can offer", and summarized its type of gameplay as "<a href="/wiki/4X" title="4X">4X</a>", meaning "e<i>X</i>plore, e<i>X</i>pand, e<i>X</i>ploit, e<i>X</i>terminate".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emrich1993_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emrich1993-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> He and later commentators noted earlier examples of this genre, including <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civilization_(computer_game)" title="Civilization (computer game)">Civilization</a></i> (1991)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-civfirst_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-civfirst-36">[36]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Reach_for_the_Stars_(video_game)" title="Reach for the Stars (video game)">Reach for the Stars</a></i> (1983).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Geryk2001HistSpaceEmpEarly_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Geryk2001HistSpaceEmpEarly-37">[37]</a></sup> The magazine's full review stated that "<i>Master of Orion</i> is one of those games where one must actually put effort into finding something inadequate about the game design, and that in itself is probably the highest praise this reviewer can give a product." The review concluded that it was "a definite Game of the Year candidate as well as Exhibit A in many divorce cases".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cirulis199312_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-cirulis199312-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In retrospective reviews, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allgame" title="Allgame">Allgame</a>, <a href="/wiki/GameSpot" title="GameSpot">GameSpot</a> and <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> regarded <i>MoO</i> as the standard by which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turn_based_strategy" title="Turn based strategy">turn based strategy</a> games set in space are judged, although Allgame regretted the lack of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiplayer" title="Multiplayer">multiplayer</a> option.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OsborneMOOatAllGame_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-OsborneMOOatAllGame-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChickMoo2Retroview_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChickMoo2Retroview-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GerykHistSpaceEmpireMOO_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-GerykHistSpaceEmpireMOO-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1996, <i>Computer Gaming World</i> ranked <i>Master of Orion</i> as the 33rd best game of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cgw199611best_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-cgw199611best-42">[42]</a></sup> In 2003, <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> ranked it as the 98th top game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> <i>Master of Orion</i> is a member of both <a href="/wiki/GameSpy" title="GameSpy">GameSpy</a>'s Hall of Fame (2001)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and GameSpot's list of the greatest games of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Master_of_Orion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two commercial sequels to <i>Master of Orion</i> have been released, <i><a href="/wiki/Master_of_Orion_II:_Battle_at_Antares" title="Master of Orion II: Battle at Antares">Master of Orion II: Battle at Antares</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Master_of_Orion_III" title="Master of Orion III">Master of Orion III</a></i>. The sequels are significantly more advanced in graphics and sound and feature large differences in gameplay, with some players claiming the original game remains the best version of the three.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jonsullivanMooMisc_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-jonsullivanMooMisc-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SirianMOO_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-SirianMOO-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1997, MicroProse released a <i>Master of Orion</i> "Jr." scenario as part of the <i>Civ II: Fantastic Worlds</i> expansion for <i><a href="/wiki/Civilization_II" title="Civilization II">Civilization II</a></i>. In 2001, <i>Star Lords</i>, developed as <i>Master of Orion</i> prototype, was released as <a href="/wiki/Freeware" title="Freeware">freeware</a> as part of the promotion for <i>Master of Orion III</i>. Also a potential future release of the MOO and MOO2 <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a> was indicated by the MOO3 developers in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In 2011, a clone of <i>MoO II</i>, titled <i>Starbase Orion</i>, was published by Chimera Software, LLC for the <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a>. The game setting has been the influence of Russian writer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergey_Lukyanenko" title="Sergey Lukyanenko">Sergey Lukyanenko</a>'s trilogy, the <i><a href="/wiki/Line_of_Delirium" title="Line of Delirium">Line of Delirium</a></i>.</p>
<p>In July 2013, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wargaming.net" title="Wargaming.net">Wargaming.net</a> bought the <i>Master of Orion</i> franchise from the Atari bankruptcy proceedings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spacesector.com_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-spacesector.com-2">[2]</a></sup> A "reimagining" subtitled <i><a href="/wiki/Master_of_Orion:_Conquer_the_Stars" title="Master of Orion: Conquer the Stars">Conquer the Stars</a></i> was announced in June 2015<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and released into <a href="/wiki/Early_access" title="Early access">early access</a> on February 26, 2016.</p>
