<p><b>Massimiliano Allegri</b> (<small>Italian pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[massimiˈljano alˈleɡri]</a></span>; born 11 August 1967) is an Italian professional <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> <a href="/wiki/Manager_(association_football)" title="Manager (association football)">manager</a>, currently in charge of Italian club <a href="/wiki/Juventus_F.C." title="Juventus F.C.">Juventus</a>, and former <a href="/wiki/Midfielder" title="Midfielder">midfielder</a>. As a footballer, Allegri spent his career playing for various Italian clubs. After beginning his managerial career in 2003 with several smaller Italian sides, he later played a key role in <a href="/wiki/U.S._Sassuolo_Calcio" title="U.S. Sassuolo Calcio">Sassuolo</a>'s rise through the lower Italian divisions, and subsequently led <a href="/wiki/Cagliari_Calcio" title="Cagliari Calcio">Cagliari</a> to their best <a href="/wiki/Serie_A" title="Serie A">Serie A</a> finish in nearly 15 years, winning the <a href="/wiki/Panchina_d%27Oro" title="Panchina d'Oro">Panchina d'Oro</a> Award for best Serie A coach in 2009. His performances as head coach of Cagliari earned him a move to <a href="/wiki/A.C._Milan" title="A.C. Milan">Milan</a> in 2010, where he remained until January 2014; in the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_Serie_A" title="2010–11 Serie A">2010–11 season</a>, Allegri helped Milan to their first Serie A title since the 2003–04 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> After joining Juventus in 2015, he immediately won the <a href="/wiki/Double_(association_football)#Italy" title="Double (association football)">domestic double</a> in his debut season, and achieved the same feat the following season.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Club_career">Club career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massimiliano_Allegri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Club career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After spending the earlier part of his career in the lower leagues, including his hometown club <a href="/wiki/A.S._Livorno_Calcio" title="A.S. Livorno Calcio">Livorno</a>, Allegri joined <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pescara_Calcio" title="Pescara Calcio">Pescara</a> in 1991. A talented and creative player, he established himself in midfield as <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Galeone" title="Giovanni Galeone">Giovanni Galeone</a>'s side won promotion to <a href="/wiki/Serie_A" title="Serie A">Serie A</a> in 1992. Allegri was an outstanding performer for Pescara in Serie A. Despite the side's relegation in last place, there was no shortage of goals and Allegri scored an impressive 12 goals from midfield. He moved on to <a href="/wiki/Cagliari_Calcio" title="Cagliari Calcio">Cagliari</a> and then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perugia_Calcio" title="Perugia Calcio">Perugia</a> and <a href="/wiki/S.S.C._Napoli" title="S.S.C. Napoli">Napoli</a> before returning to Pescara. Brief spells at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A.C._Pistoiese" title="A.C. Pistoiese">Pistoiese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aglianese_Calcio_1923" title="Aglianese Calcio 1923">Aglianese</a> followed before his retirement from active football in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uefa.com_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-uefa.com-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was banned for one year in 2001 with others six Italian footballers for match-fixing following a <a href="/wiki/Coppa_Italia" title="Coppa Italia">Coppa Italia</a> tie in 2000. The <a href="/wiki/Italian_Football_Federation" title="Italian Football Federation">Italian Football Federation</a> (FIGC) also handed one-year suspensions to <a href="/wiki/Fabio_Gallo" title="Fabio Gallo">Fabio Gallo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sebastiano_Siviglia" title="Sebastiano Siviglia">Sebastiano Siviglia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Luciano_Zauri" title="Luciano Zauri">Luciano Zauri</a> of Serie A side <a href="/wiki/Atalanta_B.C." title="Atalanta B.C.">Atalanta</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Alfredo_Aglietti" title="Alfredo Aglietti">Alfredo Aglietti</a> of <a href="/wiki/Serie_B" title="Serie B">Serie B</a>'s Pistoiese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Managerial_career">Managerial career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massimiliano_Allegri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Managerial career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massimiliano_Allegri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Allegri started his managerial career in 2004, serving as the boss of <a href="/wiki/Lega_Pro_Seconda_Divisione" title="Lega Pro Seconda Divisione">Serie C2</a> team <a href="/wiki/Aglianese_Calcio_1923" title="Aglianese Calcio 1923">Aglianese</a>, the club where he spent his two final years as a player. Following an impressive season with them, Allegri was called to head <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Grosseto_F.C." title="U.S. Grosseto F.C.">Grosseto</a>, then in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serie_C1" title="Serie C1">Serie C1</a>; his experience with the <i>Biancazzurri</i>, however, proved not to be as successful as with Aglianese, and he was ultimately sacked shortly after the beginning of the 2006–07 Serie C1 season. Shortly after being sacked by Grosseto, Allegri agreed to rejoin his mentor <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Galeone" title="Giovanni Galeone">Giovanni Galeone</a> at <a href="/wiki/Udinese_Calcio" title="Udinese Calcio">Udinese</a>, becoming part of his coaching staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This practice, however, proved to be forbidden by the Italian football laws because he was still contracted with the Tuscan club, which caused Allegri to be disqualified for three months on early 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sassuolo">Sassuolo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massimiliano_Allegri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sassuolo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 2007, Allegri became head coach of ambitious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serie_C1" title="Serie C1">Serie C1</a> team <a href="/wiki/U.S._Sassuolo_Calcio" title="U.S. Sassuolo Calcio">Sassuolo</a>, who Allegri managed to lead quickly into the top league spots. On 27 April 2008, Sassuolo mathematically ensured the 2007–08 Serie C1/A league title, thus winning a historical first promotion to <a href="/wiki/Serie_B" title="Serie B">Serie B</a> for the <i>neroverdi</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cagliari">Cagliari</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massimiliano_Allegri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cagliari">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 29 May 2008, Allegri was announced as new head coach of <a href="/wiki/Cagliari_Calcio" title="Cagliari Calcio">Cagliari</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Serie_A" title="Serie A">Serie A</a>, replacing <a href="/wiki/Davide_Ballardini" title="Davide Ballardini">Davide Ballardini</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allegri_cagliari_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-allegri_cagliari-7">[7]</a></sup> Despite a disappointing start, with five losses in the first five league matches, Allegri was confirmed by club chairman <a href="/wiki/Massimo_Cellino" title="Massimo Cellino">Massimo Cellino</a> and later went on to bring his team up to a mid-table placement in December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-relationship_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-relationship-8">[8]</a></sup> On 9 December, following a 1–0 home win to <a href="/wiki/U.S._Citt%C3%A0_di_Palermo" title="U.S. Città di Palermo">Palermo</a>, Cagliari announced to have agreed a two-year contract extension with Allegri, with a new contract that was set to expire on 1 June 2011. The contract was then revealed to have been signed on October, in the midst of the <i>rossoblu'</i>s early crisis result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>He completed the <a href="/wiki/2008%E2%80%9309_Serie_A" title="2008–09 Serie A">2008–09 season</a> in an impressive ninth place, their best result in Serie A in almost fifteen years, which was hailed as a huge result with respect to the limited resources, the lack of top-class players, and the high quality of Cagliari's attacking football style, which led the <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinians</a> not far from <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Europa_League" title="UEFA Europa League">UEFA Europa League</a> qualification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-relationship_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-relationship-8">[8]</a></sup> Such results led him to be awarded the <a href="/wiki/Panchina_d%27Oro" title="Panchina d'Oro">Panchina d'Oro</a> <i>(Golden bench)</i>, a prize awarded to the best Serie A football manager according to other managers' votes, ahead of Serie A winning coach <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mourinho" title="José Mourinho">José Mourinho</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-relationship_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-relationship-8">[8]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/2009%E2%80%9310_Serie_A" title="2009–10 Serie A">2009–10 season</a>, he managed to confirm Cagliari's high footballing levels despite the loss of regular striker <a href="/wiki/Robert_Acquafresca" title="Robert Acquafresca">Robert Acquafresca</a>, leading an impressive number of three players from his side — namely <a href="/wiki/Davide_Biondini" title="Davide Biondini">Davide Biondini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Federico_Marchetti" title="Federico Marchetti">Federico Marchetti</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Cossu_(footballer,_born_1980)" title="Andrea Cossu (footballer, born 1980)">Andrea Cossu</a> — to receive <a href="/wiki/Italy_national_football_team" title="Italy national football team">Italian national team</a> call-ups during his reign at the club.</p>
<p>On 13 April 2010, Cagliari surprisingly removed Allegri from his managerial duties despite a solid 12th place with 40 points, and youth coach Giorgio Melis — with <a href="/wiki/Gianluca_Festa" title="Gianluca Festa">Gianluca Festa</a> as his assistant — was appointed at his replacement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allegri_sacked_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-allegri_sacked-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Milan">Milan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massimiliano_Allegri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Milan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:264px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Allegri_with_Robinho.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2513" data-file-width="2429" height="271" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Allegri_with_Robinho.jpg/262px-Allegri_with_Robinho.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Allegri_with_Robinho.jpg/393px-Allegri_with_Robinho.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Allegri_with_Robinho.jpg/524px-Allegri_with_Robinho.jpg 2x" width="262"/></a>

<p>Allegri was released from his contract by Cagliari on 17 June 2010, under request of Serie A giants <a href="/wiki/A.C._Milan" title="A.C. Milan">Milan</a>, who were interested in appointing him as their new manager.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> On 25 June, Allegri was officially appointed as the new manager of Milan. In his first season in charge, Allegri led Milan to their first championship title since <a href="/wiki/2003%E2%80%9304_Serie_A" title="2003–04 Serie A">2004</a>, beating title incumbents and <a href="/wiki/Derby_della_Madonnina" title="Derby della Madonnina">cross-city rivals</a> <a href="/wiki/Inter_Milan" title="Inter Milan">Internazionale</a> in both league fixtures;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> at the time, only <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Mancini" title="Roberto Mancini">Roberto Mancini</a> had achieved that feat at a younger age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-relationship_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-relationship-8">[8]</a></sup> Allegri's Milan side, however, failed to make it past the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_Coppa_Italia" title="2010–11 Coppa Italia">semi-finals of the Coppa Italia</a>, losing to <a href="/wiki/U.S._Citt%C3%A0_di_Palermo" title="U.S. Città di Palermo">Palermo</a> 4–3 on aggregate. The team also qualified for the knockout stage of the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_UEFA_Champions_League" title="2010–11 UEFA Champions League">UEFA Champions League</a>, where they were eliminated by <a href="/wiki/Tottenham_Hotspur_F.C." title="Tottenham Hotspur F.C.">Tottenham Hotspur</a>.</p>
<p>Success continued in his second season at the club. He led Milan to their sixth <a href="/wiki/Supercoppa_Italiana" title="Supercoppa Italiana">Supercoppa Italiana</a> title, by coming from behind in a 2–1 win over city rivals Internazionale in a game played at the <a href="/wiki/Beijing_National_Stadium" title="Beijing National Stadium">Beijing National Stadium</a> on 6 August <a href="/wiki/2011_Supercoppa_Italiana" title="2011 Supercoppa Italiana">2011</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> That was as far as his success went for that season, however. A semi-final <a href="/wiki/Coppa_Italia" title="Coppa Italia">Coppa Italia</a> knockout at the hands of <a href="/wiki/Juventus_F.C." title="Juventus F.C.">Juventus</a> followed by a quarter-final <a href="/wiki/2011%E2%80%9312_UEFA_Champions_League" title="2011–12 UEFA Champions League">UEFA Champions League</a> knockout at the hands of <a href="/wiki/FC_Barcelona" title="FC Barcelona">Barcelona</a> left Milan fighting on only one front towards the end of the season — the <a href="/wiki/Scudetto" title="Scudetto">Scudetto</a>. Even that was to be lost, however, with the <i>Rossoneri</i> finishing in second place to Juventus and therefore qualifying for <a href="/wiki/2012%E2%80%9313_UEFA_Champions_League" title="2012–13 UEFA Champions League">next season's Champions League group stage</a>. The season was also marked with controversy however, as in the second half of the season, Milan had a goal by <a href="/wiki/Sulley_Muntari" title="Sulley Muntari">Sulley Muntari</a> disallowed in a key match against title rivals Juventus at the <a href="/wiki/San_Siro" title="San Siro">San Siro</a>, after they had already been leading 1–0; the match eventually ended in a 2–2 draw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-relationship_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-relationship-8">[8]</a></sup> Along with <a href="/wiki/Adriano_Galliani" title="Adriano Galliani">Adriano Galliani</a>, Allegri drew criticism for his decision to sell veteran deep-lying playmaker Andrea Pirlo to Juventus, after excluding him from the starting eleven the previous season, and deeming him to be a surplus due to his age; Pirlo went on to play a key role in Juventus's victorious Serie A title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-relationship_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-relationship-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 13 January 2012, Allegri agreed to extend his contract with Milan to the end of 2013–14 season. On 5 June 2012, Allegri stated that he intends to coach for only 10 more years and plans to retire at the age of 55 due to the fact that coaching contains a high level of stress and anxiety. He also said that he hopes to coach the Italian national side before he retires.</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<p>With the departure and retirement of several of the club's key players, Milan struggled at the beginning of the 2012–13 season, and had only collected 8 points from their first 7 games, putting Allegri in danger of getting fired early on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-relationship_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-relationship-8">[8]</a></sup> Despite all the criticism from the critics, Milan stuck with Allegri and he managed to get the best out of some younger players including <a href="/wiki/Stephan_El_Shaarawy" title="Stephan El Shaarawy">Stephan El Shaarawy</a>, Milan's home-grown player <a href="/wiki/Mattia_De_Sciglio" title="Mattia De Sciglio">Mattia De Sciglio</a> and later on <a href="/wiki/Mario_Balotelli" title="Mario Balotelli">Mario Balotelli</a>. Allegri managed to steer the Rossoneri from 16th place to a 3rd-place finish at the end of the season, with an epic 2–1 comeback victory away to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A.C._Siena" title="A.C. Siena">Siena</a>. The result gave Milan the a spot in the playoff round for the <a href="/wiki/2013%E2%80%9314_UEFA_Champions_League" title="2013–14 UEFA Champions League">2013–14 Champions League</a> campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-relationship_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-relationship-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 1 June 2013, club president <a href="/wiki/Silvio_Berlusconi" title="Silvio Berlusconi">Silvio Berlusconi</a> confirmed Allegri to remain as the manager of Milan, despite numerous speculations that he would be given the sack. On 31 December 2013, Allegri confirmed he would leave the club at the end of the season, telling <i>Gazzetta dello Sport</i> that, "...Of course this is my last Christmas at A.C. Milan."</p>
<p>On 13 January 2014 <a href="/wiki/A.C._Milan" title="A.C. Milan">Milan</a> reported that Allegri and his staff were relieved of their duties with immediate effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Juventus">Juventus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massimiliano_Allegri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Juventus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 16 July 2014, Allegri was appointed as the new head coach of <a href="/wiki/Juventus_F.C." title="Juventus F.C.">Juventus</a>, replacing <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Conte" title="Antonio Conte">Antonio Conte</a> who had resigned the day before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Although the decision to hire Allegri was initially met with ambivalence,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-relationship_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-relationship-8">[8]</a></sup> on 2 May 2015, he guided Juventus to their fourth consecutive <a href="/wiki/2014%E2%80%9315_Serie_A" title="2014–15 Serie A">Serie A title</a>, as the club defeated <a href="/wiki/U.C._Sampdoria" title="U.C. Sampdoria">Sampdoria</a> 1–0 at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stadio_Marassi" title="Stadio Marassi">Stadio Marassi</a> in Genoa; this was only the second time Juventus had won four consecutive Scudetto titles (the last time being from 1931 to 1935, when they won five Scudetto titles in a row). Having previously won the title with <a href="/wiki/A.C._Milan" title="A.C. Milan">Milan</a> in 2011, it also marked his second Scudetto title as a manager in <a href="/wiki/Serie_A" title="Serie A">Serie A</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> On 13 May 2015, Allegri guided Juventus to the <a href="/wiki/2015_UEFA_Champions_League_Final" title="2015 UEFA Champions League Final">Champions League final</a> by defeating defending champions <a href="/wiki/Real_Madrid_C.F." title="Real Madrid C.F.">Real Madrid</a> in the semifinals, 3–2 on aggregate;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> it had been twelve years since the Bianconeri had last appeared in the final, eventually losing to Milan 3–2 on penalties in <a href="/wiki/2003_UEFA_Champions_League_Final" title="2003 UEFA Champions League Final">in 2003</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> A week later, on 20 May 2015, Allegri guided Juventus to a domestic <a href="/wiki/Double_(association_football)" title="Double (association football)">double</a> by helping the club win their record tenth <a href="/wiki/2015_Coppa_Italia_Final" title="2015 Coppa Italia Final">Coppa Italia</a>, defeating <a href="/wiki/S.S._Lazio" title="S.S. Lazio">Lazio</a> 2–1 in <a href="/wiki/Overtime_(sports)" title="Overtime (sports)">extra time</a>. The Old Lady last won the trophy in 1995, breaking their twenty-year drought of having not won the competition, and making them the first team in Italy to win the trophy ten times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> On 6 June 2015, Juventus were defeated by <a href="/wiki/FC_Barcelona" title="FC Barcelona">Barcelona</a>, 3–1, in the 2015 <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">UEFA Champions League</a> <a href="/wiki/2015_UEFA_Champions_League_Final" title="2015 UEFA Champions League Final">final</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 6 July 2015, almost a year after signing with Juventus, Allegri signed a one-year extension to his current contract keeping him at the club until the end of the 2016–17 season. On 8 August 2015, Allegri led Juventus to a 2–0 victory over Lazio in the <a href="/wiki/2015_Supercoppa_Italiana" title="2015 Supercoppa Italiana">Supercoppa Italiana</a>, with new signings <a href="/wiki/Mario_Mand%C5%BEuki%C4%87" title="Mario Mandžukić">Mario Mandžukić</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paulo_Dybala" title="Paulo Dybala">Paulo Dybala</a> scoring the goals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Allegri was included in the 10-man shortlist of nominees for the <a href="/wiki/2015_FIFA_Ballon_d%27Or#FIFA_World_Coach_of_the_Year_for_Men.27s_Football" title="2015 FIFA Ballon d'Or">2015 FIFA World Coach of the Year Award</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> but was later not included among the three finalists, despite almost completing a treble in his first season as Juventus's coach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> However, on 14 December 2015, Allegri won the <a href="/wiki/Serie_A_Coach_of_the_Year" title="Serie A Coach of the Year">Serie A Coach of the Year</a> award;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sport_Mediaset_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sport_Mediaset-25">[25]</a></sup> and later on 7 March 2016, his second ever <a href="/wiki/Panchina_d%27Oro" title="Panchina d'Oro">Panchina d'Oro</a> for his success with Juventus during the 2014–15 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-juvepanchia_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-juvepanchia-26">[26]</a></sup> On 25 April, Juventus were crowned <a href="/wiki/2015%E2%80%9316_Serie_A" title="2015–16 Serie A">2015–16 Serie A</a> champions; after initially struggling during the first ten league matches of the season, Juventus went on an unbeaten streak and won 24 of their next 25 league games to come back from 12th place to defend the title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Juventus_retain_Scudetto_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Juventus_retain_Scudetto-27">[27]</a></sup> The team's unbeaten streak ended after 26 matches, and was broken following a 2–1 away loss to <a href="/wiki/Hellas_Verona_F.C." title="Hellas Verona F.C.">Verona</a> on 8 May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> On 6 May, Allegri extended his contract as Juventus manager to 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> On 21 May, he led Juventus to the domestic double once again after a 1–0 victory over Milan in the <a href="/wiki/2016_Coppa_Italia_Final" title="2016 Coppa Italia Final">2016 Coppa Italia Final</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style_of_management">Style of management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massimiliano_Allegri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Style of management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Allegri has been praised for his tactical intelligence and his ability to build effectively upon Antonio Conte's successful tactics and winning mentality as Juventus's manager, albeit in a less rigorous manner. He initially continued to use Conte's trademark <a href="/wiki/Formation_(association_football)#3.E2.80.935.E2.80.932" title="Formation (association football)">3–5–2 formation</a>, but later switched to a four-man back-line, in particular in the UEFA Champions League. Under Allegri, Juventus became slightly less aggressive and intense in their pressing off the ball, while their playing style became more patient and focussed on keeping possession and gaining territorial advantage, in order to conserve energy, tactics which he had previously used while at Milan. Although Allegri's team was still effective at winning the ball in midfield and scoring from counter-attacks, the club's attacking play on the ball usually involved a slower build-up, which mainly consisted of short passes, and fewer long balls from <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Pirlo" title="Andrea Pirlo">Pirlo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_Bonucci" title="Leonardo Bonucci">Bonucci</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stamp_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-stamp-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imagination_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-imagination-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mentality_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mentality-34">[34]</a></sup> although goalkeeper <a href="/wiki/Gianluigi_Buffon" title="Gianluigi Buffon">Buffon</a> continued to primarily play the ball out from the back with short ground passes to the defenders, he also began to use deeper goal kicks out to the wings, however.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beginnings_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-beginnings-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-superpower_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-superpower-37">[37]</a></sup> Allegri has also been lauded for his versatility, which he demonstrated by adopting and switching between several different formations during his first season with Juventus, including the <a href="/wiki/Formation_(association_football)#4.E2.80.933.E2.80.931.E2.80.932" title="Formation (association football)">4–3–1–2</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Formation_(association_football)#4.E2.80.934.E2.80.932" title="Formation (association football)">4–4–2</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Formation_(association_football)#4.E2.80.933.E2.80.933" title="Formation (association football)">4–3–3 formations</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imagination_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-imagination-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conundrum_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-conundrum-38">[38]</a></sup> which he had also previously adopted while at Milan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> in order to find the most suitable system for his players.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-top_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-top-40">[40]</a></sup> The fluidity of Juventus's formations under Allegri allowed talented players more freedom and creative licence,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stamp_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-stamp-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imagination_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-imagination-32">[32]</a></sup> and enabled the midfielders to make attacking runs into the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-top_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-top-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-title_intentions_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-title_intentions-41">[41]</a></sup> Despite implementing several changes, Allegri still managed to preserve Juventus's defensive organisation and strength: this was further demonstrated by his tendency to switch to the 3–5–2 in the final 20 minutes of closely contested matches, in order to hold on to the result,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-superpower_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-superpower-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-top_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-top-40">[40]</a></sup> as well as by his tendency to play behind the ball with two deep, tight defensive lines when facing teams that dominated possession, preferring instead to focus on breaking up play and adopting a counter-attacking style of play similar to that which he had previously also used with Milan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ode_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ode-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pirlo praised Allegri's seemingly more relaxed attitude to coaching stating that he "...brought a sense of calm" to the team, and that he aided in endowing the players with a sense of confidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-top_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-top-40">[40]</a></sup> Regarding Juventus's fluidity under Allegri, Italian football analyst Antonio Gagliardi tweeted: "Juventus defend low with a <a href="/wiki/Formation_(association_football)#5.E2.80.934.E2.80.931" title="Formation (association football)">5–4–1</a>, they press with a 4–4–2 and they attack with a <a href="/wiki/Formation_(association_football)#4.E2.80.932.E2.80.933.E2.80.931" title="Formation (association football)">4–2–3–1</a>. Systems in the future will become ever more fluid."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-credit_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-credit-45">[45]</a></sup> In his second season with the club, Allegri was praised for rebuilding the team following the departure of several key players, such as Pirlo, <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Vidal" title="Arturo Vidal">Vidal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Tevez" title="Carlos Tevez">Tevez</a>, and for his role in inspiring Juventus to a comeback to defend the league title, which included a 15-match winning streak, after a negative start.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mentality_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mentality-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trionfo_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-trionfo-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Although a 1–0 defeat to Sassuolo on 28 October 2015 left them in 12th place, 11 points from the top of the table after their first 10 league matches, Juventus went unbeaten in their next 25 fixtures, winning 24 of them, and finished the season as 2015–16 Serie A champions with three games to spare; this was the club's record fifth consecutive league title since the 2011–12 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Juventus_retain_Scudetto_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Juventus_retain_Scudetto-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ode_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ode-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trionfo_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-trionfo-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
