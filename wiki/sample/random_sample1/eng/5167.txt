<p><b>Matsuo Bashō</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">松尾 芭蕉</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, 1644–1694)</span>, born 松尾 金作, then <b>Matsuo Chūemon Munefusa</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">松尾 忠右衛門 宗房</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> was the most famous poet of the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a> in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. During his lifetime, Bashō was recognized for his works in the collaborative <i><a href="/wiki/Renku" title="Renku">haikai no renga</a></i> form; today, after centuries of commentary, he is recognized as the greatest master of <a href="/wiki/Haiku" title="Haiku">haiku</a> (then called <a href="/wiki/Hokku" title="Hokku">hokku</a>). Matsuo Bashō's poetry is internationally renowned; and, in Japan, many of his poems are reproduced on monuments and traditional sites. Although Bashō is justifiably famous in the West for his hokku, he himself believed his best work lay in leading and participating in <a href="/wiki/Renku" title="Renku">renku</a>. He is quoted as saying, "Many of my followers can write hokku as well as I can. Where I show who I really am is in linking haikai verses."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bashō was introduced to poetry at a young age, and after integrating himself into the intellectual scene of <a href="/wiki/Edo" title="Edo">Edo</a> (modern Tokyo) he quickly became well known throughout Japan. He made a living as a teacher; but then renounced the social, urban life of the literary circles and was inclined to wander throughout the country, heading west, east, and far into the northern wilderness to gain inspiration for his writing. His poems were influenced by his firsthand experience of the world around him, often encapsulating the feeling of a scene in a few simple elements.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matsuo_Bash%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MatsuoBasyoSeika.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="948" data-file-width="925" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/MatsuoBasyoSeika.jpg/220px-MatsuoBasyoSeika.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/MatsuoBasyoSeika.jpg/330px-MatsuoBasyoSeika.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/MatsuoBasyoSeika.jpg/440px-MatsuoBasyoSeika.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Bashō was born in 1644, near <a href="/wiki/Ueno,_Mie" title="Ueno, Mie">Ueno</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Iga_Province" title="Iga Province">Iga Province</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokusai_1948.2C_p._246_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kokusai_1948.2C_p._246-5">[5]</a></sup> His father may have been a low-ranking <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a>, which would have promised Bashō a career in the military, but not much chance of a notable life. His biographers traditionally claimed that he worked in the kitchens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, as a child, Bashō became a servant to Tōdō Yoshitada <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">藤堂 良忠</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>: together they shared a love for <i><a href="/wiki/Renku" title="Renku">haikai no renga</a></i>, a form of collaborative poetry composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> A sequence was opened with a verse in 5-7-5 <a href="/wiki/Mora_(linguistics)" title="Mora (linguistics)">mora</a> format; this verse was named a <i><a href="/wiki/Hokku" title="Hokku">hokku</a></i>, and would centuries later be renamed <i>haiku</i> when presented as a stand-alone work. The <i>hokku</i> would be followed by a related 7-7 mora verse by another poet. Both Bashō and Yoshitada gave themselves <i>haigō</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">俳号</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Haikai" title="Haikai">haikai</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Pen_name" title="Pen name">pen names</a>; Bashō's was <b>Sōbō <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">宗房</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span></b>, which was simply the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/On%27yomi" title="On'yomi">on'yomi</a></i> (Sino-Japanese reading) of his adult name, "Munefusa <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">宗房</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>". In 1662, the first extant poem by Bashō was published. In 1664, two of Bashō's hokku were printed in a compilation.</p>
<p>In 1665, Bashō and Yoshitada together with some acquaintances composed a hyakuin, or one-hundred-verse <i><a href="/wiki/Renku" title="Renku">renku</a></i>. In 1666, Yoshitada's sudden death brought Bashō's peaceful life as a servant to an end. No records of this time remain, but it is believed that Bashō gave up any possibility of samurai status and left home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Biographers have proposed various reasons and destinations, including the possibility of an affair between Bashō and a Shinto <i><a href="/wiki/Miko" title="Miko">miko</a></i> named Jutei <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">寿貞</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, which is unlikely to be true.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Bashō's own references to this time are vague; he recalled that "at one time I coveted an official post with a tenure of land", and that "there was a time when I was fascinated with the ways of homosexual love": there is no indication whether he was referring to real obsessions or fictional ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He was uncertain whether to become a full-time poet; by his own account, "the alternatives battled in my mind and made my life restless".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> His indecision may have been influenced by the then still relatively low status of <i>renga</i> and <i>haikai no renga</i> as more social activities than serious artistic endeavors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In any case, his poems continued to be published in anthologies in 1667, 1669, and 1671, and he published a compilation of work by himself and other authors of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Teitoku&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Teitoku (page does not exist)">Teitoku</a> school, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seashell_Game" title="The Seashell Game">The Seashell Game</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">貝おほひ</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Kai Ōi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, in 1672.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokusai_1948.2C_p._246_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kokusai_1948.2C_p._246-5">[5]</a></sup> In about the spring of that year he moved to <a href="/wiki/Edo" title="Edo">Edo</a>, to further his study of poetry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_to_fame">Rise to fame</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matsuo_Bash%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rise to fame">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fermier_celebrant_la_lune_d%27automne.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="759" data-file-width="524" height="319" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Fermier_celebrant_la_lune_d%27automne.jpg/220px-Fermier_celebrant_la_lune_d%27automne.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Fermier_celebrant_la_lune_d%27automne.jpg/330px-Fermier_celebrant_la_lune_d%27automne.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Fermier_celebrant_la_lune_d%27automne.jpg/440px-Fermier_celebrant_la_lune_d%27automne.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fermier_celebrant_la_lune_d%27automne.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bashō meets two farmers celebrating the mid-autumn moon festival in a print from <a href="/wiki/Yoshitoshi" title="Yoshitoshi">Yoshitoshi</a>'s <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hundred_Aspects_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hundred Aspects of the Moon (page does not exist)">Hundred Aspects of the Moon</a></i>. The haiku reads: "Since the crescent moon, I have been waiting for tonight."</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the fashionable literary circles of <a href="/wiki/Nihonbashi" title="Nihonbashi">Nihonbashi</a>, Bashō's poetry was quickly recognized for its simple and natural style. In 1674 he was inducted into the inner circle of the <i>haikai</i> profession, receiving secret teachings from Kitamura Kigin (1624–1705).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> He wrote this <i>hokku</i> in mock tribute to the <a href="/wiki/Shogun" title="Shogun">Shogun</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>甲比丹もつくばはせけり君が春 <i>kapitan mo / tsukubawasekeri / kimi ga haru</i>
<dl>
<dd>the Dutchmen, too, / kneel before His Lordship— / spring under His reign. [1678]</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Nishiyama_S%C5%8Din" title="Nishiyama Sōin">Nishiyama Sōin</a>, founder and leader of the <a href="/wiki/Danrin_school" title="Danrin school">Danrin school</a> of haikai, came to Edo from Osaka in 1675, Bashō was among the poets invited to compose with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It was on this occasion that he gave himself the <i>haigō</i> of Tōsei, and by 1680 he had a full-time job teaching twenty disciples, who published <i>The Best Poems of Tōsei's Twenty Disciples</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">桃青門弟独吟二十歌仙</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Tōsei-montei Dokugin-Nijukasen</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, advertising their connection to Tōsei's talent. That winter, he took the surprising step of moving across the river to Fukagawa, out of the public eye and towards a more reclusive life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> His disciples built him a rustic hut and planted a <a href="/wiki/Banana" title="Banana">banana</a> tree <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">芭蕉</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>bashō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> in the yard, giving Bashō a new <i>haigō</i> and his first permanent home. He appreciated the plant very much, but was not happy to see Fukagawa's native <a href="/wiki/Miscanthus" title="Miscanthus">miscanthus</a> grass growing alongside it:</p>
<dl>
<dd>ばせを植ゑてまづ憎む荻の二葉哉 <i>bashō uete / mazu nikumu ogi no / futaba kana</i>
<dl>
<dd>by my new banana plant / the first sign of something I loathe— / a miscanthus bud! [1680]</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Despite his success, Bashō grew dissatisfied and lonely. He began to practice <a href="/wiki/Zen" title="Zen">Zen</a> <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">meditation</a>, but it seems not to have calmed his mind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In the winter of 1682 his hut burned down, and shortly afterwards, in early 1683, his mother died. He then traveled to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yamura" title="Yamura">Yamura</a>, to stay with a friend. In the winter of 1683 his disciples gave him a second hut in Edo, but his spirits did not improve. In 1684 his disciple <a href="/wiki/Takarai_Kikaku" title="Takarai Kikaku">Takarai Kikaku</a> published a compilation of him and other poets, <i>Shriveled Chestnuts</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">虚栗</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Minashiguri</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Later that year he left Edo on the first of four major wanderings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bashō traveled alone, off the beaten path, that is, on the <a href="/wiki/Edo_Five_Routes" title="Edo Five Routes">Edo Five Routes</a>, which in medieval Japan were regarded as immensely dangerous; and, at first Bashō expected to simply die in the middle of nowhere or be killed by bandits. However, as his trip progressed, his mood improved, and he became comfortable on the road. Bashō met many friends and grew to enjoy the changing scenery and the seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> His poems took on a less introspective and more striking tone as he observed the world around him:</p>
<dl>
<dd>馬をさへながむる雪の朝哉 <i>uma wo sae / nagamuru yuki no / ashita kana</i>
<dl>
<dd>even a horse / arrests my eyes—on this / snowy morrow [1684]</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The trip took him from Edo to <a href="/wiki/Mount_Fuji" title="Mount Fuji">Mount Fuji</a>, Ueno, and <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> He met several poets who called themselves his disciples and wanted his advice; he told them to disregard the contemporary Edo style and even his own <i>Shriveled Chestnuts</i>, saying it contained "many verses that are not worth discussing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Bashō returned to Edo in the summer of 1685, taking time along the way to write more <i>hokku</i> and comment on his own life:</p>
<dl>
<dd>年暮ぬ笠きて草鞋はきながら <i>toshi kurenu / kasa kite waraji / hakinagara</i>
<dl>
<dd>another year is gone / a traveler's shade on my head, / straw sandals at my feet [1685]</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>When Bashō returned to Edo he happily resumed his job as a teacher of poetry at his <i>bashō</i> hut, although privately he was already making plans for another journey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The poems from his journey were published as <i>Account of Exposure to the Fields</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">野ざらし紀行</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Nozarashi kikō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>. In early 1686 he composed one of his best-remembered haiku:</p>
<dl>
<dd>古池や蛙飛びこむ水の音 <i>furu ike ya / kawazu tobikomu / mizu no oto</i>
<dl>
<dd>an ancient pond / a frog jumps in / the splash of water [1686]</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Historians believe this poem became instantly famous: in April, the poets of Edo gathered at the <i>bashō</i> hut for a <i>haikai no renga</i> contest on the subject of frogs that seems to have been a tribute to Bashō's <i>hokku</i>, which was placed at the top of the compilation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Bashō stayed in Edo, continuing to teach and hold contests, with an excursion in the autumn of 1687 when he traveled to the countryside for <a href="/wiki/Tsukimi" title="Tsukimi">moon watching</a>, and a longer trip in 1688 when he returned to Ueno to celebrate the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_New_Year" title="Japanese New Year">Lunar New Year</a>. At home in Edo, Bashō sometimes became reclusive: he alternated between rejecting visitors to his hut and appreciating their company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> At the same time, he enjoyed his life and had a subtle sense of humor, as reflected in his <i>hokku</i>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>いざさらば雪見にころぶ所迄 <i>iza saraba / yukimi ni korobu / tokoromade</i>
<dl>
<dd>now then, let's go out / to enjoy the snow... until / I slip and fall! [1688]</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oku_no_Hosomichi"><i>Oku no Hosomichi</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matsuo_Bash%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oku no Hosomichi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Oku_no_Hosomichi" title="Oku no Hosomichi">Oku no Hosomichi</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sora%27s_Diary" title="Sora's Diary">Sora's Diary</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Basho_in_Ogaki.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Basho_in_Ogaki.JPG/220px-Basho_in_Ogaki.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Basho_in_Ogaki.JPG/330px-Basho_in_Ogaki.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Basho_in_Ogaki.JPG/440px-Basho_in_Ogaki.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Basho_in_Ogaki.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A statue commemorating Matsuo Bashō's arrival in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C5%8Cgaki" title="Ōgaki">Ōgaki</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Bashō's private planning for another long journey, to be described in his masterwork <i>Oku no Hosomichi</i>, or <i>The Narrow Road to the Deep North</i>, culminated on May 16, 1689 (Yayoi 27, <a href="/wiki/Genroku" title="Genroku">Genroku</a> 2), when he left Edo with his student and apprentice Kawai Sora <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%B2%B3%E5%90%88%E6%9B%BE%E8%89%AF" title="ja:河合曾良">河合 曾良</a></span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> on a journey to the Northern Provinces of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honsh%C5%AB" title="Honshū">Honshū</a>. Bashō and Sora headed north to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hiraizumi" title="Hiraizumi">Hiraizumi</a>, which they reached on June 29. They then walked to the western side of the island, touring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kisakata" title="Kisakata">Kisakata</a> on <span class="nowrap">July 30</span>, and began hiking back at a leisurely pace along the coastline. During this 150-day journey Bashō traveled a total of 600 <i><a href="/wiki/Japanese_units_of_measurement#Length" title="Japanese units of measurement">ri</a></i> (2,400 km) through the northeastern areas of Honshū, returning to <a href="/wiki/Edo" title="Edo">Edo</a> in late 1691.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the time Bashō reached <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Cgaki,_Gifu" title="Ōgaki, Gifu">Ōgaki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gifu_Prefecture" title="Gifu Prefecture">Gifu Prefecture</a>, he had completed the log of his journey. He edited and redacted it for three years, writing the final version in 1694 as <i>The Narrow Road to the Interior</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">奥の細道</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Oku no Hosomichi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>. The first edition was published posthumously in 1702.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> It was an immediate commercial success and many other itinerant poets followed the path of his journey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kokusai_1948.2C_p._246_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kokusai_1948.2C_p._246-5">[5]</a></sup> It is often considered his finest achievement, featuring <i>hokku</i> such as:</p>
<dl>
<dd>荒海や佐渡によこたふ天河 <i>araumi ya / Sado ni yokotau / amanogawa</i>
<dl>
<dd>the rough sea / stretching out towards Sado / the Milky Way [1689]</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Last_years">Last years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matsuo_Bash%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Last years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MatsuoBasho-Haka-M1932.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="1000" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/MatsuoBasho-Haka-M1932.jpg/220px-MatsuoBasho-Haka-M1932.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/MatsuoBasho-Haka-M1932.jpg/330px-MatsuoBasho-Haka-M1932.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/MatsuoBasho-Haka-M1932.jpg/440px-MatsuoBasho-Haka-M1932.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MatsuoBasho-Haka-M1932.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bashō's grave in <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ctsu" title="Ōtsu">Ōtsu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shiga_Prefecture" title="Shiga Prefecture">Shiga Prefecture</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On his return to Edo in the winter of 1691, Bashō lived in his third <i>bashō</i> hut, again provided by his disciples. This time, he was not alone: he took in a nephew and his female friend, Jutei, who were both recovering from illness. He had a great many visitors.</p>
<p>Bashō continued to be uneasy. He wrote to a friend that "disturbed by others, I have no peace of mind".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> He made a living from teaching and appearances at <i>haikai</i> parties until late August 1693, when he shut the gate to his <i>bashō</i> hut and refused to see anybody for a month. Finally, he relented after adopting the principle of <i>karumi</i> or "lightness", a semi-Buddhist philosophy of greeting the mundane world rather than separating himself from it. Bashō left Edo for the last time in the summer of 1694, spending time in Ueno and Kyoto before his arrival in Osaka. He became sick with a stomach illness and died peacefully, surrounded by his disciples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Although he did not compose any formal <a href="/wiki/Death_poem" title="Death poem">death poem</a> on his deathbed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> the following, being the last poem recorded during his final illness, is generally accepted as his poem of farewell:</p>
<dl>
<dd>旅に病んで夢は枯野をかけ廻る <i>tabi ni yande / yume wa kareno wo / kake meguru</i>
<dl>
<dd>falling sick on a journey / my dream goes wandering / over a field of dried grass [1694]</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_and_literary_criticism">Influence and literary criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matsuo_Bash%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Influence and literary criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:100_views_edo_040.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="794" height="332" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/100_views_edo_040.jpg/220px-100_views_edo_040.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/100_views_edo_040.jpg/330px-100_views_edo_040.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/100_views_edo_040.jpg/440px-100_views_edo_040.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:100_views_edo_040.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
"Basho's Hermitage and Camellia Hill on the Kanda Aqueduct at Sekiguchi" from <a href="/wiki/Hiroshige" title="Hiroshige">Hiroshige</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/One_Hundred_Famous_Views_of_Edo" title="One Hundred Famous Views of Edo">One Hundred Famous Views of Edo</a></i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Rather than sticking to the formulas of <i><a href="/wiki/Kigo" title="Kigo">kigo</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">季語</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, which remain popular in Japan even today, Bashō aspired to reflect his real environment and emotions in his <i>hokku</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Even during his lifetime, the effort and style of his poetry was widely appreciated; after his death, it only increased. Several of his students compiled quotations from him about his own poetry, most notably <a href="/wiki/Mukai_Kyorai" title="Mukai Kyorai">Mukai Kyorai</a> and Hattori Dohō.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page7_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-page7-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 18th century, appreciation of Bashō's poems grew more fervent, and commentators such as Ishiko Sekisui and Moro Nanimaru went to great length to find references in his <i>hokku</i> to historical events, medieval books, and other poems. These commentators were often lavish in their praise of Bashō's obscure references, some of which were probably literary <a href="/wiki/False_cognate" title="False cognate">false cognates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page7_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-page7-32">[32]</a></sup> In 1793 Bashō was deified by the Shinto bureaucracy, and for a time criticizing his poetry was literally blasphemous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page7_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-page7-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 19th century, this period of unanimous passion for Bashō's poems came to an end. <a href="/wiki/Masaoka_Shiki" title="Masaoka Shiki">Masaoka Shiki</a>, arguably Bashō's most famous critic, tore down the long-standing orthodoxy with his bold and candid objections to Bashō's style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page7_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-page7-32">[32]</a></sup> However, Shiki was also instrumental in making Bashō's poetry accessible in English,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and to leading intellectuals and the Japanese public at large. He invented the term <i><a href="/wiki/Haiku" title="Haiku">haiku</a></i> (replacing <i><a href="/wiki/Hokku" title="Hokku">hokku</a></i>) to refer to the freestanding 5-7-5 form which he considered the most artistic and desirable part of the <i>haikai no renga</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page7_32-4"><a href="#cite_note-page7-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Critical interpretation of Bashō's poems continued into the 20th century, with notable works by Yamamoto Kenkichi, Imoto Nōichi, and Ogata Tsutomu. The 20th century also saw translations of Bashō's poems into languages and editions around the world. The position of Bashō in Western eyes as the <i>haiku</i> poet <i>par excellence</i> gives great influence to his poetry: Western preference for <i>haiku</i> over more traditional forms such as <i>tanka</i> or <i>renga</i> have rendered archetypal status to Bashō as Japanese poet and <i>haiku</i> as <a href="/wiki/Japanese_poetry" title="Japanese poetry">Japanese poetry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Some western scholars even believe that Bashō invented haiku.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The impressionistic and concise nature of Bashō's verse greatly influenced <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Pound" title="Ezra Pound">Ezra Pound</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Imagism" title="Imagism">Imagists</a>, and poets of the <a href="/wiki/Beat_Generation" title="Beat Generation">Beat Generation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two of Bashō's poems were popularized in the short story <a href="/wiki/Teddy_(story)" title="Teddy (story)">"Teddy"</a> written by <a href="/wiki/J._D._Salinger" title="J. D. Salinger">J. D. Salinger</a> and published in 1952 by <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i> magazine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1979, the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> named a crater found on Mercury after him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Union1980_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Union1980-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
