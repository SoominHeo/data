<p>The term <b>narcotic</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ɑːr/ 'ar' in 'far'">ɑːr</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span></span>/</a></span></span>, from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">ancient Greek</a> ναρκῶ <i>narkō</i>, "to make numb") originally referred medically to any <a href="/wiki/Psychoactive_drug" title="Psychoactive drug">psychoactive</a> compound with sleep-inducing properties. In the United States, it has since become associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opiates" title="Opiates">opiates</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opioids" title="Opioids">opioids</a>, commonly <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heroin" title="Heroin">heroin</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Derivative_(chemistry)" title="Derivative (chemistry)">derivatives</a> of many of the compounds found within raw opium latex. The primary three are morphine, <a href="/wiki/Codeine" title="Codeine">codeine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thebaine" title="Thebaine">thebaine</a> (while thebaine itself is only very mildly psychoactive, it is a crucial precursor in the vast majority of semi-synthetic opioids, such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrocodone" title="Hydrocodone">hydrocodone</a>). Legally speaking, the term "narcotic" is imprecisely defined and typically has negative connotations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> When used in a legal context in the U.S., a narcotic drug is simply one that is totally prohibited, or one that is used in violation of governmental regulation, such as heroin or <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">cannabis</a>.</p>
<p>In the medical community, the term is more precisely defined and generally does not carry the same negative connotations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Statutory classification of a drug as a narcotic often increases the penalties for violation of drug control statutes. For example, although federal law classifies both <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amphetamine" title="Amphetamine">amphetamines</a> as "Schedule II" drugs, the penalty for possession of cocaine is greater than the penalty for possession of amphetamines because cocaine, unlike amphetamines, is classified as a narcotic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Nations">United Nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcotic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: United Nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Single_Convention_on_Narcotic_Drugs.2C_1961">Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs, 1961</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcotic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs, 1961">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The adoption of this Convention is regarded as a milestone in the history of international drug law. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_convention_on_narcotic_drugs" title="Single convention on narcotic drugs">The Single Convention</a> codified all existing multilateral treaties on drug control and extended the existing control systems to include the cultivation of plants that were grown as the raw material of narcotic drugs. The principal objectives of the Convention are to limit the possession, use, trade, distribution, import, export, manufacture and production of drugs exclusively to medical and scientific purposes, and to address drug trafficking through international cooperation to deter and discourage drug traffickers. The Convention also established the International Narcotics Control Board, merging the Permanent Central Board and the Drug Supervisory Board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 1961 Convention seeks to control more than 116 drugs that it classifies as narcotic. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li>plant-based products such as opium and its derivatives morphine, codeine and heroin (the primary category of drug listed in the Convention);</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>synthetic narcotics such as methadone and pethidine; and</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>cannabis, coca and cocaine.</li>
</ul>
<p>The Convention divides drugs into four groups, or schedules, in order to enforce a greater or lesser degree of control for the various substances and compounds. Opium smoking and eating, coca leaf chewing, cannabis resin smoking and the non-medical use of cannabis are prohibited. The 1972 Protocol to this Convention calls for increased efforts to prevent illicit production of, traffic in and use of narcotics as defined by the Convention, while highlighting the need to provide treatment and rehabilitation services to drug abusers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="INCB_Yellow_List">INCB Yellow List</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcotic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: INCB Yellow List">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This document contains the current list of narcotic drugs under international control and additional information to assist governments in filling in the <a href="/wiki/International_Narcotics_Control_Board" title="International Narcotics Control Board">International Narcotics Control Board</a> questionnaires related to narcotic drugs, namely, form A, form B and form C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In medicine, a chemical agent that induces stupor, coma, or insensibility to pain (also called narcotic analgesic).</p>
<p>In the context of international drug control, “narcotic drug” means any drug defined as such under the 1961 Convention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_Health_Organization">World Health Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcotic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: World Health Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Studies_on_the_definition_of_counterfeit_medicines_in_WHO_member_states">Studies on the definition of <i>counterfeit medicines</i> in WHO member states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcotic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Studies on the definition of counterfeit medicines in WHO member states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>4. Assessment of the definitions of counterfeit medicines (or equivalent) in Member States</p>
<p>4.2 The nature of legal definitions: the unambiguity requirement</p>
<p>In order to avoid room for difference in interpretation, law-makers (<i>codificators</i>) sometimes deviate from etymological (definiendum plus definientia) definitions. In doing so, they approach the term from the law enforcement point of view. The best example is the definition of <i>narcotics</i> in the United Nations Conventions. Narcotics are substances and preparations that induce drowsiness, sleep, stupor, insensibility, etc., and that these effects (and their rate) are complicated to prove, e.g. during litigation. Thus, the legal definition of a narcotic is whether or not it is listed on the Schedules of the Convention. If it is on some of the Schedules, it is narcotic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lexicon_of_alcohol_and_drug_terms_published_by_the_World_Health_Organization">Lexicon of alcohol and drug terms published by the World Health Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcotic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lexicon of alcohol and drug terms published by the World Health Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term usually refers to opiates or opioids, which are called narcotic analgesics. In common parlance and legal usage, it is often used imprecisely to mean illicit drugs, irrespective of their pharmacology. For example, narcotics control legislation in Canada, USA, and certain other countries includes cocaine and cannabis as well as opioids (see also conventions, international drug). Because of this variation in usage, the term is best replaced by one with a more specific meaning (e.g. opioid).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcotic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Section 1300.01 Definitions relating to controlled substances:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>(b) As used in parts 1301 through 1308 and part 1312 of this chapter, the following terms shall have the meanings specified:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>(30) The term narcotic drug means any of the following whether produced directly or indirectly by extraction from substances of vegetable origin or independently by means of chemical synthesis or by a combination of extraction and chemical synthesis:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>(i) Opium, opiates, derivatives of opium and opiates, including their isomers, esters, ethers, salts, and salts of isomers, esters, and ethers whenever the existence of such isomers, esters, ethers and salts is possible within the specific chemical designation. Such term does not include the isoquinoline alkaloids of opium.</p>
<p>(ii) Poppy straw and concentrate of poppy straw.</p>
<p>(iii) Coca leaves, except coca leaves and extracts of coca leaves from which cocaine, <a href="/wiki/Ecgonine" title="Ecgonine">ecgonine</a> and derivatives of ecgonine or their salts have been removed.</p>
<p>(iv) Cocaine, its salts, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_isomerism" title="Optical isomerism">optical</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geometric_isomer" title="Geometric isomer">geometric isomers</a>, and salts of isomers.</p>
<p>(v) Ecgonine, its derivatives, their salts, isomers and salts of isomers.</p>
<p>(vi) Any compound, mixture, or preparation which contains any quantity of any of the substances referred to in paragraphs (b)(31)(i) through (v) of this section.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>
<p>A 1984 amendment to 21 USC (<a href="/wiki/Controlled_Substances_Act" title="Controlled Substances Act">Controlled Substances Act</a>), Section 802 expanded and revised definition of "narcotic drug", including within term poppy straw, cocaine, and ecgonine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="US_v._Stieren"><i>US v. Stieren</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcotic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: US v. Stieren">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>608 F.2d 1135</p>
<blockquote>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals,_Eighth_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals, Eighth Circuit">United States Court of Appeals, Eighth Circuit</a>. Decided Oct. 31, 1979. LAY, Circuit Judge.</p>
<p>John Arthur Stieren appeals from the judgment of conviction for possession of cocaine with intent to distribute and dispense under 21 U.S.C. § 841(a)(1). Stieren contends that the statute is unconstitutional because "cocaine is classified as a narcotic under Schedule II of 21 U.S.C. § 812(c), when as a matter of scientific and medical fact cocaine is not a narcotic but is a non-narcotic stimulant."</p>
<p>The sufficiency of the evidence is not disputed. Stieren was convicted after special agents testified that he was in possession of and attempted to sell them a large quantity of cocaine. Defendant urges that the testimony and reports by physicians and scientists demonstrate that cocaine is not a narcotic. He also cites cases which hold that cocaine is not a narcotic under the pharmacological definition of the term. State v. Erickson, 574 P.2d 1 (Alaska 1978).</p>
<p>It is within the legislative prerogative to classify cocaine, which is a non-narcotic central nervous system stimulant, as a narcotic for penalty and regulatory purposes. 21 U.S.C. § 802(16)(A). The use of cocaine poses serious problems for the community and has a high potential for abuse. Congress' choice of penalty reflects a societal policy which must be adhered to by the courts.2 Congress has the power to reclassify cocaine. This power has been delegated to the Attorney General. 21 U.S.C. § 811(a)(1). If cocaine is to be reclassified, defendant's arguments should be made to the legislative branch, not the courts.</p>
<p>We hold that Congress had a rational legislative purpose when it classified cocaine as a Schedule II narcotic drug for the purpose of imposing penalties.</p>
<p>JUDGMENT AFFIRMED.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcotic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "narcotic" is believed to have been coined by the Greek physician <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Galen</a> to refer to agents that numb or deaden, causing loss of <a href="/wiki/Feeling" title="Feeling">feeling</a> or <a href="/wiki/Paralysis" title="Paralysis">paralysis</a>. It is based on the Greek word <i>ναρκωσις</i> (narcosis)<i>,</i> the term used by <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a> for the process of numbing or the numbed state. Galen listed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mandrake_root" title="Mandrake root">mandrake root</a>, altercus (eclata),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (December 2012)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> seeds, and <a href="/wiki/Poppy_tea" title="Poppy tea">poppy juice</a> (<a href="/wiki/Opium" title="Opium">opium</a>) as the chief examples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It originally referred to any substance that relieved pain, dulled the senses, or induced sleep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Now, the term is used in a number of ways. Some people define narcotics as substances that bind at <a href="/wiki/Opioid_receptor" title="Opioid receptor">opioid receptors</a> (cellular membrane proteins activated by substances like heroin or morphine) while others refer to any illicit substance as a narcotic. From a U.S. legal perspective, narcotics refer to opium, opium derivatives, and their semi-synthetic substitutes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> though in U.S. law, due to its numbing properties, cocaine is also considered a narcotic.</p>
<p>Sense of "any illegal drug" first recorded 1926, Amer.Eng. The adj. is first attested c.1600.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> There are many different types of narcotics. The two most common forms of narcotic drugs are <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Codeine" title="Codeine">codeine</a>. Both are synthesized from opium for medicinal use. The most commonly used drug for recreational purposes created from opium is <a href="/wiki/Heroin" title="Heroin">heroin</a>. Synthesized drugs created with an opium base for use in pain management are fentanyl, oxycodone, tramadol, demarol, hydrocodone, methadone, and hydromorphone. New forms of pain medication are being created regularly. The newest drug to come out in 2014 is zohydro, an intense dosage of hydrocodone medication, the strongest yet created for pain management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analgesics">Analgesics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcotic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Analgesics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analgesics" title="Analgesics">Analgesics</a> are drugs that relieve <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">pain</a>. There are two main types: non-narcotic analgesics for mild pain, and narcotic analgesics for severe pain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
