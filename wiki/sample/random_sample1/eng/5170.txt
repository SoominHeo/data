<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Drug cartel</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>A <b>drug cartel</b> is any <a href="/wiki/Organized_crime" title="Organized crime">criminal organization</a> with the intention of supplying <a href="/wiki/Illegal_drug_trade" title="Illegal drug trade">drug trafficking</a> operations. They range from loosely managed agreements among various drug traffickers to formalized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_enterprise" title="Commercial enterprise">commercial enterprises</a>. The term was applied when the largest trafficking organizations reached an agreement to coordinate the production and distribution of cocaine. Since that agreement was broken up, drug cartels are no longer actually <a href="/wiki/Cartel" title="Cartel">cartels</a>, but the term stuck and it is now popularly used to refer to any criminal narcotics related organization, such as those in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burma</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</p>
<p>The basic structure of a drug cartel is as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>Falcons (Spanish: <i>Halcones</i>): Considered the "eyes and ears" of the streets, the "falcons" are the lowest rank in any drug cartel. They are responsible for supervising and reporting the activities of the police, the military, and rival groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>Hitmen (Spanish: <i>Sicarios</i>): The armed group within the drug cartel, responsible for carrying out assassinations, kidnappings, thefts, extortions, operating protection rackets, and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/turf_war" title="wikt:turf war">defending their <i>plaza</i> (turf)</a> from rival groups and the military.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>Lieutenants (Spanish: <i>Lugartenientes</i>): The second highest position in the drug cartel organization, responsible for supervising the hitmen and falcons within their own territory. They are allowed to carry out low-profile executions without permission from their bosses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Drug_lord" title="Drug lord">Drug lords</a> (Spanish: <i>Capos</i>): The highest position in any drug cartel, responsible for supervising the entire drug industry, appointing territorial leaders, making alliances, and planning high-profile executions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>It is worth noting that there are other operating groups within the drug cartels. For example, the drug producers and suppliers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> although not considered in the basic structure, are critical operators of any drug cartel, along with the financiers and money launderers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In addition, the arms suppliers operate in a completely different circle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and are technically not considered part of the cartel’s logistics.</p>

<p><b>Mexican cartels</b> (also known in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> as: <b>La Mafia</b> (the mafia or the mob), <b>La Maña</b> (the skill / the bad manners),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <b>Narcotraficantes</b> (Narco-Traffickers), or simply as <b>Narcos</b>) is a generic term that usually refers to several, usually rival, criminal organizations that are combated by the Mexican government in the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Drug_War" title="Mexican Drug War">Mexican War on Drugs</a> (List sorted by branches and heritage):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Venezuela">Venezuela</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drug_cartel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Venezuela">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Historically Venezuela has been a path to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> for <a href="/wiki/Illegal_drug_trade_in_Colombia" title="Illegal drug trade in Colombia">illegal drugs originating in Colombia</a>, through Central America and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and Caribbean countries such as <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>.</p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, there was an increase of cocaine trafficking through Venezuela since 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> In 2005 Venezuela severed ties with the United States <a href="/wiki/Drug_Enforcement_Administration" title="Drug Enforcement Administration">Drug Enforcement Administration</a> (DEA), accusing its representatives of spying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT2012_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT2012-69">[69]</a></sup> Following the departure of the DEA from Venezuela and the expansion of DEA's partnership with Colombia in 2005, Venezuela became more attractive to drug traffickers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VICEdrugs_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-VICEdrugs-70">[70]</a></sup> Between 2008 and 2012, Venezuela's cocaine seizure ranking among other countries declined, going from being ranked fourth in the world for cocaine seizures in 2008<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unodc.org_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-unodc.org-71">[71]</a></sup> to sixth in the world in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> The cartel groups involved include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Cuntrera-Caruana_Mafia_clan" title="Cuntrera-Caruana Mafia clan">Cuntrera-Caruana Mafia clan</a> moved to Venezuela,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roth_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-roth-73">[73]</a></sup> which became an important hideout as the clan bought hotels and founded various businesses in Caracas and Valencia, as well as an extended ranch in <a href="/wiki/Barinas_(state)" title="Barinas (state)">Barinas</a>, near the Colombian border. <i>"Venezuela has its own Cosa Nostra family as if it is Sicilian territory,"</i> according to the Italian police. <i>"The structure and hierarchy of the Mafia has been entirely reproduced in Venezuela."</i> The Cuntrera-Caruana clan had direct links with the ruling Commission of the Sicilian Mafia, and are acknowledged by the American Cosa Nostra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roth_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-roth-73">[73]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Pasquale, Paolo and Gaspare Cuntrera were expelled from Venezuela in 1992, "almost secretly smuggled out of the country, as if it concerned one of their own drug transports. It was imperative they could not contact people on the outside who could have used their political connections to stop the expulsion." Their expulsion was ordered by a commission of the Venezuelan Senate headed by Senator <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cristobal_Fernandez_Dalo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cristobal Fernandez Dalo (page does not exist)">Cristobal Fernandez Dalo</a> and his money laundering investigator, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thor_Halvorssen_Hellum" title="Thor Halvorssen Hellum">Thor Halvorssen Hellum</a>. They were arrested in September 1992 at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci-Fiumicino_Airport" title="Leonardo da Vinci-Fiumicino Airport">Fiumicino airport</a> (Rome),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fonzi_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fonzi-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> and in 1996 were sentence to 13–20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roth_73-2"><a href="#cite_note-roth-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Norte del Valle Cartel : In 2008 the leader of the Colombian <a href="/wiki/Norte_del_Valle_Cartel" title="Norte del Valle Cartel">Norte del Valle Cartel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilber_Varela" title="Wilber Varela">Wilber Varela</a>, was found murdered in a hotel in <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9rida_(state)" title="Mérida (state)">Mérida</a> in Venezuela.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> In 2010 Venezuela arrested and deported to the United States Jaime Alberto "Beto" Marin, then head of the Norte del Valle Cartel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VA200910_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-VA200910-77">[77]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Cartel_of_the_Suns" title="Cartel of the Suns">Cartel of the Suns</a> According to <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Diehl" title="Jackson Diehl">Jackson Diehl</a>. Deputy Editorial Page Editor of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>, the Bolivarian government of Venezuela shelters "one of the world’s biggest drug cartels". There have also been allegations that former president <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Hugo Chávez">Hugo Chávez</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diosdado_Cabello" title="Diosdado Cabello">Diosdado Cabello</a> being involved with drug trafficking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JDmay2015_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-JDmay2015-78">[78]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>In May 2015, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> reported from United States officials that drug trafficking in Venezuela increased significantly with Colombian drug traffickers moving from Colombia to Venezuela due to pressure from law enforcement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SUNwsj_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-SUNwsj-79">[79]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">United States Department of Justice</a> official described the higher ranks of the Venezuelan government and military as "a criminal organization", with high ranking Venezuelan officials, such as National Assembly President <a href="/wiki/Diosdado_Cabello" title="Diosdado Cabello">Diosdado Cabello</a>, being accused of drug trafficking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SUNwsj_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-SUNwsj-79">[79]</a></sup> Those involved with investigations stated that Venezuelan government defectors and former traffickers had given information to investigators and that details of those involved in government drug trafficking were increasing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SUNwsj_79-2"><a href="#cite_note-SUNwsj-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
