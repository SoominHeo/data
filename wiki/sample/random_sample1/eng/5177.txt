<p><b>Maestro</b> (stylized as <b>maestro</b>) is a multi-national <a href="/wiki/Debit_card" title="Debit card">debit card</a> service owned by <a href="/wiki/MasterCard" title="MasterCard">MasterCard</a> that was founded in 1992. Maestro cards are obtained from associate banks and can be linked to the card holder's current account, or they can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prepaid_cards" title="Prepaid cards">prepaid cards</a>. The cardholder presents the card at the <a href="/wiki/Point_of_sale" title="Point of sale">point of sale</a> (POS) and this is swiped through the <a href="/wiki/Payment_terminal" title="Payment terminal">payment terminal</a> by the assistant or the customer, inserted into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chip_and_PIN" title="Chip and PIN">chip and PIN</a> device or read by a contactless reader. The payment is authorized by the card issuer to ensure that the cardholder has sufficient funds in their account to make the purchase and the cardholder confirms the payment by either signing the sales receipt or entering their 4 to 6-digit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_Identification_Number" title="Personal Identification Number">PIN</a>, except with contactless transactions below a specified amount for which no further verification is required.</p>
<p>Maestro often requires on-line electronic authorization for every transaction, although MasterCard's rules permit the establishment of floor limits on Maestro EMV chip transactions only. Not only must the information stored in either the <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">chip</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_stripe" title="Magnetic stripe">magnetic stripe</a> be read, this has to be sent from the merchant to the issuing bank, the issuing bank then has to respond with an affirmative authorization. If the information is not read, the issuer will decline the transaction, regardless of any disposable amount on the connected account, except in the Asia Pacific region, where manual keyed entry is permitted under some circumstances. This is different from most other debit and credit cards, where the information can be entered manually into the terminal (i.e. by punching the 13 to 19 digits and the expiry date on the terminal) and still be approved by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Card_issuer" title="Card issuer">issuer</a> or stand-in processor. In most countries, other than those specified in MasterCard's rules, a PIN rather than a signature is always required to authorise a Maestro transaction, except where no CVM is required.</p>
<p>Maestro is accepted at around fifteen million point of sale outlets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acceptance_and_availability">Acceptance and availability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maestro_(debit_card)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Acceptance and availability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In Germany and Austria, Maestro replaced the <a href="/wiki/Eurocheque" title="Eurocheque">Eurocheque</a> system. Austrian Maestro cards are virtually always pure Maestro cards. German Maestro cards, however, are in most cases co-branded with the German <a href="/wiki/Girocard" title="Girocard">Girocard</a> logo. These co-branded cards work like normal Maestro cards within the Maestro network and as Girocards within the Girocard network, but they cannot be used as Maestro over the telephone or on the internet.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, Maestro cards are co-branded with the Belgian Bancontact logo (previously Bancontact/Mister Cash).</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the former <a href="/wiki/Switch_(debit_card)" title="Switch (debit card)">Switch</a> debit card system was re-branded as Maestro. Underneath the branding, however, the system was still the old Switch one and the cards were still fundamentally Switch. In 2011, MasterCard aligned UK Domestic Maestro (the former Switch) with the standard international Maestro proposition, ending its status as a separate card scheme. This change also led to the discontinuation of the <a href="/wiki/Solo_(debit_card)" title="Solo (debit card)">Solo (debit card)</a>. <a class="external autonumber" href="http://datacash.custhelp.com/cgi-bin/datacash.cfg/php/enduser/std_adp.php?p_faqid=1136&amp;p_created=1299160591" rel="nofollow">[2]</a> In January 2009 <a href="/wiki/First_Direct" title="First Direct">First Direct</a> and <a href="/wiki/HSBC_Bank_(Europe)" title="HSBC Bank (Europe)">HSBC</a> discontinued the use of Maestro card, issuing <a href="/wiki/Visa_Debit" title="Visa Debit">Visa Debit</a> cards to new customers and a gradual roll-out throughout 2009 to existing customers. In September of the same year, the British arms of the <a href="/wiki/National_Australia_Bank" title="National Australia Bank">National Australia Bank</a>, being the <a href="/wiki/Clydesdale_Bank" title="Clydesdale Bank">Clydesdale Bank</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Bank" title="Yorkshire Bank">Yorkshire Bank</a>, started the process of replacing the Maestro card with a <a href="/wiki/Debit_MasterCard" title="Debit MasterCard">Debit MasterCard</a> for their current accounts, except for the Readycash and Student accounts, for which the Maestro card continued to be issued until 2015. Likewise, in the same month the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Bank_of_Scotland" title="Royal Bank of Scotland">Royal Bank of Scotland Group</a> (Europe's largest debit card issuer which includes the <a href="/wiki/NatWest" title="NatWest">NatWest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coutts" title="Coutts">Coutts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Bank" title="Ulster Bank">Ulster Bank</a> brands) switched from Maestro to Visa Debit, a process that took two years to complete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This effectively meant that only a few smaller UK banks would be issuing Maestro cards. In 2015, Bank of Ireland UK replaced its Maestro debit cards with Visa Debit cards.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laser_(debit_card)" title="Laser (debit card)">Laser</a>, which was co-branded with Maestro, has been replaced by <a href="/wiki/Visa_Debit" title="Visa Debit">Visa Debit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Debit_MasterCard" title="Debit MasterCard">Debit MasterCard</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Laser_(debit_card)" title="Laser (debit card)">Laser</a> debit card has been phased out by all banks and ceased to operate from March 2014. Irish <a href="/wiki/Laser_(debit_card)" title="Laser (debit card)">Laser</a> cards carried Maestro co-branding from 2008 onwards. They were intended to be used with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chip_and_PIN" title="Chip and PIN">chip and PIN</a> POS systems. The chip on the card was programmed with two applications, one for <a href="/wiki/Laser_(debit_card)" title="Laser (debit card)">Laser</a> and one for Maestro. POS transactions were normally processed over the <a href="/wiki/Laser_(debit_card)" title="Laser (debit card)">Laser</a> network in Ireland and the Maestro network when the card was used abroad. Some POS terminals prompted users to manually select <a href="/wiki/Laser_(debit_card)" title="Laser (debit card)">Laser</a> or Maestro before completing the transaction. <a href="/wiki/Laser_(debit_card)" title="Laser (debit card)">Laser</a> cards could be processed as Maestro in most POS terminals worldwide for chip and PIN or swipe and sign transactions (where still accepted). Internet and telephone-based retailers, however, needed to be set up specifically to accept Irish <a href="/wiki/Laser_(debit_card)" title="Laser (debit card)">Laser</a>/Maestro cards. Transactions made with these cards were often secured by MasterCard's SecureCode system to verify the cardholder's identity. These cards were usually multi-functional and operated as a debit card as well as an ATM Card which could be used for accessing ATMs. Some banks also allowed customers to use their cards to deposit or withdraw money over the counter or at <a href="/wiki/An_Post" title="An Post">An Post</a> post offices using their debit card and PIN. Historically the cards often contained a <a href="/wiki/Cheque_guarantee_card" title="Cheque guarantee card">Cheque guarantee card</a> function indicated by a hologram. This scheme was shut down in 2011. Foreign-issued Maestro cards are still accepted in Ireland in ATMs and by many POS machines. However, acceptance of Visa and MasterCard debit/credit cards is more reliably universal at POS terminals.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, Maestro has been replaced by the banks that issued it with <a href="/wiki/Debit_MasterCard" title="Debit MasterCard">Debit MasterCard</a>. Moreover, the largest Danish bank <a href="/wiki/Danske_Bank" title="Danske Bank">Danske Bank</a> has replaced all of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cash_card" title="Cash card">cash cards</a> with Debit MasterCards.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, Maestro is a PIN-based debit card network closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_(interbank_network)" title="Cirrus (interbank network)">Cirrus</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automatic_teller_machine" title="Automatic teller machine">ATM</a> network, also owned by MasterCard. Like other PIN-debit networks in the U.S., Maestro there relies solely on a standard card and PIN, without a chip; signature-debit transactions in the U.S. are handled through the main MasterCard network or the rival <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visa_International_Service_Association" title="Visa International Service Association">Visa</a> network. RBS's former U.S. subsidiary, <a href="/wiki/Citizens_Financial_Group" title="Citizens Financial Group">Citizens Financial Group</a>, switched to Visa, though like most foreign banks with operations in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, it used MasterCard's <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_(interbank_network)" title="Cirrus (interbank network)">Cirrus</a> network and the card participated in the <a href="/wiki/MasterCard" title="MasterCard">MasterCard</a> SecureCode initiative.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, MasterCard acquired the existing Redeshop service in 2002 and rebranded it as Maestro.</li>
<li>In parts of <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>, Maestro was known as <a href="/wiki/MasterCard" title="MasterCard">MasterCard</a> <a href="/wiki/Maestro" title="Maestro">Maestro</a>, and used a different logo.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, Maestro is the card used by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banco_de_la_Nacion_Argentina" title="Banco de la Nacion Argentina">Banco de la Nacion Argentina</a> and other banks, mostly state-run or provincial banks.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, Maestro is extremely popular. It is as of 2014, the leading debit card, issued by almost all major banks in the country. It is widely accepted in POS. It works on all ATMs showing the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Suiche7B&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Suiche7B (page does not exist)">Suiche7B</a>, <a href="/wiki/MasterCard" title="MasterCard">MasterCard</a>, Conexus and <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_(interbank_network)" title="Cirrus (interbank network)">Cirrus</a> logos.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, Maestro is issued by most major banks, except <a href="/wiki/ICICI_Bank" title="ICICI Bank">ICICI Bank</a>. Maestro issuing banks include the <a href="/wiki/State_Bank_of_India" title="State Bank of India">State Bank of India</a> (India's largest bank), State Bank's affiliate banks, <a href="/wiki/Punjab_National_Bank" title="Punjab National Bank">Punjab National Bank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syndicate_Bank" title="Syndicate Bank">Syndicate Bank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Bank_of_Commerce" title="Oriental Bank of Commerce">Oriental Bank of Commerce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_Rajasthan" title="Bank of Rajasthan">Bank of Rajasthan</a>, etc.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, Maestro Cards cannot be used at point of sales locations to make purchases but the <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_(interbank_network)" title="Cirrus (interbank network)">Cirrus</a> network is accepted at a majority of cash points most of the time. The exception is the 'First International Bank of Israel' (FIBI) who do not accept <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_(interbank_network)" title="Cirrus (interbank network)">Cirrus</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_China" title="Bank of China">Bank of China</a> uses Maestro as its "international" debit card system. Also, certain Bank of China ATMs will present the user with Japanese or Korean language options upon insertion of a Maestro card.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, Maestro was a popular debit card (the third one after Visa Electron and Debit MasterCard), is still issued by 3 banks: BCR, BRD and Credit Agricole.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, Maestro is issued by banks including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sberbank" title="Sberbank">Sberbank</a>, which issues cards such as MasterCard Maestro Momentum and the Social MasterCard Maestro Debit Card.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, banks issue both Maestro and V PAY cards (the latter works only in Europe); both are usually co-branded with the national <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bancomat" title="Bancomat">Bancomat</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PagoBancomat" title="PagoBancomat">PagoBancomat</a> scheme.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, almost all banks issue Maestro debit cards, with only one bank offering <a href="/wiki/V_PAY" title="V PAY">V PAY</a> instead of Maestro cards. Prior to 2012, Dutch Maestro debit cards were co-branded with the national <a class="extiw" href="https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/PIN_(betaalkaart)" title="nl:PIN (betaalkaart)">PIN</a> scheme. This scheme has since been retired and replaced by Maestro and V-PAY.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, Maestro debit cards are issued by several banks in <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>. The main issuer was <a href="/wiki/Banca_Intesa_Beograd" title="Banca Intesa Beograd">Banca Intesa Beograd</a> until, in September 2012, <a href="/wiki/Banca_Intesa_Beograd" title="Banca Intesa Beograd">Banca Intesa Beograd</a> began to switch to Visa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, Maestro debit cards with EMV chips issued outside the Asia Pacific region, the Netherlands and Canada are temporarily unable to be used at ATMs in Japan due to an upgrade to the regional ATM network. In addition, Seven Bank announced in April 2013 that all MasterCard cards, including Maestro, would no longer be accepted at their ATMs due to a disagreement with MasterCard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, Maestro debit cards have been issued by several major banks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, as of March 2015 all four major Greek banks have replaced Maestro cards with contactless <a href="/wiki/Debit_MasterCard" title="Debit MasterCard">Debit MasterCard</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, Maestro debit cards are widely used, dual-branded with RedCompra, issued by the majority of banks (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santander_Group" title="Santander Group">Santander</a> issues only Maestro debit cards). They operate through the local Transbank and <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_(interbank_network)" title="Cirrus (interbank network)">Cirrus</a> network. Usually, Chilean Maestro cards have a small logo on the back of the card. <a href="/wiki/Debit_MasterCard" title="Debit MasterCard">Debit MasterCard</a> cards aren't issued in Chile. Chilean Maestro cards usually are contactless.</li>
</ul>
