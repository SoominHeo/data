<p><b>Maia</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Μαῖα</span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Maia</i></span>), in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_religion" title="Ancient Greek religion">ancient Greek religion</a>, is one of the <a href="/wiki/Pleiades_(Greek_mythology)" title="Pleiades (Greek mythology)">Pleiades</a> and the mother of <a href="/wiki/Hermes" title="Hermes">Hermes</a>.</p>
<p>Maia is the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Atlas_(mythology)" title="Atlas (mythology)">Atlas</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Pleione_(mythology)" title="Pleione (mythology)">Pleione</a> the <a href="/wiki/Oceanid" title="Oceanid">Oceanid</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and is the oldest of the seven Pleiades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> They were born on Mount <a href="/wiki/Mount_Kyllini" title="Mount Kyllini">Cyllene</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arcadia" title="Arcadia">Arcadia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and are sometimes called mountain <a href="/wiki/Nymph" title="Nymph">nymphs</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Oread" title="Oread">oreads</a></i>; <a href="/wiki/Simonides_of_Ceos" title="Simonides of Ceos">Simonides of Ceos</a> sang of "mountain Maia" <i>(Maiados oureias)</i> "of the lovely black eyes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Because they were daughters of Atlas, they were also called the Atlantides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mother_of_Hermes">Mother of Hermes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maia_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mother of Hermes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hermes_Maia_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen_2304.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2331" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Hermes_Maia_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen_2304.jpg/220px-Hermes_Maia_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen_2304.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Hermes_Maia_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen_2304.jpg/330px-Hermes_Maia_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen_2304.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Hermes_Maia_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen_2304.jpg/440px-Hermes_Maia_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen_2304.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hermes_Maia_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen_2304.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Hermes and Maia, detail from an <a href="/wiki/Red-figure_pottery" title="Red-figure pottery">Attic red-figure</a> <a href="/wiki/Amphora" title="Amphora">amphora</a> (<i>c.</i> 500 BC)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeric_hymn" title="Homeric hymn">Homeric <i>Hymn to Hermes</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> in the dead of night secretly begot <a href="/wiki/Hermes" title="Hermes">Hermes</a> upon Maia, who avoided the company of the gods, in a cave of Cyllene. After giving birth to the baby, Maia wrapped him in blankets and went to sleep. The rapidly maturing infant Hermes crawled away to <a href="/wiki/Thessaly" title="Thessaly">Thessaly</a>, where by night-fall of his first day he stole some of his half-brother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_(god)" title="Apollo (god)">Apollo</a>'s cattle and invented the <a href="/wiki/Lyre" title="Lyre">lyre</a> from a tortoise shell. Maia refused to believe Apollo when he claimed Hermes was the thief and Zeus then sided with Apollo. Finally, Apollo exchanged the cattle for the lyre, which became one of his identifying attributes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_nurturer">As nurturer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maia_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: As nurturer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Maia also raised the infant <a href="/wiki/Arcas" title="Arcas">Arcas</a>, the child of <a href="/wiki/Callisto_(mythology)" title="Callisto (mythology)">Callisto</a> with Zeus. Wronged by the love affair, Zeus' wife <a href="/wiki/Hera" title="Hera">Hera</a> in a jealous rage had transformed Callisto into a bear. Arcas is the <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">eponym</a> of <a href="/wiki/Arcadia" title="Arcadia">Arcadia</a>, where Maia was born.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The story of Callisto and Arcas, like that of the Pleiades, is an <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aition" title="Aition">aition</a></i> for a stellar formation, the constellations <a href="/wiki/Ursa_Major" title="Ursa Major">Ursa Major</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ursa_Minor" title="Ursa Minor">Ursa Minor</a>, the Great and Little Bear.</p>
<p>Her name is related to μαῖα (<i>maia</i>), an honorific term for older women related to μήτηρ (<i>mētēr</i>) 'mother'.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i>Maia</i> also means "<a href="/wiki/Midwife" title="Midwife">midwife</a>" in Greek.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Maia">Roman Maia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maia_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Roman Maia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cup_Mercury_Maia_CdM.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1660" data-file-width="1600" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Cup_Mercury_Maia_CdM.jpg/220px-Cup_Mercury_Maia_CdM.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Cup_Mercury_Maia_CdM.jpg/330px-Cup_Mercury_Maia_CdM.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Cup_Mercury_Maia_CdM.jpg/440px-Cup_Mercury_Maia_CdM.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cup_Mercury_Maia_CdM.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mercury and Maia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> inside a silver cup dedicated by the freedman P. Aelius Eutychus (late 2nd century AD), from a <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Roman_religion" title="Gallo-Roman religion">Gallo-Roman</a> religious site</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_ancient_Rome" title="Religion in ancient Rome">ancient Roman religion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">myth</a>, Maia embodied the concept of growth,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> as her name was thought to be related to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comparative_adjective" title="Comparative adjective">comparative adjective</a> <i>maius, maior</i>, "larger, greater." Originally, she may have been a <a href="/wiki/Homonym" title="Homonym">homonym</a> independent of the Greek Maia, whose myths she absorbed through the <a href="/wiki/Hellenization" title="Hellenization">Hellenization</a> of <a href="/wiki/Latin_literature" title="Latin literature">Latin literature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_ancient_Rome" title="Culture of ancient Rome">culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an archaic Roman prayer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Maia appears as an attribute of <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_(mythology)" title="Vulcan (mythology)">Vulcan</a>, in an <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_ancient_Roman_religion#invocatio" title="Glossary of ancient Roman religion">invocational</a> list of male deities paired with female abstractions representing some aspect of their functionality. She was explicitly identified with Earth <i>(<a href="/wiki/Terra_(mythology)" title="Terra (mythology)">Terra</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Interpretatio_graeca" title="Interpretatio graeca">Roman counterpart</a> of Gaia) and the Good Goddess <i>(<a href="/wiki/Bona_Dea" title="Bona Dea">Bona Dea</a>)</i> in at least one tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Her identity became theologically intertwined also with the goddesses <a href="/wiki/Faunus" title="Faunus">Fauna</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magna_Mater" title="Magna Mater">Magna Mater</a> ("Great Goddess", referring to the Roman form of Cybele but also a cult title for Maia), <a href="/wiki/Ops" title="Ops">Ops</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juno_(mythology)" title="Juno (mythology)">Juno</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cardea" title="Cardea">Carna</a>, as discussed at some length by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_antiquity" title="Late antiquity">late</a> <a href="/wiki/Antiquarian" title="Antiquarian">antiquarian</a> writer <a href="/wiki/Macrobius" title="Macrobius">Macrobius</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This treatment was probably influenced by the 1st-century BC scholar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Varro" title="Varro">Varro</a>, who tended to resolve a great number of goddesses into one original "Terra."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The association with Juno, whose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etruscan_religion" title="Etruscan religion">Etruscan</a> counterpart was <a href="/wiki/Uni_(mythology)" title="Uni (mythology)">Uni</a>, is suggested again by the inscription <i>Uni Mae</i> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piacenza_Liver" title="Piacenza Liver">Piacenza Liver</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The month of May (Latin <i>Maius</i>) was supposedly named for Maia, though ancient etymologists also connected it to the <i>maiores</i>, "ancestors," again from the adjective <i>maius, maior</i>, meaning those who are "greater" in terms of generational precedence. On the first day of May, the <a href="/wiki/Lares" title="Lares">Lares Praestites</a> were honored as <a href="/wiki/Tutelary_deity" title="Tutelary deity">protectors of the city</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Flamen" title="Flamen">flamen</a> of Vulcan sacrificed a pregnant sow to Maia, a customary offering to an earth goddess<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> that reiterates the link between Vulcan and Maia in the archaic prayer formula. In <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">Roman myth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(mythology)" title="Mercury (mythology)">Mercury</a> (Hermes), the son of Maia, was the father of the twin Lares, a genealogy that sheds light on the collocation of ceremonies on the May <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalends" title="Kalends">Kalends</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> On May 15, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ides_(calendar)" title="Ides (calendar)">Ides</a>, Mercury was honored as a patron of merchants and increaser of profit (through an etymological connection with <i>merx, merces</i>, "goods, merchandise"), another possible connection with Maia his mother as a goddess who promoted growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<li id="cite_note-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-2">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Hesiod, <i>Theogony</i> 938.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-3"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-3">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Pseudo-<a href="/wiki/Apollodorus_of_Athens" title="Apollodorus of Athens">Apollodorus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bibliotheca_(Pseudo-Apollodorus)" title="Bibliotheca (Pseudo-Apollodorus)">Pseudo-Apollodorus, <i>Bibliotheke</i></a> 3.10.1.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-4">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><i>Bibliotheke</i> 3.10.2; <a href="/wiki/Aratus" title="Aratus">Aratus</a>, <i>Phainomena</i> 255–263. Maia is the only one of the Pleiades named by these two sources to appear also in the rather idiosyncratic list given by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scholiast" title="Scholiast">Scholiast</a> to <a href="/wiki/Theocritus" title="Theocritus">Theocritus</a> (13.25), who says they were the daughters of the <a href="/wiki/Amazons" title="Amazons">Amazons</a>; see the note of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.G._Frazer" title="J.G. Frazer">J.G. Frazer</a> in his 1921 <a href="/wiki/Loeb_Classical_Library" title="Loeb Classical Library">Loeb Classical Library</a> edition and translation of what was then assumed to be the work of <a href="/wiki/Apollodorus_of_Athens" title="Apollodorus of Athens">Apollodorus of Athens</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Bibliotheca_(Pseudo-Apollodorus)" title="Bibliotheca (Pseudo-Apollodorus)">Bibliotheca</a></i>, vol. 2, p. 2.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-5"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-5">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Pseudo-<a href="/wiki/Apollodorus_of_Athens" title="Apollodorus of Athens">Apollodorus</a>, <i>Bibliotheke</i> 3.10.1.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-6"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-6">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="/wiki/Simonides_of_Ceos" title="Simonides of Ceos">Simonides</a>, Fragment 555.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-7"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-7">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a> 3.60.4.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-8"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-8">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><i>Bibliotheke</i> 3.101.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-9"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-9">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Vivian Nutton, <i>Ancient Medicine</i> (Routledge, 2004), p. 101.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-10"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-10">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Although the identification of Mercury is secure, based on the presence of the <a href="/wiki/Caduceus" title="Caduceus">caduceus</a>, the one-shouldered garment called the <i><a href="/wiki/Chlamys" title="Chlamys">chlamys</a></i>, and his winged head, the female figure has been identified variously. The cup is part of the <a href="/wiki/Berthouville_Treasure" title="Berthouville Treasure">Berthouville Treasure</a>, found within a <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Roman_religion" title="Gallo-Roman religion">Gallo-Roman</a> temple precinct; see Lise Vogel, <i>The Column of Antoninus Pius</i>, Loeb Classical Library Monograph (Harvard University Press, 1973), pp. 79–80, and Martin Henig, <i>Religion in Roman Britain</i>, (Taylor &amp; Francis, 1984, 2005), pp. 119–120. In <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, Mercury's regular consort is one of the Celtic goddesses, usually <a href="/wiki/Rosmerta" title="Rosmerta">Rosmerta</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Rosmerta#Etymology" title="Rosmerta">etymology of Rosmerta's name</a> as "Great Provider" suggests a theology compatible with that of Maia "the Great". The consort on the cup has also been identified as <a href="/wiki/Venus_(mythology)" title="Venus (mythology)">Venus</a> by M. Chabouillet, <i>Catalogue général et raisonné des camées et pierres gravées de la Bibliothéque Impériale</i> (Paris, 1858), p. 449. Maia is suggested by the concomitant discovery of a silver bust, not always considered part of the hoard proper but more securely identified as Maia and connected to Rosmerta; see E. Babelon, <i>Revue archéologique</i> 24 (1914) 182–190, as summarized in <i>American Journal of Archaeology</i> 19 (1915), p. 485.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-11"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-11">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Robert Turcan, <i>The Gods of Ancient Rome: Religion in Everyday Life from Archaic to Imperial Times</i> (Routledge, 2001), p. 70.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-12"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-12">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Pierre Grimal, <i>The Dictionary of Classical Mythology</i> (Blackwell, 1996, originally published in French 1951), p. 270.</span></li>

<li id="cite_note-15"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-15">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="/wiki/Macrobius" title="Macrobius">Macrobius</a>, <i>Saturnalia</i> 1.12.16–33.</span></li>

<li id="cite_note-17"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-17">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In Mario Torelli's diagram of this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haruspice" title="Haruspice">haruspicial</a> object, the names <i>Uni</i> and <i>Mae</i> appear together in a cell on the edge of the liver; see Nancy Thompson de Grummond, <i>Etruscan Myth, Sacred History, and Legend</i> (University of Pennsylvania Museum of Archaeology, 2006), p. 44 <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=TVAtdzbV-yIC&amp;dq=piacenza+liver+uni+etruscan&amp;q=%22uni%2Fmae%3B5%22#v=snippet&amp;q=%22uni%2Fmae%3B5%22&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">online.</a></span></li>
