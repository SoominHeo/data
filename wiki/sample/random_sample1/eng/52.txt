<p><b>Ellipsis</b> (plural <b>ellipses</b>; from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἔλλειψις</span>, <i>élleipsis</i>, "omission" or "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ellipsis" title="wikt:ellipsis">falling short</a>") is a series of dots (typically three, such as "…") that usually indicates an intentional omission of a word, sentence, or whole section from a text without altering its original meaning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellipsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Depending on their context and placement in a sentence, ellipses can indicate an unfinished thought, a leading statement, a slight <a href="/wiki/Pausa" title="Pausa">pause</a>, an echoing voice, or a nervous or awkward silence. <a href="/wiki/Aposiopesis" title="Aposiopesis">Aposiopesis</a> is the use of an ellipsis to trail off into silence—for example: "But I thought he was <span class="nowrap">. . ."</span> When placed at the beginning or end of a sentence, the ellipsis can also inspire a feeling of melancholy or longing.</p>
<p>The most common form of an ellipsis is a row of three periods or full stops (<b>. . .</b>) or a precomposed triple-dot glyph (<b>…</b>). The usage of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Em_dash" title="Em dash">em dash</a> (—) can overlap the usage of the ellipsis, especially in dialogue. Style guides often have their own rules governing the use of ellipses. For example, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chicago_Manual_of_Style" title="The Chicago Manual of Style">The Chicago Manual of Style</a></i> recommends that an ellipsis be formed by typing three periods, each with a space on both sides.</p>
<p>The triple-dot punctuation mark is also called a <b>suspension point</b>, <b>points of ellipsis</b>, <b>periods of ellipsis</b>, or <a href="/wiki/Colloquialism" title="Colloquialism">colloquially</a>, "<b>dot-dot-dot</b>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_writing">In writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellipsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In her book on the ellipsis, <i>Ellipsis in English Literature: Signs of Omission</i> (Cambridge University Press, 2015), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anne_Toner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anne Toner (page does not exist)">Anne Toner</a> suggests that the first use of the punctuation in the English language dates to a 1588 translation of <a href="/wiki/Terence" title="Terence">Terence</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Andria_(comedy)" title="Andria (comedy)">Andria</a></i>, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maurice_Kyffin" title="Maurice Kyffin">Maurice Kyffin</a>. In this case, however, the ellipsis consists not of dots but of short dashes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> "Subpuncting" of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> manuscripts also denotes omitted meaning and may be related.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 19th and early 20th centuries, an ellipsis was often used when a writer intentionally omitted a specific proper noun, such as a location: "Jan was born on . . . Street in Warsaw."</p>
<p>As commonly used, this juxtaposition of characters is referred to as "dots of ellipsis" in the English language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HumezHumez2008_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-HumezHumez2008-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Occasionally, it would be used in pulp fiction and other works of early 20th-century fiction to denote expletives that would otherwise have been censored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>An ellipsis may also imply an unstated alternative indicated by context. For example, when Sue says "I never drink wine . . . ", the implication is that she does drink something else—such as vodka.</p>
<p>In reported speech, the ellipsis can be used to represent an intentional silence.</p>
<p>In poetry, an ellipsis is used as a thought-pause or <a href="/wiki/Line_break_(poetry)" title="Line break (poetry)">line break</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Caesura" title="Caesura">caesura</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> or this is used to highlight sarcasm or make the reader think about the last points in the poem.</p>
<p>In news reporting, often associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brackets" title="Brackets">brackets</a>, it is used to indicate that a quotation has been condensed for space, brevity or relevance.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Herb_Caen" title="Herb Caen">Herb Caen</a>, Pulitzer-prize-winning columnist for the San Francisco Chronicle, became famous for his "three-dot journalism".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_different_languages">In different languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellipsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In different languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_American_English">In American English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellipsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In American English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ambox_globe_content.svg"><img alt="Ambox globe content.svg" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="350" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/48px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/73px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/97px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png 2x" width="48"/></a></div>
</td>
<td class="mbox-text"><span class="mbox-text-span">The examples and perspective in this section <b>may not represent a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:WikiProject_Countering_systemic_bias" title="Wikipedia:WikiProject Countering systemic bias">worldwide view</a> of the subject</b>. <p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Chicago_Manual_of_Style" title="The Chicago Manual of Style">The Chicago Manual of Style</a></i> suggests the use of an ellipsis for any omitted word, phrase, line, or paragraph from within but not at the end of a quoted passage. There are two commonly used methods of using ellipses: one uses three dots for any omission, while the second one makes a distinction between omissions within a sentence (using three dots: . . .) and omissions between sentences (using a period and a space followed by three dots: . ...). An ellipsis at the end of a sentence with a sentence following should be preceded by a period (for a total of four dots).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Modern_Language_Association" title="Modern Language Association">Modern Language Association</a> (MLA), however, used to indicate that an ellipsis must include spaces before and after each dot in all uses. If an ellipsis is meant to represent an omission, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_bracket" title="Square bracket">square brackets</a> must surround the ellipsis to make it clear that there was no pause in the original quote: [ . . . ]. Currently, the MLA has removed the requirement of brackets in its style handbooks. However, some maintain that the use of brackets is still correct because it clears confusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Modern_Language_Association" title="Modern Language Association">MLA</a> now indicates that a three-dot, spaced ellipsis ( … ) should be used for removing material from within one sentence within a quote. When crossing sentences (when the omitted text contains a period, so that omitting the end of a sentence counts), a four-dot, spaced (except for before the first dot) ellipsis (. . . . ) should be used. When ellipsis points are used in the original text, ellipsis points that are not in the original text should be distinguished by enclosing them in square brackets (e.g. "text […] text").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the Associated Press, the ellipsis should be used to condense quotations. It is less commonly used to indicate a pause in speech or an unfinished thought or to separate items in material such as show business gossip. The stylebook indicates that if the shortened sentence before the mark can stand as a sentence, it should do so, with an ellipsis placed after the period or other ending punctuation. When material is omitted at the end of a paragraph and also immediately following it, an ellipsis goes both at the end of that paragraph and in front of the beginning of the next, according to this style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Robert Bringhurst's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elements_of_Typographic_Style" title="Elements of Typographic Style">Elements of Typographic Style</a></i>, the details of typesetting ellipses depend on the character and size of the font being set and the typographer's preference. Bringhurst writes that a full space between each dot is "another Victorian eccentricity. In most contexts, the Chicago ellipsis is much too wide"—he recommends using flush dots, or <a href="/wiki/Thin_space" title="Thin space"><i>thin</i>-spaced</a> dots (up to one-fifth of an <a href="/wiki/Em_(typography)" title="Em (typography)">em</a>), or the prefabricated ellipsis character (<a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> U+2026, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_entity" title="Latin entity">Latin entity</a> <code>&amp;hellip;</code>). Bringhurst suggests that normally an ellipsis should be spaced fore-and-aft to separate it from the text, but when it combines with other punctuation, the leading space disappears and the other punctuation follows. This is the usual practice in typesetting. He provides the following examples:</p>
<table style="margin-left:3em;" summary="six examples of setting ellipsis">
<tr>
<td style="width:5em;">i … j</td>
<td style="width:5em;">k….</td>
<td style="width:5em;">l…, l</td>
<td style="width:5em;">l, … l</td>
<td style="width:5em;">m…?</td>
<td style="width:5em;">n…!</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>In legal writing in the United States, Rule 5.3 in the <i><a href="/wiki/Bluebook" title="Bluebook">Bluebook</a></i> citation guide governs the use of ellipses and requires a space before the first dot and between the two subsequent dots. If an ellipsis ends the sentence, then there are three dots, each separated by a space, followed by the final punctuation (e.g. Hah . . . ?). In some legal writing, an ellipsis is written as three <a href="/wiki/Asterisk" title="Asterisk">asterisks</a> (*** or * * *) to make it obvious that text has been omitted. (...) is also used for awkward silence.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Polish">In Polish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellipsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In Polish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When applied in <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish language</a> syntax, the ellipsis is called <span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl"><i>wielokropek</i></span>, which means "multidot". The word <i>wielokropek</i> distinguishes the ellipsis of Polish syntax from that of <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_notation" title="Mathematical notation">mathematical notation</a>, in which it is known as an <span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl"><i>elipsa</i></span>.</p>
<p>When an ellipsis replaces a fragment omitted from a quotation, the ellipsis is enclosed in parentheses or square brackets. An unbracketed ellipsis indicates an interruption or pause in speech.</p>
<p>The syntactical rules for ellipses are standardized by the 1983 Polska Norma document <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=PN-83/P-55366&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="PN-83/P-55366 (page does not exist)">PN-83/P-55366</a>, <span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl"><i>Zasady składania tekstów w języku polskim</i></span> ("Rules for setting texts in the Polish Language").</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Russian">In Russian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellipsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In Russian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The combination "ellipsis+period" is replaced by the ellipsis. The combinations "ellipsis+exclamation mark" and "ellipsis+question mark" are written in this way: !.. ?..</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Japanese">In Japanese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellipsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In Japanese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most common character corresponding to an ellipsis is called <i>3</i>-ten rīdā ("<i>3</i>-dot leaders", <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">…</span>). 2-ten rīdā exists as a character, but it is used less commonly. In writing, the ellipsis consists usually of six dots (two <i>3</i>-ten rīdā characters, <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">……</span>). Three dots (one <i>3</i>-ten rīdā character) may be used where space is limited, such as in a header. However, variations in the number of dots exist. In horizontally written text the dots are commonly vertically centered within the text height (between the <a href="/wiki/Baseline_(typography)" title="Baseline (typography)">baseline</a> and the ascent line), as in the standard Japanese <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a> fonts; in vertically written text the dots are always centered horizontally. <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">As the Japanese word for dot is pronounced "<span lang="ja-Latn" xml:lang="ja-Latn">ten</span>", the dots are colloquially called "<span lang="ja-Latn" xml:lang="ja-Latn">ten-ten-ten</span>" (<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">てんてんてん</span>, akin to the English "dot dot dot").</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a>, the ellipsis by itself represents speechlessness, or a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pregnant_pause" title="Pregnant pause">pregnant pause</a>". Depending on the context, this could be anything from an admission of guilt to an expression of being dumbfounded at another person's words or actions. As a device, the <i>ten-ten-ten</i> is intended to focus the reader on a character while allowing the character to not speak any dialogue. This conveys to the reader a focus of the narrative "camera" on the silent subject, implying an expectation of some motion or action. It is not unheard of to see inanimate objects "speaking" the ellipsis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Chinese">In Chinese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellipsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In Chinese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, the ellipsis is six dots (in two groups of three dots, occupying the same horizontal or vertical space as two characters) (i.e. <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">……</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Spanish">In Spanish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellipsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In Spanish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, ellipsis is commonly used as a substitute of <i><a href="/wiki/Et_cetera" title="Et cetera">et cetera</a></i> at the end of unfinished lists. So it means "and so forth" or "and other things".</p>
<p>Other use is the suspension of a part of a text, or a paragraph, or a phrase or a part of a word because it is obvious, or unnecessary, or implied. For instance, sometimes the ellipsis is used to avoid the complete use of expletives.</p>
<p>When the ellipse is placed alone into a <a href="/wiki/Bracket" title="Bracket">parenthesis</a> (...) or—less often—between <a href="/wiki/Bracket" title="Bracket">brackets</a> […], which is what happens usually within a text <a href="/wiki/Transcription_(linguistics)" title="Transcription (linguistics)">transcription</a>, it means the original text had more contents on the same position but are not useful to our target in the transcription. When the suppressed text is at the beginning or at the end of a text, the ellipse does not need to be placed in a parenthesis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_French">In French</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellipsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In French">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, the ellipsis is commonly used at the end of lists to represent <i>et cetera</i>. In French typography, the ellipsis is written close up to the preceding word but has a space after it. If, exceptionally, it begins a sentence, there is a space before and after.</p>
<p>However, any omitted word, phrase or line at the end of a quoted passage would be indicated like this: [...] (space before and after the square brackets but not inside).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_German">In German</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellipsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In German">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, the ellipsis in general is surrounded by spaces, if it stands for one or more omitted words. On the other side there is no space between a letter or (part of) a word and an ellipsis, if it stands for one or more omitted letters, that should stick to the written letter or letters.</p>
<p>Example for both cases, using German style: <i>The first el...is stands for omitted letters, the second ... for an omitted word.</i></p>
<p>If the ellipsis is at the end of a sentence (or between two sentences), the final full stop is omitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Example: <i>I think that ...</i></p>

<p>The use of ellipses in mathematical proofs is often discouraged because of the potential for ambiguity. For this reason, and because the ellipsis supports no systematic rules for symbolic calculation, in recent years some authors have recommended avoiding its use in mathematics altogether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Programming_languages">Programming languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellipsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Programming languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ellipsis is used as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operator_(programming)" title="Operator (programming)">operator</a> in some <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a>. The precise meaning varies by language, but it generally involves something dealing with multiple items. One of its most common uses is in defining <a href="/wiki/Variadic_function" title="Variadic function">variadic functions</a> which can take an unknown number of arguments in the C, C++ and Java languages. <i>See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ellipsis_(programming_operator)" title="Ellipsis (programming operator)">Ellipsis (programming operator)</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="HTML_and_CSS">HTML and CSS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellipsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: HTML and CSS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The CSS <code>text-overflow</code> property can be set to <code>ellipsis</code>, which cuts off text with an ellipsis when it overflows the content area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="On_the_Internet_and_in_text_messaging">On the Internet and in text messaging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellipsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: On the Internet and in text messaging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ellipsis is a non-verbal cue that is often used in computer-mediated interactions, in particular in synchronous genres, such as chat. The reason behind its popularity is the fact that it allows people to indicate in writing several functions:</p>
<ul>
<li>The sign of ellipsis can function as a floor holding device, and signal that more is to come, for instance when people break up longer turns in chat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>Dot-dot-dot can be used systematically to enact linguistic politeness, for instance indicating topic change or hesitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>Suspension dots can be <a href="/wiki/Turn_construction_unit" title="Turn construction unit">turn construction units</a> to signal silence, for example when indicating disagreement, disapproval or confusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Although an ellipsis is technically complete with three periods (...), its rise in popularity as a "trailing-off" or "silence" indicator, particularly in mid-20th-century comic strip and comic book prose writing, has led to expanded uses online. Today, extended ellipsis anywhere from two to dozens of periods have become common constructions in Internet chat rooms and text messages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dots_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-dots-20">[20]</a></sup> The extent of repetition in itself might serve as an additional contextualization or paralinguistic cue, to "extend the lexical meaning of the words, add character to the sentences, and allow fine-tuning and personalisation of the message"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> and sometimes in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>, ellipsis characters are made by entering two consecutive <i>horizontal ellipsis</i> (U+2026).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2011)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In vertical texts, the application should rotate the symbol accordingly.</p>
<p>Unicode recognizes a series of three <a href="/wiki/Full_stop" title="Full stop">period</a> characters (U+002E) as <a href="/wiki/Unicode_equivalence" title="Unicode equivalence">compatibility equivalent (though not canonical)</a> to the horizontal ellipsis character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">HTML</a>, the horizontal ellipsis character may be represented by the entity reference <code>&amp;hellip;</code> (since HTML 4.0), and the vertical ellipsis character by the entity reference <code>&amp;vellip;</code> (since HTML 5.0).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Alternatively, in HTML, <a href="/wiki/XML" title="XML">XML</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SGML" title="SGML">SGML</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Numeric_character_reference" title="Numeric character reference">numeric character reference</a> such as <code>&amp;#x2026;</code> or <code>&amp;#8230;</code> can be used.</p>

<p>The horizontal ellipsis character also appears in the following older character maps:</p>
<ul>
<li>in <a href="/wiki/Windows_code_page" title="Windows code page">Windows-1250—Windows-1258</a> and in IBM/MS-DOS <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Code_page_874" title="Code page 874">Code page 874</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Code_point" title="Code point">code</a> 85 (hexadecimal)</li>
<li>in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mac-Roman_encoding" title="Mac-Roman encoding">Mac-Roman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macintosh_Central_European_encoding" title="Macintosh Central European encoding">Mac-CentEuro</a> and several other Macintosh encodings, at code C9 (hexadecimal)</li>
<li>in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ventura_International_encoding&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ventura International encoding (page does not exist)">Ventura International encoding</a> at code C1 (hexadecimal)</li>
</ul>
<p>Note that <a href="/wiki/ISO/IEC_8859" title="ISO/IEC 8859">ISO/IEC 8859</a> encoding series provides no code point for ellipsis.</p>
<p>As with all characters, especially those outside the <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a> range, the author, sender and receiver of an encoded ellipsis must be in agreement upon what bytes are being used to represent the character. Naive text processing software may improperly assume that a particular encoding is being used, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Mojibake" title="Mojibake">mojibake</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/The_Chicago_Manual_of_Style" title="The Chicago Manual of Style">Chicago Style</a> Q&amp;A recommends to avoid the use of <b>…</b> (U+2026) character in manuscripts and to place three periods plus two nonbreaking spaces (. . .) instead, so that an editor, publisher, or designer can replace them later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Abstract_Syntax_Notation_One" title="Abstract Syntax Notation One">Abstract Syntax Notation One</a> (ASN.1), the ellipsis is used as an extension marker to indicate the possibility of type extensions in future revisions of a protocol specification. In a type constraint expression like A ::= INTEGER (0..127, ..., 256..511) an ellipsis is used to separate the extension root from extension additions. The definition of type A in version 1 system of the form A ::= INTEGER (0..127, ...) and the definition of type A in version 2 system of the form A ::= INTEGER (0..127, ..., 256..511) constitute an extension series of the same type A in different versions of the same specification. The ellipsis can also be used in compound type definitions to separate the set of fields belonging to the extension root from the set of fields constituting extension additions. Here is an example: B ::= SEQUENCE { a INTEGER, b INTEGER, ..., c INTEGER }</p>
