<p>The <b>2019 FIFA Women's World Cup</b> will be the 8th edition of the <a href="/wiki/FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup" title="FIFA Women's World Cup">FIFA Women's World Cup</a>, the quadrennial international <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_association_football" title="Women's association football">women's</a> <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> championship contested by the <a href="/wiki/List_of_women%27s_national_association_football_teams" title="List of women's national association football teams">national teams</a> of the member associations of <a href="/wiki/FIFA" title="FIFA">FIFA</a>. In March 2015, France won the right to host the event, the first time the country would host the tournament and the third time in Europe. Matches are planned for eleven cities across France. The current format of the tournament is to be among 24 national teams, including that of the host nation. The defending champions are the <a href="/wiki/United_States_women%27s_national_soccer_team" title="United States women's national soccer team">United States</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Host_selection">Host selection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2019_FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Host selection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 6 March 2014, <a href="/wiki/FIFA" title="FIFA">FIFA</a> announced that bidding had begun for the 2019 FIFA Women's World Cup. Member associations interested in hosting the tournament had to submit a declaration of interest by 15 April 2014, and provide the complete set of bidding documents by 31 October 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In principle, FIFA prefer the 2019 FIFA Women's World Cup and the <a href="/wiki/2018_FIFA_U-20_Women%27s_World_Cup" title="2018 FIFA U-20 Women's World Cup">2018 FIFA U-20 Women's World Cup</a> to be hosted by the same member association, but if circumstances require, FIFA reserves the right to award the hosting of the events separately.</p>
<p>Initially, five countries indicated interest in hosting the events: <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">Korea Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>. However, the number of bidding nations was narrowed down to two in October 2014, when the <a href="/wiki/French_Football_Federation" title="French Football Federation">French Football Federation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korea_Football_Association" title="Korea Football Association">Korea Football Association</a> submitted their official bid documents to FIFA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Both <a href="/wiki/The_Football_Association" title="The Football Association">The Football Association</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Football" title="New Zealand Football">New Zealand Football</a> registered expressions of interest by the April 2014 deadline,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> but in June 2014 it was announced that each would no longer proceed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-England.27s_FA_to_no_longer_bid_for_2019_event_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-England.27s_FA_to_no_longer_bid_for_2019_event-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/South_African_Football_Association" title="South African Football Association">South African Football Association</a> registered an expression of interest by the April 2014 deadline,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> however later decided to withdraw prior to the final October deadline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Both <a href="/wiki/Japan_Football_Association" title="Japan Football Association">Japan Football Association</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Football_Association" title="Swedish Football Association">Swedish Football Association</a> had also expressed interest in bidding for the 2019 tournament, however Japan chose to focus on the <a href="/wiki/2019_Rugby_World_Cup" title="2019 Rugby World Cup">2019 Rugby World Cup</a> and the <a href="/wiki/2020_Summer_Olympics" title="2020 Summer Olympics">2020 Summer Olympics</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> whilst Sweden decided to focus on European U-17 competitions instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The following countries made official bids for hosting the tournament by submitting their documents by 31 October 2014:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><span class="flagicon"><a href="/wiki/France" title="France"><img alt="France" class="thumbborder" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="900" height="15" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c3/Flag_of_France.svg/23px-Flag_of_France.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c3/Flag_of_France.svg/35px-Flag_of_France.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c3/Flag_of_France.svg/45px-Flag_of_France.svg.png 2x" width="23"/></a></span> <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li><span class="flagicon"><a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea"><img alt="South Korea" class="thumbborder" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="900" height="15" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Flag_of_South_Korea.svg/23px-Flag_of_South_Korea.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Flag_of_South_Korea.svg/35px-Flag_of_South_Korea.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Flag_of_South_Korea.svg/45px-Flag_of_South_Korea.svg.png 2x" width="23"/></a></span> <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">Korea Republic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>On 19 March 2015, France officially won the bid to host the Women's World Cup and the U-20 Women's World Cup. The decision came after a vote by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIFA_Executive_Committee" title="FIFA Executive Committee">FIFA Executive Committee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Qualification">Qualification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2019_FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Qualification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2019_FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup_qualification" title="2019 FIFA Women's World Cup qualification">2019 FIFA Women's World Cup qualification</a></div>
<p>The slot allocation was approved by the FIFA Council on 13–14 October 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The slots for each confederation are unchanged from <a href="/wiki/2015_FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup_qualification" title="2015 FIFA Women's World Cup qualification">that of the previous tournament</a> except the slot for the hosts is moved from CONCACAF (Canada) to UEFA (France).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Qualifying matches are expected to take place in 2017 and 2018.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Venues">Venues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2019_FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Venues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The opening match and final will be played at <a href="/wiki/Parc_Olympique_Lyonnais" title="Parc Olympique Lyonnais">Parc Olympique Lyonnais</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a> suburb of <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9cines-Charpieu" title="Décines-Charpieu">Décines</a>, with 58,000 capacity. <a href="/wiki/Roazhon_Park" title="Roazhon Park">Roazhon Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rennes" title="Rennes">Rennes</a> will host six matches—four in the group stage, one in the round of 16, and one semifinal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Ten other cities are candidates, with eight of them to be chosen to host matches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two of the stadiums were used at the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2016" title="UEFA Euro 2016">UEFA Euro 2016</a>: Parc Olympique in Lyon and Allianz Riviera in Nice. Another two stadiums were used at both the <a href="/wiki/1998_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1998 FIFA World Cup">1998 FIFA World Cup</a> and <a href="/wiki/2007_Rugby_World_Cup" title="2007 Rugby World Cup">2007 Rugby World Cup</a>: Stade de la Mosson in Montpellier and Stade de la Beaujoire in Nantes. The other stadiums seat under 30,000 spectators.</p>
