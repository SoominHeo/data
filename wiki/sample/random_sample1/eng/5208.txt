<p><b>Michael Cole Mussina</b> (born December 8, 1968), nicknamed <b>Moose</b>, is a former <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> right-handed <a href="/wiki/Starting_pitcher" title="Starting pitcher">starting pitcher</a>. Mussina played for the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a> (1991–2000) and the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> (2001–2008).</p>
<p>Mussina spent his entire career in the competitive and high-scoring <a href="/wiki/American_League_East" title="American League East">American League East</a>, won at least 11 games in 17 consecutive seasons – an American League record – and recorded a career .638 <a href="/wiki/Winning_percentage" title="Winning percentage">winning percentage</a>. Among <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitchers</a>, he ranks 33rd in all-time wins (270),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> 33rd in games started (535), 66th in innings pitched (3,562.2), and 19th in <a href="/wiki/Strikeout" title="Strikeout">strikeouts</a> (2,813). A five-time <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All-Star</a> and seven-time <a href="/wiki/Rawlings_Gold_Glove_Award" title="Rawlings Gold Glove Award">Gold Glove</a> winner, Mussina's consistency resulted in six top-five finishes in the voting for his league's <a href="/wiki/Cy_Young_Award" title="Cy Young Award">Cy Young Award</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mike_Mussina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mussina was born in <a href="/wiki/Williamsport,_Pennsylvania" title="Williamsport, Pennsylvania">Williamsport, Pennsylvania</a>. At Montoursville Area High School in <a href="/wiki/Montoursville,_Pennsylvania" title="Montoursville, Pennsylvania">Montoursville, Pennsylvania</a> he had a 24–4 <a href="/wiki/Win%E2%80%93loss_record_(pitching)" title="Win–loss record (pitching)">win–loss record</a> with a 0.87 <a href="/wiki/Earned_run_average" title="Earned run average">earned run average</a> (ERA) for the school's baseball team. He also excelled in football and basketball.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As a high school senior, Mussina just missed being the <a href="/wiki/Valedictorian" title="Valedictorian">valedictorian</a> of his graduating class. According to some reports, he intentionally came up short to avoid delivering a commencement speech.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mussina was drafted by the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a> in 1987 but chose to attend college rather than sign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="College_career">College career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mike_Mussina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: College career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mussina enrolled at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>, where he played <a href="/wiki/College_baseball" title="College baseball">college baseball</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Stanford_Cardinal_baseball" title="Stanford Cardinal baseball">Stanford Cardinal baseball</a> team. In three years with the Cardinal, Mussina compiled a 31–16 record with a 3.89 ERA. He made two <a href="/wiki/College_World_Series" title="College World Series">College World Series</a> appearances and was selected as an All-American. His junior year in 1990 was his best, finishing 14–5 with a 0.99 ERA before being drafted again by the Baltimore Orioles, this time as a first round pick (20th overall).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Mussina graduated from Stanford in 1990 with a degree in <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>. He is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Delta_Tau_Delta" title="Delta Tau Delta">Delta Tau Delta</a> fraternity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_career">Professional career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mike_Mussina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Professional career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Baltimore_Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mike_Mussina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Baltimore Orioles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mussina made his professional debut with the Class AA <a href="/wiki/Hagerstown_Suns" title="Hagerstown Suns">Hagerstown Suns</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_League_(baseball)" title="Eastern League (baseball)">Eastern League</a> in 1990. In the minor leagues, Mussina posted a 2.38 ERA in 189 innings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1992.E2.80.931995">1992–1995</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mike_Mussina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1992–1995">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1992, Mussina's first full season with the Orioles, he finished with an 18–5 record and a 2.54 ERA in 241 innings. His .783 win-loss percentage led the league, and his 1.79 <a href="/wiki/Bases_on_balls_per_9_innings_pitched" title="Bases on balls per 9 innings pitched">BB/9</a> was second best behind <a href="/wiki/Chris_Bosio" title="Chris Bosio">Chris Bosio</a>. His 4 shutouts were tied for 2nd in the league behind only <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston's</a> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Clemens" title="Roger Clemens">Roger Clemens</a>. He finished 4th in the American League <a href="/wiki/Cy_Young_Award" title="Cy Young Award">Cy Young Award</a> voting, and pitched one perfect inning in the <a href="/wiki/1992_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="1992 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">1992 All-Star Game</a>.</p>
<p>Mussina struggled in 1993 due to shoulder soreness, which placed him on the <a href="/wiki/Disabled_list" title="Disabled list">disabled list</a> (DL) from July 22 to August 19. Nonetheless, he managed to win 14 games while posting the 7th best winning percentage in the American League. Mussina also allowed 83 earned runs in only 167.2 innings of work for a 4.46 ERA while striking out 117 batters. He was voted onto the <a href="/wiki/1993_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="1993 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All-Star team</a>, however he did not pitch in the game. There was a controversial incident toward the end of the game when Mussina chose to warm up in the bullpen, despite the fact AL manager <a href="/wiki/Cito_Gaston" title="Cito Gaston">Cito Gaston</a> had told him that he would not enter the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-villagevoice.com_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-villagevoice.com-6">[6]</a></sup> Orioles fans believed Mussina was warming up in preparation to come in and pitch the ninth inning, and when Gaston put <a href="/wiki/Duane_Ward" title="Duane Ward">Duane Ward</a> in to pitch the ninth inning, the fans at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camden_Yards" title="Camden Yards">Camden Yards</a> spent the rest of the game chanting "We Want Mike" and booing Gaston very loudly, as the popular slogan "Cito Sucks" was born in Baltimore. The slogan could be seen on T-shirts or heard even years later in Baltimore any time the visiting Blue Jays came to town. Gaston was never treated well by Baltimore fans for the rest of his managerial career and he was subject to death threats for not pitching Mussina in the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Many believe Mussina threw on his own as a way of publicly showing up Gaston because he was angry at not pitching in the game. However, Mussina said he was just getting his work in, as he was scheduled to throw that day, and it was apparent Gaston did require his services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-villagevoice.com_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-villagevoice.com-6">[6]</a></sup> Mussina returned from the DL in August against the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Rangers_(baseball)" title="Texas Rangers (baseball)">Texas Rangers</a>, only to have the Orioles shut him down three weeks later in mid-September due to lower back pain.</p>
<p>Mussina returned to form in 1994, but a <a href="/wiki/1994%E2%80%9395_Major_League_Baseball_strike" title="1994–95 Major League Baseball strike">player's strike</a> cut his season short, causing him to finish with only 16 wins and 99 strikeouts in 176.1 innings of work. Mussina finished tied for 2nd in the league in wins, and his 3.06 ERA placed him 4th. He was selected to his 3rd consecutive <a href="/wiki/1994_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="1994 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All-Star Game</a> and pitched one inning, giving up one hit while striking out one batter. Mussina finished 4th in voting for that year's American League Cy Young Award.</p>
<p>In 1995, Mussina started and won <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cal_Ripken" title="Cal Ripken">Cal Ripken</a>'s record-breaking 2,131st <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_consecutive_games_played_streaks" title="Major League Baseball consecutive games played streaks">consecutive game</a> on September 6, 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ripken_boxscore_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ripken_boxscore-8">[8]</a></sup> Mussina led the league with 19 wins and had one of his finest statistical seasons. He struck out 158 batters in 221.2 innings, allowing only 81 earned runs for an ERA of 3.29. Mussina led the league with four shutouts, and he also allowed a league-low 2.03 BB/9, while his 1.069 <a href="/wiki/Walks_plus_hits_per_inning_pitched" title="Walks plus hits per inning pitched">WHIP</a> was 2nd only to <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Mariners" title="Seattle Mariners">Seattle's</a> <a href="/wiki/Randy_Johnson" title="Randy Johnson">Randy Johnson</a>. Despite his excellent season, Mussina was not elected to that year's <a href="/wiki/1995_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="1995 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All-Star Game</a>, and finished 5th in the American League Cy Young Award voting.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1996.E2.80.932000">1996–2000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mike_Mussina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1996–2000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1996, Mussina won 19 games and set a new career high of 243.1 innings. His league-leading 36 games started were also a career high. 18 of his starts that year were <a href="/wiki/Quality_start" title="Quality start">quality starts</a>. In his last start of the season, the Orioles bullpen blew a late-inning lead, costing Mussina a 20-win season. Mussina also won his 1st <a href="/wiki/Rawlings_Gold_Glove_Award" title="Rawlings Gold Glove Award">Gold Glove</a> that year.</p>
<p>Mussina did not start the <a href="/wiki/Opening_Day" title="Opening Day">Opening Day</a> game in 1997 due to elbow tendonitis. He had been the Opening Day starter for the Orioles every year since 1993. Mussina finished the season with a 15–8 record, and his 3.20 ERA was 4th best in the league. In addition, his 218 strikeouts were a career high and established a franchise record. Mussina was again selected for the <a href="/wiki/1997_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="1997 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All-Star team</a> but did not appear in the game. He finished 6th in the American League Cy Young Award voting and won his 2nd consecutive Gold Glove. In the <a href="/wiki/1997_American_League_Championship_Series" title="1997 American League Championship Series">1997 American League Championship Series</a> he pitched fifteen innings over two starts, allowing one run and four hits, and striking out twenty five—an ALCS record at the time. However, the Orioles failed to score in both of his starts, and Mussina ended up receiving no-decisions for each.</p>
<p>Mussina's 1998 season was punctuated by two separate trips to the DL, including for injury resultant from when a ball hit by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandy_Alomar,_Jr." title="Sandy Alomar, Jr.">Sandy Alomar, Jr.</a> struck him on the face and fractured his nose. Mussina still managed to win 13 games and post a 3.49 ERA, with 175 strikeouts in 206.1 innings. His <a href="/wiki/Strikeout-to-walk_ratio" title="Strikeout-to-walk ratio">strikeout-to-walk ratio</a> was good for 2nd in the league. Mussina won his 3rd consecutive <a href="/wiki/Rawlings_Gold_Glove_Award" title="Rawlings Gold Glove Award">Gold Glove</a> with a perfect 1.000 <a href="/wiki/Fielding_percentage" title="Fielding percentage">fielding percentage</a> out of 50 <a href="/wiki/Total_chances" title="Total chances">total chances</a>. On August 4, Mussina struck out <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit's</a> <a href="/wiki/Bip_Roberts" title="Bip Roberts">Bip Roberts</a> for his 1000th career strikeout.</p>
<p>In 1999, he finished 2nd in the league with 18 wins, and his 3.50 ERA and .720 win-loss percentage were good for 3rd. Mussina struck out 172 batters in 203.2 innings while walking just 52, for a 3.31 K/BB ratio. He was selected as an <a href="/wiki/1999_Major_League_Baseball_All-Star_Game" title="1999 Major League Baseball All-Star Game">All-Star</a> and pitched one inning, Mussina finished second in the American League Cy Young Award voting that year behind <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Mart%C3%ADnez" title="Pedro Martínez">Pedro Martínez</a>. He committed just one <a href="/wiki/Error_(baseball)" title="Error (baseball)">error</a> out of 61 total chances and won his 4th consecutive Gold Glove, further cementing his reputation as one of the top defensive pitchers in baseball.</p>
<p>In 2000, Mussina recorded his first and only losing season going 11–15. However, he did not pitch as badly as his record suggests, as he allowed 100 earned runs in a league-leading 237.2 innings for a 3.79 ERA. He struck out 210 batters while allowing 44 walks. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opponents_batting_average" title="Opponents batting average">Opponents batted</a> just .255 off him. He finished 6th in voting for the Cy Young Award that year.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_York_Yankees">New York Yankees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mike_Mussina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: New York Yankees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the 2000 season, Mussina decided to leave the Orioles via <a href="/wiki/Free_agent" title="Free agent">free agency</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He signed a 6-year, $88.5 million contract with the New York Yankees on November 30, 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2001.E2.80.932004">2001–2004</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mike_Mussina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2001–2004">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Mussina finished the 2001 season with a 17–11 record. He was 2nd in the league in ERA (3.15), strikeouts (214), shutouts (3), and strikeout/walk ratio (5.10), and 5th in strikeouts/9 IP (8.42) and complete games (4). Mussina pitched seven shutout innings in Game 3 of the <a href="/wiki/2001_American_League_Division_Series" title="2001 American League Division Series">2001 American League Division Series</a> and the Yankees went on to win the game 1-0, and eventually the series, becoming the only team to win a division series after losing the first two games at home. Mussina started Games 1 and 5 of the <a href="/wiki/2001_World_Series" title="2001 World Series">2001 World Series</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Diamondbacks" title="Arizona Diamondbacks">Arizona Diamondbacks</a>, posting an 0–1 record with a 4.09 ERA in 11 innings pitched.</p>

<p>In 2002, Mussina was second in the AL in walks/9 IP (1.65), third in strikeouts (182) and strikeouts/9 IP (7.60), eighth in wins (18), and ninth in walks/9 IP (2.00). He held batters to a .198 batting average when the game was tied.</p>
<p>In 2003, Mussina was third in the league in strikeouts/9 IP (8.18) and strikeout/walk ratio (4.88), fourth in strikeouts (195) and walks/9 IP (1.68), fifth in wins (17), and eighth in ERA (3.40). He held batters to a .190 batting average when there were two outs and runners in scoring position. During Game 7 of the <a href="/wiki/2003_American_League_Championship_Series" title="2003 American League Championship Series">2003 ALCS</a>, Mussina authored one of the greatest clutch pitching performances of all time. With the Yankees trailing Boston 4–0 Mussina made the first relief appearance of his career. With runners on the corners and nobody out, Mussina struck out <a href="/wiki/Jason_Varitek" title="Jason Varitek">Jason Varitek</a> before inducing <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Damon" title="Johnny Damon">Johnny Damon</a> to hit into a double play. Mussina went on to pitch 2 more scoreless innings and kept the Yankees within striking distance in a game they later came back to win.</p>
<p>In 2004, plagued by a series of injuries, Mussina ended the year with a 12-9 record and a 4.59 ERA. He was fourth in the league in strikeouts (195), and eighth in walks/9 IP (2.19).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2005.E2.80.932008">2005–2008</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mike_Mussina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2005–2008">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2005, Mussina finished with a 13–8 record and a 4.41 ERA. He was seventh in the AL in strikeouts/9 IP (7.11).</p>
<p>In 2006, he ended the season with a 15–7 record. He was second in the league in <a href="/wiki/On-base_percentage" title="On-base percentage">OBP</a> against (.279), third in the American League in walks/9 IP (1.60; a career-best), batting average against (.241), and strikeout/walk ratio (4.91), fourth in ERA (3.51), sixth in win-loss percentage (.682). He fanned <a href="/wiki/Cody_Ross" title="Cody Ross">Cody Ross</a> to reach the 2,500 strikeout mark on June 25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Mussina also became the first pitcher in <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> history to win 10 or more games for 15 consecutive seasons.</p>
<p>On November 20, 2006, Mussina and the Yankees reached a preliminary agreement, pending a physical, on a two-year, $23 million contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Earlier in the off-season the Yankees declined the 1-year, $17 million option on Mussina's previous contract. Under that back-loaded six-year, $88.5 million contract, Mussina earned $19 million in each of the last two seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2007, Mussina became just the ninth player to win 100 games with two different teams—he had won 147 with Baltimore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, with the Yankees locked in a tight pennant race, Mussina struggled and temporarily lost his spot in the rotation to prospect <a href="/wiki/Ian_Kennedy" title="Ian Kennedy">Ian Kennedy</a>. After just one relief appearance, (the first of his regular season career), Mussina returned as a starter, going 3–0 in his final four starts to end with 11-10 record and a career-high 5.15 ERA. The '07 season for Mussina and Mets' pitcher <a href="/wiki/Tom_Glavine" title="Tom Glavine">Tom Glavine</a> was the subject of a 2008 book by <a href="/wiki/John_Feinstein" title="John Feinstein">John Feinstein</a>, <i>Living on the Black: Two Pitchers, Two Teams, One Season to Remember</i>, showcasing a pivotal season for two New York City pitchers as Mussina nailed down milestone career win #250 with the Yankees and Glavine earned win #300 with the cross-town Mets.</p>
<p>In 2008, Mussina started his 8th season with the Yankees as a much-needed veteran of an inexperienced rotation. The year began with difficulty reminiscent of 2007, and many noted a sharp decline in his pitch velocity. Owner <a href="/wiki/Hank_Steinbrenner" title="Hank Steinbrenner">Hank Steinbrenner</a> suggested that Mussina should "learn how to pitch like <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Moyer" title="Jamie Moyer">Jamie Moyer</a>", and no longer rely so much on his fastball.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Although that remark was widely interpreted as a slight, Mussina joked in response that he could not pitch like Moyer because he did not throw left-handed, and afterwards he excelled, going 9–1 in his subsequent eleven starts. On June 15, he recorded his 10th win of the season, extending his American League record to 17 consecutive seasons with at least 10 wins. On September 18, Mussina notched his 18th victory of the season and led the Yankees to a 9–2 victory over the first place White Sox in his final start at <a href="/wiki/Yankee_Stadium" title="Yankee Stadium">Yankee Stadium</a>. On September 28, he won 20 games for the first time at the age of 39, with a 6–2 win over the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> at <a href="/wiki/Fenway_Park" title="Fenway Park">Fenway Park</a>, becoming the oldest first-time 20 game winner in MLB history. He finished 20–9 with a 3.37 ERA. His 67.6% first-strike-percentage was the highest among major league starters. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mussina would later finish second to <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a> pitcher <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Lee" title="Cliff Lee">Cliff Lee</a> in the voting for American League comeback player of the year honors. On November 6, he was awarded his seventh career <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_Glove_Award" title="Gold Glove Award">Gold Glove Award</a>, and the third in his career with the Yankees. He would later finish behind Lee again in the balloting for the 2008 AL Cy Young Award. Mussina's sixth-place finish was his best since 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mussina officially announced his retirement on November 20, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Mussina is the first pitcher to retire following a 20-victory season since Hall of Famer <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Koufax" title="Sandy Koufax">Sandy Koufax</a> in 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Near-perfect_games">Near-perfect games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mike_Mussina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Near-perfect games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mussina pitched several near-<a href="/wiki/Perfect_game" title="Perfect game">perfect games</a> throughout his career:</p>
<ul>
<li>On July 17, 1992, he retired the first 12 <a href="/wiki/Texas_Rangers_(baseball)" title="Texas Rangers (baseball)">Texas Rangers</a> before surrendering a double to <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Reimer" title="Kevin Reimer">Kevin Reimer</a>. Mussina retired the final 15 batters he faced for a one-hit 8–0 shutout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nearperfect1_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-nearperfect1-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>On May 30, 1997, he retired the first 25 <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Indians" title="Cleveland Indians">Cleveland Indians</a> before surrendering a single to <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Alomar_Jr." title="Sandy Alomar Jr.">Sandy Alomar Jr.</a> with one out in the ninth. Mussina struck out the last two batters for a one-hit 3–0 shutout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nearperfect2_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-nearperfect2-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>On August 4, 1998, he retired the first 23 <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> he faced before surrendering a double to <a href="/wiki/Frank_Catalanotto" title="Frank Catalanotto">Frank Catalanotto</a> with two outs in the eighth. Mussina gave up another hit in the eventual two hit 4–0 shutout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nearperfect3_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-nearperfect3-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>On August 1, 2000, he tossed a one-hitter against the Minnesota Twins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>On September 2, 2001, he retired the first 26 <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> he faced; he then ran pinch-hitter <a href="/wiki/Carl_Everett" title="Carl Everett">Carl Everett</a> (batting for <a href="/wiki/Joe_Oliver_(baseball)" title="Joe Oliver (baseball)">Joe Oliver</a>) to a 1-and-2 count before Everett blooped a single to left-center. Mussina then retired leadoff man <a href="/wiki/Trot_Nixon" title="Trot Nixon">Trot Nixon</a> on a grounder, striking out 13 batters in a one-hit 1–0 shutout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nearperfect4_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-nearperfect4-25">[25]</a></sup> The losing pitcher was <a href="/wiki/David_Cone" title="David Cone">David Cone</a>—the pitcher of <a href="/wiki/David_Cone%27s_perfect_game" title="David Cone's perfect game">the last perfect game</a> at the time, on July 18, 1999. Although Mussina did not achieve perfection, James Buckley, Jr. considered it special enough to include an appendix chapter about it in his 2002 book <i>Perfect: The Inside Story of Baseball's Sixteen Perfect Games</i>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_career_achievements">Other career achievements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mike_Mussina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other career achievements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Seven-time <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_Glove" title="Gold Glove">Gold Glove</a> award winner.</li>
<li>Placed in the top five of voting for the Cy Young Award six times.</li>
<li>1994 <a href="/wiki/Baseball_America" title="Baseball America">Baseball America</a> First-Team American League All-Star starting pitcher.</li>
<li>1999 Baseball America Second-Team American League All-Star starting pitcher.</li>
<li>Led AL in Won-Loss percentage (.783) in 1992</li>
<li>Led AL in Wins (19), Walks/9IP (2.03) and Shutouts (4) in 1995</li>
<li>Led AL in Games Started (36) in 1996</li>
<li>Led AL in Innings (237 ⅔) in 2000</li>
<li>Reached both the <a href="/wiki/2001_World_Series" title="2001 World Series">2001</a> and <a href="/wiki/2003_World_Series" title="2003 World Series">2003 World Series</a> with the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a></li>
<li>Won 15 games in a season 11 times.</li>
<li>One 20-win season (<a href="/wiki/2008_in_baseball" title="2008 in baseball">2008</a>), two 19-win seasons, three 18-win seasons, and two 17 win seasons.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Postseason_performance">Postseason performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mike_Mussina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Postseason performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mussina collected an overall 7–8 record and 3.40 ERA, with 142 strikeouts in 22 career postseason games. His finest posteason occurred in 1997 for the Baltimore Orioles when in 4 games he went 2–0 with a 1.24 ERA in 29 IP with 11 H, 4 ER, 7 BB and 41 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Notably, he twice outdueled <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Mariners" title="Seattle Mariners">Seattle Mariners</a> ace <a href="/wiki/Randy_Johnson" title="Randy Johnson">Randy Johnson</a> in head-to-head matchups during the <a href="/wiki/1997_American_League_Division_Series#Baltimore_vs._Seattle" title="1997 American League Division Series">ALDS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> A feature on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s website ranked his performance the 6th-most memorable moment at <a href="/wiki/Oriole_Park_at_Camden_Yards" title="Oriole Park at Camden Yards">Camden Yards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pitching_style">Pitching style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mike_Mussina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Pitching style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early in his career, Mussina's arsenal included a <a href="/wiki/Four-seam_fastball" title="Four-seam fastball">four-seam fastball</a> that topped out at 95 mph, a <a href="/wiki/Two-seam_fastball" title="Two-seam fastball">two-seam fastball</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Slider" title="Slider">slider</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Changeup" title="Changeup">changeup</a>, and a plus <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knuckle-curve" title="Knuckle-curve">knuckle-curve</a>. He was always a finesse pitcher, and coming up through the Orioles' organization, he was often compared to <a href="/wiki/Jim_Palmer" title="Jim Palmer">Jim Palmer</a>. He received praise for the ability to make in-game adjustments to compensate for days when he was not at his best.</p>
<p>Mussina's prolonged success was also the result of his ability to make adjustments. He added a <a href="/wiki/Split-finger_fastball" title="Split-finger fastball">splitter</a> to his repertoire and replaced his knuckle-curve with a more conventional <a href="/wiki/Curveball" title="Curveball">curveball</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> He became more skilled at changing speeds with his breaking pitches and using different arm angles to confuse batters as well as to compensate for the diminishing speed of his fastball.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Spring_training" title="Spring training">spring training</a> in 2006, Yankees catcher <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Posada" title="Jorge Posada">Jorge Posada</a> noticed the unique grip Mussina used for his changeup and promptly hit a home run off it during an intra-squad game. Posada alerted Mussina to the tip-off, and he adjusted the grip. This new changeup was difficult for batters to recognize and was considered a main reason for his success that season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mussina's remarkable results in 2008 were attributed to changes in pitching style. While in the past he was known for painting the outside corner of the plate with a mid-90s <a href="/wiki/Four-seam_fastball" title="Four-seam fastball">four-seam fastball</a>, he began to work on both sides of the plate with his diminished upper-80s fastball. Additionally, when throwing the fastball, he often used the <a href="/wiki/Two-seam_fastball" title="Two-seam fastball">two-seam grip</a>, which gives the ball late breaking motion. Despite his lower fastball velocity, Mussina maintained a significant differential in pitch speed by also lowering the velocity of his <a href="/wiki/Changeup" title="Changeup">changeup</a>. In addition to those more obvious changes, Craig Brown of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hardball_Times" title="The Hardball Times">The Hardball Times</a></i> also attributed Mussina's renaissance to excellent control, noting that he was walking fewer batters than ever before and was becoming a ground-ball pitcher for the first time in his career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout his career, Mussina also complemented his pure pitching ability by doing the little things well. He issued very few walks, held baserunners well, and fielded his position superbly.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hall_of_Fame_debate">Hall of Fame debate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mike_Mussina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Hall of Fame debate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mike-mussina.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1475" data-file-width="1106" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Mike-mussina.jpg/170px-Mike-mussina.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Mike-mussina.jpg/255px-Mike-mussina.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Mike-mussina.jpg/340px-Mike-mussina.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mike-mussina.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mussina in his final season in 2008</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Mussina's candidacy for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" title="National Baseball Hall of Fame">Hall of Fame</a> has come under recent debate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> "Do I compare to some guys who are in? I think I do", Mussina told <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> in 2006. The only other pitchers to match Mussina's 17 seasons of 10 or more victories are <a href="/wiki/Ted_Lyons" title="Ted Lyons">Ted Lyons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Glavine" title="Tom Glavine">Tom Glavine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gaylord_Perry" title="Gaylord Perry">Gaylord Perry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phil_Niekro" title="Phil Niekro">Phil Niekro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Johnson" title="Walter Johnson">Walter Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greg_Maddux" title="Greg Maddux">Greg Maddux</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warren_Spahn" title="Warren Spahn">Warren Spahn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cy_Young" title="Cy Young">Cy Young</a>, <a href="/wiki/Don_Sutton" title="Don Sutton">Don Sutton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Seaver" title="Tom Seaver">Tom Seaver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tommy_John" title="Tommy John">Tommy John</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Carlton" title="Steve Carlton">Steve Carlton</a>; all are Hall of Famers, except Tommy John. Of the 23 eligible pitchers who have at least 265 wins and an ERA of 3.69 or less, 20 are in the Hall of Fame, although if Mussina was elected, his ERA would be third highest ahead of <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Wallace_(baseball)" title="Bobby Wallace (baseball)">Bobby Wallace</a>, who was a full-time pitcher for just two seasons, and <a href="/wiki/Red_Ruffing" title="Red Ruffing">Red Ruffing</a>. Mussina's consistency is often overshadowed by the dominant peaks of contemporaries like <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Mart%C3%ADnez" title="Pedro Martínez">Pedro Martínez</a> and <a href="/wiki/Randy_Johnson" title="Randy Johnson">Randy Johnson</a>.</p>
<p>His numbers are often compared to those of <a href="/wiki/Jim_Palmer" title="Jim Palmer">Jim Palmer</a>. Mussina has two more wins and one more loss than Palmer, (both finished with a .638 winning percentage,) while Palmer's ERA is .82 lower. Mussina finished his career with 601 more strikeouts than Palmer. Baseball writer <a href="/wiki/Tim_Kurkjian" title="Tim Kurkjian">Tim Kurkjian</a> stated on the August 3, 2008, edition of Baseball Tonight: "He's a Hall of Famer. I've looked at the numbers and he's in."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Mussina's ERA may have suffered from playing at a time when hitters dominated. Mussina's career <a href="/wiki/Adjusted_ERA%2B" title="Adjusted ERA+">adjusted ERA+</a>, which takes into account the league's average ERA and the parks in which the pitcher plays, is 123—better than Hall-of-Famers <a href="/wiki/Nolan_Ryan" title="Nolan Ryan">Nolan Ryan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ted_Lyons" title="Ted Lyons">Ted Lyons</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jim_Bunning" title="Jim Bunning">Jim Bunning</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Bob_Lemon" title="Bob Lemon">Bob Lemon</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> among others.</p>
<p>He is the oldest pitcher to ever <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Win_(baseball)" title="Win (baseball)">win</a> 20 games in a season for the first time at the age of 39 in 2008. He never won the <a href="/wiki/Cy_Young_Award" title="Cy Young Award">Cy Young Award</a>, but finished in the top six of Cy Young voting nine times. Mussina also came tantalizingly close to pitching a <a href="/wiki/Perfect_game" title="Perfect game">perfect game</a> and winning a <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a>, having lost chances at achieving both in the 9th inning in 2001.</p>
<p>Only five pitchers in the history of major league baseball have as many victories as Mussina and a better winning percentage: <a href="/wiki/Lefty_Grove" title="Lefty Grove">Lefty Grove</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christy_Mathewson" title="Christy Mathewson">Christy Mathewson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grover_Cleveland_Alexander" title="Grover Cleveland Alexander">Grover Cleveland Alexander</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Clemens" title="Roger Clemens">Roger Clemens</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Randy_Johnson" title="Randy Johnson">Randy Johnson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mussina has the fourth highest career <a href="/wiki/Jaffe_WAR_Score_System" title="Jaffe WAR Score System">JAWS</a> of any pitcher not in the Hall of Fame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mussina won seven Gold Glove Awards during his career.</p>
<p>All pitchers with 100 more wins than losses who have been eligible, with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Roger_Clemens" title="Roger Clemens">Roger Clemens</a>, have been inducted to the Hall of Fame. With a record of 270–153, Mussina fits that criterion.</p>
<p>In 2014, Mussina received 20.3% of the vote on his <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_balloting,_2014" title="Baseball Hall of Fame balloting, 2014">first ballot</a> (75% required for induction).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In 2015, he received 24.6% on his <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_balloting,_2015" title="Baseball Hall of Fame balloting, 2015">second ballot</a>. He rose to 43.0% in 2016 on his <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_balloting,_2016" title="Baseball Hall of Fame balloting, 2016">third ballot</a>. In his <a href="/wiki/Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_balloting,_2017" title="Baseball Hall of Fame balloting, 2017">fourth year</a> of eligibility, support for his candidacy continued to steadily increase, as he received 51.8% of the vote.</p>
<p>Mussina was inducted into the Baltimore Orioles Hall of Fame on August 25, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mike_Mussina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mussina married Jana McKissick in 1997. The couple has two sons, Brycen and Peyton. He also adopted his step-daughter Kyra, who is Jana's from a previous marriage. He still resides in his hometown of Montoursville, where he is well known. He often helps out with the athletic programs at his high school and he operates a training camp for student athletes in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> He serves on the <a href="/wiki/Little_League_Baseball" title="Little League Baseball">Little League</a> International Board of Directors, based in nearby <a href="/wiki/South_Williamsport,_Pennsylvania" title="South Williamsport, Pennsylvania">South Williamsport, Pennsylvania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-littleleagueboard_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-littleleagueboard-43">[43]</a></sup> Mussina is the head basketball coach for the boys' team at <a href="/wiki/Montoursville_Area_High_School" title="Montoursville Area High School">Montoursville Area High School</a>. He accepted the position in June 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mussina is of <a href="/wiki/Slavs" title="Slavs">Slavic</a> descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Due to his last name, which was Americanized by his ancestors, he is often misidentified as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_American" title="Italian American">Italian American</a>; before the inaugural <a href="/wiki/World_Baseball_Classic" title="World Baseball Classic">World Baseball Classic</a> in 2006, <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_Players_Association" title="Major League Baseball Players Association">Gene Orza</a>, the chief operating officer of the <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_Players_Association" title="Major League Baseball Players Association">players union</a>, even asked him to play for Italy on that assumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2014, Mussina was announced as one of the six recipients of the 2015 <a href="/wiki/Silver_Anniversary_Awards" title="Silver Anniversary Awards">Silver Anniversary Awards</a>, presented annually by the <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a> to outstanding former student-athletes on the 25th anniversary of the end of their college sports careers. The award is based on both athletic and professional success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mussina is also a crossword puzzle enthusiast and was featured in the 2006 documentary film <i><a href="/wiki/Wordplay_(film)" title="Wordplay (film)">Wordplay</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imdb_wordplay_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-imdb_wordplay-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abcnews_wordplay_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-abcnews_wordplay-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>He is also a collector of <a href="/wiki/Tractor" title="Tractor">tractors</a> and vintage cars.</p>
