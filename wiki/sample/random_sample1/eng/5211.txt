<p><b>Microdrive</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Registered_trademark" title="Registered trademark">registered trademark</a> for miniature, 1-inch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_disk" title="Hard disk">hard disks</a> produced by <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hitachi" title="Hitachi">Hitachi</a>. These rotational media storage devices were designed to fit in <a href="/wiki/CompactFlash" title="CompactFlash">CompactFlash</a> (CF) Type II slots. The release of similar drives by other makers led to them often being referred to as "microdrives" too. As of 2015,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Microdrives are viewed as obsolete, having been eclipsed by solid-state flash media in read/write performance, storage capacity, durability, physical size, and price.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microdrive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to the 1-inch Microdrive, a <a href="/wiki/HP_Kittyhawk_microdrive" title="HP Kittyhawk microdrive">1.3-inch HDD</a> was developed and launched in 1992 by <a href="/wiki/Hewlett-Packard" title="Hewlett-Packard">HP</a> with a capacity of 20 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megabytes" title="Megabytes">MB</a>. These units weighed about 28 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gram_(unit)" title="Gram (unit)">g</a> (1 <a href="/wiki/Ounce" title="Ounce">oz</a>), with dimensions of 2.0" x 1.44" x 0.414" (50.8 mm x 36.5 mm x 10.5 mm) and were the physically smallest hard drives in the world before the Microdrive.</p>
<p>In 1999, IBM launched the first generation 1-inch Microdrive with storage capacities of 170 MB and 340 MB.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The physical dimensions of Microdrive were 1.65" x 1.42" x 0.197" (42.0 mm x 36.0 mm x 5.0 mm) and conformed to CompactFlash Type II card standard. A second generation of Microdrive was announced by IBM in 2000 with increased capacities at 512 MB and 1 GB. Following the merger of IBM and Hitachi HDD business units, Hitachi Global Storage Technologies continued the development and marketing of the Microdrive. In 2003, 2 GB and 4 GB models were announced<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> by Hitachi, followed by 6 GB capacity model in 2005.</p>

Seagate 2.5 GB 1" CF drive</div>

<p>In 2004, <a href="/wiki/Seagate_Technology" title="Seagate Technology">Seagate</a> launched 2.5 and 5 GB models, and referred to them as either 1-inch hard drives or CompactFlash hard drives due to the trademark issue. These drives were also commonly known as the <a href="/wiki/Seagate_ST1" title="Seagate ST1">Seagate ST1</a>. In 2005 Seagate launched an 8 GB model. Seagate also sold a standalone consumer product based on these drives with a product known as the Pocket Hard Drive. These devices came in the shape of a hockey puck with an integrated USB 2.0 cable.</p>
<p>In 2003-07-16, a Chinese manufacturer called GS Magicstor, Inc. (subsidiary of GS Magic, Inc.) announced it had produced 1-inch hard disk drive with capacity of 2.4GB at the beginning of the year 2003,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> originally marketed as an alternative to Microdrive by Hitachi Global Storage Technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It was to be followed by 2.2 and 4.8GB 1-inch HDD that was unveiled in 2004 International CES, with 0.8-inch HDD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2004-12-28, Hitachi Global Storage Technologies announced it had filed lawsuit against GS Magicstor, Inc., GS Magic, Inc., and Riospring, Inc. for infringement of multiple Hitachi GST's patents relating to hard disk drives,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> after GS Magic Inc. had started promoting mini-HDD (small form factor hard disk drive).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of July 2012, there are no known manufacturers of Microdrives.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">Advantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microdrive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Advantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Until 2006, Microdrives had higher capacity than CompactFlash cards.</li>
<li>Microdrives allow more write cycles, making them suitable for use as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swapspace" title="Swapspace">swapspace</a> in embedded applications.</li>
<li>Microdrives might be better at handling power loss in the middle of writing. Flash storage always needs to move some old data around while writing, to ensure the flash's finite write life is consumed equally; if there were a bug in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wear_levelling" title="Wear levelling">wear levelling</a> algorithm it could cause data loss if the card were unplugged at the exact wrong time. Data on a disk is modified in place, and hard drive algorithms have many years more experience and testing over Flash.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages">Disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microdrive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>As of 2006, Microdrive's capacity advantages were exceeded by CompactFlash cards<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> (which are the same size and are often compatible with each other), and USB flash drives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> As of 5 July 2015, 512 GB CF cards are available, offering 42 times the storage space of the largest Microdrive.</li>
<li>Being mechanical devices they are more sensitive to physical shock and temperature changes than flash memory, though in practice they are very robust and manufacturers have added several features to the more recent models to improve reliability.</li>
<li>A microdrive will generally not survive a 4-foot (1.2 metres) drop onto a hard surface whereas CF cards can survive much higher falls.</li>
<li>Microdrives are not as fast as the high-end CompactFlash cards; they generally operate at around 4–6 megabytes per second while high-end CF cards can operate at 45 megabytes per second. This may cause problems for photographers who shoot sequences of large images in rapid succession.</li>
<li>They are not designed to operate at high altitudes (over 10,000 feet), but can be safely used on most commercial aircraft as cabins are generally pressurized.</li>
<li>Outside of the professional photography market, Microdrives have more limited popularity by general consumers because many newer compact cameras lack CF/CF II slots due to the increasing popularity of portable flash media.</li>
<li>Only high capacity models are manufactured, as it is not profitable to make low-capacity Microdrives. At the end of 2005 only capacities above 2 GB are manufactured while 256 MB and 512 MB CompactFlash cards were still in production. Lower capacities are still readily available second hand on <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a> but these are usually the same price as CF cards of the same size.</li>
<li>Unlike flash storage, Microdrives would require power even when no data is being transferred to or read from them, just to keep the disk spinning in order to maintain quick access. As a result, many devices such as the iPod mini leave the drive switched off for most of the time while periodically starting it up to fetch data from it to fill the device's buffer. Microdrives will switch off after idling for more than a few seconds to counter this problem; however this means that more time is lost constantly spinning the drive up. This effect would be particularly problematic if an operating system is being run from the drive. However the drive can be forced to stay running if the host device is programmed to write random bits of data to it.</li>
<li>Certain bus-powered CF card readers lack the power needed to run a Microdrive although they do take CF II cards. When using such a device, it will usually be detected by the host but errors will occur once the user attempts to access the drive.</li>
<li>Some "OEM Only" drives use the CompactFlash form factor but only provide a 5 V IDE/ATA interface. These will not work in readers or devices which expect a 3.3 V interface and full CompactFlash functionality <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.stevesforums.com/forums/view_topic.php?id=41985&amp;forum_id=52" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>,</li>
<li>Microdrives also require some time to spin up, this is very low when compared to regular hard drives but not as fast as flash memory.</li>
<li>Since Microdrives are often integrated in another device, this can cause trouble if the drive breaks. It is usually the most fragile component of the device, and if the device is out of warranty it can be very difficult to replace the drive.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microdrive_models_by_manufacturer">Microdrive models by manufacturer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microdrive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Microdrive models by manufacturer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IBM_Microdrive">IBM Microdrive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microdrive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: IBM Microdrive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
