<p><b>Microsoft Office 2000</b> is a release of <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Office" title="Microsoft Office">Microsoft Office</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Office_suite" title="Office suite">office suite</a> for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a> family of operating systems. Released to manufacturing on March 29, 1999<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and shipped on June 7, 1999,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> it succeeded <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Office_97" title="Microsoft Office 97">Microsoft Office 97</a> and preceded <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Office_XP" title="Microsoft Office XP">Microsoft Office XP</a>. It comes in five different editions, the most comprehensive of which is Office 2000 Developer.</p>
<p>Microsoft Office 2000 was designed as a fully 32-bit suite of software developed with <a href="/wiki/Year_2000_problem" title="Year 2000 problem">Y2k bug</a> in mind. All the Office 2000 applications support <a href="/wiki/Object_Linking_and_Embedding" title="Object Linking and Embedding">Object Linking and Embedding</a> (OLE) which allows moving data automatically between various programs.</p>
<p>Office 2000 was the first version of Office to use <a href="/wiki/Windows_Installer" title="Windows Installer">Windows Installer</a> technology. This allowed <a href="/wiki/Service_pack" title="Service pack">service packs</a> to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slipstream_(computing)" title="Slipstream (computing)">slipstreamed</a> into the original installation files. The first update was called Service Release 1 (SR-1). Subsequent updates were called service packs. Service Pack 3, which was released on October 21, 2002,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> was the last service pack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>All retail editions of Office 2000 sold in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and academic copies sold in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> required the user to activate the product via the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apfeaturespr_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-apfeaturespr-7">[7]</a></sup> In the United States, after the release of SR-1, this requirement was extended to all copies that included it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apfeaturespr_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-apfeaturespr-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Product_activation" title="Product activation">Product activation</a> is however disabled in Office 2000 after April 15, 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Microsoft Office 2000 had <a href="/wiki/Easter_egg_(media)" title="Easter egg (media)">easter eggs</a>, most of which was removed from SR-1. For example, Microsoft Excel contained a hidden game called Dev Hunter; Microsoft Access had a hidden 8 ball toy; and Microsoft Word had team development credits.</p>
<p>Mainstream support for Office 2000 ended on June 30, 2004,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-support1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-support1-9">[9]</a></sup> and extended support ended on July 14, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-support1_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-support1-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Office 2000 was the final release of the Microsoft Office series to support <a href="/wiki/Windows_95" title="Windows 95">Windows 95</a>.</p>

<p><b>Microsoft Office 2000 Personal</b> was an additional SKU, solely designed for the Japanese market, that included Word 2000, Excel 2000 and Outlook 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This compilation would later become widespread as Microsoft Office 2003 Basic.</p>
