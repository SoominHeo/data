<p><b>Microwaves</b> are a form of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> with <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a> ranging from one meter to one millimeter; with <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequencies</a> between 300Â MHz (100Â cm) and 300Â GHz (0.1Â cm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This broad definition includes both <a href="/wiki/Ultra_high_frequency" title="Ultra high frequency">UHF</a> and <a href="/wiki/Extremely_high_frequency" title="Extremely high frequency">EHF</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millimeter_wave" title="Millimeter wave">millimeter wave</a>) bands, and various sources use different boundaries. In all cases, microwaves include the entire <a href="/wiki/Super_high_frequency" title="Super high frequency">SHF</a> band (3 to 30Â GHz, or 10 to 1Â cm) at minimum, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RF_engineering" title="RF engineering">RF engineering</a> often restricting the range between 1 and 100Â GHz (300 and 3Â mm). Frequencies in the microwave range are often referred to by their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_band#IEEE" title="Radio band">IEEE radar band</a> designations: <a href="/wiki/S_band" title="S band">S</a>, <a href="/wiki/C_band_(IEEE)" title="C band (IEEE)">C</a>, <a href="/wiki/X_band" title="X band">X</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ku_band" title="Ku band">K<sub>u</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/K_band_(IEEE)" title="K band (IEEE)">K</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Ka_band" title="Ka band">K<sub>a</sub> band</a>, or by similar NATO or EU designations.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Prefix" title="Prefix">prefix</a> <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/micro-" title="wikt:micro-">micro-</a></i> in <i>microwave</i> is not meant to suggest a wavelength in the micrometer range. It indicates that microwaves are "small", compared to waves used in typical <a href="/wiki/Radio_broadcasting" title="Radio broadcasting">radio broadcasting</a>, in that they have shorter wavelengths. The boundaries between <a href="/wiki/Far_infrared" title="Far infrared">far infrared</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terahertz_radiation" title="Terahertz radiation">terahertz radiation</a>, microwaves, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultra-high-frequency" title="Ultra-high-frequency">ultra-high-frequency</a> <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">waves</a> are fairly arbitrary and are used variously between different fields of study.</p>
<p>Microwaves travel by <a href="/wiki/Line-of-sight_propagation" title="Line-of-sight propagation">line-of-sight</a>; unlike lower frequency radio waves they do not refract around hills, follow the earth's surface as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_wave" title="Ground wave">ground waves</a>, or reflect from the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>, so terrestrial microwave communication links are limited by the visual horizon to about 40 miles (64 km). At the high end of the band they are absorbed by gases in the atmosphere, limiting practical communication distances to around a kilometer. Microwaves are extremely widely used in modern technology. They are used for <a href="/wiki/Point-to-point_(telecommunications)" title="Point-to-point (telecommunications)">point-to-point</a> communication links, <a href="/wiki/Wireless_network" title="Wireless network">wireless networks</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microwave_radio_relay" title="Microwave radio relay">microwave radio relay</a> networks, <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_communication" title="Satellite communication">satellite and spacecraft communication</a>, medical <a href="/wiki/Diathermy" title="Diathermy">diathermy</a> and cancer treatment, <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a>, industrial heating, <a href="/wiki/Collision_avoidance_system" title="Collision avoidance system">collision avoidance systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garage_door_opener" title="Garage door opener">garage door openers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keyless_entry_system" title="Keyless entry system">keyless entry systems</a>, and for cooking food in <a href="/wiki/Microwave_oven" title="Microwave oven">microwave ovens</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Propagation">Propagation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microwave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Propagation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Microwaves travel solely by <a href="/wiki/Line-of-sight_propagation" title="Line-of-sight propagation">line-of-sight</a> paths; unlike lower frequency radio waves they do not travel as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_wave" title="Ground wave">ground waves</a> which follow the contour of the Earth, or reflect off the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a> (<a href="/wiki/Skywave" title="Skywave">skywaves</a>). Although at the low end of the band they can pass through building walls enough for useful reception, usually rights of way cleared to the first <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_zone" title="Fresnel zone">Fresnel zone</a> are required. Therefore on the surface of the Earth microwave communication links are limited by the visual horizon to about 30 - 40 miles (48 - 64 km). Microwaves are absorbed by moisture in the atmosphere, and the attenuation increases with frequency, becoming a significant factor (<a href="/wiki/Rain_fade" title="Rain fade">rain fade</a>) at the high end of the band. Beginning at about 40Â GHz, atmospheric gases also begin to absorb microwaves, so above this frequency microwave transmission is limited to a few kilometers. A spectral band structure causes absorption peaks at specific frequencies (see graph at right). Above 100Â GHz, the absorption of electromagnetic radiation by Earth's atmosphere is so great that it is in effect <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">opaque</a>, until the atmosphere becomes transparent again in the so-called <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> and <a href="/wiki/Optical_window" title="Optical window">optical window</a> frequency ranges.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Troposcatter">Troposcatter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microwave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Troposcatter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropospheric_scatter" title="Tropospheric scatter">Tropospheric scatter</a></div>
<p>A microwave beam directed at an angle into the sky will not reflect back to Earth from the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a> as lower frequency radio waves do. However at frequencies between 0.45 and 5Â GHz a small part of the microwave energy will be randomly scattered as the beam passes through the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>. A sensitive receiver beyond the horizon with a high gain antenna focused on that area of the troposphere can pick up the signal. This technique has been used in <a href="/wiki/Tropospheric_scatter" title="Tropospheric scatter">tropospheric scatter</a> (troposcatter]] communication systems to communicate beyond the horizon, at distances up to 300 km.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Antennas">Antennas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microwave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Antennas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Their short <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> allows narrow beams of microwaves to be produced by conveniently small <a href="/wiki/Antenna_gain" title="Antenna gain">high gain</a> <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">antennas</a> from a half meter to 5 meters in diameter. Therefore beams of microwaves are used for <a href="/wiki/Point-to-point_(telecommunications)" title="Point-to-point (telecommunications)">point-to-point</a> communication links, and for <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>. An advantage of narrow beams is that they allow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_reuse" title="Frequency reuse">frequency reuse</a> by nearby transmitters. <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_antenna" title="Parabolic antenna">Parabolic ("dish") antennas</a> are the most widely used directive antennas at microwave frequencies, but <a href="/wiki/Horn_antenna" title="Horn antenna">horn antennas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slot_antenna" title="Slot antenna">slot antennas</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dielectric_lens" title="Dielectric lens">dielectric lens</a> antennas are also used. Flat <a href="/wiki/Microstrip_antenna" title="Microstrip antenna">microstrip antennas</a> are being increasingly used in consumer devices. Another directive antenna practical at microwave frequencies is the <a href="/wiki/Phased_array" title="Phased array">phased array</a>, a computer-controlled array of antennas which produces a beam which can be electronically steered in different directions. Where <a href="/wiki/Omnidirectional_antenna" title="Omnidirectional antenna">omnidirectional antennas</a> are required, for example in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wireless_device" title="Wireless device">wireless devices</a> and Wifi routers for <a href="/wiki/Wireless_LAN" title="Wireless LAN">wireless LANs</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Monopole_antenna" title="Monopole antenna">monopoles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dipole_antenna" title="Dipole antenna">dipole</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Patch_antenna" title="Patch antenna">patch antennas</a> are used.</p>
<p>At microwave frequencies, the <a href="/wiki/Transmission_line" title="Transmission line">transmission lines</a> which are used to carry lower frequency radio waves to and from antennas, such as <a href="/wiki/Coaxial_cable" title="Coaxial cable">coaxial cable</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twin_lead" title="Twin lead">parallel wire lines</a>, have excessive power losses, so when low attenuation is required microwaves are carried by metal pipes called <a href="/wiki/Waveguide_(electromagnetism)" title="Waveguide (electromagnetism)">waveguides</a>. Due to the high cost and maintenance requirements of waveguide runs, in many microwave antennas the output stage of the <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">transmitter</a> or the <a href="/wiki/RF_front_end" title="RF front end">RF front end</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Radio_receiver" title="Radio receiver">receiver</a> is located at the antenna.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Difference_between_microwave_and_radio_frequency_technology">Difference between microwave and radio frequency technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microwave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Difference between microwave and radio frequency technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>microwave</i> also has a more technical meaning in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetics" title="Electromagnetics">electromagnetics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circuit_theory" title="Circuit theory">circuit theory</a>. Apparatus and techniques may be described qualitatively as "microwave" when the frequencies used are high enough that wavelengths of signals are roughly the same as the dimensions of the equipment, so that <a href="/wiki/Lumped_element_model" title="Lumped element model">lumped-element circuit theory</a> is inaccurate, and instead <a href="/wiki/Distributed_element_model" title="Distributed element model">distributed circuit elements</a> and transmission-line theory are more useful methods for design and analysis. As a consequence, practical microwave circuits tend to move away from the discrete <a href="/wiki/Resistor" title="Resistor">resistors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capacitor" title="Capacitor">capacitors</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Inductor" title="Inductor">inductors</a> used with lower-frequency <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_waves" title="Radio waves">radio waves</a>. Open-wire and coaxial <a href="/wiki/Transmission_line" title="Transmission line">transmission lines</a> used at lower frequencies are replaced by <a href="/wiki/Waveguide" title="Waveguide">waveguides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stripline" title="Stripline">stripline</a>, and lumped-element tuned circuits are replaced by cavity <a href="/wiki/Resonator" title="Resonator">resonators</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resonant_stub" title="Resonant stub">resonant stubs</a>. In turn, at even higher frequencies, where the wavelength of the electromagnetic waves becomes small in comparison to the size of the structures used to process them, microwave techniques become inadequate, and the methods of <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a> are used.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microwave_sources">Microwave sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microwave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Microwave sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>High-power microwave sources use specialized <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tubes</a> to generate microwaves. These devices operate on different principles from low-frequency vacuum tubes, using the ballistic motion of electrons in a vacuum under the influence of controlling electric or magnetic fields, and include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetron" title="Magnetron">magnetron</a> (used in <a href="/wiki/Microwave_oven" title="Microwave oven">microwave ovens</a>), <a href="/wiki/Klystron" title="Klystron">klystron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Traveling-wave_tube" title="Traveling-wave tube">traveling-wave tube</a> (TWT), and <a href="/wiki/Gyrotron" title="Gyrotron">gyrotron</a>. These devices work in the <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> modulated mode, rather than the <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">current</a> modulated mode. This means that they work on the basis of clumps of electrons flying ballistically through them, rather than using a continuous stream of electrons.</p>
<p>Low-power microwave sources use solid-state devices such as the <a href="/wiki/Field-effect_transistor" title="Field-effect transistor">field-effect transistor</a> (at least at lower frequencies), <a href="/wiki/Tunnel_diode" title="Tunnel diode">tunnel diodes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gunn_diode" title="Gunn diode">Gunn diodes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/IMPATT_diode" title="IMPATT diode">IMPATT diodes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Low-power sources are available as benchtop instruments, rackmount instruments, embeddable modules and in card-level formats. A <a href="/wiki/Maser" title="Maser">maser</a> is a solid state device which amplifies microwaves using similar principles to the <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a>, which amplifies higher frequency light waves.</p>
<p>All warm objects emit low level microwave <a href="/wiki/Black-body_radiation" title="Black-body radiation">black-body radiation</a>, depending on their <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, so in meteorology and <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a> <a href="/wiki/Microwave_radiometer" title="Microwave radiometer">microwave radiometers</a> are used to measure the temperature of objects or terrain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sisodia_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sisodia-4">[4]</a></sup> The sun<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liou_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liou-5">[5]</a></sup> and other astronomical radio sources such as <a href="/wiki/Cassiopeia_A" title="Cassiopeia A">Cassiopeia A</a> emit low level microwave radiation which carries information about their makeup, which is studied by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_astronomer" title="Radio astronomer">radio astronomers</a> using receivers called <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescopes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sisodia_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sisodia-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation" title="Cosmic microwave background radiation">cosmic microwave background radiation</a> (CMBR), for example, is a weak microwave noise filling empty space which is a major source of information on <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">cosmology</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> theory of the origin of the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">Universe</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microwave_uses">Microwave uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microwave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Microwave uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Microwave technology is extensively used for <a href="/wiki/Point-to-point_(telecommunications)" title="Point-to-point (telecommunications)">point-to-point telecommunications</a> (i.e. non-broadcast uses). Microwaves are especially suitable for this use since they are more easily focused into narrower beams than radio waves, allowing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_reuse" title="Frequency reuse">frequency reuse</a>; their comparatively higher frequencies allow broad <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">bandwidth</a> and high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Data_transmission_rate" title="Data transmission rate">data transmission rates</a>, and antenna sizes are smaller than at lower frequencies because antenna size is inversely proportional to transmitted frequency. Microwaves are used in spacecraft communication, and much of the world's data, TV, and telephone communications are transmitted long distances by microwaves between ground stations and <a href="/wiki/Communications_satellite" title="Communications satellite">communications satellites</a>. Microwaves are also employed in <a href="/wiki/Microwave_oven" title="Microwave oven">microwave ovens</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> technology.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Communication">Communication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microwave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Communication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Point-to-point_(telecommunications)" title="Point-to-point (telecommunications)">Point-to-point (telecommunications)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microwave_transmission" title="Microwave transmission">Microwave transmission</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_communications" title="Satellite communications">Satellite communications</a></div>
<p>Before the advent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiber-optic" title="Fiber-optic">fiber-optic</a> transmission, most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long-distance_call" title="Long-distance call">long-distance</a> <a href="/wiki/Telephone_call" title="Telephone call">telephone calls</a> were carried via networks of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microwave_radio_relay" title="Microwave radio relay">microwave radio relay</a> links run by carriers such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AT%26T_Long_Lines" title="AT&amp;T Long Lines">AT&amp;T Long Lines</a>. Starting in the early 1950s, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_division_multiplex" title="Frequency division multiplex">frequency division multiplex</a> was used to send up to 5,400 telephone channels on each microwave radio channel, with as many as ten radio channels combined into one antenna for the <i>hop</i> to the next site, up to 70Â km away.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wireless_LAN" title="Wireless LAN">Wireless LAN</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protocol_(computing)" title="Protocol (computing)">protocols</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth" title="Bluetooth">Bluetooth</a> and the <a href="/wiki/IEEE_Standards_Association" title="IEEE Standards Association">IEEE</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.11" title="802.11">802.11</a> specifications used for Wi-Fi, also use microwaves in the 2.4Â GHz <a href="/wiki/ISM_band" title="ISM band">ISM band</a>, although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.11a" title="802.11a">802.11a</a> uses <a href="/wiki/ISM_band" title="ISM band">ISM band</a> and <a href="/wiki/U-NII" title="U-NII">U-NII</a> frequencies in the 5Â GHz range. Licensed long-range (up to about 25Â km) Wireless Internet Access services have been used for almost a decade in many countries in the 3.5â4.0Â GHz range. The FCC recently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2011)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> carved out spectrum for carriers that wish to offer services in this range in the U.S. â with emphasis on 3.65Â GHz. Dozens of service providers across the country are securing or have already received licenses from the FCC to operate in this band. The WIMAX service offerings that can be carried on the 3.65Â GHz band will give business customers another option for connectivity.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_area_network" title="Metropolitan area network">Metropolitan area network</a> (MAN) protocols, such as <a href="/wiki/WiMAX" title="WiMAX">WiMAX</a> (Worldwide Interoperability for Microwave Access) are based on standards such as <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.16" title="IEEE 802.16">IEEE 802.16</a>, designed to operate between 2 and 11Â GHz. Commercial implementations are in the 2.3Â GHz, 2.5Â GHz, 3.5Â GHz and 5.8Â GHz ranges.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_Broadband" title="Mobile Broadband">Mobile Broadband</a> Wireless Access (MBWA) protocols based on standards specifications such as <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.20" title="IEEE 802.20">IEEE 802.20</a> or ATIS/ANSI <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HC-SDMA" title="HC-SDMA">HC-SDMA</a> (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBurst" title="IBurst">iBurst</a>) operate between 1.6 and 2.3Â GHz to give mobility and in-building penetration characteristics similar to mobile phones but with vastly greater spectral efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phone</a> networks, like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_System_for_Mobile_Communications#Radio_interface" title="Global System for Mobile Communications">GSM</a>, use the low-microwave/high-UHF frequencies around 1.8 and 1.9Â GHz in the Americas and elsewhere, respectively. <a href="/wiki/DVB-SH" title="DVB-SH">DVB-SH</a> and <a href="/wiki/S-DMB" title="S-DMB">S-DMB</a> use 1.452 to 1.492Â GHz, while proprietary/incompatible <a href="/wiki/Satellite_radio" title="Satellite radio">satellite radio</a> in the U.S. uses around 2.3Â GHz for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_Audio_Radio_Service" title="Digital Audio Radio Service">DARS</a>.</p>
<p>Microwave radio is used in <a href="/wiki/Broadcasting" title="Broadcasting">broadcasting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunication</a> transmissions because, due to their short wavelength, highly <a href="/wiki/Directional_antenna" title="Directional antenna">directional antennas</a> are smaller and therefore more practical than they would be at longer wavelengths (lower frequencies). There is also more <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">bandwidth</a> in the microwave spectrum than in the rest of the radio spectrum; the usable bandwidth below 300Â MHz is less than 300Â MHz while many GHz can be used above 300Â MHz. Typically, microwaves are used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_news" title="Television news">television news</a> to transmit a signal from a remote location to a television station from a specially equipped van. See <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_auxiliary_service" title="Broadcast auxiliary service">broadcast auxiliary service</a> (BAS), <a href="/wiki/Remote_pickup_unit" title="Remote pickup unit">remote pickup unit</a> (RPU), and <a href="/wiki/Studio/transmitter_link" title="Studio/transmitter link">studio/transmitter link</a> (STL).</p>
<p>Most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_communications" title="Satellite communications">satellite communications</a> systems operate in the C, X, K<sub>a</sub>, or K<sub>u</sub> bands of the microwave spectrum. These frequencies allow large bandwidth while avoiding the crowded UHF frequencies and staying below the atmospheric absorption of EHF frequencies. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_TV" title="Satellite TV">Satellite TV</a> either operates in the C band for the traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TVRO" title="TVRO">large dish</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fixed_satellite_service" title="Fixed satellite service">fixed satellite service</a> or K<sub>u</sub> band for <a href="/wiki/Direct-broadcast_satellite" title="Direct-broadcast satellite">direct-broadcast satellite</a>. Military communications run primarily over X or K<sub>u</sub>-band links, with K<sub>a</sub> band being used for <a href="/wiki/Milstar" title="Milstar">Milstar</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Navigation">Navigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microwave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Navigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Satellite_navigation" title="Satellite navigation">Satellite navigation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">Navigation</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Navigation_Satellite_System" title="Global Navigation Satellite System">Global Navigation Satellite Systems</a> (GNSS) including the Chinese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beidou_navigation_system" title="Beidou navigation system">Beidou</a>, the American <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">Global Positioning System</a>((introduced in 1978)GPS) and the Russian <a href="/wiki/GLONASS" title="GLONASS">GLONASS</a> broadcast navigational signals in various bands between about 1.2Â GHz and 1.6Â GHz.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">Radar</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">Radar</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Radiolocation" title="Radiolocation">radiolocation</a> technique in which a beam of radio waves emitted by a transmitter bounces off an object and returns to a receiver, allowing the location, range, speed, and other characteristics of the object to be determined. The short wavelength of microwaves causes large reflections from objects the size of motor vehicles, ships and aircraft. Also, at these wavelengths, the high gain antennas such as <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_antenna" title="Parabolic antenna">parabolic antennas</a> which are required to produce the narrow beamwidths needed to accurately locate objects are conveniently small, allowing them to be rapidly turned to scan for objects. Therefore microwave frequencies are the main frequencies used in radar. Microwave radar is widely used for applications such as <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control" title="Air traffic control">air traffic control</a>, weather forecasting, navigation of ships, and <a href="/wiki/Speed_limit_enforcement" title="Speed limit enforcement">speed limit enforcement</a>. Long distance radars use the lower microwave frequencies since at the upper end of the band atmospheric absorption limits the range, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millimeter_wave" title="Millimeter wave">millimeter waves</a> are used for short range radar such as <a href="/wiki/Collision_avoidance_system" title="Collision avoidance system">collision avoidance systems</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_astronomy">Radio astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microwave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Radio astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a></div>
<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a> uses microwaves. Usually the naturally occurring microwave radiation is observed, but active radar experiments have also been done with objects in the solar system, such as determining the distance to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> or mapping the invisible surface of <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> through cloud cover.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Large_Millimeter_Array" title="Atacama Large Millimeter Array">Atacama Large Millimeter Array</a>, located at more than 5,000 meters (16,597Â ft) altitude in Chile, observes the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Terahertz_radiation" title="Terahertz radiation">millimetre and submillimetre</a> wavelength ranges. The world's largest ground-based astronomy project to date consists of more than 66 dishes and was built in an international collaboration by Europe, North America, East Asia and Chile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation" title="Cosmic microwave background radiation">cosmic microwave background radiation</a> (CMBR) has been mapped by a number of instrument at an ever-increasing resolution. The CMBR is understood to be a "relic radiation" from the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a>. Due to the expansion and thus cooling of the Universe, the originally high-energy radiation has been shifted into the microwave region of the radio spectrum. Sufficiently sensitive <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescopes</a> can detected the CMBR as a faint background glow, almost exactly the same in all directions, that is not associated with any star, galaxy, or other object.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heating_and_power_application">Heating and power application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microwave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Heating and power application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Microwave_oven" title="Microwave oven">microwave oven</a> passes (non-ionizing) microwave radiation at a frequency near <a href="/wiki/ISM_band" title="ISM band">2.45Â GHz (12Â cm)</a> through food, causing <a href="/wiki/Dielectric_heating" title="Dielectric heating">dielectric heating</a> primarily by absorption of the energy in water. Microwave ovens became common kitchen appliances in Western countries in the late 1970s, following the development of less expensive <a href="/wiki/Cavity_magnetron" title="Cavity magnetron">cavity magnetrons</a>. Water in the liquid state possesses many molecular interactions that broaden the absorption peak. In the vapor phase, isolated water molecules absorb at around 22Â GHz, almost ten times the frequency of the microwave oven.</p>
<p>Microwave heating is used in industrial processes for drying and <a href="/wiki/Curing_(chemistry)" title="Curing (chemistry)">curing</a> products.</p>
<p>Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fabrication_(semiconductor)" title="Fabrication (semiconductor)">semiconductor processing</a> techniques use microwaves to generate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_physics" title="Plasma physics">plasma</a> for such purposes as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reactive_ion_etching" title="Reactive ion etching">reactive ion etching</a> and plasma-enhanced <a href="/wiki/Chemical_vapor_deposition" title="Chemical vapor deposition">chemical vapor deposition</a> (PECVD).</p>
<p>Microwave frequencies typically ranging from 110 â 140Â GHz are used in <a href="/wiki/Stellarator" title="Stellarator">stellarators</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tokamak#Radio-frequency_heating" title="Tokamak">tokamak</a> experimental fusion reactors to help heat the fuel into a plasma state. The upcoming <a href="/wiki/ITER" title="ITER">ITER</a> thermonuclear reactor<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> is expected to range from 110â170Â GHz and will employ electron cyclotron resonance heating (ECRH).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Microwaves can be used to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microwave_power_transmission" title="Microwave power transmission">transmit power</a> over long distances, and post-<a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> research was done to examine possibilities. <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> worked in the 1970s and early 1980s to research the possibilities of using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_power_satellite" title="Solar power satellite">solar power satellite</a> (SPS) systems with large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_module" title="Photovoltaic module">solar arrays</a> that would beam power down to the Earth's surface via microwaves.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Less-than-lethal" title="Less-than-lethal">Less-than-lethal</a> weaponry exists that uses millimeter waves to heat a thin layer of human skin to an intolerable temperature so as to make the targeted person move away. A two-second burst of the 95Â GHz focused beam heats the skin to a temperature of 54Â Â°C (129Â Â°F) at a depth of 0.4 millimetres (<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>â<sub>64</sub></span>Â in). The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">Marines</a> are currently using this type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_denial_system" title="Active denial system">active denial system</a> in fixed installations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spectroscopy">Spectroscopy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microwave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Spectroscopy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Microwave radiation is used in <a href="/wiki/Electron_paramagnetic_resonance" title="Electron paramagnetic resonance">electron paramagnetic resonance</a> (EPR or ESR) spectroscopy, typically in the X-band region (~9Â GHz) in conjunction typically with <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a> of 0.3 T. This technique provides information on unpaired <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> in chemical systems, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radical" title="Free radical">free radicals</a> or <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a> ions such as Cu(II). Microwave radiation is also used to perform <a href="/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy" title="Rotational spectroscopy">rotational spectroscopy</a> and can be combined with <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a> as in <a href="/wiki/Microwave_enhanced_electrochemistry" title="Microwave enhanced electrochemistry">microwave enhanced electrochemistry</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microwave_frequency_measurement">Microwave frequency measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microwave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Microwave frequency measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ondamtr.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1243" data-file-width="832" height="254" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Ondamtr.JPG/170px-Ondamtr.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Ondamtr.JPG/255px-Ondamtr.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Ondamtr.JPG/340px-Ondamtr.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ondamtr.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Absorption_wavemeter" title="Absorption wavemeter">Absorption wavemeter</a> for measuring in the K<sub>u</sub> band.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Microwave frequency can be measured by either electronic or mechanical techniques.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Frequency_counter" title="Frequency counter">Frequency counters</a> or high frequency <a href="/wiki/Heterodyne" title="Heterodyne">heterodyne</a> systems can be used. Here the unknown frequency is compared with harmonics of a known lower frequency by use of a low frequency generator, a harmonic generator and a mixer. Accuracy of the measurement is limited by the accuracy and stability of the reference source.</p>
<p>Mechanical methods require a tunable resonator such as an <a href="/wiki/Absorption_wavemeter" title="Absorption wavemeter">absorption wavemeter</a>, which has a known relation between a physical dimension and frequency.</p>
<p>In a laboratory setting, <a href="/wiki/Lecher_lines" title="Lecher lines">Lecher lines</a> can be used to directly measure the wavelength on a transmission line made of parallel wires, the frequency can then be calculated. A similar technique is to use a slotted <a href="/wiki/Waveguide" title="Waveguide">waveguide</a> or slotted coaxial line to directly measure the wavelength. These devices consist of a probe introduced into the line through a longitudinal slot, so that the probe is free to travel up and down the line. Slotted lines are primarily intended for measurement of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voltage_standing_wave_ratio" title="Voltage standing wave ratio">voltage standing wave ratio</a> on the line. However, provided a <a href="/wiki/Standing_wave" title="Standing wave">standing wave</a> is present, they may also be used to measure the distance between the <a href="/wiki/Node_(physics)" title="Node (physics)">nodes</a>, which is equal to half the wavelength. Precision of this method is limited by the determination of the nodal locations.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_health">Effects on health <span id="Health_effects"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microwave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Effects on health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation_and_health" title="Electromagnetic radiation and health">Electromagnetic radiation and health</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microwave_burn" title="Microwave burn">Microwave burn</a></div>
<p>Microwaves do not contain sufficient energy to chemically change substances by ionization, and so are an example of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-ionizing" title="Non-ionizing">non-ionizing</a> radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HyperPhysics_Radiation_Interaction_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-HyperPhysics_Radiation_Interaction-17">[17]</a></sup> The word "radiation" refers to energy radiating from a source and not to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactivity" title="Radioactivity">radioactivity</a>. It has not been shown conclusively that microwaves (or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-ionizing" title="Non-ionizing">non-ionizing</a> electromagnetic radiation) have significant adverse biological effects at low levels. Some, but not all, studies suggest that long-term exposure may have a <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogenic</a> effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This is separate from the risks associated with very high-intensity exposure, which can cause heating and burns like any heat source, and not a unique property of microwaves specifically.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, it was observed that individuals in the radiation path of radar installations experienced clicks and buzzing sounds in response to microwave radiation. This <a href="/wiki/Microwave_auditory_effect" title="Microwave auditory effect">microwave auditory effect</a> was thought to be caused by the microwaves <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_induction" title="Electromagnetic induction">inducing</a> an electric current in the hearing centers of the brain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Research by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> in the 1970s has shown this to be caused by thermal expansion in parts of the inner ear. In 1955 Dr. James Lovelock was able to reanimate rats frozen at 0Â Â°C using microwave diathermy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>When injury from exposure to microwaves occurs, it usually results from dielectric heating induced in the body. Exposure to microwave radiation can produce <a href="/wiki/Cataract" title="Cataract">cataracts</a> by this mechanism,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> because the microwave heating <a href="/wiki/Denaturation_(biochemistry)" title="Denaturation (biochemistry)">denatures</a> <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Lens_(anatomy)" title="Lens (anatomy)">crystalline lens</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">eye</a> (in the same way that heat turns <a href="/wiki/Egg_white" title="Egg white">egg whites</a> white and opaque). The lens and <a href="/wiki/Cornea" title="Cornea">cornea</a> of the eye are especially vulnerable because they contain no <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessels</a> that can carry away heat. Exposure to heavy doses of microwave radiation (as from an oven that has been tampered with to allow operation even with the door open) can produce heat damage in other tissues as well, up to and including serious <a href="/wiki/Burn" title="Burn">burns</a> that may not be immediately evident because of the tendency for microwaves to heat deeper tissues with higher moisture content.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Eleanor_R._Adair" title="Eleanor R. Adair">Eleanor R. Adair</a> conducted microwave health research by exposing herself, animals and humans to microwave levels that made them feel warm or even start to sweat and feel quite uncomfortable. She found no adverse health effects other than heat.</p>

<p>The existence of radio waves was predicted by <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a> in 1864 from <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations" title="Maxwell's equations">his equations</a>. In 1888, <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Hertz" title="Heinrich Hertz">Heinrich Hertz</a> was the first to demonstrate the existence of radio waves by building a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spark_gap_transmitter" title="Spark gap transmitter">spark gap radio transmitter</a> that produced 450Â MHz microwaves, in the UHF region. The equipment he used was primitive, including a horse trough, a wrought iron point spark, and <a href="/wiki/Leyden_jar" title="Leyden jar">Leyden jars</a>. He also built the first <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_antenna" title="Parabolic antenna">parabolic antenna</a>, using a zinc gutter sheet. In 1894, Indian radio pioneer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jagdish_Chandra_Bose" title="Jagdish Chandra Bose">Jagdish Chandra Bose</a> publicly demonstrated radio control of a bell using millimeter wavelengths, and conducted research into the propagation of microwaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Perhaps the first, documented, formal use of the term <i>microwave</i> occurred in 1931:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"When trials with wavelengths as low as 18 cm were made known, there was undisguised surprise that the problem of the micro-wave had been solved so soon." <i>Telegraph &amp; Telephone Journal</i> XVII. 179/1</dd>
</dl>
<p>In 1943, the Hungarian engineer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zolt%C3%A1n_Bay" title="ZoltÃ¡n Bay">ZoltÃ¡n Bay</a> sent ultra-short radio waves to the moon, which, reflected from there, worked as a radar, and could be used to measure distance, as well as to study the moon.</p>
<p>Perhaps the first use of the word <i>microwave</i> in an astronomical context occurred in 1946 in an article "Microwave Radiation from the Sun and Moon" by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Dicke" title="Robert Dicke">Robert Dicke</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Beringer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Robert Beringer (page does not exist)">Robert Beringer</a>. This same article also made a showing in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> issued in 1951. <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Weber_(engineer)" title="Ernst Weber (engineer)">Ernst Weber</a> pioneered microwave technologies.</p>
