<p><b>Michael Norman Manley</b> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Nation" title="Order of the Nation">ON</a> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Caribbean_Community" title="Order of the Caribbean Community">OCC</a> (10 December 1924 – 6 March 1997) was a <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaican</a> politician who served as the fourth <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Jamaica" title="Prime Minister of Jamaica">Prime Minister of Jamaica</a> from 1972 to 1980 and from 1989 to 1992. Coming from a prosperous background, Manley was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_socialist" title="Democratic socialist">democratic socialist</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> According to opinion polls, he remains one of Jamaica's most popular Prime Ministers since independence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-political_career">Pre-political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Manley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pre-political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Manley, the second son of politician <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_Washington_Manley" title="Norman Washington Manley">Norman Washington Manley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edna_Manley" title="Edna Manley">Edna Manley</a>, attended <a href="/wiki/Jamaica_College" title="Jamaica College">Jamaica College</a> and then served in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Air_Force" title="Royal Canadian Air Force">Royal Canadian Air Force</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. In 1945, he enrolled at the <a href="/wiki/London_School_of_Economics" title="London School of Economics">London School of Economics</a>. He graduated in 1949, and returned to Jamaica to serve as an editor and columnist for the newspaper <i>Public Opinion</i>. At about the same time, he became involved in the <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade union</a> movement, becoming a <a href="/wiki/Negotiation" title="Negotiation">negotiator</a> for the <a href="/wiki/National_Workers_Union_(Jamaica)" title="National Workers Union (Jamaica)">National Workers Union</a>. In August 1953, he became a full-time official of that union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lentz_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lentz-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Entry_into_politics">Entry into politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Manley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Entry into politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When his father was elected prime minister of Jamaica in 1955, Manley resisted entering politics, not wanting to be seen as capitalizing on his family name. However, in 1962 he accepted an appointment to the Senate of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Jamaica" title="Parliament of Jamaica">Parliament of Jamaica</a>. He won a very close election to the Jamaican House of Representatives in 1967.</p>
<p>After his father's retirement in 1969, Manley was elected leader of the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_National_Party" title="People's National Party">People's National Party</a>, defeating <a href="/wiki/Vivian_Blake_(politician)" title="Vivian Blake (politician)">Vivian Blake</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Waters_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Waters-4">[4]</a></sup> He then served as <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Opposition_(Jamaica)" title="Leader of the Opposition (Jamaica)">leader of the Opposition</a>, until his party won in the general elections of 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lentz_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lentz-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestic_reforms">Domestic reforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Manley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Domestic reforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the election of 1972, Manley defeated the unpopular incumbent Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Shearer" title="Hugh Shearer">Hugh Shearer</a>, running on the slogans "<i>Better must come</i>", "<i>Giving power to the people</i>" and leading "<i>a government of truth</i>".</p>
<p>He instituted a series of socio-economic reforms that produced mixed results. Though he was a Jamaican from an elite family, Manley's successful <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade union</a> background helped him to maintain a close relationship with the country's poor majority, and he was a dynamic, popular leader. Unlike his father, who had a reputation for being formal and businesslike, the younger Manley moved easily among people of all strata and made Parliament accessible to the people by abolishing the requirement for men to wear <a href="/wiki/Jacket" title="Jacket">jackets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Necktie" title="Necktie">ties</a> to its sittings. In this regard he started a fashion revolution, often preferring the <a href="/wiki/Kariba_suit" title="Kariba suit">Kariba suit</a>, a type of formal bush or safari jacket with trousers and worn without a shirt and tie.</p>
<p>Under Manley, Jamaica established a minimum wage for all workers, including domestic workers. In 1974, Manley proposed free education from primary school to university. The introduction of universally free secondary education was a major step in removing the institutional barriers to private sector and preferred government jobs that required secondary diplomas. The PNP government in 1974 also formed the Jamaica Movement for the Advancement of Literacy (JAMAL), which administered adult education programs with the goal of involving 100,000 adults a year.</p>
<p>Land reform expanded under his administration. Historically, land tenure in Jamaica has been rather inequitable. Project Land Lease (introduced in 1973), attempted an integrated rural development approach, providing tens of thousands of small farmers with land, technical advice, inputs such as fertilizers and access to credit. An estimated 14 percent of idle land was redistributed through this program, much of which had been abandoned during the post-war urban migration and/or purchased by large bauxite companies.</p>
<p>The minimum voting age was lowered to 18 years, while equal pay for women was introduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> Maternity leave was also introduced, while the government outlawed the stigma of illegitimacy. The Masters and Servants Act was abolished, and a Labour Relations and Industrial Disputes Act provided workers and their trade unions with enhanced rights. The National Housing Trust was established, providing "the means for most employed people to own their own homes," and greatly stimulated housing construction, with more than 40,000 houses built between 1974 and 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Subsidised meals, transportation and uniforms for schoolchildren from disadvantaged backgrounds were introduced,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-6">[6]</a></sup> together with free education at primary, secondary, and tertiary levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-6">[6]</a></sup> Special employment programmes were also launched,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-7">[7]</a></sup> together with programmes designed to combat illiteracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-7">[7]</a></sup> Increases in pensions and poor relief were carried out,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaufman_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kaufman-8">[8]</a></sup> along with a reform of local government taxation, an increase in youth training,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceE_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceE-9">[9]</a></sup> an expansion of day care centres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceF_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceF-10">[10]</a></sup> and an upgrading of hospitals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceF_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceF-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>A worker's participation programme was introduced,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceD_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceD-11">[11]</a></sup> together with a new mental health law<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceE_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceE-9">[9]</a></sup> and the family court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceE_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceE-9">[9]</a></sup> Free health care for all Jamaicans was introduced, while health clinics and a paramedical system in rural areas were established. Various clinics were also set up to facilitate access to medical drugs. Spending on education was significantly increased, while the number of doctors and dentists in the country rose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceF_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceF-10">[10]</a></sup> Project Lend Lease, an agricultural programme designed to provide rural labourers and smallholders with more land through tenancy, was introduced, together with a National Youth Service Programme for high school graduates to teach in schools, vocational training, and the literacy programme, comprehensive rent and price controls, protection for workers against unfair dismissal, subsidies (in 1973) on basic food items,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceD_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceD-11">[11]</a></sup> and the automatic recognition of unions in the workplace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaufman_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kaufman-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Manley was the first Jamaican prime minister to support <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jamaican_republicanism" title="Jamaican republicanism">Jamaican republicanism</a> (the replacement of the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Jamaica" title="Monarchy of Jamaica">constitutional monarchy</a> with a republic). In 1975, his government established a commission into constitutional reform, which recommended that Jamaica become a republic. In July 1977, after a march to commemorate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morant_Bay_Rebellion" title="Morant Bay Rebellion">Morant Bay Rebellion</a>, Manley announced that Jamaica would become a republic by 1981. This did not occur, however.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burke_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-burke-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diplomacy">Diplomacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Manley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diplomacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Manley developed close friendships with several foreign leaders, foremost of whom were <a href="/wiki/Julius_Nyerere" title="Julius Nyerere">Julius Nyerere</a> of Tanzania, <a href="/wiki/Olof_Palme" title="Olof Palme">Olof Palme</a> of Sweden, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Trudeau" title="Pierre Trudeau">Pierre Trudeau</a> of Canada and <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a> of Cuba. With Cuba just 145 km (90 mi) north of Jamaica, he strengthened diplomatic relations between the two <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">island</a> nations, much to the dismay of <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> policymakers.</p>
<p>At the 1979 meeting of the <a href="/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement" title="Non-Aligned Movement">Non-Aligned Movement</a>, Manley strongly pressed for the development of what was called a natural alliance between the Non-Aligned movement and the Soviet Union to battle imperialism. In his speech he said, "All anti-imperialists know that the balance of forces in the world shifted irrevocably in 1917 when there was a movement and a man in the October Revolution, and Lenin was the man." Manley saw Cuba and the Cuban model as having much to offer both Jamaica and the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="Who says this? (September 2016)">weasel words</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In diplomatic affairs, Manley believed in respecting the different systems of government of other countries and not interfering in their internal affairs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Violence">Violence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Manley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Violence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Manley was Prime Minister when Jamaica experienced a significant escalation of its political culture of violence. Supporters of his opponent <a href="/wiki/Edward_Seaga" title="Edward Seaga">Edward Seaga</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Jamaica_Labour_Party" title="Jamaica Labour Party">Jamaica Labour Party</a> (JLP) and Manley's <a href="/wiki/People%27s_National_Party" title="People's National Party">People's National Party</a> (PNP) engaged in a bloody struggle which began before the 1976 election and ended when Seaga was installed as Prime Minister in 1980. While the violent political culture was not invented by Seaga or Manley, and had its roots in conflicts between the parties from as early as the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Two-party_system" title="Two-party system">two-party system</a> in the 1940s, political violence reached unprecedented levels in the 1970s. Indeed, the two elections accompanied by the greatest violence were those (1976 and 1980) in which Seaga was trying to unseat Manley.</p>
<p>In response to a wave of killings in 1974, Manley oversaw the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Gun_Court" title="Gun Court">Gun Court</a> Act and the Suppression of Crime Act, giving the police and the army new powers to seal off and disarm high-violence neighborhoods. The Gun Court imposed a mandatory sentence of indefinite imprisonment with hard labour for all firearms offences, and ordinarily tried cases <i>in camera</i>, without a jury. Manley declared that "There is no place in this society for the gun, now or ever."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-time-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Violence flared in January 1976 in anticipation of elections. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_of_Emergency" title="State of Emergency">State of Emergency</a> was declared by Manley's party the PNP in June and 500 people, including some prominent members of the JLP, were accused of trying to overthrow the government and were detained, without charges, in the <a href="/wiki/South_Camp_Adult_Correctional_Centre" title="South Camp Adult Correctional Centre">South Camp Prison</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Up-Park_Camp" title="Up-Park Camp">Up-Park Camp</a> military headquarters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jg_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-jg-14">[14]</a></sup> Elections were held on 15 December that year, while the state of emergency was still in effect. The PNP was returned to office. The State of Emergency continued into the next year. Extraordinary powers granted the police by the Suppression of Crime Act of 1974 continued to the end of the 1980s.</p>
<p>Violence continued to blight political life in the 1970s. Gangs armed by both parties fought for control of urban constituencies. In the election year of 1980 around 800 Jamaicans were killed. Jamaicans were particularly shocked by the violence at that time.</p>
<p>In the 1980 elections, Seaga's JLP won and he became Prime Minister.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition">Opposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Manley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Opposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Official_opposition" title="Official opposition">Leader of the Opposition</a> Manley became an outspoken critic of the new conservative administration. He strongly opposed intervention in Grenada after Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Bishop" title="Maurice Bishop">Maurice Bishop</a> was overthrown and executed. Immediately after committing Jamaican troops to <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Grenada" title="Invasion of Grenada">Grenada</a> in 1983, Seaga called a snap election – two years early – on the pretext that Dr Paul Robertson, General Secretary of the PNP, had called for his resignation. Manley, who may have been taken by surprise by the maneuver, led his party in a boycott of the elections, and so the Jamaica Labour Party won all seats in parliament against only marginal opposition in six of the sixty electoral constituencies.</p>
<p>Manley was largely opposed by the United States government for conducting close relations with Fidel Castro and Cuba during the Cold War. Manley even allowed Cuban technicians to work on Jamaican soil for projects. In 1977, Manley visited President Jimmy Carter at the White House to remedy the situation. Details of the meeting, however, were never disclosed.</p>
<p>During his period of opposition in the 1980s, Manley, a compelling speaker, travelled extensively, speaking to audiences around the world. He taught a graduate seminar and gave a series of public lectures at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>.</p>
<p>In 1986 Manley travelled to Britain and visited <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>. He attended a number of venues including the Afro Caribbean Resource Centre in <a href="/wiki/Winson_Green" title="Winson Green">Winson Green</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digbeth_Civic_Hall" title="Digbeth Civic Hall">Digbeth Civic Hall</a>. The mainly black audiences turned out <i>en masse</i> to hear Manley speak.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, Seaga's failure to deliver on his promises to the US and foreign investors, as well as complaints of governmental incompetence in the wake <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilbert" title="Hurricane Gilbert">Hurricane Gilbert</a>'s devastation in 1988, also contributed to his defeat by the popular Manley in the <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_general_election,_1989" title="Jamaican general election, 1989">1989 elections</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Re-election">Re-election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Manley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Re-election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By 1989 Manley had softened his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist" title="Socialist">socialist</a> rhetoric, explicitly advocating a role for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Private_enterprise" title="Private enterprise">private enterprise</a>. With the fall of the Soviet Union, he also ceased his support for a variety of international causes. In the election of that year he campaigned on a very moderate platform. Seaga's administration had fallen out of favour – both with the electorate and the US – and the PNP was elected. He was sworn in as a Member of the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Council of the United Kingdom</a> in 1989.</p>
<p>Manley's second term focused on liberalizing Jamaica's economy, with the pursuit of a free-market programme that stood in marked contrast to the interventionist economic policies pursued by Manley's first government. Various measures were, however, undertaken to cushion the negative effects of liberalisation. A Social Support Programme was introduced to provide welfare assistance for poor Jamaicans. In addition, the programme focused on creating direct employment, training, and credit for much of the population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceD_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceD-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The government also announced a 50% increase in the amount of food stamps for the most vulnerable groups (including pregnant women, nursing mothers, and children) was announced. A small number of community councils were also created. In addition, a limited land reform programme was carried out that leased and sold land to small farmers, and land plots were granted to hundreds of farmers. The government also had an admirable record in housing provision, while measures were also taken to protect consumers from illegal and unfair business practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceD_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceD-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992, citing health reasons, Manley stepped down as Prime Minister and PNP leader. His former Deputy Prime Minister, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Percival_Patterson" title="Percival Patterson">Percival Patterson</a>, assumed both offices.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Manley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Michael Manley was married five times. In 1946 he married Jacqueline Kamellard but the marriage was dissolved in 1951. Manley then married Thelma Verity in 1955; in 1960 this marriage was also dissolved. In 1966 Manley married Barbara Lewars (died in 1968); in 1972 he married Beverley Anderson but the marriage was dissolved in 1990. Beverly wrote <i>The Manley Memoirs</i> in June 2008.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Michael Manley's final marriage was to Glynne Ewart in 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Manley had five children from his five marriages: <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Manley" title="Rachel Manley">Rachel Manley</a>, Joseph Manley, Sarah Manley, Natasha Manley and David Manley.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement_and_death">Retirement and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Manley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Retirement and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Manley wrote seven books, including the award-winning <i>A History of West Indies Cricket</i>, in which he discussed the links between cricket and West Indian <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a>.</p>
<p>Michael Manley died of prostate cancer on 6 March 1997, the same day as another <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> politician, <a href="/wiki/Cheddi_Jagan" title="Cheddi Jagan">Cheddi Jagan</a> of Guyana. He is interred at the <a href="/wiki/National_Heroes_Park" title="National Heroes Park">National Heroes Park</a>, where his father Norman Manley is also interred.</p>
