<p><b>Michael Moriarty</b> (born April 5, 1941) is an American-Canadian stage and screen actor and a jazz musician. In his first acting role, on American television, he received an <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Award</a> and <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award" title="Golden Globe Award">Golden Globe Award</a>, for his portrayal of a <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi</a> <a href="/wiki/Schutzstaffel" title="Schutzstaffel">SS</a> officer, in the 1978 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mini-series" title="Mini-series">mini-series</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Holocaust_(miniseries)" title="Holocaust (miniseries)">Holocaust</a></i> and played Executive Assistant District Attorney <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benjamin_Stone_(character)" title="Benjamin Stone (character)">Benjamin Stone</a> for the first four seasons (1990-1994), on the television show <i><a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order" title="Law &amp; Order">Law &amp; Order</a>.</i> Moriarty is also, known for his roles in films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Bang_the_Drum_Slowly_(film)" title="Bang the Drum Slowly (film)">Bang the Drum Slowly</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Who%27ll_Stop_the_Rain" title="Who'll Stop the Rain">Who'll Stop the Rain</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(film)" title="Q (film)">Q: The Winged Serpent</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Stuff" title="The Stuff">The Stuff</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pale_Rider" title="Pale Rider">Pale Rider</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Troll_(film)" title="Troll (film)">Troll</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Courage_Under_Fire" title="Courage Under Fire">Courage Under Fire</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Shiloh_(film)" title="Shiloh (film)">Shiloh</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Moriarty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Moriarty, an Irish-American,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detroit,_Michigan" title="Detroit, Michigan">Detroit, Michigan</a>, the son of Elinor (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Married_and_maiden_names" title="Married and maiden names">née</a> Paul) and George Moriarty,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> a surgeon. His grandfather <a href="/wiki/George_Moriarty" title="George Moriarty">George Moriarty</a> was a third baseman, umpire, and manager in the major leagues for nearly 40 years.</p>
<p>Moriarty attended middle school at <a href="/wiki/Cranbrook_Schools" title="Cranbrook Schools">Cranbrook School</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bloomfield_Hills" title="Bloomfield Hills">Bloomfield Hills</a> before transferring to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Detroit_Jesuit_High_School" title="University of Detroit Jesuit High School">University of Detroit Jesuit High School</a> for high school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He then matriculated at <a href="/wiki/Dartmouth_College" title="Dartmouth College">Dartmouth College</a> in the class of 1963, where he was a theatre college major. After receiving his degree, he left for London, England, where he enrolled in the <a href="/wiki/London_Academy_of_Music_and_Dramatic_Art" title="London Academy of Music and Dramatic Art">London Academy of Music and Dramatic Art</a>, after receiving a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fulbright_Scholarship" title="Fulbright Scholarship">Fulbright Scholarship</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acting_career">Acting career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Moriarty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Acting career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before gaining fame in films, Moriarty worked for several years as an actor at the Guthrie Theater in Minneapolis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irish_Examiner_USA_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Irish_Examiner_USA-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1973, Moriarty was cast as the egocentric <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wiggen" title="Henry Wiggen">Henry Wiggen</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Bang_the_Drum_Slowly_(film)" title="Bang the Drum Slowly (film)">Bang the Drum Slowly</a></i>, a film about the unlikely friendship between two baseball teammates – the second being <a href="/wiki/Robert_De_Niro" title="Robert De Niro">Robert De Niro</a>, a slow-thinking catcher who becomes terminally ill. In the same year, Moriarty starred in a TV movie adaptation of Tennessee Williams' <i><a href="/wiki/The_Glass_Menagerie_(1973_film)" title="The Glass Menagerie (1973 film)">The Glass Menagerie</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Katharine_Hepburn" title="Katharine Hepburn">Katharine Hepburn</a>. Coincidentally, the film also featured <a href="/wiki/Sam_Waterston" title="Sam Waterston">Sam Waterston</a>, who later replaced Moriarty as the Executive Assistant District Attorney on <i><a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order" title="Law &amp; Order">Law &amp; Order</a></i>. Moriarty's role in <i>The Glass Menagerie</i> (as "Jim," the Gentleman Caller; Waterston played the son "Tom") won him an <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Award</a> for Best Supporting Actor of the Year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1974, Moriarty starred as rookie detective Bo Lockley in the acclaimed gritty police drama <i><a href="/wiki/Report_to_the_Commissioner" title="Report to the Commissioner">Report to the Commissioner</a></i>.</p>
<p>Moriarty won a <a href="/wiki/Tony_Award" title="Tony Award">Tony Award</a> in 1974 for his performance in the play <i>Find Your Way Home</i>. His career on the screen was slow to develop, while his theatre career was flourishing. He starred as a German SS officer in the television miniseries <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holocaust_(TV_miniseries)" title="Holocaust (TV miniseries)">Holocaust</a></i>, which earned him another Emmy. Through the 1980s, Moriarty starred in such <a href="/wiki/Larry_Cohen" title="Larry Cohen">Larry Cohen</a> movies as <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(film)" title="Q (film)">Q</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Stuff" title="The Stuff">The Stuff</a></i>, <i>It's Alive III: Island of the Alive</i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/A_Return_to_Salem%27s_Lot" title="A Return to Salem's Lot">A Return to Salem's Lot</a></i> (much later, he appeared in Cohen's <i>Masters of Horror</i> episode "<a href="/wiki/Pick_Me_Up_(Masters_of_Horror)" title="Pick Me Up (Masters of Horror)">Pick Me Up</a>"), as well as Clint Eastwood's <i><a href="/wiki/Pale_Rider" title="Pale Rider">Pale Rider</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Hanoi_Hilton" title="The Hanoi Hilton">The Hanoi Hilton</a></i>. In 1986, he starred in the fantasy science fiction movie <i><a href="/wiki/Troll_(film)" title="Troll (film)">Troll</a></i>, playing the role of Harry Potter Sr. (unrelated to the 2001 Harry Potter series.)</p>
<p>In 1989, Michael Moriarty starred in the HBO production "Tailspin – Behind the Korean Airliner Tragedy", which dramatized the Soviet Union's shoot-down of Korean Air Lines flight 007 in 1983. He portrayed U.S. Air Force Major Hank Daniels, who was largely ignored if not ridiculed for showing how the ill-fated airliner had strayed off course into airspace known by the Soviets to be used by U.S. Air Force electronic surveillance planes as they approached Soviet airspace.</p>
<p>From 1990 to 1994, Moriarty starred as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benjamin_Stone_(character)" title="Benjamin Stone (character)">Ben Stone</a> on <i>Law &amp; Order</i>. He left the show in 1994, alleging that his departure was a result of his threatening a lawsuit against then-Attorney General <a href="/wiki/Janet_Reno" title="Janet Reno">Janet Reno</a>, who had cited <i>Law &amp; Order</i> as offensively violent. Moriarty criticized Reno's comment, and claimed that not only did she want to censor shows like <i>Law &amp; Order</i> but also such fare as <i><a href="/wiki/Murder,_She_Wrote" title="Murder, She Wrote">Murder, She Wrote</a></i>. He later accused <i>Law &amp; Order</i> executive producer <a href="/wiki/Dick_Wolf" title="Dick Wolf">Dick Wolf</a> of not taking his concerns seriously, and claimed that Wolf and other network executives were "caving in" to Reno's "demands" on the issue of TV violence. On September 20, 1994 on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Howard_Stern_Show" title="The Howard Stern Show">The Howard Stern Show</a></i>, he made an offer to NBC, claiming that he would return to his role on the show if Dick Wolf was fired. Moriarty published a full-page advertisement in a Hollywood trade magazine, calling upon fellow artists to stand up with him against attempts to censor TV show content. He subsequently wrote and published <i>The Gift of Stern Angels</i>, his account of this time in his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wolf and others working on <i>Law &amp; Order</i> tell a different story, however. On November 18, 1993, Moriarty and Wolf, along with other television executives, met with Reno to dissuade her from supporting any law that would censor the show. Wolf said that Moriarty overreacted to any effect the law was likely to have on the show. <i>Law &amp; Order</i> producers claim they were forced to remove Moriarty from the series because of "erratic behavior", an example of which reportedly happened during the filming of the episode "Breeder" when, according to the episode's director, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Forney&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Arthur Forney (page does not exist)">Arthur Forney</a>, Moriarty was unable to deliver his lines with a straight face. Series and network officials deny any connection between his departure and Janet Reno. Wolf also denies that the show has become less violent, graphic or controversial since 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moriarty acted in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Detail" title="The Last Detail">The Last Detail</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Courage_Under_Fire" title="Courage Under Fire">Courage Under Fire</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Along_Came_a_Spider" title="Along Came a Spider">Along Came a Spider</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Shiloh_(film)" title="Shiloh (film)">Shiloh</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emily_of_New_Moon_(television_series)" title="Emily of New Moon (television series)">Emily of New Moon</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/James_Dean_(film)" title="James Dean (film)">James Dean</a></i>, for which he won his third <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy</a>. In 2007 he debuted his first feature-length film as screenwriter and performed the role of a man who thinks he is Adolf Hitler in <i><a href="/wiki/Hitler_Meets_Christ" title="Hitler Meets Christ">Hitler Meets Christ</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_career">Musical career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Moriarty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Musical career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to his acting career, Moriarty is a semi-professional jazz pianist and singer, as well as a classical composer. He has recorded three jazz albums (though the first, <i>Reaching Out</i>, went unreleased). He has performed live regularly in both New York and Vancouver with a jazz trio and quintet. In a 1990 concert review, <i>New York Times</i> reviewer Stephen Holden called Moriarty "a jazz pianist of considerable skill, an oddball singer with more than one vocal personality, and a writer of eccentric, jivey jazz songs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Moriarty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Moriarty is politically active, describing himself as a "centrist", and sometimes as a "realist".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moriarty announced his intention to run for the presidency in 2008 in an interview in the November 2005 issue of <i>Northwest Jazz Profile</i>, but he never formally declared his candidacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He later endorsed fellow former <i>Law &amp; Order</i> actor <a href="/wiki/Fred_Thompson" title="Fred Thompson">Fred Thompson</a> for the presidency during the 2008 Republican primaries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> as well as <a href="/wiki/Carly_Fiorina" title="Carly Fiorina">Carly Fiorina</a> during the 2016 primary election cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He has been a frequent contributor of numerous political columns to the ESR (<i>Enter Stage Right</i>) online Journal of Conservativism.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Moriarty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shortly after leaving <i>Law &amp; Order</i>, Moriarty moved to <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, declaring himself a political exile. He lived for a time in <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_Nova_Scotia" title="Halifax, Nova Scotia">Halifax, Nova Scotia</a>, where he was granted Canadian citizenship, and <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> before settling in <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>.</p>
<p>Moriarty lives in <a href="/wiki/Maple_Ridge,_British_Columbia" title="Maple Ridge, British Columbia">Maple Ridge, British Columbia</a>, where he still acts, writes and plays music. On the blog <i>Enter Stage Right</i> Moriarty writes that he was a "very bad drunk", but that as of February 1, 2004, he had been sober for three years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
