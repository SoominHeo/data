<p><b>Michael Young, Baron Young of Dartington</b> (9 August 1915 – 14 January 2002) was a British sociologist, social activist and politician who coined the term "<a href="/wiki/Meritocracy" title="Meritocracy">meritocracy</a>".</p>
<p>During an active life he was instrumental in shaping <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> thinking. When secretary of the policy committee of the Labour Party he was responsible for drafting "Let Us Face the Future", Labour's manifesto for the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1945" title="United Kingdom general election, 1945">1945 general election</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DownWith_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-DownWith-1">[1]</a></sup> was a leading protagonist on social reform, and founded or helped found a number of socially useful organisations. These include the <a href="/wiki/Which%3F" title="Which?">Consumers' Association</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Which%3F" title="Which?">Which?</a></i> magazine, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumer_Focus" title="Consumer Focus">National Consumer Council</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Open_University" title="Open University">Open University</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Extension_College" title="National Extension College">National Extension College</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Open_College_of_the_Arts" title="Open College of the Arts">Open College of the Arts</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_Line" title="Language Line">Language Line</a>, a telephone-interpreting business.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Young,_Baron_Young_of_Dartington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Michael_Young_(1915-2002)_-Highgate_Cemetery-10Aug2008.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="666" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Michael_Young_%281915-2002%29_-Highgate_Cemetery-10Aug2008.jpg/170px-Michael_Young_%281915-2002%29_-Highgate_Cemetery-10Aug2008.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Michael_Young_%281915-2002%29_-Highgate_Cemetery-10Aug2008.jpg/255px-Michael_Young_%281915-2002%29_-Highgate_Cemetery-10Aug2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Michael_Young_%281915-2002%29_-Highgate_Cemetery-10Aug2008.jpg/340px-Michael_Young_%281915-2002%29_-Highgate_Cemetery-10Aug2008.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<p>Young was born in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, the son of an Australian violinist and music critic, and an Irish <a href="/wiki/Bohemianism" title="Bohemianism">Bohemian</a> painter and actress. Until he was eight, he grew up in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, returning to England shortly before his parents' marriage broke up. He attended several schools, eventually entering <a href="/wiki/Dartington_Hall" title="Dartington Hall">Dartington Hall</a>, a new progressive school in Devon, in the 1920s. He had a long association with the small school, as student, trustee, deputy chairman and historian. He studied economics at the <a href="/wiki/London_School_of_Economics" title="London School of Economics">London School of Economics</a> then became a <a href="/wiki/Barrister" title="Barrister">barrister</a> when he applied to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Called_to_the_Bar" title="Called to the Bar">called to the Bar</a> in 1939.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career_and_thought">Political career and thought</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Young,_Baron_Young_of_Dartington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political career and thought">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, Young served as director of the <a href="/wiki/Political_and_Economic_Planning" title="Political and Economic Planning">Political and Economic Planning</a> <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tank</a> and became director of research for the Labour Party where he wrote the manifesto for the 1945 general election and the vast speakers' handbook. He served under the Labour Party government led by <a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee</a>, but left in 1950 claiming the party had run out of ideas. He called for the establishment of a <a href="/wiki/Economic_and_Social_Research_Council" title="Economic and Social Research Council">Social Science Research Council</a> and became its first director 17 years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDean2002_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDean2002-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>He began studying for a PhD at the <a href="/wiki/London_School_of_Economics" title="London School of Economics">London School of Economics</a> in 1952. His studies of housing and local government policy in East London left him disillusioned with the state of community relations and local Labour councillors. This prompted him to found the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Community_Studies" title="Institute of Community Studies">Institute of Community Studies</a>, which was his principal vehicle for exploring his ideas of social reform. Its basic tenet was to give people more say in running their lives and institutions. Butler argues that it drew upon existing bodies of research in social psychology and sociology to highlight the relevance of the extended family in modern society and to offer a model of socialist citizenship, solidarity and mutual support not tied to productive work. Young promoted the supportive kinship networks of the urban working class, and an idealized conception of the relationships between women, to suggest that family had been overlooked by the left and should be reclaimed as a progressive force. The goal was to strengthen the working-class family as set it up as a model for cooperative socialism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>He also founded the Mutual Aid Centre at this time. Young co-authored with Peter Willmott <i><a href="/wiki/Family_and_Kinship_in_East_London" title="Family and Kinship in East London">Family and Kinship in East London</a></i>, documenting and analysing the social costs of rehousing a tight-knit community in a suburban housing estate (known affectionately by sociologists as <i>Fakinel</i> and invariably pronounced with a <a href="/wiki/Cockney" title="Cockney">cockney</a> accent).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[note 1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1958, Young also wrote the influential satire <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rise_of_the_Meritocracy" title="The Rise of the Meritocracy">The Rise of the Meritocracy</a></i>, originally for the <a href="/wiki/Fabian_Society" title="Fabian Society">Fabian Society</a>, which refused to publish it. In it he coined the word "<a href="/wiki/Meritocracy" title="Meritocracy">meritocracy</a>," to which he gave negative connotations, and he became disappointed with how the concept came to be seen as an achievable concept worth pursuing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DownWith_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-DownWith-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Young's social research also contributed to the change in secondary education that led to widespread abolition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammar_schools" title="Grammar schools">grammar schools</a> and their replacement by comprehensive schools between 1965 and 1976, as well as the abolition of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eleven_plus_exam" title="Eleven plus exam">11-plus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Verification of this source citation failed to find it supporting or even relevant to the material that cites it (February 2016)">irrelevant citation</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the 1950s and 1960s Young helped to found the <a href="/wiki/Which%3F" title="Which?">Consumers' Association</a> and the National Consumer Council claiming that "politics will become less and less the politics of production, and more and more the politics of consumption", presenting the ideas in a book <i>The Chipped White Cups Of Dover</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDean2002_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDean2002-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1960 he started the Advisory Centre for Education<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> the National Extension College and, with <a href="/wiki/Peter_Laslett" title="Peter Laslett">Peter Laslett</a>, a dawn university on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglia_Television" title="Anglia Television">Anglia Television</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> which became prototypes of the Open University which <a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a> launched in 1964, building on his vision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDean2002_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDean2002-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the mid-1980s, Young co-founded <a href="/wiki/International_Alert" title="International Alert">International Alert</a>, together with <a href="/wiki/Leo_Kuper" title="Leo Kuper">Leo Kuper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Ennals" title="Martin Ennals">Martin Ennals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1987 he founded the Open College of the Arts, confounding critics who maintained that the arts could not be taught by distance methods. He also founded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_Line" title="Language Line">Language Line</a>, a telephone interpreting business, to enable non-English-speaking people to have equal access to public services. He fostered the work of many younger researchers and "social entrepreneurs", and founded the <a href="/wiki/School_for_Social_Entrepreneurs" title="School for Social Entrepreneurs">School for Social Entrepreneurs</a> in 1997. Aspects of Young's work are being developed by the <a href="/wiki/Young_Foundation" title="Young Foundation">Young Foundation</a>, created from the merger of his Institute of Community Studies and his Mutual Aid Centre, under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Geoff_Mulgan" title="Geoff Mulgan">Geoff Mulgan</a>.</p>
<p>Throughout his life, and particularly in later life, Young was concerned for older people. In 1982 he co-founded the <a href="/wiki/University_of_the_Third_Age" title="University of the Third Age">University of the Third Age</a> with Peter Laslett and <a href="/wiki/Eric_Midwinter" title="Eric Midwinter">Eric Midwinter</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> and Linkage, bringing together older people without grandchildren and young people without grandparents. In 2001 he co-founded the charity <a href="/wiki/Grandparents_Plus" title="Grandparents Plus">Grandparents Plus</a> to champion the role of the wider family in children's lives.</p>
<p>According to his friend <a href="/wiki/Eric_Midwinter" title="Eric Midwinter">Eric Midwinter</a>, "All his thought, all his incisive writing, all his brilliantly conceived schemes, all his astutely handled initiatives were guided by a salient method. He was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utopian_socialist" title="Utopian socialist">utopian socialist</a>. His thinking stemmed from the views of 19th century radicals like <a href="/wiki/Robert_Owen" title="Robert Owen">Robert Owen</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henri_de_Saint-Simon" title="Henri de Saint-Simon">Saint-Simon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Charles_Fourier" title="Charles Fourier">Charles Fourier</a>, with their hatred of massive institutionalism, be it in the hands of the public authority or of the large commercial company."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Young was a Fellow of <a href="/wiki/Churchill_College,_Cambridge" title="Churchill College, Cambridge">Churchill College, Cambridge</a>, from 1961 to 1966, and President of <a href="/wiki/Birkbeck,_University_of_London" title="Birkbeck, University of London">Birkbeck, University of London</a>, from 1989 to 1992.</p>
<p>Although an egalitarian, Young accepted a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_peerage" title="Life peerage">life peerage</a> on 20 March 1978, taking the title Baron Young of Dartington.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> His many projects required frequent travel to London and the peerage offered free rail travel and attendance allowance at a time when he had run out of money.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Young,_Baron_Young_of_Dartington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Young married three times. In 1945 he married Joan Lawton, with whom he had two sons and a daughter. They divorced and in 1960 he married <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sasha_Moorsom&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sasha Moorsom (page does not exist)">Sasha Moorsom</a>, a novelist, sculptor and painter with whom he had a son and a daughter. Young and Moorsom worked together on several projects, including in the <a href="/wiki/Township_(South_Africa)" title="Township (South Africa)">townships</a> of South Africa. Moorsom died in 1993 and in 1995 Young married Dorit Uhlemann, with whom he had a daughter.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Toby_Young" title="Toby Young">Toby Young</a>, Michael Young's son with Moorsom, is a journalist and writer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
