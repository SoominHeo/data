<p><b>Michael S. Ovitz</b> (born December 14, 1946) is an American businessman, investor, and philanthropist. He is a <a href="/wiki/Talent_agent" title="Talent agent">talent agent</a> who co-founded <a href="/wiki/Creative_Artists_Agency" title="Creative Artists Agency">Creative Artists Agency</a> (CAA) in 1975 and served as its chairman until 1995. Ovitz later served as President of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Company" title="Walt Disney Company">Walt Disney Company</a> from October 1995 to January 1997.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Ovitz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ovitz was born to a <a href="/wiki/American_Jews" title="American Jews">Jewish</a> family<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago, Illinois</a>, the son of a liquor wholesaler. Raised in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Encino,_California" title="Encino, California">Encino, California</a>, he attended <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_High_School" title="Birmingham High School">Birmingham High School</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_Nuys,_Los_Angeles" title="Van Nuys, Los Angeles">Van Nuys</a>, a classmate of <a href="/wiki/Sally_Field" title="Sally Field">Sally Field</a>. While a pre-med student at <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">UCLA</a>, he began his entertainment career as a part-time tour guide at <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal Studios</a>. Upon graduating from UCLA in 1968 with a degree in theater, film, and television, he secured a job in the mailroom at the <a href="/wiki/William_Morris_Agency" title="William Morris Agency">William Morris Agency</a>. Within a year he was promoted, becoming a highly successful television agent. Six years later, he and four other young colleagues left William Morris to found <a href="/wiki/Creative_Artists_Agency" title="Creative Artists Agency">Creative Artists Agency</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pocketful_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pocketful-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Creative_Artists_Agency">Creative Artists Agency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Ovitz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Creative Artists Agency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ovitz founded <a href="/wiki/Creative_Artists_Agency" title="Creative Artists Agency">Creative Artists Agency</a> in 1975 along with fellow William Morris Agents <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Meyer" title="Ronald Meyer">Ron Meyer</a>, Bill Haber, Rowland Perkins, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mike_Rosenfeld" title="Mike Rosenfeld">Mike Rosenfeld</a>. Borrowing only $21,000 from a bank,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-All_Things_D_interview_by_Marc_Andressen_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-All_Things_D_interview_by_Marc_Andressen-3">[3]</a></sup> the agents rented a small office, conducting business on card tables and rented chairs, their wives taking turns as agency receptionist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pocketful_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-pocketful-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under his direction, CAA quickly grew from a start-up organization to the worldâs leading talent agency, expanding from television into film, investment banking, and advertising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MuseumOfBroadcastCommunications_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MuseumOfBroadcastCommunications-4">[4]</a></sup> Ovitz was known for assembling âpackage dealsâ, wherein CAA would utilize its talent base to provide directors, actors and screenwriters to a studio, thus shifting the negotiating leverage from the studios to the talent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As CAA rose in stature Ovitz became one of the most powerful men in Hollywood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Promoted to President, then to Chairman of the Board, his roles at CAA were numerous. He served as talent agent to Hollywood actors <a href="/wiki/Tom_Cruise" title="Tom Cruise">Tom Cruise</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dustin_Hoffman" title="Dustin Hoffman">Dustin Hoffman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Costner" title="Kevin Costner">Kevin Costner</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Belushi" title="John Belushi">John Belushi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Douglas" title="Michael Douglas">Michael Douglas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Murray" title="Bill Murray">Bill Murray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sylvester_Stallone" title="Sylvester Stallone">Sylvester Stallone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Barbra_Streisand" title="Barbra Streisand">Barbra Streisand</a>, as well as directors <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barry_Levinson" title="Barry Levinson">Barry Levinson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Pollack" title="Sydney Pollack">Sydney Pollack</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He also provided corporate consulting services, helping negotiate several major international business mergers and deals including Matsushitaâs acquisition of MCA/Universal, the financial rescue of MGM/United Artists, and Sonyâs acquisition of Columbia Pictures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> His signing of Coca-Cola as a CAA client from agency McCann-Erickson had a significant impact on the advertising industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He negotiated <a href="/wiki/David_Letterman" title="David Letterman">David Letterman</a>'s move from <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> to <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a>, chronicled in the book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Late_Shift_(book)" title="The Late Shift (book)">The Late Shift: Letterman, Leno, and the Network Battle for the Night</a></i> by Bill Carter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disney_President">Disney President</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Ovitz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Disney President">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In_re_Walt_Disney_Derivative_Litigation" title="In re Walt Disney Derivative Litigation">In re Walt Disney Derivative Litigation</a></div>
<p>Ovitz resigned from CAA in 1995 to become president of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Company" title="Walt Disney Company">Walt Disney Company</a> under chairman <a href="/wiki/Michael_Eisner" title="Michael Eisner">Michael Eisner</a>. Ovitz quickly grew frustrated with his role in the company and vague definition of duties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> After a tumultuous year as Eisner's second in command, he was dismissed by Eisner in January 1997<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and left Disney with a (previously agreed upon) severance package valued at $38 million in cash and an estimated $100 million in stock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brehm_v._Eisner_2000_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brehm_v._Eisner_2000-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Disney shareholders later sued Eisner and Disney's board of directors for awarding Ovitz such a large severance package.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brehm_v._Eisner_2000_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brehm_v._Eisner_2000-13">[13]</a></sup> Later court proceedings reflect that Ovitz' stock options were granted when he was hired to induce him to join the company, not granted when he was fired. In 2005 the court upheld Disney's payment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artist_Management_Group_and_controversy">Artist Management Group and controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Ovitz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Artist Management Group and controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In January 1999, Ovitz formed CKE, comprising four distinct companies: Artist Management Group (AMG), Artist Production Group (APG), Artist Television Group (ATG) and Lynx Technology Group (LTG). In 2002 Ovitz sold AMG to <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Kwatinetz" title="Jeff Kwatinetz">Jeff Kwatinetz</a> for an estimated $12 million, which was merged into his management group <a href="/wiki/The_Firm,_Inc." title="The Firm, Inc.">The Firm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the sale of AMG, Ovitz became the subject of controversy for remarks made in a <i><a href="/wiki/Vanity_Fair_(magazine)" title="Vanity Fair (magazine)">Vanity Fair</a></i> interview,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burrough_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burrough-16">[16]</a></sup> wherein he blamed the downfall of AMG upon a Hollywood cabal led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dreamworks" title="Dreamworks">Dreamworks</a> cofounder <a href="/wiki/David_Geffen" title="David Geffen">David Geffen</a> which Ovitz described as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gay_mafia" title="Gay mafia">gay mafia</a>" (despite the fact that most of its purported members were not gay).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burrough_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burrough-16">[16]</a></sup> In addition to Geffen, the list included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a> Hollywood correspondent Bernie Weinraub, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney" title="Disney">Disney</a> Chairman (and former employer) <a href="/wiki/Michael_Eisner" title="Michael Eisner">Michael Eisner</a>; <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Lourd" title="Bryan Lourd">Bryan Lourd</a>, Kevin Huvane, and Richard Lovett, partners at CAA, <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal Studios</a> president <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Meyer" title="Ronald Meyer">Ronald Meyer</a> (Ovitz's former partner at CAA); and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vivendi_Universal_Entertainment" title="Vivendi Universal Entertainment">Vivendi Universal Entertainment</a> CEO <a href="/wiki/Barry_Diller" title="Barry Diller">Barry Diller</a>. "If I were to establish the foundation of the negativity," Ovitz stated, "it all comes down to David Geffen and Bernie Weinraub. Everything comes back to those two. Itâs the same group [quoted] in every article."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burrough_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Burrough-16">[16]</a></sup> He later apologized for his Vanity Fair comments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Ovitz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ovitz now acts as a private investor who has informally advised the careers of luminaries such as <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">Martin Scorsese</a>. Active in philanthropy, he donated $25 million in 1999 to spearhead fund raising efforts for UCLA's Medical Center,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and has contributed significantly to numerous other philanthropic endeavors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> A private investor and businessman, his notable activities have ranged from attempts to bring an NFL team to the Los Angeles Coliseum <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> to ventures in online media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ovitz is considered among the world's top 200 art collectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> His contemporary pieces include works by <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jasper_Johns" title="Jasper Johns">Jasper Johns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willem_de_Kooning" title="Willem de Kooning">Willem de Kooning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barnett_Newman" title="Barnett Newman">Barnett Newman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Rothko" title="Mark Rothko">Mark Rothko</a>, and many others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>His daughter is New York fashion designer <a href="/wiki/Kimberly_Ovitz" title="Kimberly Ovitz">Kimberly Ovitz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015 Ovitz became engaged to <a href="/wiki/Tamara_Mellon" title="Tamara Mellon">Tamara Mellon</a> despite still being legally married to his wife Judy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
