<p><b>Michael Edward O'Hanlon</b> (born May 16, 1961) is a senior fellow at <a href="/wiki/Brookings_Institution" title="Brookings Institution">The Brookings Institution</a>, specializing in defense and <a href="/wiki/Foreign_policy" title="Foreign policy">foreign policy</a> issues. He began his career as a budget analyst in the defense field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_E._O%27Hanlon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_early_career">Education and early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_E._O%27Hanlon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education and early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>O'Hanlon earned an A.B. in 1982 (in Physics), M.S.E. in 1987, <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">M.A.</a> in 1988, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ph.D" title="Ph.D">Ph.D</a> in 1991 all from <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>, and is now a visiting lecturer there. He served as a <a href="/wiki/Peace_Corps" title="Peace Corps">Peace Corps</a> volunteer in <a href="/wiki/Kinshasa" title="Kinshasa">Kinshasa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Congo</a> in the 1980s. O'Hanlon is reasonably fluent in French, having taught physics in French in the Peace Corps for two years in the Democratic Republic of Congo in the 1980s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_E._O%27Hanlon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>O'Hanlon married Cathryn Ann Garland in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> They have two daughters. In addition to his work in the U.S. foreign policy field, O'Hanlon is an activist for people with special needs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Iraq_War">The Iraq War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_E._O%27Hanlon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Iraq War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Support_and_Caution">Support and Caution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_E._O%27Hanlon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Support and Caution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Along with Brookings scholar Philip Gordon, O'Hanlon wrote in the Washington Post in late 2001 that any invasion of Iraq would be difficult and demanding and require large numbers of troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This article led to <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Adelman" title="Kenneth Adelman">Kenneth Adelman</a>'s famous prediction of a 'cakewalk' in a subsequent rebuttal in that same newspaper, but Gordon and O'Hanlon's argument was validated by subsequent events on the ground. O'Hanlon argued at a major forum on Iraq at the <a href="/wiki/American_Enterprise_Institute" title="American Enterprise Institute">American Enterprise Institute</a> (AEI) in the fall of 2002 that an invasion of Iraq could lead to 150,000 U.S. troops remaining in that country for 5 years, while expressing his view that a war should occur only if inspections failed to fully confirm the disarmament of Saddam's stocks of weapons of mass destruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>By late 2002 and early 2003, O’Hanlon appeared in the American media as a public proponent of the Iraq War. Interviewed by <a href="/wiki/Bill_O%27Reilly_(political_commentator)" title="Bill O'Reilly (political commentator)">Bill O'Reilly</a> in Fox News in February 2003, he was asked “Any doubt about going to war with Saddam?” To which he replied “Not much doubt.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>O'Hanlon predicted in early 2003 in the journal Orbis that an invasion of Iraq could lead to as many as several thousand American fatalities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> a prediction also confirmed by later developments. He decided in 2003 to create Brookings' Iraq Index,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> a web-based resource tracking trends in the country that has been perhaps Brookings most widely viewed site this decade, and which led to later decisions to create Afghanistan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and Pakistan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> indices at Brookings as well. Excerpts of these indices ran on a quarterly basis in the New York Times from 2004 through 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 9, 2007, O'Hanlon said during a panel discussion in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> that a "soft partition" of Iraq is already occurring that might break the country up into three autonomous regions – <a href="/wiki/Kurdistan" title="Kurdistan">Kurdistan</a>, "Shi'astan" and "Sunnistan".</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>Iraq is being ethnically segregated. <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_cleansing" title="Ethnic cleansing">Ethnic cleansing</a> is on its way, it's happening, and at least a couple million people have been displaced. It's becoming <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia</a> in some ways</i>, he added.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>In a July 30, 2007 op-ed piece in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> O'Hanlon and <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_M._Pollack" title="Kenneth M. Pollack">Kenneth M. Pollack</a>, just back from an 8-day <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">DOD</a>-scheduled itinerary in Iraq reported that:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>[A]s two analysts who have harshly criticized the Bush administration's miserable handling of Iraq, we were surprised by the gains we saw and the potential to produce not necessarily 'victory' but a sustainable stability that both we and the Iraqis could live with.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_E._O%27Hanlon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Critics however, have called into question the veracity of O'Hanlon's claim to have been a harsh critic of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_W._Bush_Administration" title="George W. Bush Administration">Bush administration</a>'s handling of Iraq, arguing that it was a deceitful assertion intended to lend the article increased credibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">attorney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Columnist" title="Columnist">columnist</a> <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Greenwald" title="Glenn Greenwald">Glenn Greenwald</a>, O'Hanlon and Pollack "were not only among the biggest cheerleaders for the war, but repeatedly praised the <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">Pentagon</a>'s strategy in Iraq and continuously assured Americans things were going well".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 25, 2007, he made an attempt to answer his critics in an Op-ed in Washington Post.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In response to the charge that he based his judgment on "dog-and-pony shows" in Baghdad, he claimed that his assessment was also informed by years of study of the situation through a large number of knowledgeable sources, including many that were reflected in the Iraq Index (and contributed to its sober message for much of the war).</p>
<p>Writing in the National Interest in May 2008, O’Hanlon gave himself 7 marks out of 10 for his predictions about Iraq, although he acknowledged that among his incorrect positions was his initial support for the war – given the Bush administration’s poor preparations for the post-Saddam period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Letter_by_Project_for_the_New_American_Century">Letter by Project for the New American Century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_E._O%27Hanlon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Letter by Project for the New American Century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>O'Hanlon signed a letter and a statement on postwar <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> published by the <a href="/wiki/Project_for_the_New_American_Century" title="Project for the New American Century">Project for the New American Century</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1-28-2005-Congress_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-1-28-2005-Congress-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SecondStatementPostwarIraq_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-SecondStatementPostwarIraq-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Major_Areas_of_Research">Other Major Areas of Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_E._O%27Hanlon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other Major Areas of Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_E._O%27Hanlon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Afghanistan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>O'Hanlon's 2010 book with Hassina Sherjan, an Afghan-American woman living in Kabul, is called <i>Toughing It Out in Afghanistan</i>. It largely explains and supports the Obama administration's decisions to focus on counterinsurgency in Afghanistan while greatly expanding the size of the US military presence there.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defense_Analysis">Defense Analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_E._O%27Hanlon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Defense Analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>O'Hanlon's other main areas of work over the years include studies on defense technology issues, such as missile defense and space weaponry and the future of nuclear weapons policy, on Northeast Asian security coauthored with experts such as Mike Mochizuki and Richard Bush, and on defense strategy and budget issues that follow a long Brookings tradition on the subject pioneered by scholars such as William Kaufmann, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Blechman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Barry Blechman (page does not exist)">Barry Blechman</a>, and Joshua Epstein.</p>
<p>Many of the analytical approaches that O'Hanlon employs in these various efforts were explained in his 2009 Princeton University Press book, <i>The Science of War</i>, which discusses methods of defense analysis – a subject that O'Hanlon currently teaches at Columbia, Princeton and Johns Hopkins, while also directing research in the foreign policy program at Brookings since 2009.</p>
