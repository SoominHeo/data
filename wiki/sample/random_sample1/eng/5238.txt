<p><b>Michael Collins</b> (born October 31, 1930), (<a href="/wiki/Major_general_(United_States)" title="Major general (United States)">Major General</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">USAF</a>, Ret.), is an <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Italian" title="Italian">Italian</a>-born <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> former <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronaut</a> and <a href="/wiki/Test_pilot" title="Test pilot">test pilot</a>. Selected as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronaut_Group_3" title="Astronaut Group 3">third group of fourteen astronauts</a> in 1963, he flew into <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">space</a> twice. His first spaceflight was on <a href="/wiki/Gemini_10" title="Gemini 10">Gemini 10</a>, in which he and Command Pilot <a href="/wiki/John_Young_(astronaut)" title="John Young (astronaut)">John Young</a> performed two rendezvous with different spacecraft and Collins undertook two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" title="Extra-vehicular activity">EVAs</a>. His second spaceflight was as the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">Command Module</a> Pilot for <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a>. While he stayed in orbit around the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">Buzz Aldrin</a> left in the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">Lunar Module</a> to make the first manned landing on its surface. He is one of <a href="/wiki/List_of_Apollo_astronauts" title="List of Apollo astronauts">24 people to have flown to the Moon</a>.</p>
<p>Collins was the fourth person, and third American, to perform an EVA; and is the first person to have performed more than one EVA.</p>
<p>Prior to becoming an astronaut, he attended the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Military_Academy" title="United States Military Academy">United States Military Academy</a>, and from there he joined the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> and flew <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-86" title="F-86">F-86s</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chambley-Bussieres_Air_Base" title="Chambley-Bussieres Air Base">Chambley-Bussieres Air Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. He was accepted to the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force_Test_Pilot_School" title="U.S. Air Force Test Pilot School">U.S. Air Force Experimental Flight Test Pilot School</a> at <a href="/wiki/Edwards_Air_Force_Base" title="Edwards Air Force Base">Edwards Air Force Base</a> in 1960. He unsuccessfully applied for the second astronaut group, but was accepted for the third group.</p>
<p>After retiring from NASA in 1970 he took a job in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">Department of State</a> as <a href="/wiki/Assistant_Secretary_of_State_for_Public_Affairs" title="Assistant Secretary of State for Public Affairs">Assistant Secretary of State for Public Affairs</a>. A year later he became the director of the <a href="/wiki/National_Air_and_Space_Museum" title="National Air and Space Museum">National Air and Space Museum</a>. He held this position until 1978 when he stepped down to become undersecretary of the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a>. In 1980 he took the job as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice_President" title="Vice President">Vice President</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LTV_Aerospace" title="LTV Aerospace">LTV Aerospace</a>. He resigned in 1985 to start his own business.</p>
<p>He was married to Patricia Collins until her death in April 2014. They had three children: <a href="/wiki/Kate_Collins_(actress)" title="Kate Collins (actress)">Kate</a> (born May 6, 1959), Ann (born October 31, 1961), and Michael (born February 23, 1963).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Childhood_and_education">Childhood and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Collins_(astronaut)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Childhood and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Collins was born in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy" title="Kingdom of Italy">Italy</a>, on October 31, 1930, to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_General" title="Major General">Major General</a> <a href="/wiki/James_Lawton_Collins" title="James Lawton Collins">James Lawton Collins</a>, who served in the army for 38 years. For the first 17 years of his life, Collins called <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>; <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>; <a href="/wiki/Governors_Island" title="Governors Island">Governors Island</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>; <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Antonio,_Texas" title="San Antonio, Texas">San Antonio, Texas</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Alexandria,_Virginia" title="Alexandria, Virginia">Alexandria, Virginia</a> home. He took his first ride in a plane<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2012)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> in Puerto Rico aboard a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grumman_Widgeon" title="Grumman Widgeon">Grumman Widgeon</a>. His father often told of how his own first plane ride had been in 1911 with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frank_Lahm" title="Frank Lahm">Frank Lahm</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup> He studied for two years in the <a href="/wiki/Academia_del_Perpetuo_Socorro" title="Academia del Perpetuo Socorro">Academia del Perpetuo Socorro</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan, Puerto Rico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the United States entered <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the family moved to <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> where Collins attended <a href="/wiki/St._Albans_School_(Washington,_D.C.)" title="St. Albans School (Washington, D.C.)">St. Albans School</a>, from which he graduated in 1948. His mother wanted him to enter into the diplomatic service, but he decided to follow his father, two uncles, brother and cousin into the armed services, and received an appointment to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Military_Academy" title="United States Military Academy">United States Military Academy</a> at <a href="/wiki/West_Point,_New_York" title="West Point, New York">West Point</a>, which also had the advantage of being free of tuition and other fees. He graduated with a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> degree, finishing 185th out of 527 cadets in 1952, the same class as fellow astronaut <a href="/wiki/Edward_Higgins_White" title="Edward Higgins White">Ed White</a>. His decision to join the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> for his active service was based on both the wonder of what the next 50 years might bring in <a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">aeronautics</a>, and also to avoid accusations of nepotism had he joined the Army where, among other things, his uncle, <a href="/wiki/J._Lawton_Collins" title="J. Lawton Collins">General J. Lawton Collins</a>, was the <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_Staff_of_the_United_States_Army" title="Chief of Staff of the United States Army">Chief of Staff of the United States Army</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:7–8</sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force_Academy" title="United States Air Force Academy">Air Force Academy</a> was in its initial construction phase, and would not graduate its first class for several years. In the interim, graduates of the Military Academy, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Academy" title="United States Naval Academy">Naval Academy</a> (such as fellow astronaut <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Patten_Stafford" title="Thomas Patten Stafford">Tom Stafford</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Merchant_Marine_Academy" title="United States Merchant Marine Academy">Merchant Marine Academy</a> were eligible for Air Force commissions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_service">Military service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Collins_(astronaut)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Military service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After entering the Air Force, Collins completed flight training at <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Air_Force_Base" title="Columbus Air Force Base">Columbus Air Force Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-6_Texan" title="T-6 Texan">T-6 Texan</a>, then moved to <a href="/wiki/San_Marcos_Municipal_Airport" title="San Marcos Municipal Airport">San Marcos Air Force Base</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Connally_Air_Force_Base" title="James Connally Air Force Base">James Connally Air Force Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. He was chosen for advanced day fighter training at <a href="/wiki/Nellis_Air_Force_Base" title="Nellis Air Force Base">Nellis Air Force Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>, flying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-86_Sabre" title="F-86 Sabre">F-86 Sabres</a>. This was followed by an assignment to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/21st_Fighter-Bomber_Wing" title="21st Fighter-Bomber Wing">21st Fighter-Bomber Wing</a> at <a href="/wiki/George_Air_Force_Base" title="George Air Force Base">George Air Force Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, where he learned how to deliver nuclear weapons. He transferred with the 21st when it was relocated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaumont-Semoutiers_Air_Base" title="Chaumont-Semoutiers Air Base">Chaumont-Semoutiers Air Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, in June 1954.</p>
<p>During a <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> exercise in the summer of 1956, Collins was forced to eject from an F-86 after a fire started aft of the cockpit. He was safely rescued and returned to Chaumont AB, where a wait of several hours ensued, as the base's <a href="/wiki/Flight_surgeon" title="Flight surgeon">flight surgeon</a> had joined search parties looking for Collins.</p>
<p>Collins met Patricia Finnegan, his future wife, in an officers' mess. She was from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a>, and was working for the Air Force service club. After getting engaged, they had to overcome a difference in religion. Collins was nominally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_in_the_United_States_of_America" title="Episcopal Church in the United States of America">Episcopalian</a>, while Finnegan came from a staunchly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a> family. Collins's father had been raised a Catholic, but converted to Protestantism when he married; the rest of his family remained Catholic.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> After seeking permission to marry from Finnegan's father, and delaying their wedding when Collins was redeployed to <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1956_Hungarian_Revolution" title="1956 Hungarian Revolution">1956 Hungarian Revolution</a>, they married in the summer of 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_Man_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-First_Man-3">[3]</a></sup> Daughter <a href="/wiki/Kate_Collins_(actress)" title="Kate Collins (actress)">Kate Collins</a>, born in 1959, is a successful actress.</p>
<p>After Collins was reassigned to the United States, he attended an aircraft maintenance officer course at <a href="/wiki/Chanute_Air_Force_Base" title="Chanute Air Force Base">Chanute Air Force Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>. He would later describe this school as "dismal" in his autobiography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup> Upon completing the course, he was posted to a Mobile Training Detachment (MTD) and travelled to Air Force bases, training mechanics on the servicing of new aircraft.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Test_pilot">Test pilot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Collins_(astronaut)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Test pilot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the help of his time as a member of an MTD, Collins accumulated over 1,500 hours of flying, the minimum required for the USAF Experimental Flight Test Pilot School at <a href="/wiki/Edwards_Air_Force_Base" title="Edwards Air Force Base">Edwards Air Force Base</a>, California. He successfully applied and reported on August 29, 1960, becoming a member of Class 60-C (which included future astronauts <a href="/wiki/Frank_Borman" title="Frank Borman">Frank Borman</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Irwin" title="James Irwin">Jim Irwin</a>). Following months of intensive training, Collins was one of the few chosen for a position in fighter operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:13–17</sup></p>
<p>Collins used to smoke heavily, but decided to quit in 1962 after suffering a particularly bad <a href="/wiki/Hangover" title="Hangover">hangover</a>. The next day, he spent what he described as the worst four hours of his life in the right-hand seat of a bomber flicking switches while going through the initial stages of <a href="/wiki/Nicotine" title="Nicotine">nicotine</a> withdrawal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:153–155</sup></p>
<p>The turning point for Collins in his decision to become an astronaut was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury_Atlas_6" title="Mercury Atlas 6">Mercury Atlas 6</a> flight of <a href="/wiki/John_Glenn" title="John Glenn">John Glenn</a> on February 20, 1962, and the thought of being able to circle the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> in 90 minutes. He immediately applied for the second group of astronauts that year. To raise their numbers, the Air Force sent their best applicants to a "charm school". Medical and psychiatric examinations at <a href="/wiki/Brooks_Air_Force_Base" title="Brooks Air Force Base">Brooks Air Force Base</a>, Texas and interviews at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manned_Spacecraft_Center" title="Manned Spacecraft Center">Manned Spacecraft Center</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston</a> followed. In mid-September he found that he had not been accepted, something that was a blow even though he did not really expect to be accepted. Collins still rates the <a href="/wiki/NASA_Astronaut_Group_2" title="NASA Astronaut Group 2">second group of nine</a> as the best group of astronauts ever selected by NASA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:25–33</sup></p>
<p>That same year the USAF Experimental Flight Test Pilot School became the USAF Aerospace Research Pilot School, as the Air Force tried to enter into the research of space. Collins applied for a new course offered into the basics of spaceflight (other students included <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bassett" title="Charles Bassett">Charles Bassett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Givens" title="Edward Givens">Edward Givens</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_H._Engle" title="Joseph H. Engle">Joe Engle</a>). Along with classwork they also flew up to about 90,000 feet in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-104_Starfighter" title="F-104 Starfighter">F-104 Starfighters</a>. As they passed through the top of their huge arc, they would experience a brief period of <a href="/wiki/Weightlessness" title="Weightlessness">weightlessness</a>. Finishing this course he returned to fighter ops in May 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:34–40</sup></p>
<p>At the start of June of that year, NASA once again called for astronaut applications. Collins went through the same process as with his first applications, though he did not take the psychiatric evaluation. He was at <a href="/wiki/Randolph_Air_Force_Base" title="Randolph Air Force Base">Randolph Air Force Base</a>, Texas on October 14 when <a href="/wiki/Deke_Slayton" title="Deke Slayton">Deke Slayton</a> called and asked if he was still interested in becoming an astronaut. Charlie Bassett was also accepted in the <a href="/wiki/NASA_Astronaut_Group_3" title="NASA Astronaut Group 3">same group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:40–46</sup></p>
<p>He has logged approximately 5,000 hours flying time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_program">Space program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Collins_(astronaut)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Space program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>For the third group of astronauts, training began with a 240-hour course of the basics of spaceflight. Fifty-eight hours of this was devoted to <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, something that Collins did not readily understand and in which he never became very interested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:72–73</sup> At the end, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Shepard" title="Alan Shepard">Alan Shepard</a>, who was head of the astronaut office, asked the fourteen to rank their fellow astronauts in the order they would want to fly with them in space. Collins picked <a href="/wiki/David_Scott" title="David Scott">David Scott</a> in the number one position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:77</sup></p>
<p>After this basic training, the third group were assigned specializations, with Collins receiving his first choice of pressure suits and EVA (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacewalk" title="Spacewalk">spacewalk</a>). His job was to monitor the development and act as something of a go-between for the Astronaut Office and the contractors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:114</sup> As such he was annoyed when during the secretive planning of Ed White's EVA on <a href="/wiki/Gemini_4" title="Gemini 4">Gemini 4</a>, he was not involved.</p>
<p>In late June 1965, Collins received his first crew assignment, the backup pilot for <a href="/wiki/Gemini_7" title="Gemini 7">Gemini 7</a>. He was the first of the 14 to receive a crew assignment, though would not be the first to fly; that honor went to David Scott on <a href="/wiki/Gemini_8" title="Gemini 8">Gemini 8</a>. Collins never rated himself with the super athletes of the <a href="/wiki/NASA_Astronaut_Corps" title="NASA Astronaut Corps">NASA Astronaut Corps</a>, like his fellow backup crew member <a href="/wiki/Edward_Higgins_White" title="Edward Higgins White">Ed White</a>, but he still tried to keep in shape, especially in the run up to Gemini 7, when he could have been called upon to spend 14 days in space.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Project_Gemini">Project Gemini</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Collins_(astronaut)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Project Gemini">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gemini_10" title="Gemini 10">Gemini 10</a></div>

<p>After the successful completion of Gemini 7, Collins was assigned to the prime crew of <a href="/wiki/Gemini_10" title="Gemini 10">Gemini 10</a> with <a href="/wiki/John_Young_(astronaut)" title="John Young (astronaut)">John Young</a>, with White moving onto <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a>. Their three-day mission called for them to rendezvous with two different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agena_Target_Vehicle" title="Agena Target Vehicle">Agena Target Vehicles</a>, undertake two EVAs, and perform 15 different experiments. The training went smoothly, as the crew learned the intricacies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_rendezvous" title="Orbital rendezvous">orbital rendezvous</a>, controlling the Agena and, for Collins, EVA. For what was to be the fourth ever EVA, underwater training was not undertaken, mostly because Collins just did not have the time. To train to use the nitrogen gun he would use for propulsion, a super smooth metal surface about the size of a boxing ring was set up. He would stand on a circular pad that used gas jets to raise itself off the surface. Using the nitrogen gun he would practise propelling himself across the "slippery table".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:177–198</sup> For the three day flight, Collins received <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>24.00 in travel reimbursement.</p>
<p>For his first EVA Collins did not leave the Gemini capsule, but stood up through the hatch with a device that resembled a sextant. In his biography he said he felt at that moment like a <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">Roman god</a> riding the skies in his chariot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:78</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_program">Apollo program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Collins_(astronaut)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Apollo program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Shortly after Gemini 10, Collins was assigned to the backup crew for the second manned Apollo flight, with Commander <a href="/wiki/Frank_Borman" title="Frank Borman">Frank Borman</a>, Command Module Pilot (CMP) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Patten_Stafford" title="Thomas Patten Stafford">Thomas Stafford</a> and Collins as Lunar Module Pilot (LMP). Along with learning the new <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">Apollo Command/Service Module</a> (CSM) and the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">Apollo Lunar Module</a> (LM), Collins received helicopter training, as these were thought to be the best way to simulate the landing approach of the LM. After the completion of <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">Project Gemini</a>, it was decided to cancel the Apollo 2 flight, since it would just repeat the Apollo 1 flight. In the process of crews being reassigned, Collins was moved to the CMP position on the Apollo 8 prime crew, since his new crew was Borman, Collins and <a href="/wiki/William_Anders" title="William Anders">William Anders</a>. <a href="/wiki/Deke_Slayton" title="Deke Slayton">Deke Slayton</a> had decided that the CMP should have some spaceflight experience, something that Anders did not have. Three years later, this change would be the reason Collins orbited the Moon while Armstrong and Aldrin walked on its surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_Man_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-First_Man-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:267–268</sup></p>
<p>Staff meetings were always held on Fridays in the Astronaut Office and it was here that Collins found himself on January 27, 1967. Don Gregory was running the meeting in the absence of <a href="/wiki/Alan_Shepard" title="Alan Shepard">Alan Shepard</a> and so it was he who answered the red phone to be informed that there was a fire in the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_1" title="Apollo 1">Apollo 1</a> CM. When the enormity of the situation was ascertained, it fell on Collins to go the Chaffee household to tell Martha Chaffee that her husband was dead. The Astronaut Office had learned to be proactive in informing astronauts' families of a death quickly, because of the death of <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Freeman" title="Theodore Freeman">Theodore Freeman</a> in an aircraft crash, when a newspaper reporter was the first to his house.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:269–274</sup></p>
<p>Following the delays to training as Frank Borman took part in the fire investigation, the crew of what would become <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a> started back. It would be the first manned flight of the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> on its third launch. They would use the <a href="/wiki/S-IVB" title="S-IVB">S-IVB</a> third stage to boost them into a highly elliptical Earth orbit with a high point of 4,000 miles.</p>
<p>During this time Collins and David Scott were sent by NASA to the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Air_Show" title="Paris Air Show">Paris Air Show</a> in May 1967. There they met <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmonaut" title="Cosmonaut">cosmonauts</a> <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Belyayev" title="Pavel Belyayev">Pavel Belyayev</a> and <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Feoktistov" title="Konstantin Feoktistov">Konstantin Feoktistov</a>, with whom they drank vodka on the Soviet's <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-134" title="Tupolev Tu-134">Tupolev Tu-134</a>. Collins found it interesting that some cosmonauts were doing helicopter training like their American counterparts, and Belyayev said that he hoped to make a circum-lunar flight soon. The astronauts' wives had accompanied them on the trip, and Collins and his wife Pat were somewhat forced by NASA and their friends to travel to <a href="/wiki/Metz" title="Metz">Metz</a> where they had been married ten years before. There, they found a third wedding ceremony had been arranged for them (ten years previously they had already had civil and religious ceremonies).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:278–282</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_problems">Medical problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Collins_(astronaut)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Medical problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During 1968, Collins noticed that his legs were not working as they should, first during <a href="/wiki/American_handball" title="American handball">handball</a> games, then as he walked down stairs, his knee would almost give way. His left leg also had unusual sensations when in hot and cold water. Reluctantly he sought medical advice and the diagnosis was a <a href="/wiki/Intervertebral_disc" title="Intervertebral disc">cervical</a> <a href="/wiki/Spinal_disc_herniation" title="Spinal disc herniation">disc herniation</a>, requiring two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_vertebra" title="Human vertebra">vertebrae</a> to be <a href="/wiki/Spinal_fusion" title="Spinal fusion">fused together</a>. The surgery was performed at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilford_Hall_Hospital" title="Wilford Hall Hospital">Wilford Hall Hospital</a> at <a href="/wiki/Lackland_Air_Force_Base" title="Lackland Air Force Base">Lackland Air Force Base</a>, Texas, and he spent three months in a neck brace. It also removed Collins from the crew of <a href="/wiki/Apollo_9" title="Apollo 9">Apollo 9</a> and moved <a href="/wiki/Jim_Lovell" title="Jim Lovell">Jim Lovell</a> up to the prime crew. When the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a> mission was changed from a CSM/LM in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_orbit" title="Earth orbit">Earth orbit</a>, to a CSM-only flight around the Moon, both prime and backup crews for the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apollo_9" title="Apollo 9">Apollo 9</a> swapped places.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:288–294</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_8">Apollo 8</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Collins_(astronaut)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Apollo 8">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a></div>
<p>Having trained for the flight, Collins was made a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capsule_communicator" title="Capsule communicator">capsule communicator</a> (CAPCOM), an astronaut stationed at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_Control_Center_(NASA)" title="Mission Control Center (NASA)">Mission Control</a> responsible for communicating directly with the crew during a mission. As part of the Green Team, he covered the launch phase up to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Translunar_injection" title="Translunar injection">translunar injection</a>, the rocket burn that sent Apollo 8 to the Moon. The successful completion of the first manned circum-lunar flight was followed by the announcement of the Apollo 11 crew of Armstrong, Aldrin, and Collins. At that time in January 1969, it was not certain this would be the lunar landing crew, depending on the success of <a href="/wiki/Apollo_9" title="Apollo 9">Apollo 9</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apollo_10" title="Apollo 10">Apollo 10</a> testing the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">Lunar Module</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:295–134</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_11">Apollo 11</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Collins_(astronaut)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Apollo 11">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a></div>
<p>As CMP, Collins's training was completely different from that for the LM and lunar EVA and was sometimes done without Armstrong or Aldrin being present. Along with simulators, there were size measurements for pressure suits, centrifuge training to simulate the 10 <a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force">g</a> reentry, and practicing docking with a huge rig at <a href="/wiki/Langley_Research_Center" title="Langley Research Center">NASA Langley Research Center, Hampton, Virginia</a>, just to name a few. Since he would be the active participant in the rendezvous with the LM, Collins compiled a book<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-17"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:339</sup> of 18 different rendezvous schemes for different scenarios including where the LM did not land, or launched too early or too late. This book ran for 117 pages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-18"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:339</sup></p>
<p>The famous <a href="/wiki/File:Apollo_11_insignia.png" title="File:Apollo 11 insignia.png">mission patch of Apollo 11</a> was the creation of Collins. Jim Lovell, the backup commander, mentioned the idea of eagles, a symbol of the United States. Collins liked the idea and found a photo in a <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_(magazine)" title="National Geographic (magazine)">National Geographic</a> magazine, traced it and added the lunar surface below and Earth in the background. The idea of an olive branch, a symbol of peace, came from a computer expert at the simulators. The call sign <i>Columbia</i> for the CSM came from Julian Scheer, the NASA Assistant Administrator for Public Affairs. He mentioned the idea to Collins in a conversation and Collins could not think of anything better.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_Man_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-First_Man-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:332–325</sup> It was during the training for Apollo 11 that Collins told Deke Slayton that he did not want to fly again. Slayton offered to get him back into the crew sequence after the flight, and according to Collins, this would probably have been as backup commander of <a href="/wiki/Apollo_14" title="Apollo 14">Apollo 14</a> followed by commander of <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">Apollo 17</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2009_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2009-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:343</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Michael_Collins_hatch_Columbia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="883" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Michael_Collins_hatch_Columbia.jpg/220px-Michael_Collins_hatch_Columbia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Michael_Collins_hatch_Columbia.jpg/330px-Michael_Collins_hatch_Columbia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Michael_Collins_hatch_Columbia.jpg/440px-Michael_Collins_hatch_Columbia.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Michael_Collins_hatch_Columbia.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Collins sits in the hatch of Apollo 11 Command Module after its return to the MSC's Lunar Receiving Laboratory for detailed examination</div>

<p>During his day of solo flying around the Moon, Collins never felt lonely. Although it has been said that "not since <a href="/wiki/Adam" title="Adam">Adam</a> has any human known such solitude",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Collins felt very much a part of the mission. In his autobiography he wrote that "this venture has been structured for three men, and I consider my third to be as necessary as either of the other two". During the 48 minutes of each orbit that he was out of radio contact with Earth, the feeling he reported was not loneliness, but rather "awareness, anticipation, satisfaction, confidence, almost exultation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-19"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:402</sup></p>
<p>After spending so much time with the CSM, Collins felt compelled to leave his mark on it, so during the second night following their return from the Moon, he went to the lower equipment bay of the CM and wrote:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"Spacecraft 107 — alias Apollo 11 — alias <i>Columbia</i>. The best ship to come down the line. God Bless Her. Michael Collins, CMP"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-20"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:446</sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>In a July 2009 interview with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, Collins revealed that he was very worried about Armstrong and Aldrin's safety. He was also concerned that, in the event of their deaths on the Moon, he would be forced to return to Earth alone and, as the mission's sole survivor, be regarded as "a marked man for life".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-NASA_activities">Post-NASA activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Collins_(astronaut)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Post-NASA activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>After being released from a 18-day quarantine, the crew embarked on a 45-day "Giant Leap" tour across the United States and around the world. Prior to this trip NASA Administrator <a href="/wiki/Thomas_O._Paine" title="Thomas O. Paine">Thomas O. Paine</a> had told Collins that <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/William_P._Rogers" title="William P. Rogers">William P. Rogers</a> was interested in appointing Collins to the position of <a href="/wiki/Assistant_Secretary_of_State_for_Public_Affairs" title="Assistant Secretary of State for Public Affairs">Assistant Secretary of State for Public Affairs</a>. After the crew returned to the U.S. in November, Collins sat down with Rogers and accepted the position on the urgings of President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313_1-21"><a href="#cite_note-Collins_2001_8.E2.80.9313-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:454–455</sup></p>
<p>Collins retired from NASA in 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jsc.nasa.gov_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-jsc.nasa.gov-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1971, Collins was appointed director of the <a href="/wiki/National_Air_and_Space_Museum" title="National Air and Space Museum">National Air and Space Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasmod_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasmod-8">[8]</a></sup> He held this position until 1978,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasmod_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-nasmod-8">[8]</a></sup> when he stepped down to become undersecretary of the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldmedal_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldmedal-9">[9]</a></sup> Collins completed the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Business School</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School#Advanced_Management_Program" title="Harvard Business School">Advanced Management Program</a> in 1974, and in 1980 he became Vice President of <a href="/wiki/Vought" title="Vought">LTV Aerospace</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arlington,_Virginia" title="Arlington, Virginia">Arlington, Virginia</a>. He resigned in 1985 to start his own consulting firm, Michael Collins Associates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ead.lib.virginia.edu_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ead.lib.virginia.edu-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>He retired from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force_Reserves" title="U.S. Air Force Reserves">U.S. Air Force Reserves</a> with the rank of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_General" title="Major General">Major General</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jsc.nasa.gov_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-jsc.nasa.gov-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldmedal_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-goldmedal-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>He wrote an <a href="/wiki/Autobiography" title="Autobiography">autobiography</a> in 1974 entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Carrying_the_Fire:_An_Astronaut%27s_Journeys" title="Carrying the Fire: An Astronaut's Journeys">Carrying the Fire: An Astronaut's Journeys</a></i>. It is "generally regarded as the best account of what it is like to be an astronaut."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He has also written <i>Liftoff: The Story of America's Adventure in Space</i> (1988), a history of the American space program, <i>Mission to Mars</i> (1990), a non-fiction book on human spaceflight to Mars, and <i>Flying to the Moon and Other Strange Places</i> (1976), revised and re-released as <i>Flying to the Moon: An Astronaut's Story</i> (1994), a children's book on his experiences. Along with his writing, he has painted watercolors mostly relating to his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_Everglades" title="Florida Everglades">Florida Everglades</a> home, or aircraft that he flew; they are rarely space-related. Until recently he did not sign his paintings to avoid them increasing in price just because they had his autograph on them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Collins was a long-time <a href="/wiki/Trustee" title="Trustee">trustee</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic Society</a> and presently serves as Trustee Emeritus. He is also a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Experimental_Test_Pilots" title="Society of Experimental Test Pilots">Society of Experimental Test Pilots</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Fellow" title="Fellow">fellow</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Aeronautics_and_Astronautics" title="American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics">American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics</a>. In 2008 he was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_Walk_of_Honor" title="Aerospace Walk of Honor">Aerospace Walk of Honor</a>. Collins is also an inductee of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Astronaut_Hall_of_Fame" title="U.S. Astronaut Hall of Fame">U.S. Astronaut Hall of Fame</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico_Museum_of_Space_History" title="New Mexico Museum of Space History">International Space Hall of Fame</a>.</p>

<p>He has been awarded the <a href="/wiki/NASA_Distinguished_Service_Medal" title="NASA Distinguished Service Medal">NASA Distinguished Service Medal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Distinguished_Flying_Cross_(United_States)" title="Distinguished Flying Cross (United States)">Air Force Distinguished Flying Cross</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronaut_Badge#U.S._Air_Force_astronauts" title="Astronaut Badge">Air Force Command Pilot Astronaut Wings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Medal_of_Freedom" title="Presidential Medal of Freedom">Presidential Medal of Freedom</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Gold_Medal" title="Congressional Gold Medal">Congressional Gold Medal</a>. Together with Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin, he received the <a href="/wiki/Collier_Trophy" title="Collier Trophy">Collier Trophy</a> in 1969 and the <a href="/wiki/Hubbard_Medal" title="Hubbard Medal">Hubbard Medal</a> of the National Geographic Society in 1970. The <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> honored him by naming an asteroid after him, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/6471_Collins" title="6471 Collins">6471 Collins</a>. Collins and his Apollo 11 crewmates were the 1999 recipients of the <a href="/wiki/Langley_Gold_Medal" title="Langley Gold Medal">Langley Gold Medal</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a>. Also, like the other two Apollo 11 crew members, he has a <a href="/wiki/Collins_(crater)" title="Collins (crater)">lunar crater named after him</a>. He also received the <a href="/wiki/Harmon_Trophy" title="Harmon Trophy">Harmon Trophy</a> in 1969.</p>
<p>Collins lived with his wife, Pat, in <a href="/wiki/Marco_Island,_Florida" title="Marco Island, Florida">Marco Island, Florida</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avon,_North_Carolina" title="Avon, North Carolina">Avon, North Carolina</a> until her death in April 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <i>Carrying the Fire</i> is dedicated to her.</p>
<p>Collins is one of the astronauts featured in the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Shadow_of_the_Moon_(film)" title="In the Shadow of the Moon (film)">In the Shadow of the Moon</a>.</i> He also contributed to the <a href="/wiki/In_the_Shadow_of_the_Moon_(book)" title="In the Shadow of the Moon (book)">book</a> of the same name. In 1989, some of his personal papers were transferred to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Polytechnic_Institute_and_State_University" title="Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State University">Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ead.lib.virginia.edu_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ead.lib.virginia.edu-10">[10]</a></sup> He had a small part as "Old Man" in the 2009 movie, <i><a href="/wiki/Youth_in_Revolt_(film)" title="Youth in Revolt (film)">Youth in Revolt</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>Collins is one of 82 astronauts who supports the <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_Day" title="Asteroid Day">Asteroid Day</a> campaign which was co-founded by <a href="/wiki/Apollo_9" title="Apollo 9">Apollo 9</a> astronaut <a href="/wiki/Rusty_Schweickart" title="Rusty Schweickart">Rusty Schweickart</a> and astrophysicist and <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">Queen</a> guitarist <a href="/wiki/Brian_May" title="Brian May">Brian May</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Collins_(astronaut)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>English rock group <a href="/wiki/Jethro_Tull_(band)" title="Jethro Tull (band)">Jethro Tull</a> has a song "For Michael Collins, Jeffrey and Me", which appears on the <a href="/wiki/Benefit_(album)" title="Benefit (album)"><i>Benefit</i></a> album from 1970. The song compares the feelings of misfitting from vocalist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ian_Anderson_(musician)" title="Ian Anderson (musician)">Ian Anderson</a> (and friend <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Hammond" title="Jeffrey Hammond">Jeffrey Hammond</a>) with the astronaut's own, as he is left behind by the ones who had the privilege of walking on the surface of the Moon.</li>
<li>In the 1996 TV movie <i><a href="/wiki/Apollo_11_(film)" title="Apollo 11 (film)">Apollo 11</a></i>, Collins was played by <a href="/wiki/Jim_Metzler" title="Jim Metzler">Jim Metzler</a>. In the 1998 <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> <a href="/wiki/Miniseries" title="Miniseries">miniseries</a> <i><a href="/wiki/From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon_(miniseries)" title="From the Earth to the Moon (miniseries)">From the Earth to the Moon</a></i>, he was played by <a href="/wiki/Cary_Elwes" title="Cary Elwes">Cary Elwes</a>. In the 2009 TV movie <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_Shot_(2009_film)" title="Moon Shot (2009 film)">Moon Shot</a></i>, he was played by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Lincoln" title="Andrew Lincoln">Andrew Lincoln</a>.</li>
<li>The 2002 <a href="/wiki/Mockumentary" title="Mockumentary">mockumentary</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dark_Side_of_the_Moon_(mockumentary)" title="Dark Side of the Moon (mockumentary)">Opération Lune</a></i> portrays the reason for Collins' retirement from public life as his deep disappointment at not having set foot on the Moon, and his current whereabouts as unknown.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>American rock group <a href="/wiki/The_Byrds" title="The Byrds">The Byrds</a> has a song "Armstrong, Aldrin, and Collins", a ballad of the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> Moon landing, on the album <i><a href="/wiki/Ballad_of_Easy_Rider_(album)" title="Ballad of Easy Rider (album)">Ballad of Easy Rider</a></i>.</li>
<li>In Chapter 6 of <a href="/wiki/Naoki_Urasawa" title="Naoki Urasawa">Naoki Urasawa</a>'s manga series <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Boys" title="20th Century Boys">20th Century Boys</a>, the characters express sympathy that Collins made it all the way to the moon without ever setting foot on the lunar surface.</li>
<li>In 2013, indie pop group <a href="/wiki/The_Boy_Least_Likely_To" title="The Boy Least Likely To">The Boy Least Likely To</a> released the song "Michael Collins" on the album <i>The Great Perhaps.</i> The song uses Collins' feeling that he was blessed to have the type of solitude of being truly separated from all other human contact in contrast with modern society's lack of perspective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>He was referenced by a <a href="/wiki/Saturday_Morning_Breakfast_Cereal" title="Saturday Morning Breakfast Cereal">Saturday Morning Breakfast Cereal</a> comic strip produced in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
