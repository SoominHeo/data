<p>The <b>Mai Tai</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic</a> <a href="/wiki/Cocktail" title="Cocktail">cocktail</a> based on <a href="/wiki/Rum" title="Rum">rum</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao_liqueur" title="Curaçao liqueur">Curaçao liqueur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orgeat_syrup" title="Orgeat syrup">orgeat syrup</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lime_juice" title="Lime juice">lime juice</a>, associated with <a href="/wiki/Tiki_culture" title="Tiki culture">Polynesian-style</a> settings.</p>
<p>"Maita'i" is the <a href="/wiki/Tahitian_language" title="Tahitian language">Tahitian</a> word for "good"; but the drink is spelled as two words, sometimes hyphenated or capitalized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mai_Tai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Victor J. Bergeron claimed to have invented the Mai Tai in 1944 at his restaurant, <a href="/wiki/Trader_Vic%27s" title="Trader Vic's">Trader Vic's</a>, in California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Trader Vic's rival, <a href="/wiki/Don_the_Beachcomber" title="Don the Beachcomber">Don the Beachcomber</a>, claimed to have created it in 1933 at his then-new bar named for himself (later a famous restaurant) in <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a>. Don the Beachcomber's recipe is more complex than that of Vic's and tastes quite different.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Trader Vic's story of its invention is that the Trader (Victor J. Bergeron) created it one afternoon for some friends who were visiting from Tahiti. One of those friends, Carrie Guild, tasted it and cried out: "Maita'i roa ae!" (literally "very good!", figuratively "Out of this world! The best!")—hence the name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1953 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matson_Navigation_Company" title="Matson Navigation Company">Matson Navigation Company</a> (now Matson, Inc.) commissioned Victor Bergeron to create a drink for their new <a href="/wiki/Royal_Hawaiian_Hotel" title="Royal Hawaiian Hotel">Royal Hawaiian Hotel</a>. Victor then made a variation on the Mai Tai recipe adding <a href="/wiki/Pineapple" title="Pineapple">pineapple</a> juice; this variant is still served today at the hotel.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recipe">Recipe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mai_Tai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recipe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are many recipes for Mai Tais. Eleven of them, including three versions of Trader Vic's, as well as the recipe of Don the Beachcomber, can be found at <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikibooks.org/wiki/Bartending/Cocktails/Mai_Tai" title="b:Bartending/Cocktails/Mai Tai">Wikibooks Mai Tai</a>.</p>
<blockquote>
<div class="poem">
<dl>
<dt>Classic Mai Tai<br/></dt>
</dl>
<p>45 ml aged Jamaican rum<br/>
15 ml <a href="/wiki/Orgeat_syrup" title="Orgeat syrup">Orgeat syrup</a><br/>
15 ml Triple Sec<br/>
30 ml fresh lime juice<br/>
<br/>
Served, garnished, over crushed ice.</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mai_Tai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Mai Tai became such a popular cocktail in the 1950s–60s that many restaurants, particularly <a href="/wiki/Tiki_culture" title="Tiki culture">tiki-themed</a> restaurants or bars, served them. The Mai Tai was also prominently featured in the <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Blue_Hawaii" title="Blue Hawaii">Blue Hawaii</a></i>.</p>
<p>Today, the Mai Tai is synonymous with <a href="/wiki/Tiki_culture" title="Tiki culture">Tiki culture</a> both past and present.</p>
<p>As of 2008, Trader Vic's Restaurant chain began to open small establishments called Mai Tai Bars that primarily serve cocktails and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pupus" title="Pupus">pupus</a></i> (appetizers).</p>
