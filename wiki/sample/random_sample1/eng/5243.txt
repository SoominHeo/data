<p><b>Mindscape Interactive Inc.</b> was an international <a href="/wiki/Software_publisher" title="Software publisher">software publishing</a> company, previously part of <a href="/wiki/The_Learning_Company" title="The Learning Company">The Learning Company</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and later affiliated with <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a>. As of 2004, the group had offices in Europe, Asia, Australia and South America. It had an annual turnover of €38 million and employed 150 people. Mindscape published and distributed educational and lifestyle games and software. Its most notable titles include <i><a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order:_Criminal_Intent_(video_game)" title="Law &amp; Order: Criminal Intent (video game)">Law &amp; Order: Criminal Intent</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Lego_Island" title="Lego Island">Lego Island</a></i>.</p>

<p>Mindscape began in 1983 as a software publisher in <a href="/wiki/Northbrook,_Illinois" title="Northbrook, Illinois">Northbrook, Illinois</a>. Founded by English-born Roger M. Buoy, the company established its early roots developing educational software for the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Instruments_TI-99/4A" title="Texas Instruments TI-99/4A">TI-99/4A</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Apple_II_series" title="Apple II series">Apple II</a>. It later branched out into entertainment software by becoming a Lucasfilm licensee, publishing the Indiana Jones computer games before they were taken in-house by <a href="/wiki/LucasArts" title="LucasArts">Lucasfilm Games</a> with <i><a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones_and_the_Last_Crusade_(video_game)" title="Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade (video game)">Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade</a></i>. It also released text adventures tied to famous authors and a controversial game from Tom Snyder Productions called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub_Mission" title="Sub Mission">Sub Mission</a></i>. The latter required gamers to purchase a replacement disk if they lost the mission three times.</p>
<p>Under creative director Sandy Schneider, the company became one of the earliest publishers of software for the <a href="/wiki/Macintosh" title="Macintosh">Macintosh</a>, publishing the seminal <a href="/wiki/Chris_Crawford_(game_designer)" title="Chris Crawford (game designer)">Chris Crawford</a> game, <i><a href="/wiki/Balance_of_Power_(video_game)" title="Balance of Power (video game)">Balance of Power</a></i>. It also received accolades for its publishing of the innovative Apple Macintosh adventure games, the MacVentures, which were developed by ICOM Simulations, and included <i><a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9j%C3%A0_Vu_(video_game)" title="Déjà Vu (video game)">Déjà Vu</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Uninvited_(video_game)" title="Uninvited (video game)">Uninvited</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Shadowgate" title="Shadowgate">Shadowgate</a></i>. It also published the first 3D game for home computers, <a href="/wiki/David_A._Smith_(computer_scientist)" title="David A. Smith (computer scientist)">David Alan Smith</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Colony_(video_game)" title="The Colony (video game)">The Colony</a></i>. It also published several of the early Macromind products, namely Graphic Works and Comic Works.</p>
<p>In 1984 Mindscape worked closely with Commodore in developing some of the initial applications for the Commodore Amiga. The company participated in the 1985 release of the Amiga that featured Andy Warhol and Debbie Harry.</p>
<p>In 1988, Mindscape became a <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a> licensee and published video games such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Terminator_(Nintendo_Entertainment_System)" title="The Terminator (Nintendo Entertainment System)">The Terminator</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Nintendo Entertainment System">Nintendo Entertainment System</a> (NES) and <i><a href="/wiki/Paperboy_(video_game)" title="Paperboy (video game)">Paperboy</a></i> for both Nintendo's NES and <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy" title="Game Boy">Game Boy</a> platforms. <i>Paperboy</i> becoming a huge hit worldwide selling over 2 million copies on the NES alone.</p>
<p>During the late 1980s, Mindscape was unique in the software entertainment arena by establishing a global network. Buoy opened offices around the world and installed seasoned managers to run them. By 1990, Mindscape had offices in Sydney, Australia; Chiba, Japan; Sussex, England; and Normandy, France.</p>
<p>In 1988 Mindscape became a public company raising $9.6 million. It was one of the first companies to have a successful IPO after the stock market crash of October 19, 1987.</p>
<p>Mindscape was famous for its Rock 'n Roll parties at Chicago's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Summer_Consumer_Electronics_Show" title="Summer Consumer Electronics Show">Summer Consumer Electronics Show</a> (the trade show prior to <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Entertainment_Expo" title="Electronic Entertainment Expo">E3</a>) where it released its new products and entertained its customers.</p>
<p>In 1990 <a href="/wiki/Les_Crane" title="Les Crane">Les Crane</a>'s The Software Toolworks acquired Mindscape, primarily for the Nintendo license.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cgw199001_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-cgw199001-2">[2]</a></sup> The company held one last Summer Consumer Electronics Show parties where <a href="/wiki/Dudley_Moore" title="Dudley Moore">Dudley Moore</a> introduced the <i><a href="/wiki/Miracle_Piano_Teaching_System" title="Miracle Piano Teaching System">Miracle Piano Teaching System</a></i>, an electronic learning system for the piano. However, The Software Toolworks almost sank after it over-ordered the piano hardware for <i>Miracle Piano Teaching System</i>, was not able to sell through copies of the product very quickly, and was stuck with a large <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/inventory" title="wikt:inventory">inventory</a>. Roger Buoy left the company in 1992 to pursue other entrepreneurial activities.</p>
<p>The company became involved in another round of mergers and headquarters moved to <a href="/wiki/Novato,_California" title="Novato, California">Novato, California</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marin_County" title="Marin County">Marin County</a> north of <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>. It started out publishing software for <a href="/wiki/Zenith_Data_Systems" title="Zenith Data Systems">Heath/Zenith</a> personal computers. Early products for The Software Toolworks included <i>Mychess</i> and the C/80 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C_compiler" title="C compiler">C compiler</a> for <a href="/wiki/CP/M" title="CP/M">CP/M</a>. The two most popular products were the very long-lived series <i><a href="/wiki/Mavis_Beacon_Teaches_Typing" title="Mavis Beacon Teaches Typing">Mavis Beacon Teaches Typing</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Chessmaster" title="Chessmaster">Chessmaster</a></i> series of computer games. It is also known for its contribution in the development of the <i><a href="/wiki/Lego_Island" title="Lego Island">Lego Island</a></i> PC game series.</p>
<p>In 1992, Mindscape created and released the isometric fantasy role-playing game <i><a href="/wiki/Legend_(1992_video_game)" title="Legend (1992 video game)">Legend</a></i> (known as <i>The Four Crystals of Trazere</i> in the United States) for MS-DOS, Amiga and Atari ST. Following some success worldwide, a sequel <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Worlds_of_Legend:_Son_of_the_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Worlds of Legend: Son of the Empire (page does not exist)">Worlds of Legend: Son of the Empire</a></i> was released for MS-DOS and Amiga in 1993.</p>
<p>In 1993, The Software Toolworks developed <i><a href="/wiki/Wing_Commander_(video_game)" title="Wing Commander (video game)">Wing Commander</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/Super_Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Super Nintendo Entertainment System">Super Nintendo Entertainment System</a> under the Mindscape brand in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Origin_Systems" title="Origin Systems">Origin Systems</a> at a time when The Software Toolworks was downsizing and struggling to survive. Sales from the game heavily exceeded expectations and the resulting revenue brought the company back from the brink (as evidenced by its share price which had fallen to $2 per share and was ultimately brought back up to $12 a share before the company was purchased).</p>
<p>In 1997, the internal development department was shut down. Through an undisclosed deal with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts_UK" title="Electronic Arts UK">Electronic Arts UK</a>, most of the development staff, and the game they were developing (which was close to completion) – <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dark_Omen" title="Dark Omen">Warhammer: Dark Omen</a></i> – was moved to EA UK's offices in <a href="/wiki/Guildford" title="Guildford">Guildford</a>, Surrey, and released in early 1998.</p>
<p>Below is a timeline of events occurring from 1993 to 2011:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>1993</b>: Purchases <a href="/wiki/Wargame_(video_games)" title="Wargame (video games)">wargames</a> publisher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strategic_Simulations,_Inc." title="Strategic Simulations, Inc.">SSI</a> and changes name to <b>Mindscape</b>.</li>
<li><b>1994</b>: Sold to <a href="/wiki/Pearson_PLC" title="Pearson PLC">Pearson PLC</a> for $503 million.</li>
<li><b>1998</b>: Sold to <a href="/wiki/The_Learning_Company" title="The Learning Company">The Learning Company</a> for $150 million. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>Mindscape also purchases PF Magic.</li>
<li><b>1998</b>: Sold to <a href="/wiki/Mattel" title="Mattel">Mattel</a> for around US$3.7 billion, causing a strong impact to Mattel's stock price and the ouster of its CEO. Mattel subsequently sells Mindscape to Gore Technology Group for just a share of the profits that Gores could obtain by selling the properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>2001</b>: Mindscape is made a separate company in following the purchase of the international division of <a href="/wiki/The_Learning_Company" title="The Learning Company">The Learning Company</a> from the Gore Technology Group by Jean-Pierre Nordman.</li>
<li><b>2002</b>: Montparnasse Multimedia is purchased by Mindscape.</li>
<li><b>2005</b>: Mindscape purchases the studio <a href="/wiki/Coktel_Vision" title="Coktel Vision">Coktel Vision</a> – owners of the brands ADI and Adiboo – from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vivendi_Universal_Games" title="Vivendi Universal Games">Vivendi Universal Games</a></li>
<li><b>2006</b>: <a href="/wiki/Ubisoft" title="Ubisoft">Ubisoft</a> continues to publish <i><a href="/wiki/Chessmaster" title="Chessmaster">Chessmaster</a></i> titles. <a href="/wiki/Broderbund" title="Broderbund">Broderbund</a> is the new publisher of <i><a href="/wiki/Mavis_Beacon_Teaches_Typing" title="Mavis Beacon Teaches Typing">Mavis Beacon Teaches Typing</a></i>.</li>
<li><b>2011</b>: Mindscape has shut down their last studio and retreats from the gaming industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>2011</b>: Game publisher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anuman_Interactive" title="Anuman Interactive">Anuman Interactive</a> acquired the <i>Montparnasse Multimédia</i> and <i>Alsyd</i> catalogues, as well as the <i>Horse Star</i> license, all formerly operated by Mindscape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_published">Software published</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mindscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Software published">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following is a list of software programs with their year that were published by the company.</p>
<ul>
