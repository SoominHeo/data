<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Horse sacrifice</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a> religious branches show evidence for <b>horse sacrifice</b>, and comparative mythology suggests that they derive from a <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_religion" title="Proto-Indo-European religion">Proto-Indo-European</a> (PIE) ritual.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Context">Context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horse_sacrifice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Often horses are <a href="/wiki/Animal_sacrifice" title="Animal sacrifice">sacrificed</a> in a funerary context, and interred with the deceased, a practice called <a href="/wiki/Horse_burial" title="Horse burial">horse burial</a>. There is evidence but no explicit myths from the three branches of Indo-Europeans of a major horse sacrifice ritual based on a mythical union of <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_society" title="Proto-Indo-European society">Indo-European kingship</a> and the horse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-Aryans" title="Indo-Aryans">Indian</a> <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A%C5%9Bvamedha" title="Aśvamedha">Aśvamedha</a></b> is the clearest evidence preserved, but vestiges from <a href="/wiki/Latins" title="Latins">Latin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celt" title="Celt">Celtic</a> traditions allow the reconstruction of a few common attributes.</p>
<p>Some scholars, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edgar_Polom%C3%A9" title="Edgar Polomé">Edgar Polomé</a>, regard the reconstruction of a PIE ritual as unjustified due to the difference between the attested traditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horse_sacrifice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaulish</a> personal name <i>Epomeduos</i> is from <i>ek'wo-medhu-</i> ("horse + mead"), while <i>aśvamedha</i> is either from <i>ek'wo-mad-dho-</i> ("horse + drunk") or <i>ek'wo-mey-dho-</i> ("horse + strength").</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mythology">Mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horse_sacrifice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Horse_worship" title="Horse worship">Horse worship</a> and <a href="/wiki/White_horse_(mythology)" title="White horse (mythology)">White horse (mythology)</a></div>
<p>The reconstructed myth involves the coupling of a king with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divine_mare" title="Divine mare">divine mare</a> which produced the <a href="/wiki/Divine_twins" title="Divine twins">divine twins</a>. A related myth is that of a hero magically twinned with a horse foaled at the time of his birth (for example <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuchulainn" title="Cuchulainn">Cuchulainn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pryderi" title="Pryderi">Pryderi</a>), suggested to be fundamentally the same myth as that of the divine twin horsemen by the mytheme of a "mare-suckled" hero from Greek and medieval <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serb" title="Serb">Serbian</a> evidence, or mythical horses with human traits (<a href="/wiki/Xanthos" title="Xanthos">Xanthos</a>), suggesting <a href="/wiki/Totem" title="Totem">totemic</a> identity of the Indo-European hero or king with the horse.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparative_rituals">Comparative rituals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horse_sacrifice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Comparative rituals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vedic_.28Indian.29">Vedic (Indian)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horse_sacrifice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vedic (Indian)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Ashvamedha" title="Ashvamedha">Ashvamedha</a></div>
<p>The <b>Indian</b> Ashvamedha involves the following:</p>
<ol>
<li>the sacrifice is connected with the elevation or inauguration of a member of the warrior caste<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>the ceremony took place in springtime</li>
<li>the horse sacrificed was a grey or white stallion</li>
<li>the stallion selected was one which excelled at the right side of the chariot</li>
<li>it was bathed in water</li>
<li>it was sacrificed alongside a hornless ram and a he-goat</li>
<li>the stallion was dissected and its portions awarded to various deities<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman">Roman</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horse_sacrifice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Roman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/October_Horse" title="October Horse">October Horse</a></div>
<p>The <b>Roman</b> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equus_October" title="Equus October">Equus October</a></i> ceremony involved:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>the horse was dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Mars_(mythology)" title="Mars (mythology)">Mars</a>, the Roman god of war</li>
<li>the sacrifice took place on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ides_(calendar)" title="Ides (calendar)">Ides</a> of October, but through ritual reuse was used in a spring festival (the <a href="/wiki/Parilia" title="Parilia">Parilia</a>)</li>
<li>two-horse chariot races determined the victim, which was the right-hand horse of the winning team</li>
<li>the horse is dismembered: the tail (<i>cauda</i>, possibly a euphemism for the penis) is taken to the <a href="/wiki/Regia" title="Regia">Regia</a>, the king's residence, while two factions battle for possession of the head as a talisman for the coming year</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Irish">Irish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horse_sacrifice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Irish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geraldus_Cambrensis" title="Geraldus Cambrensis">Geraldus Cambrensis</a> recorded a ceremony among the <b>Irish</b>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>There is in a northern and remote part of <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a>, among the <a href="/wiki/Tyrconnell" title="Tyrconnell">Kenelcunil</a>, a certain <a href="/wiki/List_of_Celtic_tribes#Ireland" title="List of Celtic tribes">tribe</a> which is wont to install a king over itself by an excessively savage and abominable ritual. In the presence of all the people of this land in one place, a white mare is brought into their midst. Thereupon he who is to be elevated, not to a prince but to a beast, not to a king but to an outlaw, steps forward in beastly fashion and exhibits his bestiality. Right thereafter the mare is killed and boiled piecemeal in water, and in the same water a bath is prepared for him. He gets into the bath and eats of the flesh that is brought to him, with his people standing around and sharing it with him. He also imbibes the broth in which he is bathed, not from any vessel, nor with his hand, but only with his mouth. When this is done right according to such unrighteous ritual, his rule and sovereignty are consecrated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The major points of comparison involve:</p>
<ol>
<li>The king (most likely; Geraldus is somewhat indirect) couples with the mare to be sacrificed;</li>
<li>The horse is dismembered and cooked in a cauldron, and consumed by the king who is also sitting in the cauldron.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norse">Norse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horse_sacrifice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Norse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b>Norse</b> ceremony according to the description in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hervarar_saga" title="Hervarar saga">Hervarar saga</a></i> of the Swedish inauguration of <a href="/wiki/Blot-Sweyn" title="Blot-Sweyn">Blot-Sweyn</a>, the last or next to last pagan <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_king" title="Germanic king">Germanic king</a>, c. 1080:</p>
<ol>
<li>the horse is dismembered for eating</li>
<li>the blood is sprinkled on the <a href="/wiki/Sacred_tree_at_Uppsala" title="Sacred tree at Uppsala">sacred tree at Uppsala</a>.</li>
</ol>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lsa_%C3%BE%C3%A1ttr" title="Völsa þáttr">Völsa þáttr</a></i> mentions a Norse pagan ritual involving veneration of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horse_penis" title="Horse penis">penis of a slaughtered stallion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A freshly cut horse head was also used in setting up a <a href="/wiki/Nithing_pole" title="Nithing pole">nithing pole</a> for a Norse curse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeology">Archaeology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horse_sacrifice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Archaeology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The primary archaeological context of horse sacrifice are burials, notably <a href="/wiki/Chariot_burial" title="Chariot burial">chariot burials</a>, but graves with horse remains reach from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eneolithic" title="Eneolithic">Eneolithic</a> well into historical times. <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> describes the execution of horses at the burial of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scythian" title="Scythian">Scythian</a> king, and Iron Age <a href="/wiki/Kurgan" title="Kurgan">kurgan</a> graves known to contain horses number in the hundreds. There are also frequent deposition of horses in burials in <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_India" title="Iron Age India">Iron Age India</a>. The custom is by no means restricted to Indo-European populations, but is continued by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkic_tribes" title="Turkic tribes">Turkic tribes</a>.</p>

Collecto in unum universo terrae illius populo, in medium producitur, iumentum candidum. Ad quod sullimandus ille non in principem sed in beluam, non in regem sed exlegem, coram omnibus bestialiter accedens, se quoque bestiam profitetur. Et statim iumento interfecto, et frustatim in aqua decocto, in eadem aqua balneum ei paratur. Cui insidens, de carnibus illis sibi allatis, circumstante populo suo et convescente, comedit ipse. De iure quoque quo lavatur, non vase aliquo, non manu, sed ore tantum circumquaque haurit et bibit. Quibus ita rite, non recte completis, regnum illius et dominium est confirmatum</i>: English translation from <a href="/wiki/Jaan_Puhvel" title="Jaan Puhvel">Jaan Puhvel</a>, "Aspects of Equine Functionality," in <i>Analecta Indoeuropaea</i> (Innsbruck, 1981), pp. 188–189.</span></li>
