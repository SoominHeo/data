<p><b>Majda Vrhovnik</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nom_de_guerre" title="Nom de guerre">nom de guerre</a> Lojzka<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leben_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leben-1">[1]</a></sup>) (14 April 1922 – 4 May 1945) was a <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovene</a> communist and medical student. She was a member of the District Committee of the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Communists_of_Slovenia" title="League of Communists of Slovenia">Communist Party of Slovenia</a> for <a href="/wiki/Klagenfurt" title="Klagenfurt">Klagenfurt</a> and was named a <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_People%27s_Hero" title="Order of the People's Hero">people's hero of Yugoslavia</a> after her death.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Majda_Vrhovnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Majda Vrhovnik was born 14 April 1922 in <a href="/wiki/Ljubljana" title="Ljubljana">Ljubljana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jersek_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jersek-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jakopic_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jakopic-3">[3]</a></sup> Her older brother Vladimir Vrhovnik (17 August 1916 – 28 April 1945, a.k.a. Volodja or Mirko, also a member of the Communist Party) was born in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, but the family moved to Ljubljana after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>After graduating from an upper secondary school she enrolled in the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Ljubljana" title="University of Ljubljana">University of Ljubljana</a>’s Faculty of Medicine. During her studies she was a member of the Slovene Club and the student revolutionary movement. She was accepted for membership in the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Communists_of_Yugoslavia" title="League of Communists of Yugoslavia">Communist Party of Yugoslavia</a> in 1940.</p>
<p>Vrhovnik joined the underground movement immediately after the occupation of Yugoslavia. She became a courier for the organizational secretary of the Central Committee of the Communist Party of Slovenia, Tone Tomšič (1910–1942).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jersek_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jersek-2">[2]</a></sup> When the occupying forces became aware of her activity they sentenced her to life in prison in absentia, holding her parents as hostages for several months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jersek_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jersek-2">[2]</a></sup> Vrhovnik nonetheless remained in Ljubljana. She participated in organizing an underground printshop for the resistance in Ljubljana. As a courier, she carried manuscripts for the underground printshops in Ljubljana codenamed <i>Podmornica</i> 'submarine' at Brdo Street (<span lang="sl" xml:lang="sl"><i>Cesta na Brdo</i></span>) no. 95 and <i>Tunel</i> 'tunnel' at Emona Street (<span lang="sl" xml:lang="sl"><i>Emonska cesta</i></span>) no. 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jersek_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jersek-2">[2]</a></sup> With the assistance of her brother, she managed to set up a bunker where, starting on 4 May 1943, she reproduced copies of <i>Ljudska pravica</i> (The People’s Justice), <i>Slovenski poročevalec</i> (Slovenian Reporter), <i>Radio vestnik</i> (Messenger Radio), and various other brochures for nine months.</p>
<p>At her own choosing, on 22 January 1944 she was sent to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slovenian_Littoral" title="Slovenian Littoral">Slovenian Littoral</a>. There she served as an instructor for the Young Communist League of Yugoslavia (SKOJ) for <a href="/wiki/Idrija" title="Idrija">Idrija</a> and an instructor for SKOJ training courses for the SKOJ Regional Committee for the Littoral. However, she did not remain there long, but volunteered to work in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carinthia_(state)" title="Carinthia (state)">Carinthia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leben_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leben-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1944 she was named secretary of the District Committee of the Communist Party of Slovenia for <a href="/wiki/Me%C5%BEica" title="Mežica">Mežica</a>; she crossed the <a href="/wiki/Drava" title="Drava">Drava</a> River<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and went through the Sattnitz Mountains,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> first working in the <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lkermarkt" title="Völkermarkt">Völkermarkt</a> area<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jakopic_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jakopic-3">[3]</a></sup> and then in Klagenfurt. In the fall of 1944 she became a member of the District Committee of the Communist Party of Slovenia for Klagenfurt. Disguised as a peasant girl, she spent several months in Klagenfurt and took part in organizing committees for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberation_Front_of_the_Slovene_People" title="Liberation Front of the Slovene People">Liberation Front</a>, <a href="/wiki/Espionage" title="Espionage">espionage</a>, and illegal publications for the city.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gestapo" title="Gestapo">Gestapo</a> managed to discover her through betrayal, and on 28 February 1945<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jakopic_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jakopic-3">[3]</a></sup> she was arrested in a house below <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kreuzbergl&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kreuzbergl (page does not exist)">Kreuzbergl</a> Hill in Klagenfurt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> She was tortured in prison and shot on 4 May 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jakopic_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jakopic-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Her grave remains unmarked to this day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> She was proclaimed a people's hero of Yugoslavia on 5 July 1951<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jersek_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Jersek-2">[2]</a></sup> or 20 December 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jakopic_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Jakopic-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Majda_Vrhovnik&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Majda Vrhovnik Primary School (<span lang="sl" xml:lang="sl"><i>Osnovna šola Majde Vrhovnik</i></span>) at [Simon] Gregorčič Street (<span lang="sl" xml:lang="sl"><i>Gregorčičeva ulica</i></span>) no. 16 in Ljubljana was named for Vrhovnik in 1958,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> created by merging Primary School no. 5 on Šubic Street (<span lang="sl" xml:lang="sl"><i>Šubičeva ulica</i></span>) and Primary School no. 6 on Vrtača Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A bust of Vrhovnik was created by the Slovene sculptor <a href="/wiki/Stojan_Bati%C4%8D" title="Stojan Batič">Stojan Batič</a> in 1961 and stands in front of the school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Majda Vrhovnik" was used as a pseudonym by <a href="/wiki/JBTZ_trial" title="JBTZ trial">Franci Zavrl</a>, the editor of <i><a href="/wiki/Mladina" title="Mladina">Mladina</a></i>, after his arrest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The pseudonym "Majda Vrhovnik" was also used by the <i>Mladina</i> journalist Vlado Miheljak at the same time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
