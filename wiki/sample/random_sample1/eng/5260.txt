<p><i><b>Mazinger Z</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>: <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">マジンガーZ</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <a href="/wiki/Hepburn_romanization" title="Hepburn romanization">Hepburn</a>: <i>Majingā Zetto</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, known briefly as <i><b>Tranzor Z</b></i> in the United States)</span> is a Japanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_robot" title="Super robot">super robot</a> <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a> series written and illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Go_Nagai" title="Go Nagai">Go Nagai</a>. The first manga version was serialized in <a href="/wiki/Shueisha" title="Shueisha">Shueisha</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Weekly_Sh%C5%8Dnen_Jump" title="Weekly Shōnen Jump">Weekly Shōnen Jump</a></i> from October 1972 to August 1973, and it later continued in <a href="/wiki/Kodansha" title="Kodansha">Kodansha</a> <i>TV Magazine</i> from October 1973 to September 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Go_Nagai.27s_manga_works_page_2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Go_Nagai.27s_manga_works_page_2-2">[2]</a></sup> It was adapted into an <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a> television series which aired on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuji_TV" title="Fuji TV">Fuji TV</a> from December 1972 to September 1974. A second manga series was released alongside the TV show, this one drawn by Gosaku Ota, which started and ended almost at the same time as the TV show. <i>Mazinger Z</i> has spawned several <a href="/wiki/Sequel" title="Sequel">sequels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spin-off_(media)" title="Spin-off (media)">spinoff</a> series, among them <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UFO_Robot_Grendizer" title="UFO Robot Grendizer">UFO Robot Grendizer</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mazinkaiser" title="Mazinkaiser">Mazinkaiser</a></i>.</p>
<p>It was a very popular anime in Mexico during the 1980s, where it was dubbed into Spanish directly from the Japanese version, keeping the Japanese character names and broadcasting all 92 episodes, unlike the version aired in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pacific_Rim_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pacific_Rim-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mazinger_Z&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Mazinger_characters" title="List of Mazinger characters">List of Mazinger characters</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mazinger_Z_(robot)" title="Mazinger Z (robot)">Mazinger Z</a> is an enormous super robot, constructed with a fictitious metal called <a href="/wiki/Chogokin" title="Chogokin">Super-Alloy</a> Z <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">超合金Z</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Chōgokin Zetto</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, which is forged from a new element (Japanium) mined from a reservoir found only in the sediment of Mt. Fuji, in Japan. The mecha was built by Professor Juzo Kabuto as a secret weapon against the forces of evil, represented in the series by the Mechanical Beasts of Dr. Hell. The latter was the German member of a Japanese archeological team, which discovered ruins of a lost pre-Grecian civilization on an island named Bardos (or Birdos, although some inconsistent translations have identified the island as being the actual Greek island of <a href="/wiki/Rhodes" title="Rhodes">Rhodes</a>); the civilization was loosely based on the ancient <a href="/wiki/Mycenae" title="Mycenae">Mycenae</a>, and was called the Mycéne Empire in the series. One of their findings was that the Mycene used an army of steel titans about 20 meters in height (compare with the Greek legend of <a href="/wiki/Talos" title="Talos">Talos</a>). Finding prototypes of those titans underground which could be remote-controlled and realizing their immense power on the battlefield, Dr. Hell goes insane and has all the other scientists of his research team killed except for Professor Kabuto, who manages to escape. The lone survivor goes back to Japan and attempts to warn the world of its imminent danger. Meanwhile, Dr. Hell establishes his headquarters on a mobile island, and plans to use the Mechanical Beasts to become the new ruler of the world. To counter this, Kabuto constructs Mazinger Z and manages to finish it just before being killed by a bomb planted by Hell's right-hand 'man', Baron Ashura, a half-man, half-woman being. As he lays dying, he manages to inform his grandson Kouji Kabuto about the robot and its use. Kouji becomes the robot's pilot, and from that point on battles both the continuous mechanical monsters, and the sinister henchmen sent by Doctor Hell.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tranzor_Z"><i>Tranzor Z</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mazinger_Z&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tranzor Z">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the United States, Three B. Productions Ltd., a production company headed by Bunker Jenkins, developed <i>Mazinger Z</i> for American television by producing an English-dubbed version, which Jenkins retitled <i><b>Tranzor Z</b></i>. This adaptation aired in 1985, and was, like many English-dubbed anime shows that were on American TV at the time, re-edited for American audiences. Many of the Japanese names used in <i>Mazinger Z</i> were changed for its adaptation into <i>Tranzor Z;</i> for example, Koji Kabuto became Tommy Davis, Sayaka became Jessica, Shiro became Toad, Professor Kabuto became Dr. Wells, Dr. Hell became Dr. Daemon, and Baron Ashura became Devleen. Only 65 out of the 92 episodes were dubbed into English, as 65 was the minimum amount of episodes required for syndication. An earlier English dub that was more faithful to the original was produced and aired in Hawaii for around 30 episodes in the late 1970s.</p>

<p>The Mazinger Z anime ran to a total of 92 TV episodes from 1972 to 1974. Its period of greatest popularity lasted from roughly October 1973 to March 1974, during which time it regularly scored audience ratings in the high twenties; episode 68, broadcast March 17, 1974, achieved the series' highest rating of 30.4%, making Mazinger Z one of the highest-rated anime series of all time (<a class="external text" href="http://www.mazingerz.com/ANIME/TVmazinger.html" rel="nofollow">1</a>). It culminated in the destruction of the original robot by new enemies (after Doctor Hell's final defeat in the penultimate episode) and the immediate introduction of its successor, <a href="/wiki/Great_Mazinger" title="Great Mazinger">Great Mazinger</a>, an improved version of Mazinger, along with its pilot, Tetsuya Tsurugi. The idea of replacing the first robot with <a href="/wiki/Great_Mazinger" title="Great Mazinger">Great Mazinger</a> (sometimes called Shin Mazinger Z) is a variation of a death-rebirth myth found in most Japanese action series: the title character, even if it is only a robot, is never truly defeated or destroyed, only improved upon, and replaced by the next version. Koji and Mazinger Z come back in the last episodes of Great Mazinger to help their successors defeat the forces of evil.</p>
<p>Another sequel, albeit in a different line, was introduced in 1975, with the appearance of <a href="/wiki/Grendizer" title="Grendizer">Grendizer</a>, set in the Mazinger and Great Mazinger story continuity that included Koji Kabuto as a supporting character.</p>
<p>The shows spawned so-called "team-up movies" early on, which were like longer episodes that teamed up Mazinger Z with one of <a href="/wiki/Go_Nagai" title="Go Nagai">Go Nagai</a>'s other creations, as in <i>Mazinger Z vs. Devilman</i> (マジンガーZ対デビルマン) in 1973 as well as <i>Mazinger Z Vs. Dr. Hell</i> (マジンガーＺ対ドクターヘル) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mazinger_Z_Vs._The_Great_General_of_Darkness" title="Mazinger Z Vs. The Great General of Darkness">Mazinger Z Vs. The Great General of Darkness</a></i> (マジンガーZ対暗黒大将軍) both released in 1974.</p>

<p>In the 1980s, on behalf of <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_Planning" title="Dynamic Planning">Dynamic Planning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Masami_%C5%8Cbari" title="Masami Ōbari">Masami Ōbari</a> and other independent animators ( <a href="/wiki/Toshiki_Hirano" title="Toshiki Hirano">Toshiki Hirano</a> ) not part of <a href="/wiki/Toei_Animation" title="Toei Animation">Toei Animation</a> began work on a miniseries of <i>Mazinger Z</i>. The OVA (Original Video Animation) would have been called <i>Dai-Mazinger</i> (or <i>Daimajinga</i>, 大魔神我) and would have presented the same characters known to the general public, starting with the main protagonist Koji. The robot would be more realistic: for example, it would have exhaust pipes and its rocket fists would not be able to automatically return to its arms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The news, initially protected by a tight secrecy, managed to leak and were spread by the specialized press. Toei protested, saying to Dynamic that the rights of the animation of <i>Mazinger</i> was only theirs and that they did not tolerate a <i>Mazinger</i> animated by others. As a consequence, the project <i>Daimajinga</i> was blocked. This wasn't helped with the fact that Nagai was in the middle of a court battle with Toei, suing them for not properly crediting him and not paying him royalties over the creation of <a href="/wiki/Gaiking" title="Gaiking">Gaiking</a> in 1976. However, since then the relationship between Nagai and Toei had steadily improved.</p>
<p>Thirty years after the start of the original program, Nagai's company Dynamic Planning released a continuation of the original Mazinger series as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OVA" title="OVA">OVA</a>—named <i><a href="/wiki/Mazinkaiser" title="Mazinkaiser">Mazinkaiser</a></i> (mazinkaizā)—in 2002. This work would be succeeded by the movie <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mazinkaiser:_Deathmatch!_Ankoku_Daishogun&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mazinkaiser: Deathmatch! Ankoku Daishogun (page does not exist)">Mazinkaiser: Deathmatch! Ankoku Daishogun</a></i>, which in some ways served as a partial <a href="/wiki/Remake" title="Remake">remake</a> of <i>Mazinger Z vs. the General of Darkness.</i></p>
<p>Since 2007, several rumors surfaced regarding a new series which would be based on the <i><a href="/wiki/Z_Mazinger" title="Z Mazinger">Z Mazinger</a></i> manga. In February 2009, it was officially announced a new Mazinger anime called <i><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shin_Mazinger_Shougeki!_Z_Hen" title="Shin Mazinger Shougeki! Z Hen">Shin Mazinger Shougeki! Z Hen</a></b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">真マジンガー　衝撃!　Z編</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Shin Majingā Shōgeki! Z Hen</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> which later began airing on April 4, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the 2010 June issue of the magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Hobby_Japan" title="Hobby Japan">Hobby Japan</a></i>, released on April 2010, a new OVA series was revealed. It will be called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mazinkaiser_SKL" title="Mazinkaiser SKL">Mazinkaizer SKL</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">マジンカイザーSKL</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Majinkaizā SKL</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mazinkaizer_SKL_Anime.2C_Manga.2C_Novel_Revealed_.28Updated.29_-_Anime_News_Network_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mazinkaizer_SKL_Anime.2C_Manga.2C_Novel_Revealed_.28Updated.29_-_Anime_News_Network-6">[6]</a></sup> The OVA is planned to have also a novelization, which will be serialized in <a href="/wiki/ASCII_Media_Works" title="ASCII Media Works">ASCII Media Works</a> magazine, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dengeki_Hobby" title="Dengeki Hobby">Dengeki Hobby</a></i>, and a manga, a net manga to be published in Emotion (<a href="/wiki/Bandai_Visual" title="Bandai Visual">Bandai Visual</a>) <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shu_2_Comic_Gekkin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shu 2 Comic Gekkin (page does not exist)">Shu 2 Comic Gekkin</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mazinkaizer_SKL_Anime.2C_Manga.2C_Novel_Revealed_.28Updated.29_-_Anime_News_Network_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mazinkaizer_SKL_Anime.2C_Manga.2C_Novel_Revealed_.28Updated.29_-_Anime_News_Network-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Merchandise">Merchandise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mazinger_Z&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Merchandise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mazinger remains one of <a href="/wiki/Go_Nagai" title="Go Nagai">Go Nagai</a>'s most enduring success stories, spawning many <a href="/wiki/Mazinger_Toy_Lines" title="Mazinger Toy Lines">products</a> in the realm of merchandising, model kits, plastic and die-cast metal toys (the now famous <a href="/wiki/Soul_of_Chogokin" title="Soul of Chogokin">Soul of Chogokin</a> line), action figures and other collectibles. Mazinger has also been successful in the video game area (at least in Japan), as one of the main stars in the acclaimed battle simulation game series <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Robot_Wars" title="Super Robot Wars">Super Robot Wars</a></i>, released by <a href="/wiki/Banpresto" title="Banpresto">Banpresto</a>, featuring characters and units from almost all Mazinger-related shows, alongside other anime franchises.</p>
<p>In 1994, Banpresto released an arcade game called <i>Mazinger Z</i> which was a vertical <a href="/wiki/Shoot_%27em_up" title="Shoot 'em up">shoot 'em up</a> with three selectable characters : Mazinger Z, <a href="/wiki/Great_Mazinger" title="Great Mazinger">Great Mazinger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grendizer" title="Grendizer">Grendizer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 40-feet statue of Mazinger Z was built in a suburb called "Mas del Plata" in <a href="/wiki/Tarragona" title="Tarragona">Tarragona</a> (<a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>) in the early 1980s, to serve as the suburb's entrance, yet the suburb was never completed and the statue remains there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="DVD">DVD</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mazinger_Z&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: DVD">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Discotek_Media" title="Discotek Media">Discotek Media</a> acquired the American home video rights to the show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The result was a release of all 92 episodes of the original series in 2 volumes: Mazinger Z TV Series Vol 1 Ep 1–46 and Mazinger Z TV Series Vol 2 Ep 47–92.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_and_influence">Reception and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mazinger_Z&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reception and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Mazinger Z</i> helped to create the 1970s boom in mecha anime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The series is noteworthy for introducing many of the accepted stock features of super robot anime genres: the first occurrence of mecha robots being piloted by a user from within a cockpit,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gilson_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gilson-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, the Japanese magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Animage" title="Animage">Animage</a></i> elected <i>Mazinger Z</i> TV series the eleventh best anime production of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guillermo_Del_Toro" title="Guillermo Del Toro">Guillermo Del Toro</a> has cited the show – which was a huge success in his native Mexico during the 1980s – as an important influence on <i><a href="/wiki/Pacific_Rim_(film)" title="Pacific Rim (film)">Pacific Rim</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pacific_Rim_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pacific_Rim-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
