<p>A <b>macaron</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i>mah-kə-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">ROHN</span></span></b></i></a></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[makaʁɔ̃]</a></span>) is a sweet <a href="/wiki/Meringue" title="Meringue">meringue</a>-based confection made with <a href="/wiki/Egg_white" title="Egg white">egg white</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icing_sugar" title="Icing sugar">icing sugar</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Granulated_sugar" title="Granulated sugar">granulated sugar</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almond_powder" title="Almond powder">almond powder</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_almond" title="Ground almond">ground almond</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_colouring" title="Food colouring">food colouring</a>. The macaron is commonly filled with <a href="/wiki/Ganache" title="Ganache">ganache</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buttercream" title="Buttercream">buttercream</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jam" title="Jam">jam</a> filling sandwiched between two cookies. The name is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> word <span lang="it"><i>macarone</i></span>, <span lang="it"><i>maccarone</i></span> or <span lang="it"><i>maccherone</i></span>, the <a href="/wiki/Meringue" title="Meringue">meringue</a>.</p>
<p>The confection is characterized by a smooth squared top, a ruffled circumference (referred to as the "foot" or "pied"), and a flat base. It is mildly moist and easily melts in the mouth. Macarons can be found in a wide variety of flavors that range from the traditional (<a href="/wiki/Raspberry" title="Raspberry">raspberry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolate</a>) to the new (<a href="/wiki/Foie_gras" title="Foie gras">foie gras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matcha" title="Matcha">matcha</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> They are often said to be difficult to make.</p>
<p>The related <a href="/wiki/Macaroon" title="Macaroon">macaroon</a> is often confused with the macaron. In English, most bakers have adopted the French spelling of <i>macaron</i> for the meringue-based item, to distinguish the two. This has caused confusion over the correct spelling. Some recipes exclude the use of <i>macaroon</i> to refer to this French confection while others treat the two as synonymous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macaron_vs_Macaroon_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Macaron_vs_Macaroon-3">[3]</a></sup> The two food items are different, and the terms in English distinguish them. Etymologically, the word <i>macaroon</i> is simply an <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">Anglicisation</a> of the French word <i>macaron</i> (compare <i><a href="/wiki/Balloon" title="Balloon">balloon</a></i>, from French <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ballon" title="wikt:ballon">ballon</a></i>). Multiple pronunciations are technically correct depending on personal preference and context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macaron_vs_Macaroon_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Macaron_vs_Macaroon-3">[3]</a></sup> In a <i>Slate</i> article on the topic, Stanford professor of linguistics and computer science Dan Jurafsky indicates that "macaron" (also, "macaron parisien", or "le macaron Gerbet") is the correct spelling for the confection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macaron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Macarons have been produced in the Venetian monasteries since the 8th century A.D. During the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catherine_de%27_Medici" title="Catherine de' Medici">Catherine de' Medici</a>'s Italian pastry chefs whom she brought with her in 1533 upon marrying <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_France" title="Henry II of France">Henry II of France</a> arrived in France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Larousse_Gastronomique" title="Larousse Gastronomique">Larousse Gastronomique</a></i> cites the macaron created in 1791 in a convent near <a href="/wiki/Cormery" title="Cormery">Cormery</a>. In 1792, macarons began to gain fame when two Carmelite nuns, seeking asylum in Nancy during the French Revolution, baked and sold the macaron cookies in order to pay for their housing. These nuns became known as the "Macaron Sisters". In these early stages, macarons were served without special flavors or fillings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was not until the 1830s that macarons began to be served two-by-two with the addition of jams, liqueurs, and spices. The macaron as it is known today, composed of two almond meringue discs filled with a layer of buttercream, jam, or <a href="/wiki/Ganache" title="Ganache">ganache</a> filling, was originally called the "Gerbet" or the "Paris macaron." Pierre Desfontaines of the French pâtisserie <a href="/wiki/Ladur%C3%A9e" title="Ladurée">Ladurée</a> has sometimes been credited with its creation in the early part of the 20th century, but another baker, Claude Gerbet, also claims to have invented it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jurafsky_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jurafsky-8">[8]</a></sup> French macaron bakeries became trendy in North America in the 2010s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earliest_recipe">Earliest recipe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macaron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Earliest recipe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many Italian cookbooks of the 16th century mention almond biscuits closely resembling macarons albeit under different names. The earliest known recipe dated back from the early 17th century and appears to be inspired by a French version of the recipe.</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p><b>To make French Macaroones</b><br/>
Wash a pound of the newest and the best Iordane Almonds in three or foure waters, to take away the rednesse from their out-side, lay them in a Bason of warme water all night, the next day blanche them, and dry them with a faire cloath, beat them in a stone morter, until they be reasonably fine, put to them halfe a pound of fine beaten sugar, and so beat it to a perfect Paste, then put in halfe a dozen spoonefuls of good Damaske Rose-water, three graines of Ambergreece, when you have beaten all this together, dry it on a chasingdish of coales until it grow white and stiffe, then take it off the fire, and put the whites of two Egs first beaten into froath, and so stire it well together, then lay them on wafers in fashion of little long rowles, and so bake in an Oven as hot as for Manchet, but you must first let the heat of the Oven passe over before you put them in, when they rise white and light, take them out of the Oven, and put them in a warm platter, and set them againe into the warme Oven &amp; so let them remain foure or five houres, and then they wil be thoroughly dry, but if you like them better being moist then dry them not after the first baking.<br/>
John Murrell, <i>Daily exercises for gentlewomen</i> (1617).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</div>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Method">Method</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macaron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Method">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two methods to making a macaron - the "French" method and the "Italian" method. The difference between the two is the way the meringue is made - either Italian or French meringue can be combined with ground almonds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>A macaron is made by combining icing sugar and ground almonds until fine. In a separate bowl, egg whites that are beaten until a meringue-like texture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The two elements are then folded together until they are the consistency of "shaving foam", and then are piped, left to form a skin, and baked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Sometimes, a filling is added.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_variations">American variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macaron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: American variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Flavors of macarons available in America are available in respect to the general tastes of the public. These include flavors such as mint chocolate chip, peanut butter and jelly, snickers, peach champagne, pistachio, strawberry cheesecake, candy corn, salted pretzel, chocolate peanut butter, oatmeal raisin, candy cane, cinnamon, maple bacon, pumpkin, and salted caramel popcorn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="French_regional_variations">French regional variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macaron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: French regional variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several French cities and regions claim long histories and variations, notably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorraine_(region)" title="Lorraine (region)">Lorraine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Nancy,_France" title="Nancy, France">Nancy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boulay" title="Boulay">Boulay</a>), <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(greater_region)" title="Basque Country (greater region)">Basque Country</a> (<a href="/wiki/Saint-Jean-de-Luz" title="Saint-Jean-de-Luz">Saint-Jean-de-Luz</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint-Emilion" title="Saint-Emilion">Saint-Emilion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amiens" title="Amiens">Amiens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montmorillon" title="Montmorillon">Montmorillon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Le_Dorat" title="Le Dorat">Le Dorat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sault,_Vaucluse" title="Sault, Vaucluse">Sault</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chartres" title="Chartres">Chartres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cormery" title="Cormery">Cormery</a> <a href="/wiki/Joyeuse" title="Joyeuse">Joyeuse</a> and Sainte-Croix in Burgundy.</p>
<p><span id="Macarons_d.27Amiens"></span> Macarons d'Amiens, made in <a href="/wiki/Amiens" title="Amiens">Amiens</a>, are small, round-shaped biscuit-type macarons made from <a href="/wiki/Almond_paste" title="Almond paste">almond paste</a>, fruit and honey, which were first recorded in 1855.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city of <a href="/wiki/Montmorillon" title="Montmorillon">Montmorillon</a> is well known for its macarons and has a museum dedicated to it. The Maison Rannou-Métivier is the oldest macaron bakery in Montmorillon, dating back to 1920. The traditional recipe for Montmorillon macarons remains unchanged for over 150 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The town of <a href="/wiki/Nancy,_France" title="Nancy, France">Nancy</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorraine_(region)" title="Lorraine (region)">Lorraine</a> region has a storied history with the macaron. It is said that the abbess of <a href="/wiki/Remiremont" title="Remiremont">Remiremont</a> founded an order of nuns called the "Dames du Saint-Sacrement" with strict dietary rules prohibiting the consumption of meat. Two nuns, Sisters Marguerite and Marie-Elisabeth are credited with creating the Nancy macaron to fit their dietary requirements. They became known as the 'Macaron Sisters' (Les Soeurs Macarons). In 1952, the city of Nancy honored them by giving their name to the Rue de la Hache, where the macaron was invented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Switzerland">Switzerland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macaron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Switzerland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> the Luxemburgerli (also Luxembourger) is a brand name of <a href="/wiki/Confectionery" title="Confectionery">confectionery</a> made by the <a href="/wiki/Confiserie_Spr%C3%BCngli" title="Confiserie Sprüngli">Confiserie Sprüngli</a> in <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>. A Luxemburgerli is a macaron<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> comprising two disks of almond meringue<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> with a buttercream filling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Luxemburgerli are smaller and lighter than macarons from many other vendors. It is said to be lighter and more airy in consistency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Flavors include: vanilla, chocolate, <a href="/wiki/Stracciatella_(ice_cream)" title="Stracciatella (ice cream)">stracciatella</a> (chocolate chip), caramel, hazelnut, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Champagne_(wine)" title="Champagne (wine)">champagne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amaretto" title="Amaretto">amaretto</a>, chestnut, mocha, cinnamon, lemon, <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_orange" title="Mandarin orange">mandarin</a>, and raspberry. Many flavors are seasonal. The shelf life is three to five days, refrigerated.</p>
<p>Luxemburgerli were invented by the confectioner Camille Studer who brought the recipe to Zürich after creating them in a <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a> confectionery shop (Confiserie Namur) in 1957. There, the recipe was refined for a confectionery contest. The name Luxemburgerli is derived from the nickname which a colleague bestowed on Studer, whose family originated in Luxembourg. The original name, <i>Baiser de Mousse</i> (<i>foam kiss</i> in French), perceived as appropriate for the new creation, was changed to <i>Gebäck des Luxemburgers</i> ("Luxemburger's confection") which became, in <a href="/wiki/Swiss_German" title="Swiss German">Swiss German</a>, Luxemburgerli ("little Luxembourger").</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Korea">Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macaron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Macarons are popular in <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, pronounced as "ma-ka-rong" in <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_tea_powder" title="Green tea powder">Green tea powder</a> or <a href="/wiki/Green_tea" title="Green tea">leaves</a> can be used to make green tea macarons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macaron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Macarons in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> are a popular confection known as "makaron".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> There is also a version of the same name which substitutes <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanut</a> flour for almond and is flavored in <a href="/wiki/Wagashi" title="Wagashi">wagashi</a> style, widely available in Japan. The "makaron" is featured in Japanese fashion through cell phone accessories, stickers, and cosmetics aimed towards women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popularity">Popularity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macaron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Popularity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Paris, the <a href="/wiki/Ladur%C3%A9e" title="Ladurée">Ladurée</a> chain of pastry shops has been known for its macarons for about 150 years<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Macaron&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ1_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ1-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimes1_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes1-30">[30]</a></sup> In France, <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a> sells macarons in their <a href="/wiki/McCaf%C3%A9" title="McCafé">McCafés</a> (sometimes using advertising that likens the shape of a macaron to that of a hamburger).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ1_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ1-29">[29]</a></sup> McCafé macarons are produced by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ch%C3%A2teau_Blanc&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Château Blanc (page does not exist)">Château Blanc</a>, which, like Ladurée, is a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Groupe_Holder" title="Groupe Holder">Groupe Holder</a>, though they do not use the same macaron recipe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ1_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ1-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Outside of Europe, the French-style macaron can be found in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada.com1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canada.com1-31">[31]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SeattleTimes1_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-SeattleTimes1-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LATimes1_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-LATimes1-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR1_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPR1-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adriano_Zumbo" title="Adriano Zumbo">Adriano Zumbo</a> along with the TV series <a href="/wiki/MasterChef" title="MasterChef">MasterChef</a> have seen the macaron become a popular sweet treat, and it is now sold by McDonald's in its McCafe outlets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>On a global level, March 20 celebrates "Macaron Day". Created in 2005 in Paris by la Maison <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Herm%C3%A9" title="Pierre Hermé">Pierre Hermé</a>, it is a tradition that spread across the world. On this day, participating bakeries and macaron shops around the world offer customers one free sample macaron. A percentage of all additional macaron sales is donated to a local charity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Korea, the macaron has been a 'trend'. The treat became so popular, that some companies started delivery services for macarons. Macarons have become a popular gift in Korea between friends and lovers, as a symbol of affection. Moreover, famous hotels (Lotte Department) and buffets (Ashely) began to provide mountainous stacks of macarons as a way of attracting more guests into their business.</p>
