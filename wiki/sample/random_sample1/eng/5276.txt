<p><b>Marcus J. Borg</b> (March 11, 1942 – January 21, 2015) was an American <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> scholar, theologian and author.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He was among the most widely known and influential voices in <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Christianity" title="Progressive Christianity">progressive Christianity</a>. As a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Jesus_Seminar" title="Jesus Seminar">Jesus Seminar</a>, Borg was a major figure in <a href="/wiki/Historical_Jesus" title="Historical Jesus">historical Jesus</a> scholarship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-explore_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-explore-2">[2]</a></sup> He retired as Hundere Distinguished Professor of Religion and Culture at <a href="/wiki/Oregon_State_University" title="Oregon State University">Oregon State University</a> in 2007 and died eight years later at the age of 72, of <a href="/wiki/Idiopathic_pulmonary_fibrosis" title="Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis">idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcus_Borg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Borg was born March 11, 1942, in <a href="/wiki/Fergus_Falls,_Minnesota" title="Fergus Falls, Minnesota">Fergus Falls, Minnesota</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and raised in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a> family in <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a>. After high school he attended <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concordia_College_(Moorhead)" title="Concordia College (Moorhead)">Concordia College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Moorhead,_Minnesota" title="Moorhead, Minnesota">Moorhead, Minnesota</a>. Initially planning to be an astrophysicist, he decided to major in math and physics. He later changed his major to political science and philosophy. Though plagued by doubt as a young adult, after his undergraduate studies Borg accepted a Rockefeller Brothers Theological Fellowship to study at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Theological_Seminary_(New_York)" title="Union Theological Seminary (New York)">Union Seminary</a> in New York City, where he became familiarized with <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Christianity" title="Liberal Christianity">liberal theology</a>. One of the profound influences on Borg during his seminary years was the professor and noted theologian <a href="/wiki/W._D._Davies" title="W. D. Davies">W. D. Davies</a>. After his studies at Union, he matriculated at <a href="/wiki/Mansfield_College,_Oxford" title="Mansfield College, Oxford">Mansfield College, Oxford</a>, where he earned his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M.Th." title="M.Th.">M.Th.</a> and his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D._Phil" title="D. Phil">D. Phil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcus_Borg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Borg taught at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concordia_College_(Moorhead)" title="Concordia College (Moorhead)">Concordia College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Moorhead,_Minnesota" title="Moorhead, Minnesota">Moorhead, Minnesota</a>, from 1966 to 1969 and 1972 to 1974; <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota_State_University" title="South Dakota State University">South Dakota State University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brookings,_South_Dakota" title="Brookings, South Dakota">Brookings</a> from 1975 to 1976; and <a href="/wiki/Carleton_College" title="Carleton College">Carleton College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northfield,_Minnesota" title="Northfield, Minnesota">Northfield, Minnesota</a>, from 1976 to 1979. He was a faculty member at <a href="/wiki/Oregon_State_University" title="Oregon State University">Oregon State University</a> from 1979 until his retirement in 2007 as Distinguished Professor in Religion and Culture and the Hundere Endowed Chair in Religious Studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Borg was appointed Chair of the Religious Studies Department in January 1988. The Religious Studies Department was closed at the end of the 1991–1992 academic year and Borg became a faculty member in the Philosophy Department.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> During his time at Oregon State he organized and led two nationally televised symposia, one in 1996 (Jesus at 2000), and another in 2000 (God at 2000). Borg also served as Visiting Professor of New Testament at the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_School_of_Religion" title="Pacific School of Religion">Pacific School of Religion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkeley</a> (1989–1991) and the Chism Distinguished Visiting Professor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Puget_Sound" title="University of Puget Sound">University of Puget Sound</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tacoma,_Washington" title="Tacoma, Washington">Tacoma</a>, Washington (1986–1987).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Borg has been national chair of the Historical Jesus Section of the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Biblical_Literature" title="Society of Biblical Literature">Society of Biblical Literature</a>, co-chair of its International New Testament Program Committee and president of the Anglican Association of Biblical Scholars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> On May 31, 2009, he was installed as the first canon theologian at <a href="/wiki/Trinity_Episcopal_Cathedral_(Portland,_Oregon)" title="Trinity Episcopal Cathedral (Portland, Oregon)">Trinity Episcopal Cathedral</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Borg has been a frequent collaborator with his friend <a href="/wiki/John_Dominic_Crossan" title="John Dominic Crossan">John Dominic Crossan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He was also a friend of <a href="/wiki/N._T._Wright" title="N. T. Wright">N. T. Wright</a>, despite their theological differences. The two discussed their differences in their book <i>The Meaning of Jesus: Two Visions</i> (1999, rev. 2007).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Borg has frequently been featured in programs on networks such as <i><a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">PBS</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/NPR" title="NPR">NPR</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Channel" title="National Geographic Channel">National Geographic</a></i>, and has also made appearances on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ABC_World_News" title="ABC World News">ABC World News</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Today_Show" title="The Today Show">The Today Show</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 2001, he debated <a href="/wiki/William_Lane_Craig" title="William Lane Craig">William Lane Craig</a> over the resurrection of Jesus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Borg has also debated <a href="/wiki/Craig_Blomberg" title="Craig Blomberg">Craig Blomberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_White_(theologian)" title="James White (theologian)">James White</a> on topics such as the historical reliability of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gospels" title="Gospels">gospels</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Historical_Jesus" title="Historical Jesus">historical Jesus</a>.</p>
