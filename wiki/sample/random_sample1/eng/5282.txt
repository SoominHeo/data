<p><b>Mark Eugene Russinovich</b> (born <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1966) is <a href="/wiki/Chief_technology_officer" title="Chief technology officer">CTO</a> of <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Azure" title="Microsoft Azure">Microsoft Azure</a>. He was a cofounder of software producers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winternals" title="Winternals">Winternals</a> before it was acquired by Microsoft in 2006.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Russinovich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Russinovich was born in <a href="/wiki/Salamanca" title="Salamanca">Salamanca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and was raised in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham, Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, until he was 15, when he moved with his family to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</a>. His father was a radiologist and his mother was a business administrator of his father's radiology practice in Pittsburgh. Russinovich is of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Croatian_American" title="Croatian American">Croatian ancestry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnevno_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-dnevno-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was introduced to computers when his friend's father got an <a href="/wiki/Apple_II" title="Apple II">Apple II</a> in the 1970s. He was able to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reverse_coding" title="Reverse coding">reverse engineer</a> its <a href="/wiki/Read-only_memory" title="Read-only memory">ROM</a> and write programs for it. At age 15, he bought himself his first computer, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TI99/4A" title="TI99/4A">TI99/4A</a>. About six months later his parents bought him an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_II%2B" title="Apple II+">Apple II+</a> from his local high school when it upgraded the computer labs to <a href="/wiki/Apple_IIe" title="Apple IIe">Apple IIe</a>'s. He also wrote magazine articles about Apple II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1989, Russinovich earned his <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> degree in <a href="/wiki/Computer_engineering" title="Computer engineering">computer engineering</a> from <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a>, where he was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Pi_Kappa_Alpha" title="Pi Kappa Alpha">Pi Kappa Alpha</a> Beta Sigma chapter<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The following year he received a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">Master of Science</a> degree in computer engineering from <a href="/wiki/Rensselaer_Polytechnic_Institute" title="Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute">Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute</a>. He later returned to Carnegie Mellon, where he received a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ph.D." title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> in computer engineering in 1994<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> with thesis titled <i><a class="external text" href="http://search.proquest.com/docview/304086659" rel="nofollow">Application-transparent fault management</a></i> under the supervision of Zary Segall.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Russinovich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From September 1994 through February 1996 he was a research associate with the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oregon" title="University of Oregon">University of Oregon</a>'s computer science department. From February through September 1996 he was a developer with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NuMega_Technologies" title="NuMega Technologies">NuMega Technologies</a>, where he worked on performance monitoring software for <a href="/wiki/Windows_NT" title="Windows NT">Windows NT</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sony_Affidavit_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sony_Affidavit-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1996, he and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bryce_Cogswell" title="Bryce Cogswell">Bryce Cogswell</a> cofounded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winternals_Software" title="Winternals Software">Winternals Software</a>, where Russinovich served as Chief Software Architect, and the web site <span class="url"><a class="external text" href="http://sysinternals.com" rel="nofollow">sysinternals<wbr></wbr>.com</a></span>, where Russinovich wrote and published dozens of popular Windows administration and diagnostic utilities including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autoruns" title="Autoruns">Autoruns</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filemon" title="Filemon">Filemon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regmon" title="Regmon">Regmon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Process_Explorer" title="Process Explorer">Process Explorer</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=TCPView&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="TCPView (page does not exist)">TCPView</a>, and <a href="/wiki/RootkitRevealer" title="RootkitRevealer">RootkitRevealer</a> among many others.</p>
<p>From September 1996 through September 1997 he worked as a consulting associate at OSR Open Systems Resources, Inc., a company based in <a href="/wiki/Amherst,_New_Hampshire" title="Amherst, New Hampshire">Amherst, New Hampshire</a>. From September 1997 through March 2000, he was a research staff member at <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Thomas_J._Watson_Research_Center" title="Thomas J. Watson Research Center">Thomas J. Watson Research Center</a>, researching operating system support for Web server acceleration and serving as an operating systems expert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sony_Affidavit_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sony_Affidavit-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Russinovich joined Microsoft in 2006, when it acquired Winternals Software.</p>
<p>In his role as an author, he is a regular contributor to <i>TechNet Magazine</i> and <i>Windows IT Pro</i> magazine (previously called <i>Windows NT Magazine</i>) on the subject of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Architecture_of_Windows_2000" title="Architecture of Windows 2000">Architecture of Windows 2000</a> and was co-author of <i>Inside Windows 2000</i> (third edition). Russinovich is the author of many tools used by <a href="/wiki/Windows_NT" title="Windows NT">Windows NT</a> and <a href="/wiki/Windows_2000" title="Windows 2000">Windows 2000</a> kernel-mode <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">programmers</a>, and of the <a href="/wiki/NTFS" title="NTFS">NTFS</a> file system driver for <a href="/wiki/DOS" title="DOS">DOS</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Russinovich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1996, Russinovich discovered that altering two values in the <a href="/wiki/Windows_Registry" title="Windows Registry">Windows Registry</a> of the Workstation edition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windows_NT_4" title="Windows NT 4">Windows NT 4</a> would change the installation so it was recognized as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windows_NT_Server" title="Windows NT Server">Windows NT Server</a> and allow the installation of <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_BackOffice_Server" title="Microsoft BackOffice Server">Microsoft BackOffice</a> products which were licensed only for the Server edition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ntreg_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ntreg-5">[5]</a></sup> The registry key values were guarded by a worker thread to detect tampering, and later a program called NT Tune was released to kill the monitor thread and change the values.</p>
<p>In 2005, Russinovich discovered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_rootkit" title="Sony rootkit">Sony rootkit</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> <a href="/wiki/Digital_rights_management" title="Digital rights management">DRM</a> products. Its function was to prevent users from copying their media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sony_Affidavit_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sony_Affidavit-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2006, Russinovich discovered a <a href="/wiki/Rootkit" title="Rootkit">rootkit</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norton_SystemWorks" title="Norton SystemWorks">Norton SystemWorks</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symantec_Corporation" title="Symantec Corporation">Symantec Corporation</a>. Symantec directly removed the rootkit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He also analyzed the <a href="/wiki/Windows_Metafile_vulnerability" title="Windows Metafile vulnerability">Windows Metafile vulnerability</a> and concluded that it was not a deliberate <a href="/wiki/Backdoor_(computing)" title="Backdoor (computing)">backdoor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This possibility had been raised – albeit tentatively – by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Gibson_(computer_programmer)" title="Steve Gibson (computer programmer)">Steve Gibson</a> after a cursory investigation of the nature of the exploit and its mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Russinovich's novels <i>Zero Day</i> (foreword by <a href="/wiki/Howard_Schmidt" title="Howard Schmidt">Howard Schmidt</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rogue_Code_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rogue_Code-11">[11]</a></sup> and <i>Trojan Horse</i> (foreword by <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Mitnick" title="Kevin Mitnick">Kevin Mitnick</a>) were published by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Dunne_Books" title="Thomas Dunne Books">Thomas Dunne Books</a> on March 15, 2011 and September 4, 2012. Both are in a series of popular <a href="/wiki/Techno-thriller" title="Techno-thriller">techno-thrillers</a>, that have attracted praise from industry insiders such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mikko_Hypponen" title="Mikko Hypponen">Mikko Hypponen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Suarez_(author)" title="Daniel Suarez (author)">Daniel Suarez</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rogue_Code_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rogue_Code-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A short story, "Operation Desolation"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Operation_Desolation_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Operation_Desolation-13">[13]</a></sup> was published just before Trojan Horse and takes place 1 year after the events of Zero Day. Book 3, <i>Rogue Code: A Novel</i> (Jeff Aiken Series, May 2014) deals with vulnerabilities of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NYSE" title="NYSE">NYSE</a>. It has a foreword by Haim Bodek, author of <i>The Problem of HFT: Collected Writings on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Frequency_Trading" title="High Frequency Trading">High Frequency Trading</a> &amp; Stock Market Structure Reform</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rogue_Code_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rogue_Code-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
