<p><b>Mark William Lippert</b> (born February 28, 1973) was the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassador_to_South_Korea" title="United States Ambassador to South Korea">United States Ambassador to South Korea</a>. Prior to his tour as Ambassador, Lippert had served as Chief of Staff for <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">Secretary of Defense</a> <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Hagel" title="Chuck Hagel">Chuck Hagel</a>, Chief of Staff for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Security_Council" title="United States National Security Council">National Security Council</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Assistant_Secretary_of_Defense_for_Asian_and_Pacific_Security_Affairs" title="Assistant Secretary of Defense for Asian and Pacific Security Affairs">Assistant Secretary of Defense for Asian and Pacific Security Affairs</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a>. On March 5, 2015, he was attacked by a man wielding a knife in South Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Lippert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lippert was born in and grew up in <a href="/wiki/Mariemont,_Ohio" title="Mariemont, Ohio">Mariemont, Ohio</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> a suburb just outside <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He is the son of Susan (Bridges) and James William Lippert, a lawyer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-From_the_Campaign_to_the_Battlefront_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-From_the_Campaign_to_the_Battlefront-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Lippert graduated from <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> where he studied political science as an undergraduate and received a <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">master's degree</a> in international policy studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Aide_Who_Went_to_War_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Aide_Who_Went_to_War-7">[7]</a></sup> While in graduate school at Stanford, he studied <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin Chinese</a> at <a href="/wiki/Peking_University" title="Peking University">Peking University</a> as part of a study abroad program. As a result, Lippert is known in Mandarin as <i>Lee Mokai</i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">李模楷</span>), the Chinese name he chose for himself during his stay in Beijing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Lippert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to 1999, he worked at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">State Department</a> and for <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> Senator <a href="/wiki/Dianne_Feinstein" title="Dianne Feinstein">Dianne Feinstein</a>. Lippert was a defense and foreign policy advisor to then Senator and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senate_Minority_Leader" title="Senate Minority Leader">Senate Minority Leader</a> <a href="/wiki/Tom_Daschle" title="Tom Daschle">Tom Daschle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Senate_Democratic_Policy_Committee" title="Senate Democratic Policy Committee">Senate Democratic Policy Committee</a> from 1999 to October 2000. He then served as a researcher for Senator <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Leahy" title="Patrick Leahy">Patrick Leahy</a> from October 2000 to February 2001. Lippert served on the professional staff of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Appropriations" title="United States Senate Committee on Appropriations">Senate Appropriations Committee</a>, State–Foreign Operations Subcommittee, from February 2001 to June 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lippert_Biography_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lippert_Biography-9">[9]</a></sup> In June 2005, he became a foreign policy advisor to then Senator <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>, who was then serving on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Foreign_Relations_Committee" title="United States Senate Foreign Relations Committee">Senate Foreign Relations committee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lippert_Biography_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lippert_Biography-9">[9]</a></sup> He was recruited by Senator Obama's Chief of Staff, <a href="/wiki/Pete_Rouse" title="Pete Rouse">Pete Rouse</a>, who is currently serving as <a href="/wiki/Counselor_to_the_President" title="Counselor to the President">Counselor to the President</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-From_the_Campaign_to_the_Battlefront_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-From_the_Campaign_to_the_Battlefront-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lippert was also commissioned into the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy_Reserve" title="United States Navy Reserve">Navy Reserve</a> in 2005 through the Navy's <a href="/wiki/Direct_commission_officer" title="Direct commission officer">direct commission officer</a> program as an <a href="/wiki/Military_intelligence" title="Military intelligence">intelligence officer</a>. From August 2007 until June 2008, he served about a year<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Aide_Who_Went_to_War_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Aide_Who_Went_to_War-7">[7]</a></sup> in what had been scheduled as a nine-month tour of duty in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> as an intelligence officer with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy_SEALs" title="United States Navy SEALs">Navy SEALs</a>. He received a <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Star_Medal" title="Bronze Star Medal">Bronze Star Medal</a> for his service in Iraq.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lippert_Biography_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lippert_Biography-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>After he returned from Iraq, Lippert served as a senior foreign policy advisor to then-Senator Obama's <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama_presidential_campaign,_2008" title="Barack Obama presidential campaign, 2008">2008 presidential campaign</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lippert_Biography_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lippert_Biography-9">[9]</a></sup> He was responsible for briefing Obama on emerging foreign policy issues throughout the campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He helped prepare Obama on foreign policy for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_debates,_2008" title="United States presidential debates, 2008">presidential debates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Lippert later served as deputy director for foreign policy for the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_transition_of_Barack_Obama" title="Presidential transition of Barack Obama">Obama–Biden Transition Project</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lippert_Biography_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lippert_Biography-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout his time in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">Senate</a> and during the presidential campaign, Lippert was noted for having a close relationship with then Senator Obama.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Aide_Who_Went_to_War_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-The_Aide_Who_Went_to_War-7">[7]</a></sup> He is credited for helping Obama develop his views on defense and foreign policy, particularly his support for a <a href="/wiki/Withdrawal_of_U.S._troops_from_Iraq" title="Withdrawal of U.S. troops from Iraq">withdrawal of American troops from Iraq</a>, which was completed under <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> Obama in December 2011 as well as Obama's emphasis on transnational security issues, such as <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Weapons of mass destruction">weapons of mass destruction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-From_the_Campaign_to_the_Battlefront_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-From_the_Campaign_to_the_Battlefront-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Obama_administration">Obama administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Lippert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Obama administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following Obama's inauguration in January 2009, he was appointed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deputy_Assistant_to_the_President#Organization" title="Deputy Assistant to the President">Deputy Assistant to the President</a> and Chief of Staff for the National Security Council, a position which had not existed in the <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_George_W._Bush" title="Presidency of George W. Bush">Bush administration</a>, but had existed in previous administrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lippert_Nominated_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lippert_Nominated-12">[12]</a></sup> During his time as Chief of Staff, Lippert oversaw the merger of the staffs of the Homeland Security Council, which had been created in October 2001 by <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">President Bush</a>, and the National Security Council, into a single National Security Staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Unlike his recent predecessors, then-<a href="/wiki/National_Security_Advisor_(United_States)" title="National Security Advisor (United States)">National Security Advisor</a> <a href="/wiki/General_(United_States)" title="General (United States)">General</a> <a href="/wiki/James_L._Jones" title="James L. Jones">Jim Jones</a> delegated much of the day-to-day responsibilities for the National Security Council to his deputy, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_E._Donilon" title="Thomas E. Donilon">Tom Donilon</a>, and to a couple of Obama campaign veterans, including <a href="/wiki/Denis_McDonough" title="Denis McDonough">Denis McDonough</a> and Mark Lippert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2009, Lippert resigned from the National Security Council to return to active duty in the Navy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> There was speculation surrounding Lippert's resignation that he was pushed to leave due to significant disagreements with General Jones, especially with respect to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan(2001-present)#Troop_surge" title="War in Afghanistan(2001-present)">troop surge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lippert_Nominated_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lippert_Nominated-12">[12]</a></sup> Jones accused Lippert of leaking information about him to <a href="/wiki/Bob_Woodward" title="Bob Woodward">Bob Woodward</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/Obama%27s_Wars" title="Obama's Wars">Obama's Wars</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Senate_confirms_Lippert_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Senate_confirms_Lippert-16">[16]</a></sup> Lippert was succeeded by Denis McDonough, who would go on to later serve as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deputy_National_Security_Advisor" title="Deputy National Security Advisor">Deputy National Security Advisor</a>, then <a href="/wiki/White_House_Chief_of_Staff" title="White House Chief of Staff">White House Chief of Staff</a>. Lippert had originally recruited McDonough to serve as then-Senator Obama's foreign policy advisor during his 2007 deployment to Iraq.</p>
<p>Lippert spent two years serving as an intelligence officer with the Navy SEALs. He continued to be on the White House payroll while on active duty, which is permitted by federal law, but caused some controversy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>After completing his active duty with the Navy, Lippert was nominated by President Obama in October 2011 to succeed General <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wallace_Gregson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wallace Gregson (page does not exist)">Wallace Gregson</a> as <a href="/wiki/Assistant_Secretary_of_Defense_for_Asian_and_Pacific_Security_Affairs" title="Assistant Secretary of Defense for Asian and Pacific Security Affairs">Assistant Secretary of Defense for Asian and Pacific Security Affairs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lippert_Nominated_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lippert_Nominated-12">[12]</a></sup> While Lippert's nomination was held up for several months due to <a href="/wiki/Senate_hold" title="Senate hold">holds</a> placed on it by Senators <a href="/wiki/John_McCain" title="John McCain">John McCain</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Cornyn" title="John Cornyn">John Cornyn</a> Lippert's relationship with former National Security Advisor Jim Jones,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-16" title="F-16">F-16</a> sales to <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> respectively, he was confirmed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a> in a voice vote on April 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Senate_confirms_Lippert_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Senate_confirms_Lippert-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Kurt_M._Campbell" title="Kurt M. Campbell">Kurt Campbell</a> resigned as <a href="/wiki/Assistant_Secretary_of_State_for_East_Asian_and_Pacific_Affairs" title="Assistant Secretary of State for East Asian and Pacific Affairs">Assistant Secretary of State for East Asian and Pacific Affairs</a> in February 2013, Lippert was rumoured as a possible replacement for Campbell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Lippert was named newly confirmed Secretary of Defense Chuck Hagel's Chief of Staff in early May 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ambassador_to_South_Korea">Ambassador to South Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Lippert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ambassador to South Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On May 1, 2014, President Obama announced his intention to nominate Lippert to be United States Ambassador to South Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The United States Senate voted to confirm Lippert's nomination on September 18, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> He was sworn in as the new ambassador by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Kerry" title="John Kerry">John Kerry</a> in October 2014. He arrived late to his post due to an issue with the transportation of his pet dog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Knife_attack">Knife attack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Lippert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Knife attack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>At about 7:40 a.m. on March 5, 2015, Lippert was attacked by a knife-wielding man at a restaurant attached to <a href="/wiki/Sejong_Center" title="Sejong Center">Sejong Center</a> in downtown Seoul, where Lippert was scheduled to give a speech at a meeting of the Korean Council for Reconciliation and Cooperation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-25">[25]</a></sup> The assailant, Kim Ki-jong, is a member of Uri Madang, a progressive cultural organization opposed to the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> He inflicted wounds on Lippert's left arm as well as a four-inch cut on the right side of the ambassador's face, requiring 80 stitches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-25">[25]</a></sup> Lippert underwent surgery at <a href="/wiki/Yonsei_University" title="Yonsei University">Yonsei University</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Severance_Hospital" title="Severance Hospital">Severance Hospital</a> in Seoul. While his injuries were not life-threatening, doctors stated that it would take several months for Lippert to regain use of his fingers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-25">[25]</a></sup> A police official said that the knife used in the attack was 10 inches (25 cm) long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the attack and while being subdued by security, Kim screamed that the rival Koreas should be unified and told reporters that he had attacked Lippert to protest the <a href="/wiki/Foal_Eagle" title="Foal Eagle">annual United States-South Korean joint military exercises</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_25-4"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-25">[25]</a></sup> Kim has a record of militant <a href="/wiki/Korean_nationalism" title="Korean nationalism">Korean nationalist</a> activism; he attacked the Japanese ambassador to South Korea in 2010 and was sentenced to a three-year suspended prison term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> On September 11, 2015, Kim was sentenced to twelve years in prison for the attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
