<p><b>Mark Allen Mothersbaugh</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/Ë/ primary stress follows">Ë</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/Ê/ short 'u' in 'bud'">Ê</span><span title="/Ã°/ 'th' in 'thy'">Ã°</span><span title="/Ér/ 'er' in 'finger'">Ér</span><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ÉË/ 'au' in 'fraud'">ÉË</span></span>/</a></span></span>; born May 18, 1950) is an American singer, songwriter, composer, multi-instrumentalist, record producer, author and visual artist.</p>
<p>Mothersbaugh's music career spans more than 40 years. He came to prominence in the late 1970s as co-founder, lead singer and keyboardist of the <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a> band <a href="/wiki/Devo" title="Devo">Devo</a>, which released a Top 20 hit in 1980 with the single "<a href="/wiki/Whip_It_(Devo_song)" title="Whip It (Devo song)">Whip It</a>". The band has maintained a <a href="/wiki/Cult_following" title="Cult following">cult following</a> throughout its existence. Mothersbaugh is one of the main composers of Devo's music and made major lyrical contributions to the band's songs. He is one of only two members (along with bass player <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Casale" title="Gerald Casale">Gerald Casale</a>) who have been with Devo throughout its entire history.</p>
<p>In addition to his work with Devo, Mothersbaugh has made music for television series, films and video games via his production company, <a href="/wiki/Mutato_Muzika" title="Mutato Muzika">Mutato Muzika</a>, most notably as the composer for the popular long-running animated series <i><a href="/wiki/Rugrats" title="Rugrats">Rugrats</a></i> for its entire 13-year run, as well as all three <a href="/wiki/Rugrats_(film_series)" title="Rugrats (film series)">theatrical films</a>. He also has had a solo career which has included four studio albums: <i><a href="/wiki/Muzik_for_Insomniaks,_Volume_1_and_Volume_2" title="Muzik for Insomniaks, Volume 1 and Volume 2">Muzik for Insomniaks</a></i>, <i>Muzik for the Gallery</i>, <i>Joyeux Mutato</i> and <i>The Most Powerful Healing Muzik in the Entire World</i>. In 2004, he was honored with the <a href="/wiki/Richard_Kirk" title="Richard Kirk">Richard Kirk</a> award at the <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_Music,_Inc." title="Broadcast Music, Inc.">BMI</a> Film and TV Awards for his significant contributions to film and television music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bmi.com_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bmi.com-4">[4]</a></sup> Additionally, Mothersbaugh was awarded an <a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">honorary doctorate</a> of <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humane letters</a> from <a href="/wiki/Kent_State_University" title="Kent State University">Kent State University</a> in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>His lifelong interest in creating multimedia art pieces has resulted in gallery exhibitions of items such as his "Beautiful Mutants" photograph series, postcard diaries, art rugs, sculptures and musical instruments created from salvaged organ pipes and bird vocalizations. He has married twice and is the father of two adopted children.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Mothersbaugh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mark Allen Mothersbaugh was born on May 18, 1950, in <a href="/wiki/Akron,_Ohio" title="Akron, Ohio">Akron, Ohio</a>. He is the son of Mary Margaret ("Mig") and Robert Mothersbaugh, Sr. He grew up with two younger brothers, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Mothersbaugh" title="Bob Mothersbaugh">Bob</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Mothersbaugh" title="Jim Mothersbaugh">Jim</a>, and two sisters, Amy and Susan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MM_family_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-MM_family-7">[7]</a></sup> His father appeared in early Devo films and fan events as the character <a href="/wiki/General_Boy" title="General Boy">General Boy</a> and his brothers participated in the band, although Jim's tenure was brief, appearing only on several early demos.</p>

<p>Mothersbaugh attended <a href="/wiki/Kent_State_University" title="Kent State University">Kent State University</a> as an art student, where he met <a href="/wiki/Devo" title="Devo">Devo</a> co-founders <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Casale" title="Gerald Casale">Gerald Casale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bob_Lewis_(musician)" title="Bob Lewis (musician)">Bob Lewis</a>. In early 1970, Lewis and Casale formed the idea of the "devolution" of the human race after Casale's friend <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Miller" title="Jeffrey Miller">Jeffrey Miller</a> was killed by Ohio National Guardsmen on university grounds during what came to be known as the <a href="/wiki/Kent_State_shootings" title="Kent State shootings">Kent State shootings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Intrigued by the concept, Mothersbaugh joined them, building upon it with elements of early <a href="/wiki/Post-structuralism" title="Post-structuralism">post-structuralist</a> ideas and oddball arcana, most notably unearthing the infamous <i>Jocko-Homo Heavenbound</i> pamphlet<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> (the basis for the song "<a href="/wiki/Jocko_Homo" title="Jocko Homo">Jocko Homo</a>").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This association culminated in 1973, when the trio started to play music as Devo.</p>

<p>Following the commercial failure of their sixth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Shout_(Devo_album)" title="Shout (Devo album)">Shout</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Records" title="Warner Bros. Records">Warner Bros.</a> dropped Devo. Shortly after, claiming to feel creatively unfulfilled, drummer <a href="/wiki/Alan_Myers_(drummer)" title="Alan Myers (drummer)">Alan Myers</a> left the band,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Myers_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Myers-11">[11]</a></sup> causing the remaining band members to abandon the plans for a <i>Shout</i> video LP, as well as a tour. In the interim, Mothersbaugh began composing music for the TV show <i><a href="/wiki/Pee-wee%27s_Playhouse" title="Pee-wee's Playhouse">Pee-wee's Playhouse</a></i> and released an elaborately packaged solo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_cassette" title="Audio cassette">cassette</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Muzik_for_Insomniaks,_Volume_1_and_Volume_2" title="Muzik for Insomniaks, Volume 1 and Volume 2">Musik for Insomniaks</a></i>, which was later expanded and released as two <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">CDs</a> in 1988.</p>
<p>In 1987, Devo reformed with new drummer David Kendrick, formerly of <a href="/wiki/Sparks_(band)" title="Sparks (band)">Sparks</a> to replace Myers. Their first project was a soundtrack for the flop horror film <i><a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse_Rock" title="Slaughterhouse Rock">Slaughterhouse Rock</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Toni_Basil" title="Toni Basil">Toni Basil</a> and they released the albums <i><a href="/wiki/Total_Devo" title="Total Devo">Total Devo</a></i> (1988) and <i><a href="/wiki/Smooth_Noodle_Maps" title="Smooth Noodle Maps">Smooth Noodle Maps</a></i> (1990), on <a href="/wiki/Enigma_Records" title="Enigma Records">Enigma</a>.</p>
<p>Devo had a falling out and played two shows in 1991 before breaking up. Around this time, members of Devo appeared in the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spirit_of_%2776_(1990_film)" title="The Spirit of '76 (1990 film)">The Spirit of '76</a></i>, except for Bob Mothersbaugh. Following the split, Mark Mothersbaugh established <a href="/wiki/Mutato_Muzika" title="Mutato Muzika">Mutato Muzika</a>, a commercial music production studio, along with Bob Mothersbaugh and Bob Casale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Mothersbaugh meant to further a career as a <a href="/wiki/Composer" title="Composer">composer</a>. Mothersbaugh gained considerable success in <a href="/wiki/Songwriter" title="Songwriter">writing</a> and producing music for television programs (starting with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pee_Wee%27s_Playhouse" title="Pee Wee's Playhouse">Pee Wee's Playhouse</a></i> and perhaps most famously with <i><a href="/wiki/Rugrats" title="Rugrats">Rugrats</a></i>), video games cartoons, and movies (notably working alongside director <a href="/wiki/Wes_Anderson" title="Wes Anderson">Wes Anderson</a>).</p>
<p>In 2006, Devo worked on a project with <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">Disney</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Devo_2.0" title="Devo 2.0">Devo 2.0</a>. A band of child performers was assembled and re-recorded Devo songs. A quote from the <i><a href="/wiki/Akron_Beacon_Journal" title="Akron Beacon Journal">Akron Beacon Journal</a></i> stated, "Devo recently finished a new project in cahoots with Disney called Devo 2.0, which features the band playing old songs and two new ones with vocals provided by children. Their debut album, a two disc CD/DVD combo entitled <i>DEV2.0</i>, was released on March 14, 2006. The lyrics of some of the songs were changed for <a href="/wiki/Family-friendly" title="Family-friendly">family-friendly</a> airplay, which has been claimed by the band to be a play on irony of the messages of their classic hits. Mothersbaugh doesn't rule out the idea of the band gathering in the studio, eventually, to record a new Devo album."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The album, <i><a href="/wiki/Something_for_Everybody_(Devo_album)" title="Something for Everybody (Devo album)">Something for Everybody</a></i> was eventually released in June 2010, preceded by a 12" single of "Fresh"/"What We Do".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Devo was awarded the first <a href="/wiki/Robert_Moog" title="Robert Moog">Moog</a> Innovator Award on October 29, 2010, during <a href="/wiki/Moogfest" title="Moogfest">Moogfest</a> 2010 in <a href="/wiki/Asheville,_North_Carolina" title="Asheville, North Carolina">Asheville, North Carolina</a>. The Moog Innovator Award has been said to celebrate "pioneering artists whose genre-defying work exemplifies the bold, innovative spirit of Bob Moog".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Devo was scheduled to perform at Moogfest, but Bob Mothersbaugh severely injured his hand three days prior and the band was forced to cancel. Mark Mothersbaugh and Gerald Casale collaborated with Austin, Texas, band <a href="/wiki/The_Octopus_Project" title="The Octopus Project">The Octopus Project</a> to perform "<a href="/wiki/Girl_U_Want" title="Girl U Want">Girl U Want</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Beautiful_World_(Devo_song)" title="Beautiful World (Devo song)">Beautiful World</a>" at the event instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_work">Other work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Mothersbaugh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1989, Mothersbaugh and other members of Devo were involved in the project Visiting Kids, releasing a self-titled EP on the New Rose label in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The group featured his then-wife Nancye Ferguson, as well as David Kendrick, Bob Mothersbaugh, and Bob's daughter Alex Mothersbaugh. Mark Mothersbaugh co-wrote some of the songs, and produced the album jointly with Bob Casale. A promotional video was filmed for the song "Trilobites". Visiting Kids appeared on the soundtrack to the film <i><a href="/wiki/Rockula" title="Rockula">Rockula</a></i>, as well as on the <i><a href="/wiki/Late_Show_with_David_Letterman" title="Late Show with David Letterman">Late Show with David Letterman</a></i>.</p>
<p>Since Devo, Mothersbaugh has developed a successful career writing musical scores for film and television. In film, he has worked frequently with filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Wes_Anderson" title="Wes Anderson">Wes Anderson</a>, scoring half of his feature films (<i><a href="/wiki/Bottle_Rocket" title="Bottle Rocket">Bottle Rocket</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rushmore_(film)" title="Rushmore (film)">Rushmore</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Royal_Tenenbaums" title="The Royal Tenenbaums">The Royal Tenenbaums</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Life_Aquatic_with_Steve_Zissou" title="The Life Aquatic with Steve Zissou">The Life Aquatic with Steve Zissou</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>). He also composed for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lego_Movie" title="The Lego Movie">The Lego Movie</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>His music has been a staple of the children's television shows <i><a href="/wiki/Rugrats" title="Rugrats">Rugrats</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Beakman%27s_World" title="Beakman's World">Beakman's World</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Santo_Bugito" title="Santo Bugito">Santo Bugito</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Clifford_the_Big_Red_Dog" title="Clifford the Big Red Dog">Clifford the Big Red Dog</a></i>. He also wrote the new theme song for the original <i><a href="/wiki/Felix_the_Cat" title="Felix the Cat">Felix the Cat</a></i> show when it was sold to <a href="/wiki/Broadway_Video" title="Broadway Video">Broadway Video</a>, some music for <i><a href="/wiki/Pee-wee%27s_Playhouse" title="Pee-wee's Playhouse">Pee-wee's Playhouse</a></i> in 1990 and the theme song for the <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Mario_World_(TV_series)" title="Super Mario World (TV series)">Super Mario World</a></i> TV series for <a href="/wiki/DIC_Entertainment" title="DIC Entertainment">DIC Entertainment</a> in 1991. The character design for Chuckie Finster on <i><a href="/wiki/Rugrats" title="Rugrats">Rugrats</a></i> was based on him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mothersbaugh is also known for producing music in video games including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_Computer_Entertainment" title="Sony Computer Entertainment">Sony</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Crash_Bandicoot" title="Crash Bandicoot">Crash Bandicoot</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Jak_and_Daxter" title="Jak and Daxter">Jak and Daxter</a></i> series (both music scores were created by <a href="/wiki/Josh_Mancell" title="Josh Mancell">Josh Mancell</a>), and creating music for <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">EA Games</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sims_2" title="The Sims 2">The Sims 2</a></i>. This work is often performed with <a href="/wiki/Mutato_Muzika" title="Mutato Muzika">Mutato Muzika</a>, the music production company he formed with several other former members of Devo including his brother, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Mothersbaugh" title="Bob Mothersbaugh">Bob Mothersbaugh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mothersbaugh composed "Having Trouble Sneezing", the distinctive music in the award-winning "<a href="/wiki/Get_a_Mac" title="Get a Mac">Get a Mac</a>" commercials for <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> He composed the score for the first season of the television series <i><a href="/wiki/Big_Love" title="Big Love">Big Love</a></i> but was replaced after one season by <a href="/wiki/David_Byrne" title="David Byrne">David Byrne</a> of <a href="/wiki/Talking_Heads" title="Talking Heads">Talking Heads</a>. Mothersbaugh has also composed the theme music for the American television show <i><a href="/wiki/Eureka_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Eureka (U.S. TV series)">Eureka</a></i>, broadcast on the <a href="/wiki/Syfy" title="Syfy">Syfy</a> channel. He also composed the score of the <a href="/wiki/Cartoon_Network" title="Cartoon Network">Cartoon Network</a>'s TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Regular_Show" title="Regular Show">Regular Show</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 2013, Mothersbaugh appeared on an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Aquabats!_Super_Show!" title="The Aquabats! Super Show!">The Aquabats! Super Show!</a></i>, an action-comedy series by the creators of <i>Yo Gabba Gabba!</i> starring the Devo-influenced band <a href="/wiki/The_Aquabats" title="The Aquabats">The Aquabats</a>, playing the eccentric scientist father of one of the main characters, Jimmy the Robot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mothersbaugh and Casale have also produced music for other artists including <a href="/wiki/Toni_Basil" title="Toni Basil">Toni Basil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_art">Visual art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Mothersbaugh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Visual art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although best known as a musician and composer, Mothersbaugh has also been successful as a visual artist. In November 2014, Mothersbaugh summarized his career for an interviewer, "I've done over 150 art gallery shows in the last 20 years."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 6, 2014, the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Contemporary_Art_Denver" title="Museum of Contemporary Art Denver">Museum of Contemporary Art Denver</a> (MCA Denver) announced a retrospective exhibition to bring together the first comprehensive presentation of Mothersbaugh's art and music from the beginning of his career in the early 1970s to 2014. This nationally touring exhibition was accompanied by a publication, <i>Mark Mothersbaugh: Myopia</i>, published by <a href="/wiki/Princeton_Architectural_Press" title="Princeton Architectural Press">Princeton Architectural Press</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In regard to on-camera work, Mothersbaugh hosts a drawing segment on the <a href="/wiki/Nick_Jr." title="Nick Jr.">Nick Jr.</a> television series <i><a href="/wiki/Yo_Gabba_Gabba!" title="Yo Gabba Gabba!">Yo Gabba Gabba!</a></i> called Mark's Magic Pictures, teaching children how to draw simple pictures. The pictures often come alive at the end of the segment through animation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Mothersbaugh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the age of seven, Mothersbaugh began wearing glasses to correct his severe myopia and astigmatism. Over the years, he took an interest in designing his own distinctive eyewear for use in Devo shows. He favored a particular set of stainless steel frames for regular use made by a Los Angeles shop called LA Eyeworks and says he purchased as many pairs as he could find because they tended to break or get stolen by fans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EW_specs_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-EW_specs-29">[29]</a></sup> In a joint venture with eyewear manufacturer Shane Baum, Mothersbaugh has designed his own branded frames for sale, made of beryllium with a stainless steel chrome finish, in three different styles as of 2015. The Baumvision press release states that the unisex model "Francesca" is named for one of Mothersbaugh's pug dogs which is a <a href="/wiki/Hermaphrodite#Simultaneous_hermaphrodites" title="Hermaphrodite">simultaneous hermaphrodite</a> that is also called Frank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EW_specs_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-EW_specs-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>He has been married twice. His first wife was actress Nancye Ferguson, who can be seen briefly performing with him in the 1999 superhero comedy film <i><a href="/wiki/Mystery_Men" title="Mystery Men">Mystery Men</a></i>. His current wife is Anita Greenspan, who runs the film music managing company Greenspan Kohan Management with Neil Kohan. The couple has two daughters from China, Mai Li Margaret and Hui Hui Hope, who were adopted after Greenspan learned of the practice in that country of female children being abandoned because of their gender.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MM_family_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-MM_family-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mothersbaugh is a collector and connoisseur of <a href="/wiki/Song_poem" title="Song poem">song poems</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and unusual or vintage musical devices. He is the owner of <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Scott" title="Raymond Scott">Raymond Scott</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Electronium" title="Electronium">Electronium</a> (although it is currently not functional).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors_and_awards">Honors and awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Mothersbaugh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Honors and awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mothersbaugh was honored with the <a href="/wiki/Richard_Kirk" title="Richard Kirk">Richard Kirk</a> award at the 2004 <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_Music,_Inc." title="Broadcast Music, Inc.">BMI</a> Film and TV Awards. The award is given annually to a composer who has made significant contributions to film and television music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bmi.com_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-bmi.com-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 10, 2008, Mothersbaugh was awarded an <a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">honorary doctorate</a> of <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humane letters</a> from Kent State University.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 28, 2016, Mothersbaugh was awarded the key to the city of Akron during a ceremony at the <a href="/wiki/Akron-Summit_County_Public_Library" title="Akron-Summit County Public Library">Akron-Summit County Public Library</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
