<p><b>Mark Bunker</b> is an American broadcast journalist, videographer and documentary filmmaker. He won an <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Award</a> in 2006 from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Southwest_Emmy_Awards" title="Pacific Southwest Emmy Awards">Pacific Southwest Emmy Awards</a> division of the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Television_Arts_and_Sciences" title="National Academy of Television Arts and Sciences">National Academy of Television Arts and Sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recipients_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-recipients-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thestar_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-thestar-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>He is a notable critic of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scientology" title="Church of Scientology">Church of Scientology</a>, having previously worked for <a href="/wiki/Bob_Minton" title="Bob Minton">Bob Minton</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lisa_McPherson_Trust" title="Lisa McPherson Trust">Lisa McPherson Trust</a>, and is the founder of <i>Xenu TV</i>, a website featuring videos and commentary critical of <a href="/wiki/Scientology" title="Scientology">Scientology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schmidt418_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-schmidt418-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Bunker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mark Bunker worked in radio in the Midwest. In the mid-1980s, he then moved to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> to work as a theater actor and as an actor in television commercials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-villagevoice.com_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-villagevoice.com-4">[4]</a></sup> He also worked for a company doing market research for Hollywood studios, and trained as a video editor with <a href="/wiki/KNBC" title="KNBC">KNBC</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-villagevoice.com_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-villagevoice.com-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Broadcast_journalism">Broadcast journalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Bunker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Broadcast journalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2006, Bunker along with <a href="/wiki/KUSI-TV" title="KUSI-TV">KUSI-TV</a> reporter Lena Lewis, won an <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Award</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Southwest_Emmy_Awards" title="Pacific Southwest Emmy Awards">Pacific Southwest Emmy Awards</a> division of the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Television_Arts_and_Sciences" title="National Academy of Television Arts and Sciences">National Academy of Television Arts and Sciences</a>, for a story on border issues in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego,_California" title="San Diego, California">San Diego, California</a> area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recipients_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-recipients-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thestar_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-thestar-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_Scientology">Criticism of Scientology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Bunker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticism of Scientology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mark_Bunker_and_the_Clearwater_Police.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1080" data-file-width="1920" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Mark_Bunker_and_the_Clearwater_Police.jpg/220px-Mark_Bunker_and_the_Clearwater_Police.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Mark_Bunker_and_the_Clearwater_Police.jpg/330px-Mark_Bunker_and_the_Clearwater_Police.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Mark_Bunker_and_the_Clearwater_Police.jpg/440px-Mark_Bunker_and_the_Clearwater_Police.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Bunker started Xenu TV in 1999 and moved to <a href="/wiki/Clearwater,_Florida" title="Clearwater, Florida">Clearwater, Florida</a>, where he produced videos for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lisa_McPherson_trust" title="Lisa McPherson trust">Lisa McPherson trust</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, Mark Bunker and Jeff Jacobsen, a fellow critic of the Church of Scientology, were refused service by businesses operated by Scientologists in Clearwater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oneil0923_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-oneil0923-6">[6]</a></sup> Together they filed discrimination complaints with the Pinellas County Office of Human Rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oneil0923_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-oneil0923-6">[6]</a></sup> The Office of Human Rights rejected their complaints, ruling that the church members had not broken any laws in denying them service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oneil0923_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-oneil0923-6">[6]</a></sup> Bunker, Jacobson, and other members of the Lisa McPherson Trust saw this as a sign of the escalating control the Church of Scientology held over the town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oneil0923_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-oneil0923-6">[6]</a></sup> Ray Arsenault, a <a href="/wiki/University_of_South_Florida" title="University of South Florida">University of South Florida</a> professor and then acting president of the <a href="/wiki/Pinellas" title="Pinellas">Pinellas</a> chapter of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_Liberties_Union" title="American Civil Liberties Union">American Civil Liberties Union</a>, also viewed the denials as acts of discrimination. "It really is a way of trying to bring pressure to stop them from exercising their First Amendment rights."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oneil0923_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-oneil0923-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 2010, Bunker has been producing a feature-length independent documentary film entitled <i>Knowledge Report: Scientology's Spies, Lies, and the Eternity Prize</i> concerning the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scientology" title="Church of Scientology">Church of Scientology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-knowledgereport-themovie.com_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-knowledgereport-themovie.com-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> According to Bunker, the planned release date is May 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This film is being funded by Bunker himself and through <a href="/wiki/Crowdfunding" title="Crowdfunding">crowdfunding</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Project_Chanology">Project Chanology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Bunker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Project Chanology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 2008, Bunker posted a video to <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> critical of the Internet-based group "<a href="/wiki/Anonymous_(group)" title="Anonymous (group)">Anonymous</a>" and asked them to tone down their campaign against the Church of Scientology; a movement called "<a href="/wiki/Project_Chanology" title="Project Chanology">Project Chanology</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-puzzanghera_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-puzzanghera-11">[11]</a></sup> In the video "Message to Anonymous", Bunker urged the group to work legally and pursue peaceful ways to protest Scientology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landers_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-landers-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Public_Radio" title="National Public Radio">NPR</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Morning_Edition" title="Morning Edition">Morning Edition</a></i>, Bunker has "become a revered voice to many members of Anonymous",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masters_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-masters-13">[13]</a></sup> and they refer to him as "Wise Beard Man".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Anonymous has adopted a slogan referring to Bunker: "Wise Beard Man. His words are wise, his face is beard."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landers_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-landers-12">[12]</a></sup> The refrain along with a picture of Bunker has become an <a href="/wiki/Internet_meme" title="Internet meme">Internet meme</a> on the website <a href="/wiki/4chan" title="4chan">4chan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davies0508_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-davies0508-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>When actor <a href="/wiki/Jason_Beghe" title="Jason Beghe">Jason Beghe</a> decided to leave Scientology in 2008, he contacted <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Heldal-Lund" title="Andreas Heldal-Lund">Andreas Heldal-Lund</a>, founder of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Clambake" title="Operation Clambake">Operation Clambake</a>, who convinced him to meet with Bunker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ortega_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ortega-16">[16]</a></sup> Heldal-Lund and Bunker went to Beghe's house, where Beghe participated in an interview about his experiences as a Scientologist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ortega_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-ortega-16">[16]</a></sup> Bunker published a 3-minute portion of the 3-hour interview to YouTube in mid-April 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-friedman416_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-friedman416-17">[17]</a></sup> and in the video Beghe calls Scientology "very dangerous for your spiritual, psychological, mental, emotional health and evolution".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> He also comments "I don't have an agenda. I'm just trying to help. I have the luxury of having gotten into Scientology and after having been in it, been out. And that's a perspective that people who are still in and not out do not have."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>The video was taken down from YouTube on April 17, 2008 but was reposted by multiple other YouTube users shortly thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-friedman418_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-friedman418-20">[20]</a></sup> By April 18, 2008, at least 45 users had reposted the video interview using their own YouTube accounts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schmidt418_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-schmidt418-3">[3]</a></sup> Bunker's account was also canceled on April 17, and he believes this was due to copyright issues with a clip from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Colbert_Report" title="The Colbert Report">The Colbert Report</a></i> that he had uploaded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-friedman418_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-friedman418-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metz_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-metz-21">[21]</a></sup> Bunker said that those issues had been resolved, and that YouTube should have given him time to prove that before pulling the Jason Beghe interview.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-friedman418_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-friedman418-20">[20]</a></sup> Bunker believes that YouTube removed the Beghe interview after receiving pressure from Scientology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-friedman418_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-friedman418-20">[20]</a></sup> A representative for YouTube told <i>Fox News</i> "There’s no conspiracy here.", but would not say whether Scientology pressured YouTube to remove the video, saying: "We do not comment on individual videos."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-friedman418_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-friedman418-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 24, 2009, Bunker was arrested while photographing the arrest of another individual who was protesting against Scientology outside of the organization's <a href="/wiki/Gold_Base" title="Gold Base">Gold Base</a> compound near <a href="/wiki/Hemet,_California" title="Hemet, California">Hemet, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twoprotesters_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-twoprotesters-22">[22]</a></sup> The protester was also arrested, after Scientology officials had made a <a href="/wiki/Citizen%27s_arrest" title="Citizen's arrest">citizen's arrest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twoprotesters_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-twoprotesters-22">[22]</a></sup> Bunker stated that five protesters had previously walked in front of the Scientology property, and crossed its driveway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twoprotesters_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-twoprotesters-22">[22]</a></sup> Police allege that the two individuals arrested were blocking the entrance to the facility, but according to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Press-Enterprise" title="The Press-Enterprise">The Press-Enterprise</a></i>: "protesters say they were neither trespassing nor blocking the entryway".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twoprotesters_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-twoprotesters-22">[22]</a></sup> A protester was arrested, and Bunker said that while he was photographing police deputies arrest the individual, he was arrested as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twoprotesters_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-twoprotesters-22">[22]</a></sup> The protesters were released the same day, and the Riverside County District Attorney dropped the case because of insufficient evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
