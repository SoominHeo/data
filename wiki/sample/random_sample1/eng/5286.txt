<p><b>Mark Abene</b> (born 1972), is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infosec" title="Infosec">infosec</a> expert and entrepreneur, originally from <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. Better known by his <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a> <b>Phiber Optik</b>, he was once a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hacker_groups" title="Hacker groups">hacker groups</a> <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Doom_(hacking)" title="Legion of Doom (hacking)">Legion of Doom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Masters_of_Deception" title="Masters of Deception">Masters of Deception</a>.</p>
<p>Phiber Optik was a high-profile <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hacker_(computer_security)" title="Hacker (computer security)">hacker</a> in the 1980s and early 1990s, appearing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harper%27s" title="Harper's">Harper's</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esquire_Magazine" title="Esquire Magazine">Esquire</a></i>, in debates and on <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a>. He is an important figure in the 1995 non-fiction book <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Masters_of_Deception_(book)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Masters of Deception (book) (page does not exist)">Masters of Deception — The Gang that Ruled Cyberspace</a></i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and appears in the 2016 <a href="/wiki/Adam_Curtis" title="Adam Curtis">Adam Curtis</a> documentary <a href="/wiki/HyperNormalisation" title="HyperNormalisation">HyperNormalisation</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Abene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mark Abene's first contact with computers was at around 9 years of age at a local department store, where he would often pass the time while his parents shopped. His first computer was a <a href="/wiki/TRS-80_MC-10" title="TRS-80 MC-10">TRS-80 MC-10</a> with 4 <a href="/wiki/Kilobyte" title="Kilobyte">kilobytes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Random-access_memory" title="Random-access memory">RAM</a>, a 32-column screen, no lower <a href="/wiki/Computer_case" title="Computer case">case</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Compact_Cassette" title="Compact Cassette">cassette</a> <a href="/wiki/Tape_recorder" title="Tape recorder">tape recorder</a> to load and save programs. As was customary at the time, the computer connected to a <a href="/wiki/Television_set" title="Television set">television set</a> for use as a <a href="/wiki/Computer_monitor" title="Computer monitor">monitor</a>. After receiving the gifts of a RAM upgrade (to 20K) and a 300 <a href="/wiki/Baud" title="Baud">baud</a> <a href="/wiki/Modem" title="Modem">modem</a> from his parents, he used his computer to access <a href="/wiki/CompuServe" title="CompuServe">CompuServe</a> and shortly after discovered the world of dialup <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulletin_Board_System" title="Bulletin Board System">BBSes</a> via people he met on CompuServe's "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CB_simulator" title="CB simulator">CB simulator</a>", the first nationwide online chat. On some of these BBSes, Abene discovered dialups and guest accounts to <a href="/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation" title="Digital Equipment Corporation">DEC</a> <a href="/wiki/Minicomputer" title="Minicomputer">minicomputers</a> running the <a href="/wiki/RSTS/E" title="RSTS/E">RSTS/E</a> and <a href="/wiki/TOPS-10" title="TOPS-10">TOPS-10</a> <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a> as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BOCES" title="BOCES">BOCES</a> educational program in Long Island, New York. Accessing those DEC minicomputers he realized there was a programming environment that was much more powerful than that of his own home computer, and so he began taking books out of the library in order to learn the programming languages that were now available to him. This and the ability to remotely save and load back programs that would still be there the next time he logged in had a profound effect on Abene, who came to view his rather simple computer as a window into a much larger world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Having learned about programming and fundamental security concepts during those early years, Abene further honed his skill in understanding the intricacies of the nationwide telephone network.</p>
<p>In the mid-1980s he was first introduced to members of the <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Doom_(hacking)" title="Legion of Doom (hacking)">Legion of Doom (LOD)</a>, a loosely knit group of highly respected teenage hackers who shared Abene's uncompromising desire to understand technology.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Their main focus was to explore telecommunications systems, minicomputer and <a href="/wiki/Mainframe_computer" title="Mainframe computer">mainframe</a> operating systems and large-scale packet data networks. The eventual decline of the LOD toward the late 1980s largely due to fragmentation and dissention within the group, coupled with the legal prosecution of a handful of its members, caused Abene to increasingly align himself with a local group of up-and-coming hackers, who came to be known as the <a href="/wiki/Masters_of_Deception" title="Masters of Deception">Masters of Deception (MOD)</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_tribulations">Legal tribulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Abene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legal tribulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On January 24, 1990, Abene and other MOD members had their homes searched and property seized by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Secret_Service" title="U.S. Secret Service">U.S. Secret Service</a> largely based on government suspicions of having caused <a href="/wiki/AT%26T_Corporation" title="AT&amp;T Corporation">AT&amp;T Corporation</a>'s network crash just over a week earlier on January 15 (Abene was personally accused by the Secret Service of having done as much, during the search and seizure). Some weeks later, AT&amp;T themselves admitted that the crash was the result of a flawed software update to the switching systems on their long distance network, thus, human error on their part.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In February 1991, Abene was arrested and charged with computer tampering and computer trespass in the first degree, New York state offenses. Laws at the time were considered a “gray area” concerning information security. Abene, who was a minor at the time, pleaded "not guilty" to the first two offenses and ultimately accepted a plea agreement to a lesser misdemeanor charge, and was sentenced to 35 hours of community service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsbytes_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newsbytes-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Abene and four other members of the Masters of Deception were also arrested in December 1991 and indicted by a Manhattan federal grand jury on July 8, 1992, on an 11-count charge. The indictment relied heavily on evidence collected by court-approved wire tapping of telephone conversations between MOD members. According to U.S. Attorney <a href="/wiki/Otto_G._Obermaier" title="Otto G. Obermaier">Otto Obermaier</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsbytes_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Newsbytes-4">[4]</a></sup> it was the "first investigative use of court-authorized wiretaps to obtain conversations and data transmissions of computer hackers" in the United States.</p>
<p>According to a July 9, 1992 newsletter from the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Frontier_Foundation" title="Electronic Frontier Foundation">Electronic Frontier Foundation</a>, the defendants faced a maximum term of 50 years in prison and fines of $2.5 million if found guilty on all counts. Despite the fact that Abene was a minor at the time the crimes were allegedly committed, was only involved in a small fraction of the sub-charges, and often in a passive way, a plea arrangement resulted in by far the harshest sentence: 12 months imprisonment, three years probation and 600 hours of community service.</p>
<p>After serving the one-year sentence at the Federal Prison "Camp" in <a href="/wiki/Schuylkill,_Philadelphia" title="Schuylkill, Philadelphia">Schuylkill, Pennsylvania</a>, Abene was released in November 1994. In January 1995, a huge celebration called "Phiberphest '95" was held in his honor at Manhattan's <a href="/wiki/Irving_Plaza" title="Irving Plaza">Irving Plaza</a> ballroom/nightclub. In <i>Time</i>, Joshua Quittner called him "the first underground hero of the Information Age, the Robin Hood of cyberspace."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_protests">Social protests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Abene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Social protests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many people inside and outside of the hacker world felt that Abene was made an example of, and was not judged according to earlier court standards.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Abene had built up a significant reputation in the hacker sub-culture, for example regularly appearing on the radio show <i><a href="/wiki/Off_the_Hook_(radio_program)" title="Off the Hook (radio program)">Off the Hook</a></i>, hosted by <a href="/wiki/Eric_Corley" title="Eric Corley">Eric Corley</a> (a.k.a. Emmanuel Goldstein), debating and defending the morals and motivations of hackers in public forums and in interviews, and lecturing on the history of telecommunications technology at the night courses of several New York City universities. At the time of the indictment he was working at <a href="/wiki/MindVox" title="MindVox">MindVox</a>, an early BBS/ISP founded by two New York LOD members, and subsequently on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EchoNYC" title="EchoNYC">EchoNYC</a>, a multi-user BBS and early ISP.</p>
<p>ECHO users, ECHO management themselves and hackers around the nation expected Abene to get off with probation or at most a few months of jail time. Co-defendants and previous offenders charged with "hacking" offenses had received rather lenient punishments, and given his new-found enthusiasm for using his knowledge to constructive ends, the general feeling was optimistic prior to sentencing.</p>
<p>A statement made by U.S. Attorney Otto Obermeier in conjunction with the indication, "The message that ought to be delivered with this indictment is that such conduct will not be tolerated, irrespective of the age of the particular accused or their ostensible purpose,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> was interpreted by Abene's supporters to mean that MOD was made an example of, to show that the authorities could handle the perceived "hacker threat". During sentencing, Judge Stanton said that "the defendant stands as a symbol here today," and that "hacking crimes constitute a real threat to the expanding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_highway" title="Information highway">information highway</a>", reinforcing the view that a relatively harmless "teacher" was judged as a symbol for all hackers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_life">Professional life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Abene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Professional life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Abene has spoken on the subject of security in many publications such as the New York Times, the Washington Post, the Wall Street Journal, and Time Magazine. He has appeared as a speaker at both hacker and security industry conferences worldwide and frequently visits universities to speak to students about information security.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>After some years as a security consultant, he joined forces with former Legion of Doom member Dave Buchwald and a third colleague, Andrew Brown, to create the security consulting firm Crossbar Security. Crossbar provided consulting services for third party companies, during which the principals conducted business in the U.S., Japan, Brazil, and Sweden. As a result of the "dot com" bust Crossbar ultimately went defunct in 2001, largely due to cuts in corporate security spending.</p>
<p>Abene made his acting début as "The Inside Man" in the fiction film <i><a href="/wiki/Urchin_(film)" title="Urchin (film)">Urchin</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> completed in 2006 and released in the US in February 2007, in which other hacker notables such as <a href="/wiki/Dave_Buchwald" title="Dave Buchwald">Dave Buchwald</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eric_Corley" title="Eric Corley">Emmanuel Goldstein</a> can also be seen.</p>
<p>In 2009, he founded TraceVector, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intrusion_detection" title="Intrusion detection">intrusion detection</a> firm that makes use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supercomputing" title="Supercomputing">supercomputing</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Data_analytics" title="Data analytics">data analytics</a>. He currently resides in <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a>.</p>
