<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tomitaro Makino</h1>

<p><b>Tomitaro Makino</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">牧野 富太郎</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Makino Tomitarō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, April 24, 1862 – January 18, 1957)</span> was a pioneer Japanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Botanist" title="Botanist">botanist</a> noted for his taxonomic work. He has been called "Father of Japanese Botany".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He was one of the first Japanese botanists to work extensively on classifying Japanese plants using the system developed by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>. His research resulted in documenting 50,000 specimens, many of which are represented in his <i>Makino's Illustrated Flora of Japan</i>. Despite having dropped out of <a href="/wiki/Grammar_school" title="Grammar school">grammar school</a>, he would eventually attain a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Science" title="Doctor of Science">Doctor of Science</a> degree, and his birthday is remembered as <i>Botany Day</i> in Japan.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tomitaro_Makino&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tomitaro Makino was born in <a href="/wiki/Sakawa,_K%C5%8Dchi" title="Sakawa, Kōchi">Sakawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dchi_Prefecture" title="Kōchi Prefecture">Kōchi</a> to a prestigious sake brewer. His parents died during his early childhood, and he was raised mainly by his grandmother. Though he dropped out of school after two years, he cultivated a strong interest in English, geography, and especially in botany. In 1880, he became a teacher at the <a href="/wiki/Primary_education" title="Primary education">primary school</a> in his hometown, where he published his first academic botanical paper.</p>
<p>In 1884, he moved to <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> to pursue his botanical interests at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tokyo" title="University of Tokyo">University of Tokyo</a> where he worked with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ryokichi_Yatabe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ryokichi Yatabe (page does not exist)">Ryokichi Yatabe</a>. He married in 1890 and would later have 13 children.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tomitaro_Makino&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1887, Makino started to publish an <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">academic journal</a> of botany.</p>
<p>In 1936, he published <i>Makino Book of Botany</i>, a six volume text on botany, in which he describes 6000 species, 1000 of which he discovered. He is best known for his <i>Makino's Illustrated Flora of Japan</i>, published 1940, which is still used as an encyclopedic text today.</p>
<p>In 1948, he was invited to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kokyo" title="Kokyo">Imperial Palace</a> to lecture on botany for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Hirohito" title="Emperor Hirohito">Emperor Hirohito</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tomitaro_Makino&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In total, Makino named over 2500 plants, including 1000 new <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> and 1500 new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Variety_(biology)" title="Variety (biology)">varieties</a>. In addition, he discovered about 600 new species.</p>
<p>After his death in 1957, his collection of approximately 400,000 specimens was donated to <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Metropolitan_University" title="Tokyo Metropolitan University">Tokyo Metropolitan University</a>. The Makino Herbarium in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Makino_Botanical_Garden" title="Makino Botanical Garden">Makino Botanical Garden</a> on Mount Godai in his native <a href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dchi_Prefecture" title="Kōchi Prefecture">Kōchi</a> were named in his honor. He was also named an <a href="/wiki/Citizenship" title="Citizenship">Honorary Citizen</a> of Tokyo.</p>
