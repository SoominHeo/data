<p>In <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_ancient_Rome" title="Religion in ancient Rome">ancient Roman religion</a>, the <b>Matronalia</b> (or <b>Matronales Feriae</b>) was a <a href="/wiki/Roman_festivals" title="Roman festivals">festival</a> celebrating Juno <a href="/wiki/Lucina_(goddess)" title="Lucina (goddess)">Lucina</a>, the goddess of childbirth ("<a href="/wiki/Juno_(mythology)" title="Juno (mythology)">Juno</a> who brings children into the light"), and of motherhood (<i>mater</i> is "mother" in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>) and women in general. In the original <a href="/wiki/Roman_calendar" title="Roman calendar">Roman calendar</a> traditionally thought to have been established by <a href="/wiki/Romulus" title="Romulus">Romulus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> it was the first day of the year. As the first day of March (<i>Martius</i>), the month of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_(god)" title="Mars (god)">Mars</a>, it was also the <i><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_ancient_Roman_religion#feria" title="Glossary of ancient Roman religion">Feriae</a> Martis</i>.</p>
<p>The date of the festival was associated with the dedication of a temple to Juno Lucina on the <a href="/wiki/Esquiline_Hill" title="Esquiline Hill">Esquiline Hill</a> circa 268 BCE, and possibly also a commemoration of the peace between the Romans and the <a href="/wiki/Sabines" title="Sabines">Sabines</a>. On the day, women would participate in rituals at the temple, although the details have not been preserved other than the observation that they wore their hair loose (when Roman decorum otherwise required them to wear it up), and were not allowed to wear belts or to knot their clothing in any place.</p>
<p>At home, women received gifts from their husbands and daughters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and Roman husbands were expected to offer prayers for their wives. Women were also expected to prepare a meal for the household slaves (who were given the day off work), as Roman men did at the <a href="/wiki/Saturnalia" title="Saturnalia">Saturnalia</a>.</p>
