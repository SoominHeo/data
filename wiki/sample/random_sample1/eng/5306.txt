<p><b>Martina Mariea McBride</b> (née Schiff, born July 29, 1966) is an American <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country music</a> singer-songwriter and record producer. She is known for her <a href="/wiki/Soprano" title="Soprano">soprano</a> singing range and her <a href="/wiki/Country_pop" title="Country pop">country pop</a> material.</p>
<p>McBride was signed to <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">RCA Records</a> in 1991, and made her debut the following year as a neo-traditionalist country singer with the single, "The Time Has Come".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-2">[2]</a></sup> Over time, she developed a pop-styled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crossover_(music)" title="Crossover (music)">crossover</a> sound, similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Shania_Twain" title="Shania Twain">Shania Twain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Faith_Hill" title="Faith Hill">Faith Hill</a> and has had a string of major hit singles on the <i>Billboard</i> country chart and occasionally on the adult contemporary chart. Five of these singles went to No. 1 on the country chart between 1995 and 2001, and one peaked at No. 1 on the adult contemporary chart in 2003. She has been called the "Celine Dion of Country Music" when she was recognized for her soprano singing range.</p>
<p>McBride has recorded a total of 13 studio albums, two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greatest_hits" title="Greatest hits">greatest hits</a> compilations, one "live" album, as well as two additional compilation albums. Eight of her studio albums and two of her compilations have received an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIAA" title="RIAA">RIAA</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_certification" title="Gold certification">Gold certification</a>, or higher. In the U.S. she has sold over 14 million albums. In addition, McBride has won the <a href="/wiki/Country_Music_Association" title="Country Music Association">Country Music Association</a>'s "Female Vocalist of the Year" award four times (tied with <a href="/wiki/Reba_McEntire" title="Reba McEntire">Reba McEntire</a> for the second-most wins) and the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Country_Music" title="Academy of Country Music">Academy of Country Music</a>'s "Top Female Vocalist" award three times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> She is also a 14-time <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> nominee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martina_McBride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Martina Mariea Schiff was born in <a href="/wiki/Sharon,_Kansas" title="Sharon, Kansas">Sharon, Kansas</a> on July 29, 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biography.com_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-biography.com-5">[5]</a></sup> She has two brothers, Martin and Steve, who currently play in her concert band, and a sister, Gina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biography.com_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biography.com-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TV_Guide_Bio_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-TV_Guide_Bio-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Martina's parents, Daryl and Jeanne Schiff, owned a dairy farm. Daryl, who was also a cabinetry shop owner, exposed Martina to country music at a young age. Listening to country music helped her acquire a love for singing. After school, she would spend hours singing along to the records of such popular artists as <a href="/wiki/Reba_McEntire" title="Reba McEntire">Reba McEntire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linda_Ronstadt" title="Linda Ronstadt">Linda Ronstadt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juice_Newton" title="Juice Newton">Juice Newton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeanne_Pruett" title="Jeanne Pruett">Jeanne Pruett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connie_Smith" title="Connie Smith">Connie Smith</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Patsy_Cline" title="Patsy Cline">Patsy Cline</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CMT_excerpt_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-CMT_excerpt-8">[8]</a></sup> Around the age of eight or nine, Martina began singing with a band her father fronted, "The Schiffters." As Schiff grew older her role in the band progressively increased, from simply singing, to also playing keyboard with them. She enjoyed performing in her early years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CMT_excerpt_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-CMT_excerpt-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Martina began performing with a local rock band, The Penetrators, in <a href="/wiki/Wichita,_Kansas" title="Wichita, Kansas">Wichita</a> instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CMT_excerpt_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-CMT_excerpt-8">[8]</a></sup> Then, in 1987, Schiff gathered a group of musicians called Lotus and started looking for rehearsal space; she began renting space from studio engineer John McBride. In 1988, the two married.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CMT_excerpt_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-CMT_excerpt-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio.com_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-bio.com-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>After marrying, the couple moved to <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville, Tennessee</a> in 1989 with the hope of beginning a career in country music. John McBride joined <a href="/wiki/Garth_Brooks" title="Garth Brooks">Garth Brooks</a>'s sound crew and later became his concert production manager. Martina occasionally joined her husband on the road and helped sell Garth Brooks souvenirs. In 1990, impressed by Martina's enthusiastic spirit, Brooks offered her the position of his opening act provided she could obtain a recording contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio.com_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-bio.com-9">[9]</a></sup> During this time, while her husband was working with country artists <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Daniels" title="Charlie Daniels">Charlie Daniels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Van_Shelton" title="Ricky Van Shelton">Ricky Van Shelton</a>, he also helped produce her demo tape, which helped her gain a recording contract with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RCA_Nashville_Records" title="RCA Nashville Records">RCA Nashville Records</a> in 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_career">Music career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martina_McBride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1992.E2.80.9395:_The_Time_Has_Come_and_The_Way_That_I_Am">1992–95: <i>The Time Has Come</i> and <i>The Way That I Am</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martina_McBride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1992–95: The Time Has Come and The Way That I Am">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>McBride released her debut studio album by RCA Records in 1992, titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Time_Has_Come_(Martina_McBride_album)" title="The Time Has Come (Martina McBride album)">The Time Has Come</a></i>. It was produced by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Worley" title="Paul Worley">Paul Worley</a> and Ed Seay. This album's <a href="/wiki/The_Time_Has_Come_(Martina_McBride_song)" title="The Time Has Come (Martina McBride song)">title track</a> made number 23 on the country music charts, but the next two singles both failed to make top 40.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitburn_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-whitburn-10">[10]</a></sup> Unlike her later <a href="/wiki/Country_pop" title="Country pop">country pop</a>-influenced albums, <i>The Time Has Come</i> featured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honky_tonk" title="Honky tonk">honky tonk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Country_folk" title="Country folk">country folk</a> influences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Way_That_I_Am" title="The Way That I Am">The Way That I Am</a></i> was McBride's second album. Its first two singles both brought her into the country top ten: "<a href="/wiki/My_Baby_Loves_Me_(Just_the_Way_That_I_Am)" title="My Baby Loves Me (Just the Way That I Am)">My Baby Loves Me</a>" peaked at number two, and "<a href="/wiki/Life_Number_9" title="Life Number 9">Life No. 9</a>" at number six. The former was previously a Top 10 hit in Canada for <a href="/wiki/Patricia_Conroy" title="Patricia Conroy">Patricia Conroy</a>. The third single, "<a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(Martina_McBride_song)" title="Independence Day (Martina McBride song)">Independence Day</a>", did not reach the top 10 because many radio programmers objected to the song's subject of a mother fighting back against abuse by burning the family home to the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-book-11">[11]</a></sup> "Independence Day" won Video of the Year and Song of the Year at the <a href="/wiki/Country_Music_Association" title="Country Music Association">Country Music Association</a> Awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitburn_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-whitburn-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cmt_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-cmt-12">[12]</a></sup> It also earned the song's composer, <a href="/wiki/Gretchen_Peters" title="Gretchen Peters">Gretchen Peters</a>, a nomination for the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Country_Song" title="Grammy Award for Best Country Song">Grammy Award for Best Country Song</a>. McBride performed the song at the 1995 Grammys ceremony. The fourth and fifth singles from <i>The Way That I Am</i> were less successful: "Heart Trouble" peaked at number 21, and "Where I Used to Have a Heart" fell short of top 40.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitburn_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-whitburn-10">[10]</a></sup> McBride later criticized these single choices, saying that she felt "Strangers" would have been a better followup, as that song was more popular with fans and later appeared on her first greatest-hits album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liner_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-liner-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1995.E2.80.9399:_Wild_Angels_and_Evolution">1995–99: <i>Wild Angels</i> and <i>Evolution</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martina_McBride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1995–99: Wild Angels and Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Released in 1995, <i><a href="/wiki/Wild_Angels_(Martina_McBride_album)" title="Wild Angels (Martina McBride album)">Wild Angels</a></i> accounted for another top five hit in its lead single "<a href="/wiki/Safe_in_the_Arms_of_Love" title="Safe in the Arms of Love">Safe in the Arms of Love</a>", which had previously been recorded by both <a href="/wiki/Wild_Choir" title="Wild Choir">Wild Choir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baillie_%26_the_Boys" title="Baillie &amp; the Boys">Baillie &amp; the Boys</a>, and was concurrently released in Canada by <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Wright" title="Michelle Wright">Michelle Wright</a> at the time of McBride's version. The album's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wild_Angels_(song)" title="Wild Angels (song)">title track</a> went on to become McBride's first No. 1 single on the country charts in early 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitburn_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-whitburn-10">[10]</a></sup> However, the three follow-ups,"Phones Are Ringin' All Over Town", "Swingin' Doors" and "Cry on the Shoulder of the Road" were less successful, reaching the lower regions of the top 40.</p>
<p>In early 1997, after "Cry on the Shoulder of the Road" peaked, McBride released two duets. "<a href="/wiki/Still_Holding_On" title="Still Holding On">Still Holding On</a>", a duet with <a href="/wiki/Clint_Black" title="Clint Black">Clint Black</a> which was the lead-off single to his album <i><a href="/wiki/Nothin%27_but_the_Taillights" title="Nothin' but the Taillights">Nothin' but the Taillights</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitburn_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-whitburn-10">[10]</a></sup> and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valentine_(Martina_McBride_song)" title="Valentine (Martina McBride song)">Valentine</a>", a collaboration with pop pianist <a href="/wiki/Jim_Brickman" title="Jim Brickman">Jim Brickman</a> which appeared on his album <i>Picture This</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> She also sang duet vocals on "Chances Are", a single from <a href="/wiki/Jason_Sellers" title="Jason Sellers">Jason Sellers</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/I%27m_Your_Man_(Jason_Sellers_album)" title="I'm Your Man (Jason Sellers album)">I'm Your Man</a></i> album.</p>
<p>She had her second number one on the country charts with "<a href="/wiki/A_Broken_Wing" title="A Broken Wing">A Broken Wing</a>", the lead-off to her album <i><a href="/wiki/Evolution_(Martina_McBride_album)" title="Evolution (Martina McBride album)">Evolution</a></i>, in late 1997 This album went on to produce four more top ten hits at country radio: a re-release of "Valentine", "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Happy_Girl_(song)" title="Happy Girl (song)">Happy Girl</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Wrong_Again_(song)" title="Wrong Again (song)">Wrong Again</a>" (which also went to number one), and "<a href="/wiki/Whatever_You_Say" title="Whatever You Say">Whatever You Say</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitburn_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-whitburn-10">[10]</a></sup> Towards the end of 1998, the album was certified double platinum in sales by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> for selling two million units. In addition, she also won the Country Music Association Awards' "Female Vocalist of the Year" award in 1999 and also performed for President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> during the same time.</p>
<p>Also in 1998, McBride released a Christmas album titled <i><a href="/wiki/White_Christmas_(Martina_McBride_album)" title="White Christmas (Martina McBride album)">White Christmas</a></i>, which featured a rendition of "<a href="/wiki/O_Holy_Night" title="O Holy Night">O Holy Night</a>" that first charted in 1997 and continued to re-enter the charts until 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitburn_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-whitburn-10">[10]</a></sup> She also sang a guest vocal on <a href="/wiki/Jason_Sellers" title="Jason Sellers">Jason Sellers</a>' mid-1998 single "This Small Divide".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1999.E2.80.932003:_Emotion_and_Greatest_Hits">1999–2003: <i>Emotion</i> and <i>Greatest Hits</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martina_McBride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1999–2003: Emotion and Greatest Hits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>McBride's sixth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Emotion_(Martina_McBride_album)" title="Emotion (Martina McBride album)">Emotion</a>,</i> was released in 1999. Its lead single, "<a href="/wiki/I_Love_You_(Martina_McBride_song)" title="I Love You (Martina McBride song)">I Love You</a>," reached number one on the <i>Billboard</i> country charts in 1999, and also crossed over to the Adult Contemporary chart. The song's follow-ups, "<a href="/wiki/Love%27s_the_Only_House" title="Love's the Only House">Love's the Only House</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/There_You_Are_(Martina_McBride_song)" title="There You Are (Martina McBride song)">There You Are</a>", both made top ten at country radio, and "<a href="/wiki/It%27s_My_Time_(Martina_McBride_song)" title="It's My Time (Martina McBride song)">It's My Time</a>" peaked at number eleven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitburn_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-whitburn-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, she released her first compilation, <i><a href="/wiki/Greatest_Hits_(Martina_McBride_album)" title="Greatest Hits (Martina McBride album)">Greatest Hits</a></i>. This album has been certified 3× Platinum in sales by the Recording Industry Association of America, and is her highest-selling album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riaa_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-riaa-15">[15]</a></sup> It included most of her major hits to that point, and the album track "Strangers" from the album <i>The Way That I Am</i>, which she put on the album because she felt that it should have been a single.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liner_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-liner-13">[13]</a></sup> The album also included four new songs, all of which made top ten on the country music charts between 2001 and 2003: "<a href="/wiki/When_God-Fearin%27_Women_Get_the_Blues" title="When God-Fearin' Women Get the Blues">When God-Fearin' Women Get the Blues</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Blessed_(Martina_McBride_song)" title="Blessed (Martina McBride song)">Blessed</a>" (her fifth number one), "<a href="/wiki/Where_Would_You_Be" title="Where Would You Be">Where Would You Be</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/Concrete_Angel" title="Concrete Angel">Concrete Angel</a>". <a href="/wiki/Carolyn_Dawn_Johnson" title="Carolyn Dawn Johnson">Carolyn Dawn Johnson</a> sang backing vocals on "Blessed";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liner_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-liner-13">[13]</a></sup> conversely, McBride sang backing vocals on Johnson's late-2000 debut single, titled "<a href="/wiki/Georgia_(Carolyn_Dawn_Johnson_song)" title="Georgia (Carolyn Dawn Johnson song)">Georgia</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Late in 2002, McBride also sang backing vocals on <a href="/wiki/Andy_Griggs" title="Andy Griggs">Andy Griggs</a>'s single "Practice Life".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitburn_10-8"><a href="#cite_note-whitburn-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2003.E2.80.9305:_Martina">2003–05: <i>Martina</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martina_McBride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2003–05: Martina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2003, McBride released her seventh studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Martina_(album)" title="Martina (album)">Martina</a>,</i> which celebrated womanhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-2">[2]</a></sup> The first single, "<a href="/wiki/This_One%27s_for_the_Girls" title="This One's for the Girls">This One's for the Girls</a>," went to number three on the country charts and became her only number-one hit on the Adult Contemporary charts. It also included backing vocals from <a href="/wiki/Faith_Hill" title="Faith Hill">Faith Hill</a>, Carolyn Dawn Johnson, and McBride's daughters, Delaney and Emma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitburn_10-9"><a href="#cite_note-whitburn-10">[10]</a></sup> Follow-up single "<a href="/wiki/In_My_Daughter%27s_Eyes" title="In My Daughter's Eyes">In My Daughter's Eyes</a>" was also a top five hit at both country and adult contemporary. "<a href="/wiki/How_Far" title="How Far">How Far</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/God%27s_Will" title="God's Will">God's Will</a>" from the same album both made top 20 at country radio, as did her guest appearance on <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Buffett" title="Jimmy Buffett">Jimmy Buffett</a>'s single "<a href="/wiki/Trip_Around_the_Sun" title="Trip Around the Sun">Trip Around the Sun</a>", whose chart run overlapped that of "God's Will".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitburn_10-10"><a href="#cite_note-whitburn-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, McBride won the CMA's Female Vocalist award for the fourth time, following the wins in 2003, 2002 and 1999, which tied her for the most wins in that category with Reba McEntire.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2005.E2.80.9308:_Timeless_and_Waking_Up_Laughing">2005–08: <i>Timeless</i> and <i>Waking Up Laughing</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martina_McBride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2005–08: Timeless and Waking Up Laughing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After finding success in <a href="/wiki/Country_pop" title="Country pop">country pop</a>-styled music, McBride released her next studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Timeless_(Martina_McBride_album)" title="Timeless (Martina McBride album)">Timeless</a></i>, in 2005, which consisted of country covers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-about-17">[17]</a></sup> The album included cover versions of country music standards, such as <a href="/wiki/Hank_Williams" title="Hank Williams">Hank Williams</a>' "You Win Again," <a href="/wiki/Loretta_Lynn" title="Loretta Lynn">Loretta Lynn</a>'s "You Ain't Woman Enough," and <a href="/wiki/Kris_Kristofferson" title="Kris Kristofferson">Kris Kristofferson</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Help_Me_Make_It_Through_the_Night" title="Help Me Make It Through the Night">Help Me Make It Through the Night</a>." To make the album fit its older style, McBride and her husband hired older Nashville session players and outdated analog equipment. The album sold over 250,000 copies within its first week, the highest sales start for a McBride album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-book-11">[11]</a></sup> The lead single, a cover of <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Anderson" title="Lynn Anderson">Lynn Anderson</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Rose_Garden_(Lynn_Anderson_song)" title="Rose Garden (Lynn Anderson song)">(I Never Promised You a) Rose Garden</a>", went to number 18 on the country charts, but the other two singles both failed to make top 40.</p>
<p>In 2006, McBride served as a guest coach on <i><a href="/wiki/Canadian_Idol" title="Canadian Idol">Canadian Idol</a></i>. The remaining five finalists traveled to Nashville, where McBride worked with the competitors on the songs they had chosen by country artists such as <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Lightfoot" title="Gordon Lightfoot">Gordon Lightfoot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Patsy_Cline" title="Patsy Cline">Patsy Cline</a>. Among the other guest judges that year were <a href="/wiki/Nelly_Furtado" title="Nelly Furtado">Nelly Furtado</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyndi_Lauper" title="Cyndi Lauper">Cyndi Lauper</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> McBride later joined Canadian Idol on a tour in the Spring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 2007, McBride also served as a guest coach on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_Networks" title="Fox Networks">Fox Networks</a> television series, <i><a href="/wiki/American_Idol" title="American Idol">American Idol</a>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, McBride released her ninth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Waking_Up_Laughing" title="Waking Up Laughing">Waking Up Laughing</a></i>. It was the first album in which McBride co-wrote some of the tracks. She set up her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waking_Up_Laughing_Tour" title="Waking Up Laughing Tour">Waking Up Laughing Tour</a> in 2007, which included country artists <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Atkins" title="Rodney Atkins">Rodney Atkins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Little_Big_Town" title="Little Big Town">Little Big Town</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jason_Michael_Carroll" title="Jason Michael Carroll">Jason Michael Carroll</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-about-17">[17]</a></sup> The album's lead single, "<a href="/wiki/Anyway_(Martina_McBride_song)" title="Anyway (Martina McBride song)">Anyway</a>", went to No. 5 on the Billboard Country Chart, becoming her first Top 10 hit since 2003. She also lent her voice singing "Anyway" in a Lifetime movie called, "A Life Interrupted" which premiered on April 23, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Its follow-up, "<a href="/wiki/How_I_Feel_(Martina_McBride_song)" title="How I Feel (Martina McBride song)">How I Feel</a>", reached the Top 15. In Spring 2008, McBride released <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martina_McBride:_Live_In_Concert" title="Martina McBride: Live In Concert">Martina McBride: Live In Concert</a></i>, a CD/DVD set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-about-17">[17]</a></sup> It was taped in <a href="/wiki/Moline,_Illinois" title="Moline, Illinois">Moline, Illinois</a> in September 2007.</p>
<p>In July 2007, The <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> Television Network announced a special program called <i><a href="/wiki/Six_Degrees_of_Martina_McBride" title="Six Degrees of Martina McBride">Six Degrees of Martina McBride</a></i> where individuals from around the country were challenged to find their way to McBride on their own connections and research using a maximum of six methods. The "winner" of this challenge eventually located a direct connection to McBride through her husband, John, who knew someone, who knew someone else.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> McBride recently recorded an electronically produced duet with <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>, performing his song "<a href="/wiki/Blue_Christmas_(song)" title="Blue Christmas (song)">Blue Christmas</a>" as a duet with him on his latest compilation, <i>The Elvis Presley Christmas Duets</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> A compilation collection, titled <i><a href="/wiki/Playlist:_The_Very_Best_of_Martina_McBride" title="Playlist: The Very Best of Martina McBride">Playlist: The Very Best of Martina McBride</a></i>, was released on December 16, 2008, as part of Sony BMG Playlist series. The album features 11 previously released tracks and three unreleased tracks.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008.E2.80.9310:_Shine">2008–10: <i>Shine</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martina_McBride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2008–10: Shine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>McBride wrapped up production of her tenth studio album in late 2008. The first single, "<a href="/wiki/Ride_(Martina_McBride_song)" title="Ride (Martina McBride song)">Ride</a>", was released to radio in October 2008 and debuted at No. No. 43 on the Hot Country Songs chart. It barely missed the Top 10 on the chart, peaking at number eleven in March 2009. A music video produced by Kristin Barlowe was also released at the end of the year. The album, <i><a href="/wiki/Shine_(Martina_McBride_album)" title="Shine (Martina McBride album)">Shine</a></i>, was released by RCA Records on March 24, 2009, and debuted at the top of the U.S. Country album chart and number 10 on the <i>Billboard</i> 200. McBride co-produced the album with <a href="/wiki/Dann_Huff" title="Dann Huff">Dann Huff</a>, and it featured "Sunny Side Up", a song that she co-wrote. The second single, "<a href="/wiki/I_Just_Call_You_Mine" title="I Just Call You Mine">I Just Call You Mine</a>", was released in May 2009 and reached the Top 20. The third single from <i>Shine</i> was "<a href="/wiki/Wrong_Baby_Wrong_Baby_Wrong" title="Wrong Baby Wrong Baby Wrong">Wrong Baby Wrong Baby Wrong</a>", which the Warren Brothers co-wrote with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Ellis_Orrall" title="Robert Ellis Orrall">Robert Ellis Orrall</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Love_and_Theft_(band)" title="Love and Theft (band)">Love and Theft</a> member Stephen Barker Liles.</p>
<p>McBride also initiated the Shine All Night Tour, a co-headlining venture with fellow country star and friend <a href="/wiki/Trace_Adkins" title="Trace Adkins">Trace Adkins</a> and opening act <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Buxton" title="Sarah Buxton">Sarah Buxton</a>. The tour began in November 2009 and ended in May 2010.</p>
<p>On June 10, 2010, <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> announced that McBride had collaborated on a song with <a href="/wiki/Kid_Rock" title="Kid Rock">Kid Rock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In late June 2010, McBride was nominated for a Teen Choice Award, "Favorite Country Female Artist", alongside country stars Carrie Underwood, Miranda Lambert, Taylor Swift and Gretchen Wilson.</p>
<p>In late 2010, McBride was nominated for two American Country Awards (Best Female Single &amp; Touring Artist of the year w/ Trace Adkins.) Along with the ACA nominations, she received her 14th Female Vocalist nomination for Country Music Association in October.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010.E2.80.9316:_Eleven_and_Everlasting">2010–16: <i>Eleven</i> and <i>Everlasting</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martina_McBride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2010–16: Eleven and Everlasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>McBride exited RCA in November 2010 and signed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_Nashville" title="Republic Nashville">Republic Nashville</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> She began working on a new studio album with producer <a href="/wiki/Byron_Gallimore" title="Byron Gallimore">Byron Gallimore</a>. Her first single for Republic Nashville is "<a href="/wiki/Teenage_Daughters" title="Teenage Daughters">Teenage Daughters</a>", which she also co-wrote with the Warren Brothers. McBride told <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Country_Weekly" title="Country Weekly">Country Weekly</a></i> that she co-wrote eight of the eleven songs on the album; she decided to write more frequently because she felt more confident in her songwriting ability after "Anyway" had become a hit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weekly_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-weekly-26">[26]</a></sup> An album track, "One Night" was released as a promotion for NASCAR with a music video in June 2011.</p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/I%27m_Gonna_Love_You_Through_It" title="I'm Gonna Love You Through It">I'm Gonna Love You Through It</a>" was released as the album's second single on July 25, 2011. The song became a critical and commercial hit, peaking at number 4 and becoming her first top five hit since 2006's "Anyway." The album, titled <i><a href="/wiki/Eleven_(Martina_McBride_album)" title="Eleven (Martina McBride album)">Eleven</a></i>, was released on October 11, 2011. Its third single was a cover of <a href="/wiki/Train_(band)" title="Train (band)">Train</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Marry_Me_(Train_song)" title="Marry Me (Train song)">Marry Me</a>", recorded as a duet with Train lead singer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pat_Monahan" title="Pat Monahan">Pat Monahan</a>. In September 2011, McBride was nominated for the CMA's Female Vocalist award for the 15th time, and 14th time consecutively.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">RCA Records</a> released two compilation albums in 2012, <i><a href="/wiki/Hits_and_More" title="Hits and More">Hits and More</a></i> in January and <i>The Essential Martina McBride</i> in October.</p>
<p>McBride released <i><a href="/wiki/Everlasting_(Martina_McBride_album)" title="Everlasting (Martina McBride album)">Everlasting</a></i>, a collection of R&amp;B and Soul covers, on April 8, 2014 via <a href="/wiki/Kobalt_Label_Services" title="Kobalt Label Services">Kobalt Label Services</a>. The album includes duets with <a href="/wiki/Kelly_Clarkson" title="Kelly Clarkson">Kelly Clarkson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gavin_DeGraw" title="Gavin DeGraw">Gavin DeGraw</a>, and was produced by <a href="/wiki/Don_Was" title="Don Was">Don Was</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2014, McBride received her 17th Female Vocalist nomination from the Country Music Association. This feat ties her with <a href="/wiki/Reba_McEntire" title="Reba McEntire">Reba McEntire</a> for most nominations in any vocalist category.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2016:_Reckless">2016: <i>Reckless</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martina_McBride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2016: Reckless">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2016, McBride released a new single called "Reckless" off a new album. <i><a href="/wiki/Reckless_(Martina_McBride_album)" title="Reckless (Martina McBride album)">Reckless</a></i> was released on April 29 through Nash Icon Records. It was recorded at Blackbird Studios and produced by Nathan Chapman and Dann Huff. The album includes 10 songs, one of which is the previously released single "Reckless". According to McBride, making this album felt like "coming home".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Reckless debuted at number 2 in the Billboard Top Country Albums Chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 8, 2016, McBride debuted the video for her second single, "Just Around The Corner", during the CMT Music Awards. This song is the official Band Against Cancer anthem. Band Against Cancer is a community-based movement led by Sarah Cannon (the cancer institute of HCA) in partnership with Big Machine Label Group and McBride, which aims to raise awareness, support and resources to those battling cancer. The initiative includes a series of concerts across United States, with McBride as a headliner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 2016, the singer announced a new tour called "Love Unleashed". According to McBride, the purpose of the tour was to unite people and spread love through the power of music as a response to the "tragedy and uncertainty in the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> She was also selected as one of 30 artists to perform on "<i><a href="/wiki/Forever_Country" title="Forever Country">Forever Country</a></i>", a mash-up track of <a href="/wiki/Take_Me_Home,_Country_Roads" title="Take Me Home, Country Roads">Take Me Home, Country Roads</a>, <a href="/wiki/On_the_Road_Again_(Willie_Nelson_song)" title="On the Road Again (Willie Nelson song)">On the Road Again</a> and <a href="/wiki/I_Will_Always_Love_You" title="I Will Always Love You">I Will Always Love You</a> which celebrates 50 years of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CMA_Awards" title="CMA Awards">CMA Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Charity_work">Charity work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martina_McBride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Charity work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McBride works with a variety of charities. She has served as a spokeswoman for the <a href="/wiki/National_Domestic_Violence_Hotline" title="National Domestic Violence Hotline">National Domestic Violence Hotline</a> as well as for the <a href="/wiki/National_Network_to_End_Domestic_Violence" title="National Network to End Domestic Violence">National Network to End Domestic Violence</a> and national spokeswoman for the <i>Tulsa Domestic Violence and Intervention Services</i>. Every year since 1995, she has hosted <a href="/wiki/Middle_Tennessee" title="Middle Tennessee">Middle Tennessee</a>'s <a href="/wiki/YWCA" title="YWCA">YWCA</a>, "Celebrity Auction", and it has raised nearly $400,000 so far. In 2004, she worked with "<a href="/wiki/Kids_Wish_Network" title="Kids Wish Network">Kids Wish Network</a>" to fulfill the wish of a young girl dying from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscular_Dystrophy" title="Muscular Dystrophy">Muscular Dystrophy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> McBride was awarded the "<a href="/wiki/Minnie_Pearl" title="Minnie Pearl">Minnie Pearl</a> Humanitarian Award" in 2003.</p>
<p>McBride explained that educating girls and women on domestic violence is something she works on at home with her own daughters, stating that:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">A lot of teenage girls will be first dating and they'll think, <i>'Oh he doesn't want me to see my friends. He just wants me all to himself. Isn't that sweet?'</i> Or <i>'Oh, he's just being protective. Isn't that sweet?'</i> And then it turns into something else and it's controlling. They don't recognize that until it's too late. So it's an ongoing education that you have to give young girls, I think."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>McBride has also teamed up with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loveisrespect,_National_Teen_Dating_Abuse_Helpline" title="Loveisrespect, National Teen Dating Abuse Helpline">Loveisrespect, National Teen Dating Abuse Helpline</a>, working with them on a new program called, "My Time to Shine."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>McBride appeared on the Stand Up 2 Cancer Telethon in September 2010, where she performed "<a href="/wiki/Unchained_Melody" title="Unchained Melody">Unchained Melody</a>" with <a href="/wiki/Leona_Lewis" title="Leona Lewis">Leona Lewis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Neville" title="Aaron Neville">Aaron Neville</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">Stevie Wonder</a>. Also in 2010, she hosted the YWCA for the 16th consecutive year, raising over 50,000 dollars. It totals over 500,000 dollars raised so far.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martina_McBride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1988, McBride married sound engineer John McBride and the couple have three daughters: Delaney Katharine (born December 22, 1994), Emma Justine (born March 29, 1998)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and Ava Rose Kathleen (born June 20, 2005).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> After becoming a mother, the singer reduced her touring schedule so that her daughters could have a normal upbringing. <a href="/wiki/Joe_Galante" title="Joe Galante">Joe Galante</a> said this was "an enormous choice in terms of money", but McBride had made it very clear that she wanted to be present in her daughters' lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_nominations">Awards and nominations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martina_McBride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Awards and nominations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
