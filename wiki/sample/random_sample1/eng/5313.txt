<p><b>Martin Luther King Sr.</b> (born <b>Michael King</b>; December 19, 1899 – November 11, 1984), was an American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baptist" title="Baptist">Baptist</a> <a href="/wiki/Pastor" title="Pastor">pastor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a>, and an early figure in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Movement" title="Civil Rights Movement">Civil Rights Movement</a>. He was the father of civil rights leader <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King_Jr." title="Martin Luther King Jr.">Martin Luther King Jr.</a></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Luther_King_Sr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>King was born Michael King in <a href="/wiki/Stockbridge,_Georgia" title="Stockbridge, Georgia">Stockbridge, Georgia</a>, the son of Delia (née Linsey) and James Albert King.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MyUser_Kingencyclopedia.stanford.edu_March_7_2016c_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-MyUser_Kingencyclopedia.stanford.edu_March_7_2016c-1">[1]</a></sup> He led the <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King_Jr._National_Historic_Site" title="Martin Luther King Jr. National Historic Site">Ebenezer Baptist Church</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" title="Atlanta, Georgia">Atlanta, Georgia</a>, and became a leader of the Civil Rights Movement, as the head of the <a href="/wiki/National_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Colored_People" title="National Association for the Advancement of Colored People">NAACP</a> chapter in Atlanta and of the Civic and Political League. He encouraged his son to become active in the movement.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ebenezer_Baptist_Church">Ebenezer Baptist Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Luther_King_Sr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ebenezer Baptist Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>King was a member of the Baptist Church and decided to become a preacher after being inspired by ministers who were prepared to stand up for <a href="/wiki/Racial_equality" title="Racial equality">racial equality</a>. He left Stockbridge for Atlanta, where his sister Woodie was boarding with Reverend A.D. Williams, then pastor of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Baptist_Church_(Atlanta,_Georgia)" title="First Baptist Church (Atlanta, Georgia)">First Baptist Church (Atlanta, Georgia)</a>. He attended <a href="/wiki/Dillard_University" title="Dillard University">Dillard University</a> for a two-year degree. After King started courting Williams' daughter, <a href="/wiki/Alberta_Williams_King" title="Alberta Williams King">Alberta</a>, her family encouraged him to finish his education and to become a preacher. King completed his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_school" title="High school">high school</a> education at Bryant Preparatory School, and began to preach in several black churches in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>.</p>
<p>In 1926, King started his ministerial degree at the <a href="/wiki/Morehouse_College" title="Morehouse College">Morehouse School of Religion</a>. On <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_Day" title="Thanksgiving Day">Thanksgiving Day</a> in 1926, after eight years of courtship, he married Alberta in the Ebenezer Church. The couple had three children in four years: a daughter, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Willie_Christine_King" title="Willie Christine King">Willie Christine King</a> (born 1927), <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King_Jr." title="Martin Luther King Jr.">Martin Luther King Jr.</a> (born Michael King Jr., 1929–1968), and a second son, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Daniel_Williams_King" title="Alfred Daniel Williams King">Alfred Daniel Williams King</a> (1930–1969).</p>
<p>King became leader of the Ebenezer Baptist Church in March 1931 after the death of Williams. With the country in the midst of the Great Depression, church finances were struggling, but King organized membership and fundraising drives that restored these to health. By 1934, King had become a widely respected leader of the local church. That year, he also changed his name (and that of his eldest son) from Michael King to Martin Luther King after becoming inspired during a trip to Germany by the life of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> (1483–1546), the German theologian who initiated the Protestant Reformation (though he never changed his name legally).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (January 2016)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>King was the pastor of the Ebenezer Baptist Church for four decades, wielding great influence in the black community and earning some degree of respect from the white community. He also broadcast on <a href="/wiki/WAEC" title="WAEC">WAEC</a>, a religious radio station in Atlanta.</p>
<p>In his 1950 essay <i>An Autobiography of Religious Development</i>, King Jr. wrote that his father was a major influence on his entering the ministry. He said, "I guess the influence of my father also had a great deal to do with my going in the ministry. This is not to say that he ever spoke to me in terms of being a minister, but that my admiration for him was the great moving factor; He set forth a noble example that I didn't mind following."</p>
<p>King Jr. often recounted that his father frequently sent him to work in the fields. He said that in this way he would gain a healthier respect for his forefathers.</p>
<p>In his autobiography, King Jr. remembered his father leaving a shoe shop because he and his son were asked to change seats. He said, "This was the first time I had seen Dad so furious. That experience revealed to me at a very early age that my father had not adjusted to the system, and he played a great part in shaping my <a href="/wiki/Conscience" title="Conscience">conscience</a>. I still remember walking down the street beside him as he muttered, 'I don't care how long I have to live with this system, I will never accept it.<span style="padding-right:.2em;">'</span>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another story related by King Jr. was that once the car his father was driving was stopped by a police officer, and the officer addressed the senior King as "boy". King pointed to his son, saying, "This is a boy, I'm a man; until you call me one, I will not listen to you."</p>
<p>King Jr. became an associate pastor at Ebenezer in 1948, and his father wrote a letter of recommendation for him to <a href="/wiki/Crozer_Theological_Seminary" title="Crozer Theological Seminary">Crozer Theological Seminary</a>. Despite <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theological</a> differences, father and son would later serve together as joint pastors at the church.</p>
<p>King was a major figure in the Civil Rights Movement in <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, where he rose to become the head of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAACP" title="NAACP">NAACP</a> in Atlanta and the Civic and Political League. He led the fight for equal teachers' salaries in Atlanta. He also played an instrumental role in ending <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jim_Crow_law" title="Jim Crow law">Jim Crow laws</a> in the state. King had refused to ride on Atlanta's bus system since the 1920s after a vicious attack on black passengers with no action against those responsible. King stressed the need for an educated, politically active black ministry.</p>
<p>In October 1960, when King Jr., was arrested at a peaceful sit-in in Atlanta, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Kennedy" title="Robert Kennedy">Robert Kennedy</a> telephoned the judge and helped secure his release. Although King Sr. had previously opposed Kennedy because he was a Catholic,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> he expressed his appreciation for these calls and switched his support to Kennedy. At this time, King had been a lifelong registered Republican, and had endorsed Republican <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>King Jr. soon became a popular civil rights activist. Taking inspiration from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohandas_Gandhi" title="Mohandas Gandhi">Mohandas Gandhi</a> of India, he led nonviolent protests in order to win greater rights for African Americans.</p>
<p>King Jr. was shot and killed in 1968. King Sr.'s youngest son, Alfred Daniel Williams King, died of an accidental drowning on July 21, 1969, nine days before his 39th birthday.</p>
<p>In 1969, King was one of several members of the <a href="/wiki/Morehouse_College" title="Morehouse College">Morehouse College</a> board of trustees held hostage on the campus by a group of students demanding reform in the school’s curriculum and governance. One of the students was <a href="/wiki/Samuel_L._Jackson" title="Samuel L. Jackson">Samuel L. Jackson</a>, who was suspended for his actions. Jackson subsequently became an actor and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> nominee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>King played a notable role in the nomination of <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a> as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Democratic_Party" title="United States Democratic Party">Democratic</a> candidate for President in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1976" title="U.S. presidential election, 1976">1976 election</a>. After Carter's success in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iowa_caucus" title="Iowa caucus">Iowa caucus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire_primary" title="New Hampshire primary">New Hampshire primary</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> primary, some <a href="/wiki/Social_liberalism" title="Social liberalism">liberal</a> Democrats were worried about his success and began an "ABC" ("Anyone But Carter") movement to try to head off his nomination. King pointed to Carter's leadership in ending the era of <a href="/wiki/Racial_segregation" title="Racial segregation">segregation</a> in Georgia, and helping to repeal laws restricting voting which especially disenfranchised African Americans. With King's support, Carter continued to build a coalition of black and white voters and win the nomination. King delivered the invocation at the 1976 and 1980 <a href="/wiki/Democratic_National_Convention" title="Democratic National Convention">Democratic National Conventions</a>. King was also a member of <a href="/wiki/Omega_Psi_Phi" title="Omega Psi Phi">Omega Psi Phi</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Murder_of_wife">Murder of wife</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Luther_King_Sr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Murder of wife">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>King Sr.'s wife and King Jr.'s mother, <a href="/wiki/Alberta_Williams_King" title="Alberta Williams King">Alberta</a>, was murdered by Marcus Wayne Chenault on Sunday, June 30, 1974, at the Ebenezer Baptist Church during Sunday services. Chenault was a black man from Ohio who stood up and yelled, "You are serving a false God", and began to fire from two pistols while Alberta was playing "The Lord's Prayer" on the church organ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atlanta2012-06-28_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-atlanta2012-06-28-5">[5]</a></sup> Upon capture, the assassin disclosed that his intended target was Martin Luther King Sr., who was elsewhere that Sunday. After failing to see Mr. King Sr., the killer instead fatally shot Alberta King and Rev. Edward Boykin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tfp2015-06-24_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-tfp2015-06-24-6">[6]</a></sup> Chenault stated that he was driven to murder after concluding that "black ministers were a menace to black people" and that "all Christians are my enemies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milwaukee1974-07-08_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-milwaukee1974-07-08-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life_and_death">Later life and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Luther_King_Sr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later life and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With his son's widow <a href="/wiki/Coretta_Scott_King" title="Coretta Scott King">Coretta Scott King</a>, King was present when President Carter awarded a <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Medal_of_Freedom" title="Presidential Medal of Freedom">Presidential Medal of Freedom</a> to King Jr. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posthumous_recognition" title="Posthumous recognition">posthumously</a> in 1977. In 1980, he published his <a href="/wiki/Autobiography" title="Autobiography">autobiography</a>. King died of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_attack" title="Heart attack">heart attack</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crawford_W._Long" title="Crawford W. Long">Crawford W. Long</a> Hospital in Atlanta on November 11, 1984, at age 84. He was interred next to his wife Alberta at the South View Cemetery in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" title="Atlanta, Georgia">Atlanta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_film">In film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Luther_King_Sr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Hour_of_Chaos" title="In the Hour of Chaos">In the Hour of Chaos</a></i> is a 2016 American documentary drama written and directed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayer_Mack" title="Bayer Mack">Bayer Mack</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Czar_of_Black_Hollywood" title="The Czar of Black Hollywood">The Czar of Black Hollywood</a></i>), which tells the story of King Sr.'s rise from an impoverished childhood in the violent backwoods of Georgia to become patriarch of one of the most famous – and tragedy-plagued – families in history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>From <i><a href="/wiki/The_Huffington_Post" title="The Huffington Post">The Huffington Post</a></i>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The documentary weaves strands of three stories into one. The underpinnings of the documentary are the events of the time — everything from the Atlanta Riots and the disenfranchisement of blacks throughout the South to the era of prohibition and war time. Over this background, there are two more stories — that of Daddy King and the story of Daddy’s influence on Martin Jr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Part one of <i>In the Hour of Chaos</i> aired on public television in early 2016 and the full film was released online July 1, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
