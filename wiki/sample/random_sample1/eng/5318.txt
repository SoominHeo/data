<p>The <b>Infantry Tank Mark II</b>, best known as the <b>Matilda</b>, was a <a href="/wiki/Tanks_in_the_British_Army" title="Tanks in the British Army">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Infantry_tank" title="Infantry tank">infantry tank</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The design began as the <b>A12</b> specification in 1936, as a gun-armed counterpart to the first British infantry tank, the <a href="/wiki/Machine_gun" title="Machine gun">machine gun</a> armed, two-man A11 <a href="/wiki/Matilda_I_(tank)" title="Matilda I (tank)">Infantry Tank Mark I</a>. The Mark I was also known as Matilda, and the larger A12 was initially known as the <b>Matilda II</b>, <b>Matilda senior</b> or <b>Waltzing Matilda</b>. The Mark I was abandoned in 1940, and from then on the A12 was almost always known simply as "the Matilda".</p>
<p>With its heavy armour, the Matilda II was an excellent infantry support tank but with somewhat limited speed and armament. It was the only British tank to serve from the start of the war to its end, although it is particularly associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Africa_Campaign" title="North Africa Campaign">North Africa Campaign</a>. It was replaced in front-line service by the lighter and less costly Infantry Tank Mk III <a href="/wiki/Valentine_tank" title="Valentine tank">Valentine</a> beginning in late 1941.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_history">Development history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matilda_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The split between the infantry tank and cruisers had its origins in the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> division between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mark_I_(tank)" title="Mark I (tank)">first British heavy tanks</a> and the faster <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whippet_(tank)" title="Whippet (tank)">Whippet Medium Mark A</a> and its successors the <a href="/wiki/Medium_Mark_B" title="Medium Mark B">Medium Mark B</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medium_Mark_C" title="Medium Mark C">Medium Mark C</a>. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interbellum" title="Interbellum">interbellum</a>, British tank experiments generally followed these basic classifications, which were made part of the overall doctrine with the work of Major-General <a href="/wiki/Percy_Hobart" title="Percy Hobart">Percy Hobart</a> and the influence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basil_Liddell_Hart" title="Basil Liddell Hart">Captain B.H. Liddell Hart</a>.</p>
<p>In 1934 Hobart, the then "Inspector, Royal Tank Corps", postulated in a paper two alternatives for a tank to support the infantry. One was a very small, heavily armoured, machine gun-armed model that would be fielded in large numbers to overwhelm the enemy defences. The other was a larger vehicle with a cannon as well as machine guns and heavier armour proof against enemy <a href="/wiki/Field_artillery" title="Field artillery">field artillery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> Vickers designed a tank to a General Staff specification based on the first option as the A11 Matilda. Within the limitations of military finances, the <a href="/wiki/Master-General_of_the_Ordnance" title="Master-General of the Ordnance">Master-General of the Ordnance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Elles" title="Hugh Elles">Hugh Elles</a>, went for the smaller machine gun tank and the larger cannon-armed version did not proceed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> This requirement was passed to <a href="/wiki/Vickers-Armstrongs" title="Vickers-Armstrongs">Vickers-Armstrongs</a> which had a prototype (A11E1) but with armour proof against current anti-tanks guns ready by September 1936.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first suggestion for a larger Infantry Tank was made in 1936, with specification A12. The design was produced by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Arsenal" title="Royal Arsenal">Royal Arsenal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Woolwich" title="Woolwich">Woolwich</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_Foundry" title="Vulcan Foundry">Vulcan Foundry</a> was selected as the manufacturer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFletcher.2C_Great_Tank_Scandal4_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFletcher.2C_Great_Tank_Scandal4-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jentz11_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Jentz11-1">[1]</a></sup> A12 used a number of design elements of the A7, a medium tank that was built in limited numbers in the early 1930s whose mechanical layout was used for many following designs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> With its greatly increased armor, a lack of power was seen as a problem. The solution was to use two AEC straight-six water-cooled diesel engines, used in London buses, providing up to 87 hp each. These were linked along a common shaft. Suspension was to use the 'Japanese Type' bell crank suspension used on the A7.</p>
<p>Vulcan received a contract for two wooden mock-ups and two mild-steel prototypes in November 1936. The first mock-up was delivered in April 1937 and the A12E1 prototype in April 1938. The prototypes proved excellent in a 1,000 miles (1,600 km) test, resulting in only a few changes to improve the gearbox, suspension and cooling. When war was recognised as imminent, production of the Matilda II was ordered and that of the Matilda I curtailed. The first order was placed shortly after trials were completed, with 140 ordered from Vulcan in June 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFletcher19948_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFletcher19948-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matilda_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Matilda Senior weighed around 27 long tons (27 t), more than twice as much as its predecessor, and was armed with a <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_QF_2-pounder" title="Ordnance QF 2-pounder">QF 2 pounder (40 mm)</a> <a href="/wiki/Tank_gun" title="Tank gun">tank gun</a> in a three-man <a href="/wiki/Turret" title="Turret">turret</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFletcher19945_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFletcher19945-17">[16]</a></sup> The turret traversed by hydraulic motor or by hand through 360 degrees; the gun could be elevated through an arc from −15 to +20 degrees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jentz12_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Jentz12-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[nb 2]</a></sup> One of the most serious weaknesses of the Matilda II was the lack of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_explosive" title="High explosive">high-explosive</a> round for its main gun. A high-explosive shell was designed for the 2 pounder but for reasons never explained, it was not issued and the tank's best weapon against un-armoured targets was its machine gun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like many other British <a href="/wiki/Infantry_tank" title="Infantry tank">infantry tanks</a>, it was heavily armoured. The front glacis was 78 mm (3.1 in) thick, although the nose plates top and bottom were thinner but angled. The sides of the hull were 65 to 70 millimetres (2.6 to 2.8 in), and the rear armour, protecting the engine to sides and rear, was 55 millimetres (2.2 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jentz12_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Jentz12-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[nb 3]</a></sup> The turret armour was 75 mm (3.0 in) all round. The turret roof, hull roof and engine deck were 20 millimetres (0.79 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WW2equipment_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-WW2equipment-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Matilda's armor was the heaviest of its era. Contemporary German <a href="/wiki/Panzer_III" title="Panzer III">Panzer III</a> and <a href="/wiki/Panzer_IV" title="Panzer IV">Panzer IV</a> tanks had 30 to 50 millimetres (1.2 to 2.0 in) hull armour, while the <a href="/wiki/T-34" title="T-34">T-34</a> had 40 to 47 millimetres (1.6 to 1.9 in) but angled at 60 degrees. Matilda's side and rear armor was relatively heavy even at the end of the war, when tanks like the <a href="/wiki/M4_Sherman" title="M4 Sherman">M4 Sherman</a> carried about 40 mm, and late models of the <a href="/wiki/Panther_tank" title="Panther tank">Panther</a> carried 50 mm. The shape of the nose armour was based on Christie's designs and came to a narrow point with storage lockers added on either side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFletcher19947_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFletcher19947-22">[19]</a></sup> The heavy armour of the Matilda's cast turret became legendary; for a time in 1940–1941, the Matilda earned the nickname "Queen of the Desert".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFletcher199414_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFletcher199414-23">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the Matilda possessed a degree of protection that was unmatched in the North African theatre, the sheer weight of the <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_armour" title="Vehicle armour">armour</a> on the vehicle contributed to a very low average speed of about 6 mph (9.7 km/h) on desert terrain and 16 miles per hour (26 km/h) on roads. At the time, this was not thought to be a problem, since British infantry tank doctrine valued heavy armour and trench-crossing ability over speed and cross-country mobility (which was considered to be characteristic of <a href="/wiki/Cruiser_tank" title="Cruiser tank">cruiser tanks</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Crusader_tank" title="Crusader tank">Crusader</a>). The slow speed of the Matilda was further exacerbated by a troublesome suspension and a comparatively weak power unit, which was created from two AEC 6-cylinder bus engines linked to a single shaft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFletcher19946_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFletcher19946-24">[21]</a></sup> This arrangement was complicated and time-consuming to maintain, as it required mechanics to work on each engine separately and subjected automotive components to uneven wear-and-tear. It did provide some <a href="/wiki/Redundancy_(engineering)" title="Redundancy (engineering)">mechanical redundancy</a>, since failure in one engine would not prevent the Matilda from using the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFletcher19946_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFletcher19946-24">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The tank's suspension system was that which had been developed by Vickers for their Medium C prototype in the mid-1920s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFletcher19947_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFletcher19947-22">[19]</a></sup> The tank was carried by five double wheel <a href="/wiki/Bogie" title="Bogie">bogies</a> on each side. Four of the bogies were on bellcranks in pairs, with a common horizontal coil spring. The fifth bogie at the rear was sprung against a hull bracket. Between the first bogie and the idler wheel, was a larger diameter vertically sprung "jockey wheel". The first Matildas had return rollers; these were replaced in later models by track skids, which were far easier to manufacture and to service in the field.</p>
<p>The turret carried the main armament, with the machine gun to the right in a rotating internal mantlet. Traverse was by a hydraulic system. As the gun was balanced for ease of movement by the gunner, much of the breech end was behind the trunnions. Two smoke grenade launchers were carried on the right side of the turret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFletcher19948_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFletcher19948-16">[15]</a></sup> The grenade launcher mechanisms were cut down Lee–Enfield rifles, each loaded with a smoke grenade. Its camouflage scheme was designed by Major Denys Pavitt of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Camouflage_Development_And_Training_Centre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Camouflage Development And Training Centre (page does not exist)">Camouflage Development And Training Centre</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[22]</a></sup> based on the <a href="/wiki/Dazzle_camouflage" title="Dazzle camouflage">dazzle patterns</a> of first world war ships. The design incorporated block colours, visually breaking the tank in half.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_history">Production history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matilda_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/British_armoured_fighting_vehicle_production_during_World_War_II" title="British armoured fighting vehicle production during World War II">British armoured fighting vehicle production during World War II</a></div>
<p>The first Matilda was produced in 1937, but only two were in service when war broke out in September 1939. Following the initial order from <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_Foundry" title="Vulcan Foundry">Vulcan Foundry</a>, a second order was placed shortly after with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruston_%26_Hornsby" title="Ruston &amp; Hornsby">Ruston &amp; Hornsby</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFletcher19948_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFletcher19948-16">[15]</a></sup> Some 2,987 tanks were produced by the Vulcan Foundry, <a href="/wiki/John_Fowler_%26_Co." title="John Fowler &amp; Co.">John Fowler &amp; Co.</a> of <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, Ruston &amp; Hornsby, and later by the <a href="/wiki/London,_Midland_and_Scottish_Railway" title="London, Midland and Scottish Railway">London, Midland and Scottish Railway</a> at <a href="/wiki/Horwich_Works" title="Horwich Works">Horwich Works</a>; <a href="/wiki/Harland_and_Wolff" title="Harland and Wolff">Harland and Wolff</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/North_British_Locomotive_Company" title="North British Locomotive Company">North British Locomotive Company</a> Glasgow. The last were delivered in August 1943.</p>
<p>Peak production was 1,330 in 1942, the most common model being the Mark IV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Matilda was difficult to manufacture. For example, the pointed nose was a single casting that, upon initial release from the mould, was thicker than required in some areas. To avoid a needless addition to the tank's weight, the thick areas were ground away. This process required highly skilled workers and additional time. The complex suspension and multi-piece hull side coverings also added time to manufacturing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFletcher19947_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFletcher19947-22">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Combat_history">Combat history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matilda_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Combat history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="French_Campaign_of_1940">French Campaign of 1940</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matilda_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: French Campaign of 1940">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Matilda was first used in combat by the <a href="/wiki/7th_Royal_Tank_Regiment" title="7th Royal Tank Regiment">7th Royal Tank Regiment</a> in <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_France" title="Battle of France">France in 1940</a>. Only 23 of the unit's tanks were Matilda IIs; the rest of the British Infantry Tanks in France were <a href="/wiki/Matilda_I_(tank)" title="Matilda I (tank)">A11 Matildas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[24]</a></sup> Its 2-pounder gun was comparable to other tank guns in the 37 to 45 mm range. Due to the thickness of its armour, it was largely immune, but not impervious, to the guns of the <a href="/wiki/Tanks_in_the_German_Army" title="Tanks in the German Army">German tanks</a> and anti-tank guns in France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[25]</a></sup> The Germans found the 88 mm anti-aircraft guns were the only effective counter-measure. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counter-attack_at_Arras" title="Counter-attack at Arras">counter-attack at Arras</a> of May 21, 1940, British Matilda IIs (and Matilda Is) were able to briefly disrupt German progress, but, being unsupported, they sustained heavy losses. All vehicles surviving the battles around <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dunkirk" title="Battle of Dunkirk">Dunkirk</a> were abandoned when the <a href="/wiki/British_Expeditionary_Force_(World_War_II)" title="British Expeditionary Force (World War II)">BEF</a> evacuated.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Africa_1940_to_1942">North Africa 1940 to 1942</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matilda_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: North Africa 1940 to 1942">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Up to early 1942, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Africa_campaign" title="North Africa campaign">war in North Africa</a>, the Matilda proved highly effective against Italian and German tanks, although vulnerable to the larger calibre and medium calibre anti-tank guns.</p>
<p>In late 1940, during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Compass" title="Operation Compass">Operation <i>Compass</i></a>, Matildas of the British <a href="/wiki/7th_Armoured_Division_(United_Kingdom)" title="7th Armoured Division (United Kingdom)">7th Armoured Division</a> wreaked havoc among the Italian forces in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>. The Italians were equipped with <a href="/wiki/L3/35" title="L3/35">L3 tankettes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M11/39" title="M11/39">M11/39 medium tanks</a>, neither of which had any chance against the Matildas. Italian gunners were to discover that the Matildas were impervious to a wide assortment of artillery. Matildas continued to confound the Italians as the British pushed them out of Egypt and entered <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> to take <a href="/wiki/Bardia" title="Bardia">Bardia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tobruk" title="Tobruk">Tobruk</a>. Even as late as November 1941, German infantry combat reports show the impotence of ill-equipped infantry against the Matilda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ultimately, in the rapid manoeuvre warfare often practised in the open desert of North Africa, the Matilda's low speed and unreliable steering mechanism became major problems. Another snag was the lack of a high-explosive shell (the appropriate shell existed but was not issued). When the German <i><a href="/wiki/Afrika_Korps" title="Afrika Korps">Afrika Korps</a></i> arrived in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/8.8_cm_FlaK_18/36/37/41" title="8.8 cm FlaK 18/36/37/41">88 mm anti-aircraft gun</a> was again pressed into service against the Matilda, causing heavy losses during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Battleaxe" title="Operation Battleaxe">Operation <i>Battleaxe</i></a>, when sixty-four Matildas were lost. The arrival of the more powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pak_38" title="Pak 38">50mm Pak 38 anti-tank gun</a> and <a href="/wiki/7.5_cm_Pak_40" title="7.5 cm Pak 40">75mm Pak 40 anti-tank gun</a> also provided a means for the German infantry to engage Matilda tanks at combat ranges. Nevertheless, during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Crusader" title="Operation Crusader">Operation <i>Crusader</i></a> Matilda tanks of 1st and 32nd Army Tank Brigades were instrumental in the breakout from Tobruk and the capture of the Axis fortress of Bardia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[27]</a></sup> The operation was decided by the infantry tanks, after the failure of the cruiser tank equipped 7th Armoured Division to overcome the Axis tank forces in the open desert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p>As the German army received new tanks with more powerful guns, as well as more powerful anti-tank guns and ammunition, the Matilda proved less and less effective. Firing tests conducted by the Afrikakorps showed that the Matilda had become vulnerable to a number of German weapons at ordinary combat ranges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[29]</a></sup> Due to the small size of the turret and the need to balance the gun in it, up-gunning the Matilda, without substituting a roomier turret, was impractical. There was at least one instance of the turret from the A24/A27 cruiser tank series being fitted to a Matilda, allowing the 6-pounder to be fitted. As the size of the Matilda's turret ring was 54 inches (1.37 m) vs. the 57 inches of the A27, this would entail either enlarging the turret ring, a drastic measure, or more likely superimposing a larger turret ring on the hull. A better solution might have been possible, as the Churchill Mark III with its 54 inch turret ring was armed with a 6-pounder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[30]</a></sup> It was also somewhat expensive to produce. Vickers proposed an alternative, the <a href="/wiki/Valentine_tank" title="Valentine tank">Valentine tank</a>, which had the same gun and a similar level of armour protection but on a faster and cheaper chassis derived from that of their <a href="/wiki/Cruiser_Mk_II" title="Cruiser Mk II">"heavy cruiser" Cruiser Mk II</a>. With the arrival of the Valentine in Autumn 1941, the Matilda was phased out by the British Army through attrition, with lost vehicles no longer being replaced. By the time of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_El_Alamein" title="Second Battle of El Alamein">Second Battle of El Alamein</a> (October 1942), few Matildas were in service, with many having been lost during Operation <i>Crusader</i> and then the Gazala battles in early summer of 1942. Around twenty-five took part in the battle as mine-clearing, <b>Matilda Scorpion</b> <a href="/wiki/Mine_flail" title="Mine flail">mine flail</a> tanks.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minor_campaigns">Minor campaigns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matilda_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Minor campaigns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In early 1941, a small number of Matildas were used during the <a href="/wiki/East_African_Campaign_(World_War_II)" title="East African Campaign (World War II)">East Africa Campaign</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Keren" title="Battle of Keren">Battle of Keren</a>. However, the mountainous terrain of East Africa did not allow the tanks of B Squadron <a href="/wiki/4th_Royal_Tank_Regiment" title="4th Royal Tank Regiment">4th Royal Tank Regiment</a> to be as effective as the tanks of the <a href="/wiki/7th_Royal_Tank_Regiment" title="7th Royal Tank Regiment">7th Royal Tank Regiment</a> had been in Egypt and Libya.</p>
<p>A few Matildas of the 7th RTR were present on Crete during <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Crete" title="Battle of Crete">the German invasion</a>, and all of them were lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pacific_theatre">Pacific theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matilda_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pacific theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In the Pacific <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Japanese</a> forces were lacking in heavy anti-tank guns and the Matilda remained in service with several Australian regiments in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_4th_Armoured_Brigade" title="Australian 4th Armoured Brigade">Australian 4th Armoured Brigade</a>, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_West_Pacific_Area" title="South West Pacific Area">South West Pacific Area</a>. They first saw active service in the <a href="/wiki/Huon_Peninsula_campaign" title="Huon Peninsula campaign">Huon Peninsula campaign</a> in October 1943. Matilda II tanks remained in action until the last day of the war in the Wewak, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bougainville_campaign_(1943%E2%80%9345)" title="Bougainville campaign (1943–45)">Bougainville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Borneo_campaign_(1945)" title="Borneo campaign (1945)">Borneo</a> campaigns, which made the Matilda the only British tank to remain in service throughout the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rtoww2sam_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-rtoww2sam-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_use">Soviet use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matilda_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Soviet use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Red Army received 918 of the 1,084 Matildas sent to the USSR.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rtoww2sam_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-rtoww2sam-3">[3]</a></sup> The Soviet Matildas saw action as early as the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Moscow" title="Battle of Moscow">Battle of Moscow</a> and became fairly common during 1942. Unsurprisingly, the tank was found to be too slow and unreliable. Crews often complained that snow and dirt were accumulating behind the "skirt" panels, clogging the suspension. The slowness and heavy armour made them comparable to the Red Army's <a href="/wiki/Kliment_Voroshilov_tank" title="Kliment Voroshilov tank">KV-1 heavy tanks</a>, but the Matilda had nowhere near the firepower of the KV. Most Soviet Matildas were expended during 1942 but a few served on as late as 1944. The Soviets modified the tanks with the addition of sections of steel welded to the tracks to give better grip, and redesigned the turret in order to fit a F-32 anti tank gun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rtoww2sam_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-rtoww2sam-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Following <a href="/wiki/Operation_Battleaxe" title="Operation Battleaxe">Operation <i>Battleaxe</i></a> a dozen Matildas left behind the Axis lines were repaired and put into service by the Germans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tucker_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-tucker-35">[32]</a></sup> The German designation was <i>Infanterie Panzerkampfwagen Mk.II 748(e)</i> translating roughly as "Infantry Tank Mk.II Number 748 English". The Matildas were well-regarded by their German users<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[33]</a></sup> although their use in battle caused confusion to both sides, despite extra-prominent German markings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tucker_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-tucker-35">[32]</a></sup></p>

V A gun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[41]</a></sup></dd>

<li><b>Matilda Hedgehog</b> (6)</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Officially known as the <b>Matilda Projector, Hedgehog, No. 1 Mark I</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fletcher1_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fletcher1-46">[42]</a></sup> this fitted a <a href="/wiki/Hedgehog_(weapon)" title="Hedgehog (weapon)">Hedgehog</a> 7-chambered <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(weapon)#Spigot_mortar" title="Mortar (weapon)">spigot mortar</a> in an armoured box on the rear hull of several Australian Matilda tanks. The projector was elevated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydraulic" title="Hydraulic">hydraulics</a> adapted from the Logan traversing mechanism used in <a href="/wiki/M3_Lee" title="M3 Lee">M3 Medium</a> tank turrets<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fletcher1_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fletcher1-46">[42]</a></sup> and electrically fired either individually or in a salvo of six, from the 12 o'clock position;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fletcher2_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fletcher2-47">[43]</a></sup> the fifth tube could not be fired until the turret was traversed to 1 o'clock,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fletcher2_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fletcher2-47">[43]</a></sup> to move the radio antenna out of the bomb's flightpath. Each bomb weighed 65 <a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">lb</a> (29 <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kg</a>) and contained 30 to 35 lb (14 to 16 kg) of high explosive. The range was up to 400 m (440 yd). Aiming was accomplished by pointing the entire tank; the mounting had no independent traverse,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DustSandJungle_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-DustSandJungle-48">[44]</a></sup> so accuracy was not spectacular, but adequate for the task.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fletcher2_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fletcher2-47">[43]</a></sup> Trials at <a href="/wiki/Southport,_Queensland" title="Southport, Queensland">Southport, Queensland</a>, in May 1945 were pronounced a complete success, and the Projector would have been impressive against enemy bunkers, but the war ended before it was used operationally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fletcher2_47-3"><a href="#cite_note-Fletcher2-47">[43]</a></sup></dd>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surviving_tanks">Surviving tanks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matilda_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Surviving tanks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Puckapunyal-Matilda-Hedgehog-1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1703" data-file-width="2530" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Puckapunyal-Matilda-Hedgehog-1.jpg/220px-Puckapunyal-Matilda-Hedgehog-1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Puckapunyal-Matilda-Hedgehog-1.jpg/330px-Puckapunyal-Matilda-Hedgehog-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Puckapunyal-Matilda-Hedgehog-1.jpg/440px-Puckapunyal-Matilda-Hedgehog-1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Puckapunyal-Matilda-Hedgehog-1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Matilda Hedgehog at the RAAC Museum, Puckapunyal, Australia (2007)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Around 70 Matilda IIs survive in various degrees of preservation. Around 30 are in Australia, either in museums, displayed as public monuments or in private ownership. A notable collection is that of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Armoured_Corps_Memorial_and_Army_Tank_Museum" title="Royal Australian Armoured Corps Memorial and Army Tank Museum">Royal Australian Armoured Corps Memorial and Army Tank Museum</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Puckapunyal" title="Puckapunyal">Puckapunyal</a>, Australia, which has five Matilda IIs on display, including a Matilda Frog flame–tank, a Matilda Hedgehog and a Matilda Bulldozer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tanks in running condition are owned by <a href="/wiki/The_Tank_Museum" title="The Tank Museum">The Tank Museum</a> in the United Kingdom and by several private owners in Australia. The Tank Museum at Bovington also displays the only surviving Matilda Canal Defence Light. Other examples are displayed at the <a href="/wiki/Kubinka_Tank_Museum" title="Kubinka Tank Museum">Kubinka Tank Museum</a> in Russia, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Museum_of_the_Armed_Forces_and_Military_History" title="Royal Museum of the Armed Forces and Military History">Royal Museum of the Armed Forces and Military History</a> in Belgium, the <a href="/wiki/Yad_La-Shiryon" title="Yad La-Shiryon">Yad La-Shiryon</a> museum in the <a href="/wiki/Latrun" title="Latrun">Latrun</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_des_Blind%C3%A9s" title="Musée des Blindés">Musée des Blindés</a> in France, the <a href="/wiki/Cavalry_Tank_Museum,_Ahmednagar" title="Cavalry Tank Museum, Ahmednagar">Cavalry Tank Museum</a> at <a href="/wiki/Ahmednagar" title="Ahmednagar">Ahmednagar</a> in India and the <a href="/wiki/Military_Vehicle_Technology_Foundation" title="Military Vehicle Technology Foundation">Military Vehicle Technology Foundation</a>, a private collection in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[46]</a></sup></p>
