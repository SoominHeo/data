<p><i><b>Ephedra</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gymnosperm" title="Gymnosperm">gymnosperm</a> <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a>, the only genus in its family, <b>Ephedraceae</b>, and order, <b>Ephedrales</b>. The various species of <i>Ephedra</i> are widespread in many lands, native to southwestern <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, and southwest and central <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, northern China, and western <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liz10_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-liz10-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate_climates" title="Temperate climates">temperate climates</a>, most <i>Ephedra</i> species grow on shores or in sandy soils with direct sun exposure. Common names in English include <b>joint-pine</b>, <b>jointfir</b>, <b>Mormon-tea</b> or <b>Brigham tea</b>. The <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> name for <i>Ephedra</i> species is <i>mahuang</i> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">麻黄</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">麻黃</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">máhuáng</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade–Giles">Wade–Giles</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile" xml:lang="zh-Latn-wadegile">ma-huang</span></i>; literally: "cannabis yellow"). <i>Ephedra</i> is also sometimes called sea grape (from the French <i><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">raisin de mer</span></i>), a common name for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiosperm" title="Angiosperm">flowering plant</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Coccoloba_uvifera" title="Coccoloba uvifera">Coccoloba uvifera</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_uses">Medical uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ephedra_(plant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Medical uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maou.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="1952" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Maou.jpg/170px-Maou.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Maou.jpg/255px-Maou.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Maou.jpg/340px-Maou.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maou.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Plant as used in Chinese herbology (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_medicine" title="Crude medicine">crude medicine</a>)</div>
</div>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ephedra" title="Ephedra">Ephedra</a></div>
<p>Plants of the genus <i>Ephedra</i>, including <i><a href="/wiki/Ephedra_sinica" title="Ephedra sinica">E. sinica</a></i> and others, have traditionally been used by indigenous people for a variety of medicinal purposes, including treatment of <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hay_fever" title="Hay fever">hay fever</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Common_cold" title="Common cold">common cold</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abourashed_2003_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Abourashed_2003-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Alkaloid" title="Alkaloid">alkaloids</a> <a href="/wiki/Ephedrine" title="Ephedrine">ephedrine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pseudoephedrine" title="Pseudoephedrine">pseudoephedrine</a> are active constituents of <i>E. sinica</i> and other members of the genus. These compounds are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sympathomimetic_amine" title="Sympathomimetic amine">sympathomimetics</a> with <a href="/wiki/Stimulant" title="Stimulant">stimulant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Decongestant" title="Decongestant">decongestant</a> qualities and are chemically <a href="/wiki/Substituted_amphetamine" title="Substituted amphetamine">substituted amphetamines</a>.</p>
<p>Pollen of <i>Ephedra</i> spp. was found in the <a href="/wiki/Shanidar_Cave#Shanidar_4.2C_the_.22flower_burial.22" title="Shanidar Cave">Shanidar IV</a> burial site in Iraq, which led to the suggestion that its use as a medicinal plant dates to over 60,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_B._Pettitt" title="Paul B. Pettitt">Paul B. Pettitt</a> has stated that "[a] recent examination of the microfauna from the strata into which the grave was cut suggests that the pollen was deposited by the burrowing rodent <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meriones_persicus" title="Meriones persicus">Meriones persicus</a></i>, which is common in the Shanidar microfauna and whose burrowing activity can be observed today".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It has been suggested that <i>Ephedra</i> may be the <a href="/wiki/Soma_(drink)" title="Soma (drink)">Soma</a> plant of <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-Iranian_religion" title="Proto-Indo-Iranian religion">Indo-Iranian</a> religion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adverse_Effects">Adverse Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ephedra_(plant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Adverse Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alkaloids obtained from the species of Ephedra in herbal medicines containing synthetically prepared <a href="/wiki/Pseudoephedrine" title="Pseudoephedrine">pseudoephedrine</a> and ephedrine can cause cardiovascular events. These events have been associated with arrhythmias, palpitations, tachycardia and myocardial infarction. <a href="/wiki/Caffeine" title="Caffeine">Caffeine</a> consumption in combination with ephedrine has been reported to increase the risk of these cardiovascular events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Species">Species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ephedra_(plant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Accepted species:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liz10_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-liz10-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Earliest uses of <i>Ephedra</i> spp. (mahuang) for specific illnesses date back to 5000 BC. <a href="/wiki/Ephedrine" title="Ephedrine">Ephedrine</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isomers" title="Isomers">isomers</a> were already isolated in 1881 from <i>Ephedra dystachia</i> and characterized by the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_people" title="Japanese people">Japanese</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_chemist" title="Organic chemist">organic chemist</a> <a href="/wiki/Nagai_Nagayoshi" title="Nagai Nagayoshi">Nagai Nagayoshi</a> of the 19th century. His work to access <i>Ephedra</i> drug materials to isolate a pure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical" title="Pharmaceutical">pharmaceutical</a> substance, and the systematic production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-synthetic" title="Semi-synthetic">semi-synthetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Derivative" title="Derivative">derivatives</a> thereof is relevant still today as the three species <i><a href="/wiki/Ephedra_sinica" title="Ephedra sinica">Ephedra sinica</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ephedra_vulgaris" title="Ephedra vulgaris">Ephedra vulgaris</a></i> and to a lesser extent <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ephedra_equisetina&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ephedra equisetina (page does not exist)">Ephedra equisetina</a></i> are commercially grown in <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">Mainland China</a> as a source for natural ephedrines and isomers for use in <a href="/wiki/Pharmacy" title="Pharmacy">pharmacy</a>. <i>E. sinica</i> and <i>E. vulgaris</i> usually carry six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optically_active" title="Optically active">optically active</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenylethylamines" title="Phenylethylamines">phenylethylamines</a>, mostly <a href="/wiki/Ephedrine" title="Ephedrine">ephedrine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pseudoephedrine" title="Pseudoephedrine">pseudoephedrine</a> with minor amounts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norephedrine" title="Norephedrine">norephedrine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cathine" title="Cathine">norpseudoephedrine</a> as well as the three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methylated" title="Methylated">methylated</a> analogs. Reliable information on the total alkaloid content of the crude drug is difficult to obtain. Based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HPLC" title="HPLC">HPLC</a> analyses in industrial settings, the concentrations of total alkaloids in dried <i>Herba Ephedra</i> ranged between 1 and 4%, and in some cases up to 6%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>For a review of the <a href="/wiki/Alkaloid" title="Alkaloid">alkaloid</a> distribution in different species of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i>Ephedra</i> see Jian-fang Cui (1991).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Other American and European species of <i>Ephedra</i>, e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Ephedra_nevadensis" title="Ephedra nevadensis">Ephedra nevadensis</a></i> (Nevada Mormon tea) have not been systematically assayed; based on unpublished field investigations, they contain very low levels (less than 0.1%) or none at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
