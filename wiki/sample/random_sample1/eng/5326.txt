<p>The <b>Mogao Caves</b> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">莫高窟</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Mògāo kū</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade–Giles">Wade–Giles</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile" xml:lang="zh-Latn-wadegile">Mo<sup>4</sup>-kao<sup>1</sup> k'u<sup>1</sup></span></i>), also known as the <b>Thousand Buddha Grottoes</b> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">千佛洞</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">qiān fó dòng</span></i>), form a system of 492 temples 25 km (16 mi) southeast of the center of <a href="/wiki/Dunhuang" title="Dunhuang">Dunhuang</a>, an oasis strategically located at a religious and cultural crossroads on the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a> province, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. The caves may also be known as the <b>Dunhuang Caves</b>, however, this term is also used as a collective term to include other Buddhist cave sites in the Dunhuang area, such as the <a href="/wiki/Western_Thousand_Buddha_Caves" title="Western Thousand Buddha Caves">Western Thousand Buddha Caves</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Yulin_Caves" title="Yulin Caves">Yulin Caves</a> farther away. The caves contain some of the finest examples of <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_art" title="Buddhist art">Buddhist art</a> spanning a period of 1,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unesco_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-unesco-1">[1]</a></sup> The first caves were dug out in 366 AD as places of Buddhist meditation and worship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unesco_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-unesco-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Mogao Caves are the best known of the <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> Buddhist grottoes and, along with <a href="/wiki/Longmen_Grottoes" title="Longmen Grottoes">Longmen Grottoes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yungang_Grottoes" title="Yungang Grottoes">Yungang Grottoes</a>, are one of the three famous ancient Buddhist sculptural sites of China.</p>
<p>An important cache of documents was discovered in 1900 in the so-called "Library Cave," which had been walled-up in the 11th century. The content of the library was dispersed around the world, and the largest collections are now found in Beijing, London, Paris and Berlin, and the <a href="/wiki/International_Dunhuang_Project" title="International Dunhuang Project">International Dunhuang Project</a> exists to coordinate and collect scholarly work on the <a href="/wiki/Dunhuang_manuscripts" title="Dunhuang manuscripts">Dunhuang manuscripts</a> and other material. The caves themselves are now a popular tourist destination, with a number open for visiting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAT_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAT-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Dunhuang was established as a frontier garrison outpost by the Han Dynasty <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Han" title="Emperor Wu of Han">Emperor Wudi</a> to protect against the <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a> in 111 BC. It also became an important gateway to the West, a centre of commerce along the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a>, as well as a meeting place of various people and religions such as <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>.</p>
<p>The construction of the Mogao Caves near Dunhuang is generally taken to have begun sometime in the fourth century AD. According to a book written during the reign of Tang <a href="/wiki/Wu_Zetian" title="Wu Zetian">Empress Wu</a>, <i>Fokan Ji</i> (佛龕記, <i>An Account of Buddhist Shrines</i>) by Li Junxiu (李君修), a <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist</a> monk named Lè Zūn (樂尊, which may also be pronounced Yuezun) had a vision of a thousand <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Buddhas</a> bathed in golden light at the site in 366 AD, inspiring him to build a cave here.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The story is also found in other sources, such as in inscriptions on a <a href="/wiki/Stele" title="Stele">stele</a> in cave 332; an earlier date of 353 however was given in another document, <i>Shazhou Tujing</i> (沙州土鏡, Geography of Shazhou).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He was later joined by a second monk Faliang (法良), and the site gradually grew, by the time of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Liang" title="Northern Liang">Northern Liang</a> a small community of monks had formed at the site. Members of the ruling family of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Wei" title="Northern Wei">Northern Wei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Zhou" title="Northern Zhou">Northern Zhou</a> constructed many caves here, and it flourished in the short-lived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sui_Dynasty" title="Sui Dynasty">Sui Dynasty</a>. By the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" title="Tang Dynasty">Tang Dynasty</a>, the number of caves had reached over a thousand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The caves initially served only as a place of meditation for hermit monks, but developed to serve the monasteries that sprang up nearby in the early periods, and by the Sui and Tang dynasties, Mogao Caves had become a place of worship and pilgrimage for the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> From the 4th until the 14th century, caves were constructed by monks to serve as shrines with funds from donors. These caves were elaborately painted, the cave paintings and architecture serving as aids to <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">meditation</a>, as visual representations of the quest for enlightenment, as <a href="/wiki/Mnemonic" title="Mnemonic">mnemonic</a> devices, and as teaching tools to inform those illiterate about Buddhist beliefs and stories. The major caves were sponsored by patrons such as important clergy, local ruling elite, foreign dignitaries, as well as Chinese emperors. Other caves may have been funded by merchants, military officers, and other local people such as women's groups.</p>
<p>During the Tang Dynasty, Dunhuang became the main hub of commerce of the Silk Road and a major religious centre. A large number of the caves were constructed at Mogao during this era, including the two large statues of Buddha at the site, the largest one constructed in 695 following an edict a year earlier by Tang Empress <a href="/wiki/Wu_Zetian" title="Wu Zetian">Wu Zetian</a> to build giant statues across the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-duanwenjie_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-duanwenjie-8">[8]</a></sup> The site escaped the <a href="/wiki/Great_Anti-Buddhist_Persecution" title="Great Anti-Buddhist Persecution">persecution of Buddhists</a> ordered by <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wuzong_of_Tang" title="Emperor Wuzong of Tang">Emperor Wuzong</a> in 845 as it was then under <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Empire" title="Tibetan Empire">Tibetan</a> control. As a frontier town, Dunhuang had been occupied at various times by other non-Han Chinese people. After the Tang Dynasty, the site went into a gradual decline, and construction of new caves ceased entirely after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuan_Dynasty" title="Yuan Dynasty">Yuan Dynasty</a>. By then Islam had conquered much of Central Asia, and the Silk Road declined in importance when trading via sea-routes began to dominate Chinese trade with the outside world. During the Ming Dynasty, the Silk Road was finally officially abandoned, and Dunhuang slowly became depopulated and largely forgotten by the outside world. Most of the Mogao caves were abandoned; the site, however, was still a place of pilgrimage and was used as a place of worship by local people at the beginning of the twentieth century when there was renewed interest in the site.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_revival">Discovery and revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogao_Caves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery and revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>During late nineteenth century and early twentieth century, Western explorers began to show interest in the ancient Silk Road and the lost cities of Central Asia, and those who passed through Dunhuang noted the murals, sculptures, and artifacts such as the <a href="/wiki/Stele_of_Sulaiman" title="Stele of Sulaiman">Stele of Sulaiman</a> at Mogao. The biggest discovery, however, came from a Chinese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taoist" title="Taoist">Taoist</a> named <a href="/wiki/Wang_Yuanlu" title="Wang Yuanlu">Wang Yuanlu</a> who appointed himself guardian of some of these temples around the turn of the century.</p>
<p>Some of the caves had by then been blocked by sand, and Wang set about clearing away the sand and made an attempt at repairing the site. In one such cave, on 25 June 1900, Wang discovered a walled up area behind one side of a corridor leading to a main cave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duan_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duan-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-idp_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-idp-10">[10]</a></sup> Behind the wall was a small cave stuffed with an enormous <a href="/wiki/Dunhuang_manuscripts" title="Dunhuang manuscripts">hoard of manuscripts</a>. In the next few years, Wang took some manuscripts to show to various officials who expressed varying level of interest, but in 1904 Wang re-sealed the cave following an order by the governor of Gansu.</p>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wang_Yuanlu.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Abbot <a href="/wiki/Wang_Yuanlu" title="Wang Yuanlu">Wang Yuanlu</a>, discoverer of the hidden Library Cave</div>

<p>Words of Wang's discovery drew the attention of a joint British/Indian group led by Hungarian archaeologist <a href="/wiki/Aurel_Stein" title="Aurel Stein">Aurel Stein</a> who was on an archaeological expedition in the area in 1907.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Stein negotiated with Wang to allow him to remove a significant number of manuscripts as well as the finest paintings and textiles for a fee. He was followed by a French expedition under <a href="/wiki/Paul_Pelliot" title="Paul Pelliot">Paul Pelliot</a> who acquired many thousands of items in 1908, and then by a Japanese expedition under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otani_Kozui" title="Otani Kozui">Otani Kozui</a> in 1911 and a Russian expedition under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergei_F._Oldenburg" title="Sergei F. Oldenburg">Sergei F. Oldenburg</a> in 1914. A well-known scholar <a href="/wiki/Luo_Zhenyu" title="Luo Zhenyu">Luo Zhenyu</a> edited some of the manuscripts Pelliot acquired into a volume which was then published in 1909 as "Manuscripts of the Dunhuang Caves" (敦煌石室遺書).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stein and Pelliot provoked much interest in the West about the Dunhuang Caves; however, there was initially little interest in official circles in China. Concerned that the remaining manuscripts might be lost, Luo Zhenyu and others persuaded the Ministry of Education to recover the rest of the manuscripts to be sent to Peking (<a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>) in 1910. However, not all the remaining manuscripts were taken to Peking, and of those retrieved, some were then stolen. Some of the caves were <a href="/wiki/Russians_in_China#Vandalism_of_Mogao_Caves" title="Russians in China">damaged and vandalized by White Russian soldiers</a> when they were used by the local authority in 1921 to house Russian soldiers fleeing the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Civil_War" title="Russian Civil War">civil war</a> following the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution" title="Russian Revolution">Russian Revolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E6.9D.A8.E7.A7.80.E6.B8.852006_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E6.9D.A8.E7.A7.80.E6.B8.852006-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1924, American explorer <a href="/wiki/Langdon_Warner" title="Langdon Warner">Langdon Warner</a> removed a number of murals as well as a statue from some of the caves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1939 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kuomingtang" title="Kuomingtang">Kuomingtang</a> soldiers stationed at Dunhuang caused some damage to the murals and statues at the site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The situation improved in 1941 when, following a visit by <a href="/wiki/Wu_Zuoren" title="Wu Zuoren">Wu Zuoren</a> to the site the previous year, the painter <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Daqian" title="Zhang Daqian">Zhang Daqian</a> arrived at the caves with a small team of assistants and stayed for two and a half years to repair and copy the murals. He exhibited and published the copies of the murals in 1943, which helped to publicize and give much prominence to the art of Dunhuang within China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Historian Xiang Da then persuaded <a href="/wiki/Yu_Youren" title="Yu Youren">Yu Youren</a>, a prominent member of the <a href="/wiki/Kuomintang" title="Kuomintang">Kuomintang</a> (Chinese Nationalist Party), to set up an institution, the Research Institute of Dunhuang Art (which later became the Dunhuang Academy), at Mogao in 1944 to look after the site and its contents. In 1956, the first <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Premier of the People's Republic of China">Premier of the People's Republic of China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zhou_Enlai" title="Zhou Enlai">Zhou Enlai</a>, took a personal interest in the caves and sanctioned a grant to repair and protect the site; and in 1961, the Mogao Caves were declared to be a specially protected historical monument by the <a href="/wiki/State_Council_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="State Council of the People's Republic of China">State Council</a>, and large-scale renovation work at Mogao began soon afterwards. The site escaped the widespread damage caused to many religious sites during the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Revolution" title="Cultural Revolution">Cultural Revolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Today, the site is the subject of an ongoing archaeological project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Mogao Caves became one of the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Sites" title="World Heritage Sites">World Heritage Sites</a> in 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unesco_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-unesco-1">[1]</a></sup> From 1988 to 1995 a further 248 caves were discovered to the North of the 487 caves known since the early 1900s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Library_Cave">The Library Cave</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogao_Caves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Library Cave">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dunhuang_Cave_16.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="386" data-file-width="550" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Dunhuang_Cave_16.jpg/280px-Dunhuang_Cave_16.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Dunhuang_Cave_16.jpg/420px-Dunhuang_Cave_16.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/19/Dunhuang_Cave_16.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dunhuang_Cave_16.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Picture of Cave 16, by Aurel Stein in 1907, with manuscripts piled up beside the entrance to Cave 17, the Library Cave, which is to the right in this picture.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The cave no. 17 discovered by <a href="/wiki/Wang_Yuanlu" title="Wang Yuanlu">Wang Yuanlu</a> came to be known as the Library Cave. It is sited off the entrance leading to cave no.16, and was originally used as a memorial cave for a local monk Hongbian on his death in 862. Hongbian, from a wealthy Wu family, was responsible for the construction of cave 16, and the Library Cave may have been used as his retreat in his lifetime. The cave originally contained his statue which was moved to another cave when it was used to keep manuscripts, some of which bear Hongbian's seal. Large number of documents dating from 406 to 1002 were found in the cave, heaped up in closely packed layers of bundles of scrolls. In addition to the 1,100 of bundles of scrolls, there were also over 15,000 paper books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The Library Cave also contained textiles such as banners, numerous damaged figurines of Buddhas, and other Buddhist paraphernalia. According to Stein who was the first to describe the cave in its original state:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Heaped up in layers, but without any order, there appeared in the dim light of the priest's little lamp a solid mass of manuscript bundles rising to a height of nearly ten feet, and filling, as subsequent measurement showed, close on 500 cubic feet. The area left clear within the room was just sufficient for two people to stand in.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Aurel Stein, <i>Ruins of Desert Cathay: Vol. II</i></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The Library Cave was walled off sometime early in the 11th century. A number of theories have been proposed as the reason for sealing the caves. Stein first proposed that the cave had become a waste repository for venerable, damaged and used manuscripts and hallowed paraphernalia and then sealed perhaps when the place came under threat. Following this interpretation some suggested that the handwritten manuscripts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tripitaka" title="Tripitaka">Tripitaka</a> became obsolete when printing became widespread, the older manuscripts were therefore stored away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Another suggestion is that the cave was simply used as a book storehouse for documents which accumulated over a century and a half, then sealed up when it became full.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Others, such as Pelliot, suggested an alternative scenario, that the monks hurriedly hid the documents in advance of an attack by invaders, perhaps when <a href="/wiki/Western_Xia" title="Western Xia">Xi Xia</a> invaded in 1035. This theory was proposed in light of the absence of documents from Xi Xia and the disordered state in which Pelliot found the room (perhaps a misinterpretation because unbeknownst to him the room was disturbed by Stein months before). Another theory posits that the items were from a monastic library and hidden due to threats from Muslims who were moving eastward. This theory proposes that the monks of a nearby monastery heard about the fall of the Buddhist <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Khotan" title="Kingdom of Khotan">kingdom of Khotan</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kara-Khanid_Khanate" title="Kara-Khanid Khanate">Karakhanids</a> invaders from <a href="/wiki/Kashgar" title="Kashgar">Kashgar</a> in 1006 and the destruction it caused, so they sealed their library to avoid it being destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The latest date recorded in the documents found in the cave is generally accepted to be 1002, and although other dates have been suggested, the cave was likely to have been sealed not long after that date.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dunhuang_manuscripts">Dunhuang manuscripts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogao_Caves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dunhuang manuscripts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dunhuang_manuscripts" title="Dunhuang manuscripts">Dunhuang manuscripts</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pelliot_cave163.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="570" data-file-width="416" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Pelliot_cave163.jpg/200px-Pelliot_cave163.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Pelliot_cave163.jpg/300px-Pelliot_cave163.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Pelliot_cave163.jpg/400px-Pelliot_cave163.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pelliot_cave163.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Paul_Pelliot" title="Paul Pelliot">Paul Pelliot</a> examining manuscripts in the Library Cave, 1908</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The manuscripts from the Library Cave date from fifth century until early eleventh century when it was sealed. Up to 50,000 manuscripts may have been kept there, one of the greatest treasure troves of ancient documents found. While most of them are in Chinese, a large number of documents are in various other languages such as <a href="/wiki/Tibetic_languages" title="Tibetic languages">Tibetan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Uyghur_alphabet" title="Old Uyghur alphabet">Uigur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sogdian_language" title="Sogdian language">Sogdian</a>, including the then little-known <a href="/wiki/Saka_language" title="Saka language">Khotanese</a>. They may be old hemp paper scrolls in Chinese and many other languages, Tibetan pothis, and paintings on hemp, silk or paper. The subject matter of the great majority of the scrolls is Buddhist in nature, but it also covers a diverse material. Along with the expected Buddhist canonical works are original commentaries, <a href="/wiki/Apocrypha" title="Apocrypha">apocryphal</a> works, workbooks, books of prayers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confucian" title="Confucian">Confucian</a> works, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taoist" title="Taoist">Taoist</a> works, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nestorian_Christian" title="Nestorian Christian">Nestorian Christian</a> works, works from the Chinese government, administrative documents, anthologies, glossaries, dictionaries, and calligraphic exercises.</p>
<p>Many of the manuscripts were previously unknown or thought lost, and the manuscripts provide a unique insight into the religious and secular matters of Northern China as well as other Central Asian kingdoms from the early periods up to the Tang and early Song Dynasty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The manuscripts found in the Library Cave include the earliest dated printed book, the <a href="/wiki/Diamond_Sutra" title="Diamond Sutra">Diamond Sutra</a> from 868, which was first translated from Sanskrit into Chinese in the fourth century. These scrolls also include manuscripts that ranged from the Nestorian <a href="/wiki/Jesus_Sutras" title="Jesus Sutras">Jesus Sutras</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Dunhuang_Go_Manual" title="Dunhuang Go Manual">Dunhuang Go Manual</a> and ancient music scores, as well as the image of the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_astronomy" title="Chinese astronomy">Chinese astronomy</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dunhuang_map" title="Dunhuang map">Dunhuang map</a>. These scrolls chronicle the development of Buddhism in China, record the political and cultural life of the time, and provide documentation of mundane secular matters that gives a rare glimpse into the lives of ordinary people of these eras.</p>
<p>The manuscripts were dispersed all over the world in the aftermath of the discovery. Stein's acquisition was split between Britain and India because his expedition was funded by both countries. Stein had the first pick and he was able to collect around 7,000 complete manuscripts and 6,000 fragments for which he paid £130, although these include many duplicate copies of the Diamond and <a href="/wiki/Lotus_Sutra" title="Lotus Sutra">Lotus Sutras</a>. Pelliot took almost 10,000 documents for the equivalent of £90, but, unlike Stein, Pelliot was a trained <a href="/wiki/Sinology" title="Sinology">sinologist</a> literate in Chinese, and he was allowed to examine the manuscripts freely, so he was able to pick a better selection of documents than Stein. Pelliot was interested in the more unusual and exotic of the Dunhuang manuscripts, such as those dealing with the administration and financing of the monastery and associated lay men's groups. Many of these manuscripts survived only because they formed a type of <a href="/wiki/Palimpsest" title="Palimpsest">palimpsest</a> whereby papers were reused and Buddhist texts were written on the opposite side of the <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>. Hundreds more of the manuscripts were sold by Wang to Otani Kozui and Sergei Oldenburg. Efforts are now underway to reconstitute the Library Cave manuscripts digitally, and they are now available as part of <a href="/wiki/International_Dunhuang_Project" title="International Dunhuang Project">International Dunhuang Project</a>.</p>

<p>The art of Dunhuang covers more than ten major genres, such as <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stucco" title="Stucco">stucco</a> sculpture, <a href="/wiki/Mural" title="Mural">wall paintings</a>, silk paintings, calligraphy, woodblock printing, embroidery, literature, music and dance, and popular entertainment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogao_Caves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The caves are examples of <a href="/wiki/Rock-cut_architecture" title="Rock-cut architecture">rock-cut architecture</a>, but unlike <a href="/wiki/Longmen_Grottoes" title="Longmen Grottoes">Longmen Grottoes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yungang_Grottoes" title="Yungang Grottoes">Yungang Grottoes</a>, the local rock is a rather soft <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a> conglomerate that is not suitable for either sculpture or elaborate architectural details.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Many of the early caves were developed from earlier Buddhist rock-cut <i><a href="/wiki/Chaitya" title="Chaitya">chaitya</a></i> styles seen in places such as the <a href="/wiki/Ajanta_Caves" title="Ajanta Caves">Ajanta Caves</a> in India, with a square-sectioned central column, with sculpture in niches, representing the <a href="/wiki/Stupa" title="Stupa">stupa</a> round which worshippers may <a href="/wiki/Circumambulation" title="Circumambulation">circumambulate</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Parikrama" title="Parikrama">parikrama</a></i>) and gain blessings. Others are hall caves influenced by traditional Chinese and Buddhist temple architecture. These caves may have a truncated pyramidal ceiling sometimes painted to resemble a tent, or they may have a flat or gabled ceiling that imitates traditional buildings. Some of the caves used for meditation are adaptations of the Indian <a href="/wiki/Vihara" title="Vihara">vihara</a> (monastery) cave plan and contain side-chambers just large enough for one person to sit in.</p>
<p>Many of the caves originally had wooden porches or fore-temples built out from the cliff, but most of these have decayed or been lost in other ways, with only five remaining, the two earliest of which are rare surviving examples of Song dynasty wooden architecture. The most prominent wooden building at the site, first built during the Tang dynasty, housed a large Buddha and was originally 4 storeys high, but it has been repaired at least 5 times and is no longer the original structure. A storey was added between 874-885, then repaired in the <a href="/wiki/Guiyi_Circuit" title="Guiyi Circuit">Guiyijun</a> period, and two further storeys were added during a restoration in 1898. Two further restorations were carried out in the 20th century, and the building is now a 9-storey structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fan_jinshi1_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-fan_jinshi1-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Murals">Murals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogao_Caves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Murals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dunhuang_General_Zhang_Yichao_army_detail.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="729" data-file-width="544" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Dunhuang_General_Zhang_Yichao_army_detail.jpg/220px-Dunhuang_General_Zhang_Yichao_army_detail.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Dunhuang_General_Zhang_Yichao_army_detail.jpg/330px-Dunhuang_General_Zhang_Yichao_army_detail.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Dunhuang_General_Zhang_Yichao_army_detail.jpg/440px-Dunhuang_General_Zhang_Yichao_army_detail.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The murals in the caves date from a period of over a thousand years, from the 5th to the 14th century, and many earlier ones were repainted at later points within the period. The murals are extensive, covering an area of 490,000 square feet (46,000 square metres). The most fully painted caves have paintings all over the walls and ceilings, with geometrical or plant decoration filling the spaces not taken by figurative images, which are above all of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddha" title="Buddha">Buddha</a>. Sculpture is also brightly painted. The murals are valued for the scale and richness of content as well as their artistry. Buddhist subjects are most common, however some have traditional mythical subjects and <a href="/wiki/Donor_portrait" title="Donor portrait">portraits of patrons</a>. These murals document the changing styles of Buddhist art in China for nearly a thousand years. The artistry of the murals reached its apogee during the Tang period, and the quality of the work dropped after the tenth century.</p>
<p>Early murals showed a strong Indian and Central Asian influence in the painting techniques used, the composition and style of the paintings as well as costumes worn by the figures, but a distinct Dunhuang style began to emerge during Northern Wei Dynasty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-press_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-press-29">[29]</a></sup> Motifs of Chinese, Central Asian and Indian origin may be found in a single cave, and Chinese elements increased during the Western Wei period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-press_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-press-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>A common motif in many caves is the areas entirely covered by rows of small seated Buddha figures, after which this and other "Thousand Buddhas Caves" are named. These small Buddhas were drawn using <a href="/wiki/Stencil" title="Stencil">stencils</a> so that identical figures may be replicated. Flying <a href="/wiki/Apsara" title="Apsara">apsaras</a>, or celestial beings may be depicted in the ceiling or above the Buddhas, and figures of donors may be shown along the bottom of the walls. The paintings often depict <a href="/wiki/Jataka_tales" title="Jataka tales">jataka tales</a> which are stories of the life of Buddha, or <a href="/wiki/Avadana" title="Avadana">avadana</a> which are parables of the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Karma" title="Karma">karma</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dunhuang_Mogao_mural_repainted.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1016" data-file-width="750" height="298" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Dunhuang_Mogao_mural_repainted.jpg/220px-Dunhuang_Mogao_mural_repainted.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Dunhuang_Mogao_mural_repainted.jpg/330px-Dunhuang_Mogao_mural_repainted.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Dunhuang_Mogao_mural_repainted.jpg/440px-Dunhuang_Mogao_mural_repainted.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bodhisattvas" title="Bodhisattvas">Bodhisattvas</a> started appearing during the Northern Zhou period, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avalokitesvara" title="Avalokitesvara">Avalokitesvara</a> (<a href="/wiki/Guanyin" title="Guanyin">Guanyin</a>), which was originally male but acquired female characteristics later, the most popular. Most caves show <a href="/wiki/Mahayana" title="Mahayana">Mahayana</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sravakayana" title="Sravakayana">Sravakayana</a> (<a href="/wiki/Theravada" title="Theravada">Theravada</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hinayana" title="Hinayana">Hinayana</a>) influences, although Mahayana Buddhism became the dominant form during the Sui Dynasty. An innovation of the Sui-Tang period is the visual representation of the sutra – Mahayana Buddhist teachings transformed into large complete and detailed narrative paintings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fanjinshi_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-fanjinshi-30">[30]</a></sup> One of the central features of Tang art in Mogao is the representation of the paradise of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pure_Land" title="Pure Land">Pure Land</a>, indicating the increasing popularity of this school of Mahayana Buddhism in the Tang era. The iconography of <a href="/wiki/Vajrayana" title="Vajrayana">Tantric Buddhism</a>, such as the eleven-headed or thousand-armed Avalokitesvara, also started to appear in Mogao wall paintings during the Tang period – it became popular during the Tibetan occupation of Dunhuang and the subsequent periods, especially during the <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-duanwenjie_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-duanwenjie-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dunhuang_figures_showing_shading_technique.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="742" data-file-width="1050" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Dunhuang_figures_showing_shading_technique.jpg/220px-Dunhuang_figures_showing_shading_technique.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Dunhuang_figures_showing_shading_technique.jpg/330px-Dunhuang_figures_showing_shading_technique.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Dunhuang_figures_showing_shading_technique.jpg/440px-Dunhuang_figures_showing_shading_technique.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dunhuang_figures_showing_shading_technique.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Figures showing shading technique, and on the left, the effect of darkening of the paint used in shading which changed the appearance of the figure.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>While Buddhist art is stylistically distinct from secular art, the style of paintings in the caves often reflects that of contemporary secular painting (insofar as we know of this), especially those depicting secular scenes. Donor figures are generally depicted in secular style, and may include secular events associated with them. For example, scenes depicting General <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Yichao" title="Zhang Yichao">Zhang Yichao</a>, who ruled over Dunhuang in a quasi-autonomous manner during the Late Tang period, include a commemoration of his victory over the Tibetans in 848. The portraits of donors increased in size during the period ruled by the Cao family who succeeded the Zhang family. The Caos formed alliances with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uyghur_people" title="Uyghur people">Uyghurs</a> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Uyghur_Gansu_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Uyghur Gansu Kingdom (page does not exist)">Uyghur Gansu Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Qocho" title="Kingdom of Qocho">Kingdom of Qocho</a>) and the Saka <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Khotan" title="Kingdom of Khotan">Kingdom of Khotan</a> and their portraits are featured prominently in some of the caves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dra_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-dra-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many of the figures have darkened due to oxidation of the lead-based pigments from exposure to air and light. Many early figures in the murals in Dunhuang also used painting techniques originated from India where shading was applied to achieve a 3-dimensional or <a href="/wiki/Chiaroscuro" title="Chiaroscuro">chiaroscuro</a> effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dra_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-dra-31">[31]</a></sup> However, the darkening of the paint used in shading over time resulted in heavy outlines which is not what the painters had originally intended. This shading technique is unique to Dunhuang in East Asia at this period as such shading on human faces was generally not done in Chinese paintings until much later when there were influences from European paintings. Another difference from traditional Chinese painting is the presence of figures that are semi-nude, occasionally fully nude, as figures are generally fully clothed in Chinese paintings. Many of the murals have been repaired or plastered over and repainted over the centuries, and older murals may be seen where sections of later paintings had been removed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sculptures">Sculptures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogao_Caves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sculptures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Great_Buddha,_Cave_96,_Mogao_Caves.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1360" data-file-width="2048" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Great_Buddha%2C_Cave_96%2C_Mogao_Caves.jpg/220px-Great_Buddha%2C_Cave_96%2C_Mogao_Caves.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Great_Buddha%2C_Cave_96%2C_Mogao_Caves.jpg/330px-Great_Buddha%2C_Cave_96%2C_Mogao_Caves.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Great_Buddha%2C_Cave_96%2C_Mogao_Caves.jpg/440px-Great_Buddha%2C_Cave_96%2C_Mogao_Caves.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Great_Buddha,_Cave_96,_Mogao_Caves.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Great Buddha of cave 96</div>

<p>There are around 2,400 surviving clay sculptures at Mogao. These were first constructed on a wooden frame, padded with reed, then modelled in clay stucco, and finished with paint. The giant statues however have a stone core. The <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Buddha</a> is generally shown as the central statue, often attended by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boddhisattva" title="Boddhisattva">boddhisattvas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Four_Heavenly_Kings" title="Four Heavenly Kings">heavenly kings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deva_(Buddhism)" title="Deva (Buddhism)">devas</a>, and apsaras, along with <a href="/wiki/Yaksha" title="Yaksha">yaksas</a> and other mythical creatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fanjinshi_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-fanjinshi-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grotta_nr_275.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Grotta_nr_275.JPG/250px-Grotta_nr_275.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Grotta_nr_275.JPG/375px-Grotta_nr_275.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Grotta_nr_275.JPG/500px-Grotta_nr_275.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grotta_nr_275.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Figure of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maitreya_Buddha" title="Maitreya Buddha">Maitreya Buddha</a> in cave 275 from <a href="/wiki/Northern_Liang" title="Northern Liang">Northern Liang</a> (397-439), one of the earliest caves. The crossed ankle figure with a three-disk crown shows influence from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kushan" title="Kushan">Kushan</a> art.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The early figures are relatively simple and mainly of Buddhas and Bodhisattvas. The Buddhas of Northern Wei may have two attendant Bodhisattvas, and two further disciples were added in Northern Zhou, forming a group of five.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Figures from the Sui and Tang periods may be present as larger groups of seven or nine, and some showed large-scale <a href="/wiki/Parinirvana" title="Parinirvana">parinirvana</a> scene with groups of mourners. The early sculptures were based on Indian and Central Asian prototypes, with some in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Indian" title="Greco-Indian">Greco-Indian</a> style of <a href="/wiki/Gandhara" title="Gandhara">Gandhara</a>. Over time the sculptures showed more Chinese elements and became gradually sinicized.</p>
<p>The two giant statues represent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maitreya_Buddha" title="Maitreya Buddha">Maitreya Buddha</a>. The earlier and larger one in cave 96, at 35.5 m high, was constructed in 695 under the edicts from Empress Wu Zetian who instructed the constructions of monasteries in 689 and giant statues in 694. The smaller one is 27 m tall and was constructed in 713-41.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The larger northern giant Buddha was damaged in an earthquake and had been repaired and restored multiple times, consequently its clothing, colour and gestures had been changed and only the head retains its original Early Tang appearance. The southern statue however is largely in its original form apart for its right hand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The larger Buddha is housed in a prominent wooden 9-storey structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fan_jinshi1_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-fan_jinshi1-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BeijingNationalArtMuseumofChina7.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Reproduction of the reclining Buddha of the Tibetan period from cave 158. <a href="/wiki/National_Art_Museum_of_China" title="National Art Museum of China">National Art Museum of China</a>, Beijing.</div>

<p>One type of caves constructed during the Tibetan era is the Nirvana Cave, which features a large reclining Buddha that covers the entire length of the hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Figures of mourners in murals or in sculptural forms are also depicted along the length of the hall behind the Buddha. The Buddha figure in cave 158 measures 15.6 m long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The original function of the "Library Cave" was as a shrine commemorating Hong Bian, the 9th-century monk. His portrait statue, unusual here and among all surviving works in China, was removed to another spot when the cave was sealed up in the 11th century, but has been returned now the library has been removed. There is also a stone <a href="/wiki/Stele" title="Stele">stele</a> describing his life, and the wall behind the statue is painted with attendant figure; such blending of painted sculpture and wall paintings into a single composition is very common at the site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paintings_on_silk_and_paper">Paintings on silk and paper</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogao_Caves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Paintings on silk and paper">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before the discovery in the Library Cave, original paintings from the Tang dynasty, a formative period in Chinese art, were very rare, and most of the surviving evidence was from copies made in later periods. Over a thousand paintings on silk, banners, and embroideries were found in the Library Cave, none apparently dating before the late 7th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The great majority of the paintings are anonymous, but many are of high quality, especially from the Tang. Most are sutra paintings, images of Buddha, and narrative paintings. The paintings show something of the contemporary Chinese style of the capital <a href="/wiki/Chang%27an" title="Chang'an">Chang'an</a>, but many also reflect Indian, Tibetan and Uighur painting styles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are brush paintings in ink alone, some in just two colours, as well as many in full colour. Most common are single figures, and most paintings were probably donated by an individual, who is often portrayed on a diminutive scale. The donor figures become notably more elaborate in dress by the 10th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Library Cave is equally important as a source of rare early images and texts produced by <a href="/wiki/Woodblock_printing" title="Woodblock printing">woodblock printing</a>, including the famous <a href="/wiki/Diamond_Sutra" title="Diamond Sutra">Diamond Sutra</a>, the earliest printed book to survive. Other printed images were made to be hung, often with text below containing prayers and sometimes a dedication by the pious commissioner; at least two prints were commissioned by Cao Yuanzhong, Imperial Commissioner at Dunhuang in 947. Many of the images have colour added by hand to the printed outline. Several sheets contain repeated impressions of the same block with a Buddha image. Possibly these reflect stock for cutting when sold to pilgrims, but inscriptions in some examples show these were also printed out at different times by an individual as a devotion to acquire merit. It is unclear whether such people owned their own blocks, or visited a monastery to have the images printed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Textiles">Textiles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogao_Caves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Textiles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The textiles found in the Library Cave include silk banners, altar hangings, wrappings for manuscripts, and monks' apparel (<a href="/wiki/Kasaya_(clothing)" title="Kasaya (clothing)">kāṣāya</a>). The monks normally used fabrics consisting of a patchwork of different scraps of cloth as a sign of humility; these therefore provide valuable insights into the various type of silk cloth and embroidery available at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Silk banners were used to adorn the cliff-face at the caves during festivals, and these are painted and may be embroidered. <a href="/wiki/Window_valance" title="Window valance">Valances</a> used to decorate altars and temples had a horizontal strip at the top, from which hung streamers made from strips of different cloths ending in a V that look like a modern male necktie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Caves">Caves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogao_Caves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Caves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dunhuang_mural_flying_apsarasa.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="616" data-file-width="606" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Dunhuang_mural_flying_apsarasa.jpg/220px-Dunhuang_mural_flying_apsarasa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Dunhuang_mural_flying_apsarasa.jpg/330px-Dunhuang_mural_flying_apsarasa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Dunhuang_mural_flying_apsarasa.jpg/440px-Dunhuang_mural_flying_apsarasa.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dunhuang_mural_flying_apsarasa.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Flying apsaras, or celestial beings. Caves 285, 538-539 AD, Western Wei Dynasty</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The caves were cut into the side of a cliff which is close to two kilometers long. At its height, during the Tang Dynasty, there were more than a thousands caves, but over time many of the caves were lost, including the earliest caves. 735 caves currently exist in Mogao; the best-known ones are the 487 caves located in the southern section of the cliff which are places of pilgrimage and worship. 248 caves have also been found to the north which were living quarters, meditation chambers, and burial sites for the monks. The caves at the southern section are decorated, while those at the northern section are mostly plain.</p>
<p>The caves are clustered together according to their era, with new caves from a new dynasty being constructed in different part of the cliff. From the murals, sculptures, and other objects found in the caves, the dates of around five hundred caves have been determined. Following is a list of the caves by era, compiled in the 1980s (more have been identified since):</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sixteen_Kingdoms" title="Sixteen Kingdoms">Sixteen Kingdoms</a> (366-439) - 7 caves, the oldest dated to <a href="/wiki/Northern_Liang" title="Northern Liang">Northern Liang</a> period.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Northern_Wei" title="Northern Wei">Northern Wei</a> (439-534) and <a href="/wiki/Western_Wei" title="Western Wei">Western Wei</a> (535-556) - 10 from each phase</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Northern_Zhou" title="Northern Zhou">Northern Zhou</a> (557-580) - 15 caves</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sui_Dynasty" title="Sui Dynasty">Sui Dynasty</a> (581-618) - 70 caves</li>
<li>Early Tang (618- 704) - 44 caves</li>
<li>High Tang (705-780) - 80 caves</li>
<li>Middle Tang (781-847) - 44 caves (This era in Dunhuang is also known as the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Empire" title="Tibetan Empire">Tibetan</a> period because Dunhuang was then under Tibetan occupation.)</li>
<li>Late Tang (848-906) - 60 caves (This and the subsequent periods until the Western Xia period are also known collectively as the <a href="/wiki/Guiyi_Circuit" title="Guiyi Circuit">Guiyijun</a> period (歸義軍, Return to Righteousness Army, 848-1036) when Dunhuang was ruled by the Zhang and Cao families.)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Five_Dynasties_and_Ten_Kingdoms_period" title="Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period">The Five Dynasty</a> (907-960) - 32 caves</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a> (960-1035)- 43 caves</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Western_Xia" title="Western Xia">Western Xia</a> (1036–1226) - 82 caves</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuan_Dynasty" title="Yuan Dynasty">Yuan Dynasty</a> (1227–1368) - 10 caves</li>
</ul>
