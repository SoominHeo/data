<p>An <b>ice pop</b> is a water-based frozen snack. It is also referred to as a <b>popsicle</b> (<a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>), <b>freezer pop</b> (<a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>, U.S.), <b>ice lolly</b> (<a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>), <b>ice block</b> (<a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>), <b>icy pole</b> (parts of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>), or <b>chihiro</b> (<a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miller_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-miller-1">[1]</a></sup> It is made by freezing flavored liquid (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fruit_juice" title="Fruit juice">fruit juice</a>) around a stick, generally resembling a <a href="/wiki/Tongue_depressor" title="Tongue depressor">tongue depressor</a>. Often, the juice is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_color" title="Artificial color">colored artificially</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Once the liquid freezes solid, the stick can be used as a handle to hold the ice pop. When an ice pop does not have a stick, it is called, among other names, a <b><a href="/wiki/Freezie" title="Freezie">freezie</a></b>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ice_pop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frank_Epperson" title="Frank Epperson">Frank Epperson</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oakland" title="Oakland">Oakland</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, popularized ice pops after patenting the concept of "frozen ice on a stick" in 1923.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marks-2012_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-marks-2012-3">[3]</a></sup> He initially called it the Epsicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A couple of years later, Epperson sold the rights to the invention and the <a href="/wiki/Popsicle_(brand)" title="Popsicle (brand)">Popsicle</a> brand to the Joe Lowe Company in New York City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marks-2012_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-marks-2012-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Epperson claimed to have first created an ice pop in 1905 at the age of 11 when he accidentally left a glass of powdered soda and water with a mixing stick in it on his porch during a cold night, a story still printed on the back panel of Popsicle brand treat boxes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ice_pop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:215px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Popsicle_Stick_Castle_made_with_296,000_popsicle_sticks.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="159" data-file-width="213" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4d/Popsicle_Stick_Castle_made_with_296%2C000_popsicle_sticks.jpg" width="213"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Popsicle_Stick_Castle_made_with_296,000_popsicle_sticks.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Popsicle Stick Castle made with 296,000 popsicle sticks</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the United States and Canada frozen ice on a stick is generically referred to as a <i>popsicle</i> due to the early popularity of the <a href="/wiki/Popsicle_(brand)" title="Popsicle (brand)">Popsicle</a> brand, and the word has become a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genericized_trademark" title="Genericized trademark">genericized trademark</a> to mean any ice pop or freezer pop, regardless of brand or format. (The word is a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of <i>pop</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Icicle" title="Icicle">icicle</a></i>.) They are also called an <i>ice pop</i> or <i>freezer pop</i> in the United States. In the Caicos Islands it is referred to as an ice saver. In the United Kingdom and Ireland the terms <i>ice lolly</i> and <i>ice pop</i> are used. <i>Chihiro</i> is used as a slang term in the Cayman Islands, partially derived from <i>chill</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miller_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-miller-1">[1]</a></sup> <i>Ice block</i> or <i>icy pole</i> are used in parts of Australia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and New Zealand.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Homemade_ice_pops">Homemade ice pops</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ice_pop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Homemade ice pops">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An alternative to the store-bought ice pops is making them at home using fruit juice, <a href="/wiki/Drink_mix" title="Drink mix">drinks</a>, or any freezable beverage. A classic method involves using ice cube trays and toothpicks, although various ice pop freezer molds are also available.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_record_ice_pop">World record ice pop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ice_pop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: World record ice pop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On June 22, 2005, <a href="/wiki/Snapple" title="Snapple">Snapple</a> tried to beat the existing <i><a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a></i> entry of a 1997 Dutch 21-foot (6.4 m) ice pop by attempting to erect a 25-foot (7.6 m) ice pop in New York City. The 17.5 short tons (15.9 t) of frozen juice that had been brought from <a href="/wiki/Edison,_New_Jersey" title="Edison, New Jersey">Edison, New Jersey</a> in a freezer truck melted faster than expected, dashing hopes of a new record. Spectators fled to higher ground as firefighters hosed away the melted juice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
