<p><b>Makruk</b> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th" xml:lang="th">หมากรุก</span>; <a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription"><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">rtgs</span></a>: <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Thai transliteration"><b>Mak Ruk</b></span>;  <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Thai_and_Lao" title="Help:IPA for Thai and Lao">[màːk rúk]</a></span>), or <b>Thai chess</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Board_game" title="Board game">board game</a> descended from the 6th-century <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> game of <a href="/wiki/Chaturanga" title="Chaturanga">chaturanga</a> or a close relative thereof, and therefore related to <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a>. It is regarded as the most similar living game to this common ancestor of all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chess_variant" title="Chess variant">chess variants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are around two million <a href="/wiki/Thai_people" title="Thai people">Thais</a> who can play makruk, while 5000 can play chess.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to former world chess champion <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Kramnik" title="Vladimir Kramnik">Vladimir Kramnik</a>, "Makruk Thai is more strategic than international chess. You have to plan your operations with total care since Makruk Thai can be compared to an anticipated endgame of International Chess."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

A pawn that reaches the sixth rank is always promoted to a queen <i>(med)</i>.</li>

<li>The king (called ขุน <i>khun</i>, meaning either a feudal lord or a title-holder of the lowest ranks in the ancient Thai nobility) moves like a king in international chess - one step in any direction. The game ends when the king is checkmated.</li>
</ul>

<p>In starting position, pawns are placed on the third and sixth ranks. Queens are placed at the right side of kings. Pawns promote (เบี้ยหงาย <i>bia ngai</i>, flipped cowry shell) and move like queens when they reach the sixth rank. There is no castling rule like that of international chess.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Counting_rules">Counting rules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Makruk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Counting rules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When neither side has any pawns, the game must be completed within a certain number of moves or it is declared a draw. When a piece is captured the count starts again from scratch only if it is the last piece of one side in the game.</p>
<ul>
<li>When neither side has any pawns left, mate must be achieved in 64 moves. The disadvantaged player does the counting, and may at any time choose to stop counting. If the disadvantaged side checkmates the advantage side and did not stop counting, the game is declared a draw.</li>
</ul>
<p>When the last piece (that is not the king) of the disadvantaged side is captured, the count may be started, or restarted from the aforementioned counting, by the weaker side, and the stronger side now has a maximum number of moves based on the pieces left:</p>
<ul>
<li>If there are two rooks left: 8 moves</li>
<li>If there is one rook left: 16 moves</li>
<li>If there are no rooks left, but there are two bishops: 22 moves</li>
<li>If there are no rooks left, but there is one bishop: 44 moves</li>
<li>If there are no rooks or bishops left, but there are two knights: 32 moves</li>
<li>If there are no rooks or bishops left, but there is one knight: 64 moves</li>
<li>If there are no rooks, bishops, or knights, but queens: 64 moves</li>
</ul>
<p>The weaker side pronounces aloud the counting of his fleeing moves, starting from the number of pieces left on the board, including both kings. The stronger side has to checkmate his opponent's king before the maximum number is pronounced, otherwise the game is drawn. During this process, the count may restart if the counting side would like to stop and start counting again.</p>
<p>For example, if White has two rooks and a knight against a lone black king, he has three moves to checkmate his opponent (the given value of 8 minus the total number of pieces, 5). If Black captures a white rook, the count does not automatically restart, unless Black is willing to do so, at his own disadvantage. However, many players do not understand this and restart the counting while fleeing the king.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Makruk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Here are some alternative rules, which do not apply to the standard, formal game, or have been abandoned in professional play. They are called sutras. The first free moves are similar to those in Cambodian Ouk (see below).</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Sut Khun (King Sutra)</b> can be compared to the castling rule in <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">international chess</a>. The rule allows the player to move the King to a blank square on next row, like a Knight, so long as the king has never moved.</li>
<li><b>Sut Met (Queen Sutra)</b> is the most popular sutra in informal rules. It is a first free move that allows the player to move the Queen and the Pawn in front of the Queen at the same time. Two pieces are moved in this sutra. First, move the Pawn in front of the Queen forward; then move the Queen to the blank square the pawn has just vacated, so the Queen moves two squares.</li>
<li><b>Sut Ma (Knight Sutra)</b> is a first free move that allows the player to move a Knight and a Pawn a Knight's move from that Knight in the same turn. Two pieces are moved in this sutra. First, move the Pawn which is a Knight's move from the Knight forward; then move the Knight to the blank square the Pawn has just vacated.</li>
<li><b>Takhaeng Ruea (Boat Tilting, Boat Lean)</b> Turn one or both Boat pieces upside down. This changes the Boat to be a Bia Ngai (flipped cowry shell). This reduces the power of one or two Rooks.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cambodian_chess">Cambodian chess</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Makruk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cambodian chess">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ouk_-_Cambodia_Chess.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1840" data-file-width="2732" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Ouk_-_Cambodia_Chess.jpeg/220px-Ouk_-_Cambodia_Chess.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Ouk_-_Cambodia_Chess.jpeg/330px-Ouk_-_Cambodia_Chess.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Ouk_-_Cambodia_Chess.jpeg/440px-Ouk_-_Cambodia_Chess.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ouk_-_Cambodia_Chess.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cambodian men playing Ouk</div>
</div>

<p>The variety of chess played in <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, called "Ok" (<a href="/wiki/Khmer_language" title="Khmer language">Khmer</a>: <span lang="km" xml:lang="km">អុក</span>) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khmer_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Khmer-6">[6]</a></sup> or "Ouk Chatrang"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ouk_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ouk-7">[7]</a></sup> is virtually identical to Makruk, with a couple of minor differences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-8">[8]</a></sup> On the king's first move, a player has the option of moving the king like a knight, but only if not in check and only if no pieces have been captured. On the queen's first move, the player has the option of moving the queen two squares straight ahead—again, only if no pieces have been captured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ouk_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ouk-7">[7]</a></sup> There is evidence that Ouk Chatrang has been played in Cambodia since the twelfth century, as it is depicted in several reliefs in the Angkor temples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ka_Ok">Ka Ok</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Makruk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ka Ok">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the variant "Ka Ok" (aka "Kar Ouk"), the first player to put the other in check wins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khmer_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Khmer-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tournament">Tournament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Makruk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tournament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first Ouk Chatrang tournament was held in <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a> from April 3 to 4 of 2008, upon the completion of a standardized ruleset by the <a href="/wiki/National_Olympic_Committee_of_Cambodia" title="National Olympic Committee of Cambodia">Olympic Committee of Cambodia</a> and the Cambodian Chess Association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tournament_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-tournament-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
