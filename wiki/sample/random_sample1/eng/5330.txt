<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Max Skladanowsky</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Max Skladanowsky</b> (April 30, 1863 – November 30, 1939) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_people" title="German people">German</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inventor" title="Inventor">inventor</a> and early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filmmaker" title="Filmmaker">filmmaker</a>. Along with his brother Emil, he invented the <a href="/wiki/Bioscop" title="Bioscop">Bioscop</a>, an early <a href="/wiki/Movie_projector" title="Movie projector">movie projector</a> the Skladanowsky brothers used to display the first moving picture show to a paying audience on November 1, 1895, just before the December 28, 1895 public debut of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lumi%C3%A8re_Brothers" title="Lumière Brothers">Lumière Brothers</a>' technically superior <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cin%C3%A9matographe" title="Cinématographe">Cinématographe</a> in Paris. (The Lumiere brothers had previously held a private debut of the Cinematographe on March 22, 1895.)</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Skladanowsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bioscop.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1015" data-file-width="750" height="298" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Bioscop.jpg/220px-Bioscop.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Bioscop.jpg/330px-Bioscop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Bioscop.jpg/440px-Bioscop.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bioscop.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1895 poster for <a href="/wiki/Bioscop" title="Bioscop">Bioscop</a> screenings</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Pankow_(locality)" title="Pankow (locality)">Pankow</a> near <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Glazier" title="Glazier">glazier</a>, Max Skladanowsky was apprenticed as a <a href="/wiki/Photographer" title="Photographer">photographer</a> and glass painter, which led to an interest in <a href="/wiki/Magic_lantern" title="Magic lantern">magic lanterns</a>. In 1879, he began to tour Germany and Central Europe with his father Carl and elder brother Emil, giving dissolving magic lantern shows. In the early 1890s he built a film camera along with Emil, and in 1895 the brothers produced the Bioscop. The Bioscop, which was inspired by magic-lantern technology, used two loops of 54mm film, one frame being projected alternately from each. This made it possible for the Bioscop to project at 16 frames per second, a speed sufficient to create the illusion of movement.</p>
<p>A demonstration of the Bioscop in <a href="/wiki/Pankow" title="Pankow">Pankow</a>, Berlin in July 1895 was witnessed by the directors of the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Wintergarten_theatre" title="Berlin Wintergarten theatre">Wintergarten</a> <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">music hall</a> who contracted Skladanowsky for a sum of 2500 <a href="/wiki/German_gold_mark" title="German gold mark">Goldmark</a> to present his invention as the final act in a variety performance commencing on November 1, 1895. The show was advertised as "the most interesting invention of the modern age" and played to capacity crowds for around four weeks. The show itself consisted of a number of very short films of arounds six seconds each which were rear-projected and repeated a number of times to a specially composed musical accompaniment.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-R96755,_Max_und_Eugen_Skladanowsky.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="730" data-file-width="540" height="297" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-R96755%2C_Max_und_Eugen_Skladanowsky.jpg/220px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-R96755%2C_Max_und_Eugen_Skladanowsky.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-R96755%2C_Max_und_Eugen_Skladanowsky.jpg/330px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-R96755%2C_Max_und_Eugen_Skladanowsky.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-R96755%2C_Max_und_Eugen_Skladanowsky.jpg/440px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-R96755%2C_Max_und_Eugen_Skladanowsky.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-R96755,_Max_und_Eugen_Skladanowsky.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Max Skladanowsky (right) in 1934 with his brother Eugen and the Bioscop</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Skladanowsky's invention was booked to play the <a href="/wiki/Folies_Berg%C3%A8re" title="Folies Bergère">Folies Bergère</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> from January 1896, but after the Lumière Brothers unveiled their technically superior Cinématographe show in December 1895, his contract was cancelled. Skladanowsky witnessed a performance of the Cinématographe and continued to make technical improvements to his projector and camera, touring Germany, the Netherlands, and Scandinavia throughout 1896, presenting his last show in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stettin" title="Stettin">Stettin</a> on March 30, 1897. These later shows used a more sophisticated system with a single band of film and a <a href="/wiki/Geneva_drive" title="Geneva drive">geneva drive</a> mechanism, but Skladanowsky had to stop exhibiting as the authorities refused to renew his trade licence as "too many film licences were already in circulation"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>After this Skladanowsky returned to his former photographic activities including the production of <a href="/wiki/Flip_book" title="Flip book">flip books</a> and further magic lantern shows. He also sold amateur film cameras and projectors and produced 3-D <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaglyph_image" title="Anaglyph image">anaglyph image</a> slides. His company <i>Projektion für Alle</i> also produced a number of films in the early 20th century, some directed by Eugen, his younger brother, but with little success. In his later years Skladanowsky was accused in the press of exaggerating his role in the early days of cinema, most notably by the pioneering cameraman <a href="/wiki/Guido_Seeber" title="Guido Seeber">Guido Seeber</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Skladanowsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Between the years 1895 and 1905, the brothers directed at least 25 to 30 short movies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1995, the German filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Wim_Wenders" title="Wim Wenders">Wim Wenders</a> directed a drama documentary film <i><a href="/wiki/A_Trick_of_Light" title="A Trick of Light">Die Gebrüder Skladanowsky</a></i> in collaboration with students of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Munich_Academy_for_Television_and_Film&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Munich Academy for Television and Film (page does not exist)">Munich Academy for Television and Film</a> in which Max Skladanowsky was played by <a href="/wiki/Udo_Kier" title="Udo Kier">Udo Kier</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
