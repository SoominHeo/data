<p>The <b>Max Planck Society for the Advancement of Science</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de"><i>Max-Planck-Gesellschaft zur Förderung der Wissenschaften e. V.</i></span>; abbreviated <b>MPG</b>) is a formally independent non-governmental and non-profit association of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Research_institute" title="Research institute">research institutes</a> founded in 1911 as the <a href="/wiki/Kaiser_Wilhelm_Society" title="Kaiser Wilhelm Society">Kaiser Wilhelm Society</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPGorg_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-MPGorg-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPGportrait_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MPGportrait-3">[3]</a></sup> and renamed the Max Planck Society in 1948 in honor of its former president, <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physics" title="Theoretical physics">theoretical physicist</a> <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck" title="Max Planck">Max Planck</a>. The society is funded by the federal and state governments of Germany as well as other sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPGfacts_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-MPGfacts-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPGorg_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-MPGorg-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to its primary goal, the Max Planck Society supports <a href="/wiki/Basic_research" title="Basic research">fundamental research</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">natural</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_science" title="Life science">life</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social</a> sciences, <a href="/wiki/The_arts" title="The arts">the arts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a> in its 83 (as of January 2014)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPGfacts_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-MPGfacts-2">[2]</a></sup> Max Planck Institutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPGorg_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-MPGorg-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPGportrait_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-MPGportrait-3">[3]</a></sup> The society has a total staff of approximately 17,000 permanent employees, including 5,470 scientists, plus around 4,600 non-tenured scientists and guests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPGfacts_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-MPGfacts-2">[2]</a></sup> Society budget for 2015 was about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%E2%82%AC" title="€">€</a>1.7 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPGfacts_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-MPGfacts-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Max Planck Institutes focus on excellence in research. The Max Planck Society has a world-leading reputation as a science and technology research organization, with 33 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prizes</a> awarded to their scientists, and is generally regarded as the foremost basic research organization in Europe and the world. In 2013, the Nature Publishing Index placed the Max Planck institutes fifth worldwide in terms of research published in <i>Nature</i> journals (after Harvard, MIT, Stanford and the US NIH).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In terms of total research volume (unweighted by citations or impact), the Max Planck Society is only outranked by the Chinese Academy of Sciences, the Russian Academy of Sciences and Harvard University.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The Thomson Reuters-Science Watch website placed the Max Planck Society as the second leading research organization worldwide following Harvard University, in terms of the impact of the produced research over science fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Max Planck Society and its predecessor Kaiser Wilhelm Society hosted several renowned scientists in their fields, including luminaries such as <a href="/wiki/Otto_Hahn" title="Otto Hahn">Otto Hahn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg" title="Werner Heisenberg">Werner Heisenberg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Planck_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The organization was established in 1911 as the <a href="/wiki/Kaiser_Wilhelm_Society" title="Kaiser Wilhelm Society">Kaiser Wilhelm Society</a>, or <i>Kaiser-Wilhelm-Gesellschaft</i> (KWG), a non-governmental research organisation named for the then German emperor. The KWG was one of the world's leading research organisations; its board of directors included scientists like <a href="/wiki/Walther_Bothe" title="Walther Bothe">Walther Bothe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Debye" title="Peter Debye">Peter Debye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Haber" title="Fritz Haber">Fritz Haber</a>. In 1946, <a href="/wiki/Otto_Hahn" title="Otto Hahn">Otto Hahn</a> assumed the position of President of KWG, and in 1948, the society was renamed the Max Planck Society (MPG) after its former President (1930–37) <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck" title="Max Planck">Max Planck</a>, who died in 1947.</p>
<p>The Max Planck Society has a world-leading reputation as a science and technology research organization. In 2006, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Times_Higher_Education_Supplement" title="Times Higher Education Supplement">Times Higher Education Supplement</a> rankings<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> of non-university research institutions (based on international peer review by academics) placed the Max Planck Society as No.1 in the world for science research, and No.3 in technology research (behind <a href="/wiki/AT%26T_Corporation" title="AT&amp;T Corporation">AT&amp;T Corporation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Argonne_National_Laboratory" title="Argonne National Laboratory">Argonne National Laboratory</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>).</p>
<p>The domain <i>mpg.de</i> attracted at least 1.7 million visitors annually by 2008 according to a <a href="/wiki/Compete.com" title="Compete.com">Compete.com</a> study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Since 2004, the Max Planck Research Award is conferred annually to two internationally renowned scientists, one of whom works in Germany and one in another country.</p>
<p>Calls for nominations for the award are invited on an annually rotating basis in specific sub-areas of the natural sciences and engineering, the life sciences and the human and social sciences. The objective of the Max Planck Society and the Alexander von Humboldt Foundation in presenting this joint research award is to give added momentum to specialist fields that are either not yet established in Germany or that deserve to be expanded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Max Planck Society is formally an <i><a href="/wiki/Eingetragener_Verein" title="Eingetragener Verein">eingetragener Verein</a></i>, a registered association with the institute directors as scientific members having equal voting rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The society has its registered seat in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, while the administrative headquarters are located in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>. In 2002 the cell biologist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gruss" title="Peter Gruss">Peter Gruss</a> became the MPG president; materials scientist Martin Stratmann has been President of the Max Planck Society since June 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Funding is provided predominantly from federal and state sources, but also from research and licence fees and donations. One of the larger donations was the castle <a href="/wiki/Schloss_Ringberg" title="Schloss Ringberg">Schloss Ringberg</a> near <a href="/wiki/Kreuth" title="Kreuth">Kreuth</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, which was pledged by Luitpold Emanuel in Bayern (<a href="/wiki/Duke_in_Bavaria" title="Duke in Bavaria">Duke in Bavaria</a>). It passed to the Society after the duke died in 1973, and is now used for conferences.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Max_Planck_institutes_and_research_groups">Max Planck institutes and research groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Planck_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Max Planck institutes and research groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Max_Planck_Institutes" title="List of Max Planck Institutes">List of Max Planck Institutes</a></div>
<p>The Max Planck Society consists of over 80 research institutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPGinstitutes_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-MPGinstitutes-12">[12]</a></sup> In addition, the society funds a number of Max Planck Research Groups (MPRG) and International Max Planck Research Schools (IMPRS). The purpose of establishing independent research groups at various universities is to strengthen the required networking between universities and institutes of the Max Planck Society.</p>
<p>The research units are located across Europe. In 2007 the Society established its first non-European centre, with an institute on the Jupiter campus of <a href="/wiki/Florida_Atlantic_University" title="Florida Atlantic University">Florida Atlantic University</a> focusing on neuroscience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Max Planck Institutes operate independently from, though in close cooperation with, the universities, and focus on innovative research which does not fit into the university structure due to their <a href="/wiki/Interdisciplinarity" title="Interdisciplinarity">interdisciplinary</a> or <a href="/wiki/Transdisciplinarity" title="Transdisciplinarity">transdisciplinary</a> nature or which require resources that cannot be met by the state universities.</p>
<p>Internally, Max Planck Institutes are organized into research departments headed by directors such that each MPI has several directors, a position roughly comparable to anything from full professor to department head at a university. Other core members include Junior and Senior Research Fellows.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discrimination_controversy">Discrimination controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Planck_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Discrimination controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2004 there was a controversy regarding the employment of foreign workers. It was charged that foreign <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a> students were offered contracts that were inferior to those offered to German students. The case was brought to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=European_Civil&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="European Civil (page does not exist)">European Civil</a> Court by Andrea Raccanelli and is documented on his website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The court ruled that MPG <i>must observe the principle of non-discrimination in relation to workers</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Recent developments on the issue include an article in the German newspaper <a href="/wiki/Der_Spiegel" title="Der Spiegel">Der Spiegel</a> denouncing unfair working conditions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> the reply by the MPS supporting the working conditions offered to PhD students,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> and a complaint in Parliament by the German left-wing party <a href="/wiki/The_Left_(Germany)" title="The Left (Germany)">Die Linke</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
