<p><b>Maxi Herber</b> (8 October 1920 – 20 October 2006) was a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Figure_skating" title="Figure skating">figure skater</a> who competed in <a href="/wiki/Pair_skating" title="Pair skating">pair skating</a> and <a href="/wiki/Single_skating" title="Single skating">single skating</a>. She became (and as of 2014, still remains due to new age rules) the youngest female figure skating Olympic champion (at the age of 15 years, 4 months, and 5 days) when she <a href="/wiki/Figure_skating_at_the_1936_Winter_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Pairs" title="Figure skating at the 1936 Winter Olympics – Pairs">won gold in pair skating</a> together with <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Baier" title="Ernst Baier">Ernst Baier</a> at the <a href="/wiki/1936_Winter_Olympics" title="1936 Winter Olympics">1936 Winter Olympics</a>. The duo revolutionized <a href="/wiki/Pair_skating" title="Pair skating">pair skating</a>, becoming the first team to perform <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Figure_skating_jump" title="Figure skating jump">jumps</a> side by side.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, Herber was also an accomplished single skater, winning the German nationals three times, from 1933 to 1935. She skated for the Münchner EV (Munich EV) club.</p>
<p>Herber and Baier married after their skating career ended in 1940. They had 3 children. After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> they skated in ice shows. Later the couple owned a business. In 1964 they were divorced. As a result of the divorce, she had to depend on social services to survive.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Supported by the "Deutsche Sporthilfe" (German Sport help organisation) she moved to <a href="/wiki/Oberau" title="Oberau">Oberau</a> near <a href="/wiki/Garmisch-Partenkirchen" title="Garmisch-Partenkirchen">Garmisch-Partenkirchen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>. Some years later Herber and Baier remarried, but were divorced again.</p>
<p>Herber suffered from <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson's disease">Parkinson's disease</a>. In 2000 she moved to the Lenzheim retirement home in Garmisch-Partenkirchen. Soon afterwards she had an exhibition of her <a href="/wiki/Watercolor_painting" title="Watercolor painting">watercolor</a> paintings there. She died at age 86 in <a href="/wiki/Garmisch-Partenkirchen" title="Garmisch-Partenkirchen">Garmisch-Partenkirchen</a>; Herber had been Germany's oldest living female Olympic champion.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
