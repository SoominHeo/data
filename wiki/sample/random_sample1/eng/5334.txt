<p><b>Maximinus Thrax</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Gaius Julius Verus Maximinus Augustus</i></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> c. 173 – May 238), also known as <b>Maximinus I</b>, was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Emperor" title="Roman Emperor">Roman Emperor</a> from 235 to 238.</p>
<p>Maximinus is described by several ancient sources, though none are contemporary except <a href="/wiki/Herodian" title="Herodian">Herodian</a>'s <i>Roman History</i>. He was a so-called <a href="/wiki/Barracks_emperor" title="Barracks emperor">barracks emperor</a> of the 3rd century; his rule is often considered to mark the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Crisis_of_the_Third_Century" title="Crisis of the Third Century">Crisis of the Third Century</a>. He died at <a href="/wiki/Aquileia" title="Aquileia">Aquileia</a> whilst attempting to put down a Senatorial revolt.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_to_power">Rise to power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maximinus_Thrax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rise to power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most likely Maximinus was of <a href="/wiki/Thraco-Roman" title="Thraco-Roman">Thraco-Roman</a> origin (believed so by Herodian in his writings).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> According to the notoriously unreliable <a href="/wiki/Augustan_History" title="Augustan History">Augustan History</a> (<i>Historia Augusta</i>), he was born in <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a> or <a href="/wiki/Moesia" title="Moesia">Moesia</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Gothic</a> father and an <a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">Alanic</a> mother,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> an Iranian people of the Scythian-Sarmatian branch; however, the supposed parentage is highly unlikely, as the presence of the Goths in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danubian" title="Danubian">Danubian</a> area is first attested after the beginning of the Crisis of the Third Century. British historian <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Syme" title="Ronald Syme">Ronald Syme</a>, writing that "the word 'Gothia' should have sufficed for condemnation" of the passage in the <i>Augustan History</i>, felt that the burden of evidence from <a href="/wiki/Herodian" title="Herodian">Herodian</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Syncellus" title="George Syncellus">Syncellus</a> and elsewhere pointed to Maximinus having been born in <a href="/wiki/Moesia" title="Moesia">Moesia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The references to his "Gothic" ancestry might refer to a <a href="/wiki/Thracians" title="Thracians">Thracian</a> <a href="/wiki/Getae" title="Getae">Getae</a> origin (the two populations were often confused by later writers, most notably by <a href="/wiki/Jordanes" title="Jordanes">Jordanes</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Getica" title="Getica">Getica</a></i>), as suggested by the paragraphs describing how "he was singularly beloved by the Getae, moreover, as if he were one of themselves" and how he spoke "almost pure Thracian".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>His background was, in any case, that of a provincial of low birth, and was seen by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">Senate</a> as a barbarian, not even a true Roman, despite <a href="/wiki/Caracalla" title="Caracalla">Caracalla</a>’s edict granting citizenship to all freeborn inhabitants of the Empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Southern.2C_pg._64_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Southern.2C_pg._64-7">[7]</a></sup> In many ways, Maximinus was similar to the later Thraco-Roman emperors of the 3rd-5th century (<a href="/wiki/Licinius" title="Licinius">Licinius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galerius" title="Galerius">Galerius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aureolus" title="Aureolus">Aureolus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leo_I_the_Thracian" title="Leo I the Thracian">Leo the Thracian</a>, etc.), elevating themselves, via a military career, from the condition of a common <a href="/wiki/Soldier" title="Soldier">soldier</a> in one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_legions" title="Roman legions">Roman legions</a> to the foremost positions of political power. He joined the army during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Septimius_Severus" title="Septimius Severus">Septimius Severus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Potter.2C_pg._168_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Potter.2C_pg._168-8">[8]</a></sup> but did not rise to a powerful position until promoted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Severus" title="Alexander Severus">Alexander Severus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canduci.2C_pg._61_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canduci.2C_pg._61-9">[9]</a></sup> Maximinus was in command of <a href="/wiki/Legio_IV_Italica" title="Legio IV Italica">Legio IV <i>Italica</i></a>, composed of recruits from <a href="/wiki/Pannonia" title="Pannonia">Pannonia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> who were angered by Alexander's payments to the <a href="/wiki/Alemanni" title="Alemanni">Alemanni</a> and his avoidance of war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The troops, among whom included the <a href="/wiki/Legio_XXII_Primigenia" title="Legio XXII Primigenia">Legio XXII <i>Primigenia</i></a>, elected the stern Maximinus, killing young Alexander and his mother at <i>Moguntiacum</i> (modern <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_Guard" title="Praetorian Guard">Praetorian Guard</a> acclaimed him emperor, and their choice was grudgingly confirmed by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">Senate</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Southern.2C_pg._64_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Southern.2C_pg._64-7">[7]</a></sup> who were displeased to have a peasant as emperor. His son <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Julius_Verus_Maximus" title="Gaius Julius Verus Maximus">Maximus</a> became <i><a href="/wiki/Caesar_(title)#Minor_dynastic_title" title="Caesar (title)">caesar</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Southern.2C_pg._64_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Southern.2C_pg._64-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consolidation_of_power">Consolidation of power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maximinus_Thrax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Consolidation of power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Maximinus hated the nobility and was ruthless towards those he suspected of plotting against him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canduci.2C_pg._62_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canduci.2C_pg._62-13">[13]</a></sup> He began by eliminating the close advisors of Alexander.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meckler.2C_Maximinus_Thrax_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meckler.2C_Maximinus_Thrax-14">[14]</a></sup> His suspicions may have been justified; two plots against Maximinus were foiled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The first was during a campaign across the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, when a group of officers, supported by influential senators, plotted to destroy a bridge across the river, in order to strand Maximinus in hostile territory. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> They planned to elect senator <a href="/wiki/Magnus_(Roman_usurper)" title="Magnus (Roman usurper)">Magnus</a> emperor, afterwards; but the conspiracy was discovered and the conspirators executed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canduci.2C_pg._62_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Canduci.2C_pg._62-13">[13]</a></sup> The second plot involved <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamian</a> archers who were loyal to Alexander.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> They planned to elevate <a href="/wiki/Quartinus" title="Quartinus">Quartinus</a>, but their leader Macedo changed sides and murdered Quartinus instead, although this was not enough to save his own life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canduci.2C_pg._62_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Canduci.2C_pg._62-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defence_of_frontiers">Defence of frontiers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maximinus_Thrax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Defence of frontiers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The accession of Maximinus is commonly seen as the beginning of the <i><a href="/wiki/Crisis_of_the_Third_Century" title="Crisis of the Third Century">Crisis of the Third Century</a></i> (also known as the "Military Anarchy" or the "Imperial Crisis"), the commonly applied name for the crumbling and near collapse of the Roman Empire between 235 and 284 caused by three simultaneous crises: external invasion, internal civil war, and economic collapse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canduci.2C_pg._61_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Canduci.2C_pg._61-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Maximinus' first campaign was against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alamanni" title="Alamanni">Alamanni</a>, whom Maximinus defeated despite heavy Roman casualties in a swamp in the <a href="/wiki/Agri_Decumates" title="Agri Decumates">Agri Decumates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> After the victory, Maximinus took the title <i>Germanicus Maximus</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Southern.2C_pg._64_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Southern.2C_pg._64-7">[7]</a></sup> raised his son Maximus to the rank of <a href="/wiki/Caesar_(title)" title="Caesar (title)">caesar</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princeps_iuventutis" title="Princeps iuventutis">princeps iuventutis</a>, and deified his late wife Paulina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meckler.2C_Maximinus_Thrax_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Meckler.2C_Maximinus_Thrax-14">[14]</a></sup> Maximinus may have launched a second campaign deep into Germania, defeating a Germanic tribe beyond the <a href="/wiki/Weser" title="Weser">Weser</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_at_the_Harzhorn" title="Battle at the Harzhorn">Battle at the Harzhorn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Securing the German frontier, at least for a while, Maximinus then set up a winter encampment at <a href="/wiki/Sirmium" title="Sirmium">Sirmium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pannonia" title="Pannonia">Pannonia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Southern.2C_pg._64_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Southern.2C_pg._64-7">[7]</a></sup> and from that supply base fought the <a href="/wiki/Dacia" title="Dacia">Dacians</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sarmatians" title="Sarmatians">Sarmatians</a> during the winter of 235–236.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meckler.2C_Maximinus_Thrax_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Meckler.2C_Maximinus_Thrax-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>Early in 238, in the province of <a href="/wiki/Africa_(Roman_province)" title="Africa (Roman province)">Africa</a>, a treasury official's extortions through false judgments in corrupt courts against some local landowners ignited a full-scale revolt in the province.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canduci.2C_pg._63_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canduci.2C_pg._63-21">[21]</a></sup> The landowners armed their clients and their agricultural workers and entered Thysdrus (modern <a href="/wiki/El_Djem" title="El Djem">El Djem</a>), where they murdered the offending official and his bodyguards<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and proclaimed the aged governor of the province, Marcus Antonius Gordianus Sempronianus (<a href="/wiki/Gordian_I" title="Gordian I">Gordian I</a>), and his son, <a href="/wiki/Gordian_II" title="Gordian II">Gordian II</a>, as co-emperors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The Senate in Rome switched allegiance, gave both Gordian and Gordian II the title of <a href="/wiki/Augustus_(honorific)" title="Augustus (honorific)">Augustus</a>, and set about rousing the provinces in support of the pair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Maximinus, wintering at <a href="/wiki/Sirmium" title="Sirmium">Sirmium</a> immediately assembled his army and advanced on Rome, the Pannonian legions leading the way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meckler.2C_Maximinus_Thrax_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-Meckler.2C_Maximinus_Thrax-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, in Africa, the revolt had not gone as planned. The province of Africa was bordered on the west by the province of <a href="/wiki/Numidia" title="Numidia">Numidia</a>, whose governor, <a href="/wiki/Capelianus" title="Capelianus">Capelianus</a>, nursed a long-standing grudge against the Gordians and controlled the only legionary unit (<a href="/wiki/Legio_III_Augusta" title="Legio III Augusta">III <i>Augusta</i></a>) in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> He marched on Carthage and easily overwhelmed the local militias defending the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canduci.2C_pg._63_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Canduci.2C_pg._63-21">[21]</a></sup> Gordian II was killed in the fighting and, on hearing this, Gordian I hanged himself with his belt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pupienus.2C_Balbinus.2C_and_Gordian_III">Pupienus, Balbinus, and Gordian III</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maximinus_Thrax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pupienus, Balbinus, and Gordian III">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When the African revolt collapsed, the Senate found itself in great jeopardy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Southern.2C_pg._67_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Southern.2C_pg._67-27">[27]</a></sup> Having shown clear support for the Gordians, they could expect no clemency from Maximinus when he reached Rome. In this predicament, they remained determined to defy Maximinus and elected two of their number, <a href="/wiki/Pupienus_and_Balbinus" title="Pupienus and Balbinus">Pupienus and Balbinus</a>, as co-emperors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meckler.2C_Maximinus_Thrax_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-Meckler.2C_Maximinus_Thrax-14">[14]</a></sup> When the Roman mob heard that the Senate had selected two men from the <a href="/wiki/Patrician_(ancient_Rome)" title="Patrician (ancient Rome)">patrician</a> class, men whom the ordinary people held in no great regard, they protested, showering the imperial cortège with sticks and stones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> A faction in Rome preferred Gordian's grandson (<a href="/wiki/Gordian_III" title="Gordian III">Gordian III</a>), and there was severe street fighting. The co-emperors had no option but to compromise, and, sending for the grandson of the elder Gordian they appointed him <i>Caesar</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defeat_and_death">Defeat and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maximinus_Thrax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Defeat and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Maximinus marched on Rome,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> but <a href="/wiki/Aquileia" title="Aquileia">Aquileia</a> closed its gates against him. His troops became disaffected during <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Aquileia" title="Siege of Aquileia">the unexpected siege of the city</a>, at which time they suffered from famine and disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canduci.2C_pg._62_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Canduci.2C_pg._62-13">[13]</a></sup> In May 238, soldiers of the <a href="/wiki/Legio_II_Parthica" title="Legio II Parthica">II <i>Parthica</i></a> in his camp assassinated him, his son, and his chief ministers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Southern.2C_pg._67_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Southern.2C_pg._67-27">[27]</a></sup> Their heads were cut off, placed on poles, and carried to Rome by cavalrymen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meckler.2C_Maximinus_Thrax_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-Meckler.2C_Maximinus_Thrax-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pupienus and Balbinus then became undisputed co-emperors. However, they distrusted each other, and ultimately both were murdered by the Praetorian Guard, making Gordian III sole Emperor.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maximinus_Thrax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Maximinus doubled the pay of soldiers;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Potter.2C_pg._168_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Potter.2C_pg._168-8">[8]</a></sup> this act, along with virtually continuous warfare, required higher taxes. Tax-collectors began to resort to violent methods and illegal confiscations, further alienating the governing class from everyone else.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meckler.2C_Maximinus_Thrax_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-Meckler.2C_Maximinus_Thrax-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to early church historian <a href="/wiki/Eusebius" title="Eusebius">Eusebius of Caesarea</a>, the Imperial household of Maximinus' predecessor, Alexander, had contained many <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christians</a>. Eusebius states that, hating his predecessor's household, Maximinius ordered that the leaders of the churches should be put to death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eusebius_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eusebius-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> According to Eusebius, this persecution of 235 sent <a href="/wiki/Hippolytus_of_Rome" title="Hippolytus of Rome">Hippolytus of Rome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pontian" title="Pope Pontian">Pope Pontian</a> into exile but other evidence suggests that the persecutions of 235 were local to the provinces where they occurred rather than happening under the direction of the Emperor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">Appearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maximinus_Thrax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Appearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ancient sources, ranging from the unreliable <i><a href="/wiki/Augustan_History" title="Augustan History">Historia Augusta</a></i> to <a href="/wiki/Herodian" title="Herodian">Herodian</a>, speak of Maximinus as a man of significantly greater size than his contemporaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> He is, moreover, depicted in ancient imagery as a man with a prominent brow, nose, and jaw; symptoms of <a href="/wiki/Acromegaly" title="Acromegaly">acromegaly</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> His <a href="/wiki/Thumb" title="Thumb">thumb</a> was said to be so large that he wore his wife's <a href="/wiki/Bracelet" title="Bracelet">bracelet</a> as a ring for it.</p>
<p>According to <i>Historia Augusta</i>, "he was of such size, so Cordus reports, that men said he was eight foot, six inches (c. 2.5 metres) in height".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> It is very likely however that this is one of the many 'tall tales' in the <i>Historia Augusta</i>, and is immediately suspect due to its citation of 'Cordus', one of the several fictitious authorities the work cites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although not going into the supposedly detailed portions of <i>Historia Augusta</i>, the historian Herodian, a contemporary of Maximinus, mentions him as a man of greater size, noting that: "He was in any case a man of such frightening appearance and colossal size that there is no obvious comparison to be drawn with any of the best-trained Greek athletes or warrior elite of the barbarians."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some historians interpret the stories on Maximinus' unusual height (as well as other information on his appearance, like excessive sweating and superhuman strength) as popular stereotyped attributes which do no more than intentionally turn him into a stylized embodiment of the barbarian bandit<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> or emphasize the admiration and aversion that the image of the soldier evoked in the civilian population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
