<p>A <b>bay</b> is a body of water connected to an <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a>, formed by an indentation of the shoreline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A large bay may be called a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_(geography)" title="Gulf (geography)">gulf</a></i>, a <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_seas" title="List of seas">sea</a></i>, a <i><a href="/wiki/Sound_(geography)" title="Sound (geography)">sound</a></i>, or a <i><a href="/wiki/Bight_(geography)" title="Bight (geography)">bight</a></i>. A <i><a href="/wiki/Cove" title="Cove">cove</a></i> is a smaller circular or <a href="/wiki/Oval" title="Oval">oval</a> <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coastal</a> <a href="/wiki/Inlet" title="Inlet">inlet</a> with a narrow entrance; some coves may be referred to as bays. A <i><a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjord</a></i> is a particularly steep bay shaped by glacial activity.</p>
<p>Bays can exist as the <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a> of a river, as the estuary of the <a href="/wiki/Parramatta_River" title="Parramatta River">Parramatta River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. Bays may be nested in each other; for example, <a href="/wiki/James_Bay" title="James Bay">James Bay</a> is an arm of <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">Hudson Bay</a>. Some large bays, such as the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a> and the Hudson Bay, have varied <a href="/wiki/Marine_geology" title="Marine geology">marine geology</a>.</p>
<p>The land surrounding a bay can often block <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">waves</a> and reduce the strength of <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a>. Thus bays were significant in the history of <a href="/wiki/Human_settlement" title="Human settlement">human settlement</a> because they could provide a safe place for <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a>. Later they were important in the development of sea <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a> as the safe <a href="/wiki/Anchor" title="Anchor">anchorage</a> they provide encouraged their selection as <a href="/wiki/Port" title="Port">ports</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fenh_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-fenh-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are various ways from which bays can be created. The largest bays have developed as a result of <a href="/wiki/Continental_drift" title="Continental drift">continental drift</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fenh_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-fenh-2">[2]</a></sup> As the super-continent <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> broke up along curved and indented fault lines, the continents moved apart and the world's largest bays formed. These include the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Guinea" title="Gulf of Guinea">Gulf of Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Alaska" title="Gulf of Alaska">Gulf of Alaska</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a>, which is the largest bay in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fenh_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-fenh-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another way bays form is via glacial and river erosion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fenh_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-fenh-2">[2]</a></sup> A bay formed by a glacier is a <b><a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjord</a></b>. <b><a href="/wiki/Ria" title="Ria">Rias</a></b> are created by rivers and are characterised by more gradual slopes. Most gulf and bays form by the <a href="/wiki/Fold_(geology)" title="Fold (geology)">folding</a> of the earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>, and by coastal erosion due to <a href="/wiki/Wind_wave" title="Wind wave">waves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Current_(stream)" title="Current (stream)">currents</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fenh_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-fenh-2">[2]</a></sup> Currents can make waves more constant, and soft rocks speed erosion. Hard rock eroded less quickly, leaving <a href="/wiki/Headland" title="Headland">headlands</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_California" title="Gulf of California">Gulf of California</a> is an example of a bay created by the geological process of folding.</p>
