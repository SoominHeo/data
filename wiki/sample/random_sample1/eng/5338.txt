<p>A <b>barnacle</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropod</a> constituting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infraclass" title="Infraclass">infraclass</a> Cirripedia in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subphylum_(biology)" title="Subphylum (biology)">subphylum</a> <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">Crustacea</a>, and is hence related to <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobsters</a>. Barnacles are exclusively marine, and tend to live in shallow and tidal waters, typically in erosive settings. They are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sessility_(zoology)" title="Sessility (zoology)">sessile</a> (nonmotile) <a href="/wiki/Filter_feeder" title="Filter feeder">suspension feeders</a>, and have two <a href="/wiki/Nekton" title="Nekton">nektonic</a> (active swimming) larval stages. Around 1,220 barnacle <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> are currently known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The name "Cirripedia" is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, meaning "curl-footed".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnacle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Barnacles are encrusters, attaching themselves permanently to a hard substrate. The most common, "acorn barnacles" (<a href="/wiki/Sessilia" title="Sessilia">Sessilia</a>), are <a href="/wiki/Sessility_(motility)" title="Sessility (motility)">sessile</a>, growing their shells directly onto the substrate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doyle1997_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle1997-2">[2]</a></sup> The order <i>Pedunculata</i> (<a href="/wiki/Goose_barnacle" title="Goose barnacle">goose barnacles</a> and others) attach themselves by means of a stalk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doyle1997_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle1997-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Free-living barnacles are attached to the substratum by cement glands that form the base of the first pair of <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(biology)" title="Antenna (biology)">antennae</a>; in effect, the animal is fixed upside down by means of its forehead. In some barnacles, the cement glands are fixed to a long, muscular stalk, but in most they are part of a flat membrane or calcified plate. A ring of plates surrounds the body, homologous with the <a href="/wiki/Carapace" title="Carapace">carapace</a> of other crustaceans. These consist of the <a href="/wiki/Rostrum_(anatomy)" title="Rostrum (anatomy)">rostrum</a>, two lateral plates, two carinolaterals, and a carina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In sessile barnacles, the apex of the ring of plates is covered by an <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/operculum" title="wikt:operculum">operculum</a>, which may be recessed into the carapace. The plates are held together by various means, depending on species, in some cases being solidly fused.</p>
<p>Inside the carapace, the animal lies on its back, with its limbs projecting upwards. Segmentation is usually indistinct, and the body is more or less evenly divided between the head and <a href="/wiki/Thorax" title="Thorax">thorax</a>, with little, if any, <a href="/wiki/Abdomen" title="Abdomen">abdomen</a>. Adult barnacles have few appendages on their heads, with only a single, vestigial pair of antennae, attached to the cement gland. The six pairs of thoracic limbs are referred to as "cirri", which are feathery and very long, being used to filter food, such as plankton, from the water and move it towards the mouth.</p>
<p>Barnacles have no true <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>, although a sinus close to the <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a> performs similar function, with blood being pumped through it by a series of muscles. The blood vascular system is minimal. Similarly, they have no <a href="/wiki/Gill" title="Gill">gills</a>, absorbing oxygen from the water through their limbs and the inner membrane of their carapaces. The excretory organs of barnacles are maxillary glands.</p>
<p>The main sense of barnacles appears to be touch, with the hairs on the limbs being especially sensitive. The adult also has a single eye, although this is probably only capable of sensing the difference between light and dark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IZ_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-IZ-4">[4]</a></sup> This eye is derived from the primary <a href="/wiki/Crustacean_larvae#Nauplius" title="Crustacean larvae">naupliar eye</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle">Life cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnacle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Barnacles have two distinct larval stages, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nauplius_(larva)" title="Nauplius (larva)">nauplius</a> and the cyprid, before developing into a mature adult.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nauplius">Nauplius</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnacle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nauplius">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Fertilisation" title="Fertilisation">fertilised egg</a> hatches into a nauplius: a one-eyed larva comprising a head and a <a href="/wiki/Telson" title="Telson">telson</a>, without a thorax or abdomen. This undergoes six months of growth, passing through five <a href="/wiki/Instar" title="Instar">instars</a>, before transforming into the cyprid stage. Nauplii are typically initially brooded by the parent, and released after the first moult as larvae that swim freely using <a href="/wiki/Seta" title="Seta">setae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newman_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newman-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyprid">Cyprid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnacle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cyprid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The cyprid larva is the last larval stage before adulthood. It is not a feeding stage; its role is to find a suitable place to settle, since the adults are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sessility_(zoology)" title="Sessility (zoology)">sessile</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newman_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Newman-7">[7]</a></sup> The cyprid stage lasts from days to weeks. It explores potential surfaces with modified <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(biology)#Crustaceans" title="Antenna (biology)">antennules</a>; once it has found a potentially suitable spot, it attaches head-first using its antennules, and a secreted glycoproteinous substance. Larvae assess surfaces based upon their surface texture, chemistry, relative wettability, colour, and the presence/absence and composition of a surface <a href="/wiki/Biofilm" title="Biofilm">biofilm</a>; swarming species are also more likely to attach near other barnacles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-8">[8]</a></sup> As the larva exhausts its finite energy reserves, it becomes less selective in the sites it selects. It cements itself permanently to the substrate with another proteinacous compound, and then undergoes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metamorphosis_(biology)" title="Metamorphosis (biology)">metamorphosis</a> into a juvenile barnacle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adult">Adult</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnacle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Adult">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Typical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acorn_barnacle" title="Acorn barnacle">acorn barnacles</a> develop six hard calcareous plates to surround and protect their bodies. For the rest of their lives, they are cemented to the substrate, using their feathery legs (cirri) to capture plankton.</p>
<p>Once metamorphosis is over and they have reached their adult form, barnacles continue to grow by adding new material to their heavily calcified plates. These plates are not <a href="/wiki/Ecdysis" title="Ecdysis">moulted</a>; however, like all <a href="/wiki/Ecdysozoa" title="Ecdysozoa">ecdysozoans</a>, the barnacle itself will still molt its <a href="/wiki/Cuticle#Invertebrate_zoology" title="Cuticle">cuticle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_reproduction">Sexual reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnacle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sexual reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most barnacles are <a href="/wiki/Hermaphrodite" title="Hermaphrodite">hermaphroditic</a>, although a few species are <a href="/wiki/Gonochorism" title="Gonochorism">gonochoric</a> or <a href="/wiki/Androdioecy" title="Androdioecy">androdioecious</a>. The ovaries are located in the base or stalk, and may extend into the mantle, while the testes are towards the back of the head, often extending into the thorax. Typically, recently molted hermaphroditic individuals are receptive as females. Self-fertilization, although theoretically possible, has been experimentally shown to be rare in barnacles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vic_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vic-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sessile lifestyle of barnacles makes <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction" title="Sexual reproduction">sexual reproduction</a> difficult, as the organisms cannot leave their shells to mate. To facilitate genetic transfer between isolated individuals, barnacles have extraordinarily long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penises" title="Penises">penises</a>⁠. Barnacles probably have the largest penis to body size ratio of the animal kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vic_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vic-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Barnacles can also reproduce through a method called spermcasting, in which the male barnacle releases his sperm into the water and females pick it up and fertilise their eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>Most barnacles are suspension feeders; they dwell continually in their shells, which are usually constructed of six plates,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doyle1997_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle1997-2">[2]</a></sup> and reach into the water column with modified legs. These feathery appendages beat rhythmically to draw <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a> and detritus into the shell for consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other members of the class have quite a different mode of life. For example, members of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Sacculina" title="Sacculina">Sacculina</a></i> are <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">parasitic</a>, dwelling within crabs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although they have been found at water depths to 600 m (2,000 ft),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doyle1997_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle1997-2">[2]</a></sup> most barnacles inhabit shallow waters, with 75% of species living in water depths less than 100 m (300 ft),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doyle1997_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle1997-2">[2]</a></sup> and 25% inhabiting the <a href="/wiki/Intertidal_zone" title="Intertidal zone">intertidal zone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doyle1997_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle1997-2">[2]</a></sup> Within the intertidal zone, different species of barnacles live in very tightly constrained locations, allowing the exact height of an assemblage above or below sea level to be precisely determined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doyle1997_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle1997-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the intertidal zone periodically <a href="/wiki/Desiccation" title="Desiccation">desiccates</a>, barnacles are well adapted against water loss. Their calcite shells are impermeable, and they possess two plates which they can slide across their apertures when not feeding. These plates also protect against predation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Barnacles are displaced by <a href="/wiki/Limpet" title="Limpet">limpets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mussel" title="Mussel">mussels</a>, which compete for space. They also have numerous predators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doyle1997_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle1997-2">[2]</a></sup> They employ two strategies to overwhelm their competitors: "swamping" and fast growth. In the swamping strategy, vast numbers of barnacles settle in the same place at once, covering a large patch of substrate, allowing at least some to survive in the balance of probabilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doyle1997_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle1997-2">[2]</a></sup> Fast growth allows the suspension feeders to access higher levels of the water column than their competitors, and to be large enough to resist displacement; species employing this response, such as the aptly named <i><a href="/wiki/Megabalanus" title="Megabalanus">Megabalanus</a></i>, can reach 7 cm (3 in) in length;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doyle1997_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle1997-2">[2]</a></sup> other species may grow larger still (<i><a href="/wiki/Austromegabalanus_psittacus" title="Austromegabalanus psittacus">Austromegabalanus psittacus</a></i>).</p>
<p>Competitors may include other barnacles, and disputed evidence indicates balanoid barnacles competitively displaced chthalamoid barnacles. Balanoids gained their advantage over the chthalamoids in the Oligocene, when they evolved tubular skeletons, which provide better anchorage to the substrate, and allow them to grow faster, undercutting, crushing, and smothering the latter group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stanley2008_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stanley2008-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among the most common predators on barnacles are <a href="/wiki/Whelk" title="Whelk">whelks</a>. They are able to grind through the calcareous exoskeletons of barnacles and feed on the softer inside parts. <a href="/wiki/Mussel" title="Mussel">Mussels</a> also prey on barnacle larvae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Another predator on barnacles is the starfish species <i><a href="/wiki/Pisaster_ochraceus" title="Pisaster ochraceus">Pisaster ochraceus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<p>The anatomy of parasitic barnacles is generally simpler than that of their free-living relatives. They have no carapace or limbs, having only unsegmented sac-like bodies. Such barnacles feed by extending thread-like rhizomes of living cells into their hosts' bodies from their points of attachment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IZ_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-IZ-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_taxonomy">History of taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnacle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History of taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Haeckel_Cirripedia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3294" data-file-width="2351" height="308" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Haeckel_Cirripedia.jpg/220px-Haeckel_Cirripedia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Haeckel_Cirripedia.jpg/330px-Haeckel_Cirripedia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Haeckel_Cirripedia.jpg/440px-Haeckel_Cirripedia.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Barnacles were originally classified by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georges_Cuvier" title="Georges Cuvier">Cuvier</a> as <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">Mollusca</a>, but in 1830 <a href="/wiki/John_Vaughan_Thompson" title="John Vaughan Thompson">John Vaughan Thompson</a> published observations showing the metamorphosis of the nauplius and cypris larvae into adult barnacles, and noted how these larvae were similar to those of crustaceans. In 1834 <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Burmeister" title="Hermann Burmeister">Hermann Burmeister</a> published further information, reinterpreting these findings. The effect was to move barnacles from the <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">phylum</a> of Mollusca to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Articulata_Hypothesis" title="Articulata Hypothesis">Articulata</a>, showing naturalists that detailed study was needed to reevaluate their <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darwin_Cirripedia_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Darwin_Cirripedia-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> took up this challenge in 1846, and developed his initial interest into a major study published as a series of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monographs" title="Monographs">monographs</a> in 1851 and 1854.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darwin_Cirripedia_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Darwin_Cirripedia-20">[20]</a></sup> Darwin undertook this study, at the suggestion of his friend <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Dalton_Hooker" title="Joseph Dalton Hooker">Joseph Dalton Hooker</a>, to thoroughly understand at least one species before making the generalisations needed for his theory of <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> by <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_record">Fossil record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnacle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Fossil record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The geological history of barnacles can be traced back to animals such as <i><a href="/wiki/Priscansermarinus" title="Priscansermarinus">Priscansermarinus</a></i> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Cambrian" title="Middle Cambrian">Middle Cambrian</a> (on the order of <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=510–500">510 to 500</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foster1987_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foster1987-23">[23]</a></sup> although they do not become common as skeletal remains in the fossil record until the <a href="/wiki/Neogene" title="Neogene">Neogene</a> (last 20 million years).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doyle1997_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle1997-2">[2]</a></sup> In part, their poor skeletal preservation is due to their restriction to high-energy environments, which tend to be <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosional</a> – therefore it is more common for their shells to be ground up by wave action than for them to reach a depositional setting. Trace fossils of <a href="/wiki/Acrothoracica" title="Acrothoracica">acrothoracican</a> barnacle <a href="/wiki/Bioerosion" title="Bioerosion">borings</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Rogerella" title="Rogerella">Rogerella</a></i>) are common in the fossil record from the <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a> to the recent.</p>
<p>Barnacles can play an important role in estimating paleo-water depths. The degree of disarticluation of fossils suggests the distance they have been transported, and since many species have narrow ranges of water depths, it can be assumed that the animals lived in shallow water and broke up as they were washed down-slope. The completeness of fossils, and nature of damage, can thus be used to constrain the tectonic history of regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doyle1997_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle1997-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_human_culture">In human culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnacle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In human culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Barnacles are of economic consequence, as they often attach themselves to synthetic structures, sometimes to the structure's detriment. Particularly in the case of ships, they are classified as <a href="/wiki/Biofouling" title="Biofouling">fouling</a> organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> signals in the layers of barnacle shells can potentially be used as a forensic tracking method for <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loggerhead_turtle" title="Loggerhead turtle">loggerhead turtles</a> and marine debris, such as <a href="/wiki/Shipwreck" title="Shipwreck">shipwrecks</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Flaperon" title="Flaperon">flaperon</a> suspected to be from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MH370" title="MH370">MH370</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some barnacles are considered edible by humans, including Japanese goose barnacles (<i>e.g.</i> <i><a href="/wiki/Capitulum_mitella" title="Capitulum mitella">Capitulum mitella</a></i>), and <a href="/wiki/Goose_barnacle" title="Goose barnacle">goose barnacles</a> (<i>e.g.</i> <i><a href="/wiki/Pollicipes_pollicipes" title="Pollicipes pollicipes">Pollicipes pollicipes</a></i>), a delicacy in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Molares_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Molares-27">[27]</a></sup> The resemblance of this barnacle's fleshy stalk to a goose's neck gave rise in ancient times to the notion that geese, or at least certain seagoing species of wild geese, literally grew from the barnacle. Indeed, the word "barnacle" originally referred to a species of goose, the <a href="/wiki/Barnacle_goose" title="Barnacle goose">barnacle goose</a> <i>Branta leucopsis</i>, whose eggs and young were rarely seen by humans because it breeds in the remote <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Additionally, the <a href="/wiki/Austromegabalanus_psittacus" title="Austromegabalanus psittacus">picoroco barnacle</a> is used in <a href="/wiki/Chilean_cuisine" title="Chilean cuisine">Chilean cuisine</a> and is one of the ingredients in <i><a href="/wiki/Curanto" title="Curanto">curanto</a></i>.</p>
