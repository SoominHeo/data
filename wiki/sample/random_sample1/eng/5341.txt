<p><i><b>Manduguk</b></i> (<small>Korean pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Korean" title="Help:IPA for Korean">[manduɡuk]</a></span> is a variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korea_cuisine" title="Korea cuisine">Korean</a> soup (<i><a href="/wiki/Guk" title="Guk">guk</a></i>) made by boiling <i><a href="/wiki/Mandu_(dumpling)" title="Mandu (dumpling)">mandu</a></i> (dumplings) in a beef broth or anchovy broth mixed with beaten egg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manduguk_doosan_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-manduguk_doosan-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manduguk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the 14th century records of <i>Goryeosa</i> (고려사), <i>mandu</i> had already been introduced via Central Asia during the <a href="/wiki/Goryeo" title="Goryeo">Goryeo</a> era. <i>Mandu</i> was called <i>sanghwa</i> (쌍화) or <i>gyoja</i> (교자) until the mid-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a> and became a local specialty of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyongan" title="Pyongan">Pyongan</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hamgyong" title="Hamgyong">Hamgyong</a> regions, as both wheat and buckwheat — the main ingredients for flour — were mainly cultivated in the north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Mandu</i> was made and cooked in various ways, including <i>manduguk</i>. In the Korean royal court, the dish was called <i>byeongsi</i> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%A4%A0" title="wikt:餠">餠</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%8C%99" title="wikt:匙">匙</a>) while in <a href="/wiki/Eumsik_dimibang" title="Eumsik dimibang">Eumsik dimibang</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a> cookbook, it was called <i>"seokryutang"</i> (석류탕). The exact era when <i>manduguk</i> got its modern name is unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manduguk_ekc_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-manduguk_ekc-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation_and_serving">Preparation and serving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manduguk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation and serving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tteokmanduguk_(rice_cake_dumpling_soup).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="960" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Tteokmanduguk_%28rice_cake_dumpling_soup%29.jpg/220px-Tteokmanduguk_%28rice_cake_dumpling_soup%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Tteokmanduguk_%28rice_cake_dumpling_soup%29.jpg/330px-Tteokmanduguk_%28rice_cake_dumpling_soup%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Tteokmanduguk_%28rice_cake_dumpling_soup%29.jpg/440px-Tteokmanduguk_%28rice_cake_dumpling_soup%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tteokmanduguk_(rice_cake_dumpling_soup).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
tteokmanduguk(rice cake and dumpling soup)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Dumplings are made by rolling out thin circles of dough, creating a half-moon shape and filling them with a mixture of minced meat, vegetables, tofu and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Kimchi" title="Kimchi">kimchi</a>. The dumplings are then boiled in a broth traditionally made by boiling anchovies, shitake mushroom stems and onions. Towards the end of cooking, beaten eggs are added to the broth to create a richer taste and more green onions for flavor and garnish similar to the egg drop soup of the Chinese .</p>
<p>Some variations make the broth from beef stock. The addition of <a href="/wiki/Tteok" title="Tteok">tteok</a> which is a cylindrical rice cake is common as well, changing the dish's name into <i>tteok manduguk</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
