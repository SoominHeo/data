<p>A <b>basis point</b> (often denoted as <b>bp</b>, often pronounced as "bip" or "beep"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is one hundredth of a percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inpe_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-inpe-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basis_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div style="float:right;clear:none;">
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Visualisation_parts_per.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="683" data-file-width="512" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Visualisation_parts_per.svg/200px-Visualisation_parts_per.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Visualisation_parts_per.svg/300px-Visualisation_parts_per.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Visualisation_parts_per.svg/400px-Visualisation_parts_per.svg.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Visualisation_parts_per.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Visualisation of 1%, 1‰, 1‱ and 1 ppm as the fraction of the red cube to its respective block <span class="nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Visualisation_parts_per.svg/768px-Visualisation_parts_per.svg.png">(click for larger version)</a></span></div>
</div>

<dl>
<dd>1 basis point = 1 <a href="#Permyriad">permyriad</a> = one-hundredth percent</dd>
<dd>1 bp = 1‱ = 0.01% = 0.1‰ = 10<sup>−4</sup> = <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>10000</sub></span> = 0.0001</dd>
<dd>1% = 100 bp = 100‱</dd>
</dl>
<p>Basis points are used as a convenient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unit_of_measurement" title="Unit of measurement">unit of measurement</a> in contexts where percentage differences of less than 1% are discussed. The most common example is <a href="/wiki/Interest_rate" title="Interest rate">interest rates</a>, where differences in interest rates of less than 1% per year are usually meaningful to talk about. For example, a difference of 0.10 percentage points is equivalent to a change of 10 basis points (e.g., a 4.67% rate increases by 10 basis points to 4.77%). In other words, an increase of 100 basis points means a rise by 1%.</p>
<p>Like <a href="/wiki/Percentage_point" title="Percentage point">percentage points</a>, basis points avoid the ambiguity between relative and absolute discussions about interest rates by dealing only with the absolute change in numeric value of a rate. For example, <span id="relative_vs_absolute"></span> if a report says there has been a "1% increase" from a 10% interest rate, this could refer to an increase either from 10% to 10.1% (relative, 1% of 10%), or from 10% to 11% (absolute, 1% plus 10%). If, however, the report says there has been a "100 basis point increase" from a 10% interest rate, then it is obvious that the interest rate of 10% has increased by 1.00% (the absolute change) to an 11% rate.</p>
<p>It is common practice in the financial industry to use basis points to denote a rate change in a <a href="/wiki/Financial_instrument" title="Financial instrument">financial instrument</a>, or the difference (<a href="/wiki/Yield_spread" title="Yield spread">spread</a>) between two interest rates, including the <a href="/wiki/Yield_(finance)" title="Yield (finance)">yields</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fixed_income" title="Fixed income">fixed-income</a> <a href="/wiki/Security_(finance)" title="Security (finance)">securities</a>.</p>
<p>Since certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loans" title="Loans">loans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">bonds</a> may commonly be quoted in relation to some index or underlying security, they will often be quoted as a spread over (or under) the index. For example, a loan that bears <a href="/wiki/Interest" title="Interest">interest</a> of 0.50% per annum above the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Interbank_Offered_Rate" title="London Interbank Offered Rate">London Interbank Offered Rate</a> (LIBOR) is said to be 50 basis points over LIBOR, which is commonly expressed as "L+50bps" or simply "L+50".</p>
<p>The term "basis point" has its origins in trading the "basis" or the spread between two interest rates. Since the basis is usually small, these are quoted multiplied up by 10,000, and hence a "full point" movement in the "basis" is a basis point. Contrast with <a href="/wiki/Percentage_in_point" title="Percentage in point">pips</a> in FX forward markets.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Permyriad">Permyriad</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basis_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Permyriad">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A related concept is one part per ten thousand, <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>10,000</sub></span>. The same unit is also (rarely) called a <b>permyriad,</b> literally meaning "for (every) <a href="/wiki/Myriad" title="Myriad">myriad</a> (ten thousand)". If used interchangeably, the permyriad is potentially confusing because an increase of one basis point to a 10 basis point value is generally understood to mean an increase to 11 basis points; not an increase of one part in ten thousand, meaning an increase to 10.001 basis points. This is akin to the difference between <a href="/wiki/Percentage" title="Percentage">percentage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Percentage_point" title="Percentage point">percentage point</a>. A permyriad is written with <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+2031</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">‱</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">PER TEN THOUSAND SIGN</span> (HTML <code>&amp;#8241;</code>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which looks like a <a href="/wiki/Percent_sign" title="Percent sign">percent sign</a> (%) with three zeroes to the right of the slash. (It can be regarded as a stylized form of the four zeros in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denominator" title="Denominator">denominator</a> of "<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>10,000</sub></span>", although it originates as a natural extension of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Percent" title="Percent">percent</a> (%) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permille" title="Permille">permille</a> (‰) signs).</p>
