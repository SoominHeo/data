<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Man'yōshū</i></h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

<p>The <i><b>Man'yōshū</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">万葉集</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, literally "Collection of Ten Thousand Leaves", but see <a href="/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB#Name" title="Man'yōshū"><i>Name</i></a> below)</span> is the oldest existing collection of <a href="/wiki/Japanese_poetry" title="Japanese poetry">Japanese poetry</a>, compiled sometime after 759 AD during the <a href="/wiki/Nara_period" title="Nara period">Nara period</a>. The anthology is one of the most revered of Japan's poetic compilations. The compiler, or the last in a series of compilers, is today widely believed to be <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ctomo_no_Yakamochi" title="Ōtomo no Yakamochi">Ōtomo no Yakamochi</a>, although numerous other theories have been proposed. The last datable poem in the collection is from AD 759 (#4516<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>). It contains many poems from much earlier, many of them anonymous or misattributed (usually to well-known poets), but the bulk of the collection represents the period between AD 600 and 759. The precise significance of the title is not known with certainty.</p>
<p>The collection is divided into twenty parts or books; this number was followed in most later collections. The collection contains 265 <i><a href="/wiki/Waka_(poetry)#Ch.C5.8Dka" title="Waka (poetry)">chōka</a></i> (long poems), 4,207 <i><a href="/wiki/Waka_(poetry)#Tanka" title="Waka (poetry)">tanka</a></i> (short poems), one <i>tan-renga</i> (short connecting poem), one <i><a href="/wiki/Bussokusekika" title="Bussokusekika">bussokusekika</a></i> (poems on the Buddha's footprints at <a href="/wiki/Yakushi-ji" title="Yakushi-ji">Yakushi-ji</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nara,_Nara" title="Nara, Nara">Nara</a>), four <i>kanshi</i> (Chinese poems), and 22 Chinese prose passages. Unlike later collections, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Kokin_Wakash%C5%AB" title="Kokin Wakashū">Kokin Wakashū</a></i>, there is no preface.</p>
<p>The <i>Man'yōshū</i> is widely regarded as being a particularly unique Japanese work. This does not mean that the poems and passages of the collection differed starkly from the scholarly standard (in Yakamochi's time) of Chinese literature and poetics. Certainly many entries of the <i>Man'yōshū</i> have a continental tone, earlier poems having <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confucian" title="Confucian">Confucian</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taoist" title="Taoist">Taoist</a> themes and later poems reflecting on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> teachings. Yet, the <i>Man'yōshū</i> is singular, even in comparison with later works, in choosing primarily Ancient Japanese themes, extolling <i><a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shintō</a></i> virtues of forthrightness <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">真</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>makoto</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> and virility <i>(<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%9B%8A%E8%8D%92%E7%94%B7%E6%8C%AF%E3%82%8A" title="wiktionary:益荒男振り">masuraoburi</a>)</i>. In addition, the language of many entries of the <i>Man'yōshū</i> exerts a powerful sentimental appeal to readers:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>[T]his early collection has something of the freshness of dawn. [...] There are irregularities not tolerated later, such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hypometric_line&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hypometric line (page does not exist)">hypometric lines</a>; there are evocative place names and <i><a href="/wiki/Makurakotoba" title="Makurakotoba">makurakotoba</a></i>; and there are evocative exclamations such as <i>kamo</i>, whose appeal is genuine even if incommunicable. In other words, the collection contains the appeal of an art at its pristine source with a romantic sense of venerable age and therefore of an ideal order since lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Man%27y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the name <i>Man'yōshū</i> literally means "Collection of Ten Thousand Leaves" or "Collection of Myriad Leaves", it has been interpreted variously by scholars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sengaku" title="Sengaku">Sengaku</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kamo_no_Mabuchi" title="Kamo no Mabuchi">Kamo no Mabuchi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kada_no_Azumamaro" title="Kada no Azumamaro">Kada no Azumamaro</a> considered the character 葉 <i>yō</i> to represent <i>koto no ha</i> (words), and so give the meaning of the title as "collection of countless words". <a href="/wiki/Keich%C5%AB" title="Keichū">Keichū</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kamochi_Masazumi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kamochi Masazumi (page does not exist)">Kamochi Masazumi</a> (鹿持雅澄) took the middle character to refer to an "era", thus giving "a collection to last ten thousand ages". The <i><a href="/wiki/Kanbun" title="Kanbun">kanbun</a></i> scholar <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Okada_Masayuki&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Okada Masayuki (page does not exist)">Okada Masayuki</a> (岡田正之) considered 葉 <i>yō</i> to be a metaphor comparing the massive collection of poems to the leaves on a tree. Another theory is that the name refers to the large number of pages used in the collection.</p>
<p>Of these, "collection to last ten thousand ages" is considered to be the interpretation with the most weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Periodization">Periodization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Man%27y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Periodization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The collection is customarily divided into four periods. The earliest dates to prehistoric or legendary pasts, from the time of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Y%C5%ABryaku" title="Emperor Yūryaku">Emperor Yūryaku</a> (r.?456–?479) to those of the little documented <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Y%C5%8Dmei" title="Emperor Yōmei">Emperor Yōmei</a> (r.585–587), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kogyoku_Tenno" title="Kogyoku Tenno">Saimei</a> (r.594–661), and finally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenji_Emperor" title="Tenji Emperor">Tenji</a> (r.668–671) during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taika_Reforms" title="Taika Reforms">Taika Reforms</a> and the time of <a href="/wiki/Fujiwara_no_Kamatari" title="Fujiwara no Kamatari">Fujiwara no Kamatari</a> (614–669). The second period covers the end of the seventh century, coinciding with the popularity of <a href="/wiki/Kakinomoto_no_Hitomaro" title="Kakinomoto no Hitomaro">Kakinomoto no Hitomaro</a>, one of Japan's greatest poets. The third period spans 700–c.730 and covers the works of such poets as <a href="/wiki/Yamabe_no_Akahito" title="Yamabe no Akahito">Yamabe no Akahito</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ctomo_no_Tabito" title="Ōtomo no Tabito">Ōtomo no Tabito</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yamanoue_no_Okura" title="Yamanoue no Okura">Yamanoue no Okura</a>. The fourth period spans 730–760 and includes the work of the last great poet of this collection, the compiler Ōtomo no Yakamochi himself, who not only wrote many original poems but also edited, updated and refashioned an unknown number of ancient poems.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_significance">Linguistic significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Man%27y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Linguistic significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to its artistic merits the <i>Man'yōshū</i> is important for using one of the earliest Japanese writing systems, the cumbersome <i><a href="/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dgana" title="Man'yōgana">man'yōgana</a></i>. Though it was not the first use of this writing system, which was also used in the earlier <a href="/wiki/Kojiki" title="Kojiki">Kojiki</a> (712), it was influential enough to give the writing system its name: "the <a href="/wiki/Kana" title="Kana">kana</a> of the <i>Man'yōshū</i>". This system uses Chinese characters in a variety of functions: their usual <a href="/wiki/Logogram" title="Logogram">logographic</a> sense; to represent Japanese syllables phonetically; and sometimes in a combination of these functions. The use of Chinese characters to represent Japanese syllables was in fact the genesis of the modern syllabic kana writing systems, being simplified forms (<i><a href="/wiki/Hiragana" title="Hiragana">hiragana</a></i>) or fragments (<i><a href="/wiki/Katakana" title="Katakana">katakana</a></i>) of the <i>man'yōgana</i>.</p>
<p>The collection, particularly volumes 14 and 20, is also highly valued by historical linguists for the information it provides on Japanese dialects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Translations">Translations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Man%27y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Translations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Julius_Klaproth" title="Julius Klaproth">Julius Klaproth</a> produced some early, severely flawed translations of <i>Man'yōshū</i> poetry. <a href="/wiki/Donald_Keene" title="Donald Keene">Donald Keene</a> explained in a preface to the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Society_for_the_Promotion_of_Science" title="Japan Society for the Promotion of Science">Nihon Gakujutsu Shinkō Kai</a> edition of the <i>Man'yōshū:</i></p>
<dl>
<dd>"One 'envoy' (hanka) to a long poem was translated as early as 1834 by the celebrated German orientalist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heinrich_Julius_Klaproth" title="Heinrich Julius Klaproth">Heinrich Julius Klaproth</a> (1783–1835). Klaproth, having journeyed to Siberia in pursuit of strange languages, encountered some Japanese castaways, fisherman, hardly ideal mentors for the study of 8th century poetry. Not surprisingly, his translation was anything but accurate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The <i>Man'yōshū</i> has been accepted in the Japanese Translation Series of the United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (UNESCO).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mokkan">Mokkan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Man%27y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mokkan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A total of three wooden fragments known as <i>mokkan</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">木簡</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> containing text from the <i>Man'yōshū</i> have been excavated:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>From the <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_site" title="Archaeological site">archaeological site</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kizugawa,_Kyoto" title="Kizugawa, Kyoto">Kizugawa, Kyoto</a>. A 23.4 cm long, 2.4 cm wide, 1.2 cm deep fragment. Dated between 750 and 780, it contains the first eleven characters of poem #2205 (volume 10) written in <a href="/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dgana" title="Man'yōgana">Man'yōgana</a>. Inspection with an infrared camera indicates other characters suggesting that it was used for writing practice</li>
<li>From the Miyamachi archaeological site in <a href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dka,_Shiga" title="Kōka, Shiga">Kōka, Shiga</a>. A 2 cm wide, 1 mm deep fragment was discovered in 1997 and is dated to mid 8th century. It contains poem #3807 (volume 16).</li>
<li>From the Ishigami archaeological site in <a href="/wiki/Asuka,_Nara" title="Asuka, Nara">Asuka, Nara</a>. A 9.1 cm long, 5.5 cm wide, 6 mm deep fragment was found. Dated to the late 7th century, it is the oldest of the known <i>Man'yōshū</i> fragments. It contains the first 14 characters of poem #1391 (volume 7) written in Man'yōgana.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Man%27y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>More than 150 <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of grasses and trees are included in 1500 entries of <i>Man'yōshū</i>. More than 30 of the species are found at the <i>Man'yō Botanical Garden</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E4%B8%87%E8%91%89%E6%A4%8D%E7%89%A9%E5%9C%92" title="ja:万葉植物園">万葉植物園</a></span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Manyō shokubutsu-en</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> in Japan, collectively placing them with the name and associated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tanka_(poetry)" title="Tanka (poetry)">tanka</a> for visitors to read and observe, reminding them of the ancient time in which the references were made. The first <i>Manyo shokubutsu-en</i> opened in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kasuga_Shrine" title="Kasuga Shrine">Kasuga Shrine</a> in 1932.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
