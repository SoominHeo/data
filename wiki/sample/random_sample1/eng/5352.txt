<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Terminal illness</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">This article is about fatal diseases. For the alternative definition of eyestrain, see <a href="/wiki/Computer_vision_syndrome" title="Computer vision syndrome">computer vision syndrome</a>. For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Terminal_(disambiguation)" title="Terminal (disambiguation)">Terminal</a>.</div>
<p><b>Terminal illness</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disease</a> that cannot be cured or adequately treated and that is reasonably expected to result in the <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a> of the patient within a short period of time. This term is more commonly used for progressive diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> or advanced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_disease" title="Heart disease">heart disease</a> than for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_trauma" title="Physical trauma">trauma</a>. In popular use, it indicates a disease that will soon progress until death with near absolute certainty, regardless of treatment.</p>
<p>A patient who has such an illness may be referred to as a <b>terminal patient</b>, <b>terminally ill</b> or simply <b>terminal</b>. Often, a patient is considered terminally ill when their estimated life expectancy is six months or less, under the assumption that the disease will run its normal course. The six-month standard is arbitrary,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-titleTerminal_Illness_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-titleTerminal_Illness-1">[1]</a></sup> and best available estimates of longevity may be incorrect. Consequently, though a given patient may properly be considered terminal, this is not a guarantee that the patient will die within six months. Similarly, a patient with a slowly progressing disease, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a>, may not be considered terminally ill because the best estimates of longevity were greater than six months. However, this does not guarantee that the patient will not die unexpectedly early. In general, physicians slightly overestimate the survival time of terminally ill cancer patients, so that, for example, a person who is expected to live for about six weeks would likely die around four weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid12881260_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid12881260-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terminal_illness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/End-of-life_care" title="End-of-life care">End-of-life care</a></div>
<p>By definition, there is not a cure or adequate treatment for terminal illnesses. However, some kinds of medical treatments may be appropriate anyway, such as treatment to reduce pain or ease breathing.</p>
<p>Some terminally ill patients stop all debilitating treatments to reduce unwanted side effects. Others continue aggressive treatment in the hope of an unexpected success. Still others reject conventional medical treatment and pursue <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unproven_cancer_therapy" title="Unproven cancer therapy">unproven treatments</a> such as radical dietary modifications. Patients' choices about different treatments may change over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17608872_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17608872-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Palliative_care" title="Palliative care">Palliative care</a> is normally offered to terminally ill patients, regardless of their overall disease management style, if it seems likely to help manage symptoms such as pain and improve quality of life. Hospice care, which can be provided at home or in a long-term care facility, additionally provides emotional and spiritual support for the patient and loved ones. Some <a href="/wiki/Alternative_medicine" title="Alternative medicine">alternative medicine</a> approaches, such as relaxation therapy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Massage" title="Massage">massage</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Acupuncture" title="Acupuncture">acupuncture</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> may relieve some symptoms and other causes of suffering.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caregiving">Caregiving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terminal_illness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Caregiving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For the person with a terminal illness, a <a href="/wiki/Companion_(caregiving)" title="Companion (caregiving)">caregiver</a> is often needed. The caregiver may be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nurse" title="Nurse">nurse</a>, licensed practical nurse or a family member. The individual may require assistance from a caregiver to receive medications for pain and to control symptoms of <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a>. The caregiver can assist the individual with daily living activities and assist with movement. Caregivers provide assistance with food and <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychological</a> support and ensure that the individual is comfortable.</p>
<p>The patient's family may have questions and most caregivers can provide information to help ease the mind. Doctors generally do not provide estimates for fear of instilling false hopes or obliterate an individual's hope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In most cases, the caregiver works along with physicians and follows professional instructions. Caregivers may call the physician or a nurse if the individual:</p>
<ul>
<li>experiences excessive pain.</li>
<li>is in distress or having difficulty <a href="/wiki/Breathing" title="Breathing">breathing</a>.</li>
<li>has difficulty passing urine or is <a href="/wiki/Constipation" title="Constipation">constipated</a>.</li>
<li>has fallen and appears hurt.</li>
<li>is depressed and wants to harm themselves.</li>
<li>refuses to take prescribed medications, raising <a href="/wiki/Medical_ethics" title="Medical ethics">ethical</a> concerns best addressed by a person with more extensive formal training.</li>
<li>or if the caregiver does not know how to handle the situation.</li>
</ul>
<p>Most caregivers become the patient's listeners and let the individual express fears and concerns without judgment. Caregivers reassure the patient and honor all advance directives. Caregivers respect the individual's need for privacy and usually hold all information confidential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Refusal_of_nutrition_and_hydration">Refusal of nutrition and hydration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terminal_illness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Refusal of nutrition and hydration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>People who feel they are near the end of their life often refuse food and/or water. Published studies indicate that "within the context of adequate palliative care, the refusal of food and fluids does not contribute to suffering among the terminally ill", and might actually contribute to a comfortable passage from life: "At least for some persons, starvation does correlate with reported <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euphoria_(emotion)" title="Euphoria (emotion)">euphoria</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dying">Dying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terminal_illness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Patients, healthcare workers, and recently bereaved family members often describe a "good death" in terms of effective choices made in a few areas:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10819707_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10819707-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Assurance of effective pain and symptom management.</li>
<li>Education about death and its aftermath, especially as it relates to decision-making.</li>
<li>Completion of any significant goals, such as resolving past conflicts.</li>
</ul>
<p>People who are terminally ill may not always follow recognizable <a href="/wiki/K%C3%BCbler-Ross_model" title="Kübler-Ross model">stages of grief</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-titleTerminal_illness:_Interacting_with_a_terminally_ill_loved_one_-_MayoClinic.com_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-titleTerminal_illness:_Interacting_with_a_terminally_ill_loved_one_-_MayoClinic.com-11">[11]</a></sup> For example, a person who finds strength in <a href="/wiki/Denial" title="Denial">denial</a> may never reach a point of acceptance or accommodation and may react negatively to any statement that threatens this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_mechanism" title="Defense mechanism">defense mechanism</a>. Other people find comfort in arranging their financial and legal affairs or planning their <a href="/wiki/Funeral" title="Funeral">funerals</a>.</p>
