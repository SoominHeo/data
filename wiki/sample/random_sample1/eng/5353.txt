<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">End-of-life care</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nursing" title="Nursing">nursing</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Allied_health_professions" title="Allied health professions">allied health professions</a>, <b>end-of-life care</b> (or <b>EoLC</b>) refers to <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a>, not only of patients in the final hours or days of their lives, but more broadly care of all those with a <a href="/wiki/Terminal_illness" title="Terminal illness">terminal illness</a> or terminal disease condition that has become advanced, progressive and incurable.</p>
<p>End-of-life care requires a range of decisions, including questions of <a href="/wiki/Palliative_care" title="Palliative care">palliative care</a>, patients' right to self-determination (of treatment, life), medical experimentation, the ethics and efficacy of extraordinary or hazardous medical interventions, and the ethics and efficacy even of continued routine medical interventions. In addition, end-of-life often touches upon <a href="/wiki/Rationing" title="Rationing">rationing</a> and the allocation of resources in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hospitals" title="Hospitals">hospitals</a> and national medical systems. Such decisions are informed both by technical, medical considerations, economic factors as well as <a href="/wiki/Bioethics" title="Bioethics">bioethics</a>. In addition, end-of-life treatments are subject to considerations of <a href="/wiki/Medical_ethics" title="Medical ethics">patient autonomy</a>. "Ultimately, it is still up to patients and their families to determine when to pursue aggressive treatment or withdraw <a href="/wiki/Life_support" title="Life support">life support</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-test-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_perspectives">National perspectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=End-of-life_care&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: National perspectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=End-of-life_care&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2012, Statistics Canada's <i>General Social Survey on Caregiving and care receiving</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> found that 13% of Canadians (3.7 million) aged 15 reported that at some point in their lives they had provided end-of-life or palliative care to a family member or friend. For those in their 50s and 60s, the percentage was higher, with about 20% reporting having provided palliative care to a family member or friend. Women were also more likely to have provided palliative care over their lifetimes, with 16% of women reporting having done so, compared with 10% of men. These caregivers helped terminally ill family members or friends with personal or medical care, food preparation, managing finances or providing transportation to and from medical appointments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=End-of-life_care&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>End of life care has been identified by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_Department_of_Health" title="UK Department of Health">UK Department of Health</a> as an area where quality of care has previously been "very variable", and which has not had a high profile in the NHS and social care. To address this, a national end of life care programme was established in 2004 to identify and propagate best practice,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and a national strategy document published in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> has also published a national strategy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006 just over half a million people died in England, about 99% of them adults over the age of 18, and almost two-thirds adults over the age of 75. About three-quarters of deaths could be considered "predictable" and followed a period of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronic_illness" title="Chronic illness">chronic illness</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats_uk_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-stats_uk-10">[10]</a></sup> – for example <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_disease" title="Heart disease">heart disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a>. In all, 58% of deaths occurred in an NHS hospital, 18% at home, 17% in residential care homes (most commonly people over the age of 85), and about 4% in hospices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats_uk_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-stats_uk-10">[10]</a></sup> However a majority of people would prefer to die at home or in a hospice, and according to one survey less than 5% would rather die in hospital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats_uk_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-stats_uk-10">[10]</a></sup> A key aim of the strategy therefore is to reduce the needs for dying patients to have to go to hospital and/or to have to stay there; and to improve provision for support and palliative care in the community to make this possible. One study estimated that 40% of the patients who had died in hospital had not had medical needs which required them to be there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats_uk_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-stats_uk-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015 and 2010, the UK ranked highest globally in a study of end-of-life care. The 2015 study said "Its ranking is due to comprehensive national policies, the extensive integration of palliative care into the <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">National Health Service</a>, a strong hospice movement, and deep community engagement on the issue." The studies were carried out by the <a href="/wiki/Economist_Intelligence_Unit" title="Economist Intelligence Unit">Economist Intelligence Unit</a> and commissioned by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lien_Foundation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lien Foundation (page does not exist)">Lien Foundation</a>, a Singaporean philanthropic organisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=End-of-life_care&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Hospice_care_in_the_United_States" title="Hospice care in the United States">Hospice care in the United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Connor_(psychologist)" title="Stephen Connor (psychologist)">Stephen Connor (psychologist)</a></div>
<p>Estimates show that about 27% of <a href="/wiki/Medicare_(United_States)" title="Medicare (United States)">Medicare</a>'s annual $327 billion budget ($88 billion) in 2006 goes to care for patients in their final year of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-last_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-last-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It represents 22% of all medical spending in the United States, 18% of all non-Medicare spending, and 25 percent of all <a href="/wiki/Medicaid" title="Medicaid">Medicaid</a> spending for the poor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-last_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-last-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-medical_care_and_support">Non-medical care and support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=End-of-life_care&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Non-medical care and support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_and_loved_ones">Family and loved ones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=End-of-life_care&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Family and loved ones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many times, family members are uncertain what they can do when a person is dying. Many gentle, familiar daily tasks, such as combing hair, putting lotion on delicate skin, and holding hands, are comforting and provide a meaningful method of communicating love to a dying person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Family members may be suffering emotionally due to the impending death. Their own <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fear_of_death" title="Fear of death">fear of death</a> may affect their behavior. They may feel guilty about past events in their relationship with the dying person or feel that they have been neglectful. These common emotions can result in tension, fights between family members over decisions, worsened care, and sometimes in what medical professionals call the <a href="/wiki/Daughter_from_California_syndrome" title="Daughter from California syndrome">Daughter from California syndrome</a>: a long-absent family member swooping in while a patient is dying to demand inappropriately aggressive care.</p>
<p>Family members also be coping with unrelated problems, such as physical or mental illness, emotional and relationship issues, or legal difficulties. These problems can limit their ability to be involved, civil, helpful, or present.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spiritual_care_in_end_of_life_care">Spiritual care in end of life care</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=End-of-life_care&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Spiritual care in end of life care">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pastoral_care" title="Pastoral care">Pastoral/Spiritual care</a> is of particular significance in end of life care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> 'In palliative care, responsibility for spiritual care is shared by the whole team, with leadership given by specialist practitioners such as pastoral care workers. The palliative care approach to spiritual care may, however, be transferred to other contexts and to individual practice'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Care_in_the_final_days_and_hours_of_life">Care in the final days and hours of life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=End-of-life_care&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Care in the final days and hours of life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decision_making">Decision making</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=End-of-life_care&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Decision making">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fragmented, dysfunctional, or grieving families are often unable to make timely decisions that respect the patient's wishes and values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> This can result in overtreatment, undertreatment, and other problems. For example, family members may differ over whether life extension or life quality is the main goal of treatment.</p>
<p>Family members may also be unable to grasp the inevitability of death and the risks and effects of medical and non-medical interventions. They may demand common treatments, such as antibiotics for pneumonia or drugs to reduce high blood pressure without wondering whether that person might prefer dying quickly of pneumonia or a heart attack to a long, drawn-out decline in a skilled care facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pureed_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pureed-21">[21]</a></sup> Some treatments, such as pureed foods for a person who has trouble swallowing or IV fluids for a person who is actively dying, seem harmless, but can significantly prolong the process of dying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pureed_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pureed-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_that_death_may_be_near">Signs that death may be near</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=End-of-life_care&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Signs that death may be near">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The U.S. Government <a href="/wiki/National_Cancer_Institute" title="National Cancer Institute">National Cancer Institute</a> advises that the presence of some of the following signs may indicate that death is approaching:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Drowsiness, increased sleep, and/or unresponsiveness (caused by changes in the patient's metabolism).</li>
<li>Confusion about time, place, and/or identity of loved ones; restlessness; visions of people and places that are not present; pulling at bed linens or clothing (caused in part by changes in the patient's metabolism).</li>
<li>Decreased socialization and withdrawal (caused by decreased oxygen to the brain, decreased blood flow, and mental preparation for dying).</li>
<li>Decreased need for food and fluids, and loss of appetite (caused by the body's need to conserve energy and its decreasing ability to use food and fluids properly).</li>
<li>Loss of bladder or bowel control (caused by the relaxing of muscles in the pelvic area).</li>
<li>Darkened urine or decreased amount of urine (caused by slowing of kidney function and/or decreased fluid intake).</li>
<li>Skin becoming cool to the touch, particularly the hands and feet; skin may become bluish in color, especially on the underside of the body (caused by decreased circulation to the extremities).</li>
<li>Rattling or gurgling sounds while breathing, which may be loud (<a href="/wiki/Death_rattle" title="Death rattle">death rattle</a>); breathing that is irregular and shallow; decreased number of breaths per minute; breathing that alternates between rapid and slow (caused by congestion from decreased fluid consumption, a buildup of waste products in the body, and/or a decrease in circulation to the organs).</li>
<li>Turning of the head toward a light source (caused by decreasing vision).</li>
<li>Increased difficulty controlling pain (caused by progression of the disease).</li>
<li>Involuntary movements (called <a href="/wiki/Myoclonus" title="Myoclonus">myoclonus</a>), changes in <a href="/wiki/Heart_rate" title="Heart rate">heart rate</a>, and loss of <a href="/wiki/Reflex" title="Reflex">reflexes</a> in the legs and arms are additional signs that the end of life is near.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diseases_Symptom_management">Diseases Symptom management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=End-of-life_care&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Diseases Symptom management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following are some of the most common potential problems that can arise in the last days and hours of a patient's life:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">Pain</a> -Suffering from uncontrolled pain is a significant fear of those at end of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></dt>
<dd>Typically controlled using <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a> or, in the United Kingdom, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamorphine" title="Diamorphine">diamorphine</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHS_heroin_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHS_heroin-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCP_template_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-LCP_template-26">[26]</a></sup> or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opioids" title="Opioids">opioids</a>.</dd>
<dt>Agitation</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Delirium" title="Delirium">Delirium</a>, terminal anguish, restlessness (e.g. thrashing, plucking, or twitching). Typically controlled using <a href="/wiki/Midazolam" title="Midazolam">midazolam</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCP_template_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-LCP_template-26">[26]</a></sup> or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benzodiazepines" title="Benzodiazepines">benzodiazepines</a>. <a href="/wiki/Haloperidol" title="Haloperidol">Haloperidol</a> is commonly used as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haloperidol_in_palliative_care_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haloperidol_in_palliative_care-27">[27]</a></sup> Diseases symptoms may also sometimes be alleviated by rehydration, which may reduce the effects of some toxic drug metabolites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCI_PDQ_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCI_PDQ-28">[28]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Respiratory Tract Secretions</dt>
<dd>Saliva and other fluids can accumulate in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oropharynx" title="Oropharynx">oropharynx</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_airways" title="Upper airways">upper airways</a> when patients become too weak to clear their <a href="/wiki/Throat" title="Throat">throats</a>, leading to a characteristic gurgling or rattle-like sound ("<a href="/wiki/Death_rattle" title="Death rattle">death rattle</a>"). While apparently not painful for the patient, the association of the diseases symptom with impending death can create fear and uncertainty for those at the bedside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCI_PDQ_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-NCI_PDQ-28">[28]</a></sup> The secretions may be controlled using drugs such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyoscine_hydrobromide" title="Hyoscine hydrobromide">scopolamine</a> (hyoscine),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCP_template_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-LCP_template-26">[26]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glycopyrronium" title="Glycopyrronium">glycopyrronium</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCP_template_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-LCP_template-26">[26]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Atropine" title="Atropine">atropine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCI_PDQ_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-NCI_PDQ-28">[28]</a></sup> Rattle may not be controllable if caused by deeper fluid accumulation in the bronchi or the lungs, such as occurs with pneumonia or some tumours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCI_PDQ_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-NCI_PDQ-28">[28]</a></sup></dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">Nausea</a> and vomiting</dt>
<dd>Typically controlled using <a href="/wiki/Haloperidol" title="Haloperidol">haloperidol</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haloperidol_in_palliative_care_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haloperidol_in_palliative_care-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cyclizine" title="Cyclizine">cyclizine</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCP_template_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-LCP_template-26">[26]</a></sup> or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-emetics" title="Anti-emetics">anti-emetics</a>.</dd>
<dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyspnea" title="Dyspnea">Dyspnea</a> (breathlessness)</dt>
<dd>Typically controlled using <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a> or, in the United Kingdom, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamorphine" title="Diamorphine">diamorphine</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHS_heroin_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHS_heroin-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCP_template_26-5"><a href="#cite_note-LCP_template-26">[26]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Typical care plans, such as those based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liverpool_Care_Pathway" title="Liverpool Care Pathway">Liverpool Care Pathway</a> for dying patients, pre-authorise staff to address such diseases symptoms as soon as they are needed, without needing to take time to seek further authorisation. <a href="/wiki/Subcutaneous_injection" title="Subcutaneous injection">Subcutaneous injections</a> are one preferred means of delivery when it has become difficult for patients to swallow or to take pills orally; and if repeated medication is needed, a <a href="/wiki/Syringe_driver" title="Syringe driver">syringe driver</a> (called an <a href="/wiki/Infusion_pump" title="Infusion pump">infusion pump</a> in the US) is often likely to be used, to deliver a steady low dose of medication.</p>
<p>Another means of medication delivery, available for use when the oral route is compromised, is a specialized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macy_Catheter" title="Macy Catheter">catheter</a> designed to provide comfortable and discreet administration of ongoing medications via the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rectal" title="Rectal">rectal</a> route. The catheter was developed to make rectal access more practical and provide a way to deliver and retain liquid formulations in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distal" title="Distal">distal</a> rectum so that health practitioners can leverage the established benefits of rectal <a href="/wiki/Route_of_administration" title="Route of administration">administration</a>. Its small flexible <a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">silicone</a> shaft allows the device to be placed safely and remain comfortably in the rectum for repeated administration of medications or liquids. The catheter has a small <a href="/wiki/Lumen_(anatomy)" title="Lumen (anatomy)">lumen</a>, allowing for small flush volumes to get medication to the rectum. Small volumes of medications (under 15ml) improve comfort by not stimulating the <a href="/wiki/Defecation" title="Defecation">defecation</a> response of the rectum, and can increase the overall <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(pharmacokinetics)" title="Absorption (pharmacokinetics)">absorption</a> of a given <a href="/wiki/Effective_dose_(pharmacology)" title="Effective dose (pharmacology)">dose</a> by decreasing pooling of medication and migration of medication into more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proximal" title="Proximal">proximal</a> areas of the rectum where absorption can be less effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other diseases symptoms which may occur, and may be mitigable to some extent, include <a href="/wiki/Cough" title="Cough">cough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(medical)" title="Fatigue (medical)">fatigue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fever" title="Fever">fever</a>, and in some cases <a href="/wiki/Bleeding" title="Bleeding">bleeding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCI_PDQ_28-4"><a href="#cite_note-NCI_PDQ-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
