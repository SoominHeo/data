<p>The <b>Malaysian Chinese Association (MCA)</b> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans">马来西亚华人公会</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant">馬來西亞華人公會</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Mǎláixīyà Huárén Gōnghuì</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Jyutping" title="Jyutping">Jyutping</a>: <i><span lang="yue-jyutping">maa5 loi4 sai1 aa3 waa4 jan4 gung1 wui2</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a>: <span lang="ms"><i>Persatuan Cina Malaysia</i></span>) is a uni-racial <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_Malaysia" title="List of political parties in Malaysia">political party in Malaysia</a> that seeks to represents the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Chinese" title="Malaysian Chinese">Malaysian Chinese</a> ethnicity; it is one of the three major component parties of the ruling coalition in <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Barisan_Nasional" title="Barisan Nasional">Barisan Nasional</a> (BN) in Malay, or National Front in English.</p>
<p>Along with the largest and third largest component party in BN, i.e. <a href="/wiki/United_Malays_National_Organisation" title="United Malays National Organisation">United Malays National Organisation</a> (UMNO) and <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Indian_Congress" title="Malaysian Indian Congress">Malaysian Indian Congress</a> (MIC), MCA has a significant influence over the political arena in Malaysia. Through its holding of companies such as Huaren Holdings, MCA controls two significant newspapers, (<a href="/wiki/The_Star_(Malaysia)" title="The Star (Malaysia)"><i>The Star</i></a>) which is Malaysia's best-selling English newspaper, and <i><a href="/wiki/Nanyang_Siang_Pau" title="Nanyang Siang Pau">Nanyang Siang Pau</a></i> which is one of the best-selling Chinese newspapers in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Malaysia" title="West Malaysia">West Malaysia</a>.</p>
<p>The party was once the largest party representing the Chinese community in Malaysia, but has performed poorly in elections since 2008, with the Chinese Malaysian community mostly voting for the opposition <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_Action_Party" title="Democratic Action Party">Democratic Action Party</a> (DAP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_early_years">Formation and early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_Chinese_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation and early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tcl.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1353" data-file-width="843" height="273" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Tcl.jpg/170px-Tcl.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Tcl.jpg/255px-Tcl.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Tcl.jpg/340px-Tcl.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tcl.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Tan_Cheng_Lock" title="Tan Cheng Lock">Tan Cheng Lock</a>, first president of MCA</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Malayan Chinese Association (MCA) was formed on 27 February 1949 with the implicit support by the post-World War II British colonial administration. A central purpose of the MCA at the time of its founding was to manage the specific social and welfare concerns of the populations interned in the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Village" title="New Village">New Villages</a> created under the <a href="/wiki/Briggs%27_Plan" title="Briggs' Plan">Briggs' Plan</a> in response to the <a href="/wiki/Malayan_Emergency" title="Malayan Emergency">Malayan Emergency</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gin2009_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gin2009-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The declaration that announced the MCA as a formal political party in 1951 was written by a prominent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straits_Chinese" title="Straits Chinese">Straits Chinese</a> businessman, <a href="/wiki/Tan_Cheng_Lock" title="Tan Cheng Lock">Tan Cheng Lock</a>, its first president. In general, its early members were landowners, businessmen, or otherwise better off, while the working classes in the New Villages overwhelmingly joined the <a href="/wiki/Malayan_Peoples%27_Socialist_Front" title="Malayan Peoples' Socialist Front">Socialist Front</a> instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Many prominent members of the MCA were also <a href="/wiki/Kuomintang" title="Kuomintang">Kuomintang</a> (KMT) members opposed to the <a href="/wiki/Malayan_Communist_Party" title="Malayan Communist Party">Malayan Communist Party</a>. <a href="/wiki/Leong_Yew_Koh" title="Leong Yew Koh">Leong Yew Koh</a>, was a KMT major general who became a cabinet minister and later became governor of <a href="/wiki/Malacca" title="Malacca">Malacca</a>; Malaysia's first minister of finance, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_H.S._Lee" title="Henry H.S. Lee">Henry H.S. Lee</a>, was a KMT colonel; and <a href="/wiki/Lim_Chong_Eu" title="Lim Chong Eu">Lim Chong Eu</a>, the leader of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Radical_Party_(Malaya)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Radical Party (Malaya) (page does not exist)">Radical Party</a>, and joined the MCA in 1952, was a colonel (medical) doctor in the Kuomintang.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1952, MCA joined force with <a href="/wiki/United_Malays_National_Organisation" title="United Malays National Organisation">United Malays National Organisation</a> (UMNO) on a local level to contest the <a href="/wiki/Kuala_Lumpur" title="Kuala Lumpur">Kuala Lumpur</a> municipal elections which would lead to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_Party_(Malaysia)" title="Alliance Party (Malaysia)">Alliance Party</a>. The alliance was joined by <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Indian_Congress" title="Malaysian Indian Congress">Malaysian Indian Congress</a> (MIC) in 1954 and they contested the <a href="/wiki/Malayan_general_election,_1955" title="Malayan general election, 1955">first Malayan General Election</a> in 1955 as one body, and the alliance won 51 of the 52 seat contested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> MCA won all 15 of the seats allocated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tan Cheng Lock was succeeded by Lim Chong Eu after a successful challenge by Lim for the presidency in 1958. Lim attempted to amend the party's Constitution to consolidate the power of the Central Committee, and although amendment was passed narrowly, it also split the party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Prior to the <a href="/wiki/Malayan_general_election,_1959" title="Malayan general election, 1959">1959 General Election</a>, Lim pressed for an increase of the allocated number of seats from 28 to 40, but this was refused by UMNO leader <a href="/wiki/Tunku_Abdul_Rahman" title="Tunku Abdul Rahman">Tunku Abdul Rahman</a>. Lim was forced to back down and later resigned as president, with Cheah Toon Lock taking over as acting president. Other members also resigned from MCA to contest the election as independent candidates, which cost the party some seat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The party only won 19 of the 31 seats eventually allocated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Lim himself left the party in December 1960, later becoming one of the founding members of the opposition <a href="/wiki/Parti_Gerakan_Rakyat_Malaysia" title="Parti Gerakan Rakyat Malaysia">Party Gerakan</a> in 1968. In 1961 <a href="/wiki/Tan_Siew_Sin" title="Tan Siew Sin">Tan Siew Sin</a>, son of Tan Cheng Lock and favoured by Tunku, became MCA's third President.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Tan led the party to a firm victory in the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_general_election,_1964" title="Malaysian general election, 1964">1964 General Election</a>, winning 27 of the 33 parliamentary seats contested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1969, Tan established <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tunku_Abdul_Rahman_College" title="Tunku Abdul Rahman College">Tunku Abdul Rahman College</a> after a proposal for a Chinese-language university was turned down by the government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="May_1969_.E2.80.93_1985">May 1969 – 1985</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_Chinese_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: May 1969 – 1985">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_general_election,_1969" title="Malaysian general election, 1969">third Malaysian general elections</a> were held on 10 May 1969. MCA faced strong challenges from the new, mainly Chinese, opposition parties <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_Action_Party" title="Democratic Action Party">Democratic Action Party</a> (DAP) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parti_Gerakan" title="Parti Gerakan">Parti Gerakan</a>. Of the 33 parliamentary seats contested, MCA managed to retain only 13. MCA also lost control of the <a href="/wiki/Penang_State_Legislative_Assembly" title="Penang State Legislative Assembly">Penang State Government</a> to Gerakan. The gain by the opposition parties led to tension between different communities which erupted into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/May_13_Riots" title="May 13 Riots">May 13 Riots</a>. Prior to the riots, on 12 May 1969, Tan Siew Sin announced that the party would withdraw from the Alliance, but reconsidered on 20 May and joined the <a href="/wiki/National_Operations_Council" title="National Operations Council">National Operations Council</a> formed in place of the suspended Parliament after the riots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The loss of support for MCA among the Chinese population elicited a comment by the then <a href="/wiki/Deputy_Prime_Minister_of_Malaysia" title="Deputy Prime Minister of Malaysia">Deputy Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Ismail_Abdul_Rahman" title="Ismail Abdul Rahman">Dr Ismail</a> that if MCA continue to lose support, UMNO may stop co-operating with it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ting_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ting-16">[16]</a></sup> In order to regain Chinese support, Tan attempted to broaden the appeal of the party previously seen as a party of the <i>taukeh</i> (<i><a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%A4%B4%E5%AE%B6" title="zh:头家">tou jia</a></i>, rich men), and invited professionals to join the party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ting_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-ting-16">[16]</a></sup> However, many of these were expelled after a dispute involving <a href="/wiki/Lim_Keng_Yaik" title="Lim Keng Yaik">Lim Keng Yaik</a> who then joined Gerakan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ting_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-ting-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the loss of support for MCA in the 1969 election, and the enlargement of the Alliance party in 1972 (which later became <a href="/wiki/Barisan_Nasional" title="Barisan Nasional">Barisan Nasional</a>) to include Gerakan, UMNO became even more dominant and MCA suffered a loss of status within the coalition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1973, Tan Siew Sin requested a position as Deputy Prime Minister in the cabinet reshuffle following the death of <a href="/wiki/Ismail_Abdul_Rahman" title="Ismail Abdul Rahman">Tun Dr. Ismail</a>, but this was refused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tun_Abdul_Razak" title="Tun Abdul Razak">Tun Abdul Razak</a>, which angered Tan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> On 8 April 1974, prior to the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_general_election,_1974" title="Malaysian general election, 1974">general election</a>, Tan Siew Sin resigned all of his party and government posts for health reasons.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lee_San_Choon" title="Lee San Choon">Lee San Choon</a> took over as Acting President following Tan's resignation, and was then elected President in 1975. After Tan's resignation, the cabinet posts allocated to MCA declined in importance, and MCA lost both the Finance Ministry and Trade and Industry Ministry posts it once held in 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The party performed better in the 1974 election, but lost ground again in the following <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_general_election,_1978" title="Malaysian general election, 1978">1978 general election</a>, with the MCA winning only 17 of the 28 parliamentary seats and 44 of the 60 state seats. In 1979, Michael Chen stood against Lee San Choon for the MCA Presidency but lost, and later in 1981 led a group of MCA dissidents to join Gerakan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_general_election,_1982" title="Malaysian general election, 1982">1982 general election</a> however saw a shift in fortune for MCA. Lee accepted a challenge from the opposition <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_Action_Party" title="Democratic Action Party">Democratic Action Party</a> which taunted the MCA's leadership for not daring to contest a seat with large urban Chinese majority, and contested the parliamentary seat for <a href="/wiki/Seremban" title="Seremban">Seremban</a> against the incumbent DAP Chairman Chen Man Hin. Lee won his challenge, and led his party to a resounding victory, winning 24 out of 28 allocated parliamentary seats and 55 out of 62 state seats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> After the success in the election and at the height of his career, Lee San Choon unexpectedly resigned his presidency and cabinet post for unspecified reason in 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Neo Yee Pan then led as Acting President until 1985.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1985.E2.80.932003">1985–2003</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_Chinese_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1985–2003">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1985, <a href="/wiki/Tan_Koon_Swan" title="Tan Koon Swan">Tan Koon Swan</a>, who was sacked from the party a year earlier, won the presidential election with the largest majority in the party's history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> However, in the following year, he was charged with abetting criminal breach of trust relating to his private business dealings in Singapore, and resigned from the presidency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Koon Swan also originated the Deposit-Taking <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">Cooperatives</a> (DTCs), which sought to accumulate capital for Chinese Malaysians through investments. The mismanagement of the DTCs' funds led to a scandal, with the central bank, <a href="/wiki/Bank_Negara_Malaysia" title="Bank Negara Malaysia">Bank Negara Malaysia</a>, stepping in to freeze the assets of up to 35 DTCs. The total loss was estimated to be RM3.6 billion, and the depositors only recovered 62% of their deposits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Koon Swan was succeeded by his deputy <a href="/wiki/Ling_Liong_Sik" title="Ling Liong Sik">Ling Liong Sik</a> in 1986. He assumed the presidency when the party was still rife with factionalism and faced disillusionment with the Chinese community over the Deposit-Taking Cooperatives scandal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Ling spent his early years as president working to resolve MCA's financial problems, raising funds while restructuring the party's assets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ling_liong_sik_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ling_liong_sik-30">[30]</a></sup> Ling presided over a period of relative peace within the party, and worked to maintain the interests of the Chinese community through a closed-door approach within the government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tussle_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-tussle-31">[31]</a></sup> He expanded the MCA-owned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tunku_Abdul_Rahman_College" title="Tunku Abdul Rahman College">Tunku Abdul Rahman College</a> through fund-raising and government contributions, and in 2001 set up <a href="/wiki/Universiti_Tunku_Abdul_Rahman" title="Universiti Tunku Abdul Rahman">Universiti Tunku Abdul Rahman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ling_liong_sik_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-ling_liong_sik-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tussle_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-tussle-31">[31]</a></sup> Ling led MCA to its best electoral performance thus far in the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_general_election,_1995" title="Malaysian general election, 1995">1995 General Election</a>, winning 30 of the 34 allocated parliamentary seats and 71 of the 77 state seats, and secured a majority of Chinese votes at the expense of DAP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> MCA also performed well in the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_general_election,_1999" title="Malaysian general election, 1999">1999 General Elections</a>, and the successive electoral victory boosted the party's standing within the Barisan Nasional coalition as well as Ling's personal relationship with BN leader and prime minister <a href="/wiki/Mahathir_Mohamad" title="Mahathir Mohamad">Mahathir Mohamad</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ling_liong_sik_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-ling_liong_sik-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tussle_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-tussle-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Internal power however struggles persisted. In 1993 Ling's deputy <a href="/wiki/Lee_Kim_Sai" title="Lee Kim Sai">Lee Kim Sai</a> indicated that he would challenged Ling for the presidency, but withdrew at the eleventh hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Lee eventually retired in 1996 and was replaced as deputy president by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lim_Ah_Lek&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lim Ah Lek (page does not exist)">Lim Ah Lek</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-save_MCA_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-save_MCA-35">[35]</a></sup> In 1999, the party was again wracked by factionalism. Deputy president Lim Ah Lek announced his intention to retire as a minister and agreed with Ling to nominate his protégé <a href="/wiki/Chan_Kong_Choy" title="Chan Kong Choy">Chan Kong Choy</a> to the Cabinet after the 1999 elections. However, Ling nominated his own protégé <a href="/wiki/Ong_Ka_Ting" title="Ong Ka Ting">Ong Ka Ting</a> as a minister at the expense of Chan, causing discontent with members aligned to Lim, which became known as "Team B" among party members. The Ling faction was known as "Team A."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tussle_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-tussle-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-save_MCA_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-save_MCA-35">[35]</a></sup> Tensions flared further after MCA, through its holding company Huaren, moved to acquire the independent daily <i><a href="/wiki/Nanyang_Siang_Pau" title="Nanyang Siang Pau">Nanyang Siang Pau</a></i>. This was vehemently opposed by Team B, fearing a complete control of the Chinese media by Team A. They were joined by Chinese journalists and non-governmental organisations, who made their opposition public through demonstrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tussle_31-4"><a href="#cite_note-tussle-31">[31]</a></sup> The situation turned farcical when chairs were thrown during the 2001 Youth general assembly over the issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Huaren eventually succeeded in taking over <i>Nanyang Siang Pau</i>. Huaren also controls <i><a href="/wiki/The_Star_(Malaysia)" title="The Star (Malaysia)">The Star</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/China_Press" title="China Press">China Press</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and the domination of media press caused strong resentments in the divided party, with accusations by factions within MCA as well as outside opponents of editorial interference and claims of a threat to the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_press" title="Freedom of the press">freedom of the press</a> in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mahathir, as BN leader, eventually stepped in to resolve the conflict, suggesting a "peace plan" among the factions. The scheduled 2002 party elections were cancelled, while Ling and Lim stepped down to be replaced by their respective protégés.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tussle_31-5"><a href="#cite_note-tussle-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2003.E2.80.932008">2003–2008</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_Chinese_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2003–2008">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 2003, the leadership transition occurred as planned. Ong Ka Ting, who was then a vice-president succeeded Ling Liong Sik as president, while Chan Kong Choy succeeded Lim Ah Lek as deputy president. The Ong-led MCA contributed to <a href="/wiki/Barisan_Nasional" title="Barisan Nasional">Barisan Nasional</a>'s overwhelming victory in the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_general_election,_2004" title="Malaysian general election, 2004">2004 general elections</a>. MCA won 31 of the 40 parliamentary seats and 76 of the 90 state seats allocated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> During the 2005 party elections, Teams A and B ran on a united front, easily quashing the challenge by vice-president <a href="/wiki/Chua_Jui_Meng" title="Chua Jui Meng">Chua Jui Meng</a> (for president) and secretary-general <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ting_Chew_Peh&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ting Chew Peh (page does not exist)">Ting Chew Peh</a> (for deputy president).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tussle_31-6"><a href="#cite_note-tussle-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Ong-Chan leadership continued the soft approach to protecting the Chinese community's interests,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tussle_31-7"><a href="#cite_note-tussle-31">[31]</a></sup> although tension with UMNO over racial issues flared up now and again after the 2004 election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In early 2008, vice-president and Health Minister <a href="/wiki/Chua_Soi_Lek" title="Chua Soi Lek">Chua Soi Lek</a>, a prominent <a href="/wiki/Johor" title="Johor">Johor</a> member, was involved in a sex scandal. DVDs of Chua having sex with a woman were circulated in Johor, prompting Chua to resign all his political positions, including as Member of Parliament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Chua suggested that his political enemies within the party who might have felt threatened by him for plotting his downfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_general_election,_2008" title="Malaysian general election, 2008">March 2008 general elections</a>, MCA fared badly, winning only 15 parliamentary seats and 32 state seats, less than half the number of seats they won in the previous election. Ong decided not to contest the presidency during the party elections later that year, to allow a new leader to take over. The October 2008 party election marked a realignment of the party's factions, with the return of Chua Soi Lek to the fold. Ong Ka Ting's anointed successor was vice-president <a href="/wiki/Ong_Tee_Keat" title="Ong Tee Keat">Ong Tee Keat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Meanwhile, Chua entered the race for deputy president, facing among others, <a href="/wiki/Ong_Ka_Chuan" title="Ong Ka Chuan">Ong Ka Chuan</a>, the elder brother of Ka Ting. Ong Tee Keat won the presidency comfortably, while Chua edged out Ka Chuan. Following his victory, Tee Keat pledged reform and reaching out to more young voters to revive the party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008.E2.80.93present">2008–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_Chinese_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2008–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the 2008 leadership change, factional infighting continued and the relationship between the Ong Tee Keat and Chua Soi Lek remained tense. Chua was sidelined by Ong from taking an active role in the party's leadership, and he was also excluded from government posts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> He was then sacked by MCA in August 2009 for damaging the party's image with his sex scandal more than a year prior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In response, Chua's supporters forced an extraordinary general meeting (EGM) which passed a vote of no confidence against incumbent president Ong and annulled the expulsion of Chua. The EGM, however, failed to reinstate Chua as deputy president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Ong refused to resign despite the vote of no confidence, but pledged with Chua to set aside their differences under the "greater unity plan."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> However, this was opposed by vice-president <a href="/wiki/Liow_Tiong_Lai" title="Liow Tiong Lai">Liow Tiong Lai</a> who demanded Ong step down and that new elections be held.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> This set in motion a new leadership crisis, which lasted almost six months.</p>
<p>Finally in March 2010, Chua, along with his supporters in the central committee (CC) resigned. Along with the resignations of Liow's supporters in the CC, more than two-thirds of the CC had vacated their seats, paving the way for an election per the party constitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The subsequent election saw Chua defeating incumbent Ong Tee Keat and former leader Ong Ka Ting in the race for president, while Liow defeated <a href="/wiki/Kong_Cho_Ha" title="Kong Cho Ha">Kong Cho Ha</a> in the contest for deputy president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Chua and his deputy Liow pledged to co-operate, and opened the party to non-Chinese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_general_election,_2013" title="Malaysian general election, 2013">2013 General Election</a>, MCA suffered its worst defeat ever, winning only 7 of the 37 parliamentary seats and 11 of the 90 state seats it contested, leading to calls for Chua's resignation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> MCA's poor performance in the two elections, along with continued factionalism, raised concerns over the party's relevance in the Malaysian political arena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Also as a result of its poor performance, there was no MCA representation in the cabinet for the first time since independence due to a resolution that MCA would not accept cabinet posts if it performed badly in the general election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nst_mca_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-nst_mca-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chua did not enter the following party poll for president, and in December 2013, <a href="/wiki/Liow_Tiong_Lai" title="Liow Tiong Lai">Liow Tiong Lai</a> was elected the president of MCA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Liow also reversed the resolution not to serve in the government and re-entered the cabinet in the aftermath of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MH370" title="MH370">MH370</a> incident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nst_mca_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-nst_mca-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
