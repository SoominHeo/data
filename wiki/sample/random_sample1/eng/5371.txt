<p><b>Vespoidea</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">superfamily</a> of <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenoptera</a> of <a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">class</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insecta" title="Insecta">Insecta</a>, although older <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomic</a> schemes may vary in this categorization, particularly in their recognition of a now-obsolete superfamily <b>Scolioidea</b>. The members of this group are known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wasps" title="Wasps">wasps</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ants" title="Ants">ants</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phylogenetics">Phylogenetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vespoidea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phylogenetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Newer research based on four <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_gene" title="Nuclear gene">nuclear genes</a> (elongation factor-1α F2 copy, long-wavelength <a href="/wiki/Rhodopsin" title="Rhodopsin">rhodopsin</a>, wingless and the D2–D3 regions of 28S <a href="/wiki/Ribosomal_RNA" title="Ribosomal RNA">ribosomal RNA</a>—2700 bp in total) suggests the higher-level relationships need to be changed, with Rhopalosomatidae as a <a href="/wiki/Sister_group" title="Sister group">sister group</a> of the Vespidae and the clade Rhopalosomatidae + Vespidae as sister to all other vespoids and apoids. Additionally, superfamily Apoidea is found to be within the Vespoidea, suggesting the dismantling of Vespoidea (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensu_lato" title="Sensu lato">sensu lato</a></i>) into many smaller superfamilies; Formicoidea, Scolioidea, Tiphioidea, Thynnoidea, and Pompiloidea in addition to a much more narrowly defined Vespoidea. Finally, families Mutillidae, Tiphiidae, and Bradynobaenidae were found to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" title="Paraphyletic">paraphyletic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pilgrim2008_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pilgrim2008-4">[4]</a></sup> Another recent study confirms the need for revision of high-level relationships, although the pattern of sister-group relationships within the putative Vespoidea matches the same basic pattern as the 2008 study, including a paraphyletic Bradynobaenidae and Tiphiidae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson2013_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson2013-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
