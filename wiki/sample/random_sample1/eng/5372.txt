<p><b>Malbork</b> <span class="IPA" style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Polish" title="Help:IPA for Polish">[ˈmalbɔrk]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:Pl-Malbork.ogg" title="File:Pl-Malbork.ogg"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/ff/Pl-Malbork.ogg" title="Pl-Malbork.ogg">listen</a>)</small> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Marienburg</i></span> <small>(<img alt="Ltspkr.png" data-file-height="10" data-file-width="13" height="10" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0b/Ltspkr.png" width="13"/> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9a/Marienburg.ogg" title="Marienburg.ogg">listen</a>)</small>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Civitas Beatae Virginis</i></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">town</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zulawy" title="Zulawy">Żuławy</a> region (<a href="/wiki/Vistula" title="Vistula">Vistula</a> delta), with 38,478 inhabitants (2006). Situated in the <a href="/wiki/Pomeranian_Voivodeship" title="Pomeranian Voivodeship">Pomeranian Voivodeship</a> since 1999, it was previously assigned to <a href="/wiki/Elbl%C4%85g_Voivodeship" title="Elbląg Voivodeship">Elbląg Voivodeship</a> (1975–1998). It is the capital of <a href="/wiki/Malbork_County" title="Malbork County">Malbork County</a>.</p>
<p>Founded in the 13th century by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knights_of_the_Teutonic_Order" title="Knights of the Teutonic Order">Knights of the Teutonic Order</a>, the town is noted for its medieval <a href="/wiki/Malbork_Castle" title="Malbork Castle">Malbork Castle</a>, built in the 13th Century as the Order's headquarters and of what later became known as <a href="/wiki/Royal_Prussia" title="Royal Prussia">Royal Prussia</a>.</p>
<p>Marlbork is most famously known as being the birthplace of Polish Pop sensation Radek "Shizmay" Kaluzny. He has had worldwide hits with "Pow! Pow! Pow!" and "Straight Outta Malbork".</p>

<p>The town was built in <a href="/wiki/Prussia_(region)" title="Prussia (region)">Prussia</a> around the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fortress" title="Fortress">fortress</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordensburg_Marienburg" title="Ordensburg Marienburg">Ordensburg Marienburg</a>, which was founded in 1274 on the east bank of the river <a href="/wiki/Nogat" title="Nogat">Nogat</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teutonic_Knights" title="Teutonic Knights">Teutonic Knights</a>. Both the castle and the town (named Marienburg in German and Malborg or Malbork in Polish) were named for their patron saint, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" title="Virgin Mary">Virgin Mary</a>. This fortified <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a> became the seat of the Teutonic Order and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>'s largest <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> fortress. During the <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Years%27_War_(1454%E2%80%9366)" title="Thirteen Years' War (1454–66)">Thirteen Years' War</a>, the castle of Marienburg was pawned by the Teutonic Order to their imperial soldiers from <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>. They sold the castle in 1457 to King <a href="/wiki/Casimir_IV_Jagiellon" title="Casimir IV Jagiellon">Casimir IV</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Poland_(1385%E2%80%931569)" title="Kingdom of Poland (1385–1569)">Poland</a> in lieu of indemnities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-segfozhek184_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-segfozhek184-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under continuous construction for nearly 230 years, the castle complex is actually three castles combined in one. A classic example of a medieval fortress, it is the world’s largest brick castle and one of the most impressive of its kind in Europe. The castle was in the process of being restored by the Germans when <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> broke out. During the war, the castle was over 50% destroyed. Restoration has been ongoing since the war. However, the main cathedral in the castle, fully restored just prior to the war and destroyed during the war, remains in its ruined state. The castle and its museum are listed as one of <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Sites" title="World Heritage Sites">World Heritage Sites</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_town">History of the town</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malbork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of the town">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town of Marienburg grew in the vicinity of the castle. The river <a href="/wiki/Nogat" title="Nogat">Nogat</a> and flat terrain allowed easy access for barges a hundred kilometers from the sea. During <a href="/wiki/Prussia_(region)" title="Prussia (region)">Prussia</a>'s government by the Teutonic Knights, the Order collected tolls on river traffic and imposed a monopoly on the <a href="/wiki/Amber" title="Amber">amber</a> trade. The town later became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a>, and many Hanseatic meetings were held there.</p>
<p>The Teutonic Order weakened greatly after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Grunwald" title="Battle of Grunwald">Battle of Grunwald</a> against advancing Poles and Lithuanians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Marienburg_(1410)" title="Siege of Marienburg (1410)">Siege of Marienburg (1410)</a> left the Teutonic Order in control only of the town, until it sold the castle and in 1457 transferred its seat to <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg" title="Königsberg">Königsberg</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Kaliningrad" title="Kaliningrad">Kaliningrad, Russia</a>). The town of Marienburg, under Mayor <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bartholom%C3%A4us_Blume&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bartholomäus Blume (page does not exist)">Bartholomäus Blume</a> and others, resisted the Poles for three further years. But when the Poles finally took control, Blume was hanged and quartered, and fourteen officers and three remaining Teutonic knights were thrown into dungeons, where they met a miserable end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-segfozhek184_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-segfozhek184-1">[1]</a></sup> A monument to Blume was erected in 1864.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The town became part of the Polish province of <a href="/wiki/Royal_Prussia" title="Royal Prussia">Royal Prussia</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Second_Peace_of_Thorn_(1466)" title="Second Peace of Thorn (1466)">Second Peace of Thorn (1466)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It was annexed by the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Partitions_of_Poland" title="Partitions of Poland">First Partition of Poland</a> in 1772 and became part of the Province of <a href="/wiki/West_Prussia" title="West Prussia">West Prussia</a> the following year, which became part of the newly founded <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> in 1871.</p>

<p>Under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles" title="Treaty of Versailles">Treaty of Versailles</a> after <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the inhabitants were asked in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Prussian_plebiscite" title="East Prussian plebiscite">plebiscite on July 11, 1920</a> whether they wanted to remain in Germany or join newly re-established Poland. In the town of Marienburg, 9,641 votes were cast for Germany, 165 votes for Poland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> As a result, Marienburg was included in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regierungsbezirk_Marienwerder" title="Regierungsbezirk Marienwerder">Regierungsbezirk Marienwerder</a> within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weimar_Germany" title="Weimar Germany">German</a> Province of <a href="/wiki/East_Prussia" title="East Prussia">East Prussia</a>. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weimar_Germany" title="Weimar Germany">Weimar era</a>, Marienburg was located at the tripoint between Poland, Germany and the Free State of Danzig.</p>
<p>The town was hit by an economic crisis following the end of World War I. After a brief recovery in the mid-1920s, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_depression" title="Great depression">Great Depression</a> was particularly severe in East Prussia. In January 1933, <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Hitler</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> came to power and immediately began eliminating political opponents, so that in the last semi-free elections of March 1933, 54 of Marienburg's votes went to the Nazis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>). After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_invasion_of_Poland" title="German invasion of Poland">German invasion of Poland</a> in September 1939, leaders of the Polish minority were arrested and sent to concentration camps.</p>

<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> a <a href="/wiki/Focke-Wulf" title="Focke-Wulf">Focke-Wulf</a> aircraft factory was set up at the airfield to the east of Marienburg. It was bombed twice by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">USAAF</a> in 1943 and 1944. Today the airfield belongs to the <a href="/wiki/22nd_Air_Base" title="22nd Air Base">22nd Air Base</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Polish_Air_Force" title="Polish Air Force">Polish Air Force</a>.</p>
<p>Near the end of World War II, the city was declared a <a href="/wiki/German_World_War_II_strongholds" title="German World War II strongholds">festung</a> and most of the civilian population <a href="/wiki/Evacuation_of_East_Prussia" title="Evacuation of East Prussia">fled or were evacuated</a>, with some 4,000 people opting to remain. In early 1945, Marienburg was the scene of fierce battles by the Nazis with the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a> and was almost completely destroyed. The battle lasted until March 9, 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Following the town's military capture by the Red Army, the remaining civilian population disappeared; 1,840 people remain missing. In June 1945, the town was turned over to Polish authorities who had arrived in the town in April and was permanently renamed Malbork.</p>
<p>Half a century later, in 1996, 178 corpses were found in a mass grave in Malbork; another 123 were found in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In October 2008, during excavations for the foundation of a new hotel in Malbork, a mass grave was found containing the remains of 2,116 people, a majority of whom were female. All the dead were said to have been German residents of pre-1945 Marienburg, but they could not be individually identified, nor could the cause of their deaths be definitely established. A Polish investigation concluded that the bodies, along with the remains of some dead animals, may have been buried to prevent the spread of typhus, which was extant in the turmoil at the end of World War II. On August 14, 2009, all the dead people’s remains were buried in a German military cemetery to the west of the town at <a href="/wiki/Stare_Czarnowo" title="Stare Czarnowo">Stare Czarnowo</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Neumark</i></span>) in Polish Pomerania, not far from the present-day German border.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suffering1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-suffering1-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Malbork one can also find a <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_War_Graves_Commission" title="Commonwealth War Graves Commission">Commonwealth War Graves Commission</a> cemetery <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> with 240 graves, mostly of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_War" title="Prisoner of War">POWs</a> who died in the area during both wars, especially in the World War II <a href="/wiki/Stalag_XX-B" title="Stalag XX-B">Stalag XX-B</a> camp.</p>
<p>After World War II, the town was gradually repopulated by <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Poles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish_population_transfers_(1944%E2%80%931946)" title="Polish population transfers (1944–1946)">many expelled</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish_areas_annexed_by_the_Soviet_Union" title="Polish areas annexed by the Soviet Union">Polish areas annexed by the Soviet Union</a>. In February 1946, the population of the town reached 10,017 people, then by 1965 grew further to 28,292 and by 1994 to 40,347.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mportal_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-mportal-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also following the war, the Old Town in Malbork was not rebuilt; instead the bricks from its ruins were used to rebuild the oldest sections of <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gda%C5%84sk" title="Gdańsk">Gdańsk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mportal_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-mportal-13">[13]</a></sup> As a result, with the exception of the Old Town Hall, two city gates and St. John's church, no pre-World War II buildings remain in the Old Town area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mportal_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-mportal-13">[13]</a></sup> In place of the old town, a <a href="/wiki/Housing_estate" title="Housing estate">housing estate</a> was built in the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mportal_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-mportal-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
