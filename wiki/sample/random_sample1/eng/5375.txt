<p><b>Maltose</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɔː/ 'au' in 'fraud'">ɔː</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɔː/ 'au' in 'fraud'">ɔː</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>), also known as <b>maltobiose</b> or <b>malt sugar</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Disaccharide" title="Disaccharide">disaccharide</a> formed from two units of <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> joined with an α(1→4) <a href="/wiki/Glycosidic_bond" title="Glycosidic bond">bond</a>, formed from a <a href="/wiki/Condensation_reaction" title="Condensation reaction">condensation reaction</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Isomer" title="Isomer">isomer</a> <a href="/wiki/Isomaltose" title="Isomaltose">isomaltose</a> has two glucose molecules linked through an α(1→6) bond.</p>
<p>Maltose is the second member of an important biochemical series of glucose chains. Maltose is the disaccharide produced when <a href="/wiki/Amylase" title="Amylase">amylase</a> breaks down <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>. It is found in <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germinating</a> seeds as they break down their starch stores to use for food, which is why it was named after <a href="/wiki/Malt" title="Malt">malt</a>. It is also produced when glucose is <a href="/wiki/Caramelization" title="Caramelization">caramelized</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maltose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Maltose was discovered by Irish chemist and brewer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cornelius_O%27Sullivan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cornelius O'Sullivan (page does not exist)">Cornelius O'Sullivan</a> in 1872.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Its name comes from malt, from Old English <i>mealt</i>, of Germanic origin, and the suffix '–ose', a suffix denoting names of sugars and other <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_nomenclature">Structure and nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maltose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure and nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Maltose is a <a href="/wiki/Biomolecule" title="Biomolecule">biomolecule</a> that belongs to the group of carbohydrates within the division of biomolecules into the three main groups: carbohydrates, <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>. Carbohydrates are composed of carbon, oxygen and hydrogen C, O, H and are either polyhydroxyaldehydes or polyhydroxyketones.</p>
<p>As a group, they are generally divided into <a href="/wiki/Monosaccharide" title="Monosaccharide">monosaccharides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oligosaccharide" title="Oligosaccharide">oligosaccharides</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharides</a> depending on the number of sugar subunits. Maltose is an oligosaccharide, specifically a disaccharide, formed by the union of two glucose units (<a href="/wiki/Monosaccharide" title="Monosaccharide">monosaccharide</a>). Glucose is classified as a <a href="/wiki/Hexose" title="Hexose">hexose</a> because it is composed of six carbons. The two glucoses which compose maltose are cyclized in pyran form and are joined by an <a href="/wiki/Glycosidic_bond" title="Glycosidic bond">O-glycosidic bond</a> through the first carbon (C<sub>1</sub>) of the first <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> and fourth carbon (C<sub>4</sub>) of the second <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, indicated as (1 → 4). The link is characterized as α due to the O-glycosidic bond to the anomeric carbon (C<sub>1</sub>) being in the opposite plane from the <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span>OH</span> <a href="/wiki/Substituent" title="Substituent">substituent</a> in the same ring (C<sub>6</sub> of the first glucose). If the O-glycosidic bond to the anomeric carbon (C<sub>1</sub>) was in the same plane as the <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span>OH</span> substituent, it would be classified as a β(1 → 4) bond, and the resulting molecule would be <a href="/wiki/Cellobiose" title="Cellobiose">cellobiose</a>. The anomeric carbon (C<sub>1</sub>) of the second glucose molecule, the (C<sub>1</sub>) that is not involved in an O-glycosidic bond, could be either an α- or β-anomer depending on the bond direction of the attached hydroxyl group relative to the <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span>OH</span> substituent of the same ring, resulting in either α-maltose or β-maltose.</p>
<p>Due to O-glycosidic link, maltose is a <a href="/wiki/Disaccharide" title="Disaccharide">disaccharide</a> that can reduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fehling%27s_reagent" title="Fehling's reagent">Fehling's reagent</a>. Furthermore, maltose can be obtained by <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Glycogen" title="Glycogen">glycogen</a> or <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>, polymers of linked maltoses in position α(1 → 4) and branching in position α(1 → 6). These are very abundant and form a large number of branches. <a href="/wiki/Amylase" title="Amylase">Amylase</a> enzymes produce maltose and limit dextrin. These can be further degraded by <a href="/wiki/Maltase" title="Maltase">maltase</a> enzyme to hydrolyze maltoses as glucoses and they are ready to be degrade and obtain energy in form of ATP.</p>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Isomer" title="Isomer">isomer</a> of maltose is <a href="/wiki/Isomaltose" title="Isomaltose">isomaltose</a>. This is similar to maltose but instead of bonds in position α(1 → 4), the linkage is formed in position α(1 → 6), therefore, <a href="/wiki/Glycogen" title="Glycogen">glycogen</a> branching is defined by <a href="/wiki/Isomaltose" title="Isomaltose">isomaltose</a>.maltose, isomaltose is also a <a href="/wiki/Reducing_sugar" title="Reducing sugar">reducing sugar</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maltose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Maltose has the ability to reduce the <a href="/wiki/Fehling%27s_solution" title="Fehling's solution">Fehling’s solution</a>, due to its free <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehyde</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehyde</a> group is oxidized giving a positive result, which means that the maltose is a reducing sugar.</li>
<li>Maltose in aqueous solution exhibit <a href="/wiki/Mutarotation" title="Mutarotation">mutarotation</a>, due to its anomeric carbon which can form α and β isomers. In aquose solution, it is shown a balanced way between α and β forms.</li>
<li>It has a sweet taste.</li>
<li>It is sometimes water-soluble and sometimes crystalline.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Intolerance">Intolerance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maltose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Intolerance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Homopolysaccharides of <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> are broken down by <a href="/wiki/Maltase" title="Maltase">maltase</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isomaltase" title="Isomaltase">isomaltase</a> to produce glucose. The lack of the Sucrase-Isomaltase enzyme, one of the four integral <a href="/wiki/Glycoprotein" title="Glycoprotein">glycoproteins</a>, causes <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> and maltose <a href="/wiki/Food_intolerance" title="Food intolerance">intolerance</a> and is called <a href="/wiki/Sucrose_intolerance" title="Sucrose intolerance">Congenital Sucrase-Isomaltase Deficiency</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">congenital disorder</a> is most prominent in infancy but can develop later in life. It is caused by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recessive" title="Recessive">recessive</a> autosomal <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutation</a> (mostly SI and SII). Nonetheless, depending on the type of mutation there are certain degrees of intolerance. According to this degree, <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physicians</a> may decide the amount of <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> the patient may have to remain healthy. This intolerance begins when infants begin to eat <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> that contain starch, mainly containing maltose. One can notice that a patient suffers from this <a href="/wiki/Pathology" title="Pathology">pathology</a> when they have “<a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a> cramps, bloating, excess gas production, <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a>. Upper respiratory tract and viral infections are common. These digestive problems can lead to lower than normal weight gain and growth. Possible associations are <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a> stones and copper malabsorption.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Further studies have shown that certain patients with different degrees of mutation can actually improve throughout the years, reaching a maximum improvement at ages 3 years and 6 months.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_and_absorption">Sources and absorption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maltose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sources and absorption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maltose_syrup.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1226" data-file-width="1200" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Maltose_syrup.jpg/220px-Maltose_syrup.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Maltose_syrup.jpg/330px-Maltose_syrup.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Maltose_syrup.jpg/440px-Maltose_syrup.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maltose_syrup.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Maltose syrup</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Maltose is a component of <a href="/wiki/Malt" title="Malt">malt</a>, a substance which is obtained in the process of allowing grain to soften in water and germinate. It is found in beverages, <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a>, cereal, pasta, potatoes and in many processed products which have been sweetened.</p>
<p>The production of maltose is based on the <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>, compound by <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> units α(1→4) and α(1→6) linked, meaning that the <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> number 1 is linked by a <a href="/wiki/Glycosidic_bond" title="Glycosidic bond">glycosidic bond</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> number 4 or 6 of the other glucose. The 1→4 linked <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> part is called <a href="/wiki/Amylose" title="Amylose">amylose</a> and it is a linear <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> while the 1→4 and 1→6 linked part is the <a href="/wiki/Amylopectin" title="Amylopectin">amylopectin</a>, a branched chain polymer, being 1→6 the branching point linkages.</p>
<p>This kind of <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> is catalyzed by <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> called amylases,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> classified into α-amylases and β-amylases. In the human pancreas the digestion of <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> is catalyzed by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%91-amylase" title="Α-amylase">α-amylase</a>, as the salivary enzyme. This <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> can work with <a href="/wiki/Amylose" title="Amylose">amylose</a> or <a href="/wiki/Amylopectin" title="Amylopectin">amylopectin</a>, but only the interior α(1→4) <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> linkages of the <a href="/wiki/Amylopectin" title="Amylopectin">amylopectin</a> molecules as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%91-amylase" title="Α-amylase">α-amylase</a> cannot hydrolyze the α(1→6) branching points. The futility of hydrolyzing these linkages affects the <a href="/wiki/Amylase" title="Amylase">amylase</a> action: it is weaker near them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The β-amylases are found in plants, sweet potatoes, soybeans, barley and wheat and are also in bacteria. Theses amylases produce β-maltose and β-limit <a href="/wiki/Dextrin" title="Dextrin">dextrins</a>, as a result of the enzyme’s futility of hydrolyzing α(1→6) branch linkages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In humans, maltose is broken down by the enzyme maltase so that there are two glucose molecules from which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glucose_metabolism" title="Glucose metabolism">glucose metabolism</a> obtains energy.</p>
