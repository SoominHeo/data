<p><b>Mobb Deep</b> is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" title="Hip hop">hip hop</a> duo from the <a href="/wiki/Queensbridge_Houses" title="Queensbridge Houses">Queensbridge Houses</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The group consists of <a href="/wiki/Havoc_(musician)" title="Havoc (musician)">Havoc</a> (born Kejuan Muchita) and <a href="/wiki/Prodigy_(rapper)" title="Prodigy (rapper)">Prodigy</a> (born Albert Johnson) and is "one of the most critically acclaimed hardcore East Coast Hip-Hop groups."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-How_to_Rap.2C_p._323_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Rap.2C_p._323-2">[2]</a></sup> They are known for their dark, hardcore delivery as exemplified in "<a href="/wiki/Shook_Ones_(Part_II)" title="Shook Ones (Part II)">Shook Ones (Part II)</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Mobb Deep has become one of the most successful rap duos in hip hop, having sold over three million records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-How_to_Rap.2C_p._323_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Rap.2C_p._323-2">[2]</a></sup> Mobb Deep's net worth is $10 million USD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-celebritynetworth.com_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-celebritynetworth.com-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two of their albums have been critical successes; <i><a href="/wiki/The_Infamous" title="The Infamous">The Infamous</a></i> (1995) and <i><a href="/wiki/Hell_on_Earth_(Mobb_Deep_album)" title="Hell on Earth (Mobb Deep album)">Hell on Earth</a></i> (1996).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The group briefly disbanded in 2012 but has since reunited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mobb_Deep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mobb_Deep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Havoc_(musician)" title="Havoc (musician)">Havoc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prodigy_(rapper)" title="Prodigy (rapper)">Prodigy</a> first met each other at the <a href="/wiki/High_School_of_Art_and_Design" title="High School of Art and Design">High School of Art and Design</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. Originally dubbing themselves the Poetical Prophets in 1990, the duo later changed its name to Mobb Deep in 1992 to "reflect their reputation on the streets."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1993, when Havoc and Prodigy were nineteen, they released their debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Juvenile_Hell" title="Juvenile Hell">Juvenile Hell</a></i> which was promoted by the single "<a href="/wiki/Peer_Pressure_(song)" title="Peer Pressure (song)">Peer Pressure</a>." The album sold poorly and eventually met with harsh reviews that dismissed the duo as just another hardcore group with little to distinguish it from the rest of the hip-hop world, despite production by <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Large_Professor" title="Large Professor">Large Professor</a>. In 1993 Havoc made a guest appearance on the critically acclaimed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Moon_(band)" title="Black Moon (band)">Black Moon</a> album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enta_Da_Stage" title="Enta Da Stage">Enta Da Stage</a></i> on a song titled "U Da Man." In 1994 the duo released a single titled "Shook Ones Part 1".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_to_success">Rise to success</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mobb_Deep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rise to success">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The group saw its first major success with their second album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Infamous" title="The Infamous">The Infamous</a></i>, released in 1995. Mobb Deep catapulted to the top of the hardcore hip-hop scene through Havoc and Prodigy's straightforward narration of street life. In this album, Mobb Deep portrayed the struggles of living in New York City's <a href="/wiki/Queensbridge_Houses" title="Queensbridge Houses">Queensbridge Houses</a> where Havoc grew up. Following the release of <i>The Infamous</i>, Mobb Deep became some of the most prolific artists of the East Coast. The production of this album was very dark and sample-based thanks to Havoc, who produced the beats from this point forward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Furthermore, the hit single "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shook_Ones_Pt._II" title="Shook Ones Pt. II">Shook Ones Pt. II</a>", a remix to the hit Shook Ones, received critical acclaim.</p>
<p>Mobb Deep's third album, <i><a href="/wiki/Hell_on_Earth_(Mobb_Deep_album)" title="Hell on Earth (Mobb Deep album)">Hell on Earth</a></i> was released in 1996 debuting at number six on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_charts" title="Billboard charts">Billboard Album Chart</a>; the album continued the duo's portrayal of harsh street life while further pushing them to the forefront of Hip Hop scene along with contemporary <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_hip_hop" title="East Coast hip hop">East Coast Rappers</a> like <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>, and fellow Queensbridge rapper <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>. Nas as well as Method Man and Raekwon of the Wu Tang Clan appear on 'Hell on Earth'.</p>
<p>In 1996, they appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_Organization" title="Red Hot Organization">Red Hot Organization</a>'s compilation CD, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/America_is_Dying_Slowly" title="America is Dying Slowly">America is Dying Slowly</a> alongside <a href="/wiki/Biz_Markie" title="Biz Markie">Biz Markie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fat_Joe" title="Fat Joe">Fat Joe</a> among many other prominent Hip Hop artists. This compilation was solely meant to raise awareness of the AIDS epidemic among African American men and this compilation was heralded as "a masterpiece" by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source</a> Magazine, a Hip Hop publication. 1997 Mobb Deep was featured on <a href="/wiki/Frankie_Cutlass" title="Frankie Cutlass">Frankie Cutlass</a> "Politics &amp; Bullsh*t" album track title "Know Da Game" which also features <a href="/wiki/Kool_G_Rap" title="Kool G Rap">Kool G Rap</a>. In 1998, the duo collaborated with reggae dancehall artist <a href="/wiki/Bounty_Killer" title="Bounty Killer">Bounty Killer</a> on the track "Deadly Zone" for the soundtrack to <i><a href="/wiki/Blade_(film)" title="Blade (film)">Blade</a></i>. In 1999, they released the highly anticipated <i><a href="/wiki/Murda_Muzik" title="Murda Muzik">Murda Muzik</a></i> album which, despite extensive <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">bootlegging</a>, had the majority of its songs unintentionally leaked. This resulted in delays in the official album release. When the album was officially released, it eventually debuted at number three on the Billboard 200 and quickly received <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">Platinum</a> Certification and was further promoted by the popular single "<a href="/wiki/Quiet_Storm_(song)" title="Quiet Storm (song)">Quiet Storm</a>." Shortly afterward, Prodigy released his long-awaited solo album <i><a href="/wiki/H.N.I.C._(Prodigy_album)" title="H.N.I.C. (Prodigy album)">H.N.I.C</a></i> in which he collaborated with fellow artists like (<a href="/wiki/B.G._(rapper)" title="B.G. (rapper)">B.G.</a>, <a href="/wiki/N.O.R.E." title="N.O.R.E.">N.O.R.E.</a>) and producers (including <a href="/wiki/The_Alchemist_(musician)" title="The Alchemist (musician)">The Alchemist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rockwilder" title="Rockwilder">Rockwilder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Just_Blaze" title="Just Blaze">Just Blaze</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Coast_vs._West_Coast_feud">East Coast vs. West Coast feud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mobb_Deep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: East Coast vs. West Coast feud">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mobb Deep was part of the infamous East Coast vs West Coast Hip Hop rivalry that was fueled and increasingly promoted by the private media. The beef started when Snoop Dogg and the West Coast group, Tha Dogg Pound, released "<a href="/wiki/New_York,_New_York_(Tha_Dogg_Pound_song)" title="New York, New York (Tha Dogg Pound song)">New York, New York</a>" which Mobb Deep, along with <a href="/wiki/Capone-N-Noreaga" title="Capone-N-Noreaga">Capone-N-Noreaga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tragedy_Khadafi" title="Tragedy Khadafi">Tragedy Khadafi</a> responded with the song "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L.A_L.A" title="L.A L.A">L.A L.A</a>" (This song can be found on Capone-N-Noreaga's debut album <a href="/wiki/The_War_Report" title="The War Report">The War Report</a>). This song was released during rapper <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a>'s final days of incarceration. Members of Tupac's group, <a href="/wiki/Outlawz" title="Outlawz">Outlawz</a>, allegedly attended a Mobb Deep concert; they then visited with Tupac over public rumoring that the duo had snubbed them at the concert. Tupac dissed Mobb Deep on multiple tracks, including: "Hit 'Em Up" and "When We Ride on Our Enemies" in which Tupac makes light of Prodigy's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sickle-cell" title="Sickle-cell">sickle-cell</a> disease. Additional Tupac diss tracks include: "Bomb First (My Second Reply)" where Tupac said "You're barely breathin'" and "Against All Odds," both of which were released on Tupac's posthumous studio album, <a href="/wiki/The_Don_Killuminati:_The_7_Day_Theory" title="The Don Killuminati: The 7 Day Theory">The Don Killuminati: The 7 Day Theory</a>. Mobb Deep retaliated on their 1996 release of <a href="/wiki/Hell_on_Earth_(Mobb_Deep_album)" title="Hell on Earth (Mobb Deep album)">Hell on Earth</a>, which contains a diss track entitled "Drop A Gem On 'Em."</p>
<pre>
“I was happy about it,” Havoc told Jack Thriller. “The n—- saying our names. I didn’t know what the f— the beef was about. I didn’t even care. I was like damn, did you hear that? 2Pac dissing us. We about to sell some records.” Havoc also revealed that Mobb Deep had never even “crossed paths” with Pac before his death in September 1996. He added, “And we never got a chance to cross paths with him because he passed away,” he said. “I saw him from a long distance but I never met him. [I was a] fan. But didn’t even know him.”
</pre>
<p>In the nostalgic Q&amp;A, the famed rapper/producer also recalled his time touring and working with 2Pac’s most famous rival, The Notorious B.I.G., on the song “Last Day” from Biggie’s Life After Death album.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000_to_2010">2000 to 2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mobb_Deep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2000 to 2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mobb Deep released <i><a href="/wiki/Infamy_(album)" title="Infamy (album)">Infamy</a></i> while in 2001 the song "<a href="/wiki/Burn_(Mobb_Deep_song)" title="Burn (Mobb Deep song)">Burn</a>" (featuring <a href="/wiki/Vita_(rapper)" title="Vita (rapper)">Vita</a>) was perceived as a response to Jay-Z's <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/diss_song" title="wikt:diss song">diss song</a> "Takeover" on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blueprint" title="The Blueprint">The Blueprint</a></i>, as was "Crawlin'," in which Prodigy's two verses both mention Jay-Z. The album marked a major stylistic change in which the duo moved away from a raw, minimalist, stripped-down beat toward a commercial friendly in terms with such songs as "<a href="/wiki/Hey_Luv_(Anything)" title="Hey Luv (Anything)">Hey Luv (Anything)</a>." This transition fostered accusations of "<a href="/wiki/Selling_out" title="Selling out">selling out</a>." In 2003, the group split with Loud Records and released <i>Free Agents: The Murda Mix Tape</i>, in which Havoc and Prodigy proclaimed themselves "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_agents" title="Free agents">free agents</a>" and addressed the group's split with its old label and its search for a new label. <a href="/wiki/Jive_Records" title="Jive Records">Jive Records</a> signed the duo later in the year through a deal with the group’s own imprint. Mobb Deep then released <i><a href="/wiki/Amerikaz_Nightmare" title="Amerikaz Nightmare">Amerikaz Nightmare</a></i> in 2004. The record sold poorly and lead to the group’s departure from the label.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Today, as a result of various mergers, all of Mobb Deep's studio albums from 1995 to 2004 are owned by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_Music_Entertainment" title="Sony Music Entertainment">Sony Music Entertainment</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="G-Unit_Records">G-Unit Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mobb_Deep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: G-Unit Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In June 2005, Mobb Deep announced they had signed with <a href="/wiki/G-Unit_Records" title="G-Unit Records">G-Unit Records</a>. 50 Cent had a connection to Mobb Deep as he grew up in Queens &amp; Havoc had provided production on projects by G-Unit, Lloyd Banks &amp; Tony Yayo. Mobb Deep released their eighth album, <a href="/wiki/Blood_Money_(Mobb_Deep_album)" title="Blood Money (Mobb Deep album)">Blood Money</a> in 2006. It features G-Unit members 50 Cent, Lloyd Banks, Tony Yayo &amp; Young Buck as well as <a href="/wiki/Mary_J._Blige" title="Mary J. Blige">Mary J. Blige</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Nate_Dogg" title="Nate Dogg">Nate Dogg</a>. In 2006, Mobb Deep became the first American hip-hop duo to perform in India, via VH1's Hip Hop Hustle. In late 2009, Mobb Deep were released from their contract with G Unit.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_to_present:_Break-up.2C_reunion_and_The_Infamous_Mobb_Deep">2011 to present: Break-up, reunion and <i>The Infamous Mobb Deep</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mobb_Deep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2011 to present: Break-up, reunion and The Infamous Mobb Deep">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Infamous_Mobb_Deep" title="The Infamous Mobb Deep">The Infamous Mobb Deep</a></div>
<p>On April 4 2011, Mobb Deep released a new single called "Dog Shit," featuring rapper <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>. This is the first official song by Mobb Deep since Prodigy's release from jail. It was produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Havoc_(Mobb_Deep)" title="Havoc (Mobb Deep)">Havoc</a> and Alchemist.</p>
<p>On July 27, 2012, Havoc told AllHipHop in interview that the group is on an indefinite hiatus. According to HipHopDX, Mobb Deep had a falling out after Havoc blasted Prodigy on <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a>, and eventually, a sound clip leaked of him taking numerous shots at his former Mobb Deep partner during their concert at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SXSW" title="SXSW">SXSW</a>. At first, Havoc claimed that he was hacked. However, he later confirmed it was him and originally denied because he felt that Twitter was not a place to resolve a beef. He also stated that he had a diss track aimed at Prodigy in the making, "Same Shit Different Day".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The song later turned out to be "Separated (Real from the Fake)," which appeared on Havoc's solo album <i>13</i>.</p>
<p>However, in January 2013 Prodigy said that he is sure he would record with Havoc in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Mobb Deep appeared on <a href="/wiki/Papoose_(rapper)" title="Papoose (rapper)">Papoose</a>'s debut album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nacirema_Dream" title="The Nacirema Dream">The Nacirema Dream</a></i> on the track "Aim, Shoot". They later reunited and performed for Paid Dues on March 30, 2013 and an international 20th anniversary tour starting in May 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> On March 22, 2013 the group officially reunited for an interview and explained that music is the most important thing in their lives and that they had been friends for too long to break up the friendship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around the May 2013 release of Havoc's third studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/13_(Havoc_album)" title="13 (Havoc album)">13</a></i> Havoc announced that him and Prodigy had been in the studio for over a month working on the duo's eighth studio album which was already "halfway done." He also stated that he will be doing the entire production on the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 1, 2014, the group released its eighth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Infamous_Mobb_Deep" title="The Infamous Mobb Deep">The Infamous Mobb Deep</a></i>, a double album that included one CD with original new music and one with unreleased tracks from sessions from the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Infamous" title="The Infamous">The Infamous</a></i>.</p>
