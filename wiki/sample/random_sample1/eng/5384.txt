<p>The <b>Shrine of the Cloak</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pashto_language" title="Pashto language">Pashto</a>: <i>Kerqa Sharif</i>) is located adjacent to the main mosque in <a href="/wiki/Kandahar" title="Kandahar">Kandahar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>. It contains the <i>Kerqa</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Cloak" title="Cloak">cloak</a> believed to have been worn by the Islamic prophet <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>. It is therefore widely held to be one of the holiest Islamic sites in Afghanistan, and even considered by some as the "heart of Afghanistan".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The building's exteriors are of green marble from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lashkar_Gah" title="Lashkar Gah">Lashkar Gah</a>, with tiled surfaces and gilded archways. The cloak itself is locked away inside the mosque and is rarely seen. It has been guarded by the same family for over 250 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wapo_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-wapo-2">[2]</a></sup> Its guardians have traditionally only shown the cloak to recognized leaders of Afghanistan, although in times of great crisis such as natural disasters, it has been publicly displayed as a means of reassurance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wapo_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-wapo-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cloak was given to <a href="/wiki/Emir" title="Emir">Amir</a> <a href="/wiki/Ahmad_Shah_Durrani" title="Ahmad Shah Durrani">Ahmad Shah Durrani</a> by Amir <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_Murad_Beg" title="Mohammad Murad Beg">Murad Beg</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bukhara" title="Bukhara">Bukhara</a> in 1768 in order to solidify a treaty between the two leaders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Essential_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Essential-3">[3]</a></sup> An alternate account states that when Ahmad Shah had traveled to Bukhara, he saw the cloak of <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>. He then decided to take the artifact with him to Kandahar, and asked whether he could "borrow" the cloak from its keepers. They, worrying that he might try to remove it from Bukhara, told him it could not be taken from the city. Ahmad Shah then is said to have pointed to a heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stela" title="Stela">stela</a> of stone firmly planted in the ground, saying that he would never take the cloak far from the stone. The keepers, gratified at his answer, handed him the cloak. Ahmad Shah then took the cloak, ordered the stone slab to be dug up, and carried them both back with him to Kandahar, where the stone now stands near his <i><a href="/wiki/Mazar_(mausoleum)" title="Mazar (mausoleum)">mazar</a></i> (tomb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cloak was last seen in public in 1996,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-1">[1]</a></sup> when <a href="/wiki/Mohammed_Omar" title="Mohammed Omar">Mullah Omar</a>, the up-and-coming leader of the Taliban, removed it from the shrine and donned the cloak while he stood atop a building, and in front of a large crowd of his followers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-4">[4]</a></sup> That symbolic act is commonly considered a key point in the rise of the Taliban, and Mullah Omar himself, associating him with both Ahmed Shah Durrani and the Prophet of Islam. Upon donning the cloak, the crowd began to shout "<i><a href="/wiki/Amir_al-Mu%27minin" title="Amir al-Mu'minin">Amir al-Mu'minin</a></i>" (Commander of the Faithful), a title that <a href="/wiki/Ayman_al-Zawahiri" title="Ayman al-Zawahiri">Ayman al-Zawahiri</a> still occasionally uses to refer to Mullah Omar in his radio addresses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Essential_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Essential-3">[3]</a></sup> Wahid Muzhda, an Afghan analyst and one-time high-ranking official in the Taliban foreign ministry, disputes Mullah Omar donning the cloak. "From what I know, from sources close to Omar, and from a chat with the keeper of the shrine [where the cloak is kept], Omar did not wear the cloak."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Wahid Muzhda's doubts notwithstanding, in a New York Times piece from winter 2001, the current keeper of the Shrine, Qari Shawali, claimed that he not only allowed Mullah Omar to view the cloak, but that it was borrowed and removed from the Shrine in the spring of 1996 by Mullah Omar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-4">[4]</a></sup> This was the third time in his lifetime that a man attempted to look at, let alone don, the cloak.</p>
