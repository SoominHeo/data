<p><b>Stingrays</b> are a group of <a href="/wiki/Batoidea" title="Batoidea">rays</a>, which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartilaginous_fish" title="Cartilaginous fish">cartilaginous fish</a> related to <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a>. They are classified in the suborder <b>Myliobatoidei</b> of the order <a href="/wiki/Myliobatiformes" title="Myliobatiformes">Myliobatiformes</a> and consist of eight families: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexatrygonidae" title="Hexatrygonidae">Hexatrygonidae</a> (sixgill stingray), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plesiobatidae" title="Plesiobatidae">Plesiobatidae</a> (deepwater stingray), <a href="/wiki/Urolophidae" title="Urolophidae">Urolophidae</a> (stingarees), <a href="/wiki/Urotrygonidae" title="Urotrygonidae">Urotrygonidae</a> (round rays), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dasyatidae" title="Dasyatidae">Dasyatidae</a> (whiptail stingrays), <a href="/wiki/Potamotrygonidae" title="Potamotrygonidae">Potamotrygonidae</a> (river stingrays), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gymnuridae" title="Gymnuridae">Gymnuridae</a> (butterfly rays), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myliobatidae" title="Myliobatidae">Myliobatidae</a> (eagle rays).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nelson_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nelson-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most stingrays have one or more barbed stingers (modified from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dermal_denticle" title="Dermal denticle">dermal denticles</a>) on the tail, which are used exclusively in self-defense. The stinger may reach a length around 35 cm (14 in), and its underside has two grooves with <a href="/wiki/Venom" title="Venom">venom</a> <a href="/wiki/Gland" title="Gland">glands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The stinger is covered with a thin layer of skin, the integumentary sheath, in which the venom is concentrated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A few members of the suborder, such as the <a href="/wiki/Manta_ray" title="Manta ray">manta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Porcupine_ray" title="Porcupine ray">porcupine rays</a>, do not have stingers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbaseUrogymnus_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-fishbaseUrogymnus-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stingrays are common in coastal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">subtropical</a> marine waters throughout the world. Some species, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Thorntail_stingray" title="Thorntail stingray">Dasyatis thetidis</a></i>, are found in warmer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> oceans, and others, such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plesiobatis_daviesi" title="Plesiobatis daviesi">Plesiobatis daviesi</a></i>, are found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_ocean" title="Deep ocean">deep ocean</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_stingray" title="River stingray">river stingrays</a>, and a number of <a href="/wiki/Whiptail_stingray" title="Whiptail stingray">whiptail stingrays</a> (such as the <a href="/wiki/Niger_stingray" title="Niger stingray">Niger stingray</a>), are restricted to <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a>. Most myliobatoids are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demersal" title="Demersal">demersal</a> (inhabiting the next-to-lowest zone in the <a href="/wiki/Water_column" title="Water column">water column</a>), but some, such as the <a href="/wiki/Pelagic_stingray" title="Pelagic stingray">pelagic stingray</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Eagle_ray" title="Eagle ray">eagle rays</a>, are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelagic" title="Pelagic">pelagic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are about 220 known stingray species organised into 10 families and 29 genera. Stingray species are <a href="/wiki/List_of_threatened_rays" title="List of threatened rays">progressively becoming threatened</a> or vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a>, particularly as the consequence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unregulated_fishing" title="Unregulated fishing">unregulated fishing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> As of 2013, 45 species have been listed as <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable</a> or <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">IUCN</a>. The status of some other species is poorly known, leading to their being listed as <a href="/wiki/Data_deficient" title="Data deficient">data deficient</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

</div>
<p>The flattened bodies of stingrays allow them to effectively conceal themselves in their environments. Stingrays do this by agitating the sand and hiding beneath it. Because their eyes are on top of their bodies and their mouths on the undersides, stingrays cannot see their prey; instead, they use smell and electroreceptors (<a href="/wiki/Ampullae_of_Lorenzini" title="Ampullae of Lorenzini">ampullae of Lorenzini</a>) similar to those of <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Stingrays feed primarily on <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">molluscs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, and occasionally on small fish. Some stingrays' mouths contain two powerful, shell-crushing plates, while other species only have sucking mouthparts. Stingrays settle on the bottom while feeding, often leaving only their eyes and tails visible. <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">Coral reefs</a> are favorite feeding grounds and are usually shared with sharks during high tide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stingray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When a male is courting a female, he follows her closely, biting at her pectoral disc. He then places one of his two claspers into her valve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stingrays are <a href="/wiki/Ovoviviparity" title="Ovoviviparity">ovoviviparous</a>, bearing live young in "litters" of five to 13. The female holds the embryos in the womb without a placenta. Instead, the embryos absorb nutrients from a <a href="/wiki/Yolk_sac" title="Yolk sac">yolk sac</a>, and after the sac is depleted, the mother provides uterine "milk".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/Sea_Life_London_Aquarium" title="Sea Life London Aquarium">Sea Life London Aquarium</a>, two female stingrays have delivered seven baby stingrays, although the mothers have not been near a male for two years. "Rays have been known to store sperm and not give birth until they decide the timing is right".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stingray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A stingray's diet includes small fish, snails, clams, and shrimp, and some other small sea creatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stingray_injuries">Stingray injuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stingray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Stingray injuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stingray_injury" title="Stingray injury">Stingray injury</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stringray%27s_sting.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="133" data-file-width="620" height="47" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Stringray%27s_sting.jpg/220px-Stringray%27s_sting.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Stringray%27s_sting.jpg/330px-Stringray%27s_sting.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Stringray%27s_sting.jpg/440px-Stringray%27s_sting.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stringray%27s_sting.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A stingray's stinger (ruler in cm)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Stingrays are not aggressive and only attack humans when provoked, such as if a ray is accidentally stepped on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slaughter_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Slaughter-15">[15]</a></sup> To avoid stepping on a stingray in shallow water, the water should be waded through with a shuffle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Alternatively, before wading, stones can be thrown into the water to scare stingrays away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Contact with the stinger causes local trauma (from the cut itself), pain, swelling, muscle cramps from the venom, and later may result in infection from bacteria or fungi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The injury is very painful, but seldom life-threatening unless the stinger pierces a vital area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slaughter_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Slaughter-15">[15]</a></sup> The barb usually breaks off in the wound, and surgery may be required to remove the fragments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fatal stings are very rare, but can happen,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slaughter_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Slaughter-15">[15]</a></sup> most famously in the death of <a href="/wiki/Steve_Irwin" title="Steve Irwin">Steve Irwin</a> in 2006, in which the stinger penetrated his <a href="/wiki/Thoracic_wall" title="Thoracic wall">thoracic wall</a>, causing massive trauma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_food">As food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stingray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: As food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rays are edible, and may be caught as food using fishing lines or spears.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Stingray recipes abound throughout the world, with dried forms of the wings being most common. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, stingray is commonly <a href="/wiki/Grilling" title="Grilling">grilled</a> over charcoal, then served with spicy <i><a href="/wiki/Sambal" title="Sambal">sambal</a></i> sauce, or soy sauce. Generally, the most prized parts of the stingray are the wings (flaps is the proper terminology), the "cheek" (the area surrounding the eyes), and the liver. The rest of the ray is considered too rubbery to have any culinary uses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stingray_teeth_and_jaws.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2200" data-file-width="2200" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Stingray_teeth_and_jaws.jpg/220px-Stingray_teeth_and_jaws.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Stingray_teeth_and_jaws.jpg/330px-Stingray_teeth_and_jaws.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Stingray_teeth_and_jaws.jpg/440px-Stingray_teeth_and_jaws.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stingray_teeth_and_jaws.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Stingray teeth and jaws, on display at the <a href="/wiki/American_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="American Museum of Natural History">American Museum of Natural History</a></div>

<p>While not independently valuable as a food source, the stingray's capacity to damage shell fishing grounds can lead to bounties being placed on their removal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecotourism">Ecotourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stingray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ecotourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dasyatis_americana_NOAA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2896" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Dasyatis_americana_NOAA.jpg/220px-Dasyatis_americana_NOAA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Dasyatis_americana_NOAA.jpg/330px-Dasyatis_americana_NOAA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Dasyatis_americana_NOAA.jpg/440px-Dasyatis_americana_NOAA.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dasyatis_americana_NOAA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Southern_stingray" title="Southern stingray">Southern stingray</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Stingrays are usually very docile and curious, their usual reaction being to flee any disturbance, but they sometimes brush their fins past any new object they encounter. Nevertheless, certain larger species may be more aggressive and should be approached with caution, as the stingray's defensive reflex (use of its poisoned stinger) may result in serious injury or death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stingrays are not normally visible to swimmers, but divers and snorkelers may find them in shallow, sandy waters, more so when the water is warm. In the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a>, several dive sites called <a href="/wiki/Stingray_City,_Grand_Cayman" title="Stingray City, Grand Cayman">Stingray City, Grand Cayman</a>, allow divers and snorkelers to swim with large <a href="/wiki/Southern_stingray" title="Southern stingray">southern stingrays</a> (<i>D. americana</i>) and feed them by hand. There are many companies that offer these stingray city trips, making it the number one tourist attraction in Grand Cayman. Every year hundreds of thousands of tourists visit this pristine sandbar, and virtually no one gets injured, meaning that these rays are extremely friendly to humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> A "Stingray City" in the sea surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> island of <a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a> consists of a large, shallow reserve where the rays live, and snorkeling is possible, since the rays are used to the presence of humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>, off the island of <a href="/wiki/Ambergris_Caye" title="Ambergris Caye">Ambergris Caye</a>, a popular marine sanctuary, <a href="/wiki/Hol_Chan_Marine_Reserve" title="Hol Chan Marine Reserve">Hol Chan</a>, has divers and snorkelers often gathering to watch stingrays and <a href="/wiki/Nurse_shark" title="Nurse shark">nurse sharks</a> drawn to the area by tour operators who feed the animals.</p>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Tahiti" title="Tahiti">Tahitian</a> island resorts regularly offer guests the chance to "feed the stingrays and sharks". This consists of taking a boat to the outer lagoon reefs, then standing in waist-high water while habituated stingrays swarm around, pressing right up against tourists seeking food from their hands or that being tossed into the water. The boat owners also "call in" sharks, which, when they arrive from the ocean, swoop through the shallow water above the reef and snatch food offered to them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stingray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The skin of the ray is used as an under layer for the cord or leather wrap (known as <i>ito</i> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Language" title="Japanese Language">Japanese</a>) on <a href="/wiki/Katana" title="Katana">Japanese swords</a> due to its hard, rough, skin texture that keeps the braided wrap from sliding on the handle during use. They are also used to make exotic shoes, boots, belts, wallets, jackets, and cellphone cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several ethnological sections in museums,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> such as the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>, display arrowheads and spearheads made of stingray stingers, used in <a href="/wiki/Micronesia" title="Micronesia">Micronesia</a> and elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Henry_de_Monfreid" title="Henry de Monfreid">Henry de Monfreid</a> stated in his books that before <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Horn of Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whip" title="Whip">whips</a> were made from the tail of big stingrays, and these devices inflicted cruel cuts, so in <a href="/wiki/Aden" title="Aden">Aden</a>, the British forbade their use on women and slaves. In former Spanish colonies, a stingray is called <i>raya látigo</i> ("whip ray").</p>
<p>Monfreid also wrote in several places about men of his crew suffering stingray wounds while standing and wading into <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> shallows to load or unload smuggled wares: he wrote that to "save the man's life", searing the wound with a red-hot iron was necessary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stingrays have more benefits than being used for human consumption. Their behaviours are very calm and friendly; and as such, many waterparks e.g. Discovery Cove (Owned by SeaWorld), Create a habitat within pools, where they and people are able to interact within a safe environment. SeaWorld home 'More than 200 stingrays- some with wingspans up to 5-feet'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Within the main SeaWorld park there are small pools where people are able to interact and feed the stingrays. In addition, they also have small nurseries which are home to the stingrays' pups, these are also open for interactions, but because they are young, they tend to hide beneath the sand.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossils">Fossils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stingray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fossils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although stingray <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teeth" title="Teeth">teeth</a> are rare on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seafloor" title="Seafloor">sea bottoms</a> compared to the similar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shark_teeth" title="Shark teeth">shark teeth</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scuba_diver" title="Scuba diver">scuba divers</a> searching for the latter do encounter the teeth of stingrays. <a href="/wiki/Permineralization" title="Permineralization">Permineralized</a> stingray teeth have been found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedimentary" title="Sedimentary">sedimentary</a> deposits around the world, including <a href="/wiki/Fossil_collecting" title="Fossil collecting">fossiliferous</a> outcrops in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stingray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
