<p><i><b>Mad Max Beyond Thunderdome</b></i> (also known as <i><b>Mad Max 3: Beyond Thunderdome</b></i> or simply <i><b>Mad Max 3</b></i> or <i><b>Thunderdome</b></i>) is a 1985 Australian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-apocalyptic_film" title="Post-apocalyptic film">post-apocalyptic</a> <a href="/wiki/Action_film" title="Action film">action</a> <a href="/wiki/Adventure_film" title="Adventure film">adventure film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/George_Miller_(director)" title="George Miller (director)">George Miller</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Ogilvie" title="George Ogilvie">George Ogilvie</a>, distributed by <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a>, and written by Miller and <a href="/wiki/Terry_Hayes" title="Terry Hayes">Terry Hayes</a>. In this sequel to <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Max_2" title="Mad Max 2">Mad Max 2: The Road Warrior</a></i>, Max (<a href="/wiki/Mel_Gibson" title="Mel Gibson">Mel Gibson</a>) is exiled into the desert by the corrupt ruler of Bartertown, Aunty Entity (<a href="/wiki/Tina_Turner" title="Tina Turner">Tina Turner</a>), and there encounters an isolated <a href="/wiki/Cargo_cult" title="Cargo cult">cargo cult</a> centered on a crashed <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747</a> and its deceased captain. The third installment in the <a href="/wiki/Mad_Max_(franchise)" title="Mad Max (franchise)"><i>Mad Max</i> film series</a> and, to date, is the last to feature Gibson as Max. The series was revived in 2015 with the release of a sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Max:_Fury_Road" title="Mad Max: Fury Road">Mad Max: Fury Road</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hardy" title="Tom Hardy">Tom Hardy</a> in the titular role.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_Max_Beyond_Thunderdome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the world climate altered to a parched <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_summer" title="Nuclear summer">nuclear summer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Rockatansky" title="Max Rockatansky">Max Rockatansky</a> crosses the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian" title="Australian">Australian</a> desert in a camel-drawn wagon when he is attacked by a pilot named Jedediah and his son in a <a href="/wiki/Transavia_PL-12_Airtruk" title="Transavia PL-12 Airtruk">Transavia PL-12 Airtruk</a>, stealing his wagon and belongings. Continuing on foot, Max follows their trail to the seedy community of Bartertown. While refused entry at first, Max is brought before the founder and ruler of Bartertown, the ruthless Aunty Entity. She offers to resupply his vehicle and equipment if he completes a task for her.</p>
<p>Aunty explains that Bartertown depends on a crude methane refinery powered by pig feces, which is run by a dwarf called Master and his giant bodyguard Blaster. "Master Blaster" holds an uneasy truce with Aunty for control of Bartertown; however, Master has begun to challenge Aunty's leadership. Aunty instructs Max to provoke a confrontation with Blaster in Thunderdome, a gladiatorial arena where conflicts are resolved by a duel to the death. Max enters the refinery to size up Master Blaster and befriends Pig Killer, a convict sentenced to work for slaughtering a pig to feed his family. Max finds his stolen vehicle in Master Blaster's possession, and helps disarm his booby-trapped engine to converse with him. Here he discovers that Blaster is exceptionally strong but extremely sensitive to high-pitched noises.</p>
<p>Max then faces Blaster in the Thunderdome and uses his weakness to gain the upper hand. He refuses to kill him after discovering he is <a href="/wiki/Developmental_disability" title="Developmental disability">developmentally disabled</a> and has the functional mentality of a child, telling Aunty it was not part of their deal, revealing her plot. Master, previously unaware of this covert deal to kill Blaster, is furious and vows to shut down the refinery and, by extension, Bartertown. An enraged Aunty has Blaster executed, Master imprisoned, and Max exiled, bound, masked, and sent on a horse in a random direction to the wasteland. As his horse perishes in a sinkhole, Max frees himself and presses on.</p>
<p>Near death, Max is found by a desert dweller named Savannah Nix, who hauls him back to her home, a primitive community of children and teenagers who live in an oasis. The children, survivors of a crashed <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747</a>, were left by their parents who went to find civilization. They believe Max to be the flight captain, returned to fix the aeroplane and fly them to civilization. Max denies this and insists that they remain in the relative safety of the oasis, knowing that the only "civilization" within reach is Bartertown.</p>
<p>Some of the children, led by Savannah, leave anyway, determined to find the prophesied "Tomorrow-morrow Land." Max stops them by force, but another tribe member known as Scrooloose, sets them free during the night and leaves with them. Their leader, Slake M'Thirst, asks Max to go after them, and he agrees, taking a few of the children with him to help. They find Savannah's group in danger but are unable to save one of the children from a sinkhole. With no supplies left, they are forced to head for Bartertown.</p>
<p>The group sneak in via the underground, and, with Pig Killer's help, free Master and escape in a train-truck, destroying Bartertown's methane refinery in the process. Aunty leads the inhabitants in pursuit, catching up to the train. Max's group slows them down while Scrooloose hijacks one of the pursuing vehicles, which happens to be Max's stolen vehicle. The group comes across Jedediah and his son, and Max coerces Jedediah into helping his group escape with their aeroplane. Max uses his vehicle to clear a path through Aunty's men, allowing the aeroplane to take off and escape, leaving him at Aunty's mercy. Aunty spares his life, having come to respect him; she says sardonically, "Well — ain't we a pair, raggedy man? Goodbye, soldier," and departs to presumably make good on her vow to rebuild Bartertown.</p>
<p>Jedediah flies the children to the coast, where they discover the nuclear-devastated ruins of <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>. Years later, the children have established a small society of themselves and other lost wanderers in the ruins. Savannah, now leader of the group, recites a nightly story of their journey and the man who saved them, as Max, still alive in the desert, wanders on to places unknown.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cast">Cast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_Max_Beyond_Thunderdome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mel_Gibson" title="Mel Gibson">Mel Gibson</a> as <a href="/wiki/Max_Rockatansky" title="Max Rockatansky">"Mad" Max Rockatansky</a>, a former MFP officer and lone warrior. Max roves the desert aimlessly, his existence entirely based around <a href="/wiki/Self-sufficiency" title="Self-sufficiency">self-survival</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tina_Turner" title="Tina Turner">Tina Turner</a> as Aunty Entity, the ruthless, determined ruler of Bartertown. Entity is a glamorous, Amazon-like figure who recognises a strength of character in Max, and hopes to exploit him to gain sole control of Bartertown from Master. Despite her brutality and Bartertown's chaos, Entity is an intelligent, cultured woman, who holds a hope of one day rebuilding society to its former glory. In regard of the character, Miller said, "We needed someone whose vitality and intelligence would make her control over Bartertown credible. She had to be a positive character rather than a conventional evil 'bad guy.' We had worked on the script with [Turner] in mind. But we had no idea if she'd be interested."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bruce_Spence" title="Bruce Spence">Bruce Spence</a> as Jedediah the Pilot, a marauding pilot who trades stolen goods in Bartertown. Spence previously played the Gyro Captain in <i>Mad Max 2</i>. "They were well into the shoot when they offered me a part described as 'not the Gyro Captain but kind of like the Gyro Captain!'" said Spence. "They said there's kind of a reflection of him and that they were having difficulty casting the role so they thought to themselves, 'Why not Bruce!'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Adam_Cockburn_(actor)" title="Adam Cockburn (actor)">Adam Cockburn</a> as Jedediah Jr., Jedediah's son, who often helps his father steal supplies, flying his father's aeroplane while Jedediah procures the goods.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Frank_Thring" title="Frank Thring">Frank Thring</a> as The Collector, head of Bartertown's trade and exchange network.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Angelo_Rossitto" title="Angelo Rossitto">Angelo Rossitto</a> as Master, a diminutive former engineer, who parlays his technical expertise into building the methane extractor responsible for Bartertown's electricity. When the film begins, Master has grown power-crazed under the protection of Blaster, and the reverence he receives from the people of Bartertown. He frequently humiliates Entity into acknowledging his power over her. When Blaster is killed however, Master becomes a far more subdued, humble character, and eventually escapes with the help of Max, Pig Killer, and the children.</li>
<li>Paul Larsson as Blaster, Master's silent, mentally-impaired bodyguard.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Angry_Anderson" title="Angry Anderson">Angry Anderson</a> as Ironbar Bassey, head of Bartertown's security and Aunty Entity's Number One Henchman. Despite his short height he is a fierce warrior figure, wearing a doll's head standard on his back, who comes to dislike Max more and more as the film proceeds. After several near-death incidents where his survival becomes more and more unlikely, he is presumably killed in the film's climactic chase, last seen giving the middle finger to the escaping heroes.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Robert_Grubb" title="Robert Grubb">Robert Grubb</a> as Pig Killer, a convict in Bartertown sentenced to work in the methane refinery, shovelling pig feces. He befriends Max, and when Max and the children return to rescue Master, Pig Killer escapes to help them.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Helen_Buday" title="Helen Buday">Helen Buday</a> as Savannah Nix, leader of a tribe of child survivors (or the children of those survivors) from a crashed 747. Savannah is the one who ensures the tribe remembers its past through the "tells" and acts as a surrogate mother figure to many of them. She is also the partner of Slake.</li>
<li>Tom Jennings as Slake M'Thirst, the male leader of the child tribe.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hodgeman" title="Edwin Hodgeman">Edwin Hodgeman</a> as Dr. Dealgood, the flamboyant Master of Ceremonies and chief auctioneer of Bartertown.</li>
<li>Rod Zuanic as Scrooloose.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/George_Spartels" title="George Spartels">George Spartels</a> as Blackfinger.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_Max_Beyond_Thunderdome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Mad Max Beyond Thunderdome</i> was the first <i>Mad Max</i> film made without producer <a href="/wiki/Byron_Kennedy" title="Byron Kennedy">Byron Kennedy</a>, who was killed in a helicopter crash in 1983 while location scouting for the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> While the film was in development before Kennedy's death, director <a href="/wiki/George_Miller_(director)" title="George Miller (director)">George Miller</a> was hesitant to continue without his producing partner. "I was reluctant to go ahead," said Miller. "And then there was a sort of need to – let's do something just to get over the shock and grief of all of that."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> A title card at the end of film reads: "...For Byron".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Miller ended up co-directing the film with <a href="/wiki/George_Ogilvie" title="George Ogilvie">George Ogilvie</a>, with whom he had worked on the 1983 miniseries <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Dismissal_(TV_miniseries)" title="The Dismissal (TV miniseries)">The Dismissal</a></i>. "I had a lot on my plate," said Miller. "I asked my friend George Ogilvie, who was working on the mini-series, 'Could you come and help me?' But I don't remember the experience because I was doing it to just... You know, I was grieving."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Together, Miller and Ogilvie used a group workshopping rehearsal technique that they had developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stratton2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-stratton2-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Exterior location filming took place primarily in the mining town of <a href="/wiki/Coober_Pedy" title="Coober Pedy">Coober Pedy</a>, with the set for Bartertown built at an old brickworks (the Brickpit) at <a href="/wiki/Homebush_Bay" title="Homebush Bay">Homebush Bay</a> in Sydney's western suburbs and the children's camp shot at the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Mountains_(New_South_Wales)" title="Blue Mountains (New South Wales)">Blue Mountains</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stratton2_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-stratton2-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> "<i>Mad Max Beyond Thunderdome</i> proved far more challenging than <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Max_2" title="Mad Max 2">The Road Warrior</a></i>," said cinematographer <a href="/wiki/Dean_Semler" title="Dean Semler">Dean Semler</a>. "We were dealing with more varied environments than before and it was essential that each of the worlds created for the film have a distinctly different look."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_Max_Beyond_Thunderdome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mad_Max_Beyond_Thunderdome_(soundtrack)" title="Mad Max Beyond Thunderdome (soundtrack)">Mad Max Beyond Thunderdome (soundtrack)</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">musical score</a> for <i>Mad Max Beyond Thunderdome</i> was composed by <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Jarre" title="Maurice Jarre">Maurice Jarre</a>, replacing <a href="/wiki/Brian_May_(composer)" title="Brian May (composer)">Brian May</a>, who composed the music for the previous two films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The film also features two songs performed by <a href="/wiki/Tina_Turner" title="Tina Turner">Tina Turner</a>, "<a href="/wiki/We_Don%27t_Need_Another_Hero_(Thunderdome)" title="We Don't Need Another Hero (Thunderdome)">We Don't Need Another Hero (Thunderdome)</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/One_of_the_Living" title="One of the Living">One of the Living</a>," with the latter replacing Jarre's opening titles music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A soundtrack album was originally released by <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a> in 1985. It includes Turner's "We Don't Need Another Hero (Thunderdome)", which reached #1 in Canada, #2 in the US and #3 in the British single charts; it plays over the end credits. "One of the Living" was rerecorded for single release, and reached #15 in both Canada and the US, but only #55 in Britain. A double CD containing only Jarre's original music was issued in 2010 on Tadlow Music/Silva Screen Records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_Max_Beyond_Thunderdome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the film's budget was more extravagant than its predecessors', its box office yield was only moderate in comparison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stratton2_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-stratton2-10">[10]</a></sup> <i>Beyond Thunderdome</i> grossed A$4,272,802 at the box office in Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_Max_Beyond_Thunderdome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Critical reaction to the film was generally positive; it holds an 81% rating on <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> based on 47 reviews with a consensus "Beyond Thunderdome deepens the Mad Max character without sacrificing the amazing vehicle choreography and stunts that made the originals memorable",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> although reviewers disagreed as to whether they considered the film the highest or lowest point of the <i>Mad Max</i> trilogy. Most of the criticism focused on the children in the second half of the film, whom many found too similar to the <a href="/wiki/Lost_Boys_(Peter_Pan)" title="Lost Boys (Peter Pan)">Lost Boys</a> from the story of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Pan" title="Peter Pan">Peter Pan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Though uncredited, Robert C. Cumbow of <i><a href="/wiki/Slant_Magazine" title="Slant Magazine">Slant Magazine</a></i> identifies "whole ideas, themes and characterizations" adopted from <i><a href="/wiki/Riddley_Walker" title="Riddley Walker">Riddley Walker</a></i>, a 1980 post-apocalyptic novel by <a href="/wiki/Russell_Hoban" title="Russell Hoban">Russell Hoban</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> On the other hand, critics praised the Thunderdome scene in particular; film critic <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun-Times" title="Chicago Sun-Times">Chicago Sun-Times</a></i> called the Thunderdome "the first really original movie idea about how to stage a fight since we got the first karate movies" and praised the fight between Max and Blaster as "one of the great creative action scenes in the movies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Ebert awarded the film 4 stars out of 4 and later placed the film on his list of the 10 best pictures of 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> wrote that the film "opens strong" and has good acting from Gibson, Turner, and the children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite mostly positive reviews from critics, some fans of the series have criticised the film for being "Hollywood-ized" and having a lighter tone than its predecessors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mad_Max_Beyond_Thunderdome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mad_Max_series_legacy_and_influence_in_popular_culture" title="Mad Max series legacy and influence in popular culture">Mad Max series legacy and influence in popular culture</a></div>
<p>As with the previous installments of the <i>Mad Max</i> series, <i>Mad Max Beyond Thunderdome</i> has influenced popular culture in various regards. The term "<a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Thunderdome_(disambiguation)" title="Thunderdome (disambiguation)">thunderdome</a>" is now used in various contexts in which its meaning is similar to the sense in which it appears in the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Chris_Weitz" title="Chris Weitz">Chris Weitz</a> has cited the film as an influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film is recognized by <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">American Film Institute</a> in these lists:</p>

</ul>
</li>

<ul>

</ul>

</ul>
