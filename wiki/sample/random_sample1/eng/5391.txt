<p>The <b>Massachusetts Bay Colony</b> (1628–1691) was an <a href="/wiki/English_overseas_possessions" title="English overseas possessions">English settlement</a> on the east coast of North America in the 17th century in and around the broad opening of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay" title="Massachusetts Bay">Massachusetts Bay</a>, the northernmost predecessor colony of the several colonies later reorganized as the <i><a href="/wiki/Province_of_Massachusetts_Bay" title="Province of Massachusetts Bay">Province of Massachusetts Bay</a></i>. The lands of the settlement were located in central <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> in what is now <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, with initial settlements situated on two natural harbors and surrounding land, about 15.4 miles (24.8 km) apart<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DisByLand_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-DisByLand-1">[1]</a></sup>—the areas around the present-day cities of <a href="/wiki/Salem,_Massachusetts" title="Salem, Massachusetts">Salem</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston</a>.</p>
<p>The territory nominally administered by the colony included much of present-day central <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, including portions of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. states</a> of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>. Territory claimed but never administered by the colonial government extended as far west as the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. The earlier Dutch colony of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Netherlands" title="New Netherlands">New Netherlands</a> disputed many of these claims, arguing that they held rights to lands beyond Rhode Island up to the western side of Cape Cod and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plymouth_Bay_Colony" title="Plymouth Bay Colony">Plymouth Bay Colony</a>.</p>
<p>The Massachusetts Bay Colony was founded by the owners of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay_Company" title="Massachusetts Bay Company">Massachusetts Bay Company</a>, which included investors in the failed Dorchester Company which had established a short-lived settlement on <a href="/wiki/Cape_Ann" title="Cape Ann">Cape Ann</a> in 1623. The Massachusetts Bay Colony began in 1628 and was the company's second attempt at colonization. The colony was successful, with about 20,000 people <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritan_migration_to_New_England_(1620%E2%80%931640)" title="Puritan migration to New England (1620–1640)">migrating to New England</a> in the 1630s. The population was strongly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritan" title="Puritan">Puritan</a>, and its governance was dominated by a small group of leaders who were strongly influenced by Puritan religious leaders. <a href="/wiki/List_of_colonial_governors_of_Massachusetts" title="List of colonial governors of Massachusetts">Its governors</a> were elected, and the electorate were limited to <a href="/wiki/Freeman_(Colonial)" title="Freeman (Colonial)">freemen</a> who had been examined for their religious views and formally admitted to the local church. As a consequence, the colonial leadership exhibited intolerance to other religious views, including <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Anglican</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaker" title="Quaker">Quaker</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baptist" title="Baptist">Baptist</a> theologies.</p>
<p>The colonists initially had decent relationships with the local <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Indian populations</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, but frictions arose over cultural differences which were further exacerbated by <a href="/wiki/Dutch_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Dutch colonization of the Americas">Dutch colonial expansion</a>. These led first to the <a href="/wiki/Pequot_War" title="Pequot War">Pequot War</a> (1636–38) and then to <a href="/wiki/King_Philip%27s_War" title="King Philip's War">King Philip's War</a> (1675–78), after which most of the Indians in southern New England made peace treaties with the colonists (apart from the Pequot tribe, whose survivors largely merged with the Narragansett and Mohegan tribes after the Pequot War).</p>
<p>The colony was economically successful, engaging in trade with England and the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>. A shortage of <a href="/wiki/Hard_currency" title="Hard currency">hard currency</a> in the colony prompted it to <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_pound#Coins" title="Massachusetts pound">establish a mint</a> in 1652. Political differences with England after the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(England)" title="Restoration (England)">English Restoration</a> led to the revocation of the colonial charter in 1684. <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">King James II</a> established the <a href="/wiki/Dominion_of_New_England" title="Dominion of New England">Dominion of New England</a> in 1686 to bring all of the New England colonies under firmer crown control. The dominion collapsed after the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a> of 1688 deposed James, and the colony reverted to rule under the revoked charter until the charter for the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Massachusetts_Bay" title="Province of Massachusetts Bay">Province of Massachusetts Bay</a> was issued in 1691, which combined the Massachusetts Bay territories with those of the <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Colony" title="Plymouth Colony">Plymouth Colony</a> and proprietary holdings on <a href="/wiki/Nantucket" title="Nantucket">Nantucket</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martha%27s_Vineyard" title="Martha's Vineyard">Martha's Vineyard</a>. Sir <a href="/wiki/William_Phips" title="William Phips">William Phips</a> arrived in 1692 bearing the charter and formally took charge of the new province. The political and economic dominance of New England by the modern state of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> was made possible in part by the early dominance in these spheres by the Massachusetts Bay colonists.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_settlements">Early settlements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early settlements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tribal_Territories_Southern_New_England.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1169" data-file-width="1701" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Tribal_Territories_Southern_New_England.png/300px-Tribal_Territories_Southern_New_England.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Tribal_Territories_Southern_New_England.png/450px-Tribal_Territories_Southern_New_England.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Tribal_Territories_Southern_New_England.png/600px-Tribal_Territories_Southern_New_England.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tribal_Territories_Southern_New_England.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>
</div>
<p>Prior to the arrival of Europeans on the eastern shore of <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, the area around <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay" title="Massachusetts Bay">Massachusetts Bay</a> was the territory of several <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian-speaking</a> tribes, including the <a href="/wiki/Massachusett" title="Massachusett">Massachusett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nauset" title="Nauset">Nauset</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wampanoag" title="Wampanoag">Wampanoag</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Pennacook" title="Pennacook">Pennacooks</a> occupied the <a href="/wiki/Merrimack_River" title="Merrimack River">Merrimack River</a> valley to the north, and the <a href="/wiki/Nipmuc" title="Nipmuc">Nipmuc</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pocumtuc" title="Pocumtuc">Pocumtuc</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mahican" title="Mahican">Mahican</a> occupied the western lands of present-day <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, although some of those tribes were under tribute to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohawk_nation" title="Mohawk nation">Mohawks</a>, who were expanding aggressively from present-day upstate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The total Indian population in 1620 has been estimated to be 7,000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H129_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-H129-3">[3]</a></sup> with the population of New England at 15–18,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This number was significantly larger as late as 1616; in later years, contemporary chroniclers interviewed Indians who described a major pestilence that killed between one and two thirds of the population. The land-use patterns of the natives included plots cleared for agricultural purposes, and woodland territories for the hunting of game. Land divisions between the tribes were well understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H129_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-H129-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the early 17th century, several European explorers charted the area, including <a href="/wiki/Samuel_de_Champlain" title="Samuel de Champlain">Samuel de Champlain</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Smith_(explorer)" title="John Smith (explorer)">John Smith</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Plans began in 1606 for the first permanent British settlements on the east coast of North America. On April 10, 1606, King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" title="James I of England">James I of England</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_VI_of_Scotland" title="James VI of Scotland">James VI of Scotland</a>) granted a charter forming two <a href="/wiki/Joint-stock_company" title="Joint-stock company">joint-stock companies</a>. Neither of these corporations was given a name by this charter, but the territories were named as the "first Colony" (fourth paragraph of charter) and "second Colony" (fifth paragraph of charter), over which they were respectively authorized to settle and to govern. Under this charter, the "first Colony" and the "second Colony" were to be ruled by a "Council" composed of 13 individuals in each colony. The charter provided for an additional council of 13 persons to have overarching responsibility for the combined enterprise. No name was given to either the company or council governing the respective colonies; the council governing the whole was named "Council of Virginia."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The "first Colony" ranged from the 34th- to 41st-degree latitude north; the "second Colony" ranged from the 38th- to 45th-degree latitude. (Note that the "first Colony" and the "second Colony" overlapped. The 1629 charter of Charles I asserted that the second Colony ranged from 40th to 48th degrees north latitude, which reduces the overlap.) The investors appointed to govern over any settlements in the "first Colony" were from London; the investors appointed to govern over any settlements in the "second Colony" were from the "Town of Plimouth in the County of Devon."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <a href="/wiki/London_Company" title="London Company">London Company</a> proceeded to establish <a href="/wiki/Jamestown,_Virginia" title="Jamestown, Virginia">Jamestown</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Company" title="Plymouth Company">Plymouth Company</a> under the guidance of Sir <a href="/wiki/Ferdinando_Gorges" title="Ferdinando Gorges">Ferdinando Gorges</a> covered the more northern area, including present-day New England, and established the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sagadahoc_Colony" title="Sagadahoc Colony">Sagadahoc Colony</a> in 1607 in present-day Maine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The experience proved exceptionally difficult for the 120 settlers, however, and the surviving colonists abandoned the colony after only one year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Gorges noted that "there was no more speech of settling plantations in those parts" for a number of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> English ships continued to come to the New England area for fishing and trade with the Indians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Plymouth_Colony">Plymouth Colony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Plymouth Colony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In December 1620, a group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilgrim_(Plymouth_Colony)" title="Pilgrim (Plymouth Colony)">Pilgrims</a> established <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Colony" title="Plymouth Colony">Plymouth Colony</a> just to the south of Massachusetts Bay, seeking to preserve their cultural identity and attain religious independence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Plymouth's colonists faced great hardships and earned few profits for their investors, who sold their interests to the settlers in 1627.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Edward_Winslow" title="Edward Winslow">Edward Winslow</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Bradford_(Plymouth_governor)" title="William Bradford (Plymouth governor)">William Bradford</a>, two of its leaders, were likely authors of a work published in England in 1622 called <i><a href="/wiki/Mourt%27s_Relation" title="Mourt's Relation">Mourt's Relation</a></i>. This book in some ways resembles a promotional tract intended to encourage further migration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> There were other short-lived colonial settlements in 1623 and 1624 at present-day <a href="/wiki/Weymouth,_Massachusetts" title="Weymouth, Massachusetts">Weymouth, Massachusetts</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Wessagusset_Colony" title="Wessagusset Colony">Wessagusset Colony</a> of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Weston_(merchant_adventurer)" title="Thomas Weston (merchant adventurer)">Thomas Weston</a> and an effort by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Gorges" title="Robert Gorges">Robert Gorges</a> to establish an overarching colonial structure both failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cape_Ann_settlement">Cape Ann settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cape Ann settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1623, the <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Council_for_New_England" title="Plymouth Council for New England">Plymouth Council for New England</a> (successor to the Plymouth Company) established a small fishing village at <a href="/wiki/Cape_Ann" title="Cape Ann">Cape Ann</a> under the supervision of the Dorchester Company, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Gardner_(Planter)" title="Thomas Gardner (Planter)">Thomas Gardner</a> as its overseer. This company was originally organized through the efforts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritan" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> minister <a href="/wiki/John_White_(colonist_priest)" title="John White (colonist priest)">John White</a> (1575–1648) of <a href="/wiki/Dorchester,_Dorset" title="Dorchester, Dorset">Dorchester</a>, in the English county of <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a>. White has been called "the father of the Massachusetts Colony" because of his influence in establishing this settlement and despite the fact that he never emigrated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The Cape Ann settlement was not profitable, and the financial backers of the Dorchester Company terminated their support by the end of 1625. Their settlement was abandoned at present-day <a href="/wiki/Gloucester,_Massachusetts" title="Gloucester, Massachusetts">Gloucester</a>, but a few settlers remained in the area, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roger_Conant_(Salem)" title="Roger Conant (Salem)">Roger Conant</a>, establishing a settlement a little further south, near the village of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naumkeag_tribe" title="Naumkeag tribe">Naumkeag tribe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_formation_of_the_colony">Legal formation of the colony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legal formation of the colony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Archbishop <a href="/wiki/William_Laud" title="William Laud">William Laud</a> was a favorite advisor of <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">King Charles I</a> and a dedicated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglican" title="Anglican">Anglican</a>, and he sought to suppress the religious practices of Puritans and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonconformism" title="Nonconformism">nonconforming beliefs</a> in England. The persecution of many Puritans in the 1620s led them to believe that religious reform would not be possible while Charles was king, and many decided to seek a new life in the New World.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>John White continued to seek funding for a colony. On 19 March 1627/8,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> the Council for New England issued a land grant to a new group of investors that included a few holdovers from the Dorchester Company. The land grant was for territory between the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Merrimack_River" title="Merrimack River">Merrimack</a> Rivers, including a three-mile (4.8 km) buffer to the north of the Merrimack and to the south of the Charles, that extended from "the Atlantick and westerne sea and ocean on the east parte, to the South sea on the west parte."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morison32_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morison32-21">[21]</a></sup> The company to whom the grant was sold was styled "The New England Company for a Plantation in Massachusetts Bay".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The company elected <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Cradock" title="Matthew Cradock">Matthew Cradock</a> as its first governor, and immediately began organizing provisions and recruiting settlers.</p>
<p>The company sent approximately 100 new settlers with provisions to join Conant in 1628, led by Governor's Assistant <a href="/wiki/John_Endecott" title="John Endecott">John Endecott</a>, one of the grantees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The next year, Naumkeag was renamed <a href="/wiki/Salem,_Massachusetts" title="Salem, Massachusetts">Salem</a> and fortified by another 300 settlers, led by Rev. <a href="/wiki/Francis_Higginson" title="Francis Higginson">Francis Higginson</a>, one of the first ministers of the settlement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The first winters were difficult, with colonists struggling against disease and starvation, resulting in a significant number of deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company leaders sought a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Charter" title="Royal Charter">Royal Charter</a> for the colony because they were concerned about the legality of conflicting land claims given to several companies (including the New England Company) for the little-known territories of the New World, and because of the increasing number of Puritans who wanted to join the company. Charles granted the new charter on 4 March 1628/9,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> superseding the land grant and establishing a legal basis for the new English colony at Massachusetts. It was not apparent whether Charles knew that the Company was meant to support the Puritan emigration, and he was likely left to assume that it was purely for business purposes, as was the custom. The charter omitted a significant clause: the location for the annual stockholders' meeting. Charles dissolved Parliament in 1629, whereupon the company's directors met to consider the possibility of moving the company's seat of governance to the colony. This was followed by the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Agreement" title="Cambridge Agreement">Cambridge Agreement</a> later that year, in which a group of investors agreed to emigrate and work to buy out others who would not emigrate.</p>
<p>The Massachusetts Bay Colony became the first English chartered colony whose board of governors did not reside in England. This independence helped the settlers to maintain their Puritan religious practices with very little oversight by the king, Archbishop Laud, and the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Church</a>. The charter remained in force for 55 years; <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> revoked it in 1684 as a result of colonial insubordination with trade, tariff, and navigation laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonial_history">Colonial history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Colonial history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A flotilla of ships sailed from England beginning in April 1630, sometimes known as the <a href="/wiki/Winthrop_Fleet" title="Winthrop Fleet">Winthrop Fleet</a>. The fleet began arriving at Salem in June and carried more than 700 colonists, Governor <a href="/wiki/John_Winthrop" title="John Winthrop">John Winthrop</a>, and the colonial charter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Winthrop is reputed to have delivered his famous "<a href="/wiki/City_upon_a_Hill" title="City upon a Hill">City upon a Hill</a>" sermon either before or during the voyage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sounding_board_detail_Old_Ship_Church.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="563" data-file-width="557" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Sounding_board_detail_Old_Ship_Church.jpg/250px-Sounding_board_detail_Old_Ship_Church.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Sounding_board_detail_Old_Ship_Church.jpg/375px-Sounding_board_detail_Old_Ship_Church.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Sounding_board_detail_Old_Ship_Church.jpg/500px-Sounding_board_detail_Old_Ship_Church.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sounding_board_detail_Old_Ship_Church.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Detail of <a href="/wiki/Sounding_board" title="Sounding board">sounding board</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Ship_Church" title="Old Ship Church">Old Ship Church</a> (1681), <a href="/wiki/Hingham,_Massachusetts" title="Hingham, Massachusetts">Hingham, Massachusetts</a>, oldest Puritan <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meetinghouse" title="Meetinghouse">meetinghouse</a> in Massachusetts.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>For the next ten years, there was a steady exodus of Puritans from England, with about 10,000 people emigrating to Massachusetts and the neighbouring colonies, a phenomenon now called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Migration_(Puritan)" title="Great Migration (Puritan)">Great Migration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Many ministers reacted to the repressive religious policies of England, making the trip with their flocks. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Cotton_(puritan)" title="John Cotton (puritan)">John Cotton</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roger_Williams_(theologian)" title="Roger Williams (theologian)">Roger Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hooker" title="Thomas Hooker">Thomas Hooker</a>, and others became leaders of Puritan congregations. Religious divisions and the need for additional land prompted a number of migrations that resulted in the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Colony" title="Connecticut Colony">Connecticut Colony</a> (by Hooker) and the <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_Rhode_Island_and_Providence_Plantations" title="Colony of Rhode Island and Providence Plantations">Colony of Rhode Island and Providence Plantations</a> (by Williams and <a href="/wiki/Anne_Hutchinson" title="Anne Hutchinson">Anne Hutchinson</a>). Minister <a href="/wiki/John_Wheelwright" title="John Wheelwright">John Wheelwright</a> was banished in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/Antinomian_Controversy" title="Antinomian Controversy">Antinomian Controversy</a> (like Anne Hutchinson), and he moved north to found <a href="/wiki/Exeter,_New_Hampshire" title="Exeter, New Hampshire">Exeter, New Hampshire</a>.</p>
<p>The advent of the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> in the early 1640s brought a halt to major migration, and a significant number of men returned to England to fight in the war. Massachusetts authorities were sympathetic to the <a href="/wiki/Roundhead" title="Roundhead">Parliamentary cause</a>, and had generally positive relationships with the governments of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Commonwealth" title="English Commonwealth">English Commonwealth</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Protectorate" title="The Protectorate">The Protectorate</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a>. The colony's economy began to diversify in the 1640s, as the fur trading, lumber, and fishing industries found markets in Europe and the West Indies, and the colony's shipbuilding industry developed. The growth of a generation of people who were born in the colony and the rise of a merchant class began to slowly change the political and cultural landscape of the colony, even though its governance continued to be dominated by relatively conservative Puritans.</p>
<p>Colonial support for the Commonwealth presented problems upon the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Restoration" title="English Restoration">restoration</a> to the throne of Charles II in 1660. Charles sought to extend royal influence over the colonies, which Massachusetts resisted more than the other colonies. For example, the colonial government repeatedly refused requests by Charles and his agents to allow the Church of England to become established, and it resisted adherence to the <a href="/wiki/Navigation_Acts" title="Navigation Acts">Navigation Acts</a>, laws that constrained colonial trade.</p>
<p>All of the New England colonies were ravaged by <a href="/wiki/King_Philip%27s_War" title="King Philip's War">King Philip's War</a> (1675–1676), when the Indians of southern New England rose up against the colonists and were decisively defeated, although at great cost in life to the colonies. The Massachusetts frontier was particularly hard hit, with several communities being abandoned in the <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_River" title="Connecticut River">Connecticut</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swift_River_(Ware_River)" title="Swift River (Ware River)">Swift</a> River valleys. By the end of the war, most of the Indian population of southern New England made peace treaties with the colonists.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revocation_of_charter">Revocation of charter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Revocation of charter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the English Restoration in 1660, matters of colonial administration drew the king's attention. Massachusetts in particular was reluctant to agree that the king had any sort of authority to control its governance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> This led to crises in the 1660s and late 1670s in which steps were first planned, and then executed in England to vacate the colonial charter. The <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Trade" title="Board of Trade">Lords of Trade</a> had decided for a variety of reasons to consolidate the New England colonies; they issued <i><a href="/wiki/Quo_warranto" title="Quo warranto">quo warranto</a></i> writs in 1681 for the charters of several North American colonies, including Massachusetts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The Massachusetts writ was never served for technical reasons, and the charter was not formally vacated until the chancery court issued a <i><a href="/wiki/Scire_facias" title="Scire facias">scire facias</a></i> writ formally annulling the charter on June 18, 1684.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The proceedings were arranged so that the time had expired for the colonial authorities to defend the charter, before they even learned of the event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unifications_and_restoration">Unifications and restoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Unifications and restoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1686, the colony's territory was administratively unified by <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">James II of England</a> with the other New England colonies in the <a href="/wiki/Dominion_of_New_England" title="Dominion of New England">Dominion of New England</a>. The dominion was governed by Sir <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Andros" title="Edmund Andros">Edmund Andros</a> without any local representation beyond hand-picked councillors, and was extremely unpopular in New England. Massachusetts authorities conspired to <a href="/wiki/1689_Boston_revolt" title="1689 Boston revolt">have Andros arrested</a> in April 1689 after the 1688 <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a> in England, and they reestablished government under the forms of the vacated charter. However, dissenters from the Puritan rule argued that the government lacked a proper constitutional foundation, and some of its actions were resisted on that basis. The years from 1689 to 1692 were also difficult ones, since the colony was at the forefront of <a href="/wiki/King_William%27s_War" title="King William's War">King William's War</a>, and its frontier communities were ravaged by attacks organized in <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a> and conducted by French and Indian raiding parties.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">King William III</a> issued a charter in 1691, despite efforts by Massachusetts agents to revive the old colonial charter. It was chiefly negotiated by <a href="/wiki/Increase_Mather" title="Increase Mather">Increase Mather</a> in his role as the colony's ambassador-extraordinary,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> unifying Massachusetts Bay with <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Colony" title="Plymouth Colony">Plymouth Colony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martha%27s_Vineyard" title="Martha's Vineyard">Martha's Vineyard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nantucket" title="Nantucket">Nantucket</a>, and territories that roughly encompass present-day <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> to form the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Massachusetts_Bay" title="Province of Massachusetts Bay">Province of Massachusetts Bay</a>. This new charter additionally extended voting rights to non-Puritans, an outcome that Mather had tried to avoid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-_37-0"><a href="#cite_note--37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Life could be quite difficult in the early years of the colony. Many colonists lived in fairly crude structures, including <a href="/wiki/Dugout_(shelter)" title="Dugout (shelter)">dugouts</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wigwams" title="Wigwams">wigwams</a>, and dirt-floor huts made using <a href="/wiki/Wattle_and_daub" title="Wattle and daub">wattle and daub</a> construction. Construction improved in later years, and houses began to be sheathed in <a href="/wiki/Clapboard_(architecture)" title="Clapboard (architecture)">clapboard</a>, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thatch" title="Thatch">thatch</a> or plank roofs and wooden chimneys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Wealthier individuals would extend their house by adding a leanto on the back, which allowed a larger kitchen (possibly with a brick or stone chimney including an oven), additional rooms, and a sleeping loft. These houses were the precursors to what is now called the <a href="/wiki/Saltbox" title="Saltbox">saltbox</a> style of architecture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Interiors became more elaborate in later years, with <a href="/wiki/Plaster" title="Plaster">plaster</a> walls, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wainscoting" title="Wainscoting">wainscoting</a>, and potentially expensive turned woodwork in the most expensive homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L59_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-L59-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Colonists arriving after the first wave found that the early towns did not have room for them. Seeking land of their own, groups of families would petition the government for land on which to establish a new town; the government would typically allow the group's leaders to select the land. These grants were typically about 40 square miles (10,000 ha), and were located sufficiently near other towns to facilitate defense and social support. The group leaders would also be responsible for acquiring <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_title_in_the_United_States" title="Aboriginal title in the United States">native title</a> to the lands that they selected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> By this means, the colony expanded into the interior, spawning settlements in adjacent territories as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The land within a town would be divided by communal agreement, usually allocating by methods that originated in England. Outside a town center, land would be allocated for farming, some of which might be held communally. Farmers with large plots of land might build a house near their properties on the outskirts of the town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> A town center that was well laid out would be fairly compact, with a tavern, school, possibly some small shops, and a <a href="/wiki/Colonial_meeting_house" title="Colonial meeting house">meeting house</a> that was used for civic and religious functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L59_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-L59-40">[40]</a></sup> The meeting house would be the center of the town's political and religious life. Church services might be held for several hours on Wednesday and all day Sunday. Puritans did not observe annual holidays, especially <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>, which they said had <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagan</a> roots. Annual town meetings would be held at the meeting house, generally in May, to elect the town's representatives to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_and_General_Court" title="Great and General Court">general court</a> and to transact other community business. Towns often had a <a href="/wiki/Village_green" title="Village green">village green</a>, used for outdoor celebrations and activities such as military exercises of the town's <a href="/wiki/Trainband" title="Trainband">trainband</a> or <a href="/wiki/Militia_(United_States)" title="Militia (United States)">militia</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_family_life">Marriage and family life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Marriage and family life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many of the early colonists who migrated from England came with some or all of their family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> It was expected that individuals would marry fairly young and begin producing offspring. Infant mortality rates were comparatively low, as were instances of childhood death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Men who lost their wives often remarried fairly quickly, especially if they had children needing care. Older widows would also sometimes marry for financial security. It was also normal for older widowed parents to live with one of their children. Due to the Puritan perception of marriage as a civil union, divorce did sometimes occur and could be pursued by both genders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sexual activity was expected to be confined to marriage. Sex outside of marriage was considered <a href="/wiki/Fornication" title="Fornication">fornication</a> if neither partner was married, and <a href="/wiki/Adultery" title="Adultery">adultery</a> if one or both were married to someone else. Fornication was generally punished by fines and pressure to marry; a woman who gave birth to an illegitimate child could also be fined. Adultery and <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rape</a> were more serious crimes, and both were punishable by death. Rape, however, required more than one witness, and was therefore rarely prosecuted. Sexual activity between men was called <a href="/wiki/Sodomy" title="Sodomy">sodomy</a>, and was also punishable by death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within the marriage, the husband was typically responsible for supplying the family's financial needs, although it was not uncommon for women to work in the fields and to perform some sort of home labor (for example, spinning thread or weaving cloth) to supplement the family income. Women were almost exclusively responsible for seeing to the welfare of the children.</p>
<p>Children were baptized at the local meeting house within a week of being born. The mother was usually not present because she was still recovering from the birth, and the child's name was usually chosen by the father. Names were propagated within the family, and names would be reused when infants died. If an adult died without issue, his (or her) name could be carried on when the siblings of the deceased named children in his memory.</p>
<p>Most children received some form of schooling, something which the colony's founders believed to be important for forming a proper relationship with God. Towns were obligated to provide education for their children, which was usually satisfied by hiring a teacher of some sort. The quality of these instructors varied, from minimally educated local people to Harvard-educated ministers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The structure of the colonial government changed over the lifetime of the charter. The <a href="/wiki/Charter_colony" title="Charter colony">colonial charter</a> was designed for the management of a corporation, and the needs of the colonial government did not always fit well into this model. The result was that the government began with a corporate organization that included a governor and deputy governor, a general court of its shareholders (known as "<a href="/wiki/Freeman_(Colonial)" title="Freeman (Colonial)">freemen</a>"), and a council of assistants similar to a <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H103_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-H103-48">[48]</a></sup> It ended with a governor and deputy governor, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicameral_legislature" title="Bicameral legislature">bicameral legislature</a> that included a representative lower house, and a body of freemen, a subset of the colony's adult inhabitants who were authorized to vote in elections. The council of assistants sat as the upper house of the legislature and served as the judicial court of last appeal.</p>

<p>The charter granted the general court the authority to elect officers and to make laws for the colony. Its first meeting in America was held in October 1630, but it was attended by only eight freemen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H103_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-H103-48">[48]</a></sup> They formed the first council of assistants, and voted (contrary to the terms of the charter) that the governor and deputy should be elected by them, from their number.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H105_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-H105-49">[49]</a></sup> This was modified in the next session of the general court, in which the governor and deputy were to be elected by the general court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H106_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-H106-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>An additional 116 settlers were admitted to the general court as freemen in 1631, but most of the governing power, as well as the judicial power, remained with the council of assistants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> They also enacted a law specifying that only those men who "are members of some of the churches" in the colony were eligible to become freemen and gain the vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H105_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-H105-49">[49]</a></sup> This restriction on the franchise was not liberalized until after the English Restoration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H106_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-H106-50">[50]</a></sup> The process by which individuals became members of one of the colony's churches involved a detailed questioning by the church elders of their beliefs and religious experiences; as a result, only individuals whose religious views accorded with those of the church leadership were likely to become members and gain the ability to vote in the colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> After a protest over the imposition of taxes by a meeting of the council of assistants, the general court ordered each town to send two representatives, known as deputies, to meet with the court to discuss matters of taxation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H107_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-H107-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Questions of governance and representation arose again in 1634, when several deputies demanded to see the charter, which the assistants had kept hidden from public view. The deputies learned of the provisions that the general court should make all laws, and that all freemen should be members of the general court. They then demanded that the charter be enforced to the letter, which Governor Winthrop pointed out was impractical given the growing number of freemen. The parties reached a compromise, and agreed that the general court would be made up of two deputies elected by each town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H107_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-H107-53">[53]</a></sup> The 1634 election resulted in the election of Dudley as governor, and the general court proceeded to reserve for itself a large number of powers, including those of taxation, distribution of land, and the admission of freemen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The transformation was complete: a trading company had become a (somewhat) representative democracy. A legal case in 1642 brought about the separation of the council of assistants into an upper house of the general court. The case involved a widow's lost pig and had been overturned by the general court; but the assistants had sat in judicial decision on the case and voted as a body to veto the general court's act.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The consequence of the ensuing debate was that the general court voted in 1644 that the council of assistants would sit and deliberate separately from the general court (they had sat together until then), the concurrence of both bodies being required for the passage of legislation. Judicial appeals were to be decided by a joint session, since otherwise the assistants would be in the position to veto attempts to overturn their own decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> A group of emigrants had bought all the Massachusetts Bay Company's stock and brought the Charter to America in 1630; neither the English king nor Parliament nor an English company exerted any influence in Massachusetts Bay Colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> So it was in effect a self-ruling republic for some decades, also practicing <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_powers" title="Separation of powers">separation of powers</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laws_and_judiciary">Laws and judiciary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Laws and judiciary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1641, the colony formally adopted its first code of laws, the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Body_of_Liberties" title="Massachusetts Body of Liberties">Massachusetts Body of Liberties</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> written or compiled by <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Ward" title="Nathaniel Ward">Nathaniel Ward</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MassBOL_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-MassBOL-59">[59]</a></sup> This document consisted of 100 civil and criminal laws based upon the social sanctions recorded in the Bible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dow200_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dow200-60">[60]</a></sup> These laws formed the nucleus of colonial legislation until independence, and contained some provisions that were fairly advanced for the time. Among these provisions were the ideas of <a href="/wiki/Equal_Protection_Clause" title="Equal Protection Clause">equal protection</a> and <a href="/wiki/Double_jeopardy" title="Double jeopardy">double jeopardy</a> that were later enshrined in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MassBOL_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-MassBOL-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many things were frowned upon culturally which modern readers might consider relatively trivial actions, and some led to criminal prosecution. These included smoking <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a>, abusing one's mother-in-law, profane dancing, pulling hair, sleeping during church services, riding behind two men (a woman named Lydia was convicted of this), playing cards, and engaging in any number of activities on the Sabbath.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>The colony's council of assistants sat as the final court of appeal, and as the principal court for criminal issues of "life, limb, or banishment" and civil issues where the damages exceeded £100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dow200_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dow200-60">[60]</a></sup> Lesser offenses were heard in county courts or by commissioners appointed for hearing minor disputes. The lower courts were also responsible for issuing licenses and for matters such as probate. Juries were authorized to decide questions of both fact and law, although the court was able to decide in the event that a jury failed to reach a decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Sentences for offenses included fines and corporal punishments such as <a href="/wiki/Flagellation" title="Flagellation">whipping</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stocks" title="Stocks">sitting in the stocks</a>, with the punishments of banishment from the colony and death by hanging being reserved for the most serious offenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Evidence was sometimes based on hearsay and superstition; for example, the "ordeal of touch" was used in 1646 (in which someone accused of murder is forced to touch the dead body; if blood appears, the accused is deemed guilty) to convict and execute a woman accused of murdering her newborn child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Bodies of individuals hanged for <a href="/wiki/Piracy" title="Piracy">piracy</a> were sometimes <a href="/wiki/Gibbeting" title="Gibbeting">gibbeted</a> (publicly displayed) on harbor islands visible to seagoing vessels. (<a href="/wiki/Nixes_Mate" title="Nixes Mate">Nixes Mate</a> was one such site where pirate <a href="/wiki/William_Fly" title="William Fly">William Fly</a>'s body was displayed, now little more than a granite outcrop.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_criminal_prosecutions">Notable criminal prosecutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Notable criminal prosecutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>One of the first people to be executed in the colony was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorothy_Talbye_Trial" title="Dorothy Talbye Trial">Dorothy Talbye</a>, who was apparently delusional. She was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hanged" title="Hanged">hanged</a> in 1638 for murdering her daughter, as the <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> of Massachusetts made no distinction at the time between <a href="/wiki/Insanity" title="Insanity">insanity</a> (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mental_illness" title="Mental illness">mental illness</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criminal" title="Criminal">criminal</a> behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Midwife <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Jones_(Puritan_midwife)" title="Margaret Jones (Puritan midwife)">Margaret Jones</a> was convicted of being a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Witch" title="Witch">witch</a> and hanged in 1648 after the condition of patients allegedly worsened in her care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>The colonial leadership was the most active in New England in the persecution of <a href="/wiki/Quakers" title="Quakers">Quakers</a>. In 1660, one of the most notable instances was English Quaker <a href="/wiki/Mary_Dyer" title="Mary Dyer">Mary Dyer</a> who was hanged in Boston for repeatedly defying a law banning Quakers from the colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SDC_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-SDC-68">[68]</a></sup> Dyer was one of the four executed Quakers known as the <a href="/wiki/Boston_martyrs" title="Boston martyrs">Boston martyrs</a>. Executions ceased in 1661 when <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">King Charles II</a> explicitly forbade Massachusetts from executing anyone for professing Quakerism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CHLS_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-CHLS-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_England_Confederation">New England Confederation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: New England Confederation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1643, Massachusetts Bay joined <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Colony" title="Plymouth Colony">Plymouth Colony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Colony" title="Connecticut Colony">Connecticut Colony</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Haven_Colony" title="New Haven Colony">New Haven Colony</a> in the <a href="/wiki/New_England_Confederation" title="New England Confederation">New England Confederation</a>, a loose coalition organized primarily to coordinate military and administrative matters among the Puritan colonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> It was most active in the 1670s during King Philip's War.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> (New Hampshire had not yet been organized as a separate province, and both it and Rhode Island were excluded because they were not Puritan.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy_and_trade">Economy and trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Economy and trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early years, the colony was highly dependent on the import of staples from England and was supported by the investments of a number of wealthy immigrants. Certain businesses were quick to thrive, notably shipbuilding, fisheries, and the fur and lumber trades. As early as 1632, ships built in the colony began trading with other colonies, England, and foreign ports in Europe. By 1660, the colony's merchant fleet was estimated at 200 ships and, by the end of the century, its shipyards were estimated to turn out several hundred ships annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> In the early years, the fleet principally carried fish to destinations from the West Indies to Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> It was common for a merchant to ship dried fish to Portugal or Spain, pick up wine and oil for transport to England, and then carry finished goods from England or elsewhere back to the colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> This and other patterns of trade became illegal following the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Navigation_Acts" title="Navigation Acts">Navigation Acts</a> in 1651, turning colonial merchants who continued these trading patterns into <i>de facto</i> smugglers. Many colonial authorities were merchants or were politically dependent on them, and they opposed being required by the crown to collect duties imposed by those acts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fur trade only played a modest role in the colony's economy because its rivers did not connect its centers well with the Indians who engaged in fur trapping. Timber began to take on an increasingly important role in the economy, especially for naval purposes, after conflicts between England and the Dutch depleted England's supplies of ship masts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<p>The colony's economy depended on the success of its trade, in part because its land was not as suitable for agriculture as that of other colonies such as <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_Virginia" title="Colony of Virginia">Virginia</a>, where large plantations could be established. The fishery was important enough that those involved in it were exempted from taxation and military service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Larger communities supported craftsmen skilled in providing many of the necessities of 17th century life. Some income-producing activities took place in the home, such as <a href="/wiki/Carding" title="Carding">carding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spinning_(textiles)" title="Spinning (textiles)">spinning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">weaving</a> of wool and other fibers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>Goods were transported to local markets over roads that were sometimes little more than widened Indian trails.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Towns were required to maintain their roads, on penalty of fines, and the colony required special town commissions to lay out roads in a more sensible manner in 1639. Bridges were fairly uncommon, since they were expensive to maintain, and fines were imposed on their owners for the loss of life or goods if they failed. Consequently, most river crossings were made by ferry. Notable exceptions were a bridge across the Mystic River constructed in 1638, and another over the <a href="/wiki/Saugus_River" title="Saugus River">Saugus River</a>, whose upkeep costs were subsidized by the colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>The colonial government attempted to regulate the economy in a number of ways. On several occasions, it passed laws regulating wages and prices of economically important goods and services, but most of these initiatives did not last very long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> The trades of shoe-making and coopering (barrel-making) were authorized to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guilds" title="Guilds">guilds</a>, making it possible to set price, quality, and expertise levels for their work. The colony set standards governing the use of weights and measures. For example, mill operators were required to weigh grain before and after milling, to ensure that the customer received back what he delivered (minus the miller's percentage).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Puritan dislike of ostentation led the colony to also regulate expenditures on what it perceived as luxury items. Items of personal adornment were frowned upon, such as lace and costly silk outerwear in particular. Attempts to ban these items failed, and the colony resorted to laws restricting their display to those who could demonstrate £200 in assets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of the people who arrived during the first 12 years emigrated from two regions of England. Many of the colonists came from the county of <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a>, northeast of London, and a large group also came from <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a> in the southwest of England. These areas provided the bulk of the migration, although colonists also came from other regions of England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> The pattern of migration often centered around specific Nonconformist clergy who sought to leave England under threat from Archbishop Laud, who encouraged their flock to accompany them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> One characteristic unique to the New England colonies (as distinguished from some of the other English colonies) was that most of the immigrants were emigrating for religious and political reasons, rather than economic ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>The preponderance of the immigrants were well-to-do gentry and skilled craftsmen. They brought with them apprentices and servants, the latter of whom were sometimes in <a href="/wiki/Indentured_servant" title="Indentured servant">indentured servitude</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H54_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-H54-88">[88]</a></sup> Few titled nobility emigrated, even though some supported the emigration politically and financially and also acquired land holdings in Massachusetts and other colonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Merchants also represented a significant proportion of the migrants, often the children of the gentry, and they played an important role in establishing the economy of the colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H54_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-H54-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the start of the English Civil War in 1642, emigration came to a comparative standstill, and some colonists even returned to England to fight for the <a href="/wiki/Roundhead" title="Roundhead">Parliamentary cause</a>. In the following years, most of the immigrants came for economic reasons; they were merchants, seamen, and skilled craftsmen. Following the revocation of the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Nantes" title="Edict of Nantes">Edict of Nantes</a> in 1685, the colony also saw in an influx of French Protestant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huguenots" title="Huguenots">Huguenots</a>. During the period of the charter colony, small numbers of <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scots</a> immigrated, but these were assimilated into the colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> The population of Massachusetts remained largely English in character until the 1840s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">Slavery</a> existed but was not widespread within the colony. Some Indians captured in the Pequot War were enslaved, with those posing the greatest threat being transported to the West Indies and exchanged for goods and slaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> Governor John Winthrop owned a few Indian slaves,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> and Governor <a href="/wiki/Simon_Bradstreet" title="Simon Bradstreet">Simon Bradstreet</a> owned two black slaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> The Body of Liberties enacted in 1641 included rules governing the treatment and handling of slaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> Bradstreet reported in 1680 that the colony had 100 to 120 slaves, but historian Hugh Thomas documents evidence suggesting that there may have been a somewhat larger number.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Massachusetts" title="Geography of Massachusetts">Geography of Massachusetts</a></div>
<p>The Massachusetts colony was dominated by its rivers and coastline. Major rivers included the Charles and Merrimack, as well as a portion of the <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_River" title="Connecticut River">Connecticut River</a>, which has been used to transport furs and timbers to <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Sound" title="Long Island Sound">Long Island Sound</a>. Cape Ann juts into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Maine" title="Gulf of Maine">Gulf of Maine</a>, providing harbors for fishermen plying the fishing banks to the east, and Boston's harbor provided secure anchorage for seagoing commercial vessels. Development in Maine was restricted to coastal areas, and large inland areas remained under native control until after King Philip's War, particularly the uplands in what is now <a href="/wiki/Worcester_County,_Massachusetts" title="Worcester County, Massachusetts">Worcester County</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boundaries">Boundaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Massachusetts_Bay_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Boundaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Northern_boundary_of_Massachusetts" title="Northern boundary of Massachusetts">Northern boundary of Massachusetts</a></div>
<p>The colonial charter specified that the boundaries were to be from three miles (4.8 km) north of the Merrimack River to three miles south of the Charles River and westward to the "South Sea" (i.e., the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morison32_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morison32-21">[21]</a></sup> At the time, the course of neither of the rivers was known for any significant length, which eventually led to boundary disputes with the colony's neighbors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> The colony's claims were large, but the practicalities of the time meant that they never actually controlled any land further west than the Connecticut River valley. The colony also claimed additional lands by conquest and purchase, further extending the territory that it administered.</p>
<p>The southeastern boundary with the Plymouth Colony was first surveyed in 1639 and accepted by both colonies in 1640.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> It is known in Massachusetts as the "Old Colony Line", and is still visible as the boundary between Norfolk County to the north and Bristol and Plymouth Counties to the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<p>The northern boundary was originally thought to be roughly parallel to the latitude of the mouth of the Merrimack River, since the river was assumed to flow primarily west. This was found not to be the case and, in 1652, Governor Endecott sent a survey party to locate the northernmost point on the Merrimack. Local Indians guided the party to the outlet of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Winnipesaukee" title="Lake Winnipesaukee">Lake Winnipesaukee</a>, incorrectly claiming that as the Merrimack's source. (The Merrimack's principal tributary goes significantly further north as the <a href="/wiki/Pemigewasset_River" title="Pemigewasset River">Pemigewasset River</a>.) The survey party carved lettering into a rock there (now called <a href="/wiki/Endicott_Rock" title="Endicott Rock">Endicott Rock</a>), and its latitude was taken to be the colony's northern boundary. When extended eastward, this line was found to meet the Atlantic near <a href="/wiki/Casco_Bay" title="Casco Bay">Casco Bay</a> in present-day Maine.</p>
<p>Following this discovery, the colonial magistrates began proceedings to bring existing settlements under their authority in southern New Hampshire and Maine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> This extension of the colonial claim conflicted with several proprietary grants owned by the heirs of <a href="/wiki/John_Mason_(governor)" title="John Mason (governor)">John Mason</a> and Sir <a href="/wiki/Ferdinando_Gorges" title="Ferdinando Gorges">Ferdinando Gorges</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> The Mason heirs pursued their claims in England, and the result was the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_New_Hampshire" title="Province of New Hampshire">Province of New Hampshire</a> in 1679.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> The current boundary between Massachusetts and New Hampshire was not fixed until the 18th century. In 1678, the colony purchased the claims of the Gorges heirs, gaining control over the territory between the Piscataqua and Kennebec Rivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> The colony and later the province and state retained control of Maine until it was granted statehood in 1820.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup></p>
<p>The colony performed a survey in 1642 to determine its southern boundary west to the Connecticut River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> This line, south of the present boundary, was protested by Connecticut, but stood until the 1690s, when Connecticut performed its own survey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> Most of today's Massachusetts boundaries with its neighbors were fixed in the 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> The most significant exception was the eastern boundary with Rhode Island, which required extensive litigation, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">Supreme Court</a> rulings, before it was finally resolved in the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lands which had previously belonged to the Pequots to the southwest were divided after the Pequot War in present-day Rhode Island and eastern Connecticut. Claims were disputed in this area for many years, particularly between Connecticut and Rhode Island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> Massachusetts administered <a href="/wiki/Block_Island" title="Block Island">Block Island</a> and the area around present-day <a href="/wiki/Stonington,_Connecticut" title="Stonington, Connecticut">Stonington, Connecticut</a> as part of these spoils of war,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> and was one of several claimants to land in what was known as Narragansett Country (roughly <a href="/wiki/Washington_County,_Rhode_Island" title="Washington County, Rhode Island">Washington County, Rhode Island</a>). Massachusetts lost all of these territories in the 1660s, when Connecticut and Rhode Island received their royal charters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup></p>
