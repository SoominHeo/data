<p><i><b>MathWorld</b></i> is an online <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> reference work, created and largely written by <a href="/wiki/Eric_W._Weisstein" title="Eric W. Weisstein">Eric W. Weisstein</a>. It is sponsored by and licensed to Wolfram Research, Inc. and was partially funded by the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a>'s <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Digital_Library" title="National Science Digital Library">National Science Digital Library</a> grant to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana%E2%80%93Champaign" title="University of Illinois at Urbana–Champaign">University of Illinois at Urbana–Champaign</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MathWorld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eric W. Weisstein, the creator of the site, was a <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> student who got into the habit of writing notes on his mathematical readings. In 1995 he put his notes online and called it "Eric's Treasure Trove of Mathematics". It contained hundreds of pages/articles, covering a wide range of mathematical topics. The site became popular as an extensive single resource on mathematics on the web. Weisstein continuously improved the notes and accepted corrections and comments from online readers. In 1998, he made a contract with <a href="/wiki/CRC_Press" title="CRC Press">CRC Press</a> and the contents of the site were published in print and <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROM</a>, titled "CRC Concise Encyclopedia of Mathematics". The free online version became only partially accessible to the public. In 1999 Weisstein went to work for Wolfram Research, Inc. (WRI), and WRI renamed the Math Treasure Trove to <i>MathWorld</i> and hosted it on the company's website<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> without access restrictions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="CRC_lawsuit">CRC lawsuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MathWorld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: CRC lawsuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2000, CRC Press sued WRI, WRI president <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Wolfram" title="Stephen Wolfram">Stephen Wolfram</a>, and author Eric Weisstein, due to what they considered a breach of contract: that the <i>MathWorld</i> content was to remain in print only. The site was taken down by a court injunction. The case was later settled out of court, with WRI paying an unspecified amount and complying with other <a href="/wiki/Stipulation" title="Stipulation">stipulations</a>. Among these stipulations is the inclusion of a copyright notice at the bottom of the website and broad rights for the CRC Press to produce <i>MathWorld</i> in printed book form. The site then became once again available free to the public.</p>
<p>This case made a wave of headlines in online publishing circles. The <i><a href="/wiki/PlanetMath" title="PlanetMath">PlanetMath</a></i> project was a result of MathWorld's being unavailable.</p>
