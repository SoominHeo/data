<p>In the <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802" title="IEEE 802">IEEE 802</a> reference model of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_networking" title="Computer networking">computer networking</a>, the <b>medium access control</b> or <b>media access control</b> (<b>MAC</b>) layer is the lower sublayer of the <a href="/wiki/Data_link_layer" title="Data link layer">data link layer</a> (layer 2) of the seven-layer <a href="/wiki/OSI_model" title="OSI model">OSI model</a>. The MAC sublayer provides addressing and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Channel_access" title="Channel access">channel access</a> control mechanisms that make it possible for several <a href="/wiki/Terminal_(telecommunication)" title="Terminal (telecommunication)">terminals</a> or network nodes to communicate within a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiple_access" title="Multiple access">multiple access</a> network that incorporates a shared medium, e.g. an <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> network. The hardware that implements the MAC is referred to as a <i>media access controller</i>.</p>
<p>The MAC sublayer acts as an interface between the <a href="/wiki/Logical_link_control" title="Logical link control">logical link control</a> (LLC) sublayer and the network's <a href="/wiki/Physical_layer" title="Physical layer">physical layer</a>. The MAC layer emulates a full-duplex logical communication channel in a multi-point network. This channel may provide <a href="/wiki/Unicast" title="Unicast">unicast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multicast" title="Multicast">multicast</a> or <a href="/wiki/Broadcasting_(networking)" title="Broadcasting (networking)">broadcast</a> communication service.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions_performed_in_the_MAC_sublayer">Functions performed in the MAC sublayer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Media_access_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Functions performed in the MAC sublayer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to IEEE Std 802-2001 section 6.2.3 "MAC sublayer", the primary functions performed by the MAC layer are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Frame delimiting and recognition</li>
<li>Addressing of destination stations (both as individual stations and as groups of stations)</li>
<li>Conveyance of source-station addressing information</li>
<li>Transparent data transfer of LLC PDUs, or of equivalent information in the Ethernet sublayer</li>
<li>Protection against errors, generally by means of generating and checking frame check sequences</li>
<li>Control of access to the physical transmission medium</li>
</ul>
<p>In the case of <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a>, according to 802.3-2002 section 4.1.4, the functions required of a MAC are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>receive/transmit normal frames</li>
<li>half-duplex retransmission and backoff functions</li>
<li>append/check FCS (<a href="/wiki/Frame_check_sequence" title="Frame check sequence">frame check sequence</a>)</li>
<li>interframe gap enforcement</li>
<li>discard malformed frames</li>
<li>prepend(tx)/remove(rx) preamble, SFD (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Start_frame_delimiter" title="Start frame delimiter">start frame delimiter</a>), and padding</li>
<li>half-duplex compatibility: append(tx)/remove(rx) MAC address</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Addressing_mechanism">Addressing mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Media_access_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Addressing mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The local network addresses used in <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802" title="IEEE 802">IEEE 802</a> networks and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FDDI" title="FDDI">FDDI</a> networks are called <a href="/wiki/MAC_address" title="MAC address">media access control addresses</a>; they are based on the addressing scheme that was used in early <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> implementations. A MAC address is intended as a unique serial number. MAC addresses are typically assigned to network interface hardware at the time of manufacture. The most significant part of the address identifies the manufacturer, who assigns the remainder of the address, thus provide a potentially unique address. This makes it possible for frames to be delivered on a network link that interconnects hosts by some combination of <a href="/wiki/Repeater" title="Repeater">repeaters</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Network_hub" title="Network hub">hubs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Network_bridge" title="Network bridge">bridges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Network_switch" title="Network switch">switches</a>, but not by <a href="/wiki/Network_layer" title="Network layer">network layer</a> <a href="/wiki/Router_(computing)" title="Router (computing)">routers</a>. Thus, for example, when an <a href="/wiki/Internet_Protocol" title="Internet Protocol">IP</a> packet reaches its destination (sub)network, the destination IP address (a layer 3 or network layer concept) is resolved with the <a href="/wiki/Address_Resolution_Protocol" title="Address Resolution Protocol">Address Resolution Protocol</a> for <a href="/wiki/IPv4" title="IPv4">IPv4</a>, or by <a href="/wiki/Neighbor_Discovery_Protocol" title="Neighbor Discovery Protocol">Neighbor Discovery Protocol</a> (IPv6) into the MAC address (a layer 2 concept) of the destination host.</p>
<p>Examples of physical networks are <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> networks and <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> networks, both of which are IEEE 802 networks and use IEEE 802 48-bit MAC addresses.</p>
<p>A MAC layer is not required in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full-duplex" title="Full-duplex">full-duplex</a> <a href="/wiki/Point-to-point_(telecommunications)" title="Point-to-point (telecommunications)">point-to-point</a> communication, but address fields are included in some point-to-point protocols for compatibility reasons.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Channel_access_control_mechanism">Channel access control mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Media_access_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Channel access control mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The channel access control mechanisms provided by the MAC layer are also known as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiple_access_protocol" title="Multiple access protocol">multiple access protocol</a>. This makes it possible for several stations connected to the same <a href="/wiki/Transmission_medium" title="Transmission medium">physical medium</a> to share it. Examples of shared physical media are <a href="/wiki/Bus_network" title="Bus network">bus networks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ring_network" title="Ring network">ring networks</a>, hub networks, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wireless_networks" title="Wireless networks">wireless networks</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half-duplex" title="Half-duplex">half-duplex</a> point-to-point links. The multiple access protocol may detect or avoid data packet <a href="/wiki/Collision_(telecommunications)" title="Collision (telecommunications)">collisions</a> if a packet mode <a href="/wiki/Contention_(telecommunications)" title="Contention (telecommunications)">contention</a> based <a href="/wiki/Channel_access_method" title="Channel access method">channel access method</a> is used, or reserve resources to establish a logical channel if a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circuit-switched" title="Circuit-switched">circuit-switched</a> or channelization-based channel access method is used. The channel access control mechanism relies on a physical layer <a href="/wiki/Multiplexing" title="Multiplexing">multiplex</a> scheme.</p>
<p>The most widespread multiple access protocol is the contention based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CSMA/CD" title="CSMA/CD">CSMA/CD</a> protocol used in Ethernet networks. This mechanism is only utilized within a network collision domain, for example an Ethernet bus network or a hub-based star topology network. An Ethernet network may be divided into several collision domains, interconnected by bridges and switches.</p>
<p>A multiple access protocol is not required in a switched <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full-duplex" title="Full-duplex">full-duplex</a> network, such as today's switched Ethernet networks, but is often available in the equipment for compatibility reasons.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="MAC_in_cellular_networks">MAC in cellular networks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Media_access_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: MAC in cellular networks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cellular_network" title="Cellular network">Cellular networks</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/GSM" title="GSM">GSM</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UMTS" title="UMTS">UMTS</a> or <a href="/wiki/LTE_(telecommunication)" title="LTE (telecommunication)">LTE</a> networks, also use a MAC layer. The MAC protocol in cellular networks is designed to maximize the utilization of the expensive licensed spectrum. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miao_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miao-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Air_interface" title="Air interface">air interface</a> of a cellular network is at layers 1 and 2 of the OSI model; at layer 2, it is divided into multiple protocol layers. In UMTS and LTE, those protocols are the <a href="/wiki/PDCP" title="PDCP">Packet Data Convergence Protocol</a> (PDCP), the <a href="/wiki/Radio_Link_Control" title="Radio Link Control">Radio Link Control</a> (RLC) protocol, and the MAC protocol. The base station has the absolute control over the air interface and schedules the downlink access as well as the uplink access of all devices. The MAC protocol is specified by <a href="/wiki/3GPP" title="3GPP">3GPP</a> in TS 25.321<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> for UMTS and TS 36.321<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> for LTE.</p>
