<p><b>2060 Chiron</b>, also known as <b>95P/Chiron</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Minor_planet" title="Minor planet">minor planet</a> in the outer <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbiting</a> the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> between <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a>. Discovered in 1977 by <a href="/wiki/Charles_T._Kowal" title="Charles T. Kowal">Charles T. Kowal</a> (<a href="/wiki/Precovery" title="Precovery">precovery</a> images have been found as far back as 1895),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perihelion_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Perihelion-7">[7]</a></sup> it was the first-identified member of a new class of objects now known as <a href="/wiki/Centaur_(minor_planet)" title="Centaur (minor planet)">centaurs</a>—bodies orbiting between the <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">asteroid belt</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">Kuiper belt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[a]</a></sup> Besides the four giant planets, Chiron and <a href="/wiki/10199_Chariklo" title="10199 Chariklo">10199 Chariklo</a>, also a centaur, are the only other bodies in the Solar System known to have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_ring" title="Planetary ring">rings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spacedaily_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-spacedaily-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although it was initially called an <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> and classified only as a <a href="/wiki/Minor_planet" title="Minor planet">minor planet</a> with the designation "2060 Chiron", it was later found to exhibit behavior typical of a <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a>. Today it is classified as both a minor planet and a comet, and is accordingly also known by the cometary designation "95P/Chiron".</p>
<p>Chiron is named after the <a href="/wiki/Centaur" title="Centaur">centaur</a> <a href="/wiki/Chiron" title="Chiron">Chiron</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Brown" title="Michael E. Brown">Michael Brown</a> lists it as <a href="/wiki/List_of_possible_dwarf_planets" title="List of possible dwarf planets">possibly a dwarf planet</a> with a measured diameter of 206 km<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown-dplist_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-dplist-10">[9]</a></sup> (128 miles) which is near the lower limit for an icy dwarf planet (around 200 km, or 124 miles).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2060_Chiron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2060_Chiron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chiron was discovered on 1 November 1977 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Kowal" title="Charles Kowal">Charles Kowal</a> from images taken on 18 October at <a href="/wiki/Palomar_Observatory" title="Palomar Observatory">Palomar Observatory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kowal_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kowal-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> It was given the temporary designation of <b>1977 UB</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campins_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Campins-13">[12]</a></sup> It was found near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphelion" title="Aphelion">aphelion</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kowal_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kowal-11">[10]</a></sup> and at the time of discovery it was the most distant known minor planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[b]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campins_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Campins-13">[12]</a></sup> Chiron was even claimed as the <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Tenth_planet_(disambiguation)" title="Tenth planet (disambiguation)">tenth planet</a> by the press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collander-Brown2000_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collander-Brown2000-15">[13]</a></sup> Chiron was later found on several <a href="/wiki/Precovery" title="Precovery">precovery</a> images, going back to 1895, which allowed its orbit to be accurately determined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kowal_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kowal-11">[10]</a></sup> It had been at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a> in 1945 but was not discovered then because there were few searches being made at that time, and these were not sensitive to slow-moving objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kowal_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kowal-11">[10]</a></sup> The Lowell Observatory's survey for distant planets would not have gone down faint enough in the 1930s and did not cover the right region of the sky in the 1940s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kowal_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Kowal-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2060_Chiron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was named Chiron in 1979<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campins_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Campins-13">[12]</a></sup> after <a href="/wiki/Chiron" title="Chiron">Chiron</a>, one of the <a href="/wiki/Centaur" title="Centaur">centaurs</a>; it was suggested that the names of other centaurs be reserved for objects of the same type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kowal_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-Kowal-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">Orbit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2060_Chiron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Orbit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chiron's orbit was found to be highly <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">eccentric</a> (0.37), with <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">perihelion</a> just inside the orbit of <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">aphelion</a> just outside the perihelion of <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> (it does not reach the average distance of Uranus, however). According to the program <a class="external text" href="http://chemistry.unina.it/~alvitagl/solex/" rel="nofollow">Solex</a>, Chiron's closest approach to Saturn in modern times was around May 720, when it came within 30.5±2.0 million km of Saturn. During this passage Saturn's gravity caused Chiron's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">semi-major axis</a> to decrease from 14.55±0.12 AU<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chiron700AD_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chiron700AD-16">[14]</a></sup> to 13.7 AU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-3">[3]</a></sup> It does not come nearly as close to Uranus; Chiron crosses Uranus's orbit where the latter is farther than average from the Sun.</p>
<p>Chiron attracted considerable interest because it was the first object discovered in such an orbit, well outside the <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">asteroid belt</a>. Chiron is classified as a centaur, the first of a class of objects orbiting between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outer_planet" title="Outer planet">outer planets</a>. Chiron is a Saturn–Uranus object because its perihelion lies in Saturn's zone of control and its aphelion lies in that of Uranus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horner2004_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Horner2004-17">[15]</a></sup> Centaurs are not in stable orbits and will be removed by gravitational <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">perturbation</a> by the giant planets over a period of millions of years, moving to different orbits or leaving the Solar System altogether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jewitt2006_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jewitt2006-18">[16]</a></sup> Chiron is probably a refugee from the <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">Kuiper belt</a> and will probably become a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short-period_comet" title="Short-period comet">short-period comet</a> in about a million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horner2004_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Horner2004-17">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chiron came to <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">perihelion</a> (closest point to the Sun) in 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The visible and near-<a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> spectrum of Chiron is neutral,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campins_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Campins-13">[12]</a></sup> and is similar to that of <a href="/wiki/C-type_asteroid" title="C-type asteroid">C-type asteroids</a> and the nucleus of <a href="/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet" title="Halley's Comet">Halley's Comet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luu_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Luu-21">[19]</a></sup> The near-infrared spectrum of Chiron shows absence of water ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luu2000_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Luu2000-22">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The assumed size of an object depends on its <a href="/wiki/Absolute_magnitude#Solar_System_bodies_.28H.29" title="Absolute magnitude">absolute magnitude</a> (H) and the <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> (the amount of light it reflects). In 1984 Lebofsky estimated Chiron to be about 180 km in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groussin2004_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Groussin2004-19">[17]</a></sup> Estimates in the 1990s were closer to 150 km in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groussin2004_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Groussin2004-19">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Occultation#Occultations_by_asteroids" title="Occultation">Occultation</a> data from 1993 suggests a diameter of about 180 km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groussin2004_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-Groussin2004-19">[17]</a></sup> Combined data from the <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">Spitzer Space Telescope</a> in 2007 and the Herschel Space Observatory in 2011 suggests that Chiron is <span class="nowrap">218 ± 20 km</span> in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOsCool8_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-TNOsCool8-4">[4]</a></sup> Therefore, Chiron may be as large as <a href="/wiki/10199_Chariklo" title="10199 Chariklo">10199 Chariklo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spitzer_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-spitzer-20">[18]</a></sup> The diameter of Chiron is difficult to estimate in part because the true absolute magnitude of its nucleus is uncertain due to its highly variable cometary activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOsCool8_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-TNOsCool8-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Its rotational period is 5.917813 hours, a value determined by observing its distinct light curve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campins_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Campins-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cometary_behavior">Cometary behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2060_Chiron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cometary behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 1988, at 12 AU from the Sun, Chiron brightened by 75 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hartmann1990_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-hartmann1990-23">[21]</a></sup> This is behavior typical of comets but not asteroids. Further observations in April 1989 showed that Chiron had developed a cometary <a href="/wiki/Coma_(cometary)" title="Coma (cometary)">coma</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meech1989_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-meech1989-24">[22]</a></sup> A tail was detected in 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campins_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-Campins-13">[12]</a></sup> Chiron differs from other comets in that <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> is not a major component of its coma, because it is too far from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> for water to sublimate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meech90_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meech90-25">[23]</a></sup> At the time of its discovery, Chiron was close to aphelion, whereas the observations showing a coma were done closer to perihelion, perhaps explaining why no cometary behavior had been seen earlier. The fact that Chiron is still active probably means it has not been in its current orbit very long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perihelion_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Perihelion-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chiron is officially designated as both a comet—95P/Chiron—and a minor planet,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOsCool8_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-TNOsCool8-4">[4]</a></sup> an indication of the sometimes fuzzy dividing line between the two classes of object. The term proto-comet has also been used. Being about 220 km in diameter, it is unusually large for a <a href="/wiki/Comet_nucleus" title="Comet nucleus">comet nucleus</a>. Chiron was the first member of a new family of Chiron-type comets with (<a href="/wiki/Tisserand%27s_parameter" title="Tisserand's parameter">T<sub>Jupiter</sub></a> &gt; 3; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">a</a> &gt; a<sub>Jupiter</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-3">[3]</a></sup> Other Chiron-type comets include: <a href="/wiki/39P/Oterma" title="39P/Oterma">39P/Oterma</a>, <a href="/wiki/165P/LINEAR" title="165P/LINEAR">165P/LINEAR</a>, <a href="/wiki/166P/NEAT" title="166P/NEAT">166P/NEAT</a>, and <a href="/wiki/167P/CINEOS" title="167P/CINEOS">167P/CINEOS</a>. There are also non-centaurs that are classified as comets, such as <a href="/wiki/4015_Wilson%E2%80%93Harrington" title="4015 Wilson–Harrington">4015 Wilson–Harrington</a>, <a href="/wiki/7968_Elst%E2%80%93Pizarro" title="7968 Elst–Pizarro">7968 Elst–Pizarro</a>, and <a href="/wiki/118401_LINEAR" title="118401 LINEAR">118401 LINEAR</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dualstatus_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-dualstatus-26">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the discovery of Chiron, other centaurs have been discovered, and nearly all are currently classified as minor planets, but are being observed for possible cometary behavior. <a href="/wiki/60558_Echeclus" title="60558 Echeclus">60558 Echeclus</a> has displayed a cometary coma and now also has the cometary designation 174P/Echeclus. After passing perihelion in early 2008, <a href="/wiki/52872_Okyrhoe" title="52872 Okyrhoe">52872 Okyrhoe</a> significantly brightened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cometary_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-cometary-27">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rings">Rings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2060_Chiron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chiron is suspected to have two rings,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakdawalla2015_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lakdawalla2015-28">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ortiz2015_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ortiz2015-29">[27]</a></sup> similar to the better-established <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Chariklo" title="Rings of Chariklo">rings of</a> <a href="/wiki/10199_Chariklo" title="10199 Chariklo">Chariklo</a>. Based on unexpected occultation events observed in stellar-occultation data obtained on 7 November 1993, 9 March 1994, and 29 November 2011, which were initially interpreted as resulting from jets associated with Chiron's comet-like activity, Chiron's rings are proposed to be 324 (± 10) km in radius and sharply defined. Their changing appearance at different viewing angles can largely explain the long-term variation in Chiron's brightness and hence estimates of Chiron's albedo and size. Moreover, it can, by assuming that the water ice is in Chiron's rings, explain the changing intensity of the infrared water-ice absorption bands in Chiron's spectrum, including their disappearance in 2001 (when the rings were edge-on). Also, the geometric albedo of Chiron's rings as determined by spectroscopy is consistent with that used to explain Chiron's long-term brightness variations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ortiz2015_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ortiz2015-29">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The preferred pole of Chiron's rings is, in <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinate_system" title="Ecliptic coordinate system">ecliptic coordinates</a>, <i>λ</i> = <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000251327412287183♠</span>144°<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>10°</span>, <i>β</i> = <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6999418879020478640♠</span>24°<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>10°</span>. The rings' width, separation, and optical depths are nearly identical to those of Chariklo's rings, indicating that the same type of structure is responsible for both. Moreover, both their rings are within their respective <a href="/wiki/Roche_limit" title="Roche limit">Roche limits</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ortiz2015_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ortiz2015-29">[27]</a></sup></p>
