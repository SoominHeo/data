<p><b>Mae Hong Son Province</b> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th" xml:lang="th">แม่ฮ่องสอน</span>, <small>pronounced</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Thai_and_Lao" title="Help:IPA for Thai and Lao">[mɛ̂ː.hɔ̂ŋ.sɔ̌ːn]</a></span>; formerly called <b>Mae Rong Son</b>), also spelled <i>Maehongson</i>, <i>Mae Hong Sorn</i> or <i>Maehongsorn</i>, is one of the northern <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Thailand" title="Provinces of Thailand">provinces</a> (<i>changwat</i>) of <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, on the country's western border. Neighboring provinces are (clockwise from north) <a href="/wiki/Shan_State" title="Shan State">Shan State</a> of <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiang_Mai_province" title="Chiang Mai province">Chiang Mai</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tak_province" title="Tak province">Tak</a>. To the west, the province borders <a href="/wiki/Kayin_State" title="Kayin State">Kayin State</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kayah_State" title="Kayah State">Kayah State</a> of <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>.</p>
<p>Mae Hong Son's nickname is "the city of three mists". It is hemmed in by the high <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">mountain ranges</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Shan_Hills" title="Shan Hills">Shan Hills</a> and is the most mountainous province in Thailand, occupying 13,814 square kilometres (5,334 sq mi). The province is often covered with mist. Mae Hong Son town was originally established in the early 19th century as an elephant training camp as ordered by the then King of Chiang Mai.</p>
<p>As of 2012<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mae_Hong_Son_Province&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> Mae Hong Son was the poorest province in Thailand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nation-20140923_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-nation-20140923-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Mae Hong Son Province is approximately 924 kilometres (574 mi) north of <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a>. To the north and west it connects to a total of three states in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_of_Burma" title="Union of Burma">Union of Burma</a>, namely the southern portion of Shan State, Kayah State, and <a href="/wiki/Kawthoolei" title="Kawthoolei">Kawthoolei State</a>, via the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Thanon_Thongchai_Mountains" title="West Thanon Thongchai Mountains">West Thanon Thongchai Mountains</a>, and the rivers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salween" title="Salween">Salween</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moei_River" title="Moei River">Moei</a>. These formations serve as natural boundaries between the countries. To the south, it connects to the district of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoe_Tha_Song_Yang" title="Amphoe Tha Song Yang">Tha Song Yang</a> and Tak, via the rivers <a href="/wiki/Yuam_River" title="Yuam River">Yuam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ngao_River" title="Ngao River">Ngao</a>, which serve as a provincial boundary. To the east it connects to the districts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoe_Wiang_Haeng" title="Amphoe Wiang Haeng">Wiang Haeng</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoe_Chiang_Dao" title="Amphoe Chiang Dao">Chiang Dao</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoe_Mae_Taeng" title="Amphoe Mae Taeng">Mae Taeng</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoe_Mae_Chaem" title="Amphoe Mae Chaem">Mae Chaem</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoe_Hot" title="Amphoe Hot">Hot</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoe_Omkoi" title="Amphoe Omkoi">Omkoi</a> in Chiang Mai Province, via the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Thanon_Thongchai_Mountains" title="Central Thanon Thongchai Mountains">central</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Thanon_Thongchai_Mountains" title="East Thanon Thongchai Mountains">east</a> sections of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thanon_Thongchai_Mountains" title="Thanon Thongchai Mountains">Thanon Thongchai</a> mountain ranges, which serve as a boundary between the two provinces.</p>
<p>Every district in Mae Hong Son Province shares a common border, measuring approximately 483 kilometres in total length, with Myanmar. The common border consists of approximately 326 km (203 mi) of land boundary and 157 km (98 mi) of river boundary (not counting the Salween, 127 km (79 mi), and Moei, 30 km (19 mi)).</p>
<p>Most of the areas of Mae Hong Son Province are the complex mountain ranges of the <a href="/wiki/Thai_highlands" title="Thai highlands">Thai highlands</a>, parts of which are still covered with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_forest" title="Rain forest">rain forest</a>. Of the approximately 6,976,650 <a href="/wiki/Rai_(unit)" title="Rai (unit)">rai</a> of national forest reserves, it is estimated that 88 percent is pristine virgin forest.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Daen_Lao_Range" title="Daen Lao Range">Daen Lao Range</a>, in the northernmost portion of the province, marks the northern boundary between Thailand and Burma, while the <a href="/wiki/Dawna_Range" title="Dawna Range">Dawna Range</a> in the west serves as the boundary between Thailand and Burma. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thanon_Thongchai_Range" title="Thanon Thongchai Range">Thanon Thongchai Range</a> in the east of the province serves as the boundary between the provinces of Mae Hong Son and Chiang Mai. The highest point of the province is Doi Mae Ya (ยอดเขาแม่ยะ), in the <a href="/wiki/Pai_District" title="Pai District">Pai District</a> in the province's northeast, at 2,005 metres (6,578 ft) elevation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mae_Hong_Son_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The territory of Mae Hong Son Province was formerly part of <a href="/wiki/Mawkmai_State" title="Mawkmai State">Mawkmai State</a>, one of the <a href="/wiki/Shan_States" title="Shan States">Shan States</a> which had been founded in 1767 by Hsai Khiao, hailing from a noble family of <a href="/wiki/Chiang_Mai" title="Chiang Mai">Chiang Mai</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a result of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Siamese_Boundary_Commission&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anglo-Siamese Boundary Commission (page does not exist)">Anglo-Siamese Boundary Commission</a> of 1892-93 Mae Hong Son District was ceded to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siam" title="Siam">Siam</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> but the adjacent Möngmaü and Mehsakun trans-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salween" title="Salween">Salween</a> Districts also claimed by Siam (as territories on the eastern side of the Salween River), were kept as part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Burma" title="British Burma">British Burma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGI_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGI-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>The population in the province consists of <a href="/wiki/Hill_tribe_(Thailand)" title="Hill tribe (Thailand)">hill tribes</a> (63 percent), including the <a href="/wiki/Hmong_people" title="Hmong people">Hmong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yao_people" title="Yao people">Yao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lahu_people" title="Lahu people">Lahu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lisu_people" title="Lisu people">Lisu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Akha_people" title="Akha people">Akha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karen_people" title="Karen people">Karen</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Shan_people" title="Shan people">Shan</a> (Thai Yai). The province has the lowest population density of all the provinces of Thailand.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Karen are not one single group, but rather a mix of closely related tribes. Padaung is a Karen language from the <a href="/wiki/Shan_State" title="Shan State">Shan State</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kayah_State" title="Kayah State">Kayah State</a>. Among the smallest of the Karen tribes in Thailand are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kayan_Lahwi" title="Kayan Lahwi">Kayan Lahwi</a>. This group's women are recognized by the large brass rings they wear around their necks.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbols">Symbols</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mae_Hong_Son_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Symbols">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seals_of_The_Provinces_of_Thailand" title="Seals of The Provinces of Thailand">provincial seal</a>, <i>Rup chang nai thong nam</i> (รูปช้างในท้องน้ำ), meaning "image of an elephant in a body of water", is a reference to the training of wild elephants for battle and various types of animal labour. "Rup chang nai thong nam" was selected as the provincial seal because it refers to Mae Hong Son's founding, which began with Lord Kaeo of Ma being sent to capture elephants for the Lord of Chiang Mai (1825–1846). Once in Mae Hong Son, he gathered the scattered Shan settlements to establish two main villages, Ban Pang Mu and Ban Mae Hong Son, that would be ruled by their elected leaders.</p>
<p>The provincial flag is horizontally divided in dark brown/blue/dark brown (1:3:1) with the image of the provincial seal in the middle of the flag.</p>
<p>The provincial tree is the Millettia brandisiana (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th" xml:lang="th">กระพี้จั่น</span>; <a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription"><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">rtgs</span></a>: <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Thai transliteration"><i>kraphichan</i></span>).</p>
<p>The provincial flower is the <a href="/wiki/Tithonia_diversifolia" title="Tithonia diversifolia">tree marigold</a> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th" xml:lang="th">บัวตอง</span>; <a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription"><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">rtgs</span></a>: <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Thai transliteration"><i>buatong</i></span>).</p>
<p>The official provincial slogan promoted by the Thai government is หมอกสามฤดู กองมูเสียดฟ้า ป่าเขียวขจี ผู้คนดี ประเพณีงาม ลือนามถิ่นบัวตอง (<a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription"><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">rtgs</span></a>: <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Thai transliteration"><i>mok sam ruedu, kong mu siat fa, pa khiao khachi, phu khon di, prapheni ngam, lue nam thin bua tong</i></span>) "Mists throughout the three seasons, the Kong Mu (monastery) that scrapes the sky, verdant forests, gentle people, beautiful customs; renowned land of sunflowers".</p>
