<p><b>Maxwell Sieben "Max" Baucus</b> (born December 11, 1941) is an American politician and diplomat who was a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senator</a> from <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a> from 1978 to 2014. Having served there for nearly 36 years, Baucus is the longest-serving Senator in Montana history. A member of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a>, Baucus was the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassador_to_China" title="United States Ambassador to China">United States Ambassador to China</a> from 2014 to 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sixth_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-sixth-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the Chairman of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Finance" title="United States Senate Committee on Finance">Senate Committee on Finance</a>, Baucus played an influential role in the debate over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_care_reform_in_the_United_States" title="Health care reform in the United States">health care reform in the United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cjr.org_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-cjr.org-3">[3]</a></sup> He was also chairman of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress_Joint_Committee_on_Taxation" title="United States Congress Joint Committee on Taxation">Joint Committee on Taxation</a>, a member of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Agriculture,_Nutrition_and_Forestry" title="United States Senate Committee on Agriculture, Nutrition and Forestry">Senate Committee on Agriculture, Nutrition and Forestry</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Environment_and_Public_Works" title="United States Senate Committee on Environment and Public Works">Senate Committee on Environment and Public Works</a>, and was chairman of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Environment_and_Public_Works_Subcommittee_on_Transportation_and_Infrastructure" title="United States Senate Environment and Public Works Subcommittee on Transportation and Infrastructure">Subcommittee on Transportation and Infrastructure</a>. Before his election to the Senate, Baucus was a member of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a> from 1975 to 1978, representing Montana's <a href="/wiki/Montana%27s_1st_congressional_district" title="Montana's 1st congressional district">1st congressional district</a>. He previously served in the <a href="/wiki/Montana_House_of_Representatives" title="Montana House of Representatives">Montana House of Representatives</a> from 1973 to 1974.</p>
<p>On August 9, 2011, <a href="/wiki/Party_leaders_of_the_United_States_Senate" title="Party leaders of the United States Senate">Senate Majority Leader</a> <a href="/wiki/Harry_Reid" title="Harry Reid">Harry Reid</a> appointed Baucus to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress_Joint_Select_Committee_on_Deficit_Reduction" title="United States Congress Joint Select Committee on Deficit Reduction">United States Congress Joint Select Committee on Deficit Reduction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> On April 23, 2013, Baucus announced he would retire from the U.S. Senate at the end of his term in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 18, 2013, <i><a href="/wiki/Politico" title="Politico">Politico</a></i> reported that the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> had selected Baucus to be the United States Ambassador to China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> On February 6, 2014, Baucus was confirmed by a vote of 96-0 with three Republicans absent and Baucus himself voting "Present".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He resigned from his Senate seat on the same day.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life.2C_education.2C_and_early_career">Early life, education, and early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life, education, and early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Max Baucus was born <b>Maxwell Sieben Enke</b> on December 11, 1941 in <a href="/wiki/Helena,_Montana" title="Helena, Montana">Helena, Montana</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/Jean_Baucus" title="Jean Baucus">Jean Baucus</a> (née Sheriff), who was from a wealthy ranching family, and Stephen Enke, Ph.D., a demographer and economist. Baucus lived in California until he was two, when his mother left his father and returned to Helena. She later married John J. Baucus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> His father, born in <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, Canada, was of German and Scottish descent, and his mother had English and German ancestry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Baucus graduated from Helena High School in 1959.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>After attending local public schools in his hometown of Helena, he attended <a href="/wiki/Carleton_College" title="Carleton College">Carleton College</a> in Minnesota for a year before transferring to <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>, where he received a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">Bachelor of Arts</a> degree in economics in 1964, and was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Sigma_Alpha_Epsilon" title="Sigma Alpha Epsilon">Sigma Alpha Epsilon</a> fraternity. After graduating, he attended <a href="/wiki/Stanford_Law_School" title="Stanford Law School">Stanford Law School</a> and graduated with a <a href="/wiki/Juris_Doctor" title="Juris Doctor">Juris Doctor</a> in 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baucus_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-baucus-11">[11]</a></sup> After finishing law school, Baucus spent three years as a lawyer at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> He moved back to his native Montana in 1971 to serve as the executive director of the state's Constitutional Convention, opening a law office in <a href="/wiki/Missoula,_Montana" title="Missoula, Montana">Missoula, Montana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In November 1972, Baucus was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Montana_House_of_Representatives" title="Montana House of Representatives">Montana House of Representatives</a> as a state representative from Missoula. In November 1974 he was elected to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a>, and was re-elected in 1976.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Baucus was elected to the U.S. Senate on November 7, 1978 for the term beginning January 3, 1979, but was subsequently appointed to the seat by Montana's Democratic Governor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Lee_Judge" title="Thomas Lee Judge">Thomas Lee Judge</a> on December 15, 1978 to fill the brief vacancy created by Senator <a href="/wiki/Paul_G._Hatfield" title="Paul G. Hatfield">Paul G. Hatfield</a>'s resignation. On April 23, 2013, a Democratic official confirmed that Baucus would not seek a seventh term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>Baucus was a <a href="/wiki/Moderate" title="Moderate">moderate</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> member of the Senate, frequently breaking with his party on the issues of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxes_in_the_United_States" title="Taxes in the United States">taxes</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_policy_of_the_United_States" title="Environmental policy of the United States">environment</a>, health care, and <a href="/wiki/Gun_politics_in_the_United_States" title="Gun politics in the United States">gun control</a>. The web site That's My Congress gives him a 23 percent rating on progressive issues it tracks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/NARAL_Pro-Choice_America" title="NARAL Pro-Choice America">NARAL Pro-Choice America</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Political_action_committee" title="Political action committee">political action committee</a> endorsed Baucus during his <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Montana,_2008" title="United States Senate election in Montana, 2008">2008 election</a> campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_rights">Civil rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Civil rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_Liberties_Union" title="American Civil Liberties Union">American Civil Liberties Union</a> rated Baucus at 60 percent in December 2002, indicating a mixed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_rights" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a> voting record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oti_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-oti-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Voting_rights_for_Washington.2C_D.C.">Voting rights for Washington, D.C.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Voting rights for Washington, D.C.">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Baucus voted against giving voting representation to the District of Columbia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="LGBT_rights">LGBT rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: LGBT rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1996, Baucus voted in favor of the <a href="/wiki/Defense_of_Marriage_Act" title="Defense of Marriage Act">Defense of Marriage Act</a>. In June 2012, he spoke out in support for <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Prior to that, he voted against a <a href="/wiki/Federal_Marriage_Amendment" title="Federal Marriage Amendment">proposed constitutional ban</a> in 2004 and 2006. He has supported measures to curb job discrimination and hate crimes based on <a href="/wiki/Sexual_orientation" title="Sexual orientation">sexual orientation</a>. He voted in favor of the <a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Ask,_Don%27t_Tell_Repeal_Act_of_2010" title="Don't Ask, Don't Tell Repeal Act of 2010">Don't Ask, Don't Tell Repeal Act of 2010</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tobacco_tax.2FRoll_your_own_cigarette_outlawing">Tobacco tax/Roll your own cigarette outlawing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tobacco tax/Roll your own cigarette outlawing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2012, after receiving donations from <a href="/wiki/Altria" title="Altria">Altria</a> (the parent company of <a href="/wiki/Philip_Morris_USA" title="Philip Morris USA">Philip Morris USA</a>), Baucus successfully pushed through Congress a transportation bill that outlawed "roll your own cigarette" stores by making it illegal for them to operate the cigarette rolling machines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gun_politics">Gun politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Gun politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1994, Baucus cast a pivotal vote in favor of Senator <a href="/wiki/Dianne_Feinstein" title="Dianne Feinstein">Dianne Feinstein</a>'s (D-CA) "Assault Weapons" Ban.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 2013, Baucus was one of four Democrats to vote against the <a href="/wiki/Joe_Manchin" title="Joe Manchin">Manchin</a>-<a href="/wiki/Pat_Toomey" title="Pat Toomey">Toomey</a> Amendment to expand <a href="/wiki/Background_check" title="Background check">background checks</a> for potential gun buyers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1999, Baucus was the only Democrat to vote against an amendment by Senator <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lautenberg" title="Frank Lautenberg">Frank Lautenberg</a> (D-<a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">NJ</a>) that sought to "regulate the sale of firearms at gun shows". Baucus can be frequently found hunting and fishing on public lands around Montana.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_issues">Economic issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Economic issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Baucus has a 74% pro-business voting record as rated by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Chamber_of_Commerce" title="United States Chamber of Commerce">United States Chamber of Commerce</a>. He twice voted to make filing <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy" title="Bankruptcy">bankruptcy</a> more difficult for debtors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Max_Baucus_on_the_issues_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Max_Baucus_on_the_issues-25">[25]</a></sup> once in July 2001 to restrict rules on <a href="/wiki/Personal_bankruptcy" title="Personal bankruptcy">personal bankruptcy</a>, and a second time in March 2005 to include means-testing and restrictions for bankruptcy filers. He has frequently visited places of employment within the state and has personally participated in activities that he calls "Work Days." He has also hosted economic development conferences.</p>
<p>In March 2005, Baucus voted against repealing tax subsidies benefiting companies that <a href="/wiki/Outsourcing" title="Outsourcing">outsource</a> U.S. jobs offshore. On January 4, 2007, he wrote an editorial in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" title="Wall Street Journal">Wall Street Journal</a></i> calling on Democrats to renew President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>'s fast-track authority for international trade deals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In response, the Montana State Senate passed a resolution, 44-6, "that the U.S. Congress be urged to create a replacement for the outdated fast track system."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 27, 2009, he introduced the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreign_Account_Tax_Compliance_Act_of_2009" title="Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act of 2009">Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act of 2009</a> to Congress together with Representative <a href="/wiki/Charles_Rangel" title="Charles Rangel">Charles Rangel</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_issues">Environmental issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Environmental issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Baucus's environmental record in Congress was mixed. He supported Democratic leadership in voting against oil and gas subsidies and <a href="/wiki/Arctic_National_Wildlife_Refuge" title="Arctic National Wildlife Refuge">Arctic National Wildlife Refuge</a> drilling, as well as by voting in favor of national standards to reduce oil consumption and spur the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen-powered_car" title="Hydrogen-powered car">hydrogen-powered cars</a>. But he voted against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corporate_average_fuel_economy" title="Corporate average fuel economy">corporate average fuel economy</a> standards (CAFE) and on increasing federal funds for <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a>. He was a leader in the massive <a href="/wiki/Montana_Legacy_Project" title="Montana Legacy Project">Montana Legacy Project</a> conservation project. The <a href="/wiki/League_of_Conservation_Voters" title="League of Conservation Voters">League of Conservation Voters</a> (LCV) gave Baucus a 79% rating for 2012; Baucus' lifetime LCV rating is 68%.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_affairs">Foreign affairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Foreign affairs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuba">Cuba</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cuba">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As a senator, Baucus opposed the <a href="/wiki/United_States_embargo_against_Cuba" title="United States embargo against Cuba">United States embargo against Cuba</a> and travel restrictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iraq_War">Iraq War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Iraq War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Baucus voted for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Authorization_for_Use_of_Military_Force_Against_Iraq_Resolution_of_2002" title="Authorization for Use of Military Force Against Iraq Resolution of 2002">Authorization for Use of Military Force Against Iraq Resolution of 2002</a>. He voted with a majority of Democrats against Senator <a href="/wiki/John_Kerry" title="John Kerry">John Kerry</a>'s amendment stipulating a firm deadline for <a href="/wiki/Withdrawal_of_U.S._troops_from_Iraq" title="Withdrawal of U.S. troops from Iraq">withdrawal of American combat personnel</a> from <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 29, 2006,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Baucus's nephew <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">Marine</a> Cpl. Phillip E. Baucus was killed in combat in Al <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anbar_Province" title="Anbar Province">Anbar Province</a>. Phillip, a 28-year-old resident of <a href="/wiki/Wolf_Creek,_Montana" title="Wolf Creek, Montana">Wolf Creek, Montana</a>, had been a member of the <a href="/wiki/3rd_Light_Armored_Reconnaissance_Battalion" title="3rd Light Armored Reconnaissance Battalion">3rd Light Armored Reconnaissance Battalion</a>, <a href="/wiki/1st_Marine_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Marine Division (United States)">1st Marine Division</a>, <a href="/wiki/I_Marine_Expeditionary_Force" title="I Marine Expeditionary Force">I Marine Expeditionary Force</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 10, 2007, the day of President Bush's address on his <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War_troop_surge_of_2007" title="Iraq War troop surge of 2007">plan to increase troop levels</a> in Iraq, Baucus spoke against the increases and called for a phased withdrawal of U.S. troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Israel">Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Baucus was one of the Senate's largest career recipients of <a href="/wiki/Israel_lobby_in_the_United_States" title="Israel lobby in the United States">pro-Israel</a> <a href="/wiki/Political_action_committee" title="Political action committee">Political Action Committee</a> (PAC) contributions, receiving $550,589 since 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vietnam">Vietnam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Vietnam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On July 14, 2009, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> published an editorial on Baucus's attitude over Vietnam’s "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tra_fish&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tra fish (page does not exist)">tra</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Basa_fish" title="Basa fish">basa</a>" <a href="/wiki/Pangasius" title="Pangasius">pangasius</a>, saying there was no reason for America to launch a <a href="/wiki/Trade_war" title="Trade war">trade war</a> with <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> over <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> "He's dead right about a trade issue now percolating in Washington," said the newspaper. The article, entitled "Max Baucus's Fish Sense - Protectionism often hurts the protectionist," outlined this controversial topic in Washington and underlined the possibility that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Agriculture" title="U.S. Department of Agriculture">U.S. Department of Agriculture</a> could effectively ban imports from Vietnam of pangasius fish, which are similar to U.S.-produced <a href="/wiki/Catfish" title="Catfish">catfish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ostensible reason for the move would be food safety and the USDA is considering whether Vietnamese fish should be subject to a stricter safety inspection regime. But the article noted that there have been no reported cases of Vietnamese fish making American consumers sick and the proposed inspections would be onerous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> It quoted Baucus as telling <i>Congress Daily</i>, "If we expect other countries to follow the rules and drop these restrictions, it is critical that we play by the rules and do not block imports for arbitrary or unscientific reasons."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Baucus went on a trade mission in Japan, inspecting the <a href="/wiki/Tesla_Motors" title="Tesla Motors">Tesla Motors</a> facility in Tokyo, saying "Tesla is a great example of how exports and trade help the U.S. economy and create good-paying jobs at home in America. Our trade ties overseas help Tesla sell a world-class product that's manufactured in the U.S. by top-notch American workers," Baucus said. "There are millions of consumers in Japan and across the Pacific ready to buy more American-made goods, and we can continue to open those markets and boost U.S. exports with an aggressive trade agenda."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Equities.com_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Equities.com-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_care_reform">Health care reform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Health care reform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/America%E2%80%99s_Healthy_Future_Act" title="America’s Healthy Future Act">America’s Healthy Future Act</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Senate_finance_committee">Senate finance committee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Senate finance committee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As chairman of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senate_Finance_Committee" title="Senate Finance Committee">Senate Finance Committee</a>, Baucus called the first Senate meeting of interested parties before the committee to discuss <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_care_reform_in_the_United_States" title="Health care reform in the United States">health care reform</a>, including representatives from pharmaceutical groups, insurance companies, and HMOs and hospital management companies. The meeting was controversial because it did not include representatives from groups calling for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single-payer_health_care" title="Single-payer health care">single-payer health care</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_to_single_payer_health_care">Opposition to single payer health care</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Opposition to single payer health care">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Advocate groups attended a Senate Finance Committee meeting in May 2009 to protest their exclusion as well as statements by Baucus that "single payer was not an option on the table." Baucus later had eight protesters removed by police who arrested them for disrupting the hearing. Many of the single-payer advocates claimed it was a "<a href="/wiki/Pay_to_play" title="Pay to play">pay to play</a>" event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schultz_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-schultz-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> A representative of the <a href="/wiki/Business_Roundtable" title="Business Roundtable">Business Roundtable</a>, which includes 35 memberships of <a href="/wiki/Health_maintenance_organization" title="Health maintenance organization">health maintenance organizations</a>, health insurance and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical" title="Pharmaceutical">pharmaceutical</a> companies, admitted that other countries, with lower health costs, and higher quality of care, such as those with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single-payer_health_care" title="Single-payer health care">single-payer</a> systems, have a competitive advantage over the United States with its <a href="/wiki/Privatization" title="Privatization">private system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rn_sh_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-rn_sh-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the next meeting on health care reform of the Senate Finance Committee, Baucus had five more doctors and nurses removed and arrested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Baucus admitted a few weeks later in June 2009 that it was a mistake to rule out a single payer plan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-48">[48]</a></sup> because doing so alienated a large, vocal constituency and left President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>’s proposal of a <a href="/wiki/Public_health_insurance_option" title="Public health insurance option">public health plan</a> to compete with private insurers as the most liberal position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Baucus has used the term "uniquely American solution" to describe the end point of current health reform and has said that he believes America is not ready yet for any form of single payer health care. This is the same term the insurance trade association, America’s Health Insurance Plans (AHIP), is using. AHIP has launched the Campaign for an American Solution, which argues for the use of private health insurance instead of a government backed program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cjr.org_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-cjr.org-3">[3]</a></sup> Critics have said that <a href="/wiki/Medicare_(United_States)" title="Medicare (United States)">Medicare</a> is already effectively a single-payer system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Conflict_of_interest_charges">Conflict of interest charges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Conflict of interest charges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Baucus has been criticized for his ties to the health insurance and pharmaceutical industries, and was one of the largest beneficiaries in the Senate of campaign contributions from these industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schultz_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-schultz-41">[41]</a></sup> From 2003-08, Baucus received $3,973,485 from the health sector, including $852,813 from pharmaceutical companies, $851,141 from health professionals, $784,185 from the insurance industry and $465,750 from HMOs/health services, according to the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Responsive_Politics" title="Center for Responsive Politics">Center for Responsive Politics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> A 2006 study by <a href="/wiki/Public_Citizen" title="Public Citizen">Public Citizen</a> found that between 1999 and 2005 Baucus, along with former Senate majority leader <a href="/wiki/Bill_Frist" title="Bill Frist">Bill Frist</a>, took in the most special-interest money of any senator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ari_Berman_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ari_Berman-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Only three senators have more former staffers working as lobbyists on <a href="/wiki/K_Street_(Washington,_D.C.)" title="K Street (Washington, D.C.)">K Street</a>, at least two dozen in Baucus' case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ari_Berman_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ari_Berman-52">[52]</a></sup> Several of Baucus's ex-staffers, including former chief of staff <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=David_Castagnetti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="David Castagnetti (page does not exist)">David Castagnetti</a>, are now working for the pharmaceutical and health insurance industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carol_Eisenberg_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carol_Eisenberg-53">[53]</a></sup> Castagnetti co-founded the lobbying firm of Mehlman Vogel Castagnetti, which represents <i>America’s Health Insurance Plans Inc</i>, the national trade group of health insurance companies, the Medicare Cost Contractors Alliance, as well as <a href="/wiki/Amgen" title="Amgen">Amgen</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AstraZeneca_PLC" title="AstraZeneca PLC">AstraZeneca PLC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Merck_%26_Co." title="Merck &amp; Co.">Merck &amp; Co.</a> Another former chief of staff, Jeff Forbes, opened his own lobbying shop and to represent the <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_Research_and_Manufacturers_of_America" title="Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America">Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America</a> and the Advanced Medical Technology Association, among other groups.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A statistical analysis of the impact of political contributions on individual senators' support for the public insurance option conducted by <a href="/wiki/Nate_Silver" title="Nate Silver">Nate Silver</a> has suggested that Baucus was an unlikely supporter of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_option" title="Public option">public option</a> in the first place. Based on Baucus's political ideology and the per capita health care spending in Montana, Silver's model projects that there would be only a 30.6% probability of Baucus supporting a public insurance option even if he had received no relevant campaign contributions. Silver calculates that the impact on Baucus of the significant campaign contributions that he has received from the health care industry further reduces the probability of his supporting a public insurance option from 30.6% to 0.6%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>In response to the questions raised by the large amount of funding he took from the health care industry, Baucus declared a moratorium as of July 1, 2009 on taking more special interest money from health care political action committees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washingtonpost.com_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-washingtonpost.com-55">[55]</a></sup> Baucus, however, refused to return as part of his moratorium any of the millions of dollars he has received from health care industry interests before July 1, 2009, or to rule out a resumption of taking the same or greater health care industry contributions in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washingtonpost.com_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-washingtonpost.com-55">[55]</a></sup> His policy on not taking health care industry money reportedly still allowed him to accept money from lobbyists or corporate executives, who, according to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>, continued to make donations after July 1, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washingtonpost.com_55-2"><a href="#cite_note-washingtonpost.com-55">[55]</a></sup> A watchdog group found that in July 2009 Baucus accepted additional money from the health care industry in violation of his own self-defined moratorium terms, reportedly leading Baucus to return those monies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tax_policy">Tax policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Tax policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Baucus voted for the <a href="/wiki/Economic_Growth_and_Tax_Relief_Reconciliation_Act_of_2001" title="Economic Growth and Tax Relief Reconciliation Act of 2001">Bush tax cuts</a> in 2001. He has usually voted against repealing portions of that bill and against repealing more recent tax cut bills that benefit upper income taxpayers. In 2008, he voted in favor of permanently repealing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estate_tax" title="Estate tax">estate tax</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Max_Baucus_on_the_issues_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Max_Baucus_on_the_issues-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ability_to_seek_legal_redress">Ability to seek legal redress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Ability to seek legal redress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He was one of 26 senators to vote against the <a href="/wiki/Class_Action_Fairness_Act_of_2005" title="Class Action Fairness Act of 2005">Class Action Fairness Act of 2005</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_campaigns">Political campaigns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Political campaigns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2002">2002</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: 2002">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Montana,_2002" title="United States Senate election in Montana, 2002">United States Senate election in Montana, 2002</a></div>
<p>The 2002 Montana elections got national attention when Baucus's opponent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montana_State_Senate" title="Montana State Senate">state senator</a> Mike Taylor, accused Baucus of having implied that Taylor was <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">gay</a> in a campaign ad. The ad was paid for by the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Senatorial_Campaign_Committee" title="Democratic Senatorial Campaign Committee">Democratic Senatorial Campaign Committee</a>, not by the Baucus campaign. The ad, which alleged that Taylor had <a href="/wiki/Embezzlement" title="Embezzlement">embezzled</a> funds from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmetology_school" title="Cosmetology school">cosmetology school</a> he once owned, showed footage from the early 1980s of Taylor massaging another man's face while wearing a tight suit with an open shirt. Taylor dropped out of the race and Baucus won with 63 percent of the vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008">2008</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: 2008">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Montana,_2008" title="United States Senate election in Montana, 2008">United States Senate election in Montana, 2008</a></div>
<p>Baucus sought re-election in 2008 in Montana, a state that has seen political change starting in 2004 when it elected Democratic Governor <a href="/wiki/Brian_Schweitzer" title="Brian Schweitzer">Brian Schweitzer</a> and then in 2006 Democratic Senator <a href="/wiki/Jon_Tester" title="Jon Tester">Jon Tester</a> by a slim margin. Montana was the only state in the U.S. to turn over a chamber of its legislature to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Republican_Party" title="United States Republican Party">Republican</a> control in 2006. The legislative chamber had a one-seat Democratic majority that then became a one-seat Republican majority.</p>
<p>Baucus raised a record amount of money for his 2008 re-election bid, 91 percent of which came from individuals living outside of Montana.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Similarly, according to the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Responsive_Politics" title="Center for Responsive Politics">Center for Responsive Politics</a>, Baucus's 2008 campaign raised $11.6 million, only 13 percent of which came from Montana donors; the rest included millions from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_care_industry" title="Health care industry">health care</a> and other industries overseen by Finance and Baucus's other committees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr.org_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-npr.org-59">[59]</a></sup> The overwhelming ratio of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interest_group" title="Interest group">special interest</a> and out-of-state dollars to donations from Montana donors have raised questions:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>So as Baucus and other lawmakers attempt to craft a bill that can smash through a virtual gridlock of interests, the awkward question lingers: To whom are they more attentive, their voting constituencies back home or the dollar constituencies who are at the Capitol every day?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr.org_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-npr.org-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>As a result of Baucus's significant fund-raising advantage, in the week that he announced his intention to run for re-election, he opened eight state offices—one more than he had official offices in the state. Baucus also announced that he had hired 35 full-time campaign staff members. Baucus won re-election in a <a href="/wiki/Landslide_victory" title="Landslide victory">landslide victory</a>, with 73% of the vote, carrying every county in the state.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_Ambassador_to_China">United States Ambassador to China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: United States Ambassador to China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On December 26, 2013 President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Obama</a> announced his intent to nominate Baucus to the post of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassador_to_China" title="United States Ambassador to China">Ambassador to China</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The President submitted Baucus's nomination to the Senate on January 7, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>A hearing on his nomination was held by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Foreign_Relations" title="United States Senate Committee on Foreign Relations">United States Senate Committee on Foreign Relations</a> on January 28, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> His nomination was later reported to the full <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> on February 4, 2014 and a vote for confirmation would later be considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> He was later confirmed by the full <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a> on February 6, 2014 by a vote of 96–0 and Baucus himself voting "Present".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Max Baucus was later sworn in by Vice President <a href="/wiki/Joe_Biden" title="Joe Biden">Joe Biden</a> on February 21, 2014;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> with his swearing ending the ambassadorship of <a href="/wiki/Gary_Locke" title="Gary Locke">Gary Locke</a>. Baucus will end his ambassadorship in January 2017, as President-elect <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> nominated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor_of_Iowa" title="Governor of Iowa">Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Terry_Branstad" title="Terry Branstad">Terry Branstad</a> from <a href="/wiki/Iowa" title="Iowa">Iowa</a> as the next Ambassador to China.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Baucus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Baucus came under fire from critics calling him a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inside_the_Beltway_(terminology)" title="Inside the Beltway (terminology)">beltway insider</a> who no longer really lives in Montana and only occasionally comes to visit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Until 1991, Baucus owned a house in Missoula, where he practiced law for three years before running for Congress in 1974. He didn't own a home again in Montana until February 2002, when he bought half of <a href="/wiki/Jean_Baucus" title="Jean Baucus">his mother's</a> house from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sieben_Ranch_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sieben Ranch Company (page does not exist)">Sieben Ranch Company</a>, the ranch started by Baucus's great-grandfather in 1897. The ranch company, and Baucus's mother, still own the other half of the house. Baucus lives in Washington, D.C.'s Capitol Hill district. As of November 2007, the <i><a href="/wiki/Missoulian" title="Missoulian">Missoulian</a></i> newspaper reported he owned no other property in Montana.</p>
<p>In April 2009, The Associated Press reported that Baucus and his second wife, the former Wanda Minge, are divorcing after 25 years of marriage and have "parted ways amicably and with mutual respect.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.yahoo.com_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-news.yahoo.com-67">[67]</a></sup> Starting in 2008, Senator Baucus has been romantically linked with his state office director, Melodee Hanes, whom he later nominated to the vacant position of <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_District_of_Montana" title="United States District Court for the District of Montana">U.S. Attorney in Montana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Hanes then withdrew her nomination before the conflict of interest was discovered, because according to Baucus they wanted to be together in Washington, D.C. Both the Senator and Ms. Hanes had ended their marriages within the previous year. Senator Baucus claims he was separated from his wife before he began seeing Ms. Hanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google.com_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-google.com-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google.com_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-google.com-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Konane.27s_Blog_linking_to_resume_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Konane.27s_Blog_linking_to_resume-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 2, 2011, Baucus married Ms. Hanes at the Sieben Ranch in Montana.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>Baucus has one son, Zeno, by his first wife, Ann Geracimos. Baucus and Geracimos divorced in 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>Baucus has completed a 50-mile <a href="/wiki/Ultramarathon" title="Ultramarathon">ultramarathon</a> and has crewed for winner and fellow Montana native <a href="/wiki/Nikki_Kimball" title="Nikki Kimball">Nikki Kimball</a> at the 100-mile <a href="/wiki/Western_States_Endurance_Run" title="Western States Endurance Run">Western States Endurance Run</a>, which he hopes to run in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
