<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">MacPherson strut</h1>

<p>The <b>MacPherson strut</b> is a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automotive</a> <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(vehicle)" title="Suspension (vehicle)">suspension</a> system that uses the top of a telescopic damper as the upper steering pivot. It is widely used in the front suspension of modern vehicles and is named for American automotive engineer <a href="/wiki/Earle_S._MacPherson" title="Earle S. MacPherson">Earle S. MacPherson</a>, who originally invented and developed the design.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MacPherson_strut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Earle_S._MacPherson" title="Earle S. MacPherson">Earle S. MacPherson</a> was appointed the chief engineer of Chevrolet's Light Car project in 1945, to develop new smaller cars for the immediate post-war market. This gave rise to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chevrolet_Cadet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chevrolet Cadet (page does not exist)">Chevrolet Cadet</a>. By 1946 three prototypes of the Cadet design had been produced. These incorporated the first MacPherson struts, giving independent suspension both front and rear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The Cadet project was cancelled in 1947 and the disgruntled MacPherson was enticed away to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ford" title="Ford">Ford</a>. Patents were filed in both 1947 for GM<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacPherson.2C_1947_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-MacPherson.2C_1947-2">[2]</a></sup> and 1949 for Ford,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacPherson.2C_1949_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MacPherson.2C_1949-3">[3]</a></sup> the 1949 patent<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> citing designs by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Guido_Fornaca&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Guido Fornaca (page does not exist)">Guido Fornaca</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIAT" title="FIAT">FIAT</a> in the mid-1920s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fornaca.2C_1929_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fornaca.2C_1929-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The strut suspension of the pre-war <a href="/wiki/Stout_Scarab" title="Stout Scarab">Stout Scarab</a> would also have been a likely influence and like Stout's own influences, the widespread use of long-travel struts in aircraft landing gear was well known by this time. French <a href="/wiki/Cottin_%26_Desgouttes" title="Cottin &amp; Desgouttes">Cottin-Desgouttes</a> utilized a similar design, albeit with less sophisticated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaf_springs" title="Leaf springs">leaf springs</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> however the Cottin-Desgouttes front suspension was in turn inspired by American engineer <a href="/wiki/J._Walter_Christie" title="J. Walter Christie">J. Walter Christie</a>'s 1904 design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>MacPherson originally created the design for use at all four wheels, but it is more commonly used for the front suspension only, where it provides a <a href="/wiki/Steering" title="Steering">steering</a> pivot as well as a suspension mounting for the wheel.</p>
<p>Following MacPherson's arrival at Ford, the first production car to feature MacPherson struts was the British-built 1950 <a href="/wiki/Ford_Consul" title="Ford Consul">Ford Consul</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Ford_Zephyr" title="Ford Zephyr">Zephyr</a>.</p>
<p>The first production car is widely thought to be the French 1949 <a href="/wiki/Ford_Vedette" title="Ford Vedette">Ford Vedette</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4Car_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-4Car-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> but this was already developed before MacPherson with an independent front suspension based on wishbones and an upper coil spring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ate.2C_MacPherson_strut_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ate.2C_MacPherson_strut-13">[13]</a></sup> Only in 1954, after the Vedette factory had been purchased by <a href="/wiki/Simca" title="Simca">Simca</a>, did the revised <a href="/wiki/Simca_Vedette" title="Simca Vedette">Simca Vedette</a> switch to using front struts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ate.2C_MacPherson_strut_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ate.2C_MacPherson_strut-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MacPherson_strut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A MacPherson strut uses a wishbone, or a substantial compression link stabilized by a secondary link, which provides a bottom mounting point for the hub carrier or <a href="/wiki/Axle" title="Axle">axle</a> of the wheel. This lower arm system provides both lateral and longitudinal location of the wheel. The upper part of the hub carrier is rigidly fixed to the bottom of the outer part of the strut proper; this slides up and down the inner part of it, which extends upwards directly to a mounting in the body shell of the vehicle. The line from the strut's top mount to the bottom ball joint on the control arm gives the <a href="/wiki/Kingpin_(automotive_part)" title="Kingpin (automotive part)">steering axis inclination</a>. The strut's axis may be angled inwards from the steering axis at the bottom, to clear the tyre; this makes the bottom follow an arc when steering.</p>
<p>To be really successful, the MacPherson strut required the introduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unibody" title="Unibody">unitary</a> construction, because it needs a substantial vertical space and a strong top mount, which unibodies can provide, while benefiting them by distributing stresses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Setright.2C_p.1235_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Setright.2C_p.1235-14">[14]</a></sup> The strut will usually carry both the coil <a href="/wiki/Spring_(device)" title="Spring (device)">spring</a> on which the body is suspended and the <a href="/wiki/Shock_absorber" title="Shock absorber">shock absorber</a>, which is usually in the form of a cartridge mounted within the strut (see <a href="/wiki/Coilover" title="Coilover">coilover</a>). The strut can also have the <a href="/wiki/Steering" title="Steering">steering</a> arm built into the lower outer portion. The whole assembly is very simple and can be preassembled into a unit; also by eliminating the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_wishbone" title="Double wishbone">upper control arm</a>, it allows for more width in the engine compartment, which is useful for smaller cars, particularly with <a href="/wiki/Transverse_engine" title="Transverse engine">transverse</a>-mounted engines such as most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Front_wheel_drive" title="Front wheel drive">front wheel drive</a> vehicles have. It can be further simplified, if needed, by substituting an <a href="/wiki/Anti-roll_bar" title="Anti-roll bar">anti-roll bar</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torsion_bar" title="Torsion bar">torsion bar</a>) for the <a href="/wiki/Radius_rod" title="Radius rod">radius arm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Setright.2C_p.1235_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Setright.2C_p.1235-14">[14]</a></sup> For those reasons, it has become almost ubiquitous with low cost manufacturers. Furthermore, it offers an easy method to set suspension geometry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many modern implementations replace the lower control arm by a wishbone. An <a href="/wiki/Anti-roll_bar" title="Anti-roll bar">anti-roll bar</a> is optional and if present is attached by a ball-jointed rod to the spring-damper or by a ball or elastomerically jointed rod to the wishbone.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages_and_disadvantages">Advantages and disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=MacPherson_strut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Advantages and disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although it is a popular choice, due to its simplicity and low manufacturing cost, the design has a few disadvantages in the quality of ride and the handling of the car. <a href="/wiki/Geometric_analysis" title="Geometric analysis">Geometric analysis</a> shows it cannot allow vertical movement of the wheel without some degree of either <a href="/wiki/Camber_angle" title="Camber angle">camber angle</a> change, sideways movement, or both. It is not generally considered to give as good handling as a <a href="/wiki/Double_wishbone_suspension" title="Double wishbone suspension">double wishbone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Multi-link_suspension" title="Multi-link suspension">multi-link</a> suspension, because it allows the engineers less freedom to choose camber change and roll center.</p>
<p>Despite these drawbacks, the MacPherson strut setup is still used on high performance cars such as the <a href="/wiki/Porsche_911" title="Porsche 911">Porsche 911</a>, several <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz" title="Mercedes-Benz">Mercedes-Benz</a> models and lower <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMWs</a> models (including the new Mini but excluding the 2007 X5,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Autocar_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Autocar-16">[16]</a></sup> 2009 7-series, 2011 5-series and 5-series GT).</p>
<p>Porsche 911 up until the 1989 model year (964) used a similar strut design that does not have coil springs, using <a href="/wiki/Torsion_bar_suspension" title="Torsion bar suspension">torsion bar suspension</a> instead.</p>
