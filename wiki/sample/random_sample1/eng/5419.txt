<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Muggle</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">This article is about a term in the <i>Harry Potter</i> book series. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Muggle_(disambiguation)" title="Muggle (disambiguation)">Muggle (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a></i> book series, a <b>Muggle</b> is a person who lacks any sort of magical ability and was not born in a magical family. Muggles can also be described as people who do not have any magical blood in them. It differs from the term <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_purity_(Harry_Potter)#Squibs" title="Blood purity (Harry Potter)">Squib</a></i>, which refers to a person with one or more magical parents yet without any magical power/ability, and from the term <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muggle-born" title="Muggle-born">Muggle-born</a> (or the derogatory and offensive term <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mudblood#Muggle-born" title="Mudblood">mudblood</a></i>), which refers to a person with magical abilities but with non-magical parents. The equivalent term used by the <a href="/wiki/Fictional_universe" title="Fictional universe">in-universe</a> magic community of America is <b>No-Maj</b>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_in_Harry_Potter">Usage in <i>Harry Potter</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muggle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage in Harry Potter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>Muggle</i> is sometimes used in a <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">pejorative</a> manner in the books. Since <i>Muggle</i> refers to a person who is a member of the non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magic_(Harry_Potter)" title="Magic (Harry Potter)">magical</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wizarding_world" title="Wizarding world">community</a>, Muggles are simply ordinary human beings rather than witches and wizards. According to the author, <a href="/wiki/J._K._Rowling" title="J. K. Rowling">J. K. Rowling</a>, a quarter of the annual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hogwarts_School_of_Witchcraft_and_Wizardry" title="Hogwarts School of Witchcraft and Wizardry">Hogwarts</a> intake have two non-magical parents;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> there have also been some children known to have been born to one magical and one non-magical parent. Children of this mixed parentage are called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_purity_(Harry_Potter)" title="Blood purity (Harry Potter)">half-bloods</a></i> (strictly speaking, they are 'Literal Half-bloods'); children with recent Muggle ancestry on the one side or the other are also called half-bloods. The most prominent Muggle-born in the Harry Potter series is <a href="/wiki/Hermione_Granger" title="Hermione Granger">Hermione Granger</a>, who had two Muggles of unspecified names as parents. A witch or wizard with all magical heritage is called a pure-blood.</p>
<p>In the <i>Harry Potter</i> books, non-magical people are often portrayed as foolish, sometimes befuddled characters, who are completely ignorant of the Wizarding world that exists in their midst. If, by unfortunate means, non-magical people do happen to observe the working of magic, the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Magic" title="Ministry of Magic">Ministry of Magic</a> sends Obliviators to cast Memory Charms upon them causing them to forget the event.</p>
<p>Some Muggles, however, know of the wizarding world. These include Muggle parents of magical children, such as <a href="/wiki/Hermione_Granger" title="Hermione Granger">Hermione Granger</a>'s parents, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supporting_Harry_Potter_characters#The_Muggle_Prime_Minister_of_Britain" title="Supporting Harry Potter characters">Muggle Prime Minister</a> (and his predecessors), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dursley_family" title="Dursley family">Dursley family</a> (Harry Potter's non-magical and only living relatives), and the non-magical spouses of some witches and wizards.</p>
<p>Rowling has stated she created the word "Muggle" from "mug", an English term for someone who is easily fooled. She added the "-gle" to make it sound less demeaning and more "cuddly".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 'muggle' is, according to Abbott Walter Bower, the author of the <i>Scotichronicon</i>, "an Englishman's tail". In Alistair Moffat's book, <i>A History of the Borders from Early Times</i> it is stated that there was a widely held 13th century belief amongst Scots that Englishmen had tails.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Ernest Bramah referred to "the artful Muggles" in a detective story published decades before the Potter books ("The Ghost at Massingham Mansions",in The Eyes of Max Carrados, Doran, New York, 1924).</p>
<p>Rowling herself was sued for using the word "muggle" in the Harry Potter books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_usages">Other usages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muggle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other usages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>muggle</i>, or <i>muggles</i>, is now used in various contexts in which its meaning is similar to the sense in which it appears in the Harry Potter book series. Generally speaking, it is used by members of a group to describe those outside the group, comparable to <i>civilian</i> as used by military personnel. Whereas in the books <i>muggle</i> is consistently capitalised, in other uses it is often predominately lowercase.</p>
<ul>
<li>According to the BBC quiz show <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/QI_(H_series)" title="QI (H series)">QI</a>, in the episode <i>Hocus Pocus</i>, <i>muggle</i> was a 1930s jazz slang word for someone who uses cannabis. <i>"Muggles"</i> is the title of a <a href="/wiki/Muggles_(recording)" title="Muggles (recording)">1928 recording</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_Armstrong_and_His_Orchestra" title="Louis Armstrong and His Orchestra">Louis Armstrong and His Orchestra</a>.</li>
<li><i>Muggle</i> was added to the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a> in 2003, where it is said to refer to a person who is lacking a skill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Muggle</i> is used in informal English by members of small, specialised groups, usually those that consider their activities to either be analogous to or directly involve magic (such as within <a href="/wiki/Hacker_culture" title="Hacker culture">hacker culture</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pagan" title="Pagan">pagans</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neopagan" title="Neopagan">Neopagans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wicca" title="Wicca">Wiccans</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> to refer to those outside the group.</li>
<li><i>Muggle</i> (or <i>geomuggle</i>) is used by geocachers to refer to those not involved in or aware of the sport of <a href="/wiki/Geocaching" title="Geocaching">geocaching</a>. A cache that has been tampered with by non-participants is said to be plundered or <i>muggled</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Muggle</i> is similarly used by <a href="/wiki/Hash_House_Harriers" title="Hash House Harriers">Hash House Harriers</a> to refer to members of the public who are not hashers.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muggle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> science fiction <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_drama" title="Television drama">drama</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Heroes_(TV_series)" title="Heroes (TV series)">Heroes</a></i> features a <a href="/wiki/Pomeranian_(dog)" title="Pomeranian (dog)">dog</a> named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_characters_in_Heroes" title="List of characters in Heroes"><i>Mr. Muggles</i></a>, who is owned by the Bennet family. The writers of the show have stated that the dog's name is an <a href="/wiki/Allusion" title="Allusion">allusion</a> to the <i>Harry Potter</i> series as, like <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_(character)" title="Harry Potter (character)">Harry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claire_Bennet" title="Claire Bennet">Claire Bennet</a> has been <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">adopted</a> by a family who does not have any special abilities.</li>
</ul>
