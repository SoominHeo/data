<p>The <b>Murray River</b> (or <b>River Murray</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[n 1]</a></sup>) is Australia's longest river, at 2,508 kilometres (1,558 mi) in length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup> The Murray rises in the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Alps" title="Australian Alps">Australian Alps</a>, draining the western side of Australia's highest mountains, and then meanders across Australia's inland plains, forming the border between the <a href="/wiki/States_and_territories_of_Australia" title="States and territories of Australia">states</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a> as it flows to the northwest into <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a>. It turns south at <a href="/wiki/Morgan,_South_Australia" title="Morgan, South Australia">Morgan</a> for its final 315 kilometres (196 mi), reaching the ocean at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Alexandrina_(South_Australia)" title="Lake Alexandrina (South Australia)">Lake Alexandrina</a>.</p>
<p>The water of the Murray flows through several terminal lakes that fluctuate in <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> (and were often fresh until recent decades) including Lake Alexandrina and <a href="/wiki/Coorong_National_Park" title="Coorong National Park">The Coorong</a> before emptying through the <a href="/wiki/Murray_Mouth" title="Murray Mouth">Murray Mouth</a> into the southeastern portion of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, often referenced on Australian maps as the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Goolwa,_South_Australia" title="Goolwa, South Australia">Goolwa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MDBC_mouth_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MDBC_mouth-3">[2]</a></sup> Despite discharging considerable volumes of water at times, particularly before the advent of largescale river regulation, the mouth has always been comparatively small and shallow.</p>
<p>As of 2010, the Murray River system receives 58 percent of its natural flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> It is perhaps Australia's most important irrigated region, and it is widely known as the food bowl of the nation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Murray River forms part of the 3,750 km (2,330 mi) long combined <a href="/wiki/Murray%E2%80%93Darling_basin" title="Murray–Darling basin">Murray–Darling river system</a> which drains most of inland Victoria, New South Wales, and southern <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>. Overall the catchment area is one seventh of Australia's total land mass. The Murray carries only a small fraction of the water of comparably-sized rivers in other parts of the world, and with a great annual variability of its flow. In its natural state it has even been known to dry up completely during extreme droughts, although that is extremely rare, with only two or three instances of this occurring since official record keeping began.</p>
<p>The Murray River makes up most of the border between the Australian states of Victoria and New South Wales. Where it does, the border is the top of the bank of the southern side of the river (i.e., none of the river itself is actually in Victoria).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> This was determined in a 1980 ruling by <a href="/wiki/Ninian_Stephen" title="Ninian Stephen">Justice Ninian Stephen</a> of the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Australia" title="High Court of Australia">High Court of Australia</a>, which settled the question as to which state had jurisdiction in the unlawful death of a man on an island in the middle of the river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> This boundary definition can be ambiguous, since the river changes its course over time, and some of the river banks have been modified.</p>
<p>West of the line of <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> 141°E, the river continues as the border between Victoria and South Australia for approximately 11 km (6.8 mi), where this is the only stretch where a state border runs down the middle of the river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> This was due to a miscalculation during the 1840s, when the border was originally surveyed. Past this point, the Murray River is entirely within the state of South Australia.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="River_life">River life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: River life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Murray River (and associated tributaries) support a variety of unique river life adapted to its vagaries. This includes a variety of native fish such as the famous <a href="/wiki/Murray_cod" title="Murray cod">Murray cod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trout_cod" title="Trout cod">trout cod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golden_perch" title="Golden perch">golden perch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macquarie_perch" title="Macquarie perch">Macquarie perch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bidyanus_bidyanus" title="Bidyanus bidyanus">silver perch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eel-tailed_catfish" title="Eel-tailed catfish">eel-tailed catfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australian_smelt" title="Australian smelt">Australian smelt</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_carp_gudgeon" title="Western carp gudgeon">western carp gudgeon</a>, and other aquatic species like the Murray short-necked turtle, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Murray_Crayfish" title="Murray Crayfish">Murray River crayfish</a>, broad-clawed yabbies, and the large clawed <i>Macrobrachium</i> shrimp, as well as aquatic species more widely distributed through southeastern Australia such as common longnecked turtles, <a href="/wiki/Common_yabby" title="Common yabby">common yabbies</a>, the small claw-less <i><a href="/wiki/Paratya" title="Paratya">paratya</a></i> shrimp, water rats, and <a href="/wiki/Platypus" title="Platypus">platypus</a>. The Murray River also supports fringing corridors and forests of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_red_gum" title="River red gum">river red gum</a>.</p>
<p>The health of the Murray River has declined significantly since European settlement, particularly due to river regulation, and much of its aquatic life including native fish are now declining, rare or endangered. Recent extreme droughts (2000–07) have put significant stress on river red gum forests, with mounting concern over their long-term survival. The Murray has also flooded on occasion, the most significant of which was the <a href="/wiki/1956_Murray_River_flood" title="1956 Murray River flood">flood of 1956</a>, which inundated many towns on the lower Murray and which lasted for up to six months.</p>
<p>Introduced fish species such as <a href="/wiki/Common_carp" title="Common carp">carp</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Gambusia" title="Gambusia">gambusia</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Misgurnus" title="Misgurnus">weather loach</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_perch" title="European perch">redfin perch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brown_trout" title="Brown trout">brown trout</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_trout" title="Rainbow trout">rainbow trout</a> have also had serious negative effects on native fish, while carp have contributed to environmental degradation of the Murray River and tributaries by destroying aquatic plants and permanently raising <a href="/wiki/Turbidity" title="Turbidity">turbidity</a>. In some segments of the Murray River, carp have become the only species found.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_history">Ancient history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ancient history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lake_Bungunnia">Lake Bungunnia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lake Bungunnia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Between 2.5 and 0.5 million years ago the Murray River terminated in a vast freshwater lake called Lake Bungunnia. Lake Bungunnia was formed by earth movements that blocked the Murray River near <a href="/wiki/Swan_Reach,_South_Australia" title="Swan Reach, South Australia">Swan Reach</a> during this period of time. At its maximum extent Lake Bungunnia covered 33,000 km<sup>2</sup> (12,741 sq mi), extending to near the <a href="/wiki/Menindee_Lakes" title="Menindee Lakes">Menindee Lakes</a> in the north and to near <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Bend,_Victoria" title="Boundary Bend, Victoria">Boundary Bend</a> on the Murray in the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> The draining of Lake Bungunnia occurred approximately 600,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Deep clays deposited by the lake are evident in cliffs around Chowilla in South Australia. Considerably higher rainfall would have been required to keep such a lake full; the draining of Lake Bungunnia appears to mark the end of a wet phase in the history of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Murray-Darling_Basin" title="Murray-Darling Basin">Murray-Darling Basin</a> and the onset of widespread arid conditions similar to today. A species of <i>Neoceratodus</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queensland_Lungfish" title="Queensland Lungfish">lungfish</a> existed in Lake Bungunnia (McKay &amp; Eastburn, 1990); today <i>Neoceratodus</i> lungfish are only found in several <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a> rivers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cadell_Fault_and_formation_of_the_Barmah_Red_Gum_Forests">Cadell Fault and formation of the Barmah Red Gum Forests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cadell Fault and formation of the Barmah Red Gum Forests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cadell_Fault" title="Cadell Fault">Cadell Fault</a></div>
<p>The noted Barmah Red Gum Forests owe their existence to the <a href="/wiki/Cadell_Fault" title="Cadell Fault">Cadell Fault</a>. About 25,000 years ago, displacement occurred along the Cadell fault, raising the eastern edge of the fault, which runs north-south, 8 to 12 m (26 to 39 ft) above the floodplain. This created a complex series of events. A section of the original Murray River channel immediately behind the fault was abandoned, and it exists today as an empty channel known as Green Gully. The Goulburn River was dammed by the southern end of the fault to create a natural lake.</p>
<p>The Murray River flowed to the north around the Cadell Fault, creating the channel of the <a href="/wiki/Edward_River" title="Edward River">Edward River</a> which exists today and through which much of the Murray River's waters still flow. Then the natural dam on the Goulburn River failed, the lake drained, and the Murray River avulsed to the south and started to flow through the smaller Goulburn River channel, creating "The Barmah Choke" and "The Narrows" (where the river channel is unusually narrow), before entering into the proper Murray River channel again.</p>
<p>This complex series of events, however, diverts attention from the primary result of the Cadell Fault — that the west-flowing water of the Murray River strikes the north-south fault and diverts both north and south around the fault in the two main channels (Edward and ancestral Goulburn) as well as a fan of small streams, and regularly floods a large amount of low-lying country in the area. These conditions are perfect for River Red Gums, which rapidly formed forests in the area. Thus the displacement of the Cadell Fault 25,000 BP led directly to the formation of the famous Barmah River Red Gum Forests.</p>
<p>The Barmah Choke and The Narrows mean the amount of water that can travel down this part of the Murray River is restricted. In times of flood and high irrigation flows the majority of the water, in addition to flooding the Red Gum forests, actually travels through the Edward River channel. The Murray River has not had enough flow power to naturally enlarge The Barmah Choke and The Narrows to increase the amount of water they can carry.</p>
<p>The Cadell Fault is quite noticeable as a continuous, low, earthen embankment as one drives into Barmah from the west, although to the untrained eye it may appear man-made.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Murray_mouth">Murray mouth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Murray mouth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Murray_Mouth" title="Murray Mouth">Murray Mouth</a></div>
<p>The Murray Mouth is the point at which the Murray River empties into the sea,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MDBC_mouth_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-MDBC_mouth-3">[2]</a></sup> and the interaction between its shallow, shifting and variable currents and the open sea can be complex and unpredictable. During the peak period of Murray River commerce (roughly 1855 to 1920), it presented a major impediment to the passage of goods and produce between Adelaide and the Murray settlements, and many vessels foundered or were wrecked there.</p>
<p>Since the early 2000s, <a href="/wiki/Dredging" title="Dredging">dredging</a> machines have operated at the Murray Mouth, moving sand from the channel to maintain a minimal flow from the sea and into the <a href="/wiki/Coorong_National_Park" title="Coorong National Park">Coorong's</a> lagoon system. Without the 24-hour dredging, the Mouth would silt up and close, cutting the supply of fresh sea-water into the <a href="/wiki/Coorong_National_Park" title="Coorong National Park">Coorong</a>, which would then warm up, stagnate and die.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mythology">Mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Being one of the major river systems in one of the driest continents of Earth, the Murray has significant cultural relevance to <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australians" title="Indigenous Australians">Indigenous Australians</a>. According to the peoples of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Alexandrina_(South_Australia)" title="Lake Alexandrina (South Australia)">Lake Alexandrina</a>, the Murray was created by the tracks of the Great Ancestor, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ngurunderi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ngurunderi (page does not exist)">Ngurunderi</a>, as he pursued <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pondi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pondi (page does not exist)">Pondi</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Murray_Cod" title="Murray Cod">Murray Cod</a>. The chase originated in the interior of New South Wales. Ngurunderi pursued the fish (who, like many totem animals in Aboriginal myths, is often portrayed as a man) on rafts (or <i>lala</i>) made from <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">red gums</a> and continually launched spears at his target. But Pondi was a wily prey and carved a weaving path, carving out the river's various tributaries. Ngurunderi was forced to beach his rafts, and often create new ones as he changed from reach to reach of the river.</p>
<p>At Kobathatang, Ngurunderi finally got lucky, and struck Pondi in the tail with a spear. However, the shock to the fish was so great it launched him forward in a straight line to a place called Peindjalang, near <a href="/wiki/Tailem_Bend,_South_Australia" title="Tailem Bend, South Australia">Tailem Bend</a>. Eager to rectify his failure to catch his prey, the hunter and his two wives (sometimes the escaped sibling wives of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Waku_and_Kanu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Waku and Kanu (page does not exist)">Waku and Kanu</a>) hurried on, and took positions high on the cliff on which Tailem Bend now stands. They sprung an ambush on Pondi only to fail again. Ngurunderi set off in pursuit again, but lost his prey as Pondi dived into Lake Alexandrina. Ngurunderi and his women settled on the shore, only to suffer bad luck with fishing, being plagued by a water fiend known as <a href="/wiki/Muldjewangk" title="Muldjewangk">Muldjewangk</a>. They later moved to a more suitable spot at the site of present-day <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ashville,_South_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ashville, South Australia (page does not exist)">Ashville</a>. The twin summits of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Misery,_South_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mount Misery, South Australia (page does not exist)">Mount Misery</a> are supposed to be the remnants of his rafts, they are known as <i>Lalangengall</i> or <i>the two watercraft</i>.</p>
<p>Remarkably, this story of a hunter pursuing a <a href="/wiki/Murray_cod" title="Murray cod">Murray cod</a> that carved out the Murray persists in numerous forms in various language groups that inhabit the enormous area spanned by the Murray system. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wotojobaluk&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wotojobaluk (page does not exist)">Wotojobaluk</a> people of Victoria tell of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Totyerguil&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Totyerguil (page does not exist)">Totyerguil</a> from the area now known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swan_Hill,_Victoria" title="Swan Hill, Victoria">Swan Hill</a> who ran out of spears while chasing <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Otchtout&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Otchtout (page does not exist)">Otchtout</a> the cod.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_exploration">European exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: European exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first Europeans to encounter the river were <a href="/wiki/Hume_and_Hovell_expedition" title="Hume and Hovell expedition">Hamilton Hume and William Hovell</a>, who crossed the river where <a href="/wiki/Albury" title="Albury">Albury</a> now stands in 1824: <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_Hume" title="Hamilton Hume">Hume</a> named it the <i>Hume River</i> after his father. In 1830 <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sturt" title="Charles Sturt">Captain Charles Sturt</a> reached the river after travelling down its tributary the <a href="/wiki/Murrumbidgee_River" title="Murrumbidgee River">Murrumbidgee River</a> and named it the <i>Murray River</i> in honour of the then British <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_War_and_the_Colonies" title="Secretary of State for War and the Colonies">Secretary of State for War and the Colonies</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Murray_(British_Army_officer)" title="George Murray (British Army officer)">Sir George Murray</a>, not realising it was the same river that Hume and Hovell had encountered further upstream.</p>
<p>Sturt continued down the remaining length of the Murray to finally reach <a href="/wiki/Lake_Alexandrina_(South_Australia)" title="Lake Alexandrina (South Australia)">Lake Alexandrina</a> and the river's mouth. The area of the <a href="/wiki/Murray_Mouth" title="Murray Mouth">Murray Mouth</a> was explored more thoroughly by Captain <a href="/wiki/Collet_Barker" title="Collet Barker">Collet Barker</a> in 1831.</p>
<p>In 1852 <a href="/wiki/Francis_Cadell_(explorer)" title="Francis Cadell (explorer)">Francis Cadell</a>, in preparation for the launch of his steamer service, explored the river in a canvas boat, travelling 1,300 miles (2,100 km) downstream from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swan_Hill,_Victoria" title="Swan Hill, Victoria">Swan Hill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1858 the Government <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoologist" title="Zoologist">Zoologist</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Blandowski" title="William Blandowski">William Blandowski</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Krefft" title="Gerard Krefft">Gerard Krefft</a>, explored the lower reaches of the Murray and Darling rivers, compiling a list of birds and <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PSMelbourneLock11.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2304" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/PSMelbourneLock11.jpg/220px-PSMelbourneLock11.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/PSMelbourneLock11.jpg/330px-PSMelbourneLock11.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/PSMelbourneLock11.jpg/440px-PSMelbourneLock11.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PSMelbourneLock11.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The P.S. Melbourne passing through Lock 11 at Mildura</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The lack of an <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a> means that shipping cannot enter the Murray from the sea. However, in the 19th century the river supported a substantial commercial trade using shallow-draft <a href="/wiki/Paddle_steamer" title="Paddle steamer">paddle steamers</a>, the first trips being made by two boats from South Australia on the spring flood of 1853. The <i>Lady Augusta</i>, captained by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Cadell_(explorer)" title="Francis Cadell (explorer)">Francis Cadell</a>, reached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swan_Hill,_Victoria" title="Swan Hill, Victoria">Swan Hill</a> while another, <i>Mary Ann</i>, captained by <a href="/wiki/William_Randell" title="William Randell">William Randell</a>, made it as far as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moama,_New_South_Wales" title="Moama, New South Wales">Moama</a> (near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Echuca,_Victoria" title="Echuca, Victoria">Echuca</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> In 1855 a steamer carrying gold-mining supplies reached Albury but Echuca was the usual turn-around point though small boats continued to link with up-river ports such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tocumwal,_New_South_Wales" title="Tocumwal, New South Wales">Tocumwal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wahgunyah,_Victoria" title="Wahgunyah, Victoria">Wahgunya</a> and Albury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The arrival of steamboat transport was welcomed by pastoralists who had been suffering from a shortage of transport due to the demands of the gold fields. By 1860 a dozen steamers were operating in the high water season along the Murray and its tributaries. Once the railway reached Echuca in 1864, the bulk of the <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">woolclip</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Riverina" title="Riverina">Riverina</a> was transported via river to Echuca and then south to Melbourne.</p>
<p>The Murray was plagued by "snags", fallen trees submerged in the water, and considerable efforts were made to clear the river of these threats to shipping by using <a href="/wiki/Barge" title="Barge">barges</a> equipped with steam-driven <a href="/wiki/Winch" title="Winch">winches</a>. In recent times, efforts have been made to restore many of these "snags" by placing dead gum trees back into the river. The primary purpose of this is to provide habitat for fish species whose breeding grounds and shelter were eradicated by the removal of "snags".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Night_travel_on_the_Murray_c1880.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="674" data-file-width="890" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Night_travel_on_the_Murray_c1880.jpg/220px-Night_travel_on_the_Murray_c1880.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Night_travel_on_the_Murray_c1880.jpg/330px-Night_travel_on_the_Murray_c1880.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Night_travel_on_the_Murray_c1880.jpg/440px-Night_travel_on_the_Murray_c1880.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Drawing of a <a href="/wiki/Paddle_steamer" title="Paddle steamer">paddle steamer</a> travelling the Murray at night, c.1880</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The volume and value of river trade made Echuca Victoria's second port and in the decade from 1874 it underwent considerable expansion. By this time up to thirty steamers and a similar number of barges were working the river in season. River transport began to decline once the railways touched the Murray at numerous points. The unreliable levels made it impossible for boats to compete with the rail and later <a href="/wiki/Road_transport" title="Road transport">road transport</a>. However, the river still carries pleasure boats along its entire length.</p>
<p>Today, most traffic on the river is recreational. Small private boats are used for <a href="/wiki/Water_skiing" title="Water skiing">water skiing</a> and fishing. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houseboats" title="Houseboats">Houseboats</a> are common, both commercial for hire and privately owned. There are a number of both historic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paddle_steamers" title="Paddle steamers">paddle steamers</a> and newer boats offering cruises ranging from half an hour to 5 days. In 2009, British Adventurer <a href="/wiki/David_Cornthwaite" title="David Cornthwaite">David Cornthwaite</a> walked and kayaked 2476 km along the Murray River from source to sea.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="River_crossings">River crossings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: River crossings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Murray_River_crossings" title="Murray River crossings">Murray River crossings</a></div>
<p>The Murray River has been a significant barrier to land-based travel and trade. Many of the Ports for transport of goods along the Murray have also developed as places to cross the river, either by bridge or ferry. The first bridge to cross the Murray, which was built in 1869, is in the town of Murray Bridge, formerly called Edwards Crossing.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_storage_and_irrigation">Water storage and irrigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Water storage and irrigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Murray_howlong.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="239" data-file-width="341" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Murray_howlong.jpg/220px-Murray_howlong.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Murray_howlong.jpg/330px-Murray_howlong.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/91/Murray_howlong.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Murray_howlong.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A branch of the Murray in its middle reaches, near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Howlong,_New_South_Wales" title="Howlong, New South Wales">Howlong, New South Wales</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Small-scale pumping plants began drawing water from the Murray in the 1850s and the first high-volume plant was constructed at Mildura in 1887. The introduction of pumping stations along the river promoted an expansion of farming and led ultimately to the development of <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> areas (including the <a href="/wiki/Murrumbidgee_Irrigation_Area" title="Murrumbidgee Irrigation Area">Murrumbidgee Irrigation Area</a>).</p>
<p>In 1915, the three Murray states – New South Wales, Victoria, and South Australia – signed the River Murray Agreement which proposed the construction of storage reservoirs in the river's headwaters as well as at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Victoria,_New_South_Wales" title="Lake Victoria, New South Wales">Lake Victoria</a> near the South Australian border. Along the intervening stretch of the river a series of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canal_lock" title="Canal lock">locks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weir" title="Weir">weirs</a> were built. These were originally proposed to support navigation even in times of low water, but riverborne transport was already declining due to improved <a href="/wiki/Highway" title="Highway">highway</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railway" title="Railway">railway</a> systems.</p>
<p>Four large reservoirs were built along the Murray. In addition to Lake Victoria (completed late 1920s), these are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Hume" title="Lake Hume">Lake Hume</a> near <a href="/wiki/Albury%E2%80%93Wodonga" title="Albury–Wodonga">Albury–Wodonga</a> (completed 1936), <a href="/wiki/Lake_Mulwala" title="Lake Mulwala">Lake Mulwala</a> at <a href="/wiki/Yarrawonga,_Victoria" title="Yarrawonga, Victoria">Yarrawonga</a> (completed 1939), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Dartmouth" title="Lake Dartmouth">Lake Dartmouth</a>, which is actually on the <a href="/wiki/Mitta_Mitta_River" title="Mitta Mitta River">Mitta Mitta River</a> upstream of Lake Hume (completed 1979). The Murray also receives water from the complex dam and pipeline system of the <a href="/wiki/Snowy_Mountains_Scheme" title="Snowy Mountains Scheme">Snowy Mountains Scheme</a>. An additional reservoir was proposed in the 1960s at <a href="/wiki/Chowilla_Dam" title="Chowilla Dam">Chowilla Dam</a> which was to have been built in South Australia and would have flooded land mostly in Victoria and New South Wales. This reservoir was cancelled in favour of building Dartmouth Dam due to costs and concerns relating to increased salinity.</p>
<p>From 1935 to 1940 a series of barrages was built near the <a href="/wiki/Murray_Mouth" title="Murray Mouth">Murray Mouth</a> to stop seawater egress into the lower part of the river during low flow periods. They are the Goolwa Barrage, located at 632 metres (2,073 ft), Mundoo Channel Barrage at 800 metres (2,600 ft), Boundary Creek Barrage at 243 metres (797 ft), Ewe Island Barrage at 853 metres (2,799 ft), and Tauwitchere Barrage at 3.6 kilometres (2.2 mi).</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Murray_berri.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="163" data-file-width="326" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Murray_berri.jpg/220px-Murray_berri.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e1/Murray_berri.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Murray_berri.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Dead and dying <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">River Red Gums</a> on the lower Murray near <a href="/wiki/Berri,_South_Australia" title="Berri, South Australia">Berri, South Australia</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>These dams inverted the patterns of the river's natural flow from the original winter-spring flood and summer-autumn dry to the present low level through winter and higher during summer. These changes ensured the availability of water for irrigation and made the Murray Valley Australia's most productive agricultural region, but have seriously disrupted the life cycles of many ecosystems both inside and outside the river, and the irrigation has led to <a href="/wiki/Salinity_in_Australia" title="Salinity in Australia">dryland salinity</a> that now threatens the agricultural industries.</p>
<p>The disruption of the river's natural flow, runoff from agriculture, and the introduction of pest species like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_carp" title="European carp">European carp</a> has led to serious environmental damage along the river's length and to concerns that the river will be unusably salty in the medium to long term – a serious problem given that the Murray supplies 40 percent of the water supply for <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a>. Efforts to alleviate the problems proceed but disagreement between various groups stalls progress.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (December 2011)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Locks">Locks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Locks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lock_1_and_weir_at_Blanchetown.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Lock_1_and_weir_at_Blanchetown.JPG/220px-Lock_1_and_weir_at_Blanchetown.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Lock_1_and_weir_at_Blanchetown.JPG/330px-Lock_1_and_weir_at_Blanchetown.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Lock_1_and_weir_at_Blanchetown.JPG/440px-Lock_1_and_weir_at_Blanchetown.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

Lock 11, Mildura.</div>

<p>Lock 1 was completed near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blanchetown,_South_Australia" title="Blanchetown, South Australia">Blanchetown</a> in 1922. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torrumbarry_weir" title="Torrumbarry weir">Torrumbarry weir</a> downstream of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Echuca,_Victoria" title="Echuca, Victoria">Echuca</a> began operating in December 1923. Of the numerous locks that were proposed, only thirteen were completed; Locks 1 to 11 on the stretch downstream of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mildura,_Victoria" title="Mildura, Victoria">Mildura</a>, Lock 15 at <a href="/wiki/Euston,_New_South_Wales" title="Euston, New South Wales">Euston</a> and Lock 26 at <a href="/wiki/Torrumbarry" title="Torrumbarry">Torrumbarry</a>. Construction of the remaining weirs purely for navigation purposes was abandoned in 1934. The last lock to be completed was Lock 15, in 1937.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Locks_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Locks-14">[13]</a></sup> Lock 11, just downstream of Mildura, creates a 100-kilometre (62 mi) long lock pool which aided irrigation pumping from Mildura and <a href="/wiki/Red_Cliffs,_Victoria" title="Red Cliffs, Victoria">Red Cliffs</a>.</p>
<p>Each lock has a navigable passage next to it through the weir, which is opened during periods of high river flow, when there is too much water for the lock. The weirs can be completely removed, and the locks completely covered by water during flood conditions. Lock 11 is unique in that the lock was built inside a bend of the river, with the weir in the bend itself. A channel was dug to the lock, creating an island between it and the weir. The weir is also of a different design, being dragged out of the river during high flow, rather than lifted out.</p>
