<p>The <b>Murray cod</b> (<i>Maccullochella peelii</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> predatory freshwater <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Maccullochella" title="Maccullochella">Maccullochella</a></i> genus and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Percichthyidae" title="Percichthyidae">Percichthyidae</a> family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOA_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOA-2">[2]</a></sup> Although the species is called a <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">vernacular</a>, it is not related to the Northern Hemisphere marine cod (<i><a href="/wiki/Gadus" title="Gadus">Gadus</a></i>) species. The Murray cod is an important part of Australia's <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a> wildlife and is found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Murray-Darling" title="Murray-Darling">Murray-Darling</a> River system in Australia. The Murray cod is the largest exclusively freshwater fish in Australia, and one of the largest in the world. Other common names for Murray cod include <b>cod</b>, <b>greenfish</b>, <b>goodoo</b>, <b>Mary River cod</b>, <b>Murray perch</b>, <b>ponde</b>, <b>pondi</b> and <b>Queensland freshwater cod</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOA_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOA-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The scientific name of Murray cod derives from an early Australian fish researcher with the surname McCulloch and the river from which the explorer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_Mitchell" title="Major Mitchell">Major Mitchell</a> first scientifically described the species, the <a href="/wiki/Peel_River_(New_South_Wales)" title="Peel River (New South Wales)">Peel River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOA_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOA-2">[2]</a></sup> This was for a number of years changed to <i>M. peelii peelii</i> to differentiate Murray cod from <a href="/wiki/Mary_River_cod" title="Mary River cod">Mary River cod</a>, which were designated as a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of Murray cod. However, as of 2010, Mary River cod have been raised to full species status (<i>M. mariensis</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nocketal2010_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nocketal2010-3">[3]</a></sup> thus Murray cod have reverted simply to <i>M. peelii</i>.</p>
<p>Murray cod populations have declined severely since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_colonisation_of_Australia" title="British colonisation of Australia">European colonisation</a> of Australia due to a number of causes, including severe overfishing, river regulation, and habitat degradation and are now a listed threatened species. However, they once inhabited almost the entire <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Murray-Darling_basin" title="Murray-Darling basin">Murray-Darling basin</a>, Australia's largest river system, in very great numbers.</p>
<p>A long-lived fish, adult Murray cod are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnivorous" title="Carnivorous">carnivorous</a> and mainly eat other fish. The species exhibits a high degree of parental care for their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_egg" title="Fish egg">eggs</a>, which are <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">spawned</a> in the spring and are generally laid in hollow logs or on other hard surfaces. Murray cod are a popular angling target and <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a> species. Often available through the aquarium trade, they are also a popular aquarium species in Australia.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_cod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maccullochella_peelii_peelii.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="449" data-file-width="613" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Maccullochella_peelii_peelii.jpg/220px-Maccullochella_peelii_peelii.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Maccullochella_peelii_peelii.jpg/330px-Maccullochella_peelii_peelii.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Maccullochella_peelii_peelii.jpg/440px-Maccullochella_peelii_peelii.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maccullochella_peelii_peelii.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Murray cod, displaying characteristic mottled green colouration: The fish was carefully released after the photo.</div>

<p>The Murray cod is a large <a href="/wiki/Grouper" title="Grouper">grouper</a>-like fish with a deep, elongated body<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CadwalladerBackhouse1983_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-CadwalladerBackhouse1983-4">[4]</a></sup> that is round in cross section. It has a broad, scooped head, and a large mouth lined with pads of very small, needle-like teeth. The jaws of the Murray cod are equal, or the lower jaw protrudes slightly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen-et-al2002_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-et-al2002-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDowall1996_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-McDowall1996-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nfacod_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nfacod-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The spiny <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal fin</a> of Murray cod is moderate to low in height and is partially separated by a notch from the high, rounded soft dorsal fin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDowall1996_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-McDowall1996-6">[6]</a></sup> Soft dorsal, anal, and caudal (tail) fins are all large and rounded, and are dusky grey or black with distinct white edges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CadwalladerBackhouse1983_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-CadwalladerBackhouse1983-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nfacod_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-nfacod-7">[7]</a></sup> The large, rounded pectoral fins are usually similar in colour to the flanks. The pelvic fins are large, angular, and set forward of the pectoral fins. The leading white-coloured rays on the pelvic fins split into two trailing white filaments,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDowall1996_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-McDowall1996-6">[6]</a></sup> while the pelvic fins themselves are usually a translucent white or cream, tending toward opacity in large fish.</p>
<p>Murray cod are white to cream on their ventral (belly) surfaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDowall1996_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-McDowall1996-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nfacod_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-nfacod-7">[7]</a></sup> Their backs and flanks are usually yellowish-green to green, overlain with heavy darker green, but occasionally brown or black, mottling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen-et-al2002_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-et-al2002-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDowall1996_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-McDowall1996-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nfacod_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-nfacod-7">[7]</a></sup> The effect is a marbled appearance sometimes reminiscent of a leopard's markings. Colouration is related to water clarity;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nfacod_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-nfacod-7">[7]</a></sup> colouration is intense in fish from clear water <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a>. Small to medium-sized Murray cod from clear-water habitats often have striking and very distinct colouration. Very large fish tend towards a speckled grey-green colouration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trueman2007_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-trueman2007-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Size">Size</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_cod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Size">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Murray cod are large fish, with adult fish regularly reaching 80â100Â cm (31â39Â in) in length in all but the very smallest waterways. Murray cod are capable of growing well over 1Â m (3.3Â ft) in length and the largest on record was over 1.8Â m (5.9Â ft) and about 113Â kg (249Â lb) in weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDowall1996_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-McDowall1996-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1989_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1989-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland2005_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland2005-10">[10]</a></sup> Large breeding fish are rare in most wild populations today due to overfishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kearney.26kildea2001_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-kearney.26kildea2001-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSW.Fish.unpubl.data_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSW.Fish.unpubl.data-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicol-et-al2005_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nicol-et-al2005-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stuart.26Koehn2007_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stuart.26Koehn2007-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>Murray cod continue a pattern present in Murray-Darling native fish genera of speciation into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lowland_(freshwater_ecology)" title="Lowland (freshwater ecology)">lowland</a> and specialist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upland_(freshwater_ecology)" title="Upland (freshwater ecology)">upland</a> species: Murray cod are the primarily lowland species and the endangered <a href="/wiki/Trout_cod" title="Trout cod">trout cod</a> are the specialist upland species. The pattern is slightly blurred in the cod species because, being adaptable and successful fish, Murray cod push significant distances into upland habitats, while the now endangered <a href="/wiki/Trout_cod" title="Trout cod">trout cod</a> stray (or did stray, before their decline) well down the upland/lowland transition zone, which can be extensive in Murray-Darling Rivers. Nevertheless, the basic pattern of <a href="/wiki/Speciation" title="Speciation">speciation</a> into a primarily lowland species and a specialist upland species is present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trueman2007_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-trueman2007-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Murray cod, like a number of other Murray-Darling native fish species, have also managed to cross the <a href="/wiki/Great_Dividing_Range" title="Great Dividing Range">Great Dividing Range</a> at least once through natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_capture" title="River capture">river capture</a> events, leading to several species and subspecies of coastal cod. The best known are <a href="/wiki/Eastern_freshwater_cod" title="Eastern freshwater cod">eastern freshwater cod</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Clarence_River_(New_South_Wales)" title="Clarence River (New South Wales)">Clarence River</a> system in northern <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, and Mary River cod of the <a href="/wiki/Mary_River_(Queensland)" title="Mary River (Queensland)">Mary River</a> system in south eastern <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, both of which are endangered, but survive today. Coastal cod were also found in the <a href="/wiki/Richmond_River" title="Richmond River">Richmond River</a> system in northern New South Wales and the <a href="/wiki/Brisbane_River" title="Brisbane River">Brisbane River</a> system in southern Queensland, but are now extinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nfacod_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-nfacod-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nswdf79r_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-nswdf79r-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_cod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1800s and early 1900s, commercial fishermen, recreational fishermen, riverside residents, and some fisheries scientists (e.g. Anderson, Stead, Langtry) distinctly recognised two species of cod in the southern Murray-Darling basin, Murray cod and trout cod or "blue nose cod". Taxonomically however, confusion abounded. Ignoring glaring differences in size at sexual maturity, and via some rather unscientific reasoning, some prominent fisheries scientists (e.g. Whitley) insisted on recognising only one species of codâthe Murray cod (then named <i>Maccullochella macquariensis</i>, after an early Australian fish researcher with the surname McCulloch<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mdbcnames_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-mdbcnames-16">[16]</a></sup> and the Macquarie River in <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> where the <a href="/wiki/Holotype" title="Holotype">holotype</a> was captured<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mdbcnames_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-mdbcnames-16">[16]</a></sup>). Then, as trout cod declined into near extinction over the 1900s, the distinction between the two species was further eroded and finally questioned. In the 1970s, early genetic techniques confirmed that trout cod were a separate <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> and further showed that the original "Murray cod" specimen was in fact a trout cod. Following the rules of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">scientific classification</a>, the name <i>M. macquariensis</i> remained with the original specimen, now known to be the trout cod, and a new name, <i>M. peelii</i>, for the Peel River<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mdbcnames_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-mdbcnames-16">[16]</a></sup> where the new holotype was captured, was coined for the Murray cod. Subsequently, two further cod were identified as separate species, the eastern freshwater cod (<i>M. ikei</i>) and the Mary River cod (<i>M. mariensis</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nocketal2010_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nocketal2010-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nfacod_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-nfacod-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Range">Range</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_cod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Range">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Murray cod is named after the <a href="/wiki/Murray_River" title="Murray River">Murray River</a>, part of the Murray-Darling basin in eastern Australia, Australia's largest and most important river system, draining around 14% of the continent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CadwalladerBackhouse1983_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-CadwalladerBackhouse1983-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mdbcstats_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-mdbcstats-17">[17]</a></sup> The Murray cod's natural range encompasses virtually the whole Murray-Darling basin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen-et-al2002_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-et-al2002-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDowall1996_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-McDowall1996-6">[6]</a></sup> particularly the lowland areas, and extending well into upland areas â to about 700Â m (2,297Â ft) elevation in the southern half of the basin and to about 1,000Â m (3,281Â ft) in the northern half of the basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nfacod_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-nfacod-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>Consequently, Murray cod inhabit a remarkably wide variety of habitats, from cool, clear, fast-flowing streams with riffle-and-pool structure and rocky <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(marine_biology)" title="Substrate (marine biology)">substrates</a> in upland areas to large, slow flowing, meandering rivers in the extensive alluvial lowland reaches of the Murray-Darling basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CadwalladerBackhouse1983_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-CadwalladerBackhouse1983-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDowall1996_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-McDowall1996-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1989_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1989-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland2005_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland2005-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>Murray cod have died out in many of their upland habitats, particularly in the southern Murray-Darling basin, due to a combination of <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siltation" title="Siltation">siltation</a>, dams and <a href="/wiki/Weir" title="Weir">weirs</a> blocking migration, pollution from <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>-based sheep-dips, <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, and in some cases, introduced <a href="/wiki/Trout" title="Trout">trout</a> stockings, which causes competition between juvenile Murray cod and introduced <a href="/wiki/Trout" title="Trout">trout</a> species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland2005_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland2005-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Age">Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_cod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Murray cod are very long-lived, which is characteristic of many freshwater native fish in Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nfacod_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-nfacod-7">[7]</a></sup> Longevity is a survival strategy in variable Australian environment to ensure that most adults participate in at least one exceptional <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">spawning</a> and recruitment event, which are often linked to unusually wet <a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a" title="La NiÃ±a">La NiÃ±a</a> years and may only occur every one or two decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nfacod_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-nfacod-7">[7]</a></sup> Murray cod are the most long-lived freshwater native fish in Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CadwalladerBackhouse1983_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-CadwalladerBackhouse1983-4">[4]</a></sup> The oldest Murray cod aged yet was 48 years of age,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AndersonEtAl1992_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-AndersonEtAl1992-18">[18]</a></sup> and the even larger specimens of years past leave little doubt that the species can reach considerably greater ages, of 70 years or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland2005_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland2005-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_cod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Murray cod is the apex aquatic predator in the rivers of the Murray-Darling basin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOA_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOA-2">[2]</a></sup> and will eat almost anything smaller than itself and anything in its way, including finned fishes such as smaller Murray cod, <a href="/wiki/Golden_perch" title="Golden perch">golden perch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bidyanus_bidyanus" title="Bidyanus bidyanus">silver perch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bony_bream" title="Bony bream">bony bream</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eel-tailed_catfish" title="Eel-tailed catfish">eel-tailed catfish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_carp_gudgeon" title="Western carp gudgeon">western carp gudgeon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australian_smelt" title="Australian smelt">Australian smelt</a> and introduced fish such as <a href="/wiki/Carp" title="Carp">carp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goldfish" title="Goldfish">goldfish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/European_perch" title="European perch">redfin (English perch)</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Common_yabby" title="Common yabby">yabbies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atyidae" title="Atyidae">freshwater shrimp</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Murray_crayfish" title="Murray crayfish">Murray crayfish</a>. Fish compose the majority of the diet of mature Murray cod in lowland river and impoundment habitats, and that Murray cod are <a href="/wiki/Apex_predator" title="Apex predator">apex predators</a> in these habitats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebner2006_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ebner2006-19">[19]</a></sup> Murray cod have also been known to eat <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">ducks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cormorant" title="Cormorant">cormorants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chelidae" title="Chelidae">freshwater turtles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physignathus_lesueurii" title="Physignathus lesueurii">water dragons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mice" title="Mice">mice</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>. The observations of the recreational fishermen fishing for Murray cod with surface lures at night reveal that the popular description of Murray cod as a <a href="/wiki/Demersal_zone" title="Demersal zone">demersal</a> <a href="/wiki/Ambush_predator" title="Ambush predator">ambush predator</a> is only partially correct. While this behaviour is typical during the day, at night, Murray cod are active <a href="/wiki/Pelagic_zone" title="Pelagic zone">pelagic</a> predators, venturing into shallow waters and frequently taking prey from the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schultz2006_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-schultz2006-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_cod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Murray cod reach sexual maturity between four and six years of age, generally five years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland2005_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland2005-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1998a_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1998a-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1998b_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1998b-22">[22]</a></sup> Sexual maturity in Murray cod is dependent on age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1998a_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1998a-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1998b_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1998b-22">[22]</a></sup> Therefore, roughly 70% of wild river Murray cod, with their slower growth rate, have reached sexual maturity by 50Â cm (20Â in) in length. Wild Murray cod in impoundments like <a href="/wiki/Lake_Mulwala" title="Lake Mulwala">Lake Mulwala</a>, with their faster growth rates, do not reach sexual maturity until they are well over 60Â cm (2.0Â ft) in length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland2005_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland2005-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1998a_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1998a-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1998b_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1998b-22">[22]</a></sup> These data strongly indicate the 60-cm (20-in) size limit for Murray cod is inadequate and should be increased substantially to allow for a greater chance of reproduction before capture.</p>
<p>Large female Murray cod in the 15â to 35-kg (35â to 80-lb) range are the most important breeders because they produce the most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_egg" title="Fish egg">eggs</a> and for other reasons;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland2005_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland2005-10">[10]</a></sup> large females in most fish species are also important because they produce larger larvae with larger yolk sacs, and are also more experienced breeders that display optimal breeding behaviours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stuart.26Koehn2007_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stuart.26Koehn2007-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trippel1995_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trippel1995-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MarteinsdottirSteinarsson1998_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-MarteinsdottirSteinarsson1998-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MarteinsdottirBegg2002_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-MarteinsdottirBegg2002-25">[25]</a></sup> Such large females may also have valuable, successful genes to pass on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stuart.26Koehn2007_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stuart.26Koehn2007-14">[14]</a></sup> All of these factors mean the spawnings of large female fish have far higher larval survival rates and make far greater reproductive contributions than the spawnings of small female fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stuart.26Koehn2007_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-Stuart.26Koehn2007-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trippel1995_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Trippel1995-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MarteinsdottirSteinarsson1998_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-MarteinsdottirSteinarsson1998-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MarteinsdottirBegg2002_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-MarteinsdottirBegg2002-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Female Murray cod, upon first reaching sexual maturity, have egg counts of no more than 10,000. Very large female Murray cod can have egg counts as high as 80,000â90,000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1989_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1989-9">[9]</a></sup> although a recent, very large 33-kg specimen yielded an egg count of 110,000 viable eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stuart.26Koehn2007_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-Stuart.26Koehn2007-14">[14]</a></sup> Egg counts in female Murray cod of all sizes are relatively low compared to many fish species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stuart.26Koehn2007_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-Stuart.26Koehn2007-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KoehnOConnor1990_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-KoehnOConnor1990-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Murray cod <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">spawn</a> in spring, cued by rising water temperatures and increasing <a href="/wiki/Photoperiodism" title="Photoperiodism">photoperiod</a> (daylight length). Initially, fish biologists working with Murray cod considered spring floods and temperatures of 20â21Â Â°C (68â70Â Â°F) to be necessary<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KoehnOConnor1990_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-KoehnOConnor1990-26">[26]</a></sup> and that spring flooding is critical for successful recruitment (i.e. survival to juvenile stages) of young cod by providing an influx of pelagic zooplankton and early life-stage <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">macroinvertebrates</a> off the flood plain into the main river channel for first feeding, but more recent research has shown Murray cod breed annually, with or without spring floods, and at temperatures as low as 15Â Â°C (59Â Â°F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDowall1996_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-McDowall1996-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland2005_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland2005-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1998a_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1998a-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1998b_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1998b-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Humphries2005_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Humphries2005-27">[27]</a></sup> Additionally, recent research has shown abundant epibenthic/epiphytic (bottom dwelling/edge clinging) prey in unflooded lowland rivers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Humphries2005_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Humphries2005-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-King2004_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-King2004-28">[28]</a></sup> traits in Murray cod larvae that should allow survival in a variety of challenging conditions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-King2005_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-King2005-29">[29]</a></sup> and a significant proportion of Murray cod larvae feeding successfully in unflooded rivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-King2005_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-King2005-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KaminskasHumphries2009_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-KaminskasHumphries2009-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Latest research has also shown that Murray cod in fact live their entire lifecycle within the main channel of the stream. Earlier ideas that Murray cod spawn on floodplains, or the larvae feed on floodplains, are incorrect. Murray cod breed in the main river channel or, in times of spring flood, the inundated upper portion of the main channel and tributary channels, but not on floodplains. Murray cod larvae feed within the main river channel or, in times of spring flood, on the inundated upper portion of the main channel and the channel/floodplain boundary, but not on the floodplain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koehn-in-prep_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Koehn-in-prep-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spawning is preceded by significant upstream migrations if high spring flows or floods allow. Radio-tracked Murray cod in the Murray River have migrated up to 120Â km (75Â mi) upstream to spawn, before returning to exactly the same snag from where they departed, an unusual homing behaviour in a freshwater fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koehn-in-prep_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Koehn-in-prep-31">[31]</a></sup> Decades of observations by recreational and commercial fishermen suggest such spring spawning migrations are common across the Murray cod's geographical range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nfacod_7-10"><a href="#cite_note-nfacod-7">[7]</a></sup> Spawning is initiated by pairing up and courtship rituals. During the courtship ritual a spawning site is selected and cleaned â hard surfaces such as rocks in upland rivers and impoundments, and logs and occasionally clay banks in lowland rivers, at a depth of 2â3 metres (6.6â9.8Â ft), are selected. The female lays the large adhesive eggs as a mat on the spawning surface, which the male fertilises. The female then leaves the spawning site. The male remains to guard the eggs during incubation, which takes six to 10 days (depending on water temperature), and to guard the hatched larvae for a further week or so until they disperse. Larvae disperse from the nest site by drifting in river currents at night, and continue this behaviour around four to seven days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland2005_10-8"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland2005-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1998a_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1998a-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1998b_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1998b-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Humphries2005_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Humphries2005-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lake1967_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lake1967-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cadwallader1977_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cadwallader1977-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1983_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1983-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KoehnHarrington2005_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-KoehnHarrington2005-35">[35]</a></sup> During this dispersal process, larvae simultaneously absorb the remainder of their yolk sac and begin to feed on pelagic <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a>, small, early life-stage <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">macroinvertebrates</a> and epibenthic/epiphytic (bottom dwelling/edge clinging) microinvertebrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-King2005_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-King2005-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KaminskasHumphries2009_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-KaminskasHumphries2009-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The relationship between river flows and Murray cod recruitment are more complex than first thought, and in less regulated rivers, Murray cod may be able to recruit under a range of conditions including stable low flows. (Significant recruitment of Murray cod in low-flow conditions in less regulated lowland rivers has now been proven.) This information also suggests that nonriver-regulation-related causes of degradation are playing a larger role in the survival and recruitment of Murray cod larvae than first thought; competition from extremely large numbers of invasive <a href="/wiki/Carp" title="Carp">carp</a> larvae are negatively affecting the survival and recruitment of Murray cod larvae to a much greater degree than first thought;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonkin-et-al2006_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tonkin-et-al2006-36">[36]</a></sup> and that decades of overfishing is playing a far larger role in the current state of Murray cod stocks, through depletion of spawning adults, than first thought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kearney.26kildea2001_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-kearney.26kildea2001-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>These findings do not mean that river regulation and water extraction have not had adverse effects on fish stocks. Rather, river regulation has been a major factor in the decline of Murray cod and other native fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen-et-al2002_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-et-al2002-5">[5]</a></sup> Thermal pollution is also a major problem, evidence indicates strong Murray cod recruitment events (which may be important for sustaining Murray cod populations over the long term) can result from spring flooding, and the health of Australian lowland <a href="/wiki/River_ecosystem" title="River ecosystem">river ecosystems</a> generally rely on periodic spring flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kearney.26kildea2001_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-kearney.26kildea2001-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-King_Env._Flow_2008_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-King_Env._Flow_2008-37">[37]</a></sup> Also, due to the regulation of most of the rivers in the Murray-Darling River system, mainly for <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> purposes, only exceptional spring floods manage to "break free". The long-term viability of wild Murray cod, other native fish species and river ecosystems, in the face of this fact, are of great concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kearney.26kildea2001_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-kearney.26kildea2001-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_cod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Murray cod were originally the most common large native fish in the Murray-Darling basin. Contrary to some fishery department literature, the first serious declines in Murray cod were caused by extremely severe overfishing. In the latter half of the 1800s and the early 1900s, Murray cod â very large, very long-lived fish â were caught in unimaginable numbers by both commercial and recreational fishermen. For example, one commercial fishing operation commenced on the Murray River near <a href="/wiki/Echuca" title="Echuca">Echuca</a> in 1855, targeting Murray cod over hundreds of kilometres of river, and yet within eight years, grave concerns over the sustainability of this operation, and complaints about the near-absence of Murray cod in their heavily fished grounds, were being raised in the main state newspaper, The Argus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Humphries_Winemiller_2009_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Humphries_Winemiller_2009-38">[38]</a></sup> Yet, fishing effort continued to increase in the region, so in the late 1880s and early 1890s, between 40,000 and 150,000Â kg of mostly Murray cod (between 7,500 and 27,000 fish, at an average weight of 5.5Â kg) were caught near Echuca.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Humphries_Winemiller_2009_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Humphries_Winemiller_2009-38">[38]</a></sup> Similarly, in 1883, more than 147,000Â kg of Murray cod were sent to Melbourne from just one river town (Moama).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1989_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1989-9">[9]</a></sup> By the 1920s Murray cod had been overfished to the point where large-scale commercial fishing operations were no longer feasible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1989_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1989-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland2005_10-9"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland2005-10">[10]</a></sup> Recreational fishermen took similarly excessive hauls during this era, using rods and reels, handlines, setlines, drum nets, gill nets, and even explosives, with hauls often either wasted or illegally sold. Perhaps this extreme overfishing and its impacts of wild Murray cod stocks is best summarised by a short article in the <i>Register News</i> (a South Australian newspaper) in 1929:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>In [the last] 29 years 26,214,502 lbs (nearly 11,703 tons) [11,915,683 kg] of Murray cod has been eaten by the people of Melbourne. The Superintendent of Markets (Mr G. B. Minns) included these figures in a statement he made today pointing out that the supply was declining. In 1918, the peak year, 2,229,024Â lbÂ [1,011,068Â kg] was received at the market, but since 1921, when 1,101,520Â lbÂ [499,640Â kg] was sent to Melbourne, supply has decreased. Last year [1928] it was only 551,040Â lbÂ [249,950Â kg].</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Register_News_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Register_News-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Incredibly, 20 years later, the aquatic ecologist J. O. Langtry was still compelled to criticise the extremely heavy, lawless, fishing pressure, in the form of both uncontrolled small-scale commercial fishing and rampant illegal fishing, with which he was confronted in all reaches of the Murray River he investigated in 1949â1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cadwallader1977_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cadwallader1977-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Indeed, a thorough reading of historical newspaper articles and historical government reports reveals that the whole history of wild Murray cod between the midâ1800s and the midâ1900s was one of citizen agitation, government inaction, and ongoing stock decline. For decade after decade, riverside residents, commercial fishermen, recreational fishermen, local fisheries inspectors, fish retailers, and others agitated in newspapers and other fora about the evidently, continuously declining Murray cod stocks, to be met in turn either with government denials, or conversely, with various ineffective inquiries into Murray cod stocks and fisheries, and various ineffective control measures. For decade after decade, debate about excessive fishing pressure, number of fishermen, number of nets, net mesh size, bag limits, minimum size limits and take of small cod, closed seasons and the taking of spawning cod full of eggs during spring, and other sundry issues, raged on and on without proper resolution, with fishing regulations either not amended, or amended and largely unenforced and completely ignored, and with heavy commercial, recreational and illegal fishing pressure continuing unrelenting and unabated. The end result was a Murray cod population, initially unimaginably abundant, continually fished down and down and down without pause, until in the early to mid 20th century a number of other factors such as river regulation (listed below) emerged to drive the species even further into decline. All of these drivers of decline have left this iconic Australian fish in a perilous situation. There are now grave concerns for the long-term survival of wild Murray cod populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kearney.26kildea2001_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-kearney.26kildea2001-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koehn2004_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Koehn2004-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-codwatch25_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-codwatch25-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<p>Although angler effects are sometimes disregarded in the overall picture today, recent population studies have shown that while all year classes are well represented up to the minimum legal angling size (now 60Â centimetres in most states), above that size, numbers of fish are dramatically reduced almost to the point of non-existence in many waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicol-et-al2005_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nicol-et-al2005-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koehn2004_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Koehn2004-40">[40]</a></sup> Some emphasis has been made of the results of two small surveys which suggested a majority of Murray cod are released by anglers. However, there are valid questions as to the representativeness of these surveys, these surveys do not explain the dramatic disappearance of large numbers of young Murray cod at exactly the minimum size limit, and most importantly, any emphasis on these surveys miss the fundamental point â as a large, long-lived species with relatively low fecundity and delayed sexual maturity wild Murray cod populations are extremely vulnerable to overfishing, even with only modest angler-kill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicol-et-al2005_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nicol-et-al2005-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koehn2004_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-Koehn2004-40">[40]</a></sup> A tightening of fishing regulations for wild Murray cod, as referred to above, and a switch by fishermen to a largely <a href="/wiki/Catch_and_release" title="Catch and release">catch and release</a> approach for wild Murray cod would alleviate this problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicol-et-al2005_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nicol-et-al2005-13">[13]</a></sup> Recognising these issues, in late 2014 the New South Wales and Victorian fishery departments amended their regulations so that a slot limit of 55 to 75Â cm now applies in these states. (I.e. Only Murray cod between 55 and 75Â cm may be taken; those above and below this size range or "slot" must be released.) This measure should have positive effects for the Murray cod population by protecting and increasing the proportion of large breeding Murray cod.</p>
<p>Another issue is that Murray cod caught and released in winter, while developing their eggs, or in spring prior to spawning, resorb their eggs and do not spawn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland2005_10-10"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland2005-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lake1967_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lake1967-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1988_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1988-42">[42]</a></sup> This may be a minor issue compared to some of the other threats facing Murray cod, nevertheless, concerned fishermen try to avoid catching wild Murray cod at these times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nfacod_7-11"><a href="#cite_note-nfacod-7">[7]</a></sup> At this point in time a closed season is in place for the spring spawning period, during which anglers are not allowed to target Murray cod, even on a catch and release basis.</p>
<p>State Government Fisheries departments support Murray cod populations by stocking with hatchery bred fish, especially in man made lakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koehn2004_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-Koehn2004-40">[40]</a></sup> Important issues affecting restoration of cod populations, such as the need for spring floods and excessive angler take, are slowly being acknowledged <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koehn2004_40-4"><a href="#cite_note-Koehn2004-40">[40]</a></sup> but are yet to be definitely addressed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKeller2004_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-McKeller2004-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_river_regulation">Effects of river regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_cod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Effects of river regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Murray River and southern tributaries originally displayed a pattern of high flows in winter, high flows and floods in spring, and low flows in summer and autumn. The breeding of Murray cod and other Murray-Darling native fish was adapted to these natural flow patterns. River regulation for irrigation has reversed these natural flow patterns, with negative effects on the breeding and recruitment of Murray cod. The Murray and most southern tributaries now experience high <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> flows in summer and autumn and low flows in winter and spring. Small and medium floods including the once annual spring flood-pulse have been completely eliminated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen-et-al2002_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-et-al2002-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDowall1996_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-McDowall1996-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koehn2004_40-5"><a href="#cite_note-Koehn2004-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sharing_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-sharing-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is estimated that flows at the river mouth by 1995 had declined to only 27% of natural outflows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mdbcaudit1995_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-mdbcaudit1995-45">[45]</a></sup> The probability of the bottom end of the Murray experiencing drought like flows had increased from 5% under natural conditions to 60% by 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mdbcaudit1995_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-mdbcaudit1995-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thermal_pollution" title="Thermal pollution">Thermal pollution</a> is the artificial reduction in water temperatures, especially in summer and autumn, caused when frigid water is released from the bottom of reservoirs for irrigation demands. Such temperature suppression typically extends several hundred kilometres downstream. Thermal pollution inhibits both the breeding of Murray cod and the survival of Murray cod larvae, and in extreme cases inhibits even the survival of adult Murray cod.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nfacod_7-12"><a href="#cite_note-nfacod-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rare floods that do break free of the dams and weirs of the Murray-Darling system have their magnitude and duration deliberately curtailed by river regulators. Increasing research indicates this management practice is very harmful and drastically reduces the general <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> benefits and breeding and recruitment opportunities for Murray cod and other Murray-Darling native fish species these now rare floods can provide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen-et-al2002_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-et-al2002-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDowall1996_6-11"><a href="#cite_note-McDowall1996-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koehn2004_40-6"><a href="#cite_note-Koehn2004-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sharing_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-sharing-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_barriers_to_fish_movement">Physical barriers to fish movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_cod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Physical barriers to fish movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dams, <a href="/wiki/Weir" title="Weir">weirs</a> and other instream barriers block the migration of adult and juvenile Murray cod and prevent recolonisation of habitats and maintenance of isolated populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nfacod_7-13"><a href="#cite_note-nfacod-7">[7]</a></sup> Additionally, recent study has proven approximately 50% of Murray cod larvae are killed when they pass through undershot weirs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baumgartner-et-al2006_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baumgartner-et-al2006-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_degradation_.2F_siltation">Habitat degradation / siltation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_cod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Habitat degradation / siltation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hundreds of thousands, perhaps more than a million, submerged timber "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snags" title="Snags">snags</a>", mainly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_Red_Gum" title="River Red Gum">River Red Gum</a>, have been removed from lowland reaches of the Murray-Darling basin over the past 150 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNally-et-all2002_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-MacNally-et-all2002-47">[47]</a></sup> The removal of such a vast number of snags has had devastating impacts on Murray cod and river ecosystems. Snags are critical habitats and spawning sites for Murray cod. Snags are also critical for the functioning of lowland river ecosystems â as one of the few hard <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(marine_biology)" title="Substrate (marine biology)">substrates</a> in lowland river channels composed of fine silts snags are crucial sites for <a href="/wiki/Biofilm" title="Biofilm">biofilm</a> growth, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroinvertebrate" title="Macroinvertebrate">macroinvertebrate</a> grazing and general in-stream productivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koehn2004_40-7"><a href="#cite_note-Koehn2004-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNally-et-all2002_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-MacNally-et-all2002-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vegetation clearing and cattle trampling river banks create severe <a href="/wiki/Siltation" title="Siltation">siltation</a>, which fill in pools, degrade river ecosystems and make rivers and streams uninhabitable for Murray cod.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDowall1996_6-12"><a href="#cite_note-McDowall1996-6">[6]</a></sup> This is exacerbated by removal of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riparian" title="Riparian">riparian</a> (riverbank) vegetation which causes siltation and degrades river ecosystems in many ways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nfacod_7-14"><a href="#cite_note-nfacod-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overfishing">Overfishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_cod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Overfishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While extremely severe commercial and recreational overfishing in the 1800s and the early 1900s caused the first strong declines of Murray cod, <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a> by recreational fishermen, aided by inadequate fishing regulations, continues today and remains an extremely serious threat to Murray cod. The current size limit of 60Â centimetres in most states is inadequate now that scientific studies have documented average size at sexual maturity in Murray cod.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicol-et-al2005_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nicol-et-al2005-13">[13]</a></sup> This and catch data and computer modelling exercises<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicol-et-al2005_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-Nicol-et-al2005-13">[13]</a></sup> on wild Murray cod stocks indicate measures such as raising the size limit to 70Â centimetres and reducing the bag and possession limits from 2 and 4 fish respectively to 1 fish are urgently needed to maintain the long-term viability of wild Murray cod populations. As of November 2014, the NSW Department of Fisheries has introduced a maximum size limit of 75Â cm for Murray Cod to provide protection for large breeding fish, as well as a new minimum size limit of 55Â cm.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduced_carp">Introduced carp</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_cod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Introduced carp">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Evidence is mounting that there is serious competition for food between larval/early juvenile introduced carp and larval/early juvenile native fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonkin-et-al2006_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tonkin-et-al2006-36">[36]</a></sup> Introduced carp dominate the fish <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">faunas</a> of lowland Murray-Darling rivers; the sheer amount of <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> carp now take up, and the large numbers of larvae carp produce, causes serious negative effects on river ecosystems and native fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tonkin-et-al2006_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tonkin-et-al2006-36">[36]</a></sup> Carp are the main vector of the introduced <i>Lernaea</i> parasite (<i>Lernaea cyprincea</i>) and serious vectors of the introduced Asian fish tapeworm (<i><a href="/wiki/Bothriocephalus_acheilognathi" title="Bothriocephalus acheilognathi">Bothriocephalus acheilognathi</a></i>).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduced_pathogens">Introduced pathogens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_cod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Introduced pathogens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Murray cod have soft skin and very fine scales that leave them vulnerable to infection from exotic disease organisms. The following exotic disease organisms all seriously affect wild Murray cod; all have been introduced by imports of exotic fish. <i>Chilodenella</i> is a single-celled, parasitic <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoa</a> that infects the skin of Murray cod and has caused a number of serious kills of wild Murray cod.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland_and_Ingram.2C_1991_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland_and_Ingram.2C_1991-48">[48]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Saprolegnia" title="Saprolegnia">Saprolegnia</a></i> is a fungus-like oomycete or "water mould" that frequently infects Murray cod eggs and the skin of Murray cod that have been roughly handled through poor <a href="/wiki/Catch_and_release" title="Catch and release">catch and release technique</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland_and_Ingram.2C_1991_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland_and_Ingram.2C_1991-48">[48]</a></sup> (It is essential that Murray cod intended for release only touch cool wet surfaces and are <b>not</b> put down on any hard, dry, rough or hot surfaces, e.g. boat gunwales, boat floors, dry grass, dry rocks, gravel banks, dry towels or mats, etc. Hands should also be wetted before touching them. They must not be hung vertically by the mouth or gill covers.) Wild Murray cod populations across their range suffer extremely severe infestations of <i>Lernaea</i> or "anchor worm", a parasitic <a href="/wiki/Copepod" title="Copepod">copepod</a> vectored by introduced <a href="/wiki/Carp" title="Carp">carp</a> and that burrows into the skin of Murray cod.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland_and_Ingram.2C_1991_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland_and_Ingram.2C_1991-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSW_Rivers_Survey_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSW_Rivers_Survey-49">[49]</a></sup> <i>Lernaea</i> puncture wounds are often secondarily infected by bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khalifa_and_Post.2C_1976_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Khalifa_and_Post.2C_1976-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berry_et_al..2C_1991_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berry_et_al..2C_1991-51">[51]</a></sup> Severe <i>Lernaea</i> infestations probably causes the death of many more adult Murray cod than commonly recognised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodwin.2C_1999_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodwin.2C_1999-52">[52]</a></sup> Ebner<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebner2006_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ebner2006-19">[19]</a></sup> reports a young adult Murray cod seemingly killed by severe <i>Lernaea</i> infestation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_cod&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Murray cod play a very important role in the mythology of many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Aborigines" title="Australian Aborigines">Aboriginal</a> tribes in the Murray-Darling basin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen-et-al2002_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-et-al2002-5">[5]</a></sup> and for some tribes, particular those living along the Murray River, Murray cod were the icon species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">myths</a> of these tribes describe the creation of the Murray River by a gigantic Murray cod fleeing down a small creek to escape from a renowned hunter. In these myths, the fleeing Murray cod enlarges the river and the beating of its tail create the bends in it. The cod is eventually speared near the terminus of the Murray River, chopped into pieces, and the pieces thrown back into the river. The pieces become all the other fish species of the river. The cod's head is kept intact, told to "keep being Murray cod", and also thrown back into the river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland1989_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland1989-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowland2005_10-11"><a href="#cite_note-Rowland2005-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
