<p><b>Commerson's dolphin</b> (<i>Cephalorhynchus commersonii</i>), also referred to by the common names <b>skunk dolphin</b>, <b>piebald dolphin</b> or <b>panda dolphin</b>, is a small <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_dolphin" title="Oceanic dolphin">oceanic dolphin</a> of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Cephalorhynchus" title="Cephalorhynchus">Cephalorhynchus</a></i>. Commerson's dolphin has two geographically-isolated but locally-common subspecies. The principal subspecies, <i>C.c.commersonii</i>, has sharply-delineated black-and-white patterning and is found around the tip of South America. The secondary subspecies, <i>C.c.kerguelenensis</i>, is larger than <i>C.c.commersonii</i>, has a less-sharply delineated dark and light grey patterning with a white ventral band, and is found around the <a href="/wiki/Kerguelen_Islands" title="Kerguelen Islands">Kerguelen Islands</a> in the Indian Ocean.</p>
<p>The dolphin is named for French naturalist Dr <a href="/wiki/Philibert_Commerson" title="Philibert Commerson">Philibert Commerson</a>, who first described them in 1767 after sighting them in the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Magellan" title="Strait of Magellan">Strait of Magellan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_and_distribution">Population and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commerson%27s_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Population and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two disjunct subspecies of the dolphin are found in geographically disparate areas separated by 130° of longitude and about 8,500 km (5,300 mi); it is not known why they are thus distributed. Global populations are unknown, but the species is accepted to be locally common.</p>
<p>The main subspecies, <i>C.c.commersonii</i>, is found inshore in various inlets in Argentina including <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Deseado" title="Puerto Deseado">Puerto Deseado</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Magellan" title="Strait of Magellan">Strait of Magellan</a> and around <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a>, and near the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a>. A survey in 1984 estimated there to be 3,400 individuals in the Strait of Magellan.</p>
<p>Dolphins of the second subspecies, <i>C.c.kerguelenensis</i>, were discovered in the 1950s. They reside near the <a href="/wiki/Kerguelen_Islands" title="Kerguelen Islands">Kerguelen Islands</a> in the southern part of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, and prefer shallow waters.</p>
<p>In 2004, a <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a> individual of unconfirmed origin was sighted on <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa's</a> <a href="/wiki/Agulhas_Bank" title="Agulhas Bank">Agulhas Bank</a>, a location 4,200 km (2,600 mi) from the Kerguelen Islands and 6,300 km (3,900 mi) from South America. Though the Kerguelen Islands are closer, such a journey would require swimming against the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circumpolar_Current" title="Antarctic Circumpolar Current">Antarctic Circumpolar Current</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commerson%27s_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="C.c.commersonii">C.c.commersonii</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commerson%27s_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: C.c.commersonii">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Commerson_Dolphin.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1064" data-file-width="1600" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Commerson_Dolphin.jpg/220px-Commerson_Dolphin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Commerson_Dolphin.jpg/330px-Commerson_Dolphin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Commerson_Dolphin.jpg/440px-Commerson_Dolphin.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

A Commerson's dolphin in the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Magellan" title="Strait of Magellan">Strait of Magellan</a></div>

<p>The <i>commersonii</i> subspecies has a black head, <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal fin</a>, and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fluke" title="wikt:fluke">fluke</a>, with a white throat and body. The demarcation between the two colours is very clear-cut. This stocky creature is one of the smallest of all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cetaceans" title="Cetaceans">cetaceans</a>, growing to around 1.5 m (5 ft). A mature female caught off of southern <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a>, at 23 kg (51 lb) and 1.36 m (4.5 ft), may be the smallest adult cetacean on record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Its appearance resembles that of a <a href="/wiki/Porpoise" title="Porpoise">porpoise</a>, but its conspicuous <a href="/wiki/Commerson%27s_dolphin#Behaviour" title="Commerson's dolphin">behaviour</a> is typical of a dolphin. The dorsal fin has a long, straight leading edge which ends in a curved tip. The trailing is typically concave but not falcate. The fluke has a notch in the middle. This dolphin has no rostrum (beak).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Sexes are easily distinguished by the different shape of the black blotch on the belly — it is shaped like a teardrop in males but is more rounded in females. Females reach breeding age at six to 9 years. Males reach sexual maturity at about the same age. Mating occurs in the spring and summer and calving occurs after a gestation period of 11 months. The Commerson's dolphin has been known to live up to eighteen years in the wild, while in captivity the oldest individual was at least 33 years old at the time of death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="C.c.kerguelenensis"><i>C.c.kerguelenensis</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commerson%27s_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: C.c.kerguelenensis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dolphins of the <i>kerguelenensis</i> subspecies tend to be larger than those of <i>C.c.commersonii</i>, and differ in patterning in that they are dark grey instead of black, and light grey instead of white, except ventrally. The demarcation between areas of the pattern is also less clearly demarcated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commerson%27s_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Commerson's dolphin is very active. It is often seen swimming rapidly on the surface and leaping from the water. It also spins and twists as it swims and may surf on breaking waves when very close to the shore. It will bow-ride and swim behind fast-moving boats. It is also known to swim upside-down, which is thought to improve the visibility of its prey.</p>
<p>This dolphin feeds on a mix of coastal and <a href="/wiki/Pelagic_fish" title="Pelagic fish">pelagic fish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a>. Those in the South American subpopulation supplement their diets with <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commerson%27s_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> lists Commerson's dolphin as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Data_Deficient" title="Data Deficient">Data Deficient</a> in its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_List_of_Threatened_Species" title="Red List of Threatened Species">Red List of Threatened Species</a>. The proximity of the dolphin to the shore makes accidental killing in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gillnet" title="Gillnet">gillnets</a> a common occurrence. The dolphin was killed for use as crab <a href="/wiki/Bait_(luring_substance)" title="Bait (luring substance)">bait</a> by some Argentinian and Chilean fishermen in the 1970s and 1980s, but this practice has since been curtailed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Commerson's dolphin population of South America is listed on Appendix II<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Appendices_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Appendices-8">[8]</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Conservation_of_Migratory_Species_of_Wild_Animals" title="Convention on the Conservation of Migratory Species of Wild Animals">Convention on the Conservation of Migratory Species of Wild Animals</a> (CMS). It is listed on Appendix II<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Appendices_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Appendices-8">[8]</a></sup> as it has an unfavourable conservation status or would benefit significantly from international co-operation organised by tailored agreements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Captivity">Captivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commerson%27s_dolphin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Captivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>About two dozen Commerson's dolphins live in aquariums around the world.</p>
