<p><b>Beta Comae Berenices</b> (β Comae Berenices, β Com) is a <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">main sequence</a> dwarf <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> in the northern <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Coma_Berenices" title="Coma Berenices">Coma Berenices</a>. It is located at a distance of about 29.78 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> (9.13 <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsecs</a>) from Earth. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_letter" title="Greek letter">Greek letter</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_(letter)" title="Beta (letter)">beta</a> (β) usually indicates that the star has the second highest <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">visual magnitude</a> in the constellation. However, with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_visual_magnitude" title="Apparent visual magnitude">apparent visual magnitude</a> of 4.3,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clpl4_99_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-clpl4_99-2">[2]</a></sup> this star is actually slightly brighter than <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Comae_Berenices" title="Alpha Comae Berenices">α Comae Berenices</a>. It can be seen with the naked eye, but may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bortle_Dark-Sky_Scale" title="Bortle Dark-Sky Scale">too dim</a> to be viewed from a built-up urban area.</p>
<p>The star is similar to the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, being only slightly larger and brighter in <a href="/wiki/Absolute_magnitude" title="Absolute magnitude">absolute magnitude</a>. It has a <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">stellar classification</a> of G0 V,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aj121_4_2148_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-aj121_4_2148-3">[3]</a></sup> compared to G2 V for the Sun. The <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">effective temperature</a> of the outer envelope is 5,936 K,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj746_1_101_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-apj746_1_101-7">[7]</a></sup> giving it a yellow hue of a G-type star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csiro_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-csiro-11">[11]</a></sup> In terms of age it is younger than the Sun, being about 3 billion years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj687_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-apj687-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Observations of short term variations in the chromatic activity suggest that the star undergoes differential rotation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apjp2l_393_2_L63_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-apjp2l_393_2_L63-12">[12]</a></sup> with a rotation period of about 11–13 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj475_303_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-apj475_303-8">[8]</a></sup> Its surface has a measured activity cycle of 16.6 years, compared to 11 years on the Sun. It may also have a secondary activity cycle of 9.6 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj438_1_269_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-apj438_1_269-13">[13]</a></sup> At one time it was thought that this star might have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscopic_binary" title="Spectroscopic binary">spectroscopic companion</a>. However, this was ruled out by means of more accurate radial velocity measurements. No <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a> have yet been detected around it, and there is no evidence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circumstellar_disk" title="Circumstellar disk">dusty disk</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitable_zone" title="Habitable zone">habitable zone</a> for this star, defined as the locations where liquid water could be present on an Earth-like planet, is 0.918–1.042 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_Units" title="Astronomical Units">AU</a>, where 1 AU is the average distance from the Earth to the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa555_A104_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-aaa555_A104-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
