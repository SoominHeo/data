<p><b>Muskogee</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a> in and the <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">county seat</a> of <a href="/wiki/Muskogee_County,_Oklahoma" title="Muskogee County, Oklahoma">Muskogee County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR6_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR6-2">[2]</a></sup> Home to <a href="/wiki/Bacone_College" title="Bacone College">Bacone College</a>, it lies approximately 48 miles southeast of <a href="/wiki/Tulsa,_Oklahoma" title="Tulsa, Oklahoma">Tulsa</a>. The population of the city was 39,223 as of the 2010 census, a 2.4 percent increase from 38,310 at the 2000 census,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> making it the eleventh-largest city in Oklahoma.</p>
<p>The 1951 film <i><a href="/wiki/Jim_Thorpe" title="Jim Thorpe">Jim Thorpe</a>, All American,</i> starring <a href="/wiki/Burt_Lancaster" title="Burt Lancaster">Burt Lancaster</a>, was filmed on the campus of <a href="/wiki/Bacone_College" title="Bacone College">Bacone Indian College</a> at Muskogee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thorpe_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-thorpe-4">[4]</a></sup> Two feature films were recently shot in Muskogee: <i>Salvation</i> (2007) and <i>Denizen</i> (2010).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saltlake_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-saltlake-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muskogee,_Oklahoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Muskogee" title="History of Muskogee">History of Muskogee</a></div>
<p>French fur traders were believed to have established a temporary village near the future Muskogee in 1806, but the first permanent European-American settlement was established in 1817 on the south bank of the <a href="/wiki/Verdigris_River" title="Verdigris River">Verdigris River</a>, north of present-day Muskogee.</p>
<p>After the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Removal_Act" title="Indian Removal Act">Indian Removal Act</a> of 1830 under President <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Jackson" title="Andrew Jackson">Andrew Jackson</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Muscogee_(Creek)_Nation" title="Muscogee (Creek) Nation">Creek</a> Indians were one of the Five Civilized Tribes forced out of the American Southeast to Indian Territory. They were accompanied by their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slaves" title="Slaves">slaves</a> to this area. The Indian Agency, a two-story stone building, was built here in Muskogee. It was a site for meetings among the leaders of the Five Civilized Tribes. Today it serves as a museum. At the top of what is known as Agency Hill, it is within Honor Heights Park on the west side of Muskogee.</p>
<p>In 1872, the <a href="/wiki/Missouri%E2%80%93Kansas%E2%80%93Texas_Railroad" title="Missouri–Kansas–Texas Railroad">Missouri–Kansas–Texas Railroad</a> was extended to the area. A federal court was established in Muskogee in 1889, around the same time that Congress opened portions of Indian Territory to non-Native settlers via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_rush" title="Land rush">land rushes</a>. The city was incorporated on March 19, 1898.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waits_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-waits-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>Ohio native <a href="/wiki/Charles_N._Haskell" title="Charles N. Haskell">Charles N. Haskell</a> moved to the city in March 1901.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haskell_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-haskell-7">[7]</a></sup> He was instrumental in building on the land rush; he stimulated expansion of the town of more than 4,000 people to a center of business and industry by 1910, with a population of more than 25,000 inhabitants. Haskell built the first five-story business block in Oklahoma Territory; he built and owned fourteen brick buildings in the city. Most importantly, he organized and built most of the railroads running into the city, which connected it to other markets and centers of population, stimulating its business and retail, and attracting new residents.</p>
<p>As Muskogee’s economic and business importance grew, so did its political power. In the years before the territory was admitted as a state, the Five Civilized Tribes continued to work on alternatives to keep some independence from European Americans. They met together August 21, 1905 to propose the <a href="/wiki/State_of_Sequoyah" title="State of Sequoyah">State of Sequoyah</a>, to be controlled by Native Americans. They met in Muskogee to draft its constitution, planning to have Muskogee serve as the State's capital. The proposal was vetoed by <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">US President</a> <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> and mostly ignored by Congress; the proposed State of Sequoyah was never authorized. The US admitted the State of Oklahoma to the Union on November 16, 1907 as the 46th State.</p>
<p>Muskogee attracted national and international attention when, in May 2008, voters elected <a href="/wiki/John_Tyler_Hammons" title="John Tyler Hammons">John Tyler Hammons</a> as mayor. Nineteen years old at the time of his election, Hammons is among the youngest mayors in American history.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muskogee,_Oklahoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Muskogee is an economic center for eastern Oklahoma and operates the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Muskogee" title="Port of Muskogee">Port of Muskogee</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_River" title="Arkansas River">Arkansas River</a>, which is accessible from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>.</p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city has a total area of 38.8 square miles (100 km<sup>2</sup>), of which 37.3 square miles (97 km<sup>2</sup>) is land and 1.4 square miles (3.6 km<sup>2</sup>) (3.69%) is water. Muskogee is near the <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_River" title="Arkansas River">Arkansas River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verdigris_River" title="Verdigris River">Verdigris River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grand_River_(Oklahoma)" title="Grand River (Oklahoma)">Grand River</a>.</p>
<p>It is served by <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_62_in_Oklahoma" title="U.S. Route 62 in Oklahoma">U.S. Route 62</a>, <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_64" title="U.S. Route 64">U.S. Route 64</a>, <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_69" title="U.S. Route 69">U.S. Route 69</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_State_Highway_16" title="Oklahoma State Highway 16">Oklahoma State Highway 16</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_State_Highway_165" title="Oklahoma State Highway 165">Oklahoma State Highway 165</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oklahoma_State_Highway_351" title="Oklahoma State Highway 351">Oklahoma State Highway 351</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Muskogee_Turnpike" title="Muskogee Turnpike">Muskogee Turnpike</a>.</p>
<p>Muskogee lies in the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_River_Valley" title="Arkansas River Valley">Arkansas River Valley</a> and has a low, sea-level elevation compared to much of the rest of the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archsurvey_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-archsurvey-8">[8]</a></sup> The city is on the boundary of the oak and hickory forest region of eastern Oklahoma and the prairie, <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> region of northeastern Oklahoma. It is a suburban community of Tulsa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archsurvey_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-archsurvey-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city's climate is considerably warmer and more humid than other parts of the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archsurvey_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-archsurvey-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR2_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-14">[14]</a></sup> of 2000, there were 38,310 people, 15,523 households, and 9,950 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 1,026.0 people per square mile (396.1/km²). There were 17,517 housing units at an average density of 469.1 per square mile (181.1/km²). The racial makeup of the city was 61.12% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 17.90% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 12.34% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 0.90% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.02% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 1.57% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 6.16% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 3.28% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 15,523 households out of which 29.3% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 45.2% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 15.4% had a female householder with no husband present, and 35.9% were non-families. 31.8% of all households were made up of individuals and 14.8% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.39 and the average family size was 3.00.</p>
<p>In the city the population was spread out with 25.7% under the age of 18, 9.7% from 18 to 24, 25.8% from 25 to 44, 21.4% from 45 to 64, and 17.4% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 37 years. For every 100 females, there were 88.8 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 83.4 males.</p>
<p>The median income for a household in the city was $26,418, and the median income for a family was $33,358. Males had a median income of $28,153 versus $20,341 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $15,351. About 14.6% of families and 19.2% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 25.9% of those under age 18 and 14.3% of those age 65 or over.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts_and_culture">Arts and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muskogee,_Oklahoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Arts and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Muskogee is home to <a href="/wiki/Honor_Heights_Park" title="Honor Heights Park">Honor Heights Park</a>, a <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> memorial park, notable for its <a href="/wiki/Azalea" title="Azalea">azaleas</a> and the annual Azalea Festival held each April. During the winter, people from across all 50 states travel to Muskogee to see Honor Heights transformed into the Garden of Lights, a 132-acre (0.53 km<sup>2</sup>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christmas_lights_(holiday_decoration)" title="Christmas lights (holiday decoration)">Christmas lights</a> display.</p>
<p>Muskogee has six museums. The <a href="/wiki/Five_Civilized_Tribes_Museum" title="Five Civilized Tribes Museum">Five Civilized Tribes Museum</a> preserves the art and culture of the Five Civilized Tribes. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=U.S.S._Batfish_and_War_Memorial_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="U.S.S. Batfish and War Memorial Park (page does not exist)">U.S.S. Batfish and War Memorial Park</a>'s major attraction is the submarine USS <i>Batfish</i>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_Rivers_Museum" title="Three Rivers Museum">Three Rivers Museum</a> chronicles the history of the Three Rivers area and the railroads that helped create it. The <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_Music_Hall_of_Fame" title="Oklahoma Music Hall of Fame">Oklahoma Music Hall of Fame</a> has been honoring Oklahoma musicians since 1997. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas-Foreman_Historic_Home&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thomas-Foreman Historic Home (page does not exist)">Thomas-Foreman Historic Home</a> is an 1898 farm house preserved with the furnishings of the Indian Territory Judge <a href="/wiki/John_R._Thomas" title="John R. Thomas">John R. Thomas</a> and his daughter and son-in-law Grant and Carolyn Foreman, Oklahoma historians and authors. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ataloa_Lodge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ataloa Lodge (page does not exist)">Ataloa Lodge</a> is on the campus of Bacone College.</p>
<p>Two feature films were recently shot in Muskogee through a tax incentive program offered by the state: <i>Salvation</i> (2007) and <i>Denizen</i> (2010). Writer and director <a href="/wiki/J.A._Steel" title="J.A. Steel">J.A. Steel</a> produced both films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saltlake_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-saltlake-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Film Star &amp; Producer Ford Austin premiered his cult smash feature film "Cerebral Print: the Secret Files at the Roxy Theatre in 2003. The theatre sold out. The following year, Mr. Austin went on to shoot his Award winning short film " The Lady Blades" on Main Street with his longtime friends And Muskogee natives Oscar Ray &amp; Shiron Butterfly of the Bare Bones International Independent Film Festival.</p>
<p>Muskogee is home to the <a class="external text" href="http://www.okcastle.com/" rel="nofollow">Castle of Muskogee</a>. The Castle hosts Fourth of July Fireworks sales, a Halloween festival 'Haunted Castle', a drive-thru Christmas Kingdom and indoor Castle Christmas experience, and the Oklahoma Renaissance Festival, founded in 1995. The Renaissance festival draws in tens of thousands each year, hosting jousts, dancing, vendors and other events.</p>
<p>At the center of Muskogee's flourishing arts scene is <a class="external text" href="http://www.muskogeelittletheatre.com/" rel="nofollow">Muskogee Little Theatre</a> (MLT). MLT was established in 1972 from the unused Sequoyah Elementary School. The theatre puts on up to eight shows per year including youth theatre, senior theatre, holiday shows, and general community productions. The theatre is also committed to <a class="external text" href="http://muskogeelittletheatre.com/index.php?option=com_content&amp;task=category&amp;sectionid=1&amp;id=13&amp;Itemid=29" rel="nofollow">education</a> and development arts programs including Youth Theatre camp, voice lessons, mentorships, weekend workshops and more.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/City_of_Muskogee_Foundation" title="City of Muskogee Foundation">City of Muskogee Foundation</a> provides grants to community organizations and non-profit groups throughout the Muskogee community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muskogee,_Oklahoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Muskogee" title="Government of Muskogee">Government of Muskogee</a></div>
<p>Muskogee is governed by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council-manager_government" title="Council-manager government">council-manager</a> form of municipal government. The city manager is the administrative leader of the government and is appointed by the city council. The city's ceremonial head is the mayor, who is a voting, at-large member of the council with limited administrative power.</p>
<p>The current city manager, Howard W. Brown Jr, was appointed in 2014. The current mayor is Bob Coburn.</p>
<p>The city is divided into four wards, with two members of the city council elected from each. Each member of the council is elected by the city as a whole but must reside in the ward he or she represents. Elections are held on the first Tuesday in April in each even-numbered year. All elections are non-partisan; the mayor and the members of the city council receive no salary or compensation for their services.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="State_and_federal_representation">State and federal representation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muskogee,_Oklahoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: State and federal representation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Muskogee is represented by two <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_House_of_Representatives" title="Oklahoma House of Representatives">state representatives</a>: State Representative Jerry McPeak representing House District 13 and State Representative Arthur Hulbert representing House District 14. District 13 includes the west side of Muskogee while District 14 includes the east half of Muskogee. The city is represented in the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_Senate" title="Oklahoma Senate">state senate</a> by State Senator <a href="/wiki/Earl_Garrison" title="Earl Garrison">Earl Garrison</a> (D-Muskogee), in Senate District 9, which includes all of <a href="/wiki/Muskogee_County,_Oklahoma" title="Muskogee County, Oklahoma">Muskogee County, Oklahoma</a>.</p>
<p>Both former <a href="/wiki/Attorney_General_of_Oklahoma" title="Attorney General of Oklahoma">Attorney General of Oklahoma</a> <a href="/wiki/Drew_Edmondson" title="Drew Edmondson">Drew Edmondson</a> and former <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_Superintendent_of_Public_Instruction" title="Oklahoma Superintendent of Public Instruction">Oklahoma Superintendent of Public Instruction</a> <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Garrett" title="Sandy Garrett">Sandy Garrett</a> are Muskogee natives.</p>
<p>The city is part of <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma%27s_2nd_congressional_district" title="Oklahoma's 2nd congressional district">Oklahoma's 2nd congressional district</a>, represented by U.S. Representative <a href="/wiki/Markwayne_Mullin" title="Markwayne Mullin">Markwayne Mullin</a> (R-Muskogee). The city is also home to U.S. Senator <a href="/wiki/Tom_Coburn" title="Tom Coburn">Tom Coburn</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crime">Crime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muskogee,_Oklahoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Crime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1995, crime rate has risen by 45 percent in Muskogee. According to records by the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_State_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Oklahoma State Bureau of Investigation">Oklahoma State Bureau of Investigation</a>, in 2002, 2008 and 2009, no murders were committed for the entire year. However, on April 10, 2010, a deadly shooting broke out at the Arrowhead Mall injuring at least five people and leaving one dead; witnesses say that the shooting was gang-related. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muskogee,_Oklahoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two primary public school districts in the city of Muskogee: <a href="/wiki/Muskogee_Public_Schools" title="Muskogee Public Schools">Muskogee Public Schools</a>, which include the vast majority of the city limits and a large portion of <a href="/wiki/Muskogee_County,_Oklahoma" title="Muskogee County, Oklahoma">Muskogee County</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hilldale_Public_Schools" title="Hilldale Public Schools">Hilldale Public Schools</a>, which covers a small southern portion of the city limits and some parts of the County south of Muskogee. Additional smaller school districts serve the smaller communities of Muskogee County.</p>
<p>Notably, Muskogee is the location of the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_School_for_the_Blind" title="Oklahoma School for the Blind">Oklahoma School for the Blind</a>, a special institution for meeting the educational needs of blind and visually impaired students residing in the state of Oklahoma.</p>
<p>Muskogee is home to four institutions of higher education: the public four-year <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_State_University" title="Northeastern State University">Northeastern State University</a>, the public two-year <a href="/wiki/Connors_State_College" title="Connors State College">Connors State College</a>, the public <a href="/wiki/Indian_Capital_Technology_Center" title="Indian Capital Technology Center">Indian Capital Technology Center</a> and the private four-year <a href="/wiki/Bacone_College" title="Bacone College">Bacone College</a>, which is the oldest college in the state of Oklahoma.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muskogee,_Oklahoma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Muskogee was commemorated in the 1969 <a href="/wiki/Merle_Haggard" title="Merle Haggard">Merle Haggard</a> song "<a href="/wiki/Okie_from_Muskogee_(song)" title="Okie from Muskogee (song)">Okie from Muskogee</a>".</li>
<li>The song "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Up_Against_the_Wall_Redneck_Mother&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Up Against the Wall Redneck Mother (page does not exist)">Up Against the Wall Redneck Mother</a>" written by Oklahoma native <a href="/wiki/Ray_Wylie_Hubbard" title="Ray Wylie Hubbard">Ray Wylie Hubbard</a> and famously recorded by <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Jeff_Walker" title="Jerry Jeff Walker">Jerry Jeff Walker</a> is a satire of small-town life playfully aimed at Muskogee, which is made evident in the last line of the song: "Muskogee, Oklahoma, U.S.A."</li>
<li>In the sitcom <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friends_(TV_series)" title="Friends (TV series)">Friends</a></i>, Chandler becomes excited when he hears a mention of Muskogee, saying that it's "only four hours from Tulsa," where he resides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In reality, Muskogee is only 40 minutes from Tulsa.</li>
<li>In the film <a href="/wiki/Twister_(1996_film)" title="Twister (1996 film)">Twister (1996 film)</a>, the team headed by Helen Hunt's character Jo Harding was based out of the fictional Muskogee State College</li>
</ul>
