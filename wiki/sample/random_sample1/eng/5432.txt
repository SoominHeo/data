<p><b>Dust</b> consists of <a href="/wiki/Particle" title="Particle">particles</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a> that come from various sources such as soil, dust lifted by weather (an <a href="/wiki/Aeolian_processes" title="Aeolian processes">aeolian process</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_eruptions" title="Volcanic eruptions">volcanic eruptions</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">pollution</a>. Dust in homes, offices, and other human environments contains small amounts of plant <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hair" title="Hair">human</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fur" title="Fur">animal hairs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Textiles" title="Textiles">textile fibers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper fibers</a>, minerals from outdoor soil, <a href="/wiki/Human_skin" title="Human skin">human skin</a> cells, burnt <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorite</a> particles, and many other materials which may be found in the local environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hesskosa_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-hesskosa-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestic_dust_and_humans">Domestic dust and humans</span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/House_dust_mite" title="House dust mite">House dust mites</a> are present indoors wherever humans live. Positive tests for dust mite allergies are extremely common among people with asthma. Dust mites are microscopic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arachnids" title="Arachnids">arachnids</a> whose primary food is dead human skin cells, but they do not live on living people. They and their feces and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allergens" title="Allergens">allergens</a> they produce are major constituents of house dust, but because they are so heavy they are not suspended for long in the air. They are generally found on the floor and other surfaces until disturbed (by walking, for example). It could take somewhere between twenty minutes and two hours for dust mites to settle back down out of the air.</p>
<p>Dust mites are a nesting species that prefers a dark, warm, and humid climate. They flourish in mattresses, bedding, upholstered furniture, and <a href="/wiki/Carpet" title="Carpet">carpets</a>. Their feces include enzymes that are released upon contact with a moist surface, which can happen when a person inhales, and these enzymes can kill cells within the human body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> House dust mites did not become a problem until humans began to use textiles, such as western style blankets and clothing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Atmospheric or wind-borne dust, also known as <i>aeolian dust</i>, comes from arid and dry regions where high velocity winds are able to remove mostly silt-sized material, deflating susceptible surfaces. This includes areas where grazing, ploughing, vehicle use, and other human activities have further destabilized the land, though not all source areas have been largely affected by <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic impacts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onlinelibrary.wiley.com_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-onlinelibrary.wiley.com-4">[4]</a></sup> One-third of the global land area is covered by dust-producing surfaces, made up of <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">hyper-arid regions</a> like the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> which covers 0.9 billion hectares, and <a href="/wiki/Drylands" title="Drylands">drylands</a> which occupy 5.2 billion hectares.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dust in the atmosphere is produced by <a href="/wiki/Saltation_(geology)" title="Saltation (geology)">saltation</a> and sandblasting of sand-sized grains, and it is transported through the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>. This airborne dust is considered an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate" title="Particulate">aerosol</a> and once in the atmosphere, it can produce strong local <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a>. Saharan dust in particular can be transported and deposited as far as the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazonia" title="Amazonia">Amazonia</a>, and may affect air temperatures, cause <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">ocean cooling</a>, and alter rainfall amounts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onlinelibrary.wiley.com_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-onlinelibrary.wiley.com-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East">Middle East</span></h3>
<p>Dust in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> has been a historic phenomenon. Recently, because of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> and the escalating process of <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a>, the problem has worsened dramatically. As a multi-factor phenomenon, there is not yet a clear consensus on the sources or potential solutions to the problem.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, the dust is already affecting more than 5 million people directly, and has emerged as a serious government issue in recent years. In the province of <a href="/wiki/Khuzestan_Province" title="Khuzestan Province">Khuzestan</a> it has led to the severe reduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_quality" title="Air quality">air quality</a>. The amount of pollutants in the air has surpassed more than 50 times the normal level several times in a year. Recently, initiatives such as Project-Dust have been established to directly study the Middle Eastern dust.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Road_dust">Road dust</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Road_debris" title="Road debris">Road debris</a></div>
<p>Dust kicked up by vehicles traveling on <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">roads</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> may make up 33% of <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Road dust consists of deposits of <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas" title="Exhaust gas">vehicle exhausts and industrial exhausts</a>, particles from tire and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brake_dust" title="Brake dust">brake wear</a>, dust from paved roads or <a href="/wiki/Pothole" title="Pothole">potholes</a>, and dust from construction sites. Road dust is a significant source contributing to the generation and release of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate" title="Particulate">particulate matter</a> into the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Control of road dust is a significant challenge in urban areas, and also in other locations with high levels of vehicular traffic upon unsealed roads, such as <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfill</a> dumps.</p>
<p>Road dust may be suppressed by mechanical methods like <a href="/wiki/Street_sweeper" title="Street sweeper">street sweeper</a> vehicles equipped with <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_cleaner" title="Vacuum cleaner">vacuum cleaners</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> vegetable oil sprays,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usroads.com_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-usroads.com-10">[10]</a></sup> or with water sprayers. Improvements in automotive engineering have reduced the amount of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PM10" title="PM10">PM10s</a> produced by road traffic; the proportion representing re-suspension of existing particulates has, increased as a result.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coal_dust">Coal dust</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coal_dust" title="Coal dust">Coal dust</a> is responsible for the lung disease known as <a href="/wiki/Pneumoconiosis" title="Pneumoconiosis">pneumoconiosis</a>, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_worker%27s_pneumoconiosis" title="Coal worker's pneumoconiosis">black lung</a> disease that occurs among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_miners" title="Coal miners">coal miners</a>. The danger of coal dust resulted in environmental legislation regulating work place air quality in some jurisdictions. In addition, if enough coal dust is dispersed within the air in a given area, in very rare circumstances, it can create an <a href="/wiki/Dust_explosion" title="Dust explosion">explosion</a> hazard under certain circumstances. These circumstances are typically within confined spaces.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dust_control">Dust control</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Control_of_atmospheric_dust">Control of atmospheric dust</span></h3>
<p>Most governmental EPAs, including the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) mandate that facilities that generate dust, minimize or mitigate the production of dust in their operation. The most frequent dust control violations occur at new residential housing developments in <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> areas. United States Federal law requires that construction sites obtain permits to conduct earth moving, clearing of areas, to include plans to control dust <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pollutant" title="Air pollutant">emissions</a> when the work is being carried out. Control measures include such simple practices as spraying construction and <a href="/wiki/Demolition" title="Demolition">demolition</a> sites with water, and preventing the tracking of dust onto adjacent roads.</p>
<p>Some of the issues include:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Some of these require support (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Reducing dust related health risks that include allergic reactions, pneumonia and asthmatic attacks.</li>
<li>Improving visibility and road safety.</li>
<li>Providing cleaner air, cleaner vehicles and cleaner homes and promoting better health.</li>
<li>Improving crop productivity in agriculture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="How does controlling dust improve productivity?date=October 2015 (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Reducing vehicle maintenance costs by lowering the levels of dust that clog filters, bearings and machinery.</li>
<li>Reducing driver fatigue, maintenance on suspension systems and improving fuel economy.</li>
<li>Increasing cumulative effect - each new application builds on previous residuals reducing re-application rate *while improving performance.</li>
</ul>
<p>US federal laws require dust control on sources such as vacant lots, unpaved parking lots, and unpaved roads. Dust in such places may be suppressed by mechanical methods,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> including paving or laying down gravel, or stabilizing the surface with water, vegetable oils<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usroads.com_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-usroads.com-10">[10]</a></sup> or other <a href="/wiki/Dust_abatement" title="Dust abatement">dust suppressants</a>, or by using water misters to suppress dust that is already airborne.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>Dust control is the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/suppression" title="wikt:suppression">suppression</a> of solid particles with diameters less than 500 micrometers. Dust poses a health threat to children,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> older people, and those with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Respiratory_illness" title="Respiratory illness">respiratory illnesses</a>.</p>
<p>House dust can become airborne easily. Care is required when removing dust to avoid causing the dust to become airborne. A <a href="/wiki/Feather_duster" title="Feather duster">feather duster</a> tends to agitate the dust so it lands elsewhere. Products like <a href="/wiki/Pledge_(brand)" title="Pledge (brand)">Pledge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swiffer" title="Swiffer">Swiffer</a> are specifically made for removing dust by trapping it with sticky chemicals.</p>
<p>Certified <a href="/wiki/HEPA" title="HEPA">HEPA</a> (tested to MIL STD 282) can effectively trap 99.97% of dust at 0.3 micrometers. Not all HEPA (type/media) filters can effectively stop dust; while <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_cleaner" title="Vacuum cleaner">vacuum cleaners</a> with HEPA (type/media) filters, water, or cyclones may filter more effectively than without, they may still exhaust millions of particles per cubic foot of air circulated. <a href="/wiki/Central_vacuum_cleaner" title="Central vacuum cleaner">Central vacuum cleaners</a> can be effective in removing dust, especially if they are exhausted directly to the outdoors.</p>
<p>Air filtering appliances differ greatly in their effectiveness. Laser particle counters are an effective way to measure filter effectiveness, medical grade instruments can test for particles as small as 0.3 micrometers. In order to test for dust in the air, there are several options available. Pre weighted filter and matched weight filters made from <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">polyvinyl chloride</a> or mixed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cellulose_ester" title="Cellulose ester">cellulose ester</a> are suitable for respirable dust (less than 10 micrometers in diameter).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Control_of_dust_resistance_on_surfaces">Control of dust resistance on surfaces</span></h3>
<p>A dust resistant <a href="/wiki/Surface_(topology)" title="Surface (topology)">surface</a> is a state of prevention against dust contamination or damage, by a <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">design</a> or treatment of materials and items in <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a> or through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Repair" title="Repair">repair</a> process<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. A reduced <a href="/wiki/Tacticity" title="Tacticity">tacticity</a> of a synthetic layer or covering can protect surfaces and release small molecules that could have remained attached. A panel, container or enclosure with <a href="/wiki/Hemming_and_seaming" title="Hemming and seaming">seams</a> may feature types of strengthened <a href="/wiki/Structural_rigidity" title="Structural rigidity">rigidity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sealant" title="Sealant">sealant</a> to vulnerable <a href="/wiki/Edge_(geometry)" title="Edge (geometry)">edges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Join_(topology)" title="Join (topology)">joins</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dust_in_outer_space">Dust in outer space</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">Cosmic dust</a> is widely present in space, where gas and dust clouds are primary precursors for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_systems" title="Planetary systems">planetary systems</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Zodiacal_light" title="Zodiacal light">zodiacal light</a>, as seen in a dark night sky, is produced by sunlight reflected from particles of dust in orbit around the Sun. The tails of <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a> are produced by emissions of dust and ionized gas from the body of the comet. Dust also covers solid planetary bodies, and vast <a href="/wiki/Dust_storm" title="Dust storm">dust storms</a> occur on Mars that cover almost the entire planet. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_dust" title="Interstellar dust">Interstellar dust</a> is found between the stars, and high concentrations produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffuse_nebulae" title="Diffuse nebulae">diffuse nebulae</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reflection_nebulae" title="Reflection nebulae">reflection nebulae</a>.</p>
<p>Dust is widely present in the galaxy. Ambient radiation heats dust and re-emits radiation into the microwave band, which may distort the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">cosmic microwave background</a> power spectrum. Dust in this regime has a complicated <a href="/wiki/Emission_spectrum" title="Emission spectrum">emission spectrum</a>, and includes both thermal dust emission and <a href="/wiki/Spinning_dust" title="Spinning dust">spinning dust</a> emission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dust samples returned from outer space may provide information about conditions in the early solar system. Several spacecraft have sought to gather samples of dust and other materials. Among these craft was <a href="/wiki/Stardust_(spacecraft)" title="Stardust (spacecraft)">Stardust</a>, which flew past <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comet_Wild_2" title="Comet Wild 2">Comet Wild 2</a> in 2004, and returned a capsule of the <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a>'s remains to Earth in January 2006. In 2010 the <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hayabusa_(spacecraft)" title="Hayabusa (spacecraft)">Hayabusa</a> spacecraft returned samples of dust from the surface of an <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a>.</p>
