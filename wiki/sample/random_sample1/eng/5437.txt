<p>A <b>multirotor</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or <b>multicopter</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Rotorcraft" title="Rotorcraft">rotorcraft</a> with more than two <a href="/wiki/Helicopter_rotor" title="Helicopter rotor">rotors</a>. An advantage of multirotor aircraft is the simpler rotor mechanics required for flight control. Unlike single- and double-rotor <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopters</a> which use complex <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Variable_pitch_rotor" title="Variable pitch rotor">variable pitch rotors</a> whose pitch varies as the blade rotates for flight stability and control, multirotors often use fixed-<a href="/wiki/Blade_pitch" title="Blade pitch">pitch</a> blades; control of vehicle motion is achieved by varying the relative speed of each rotor to change the thrust and torque produced by each.</p>
<p>Due to their ease of both construction and control, multirotor aircraft are frequently used in <a href="/wiki/Radio-controlled_aircraft" title="Radio-controlled aircraft">radio control aircraft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">UAV</a> projects<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> in which the names <b>tricopter</b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Quadcopter" title="Quadcopter">quadcopter</a></b>, <b>hexacopter</b> and <b>octocopter</b> are frequently used to refer to 3-, 4-, 6- and 8-rotor helicopters, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>In order to allow more power and stability at reduced weight <a href="/wiki/Coaxial_rotors" title="Coaxial rotors">coaxial rotors</a> can be employed, in which each arm has two motors, running in opposite directions (one facing up and one facing down).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multirotor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cierva_Air_Horse" title="Cierva Air Horse">Cierva Air Horse</a> - a British three-rotor "heavy lift" helicopter first flying in 1948. Three rotors were used to give a large lift without compromising rotor strength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>e-volo - a German prototype electric multicopter with 16 rotors, the first electric multicopter in the world to achieve manned flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The large number of low-cost motors make it economical, quiet and provide redundancy with ability to maintain control with up to four failed motors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
