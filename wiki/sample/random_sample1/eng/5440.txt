<p>A <b>contusion</b>, commonly known as a <b>bruise</b>, is a type of <a href="/wiki/Hematoma" title="Hematoma">hematoma</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissue</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> in which <a href="/wiki/Capillary" title="Capillary">capillaries</a> and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Venule" title="Venule">venules</a> are damaged by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_trauma" title="Physical trauma">trauma</a>, allowing <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> to seep, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemorrhage" title="Hemorrhage">hemorrhage</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extravasate" title="Extravasate">extravasate</a> into the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Interstitial_cell" title="Interstitial cell">interstitial</a> tissues. Bruises, which do not blanch under pressure, can involve capillaries at the level of <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subcutaneous_tissue" title="Subcutaneous tissue">subcutaneous tissue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscle</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Bruises are not to be confused with other similar-looking lesions primarily distinguished by their <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a> or causation. These lesions include <a href="/wiki/Petechia" title="Petechia">petechia</a> (&lt; 3 mm result from numerous and diverse etiologies such as adverse reactions from medications such as warfarin, straining, asphyxiation, <a href="/wiki/Platelet" title="Platelet">platelet</a> disorders and diseases such as cytomegalovirus),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Purpura" title="Purpura">purpura</a> (3 mm to 1 cm, classified as palpable purpura or non-palpable purpura and indicates various pathologic conditions such as thrombocytopenia),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ecchymosis" title="Ecchymosis">ecchymosis</a> (&gt;1 cm caused blood dissecting through tissue planes and settled in an area remote from the site of trauma or pathology such as periorbital ecchymosis, i.e.,"raccoon eyes" , arising from a basilar skull fracture or from a neuroblastoma).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a type of hematoma, a bruise is always caused by internal bleeding into the interstitial tissues which does not break through the skin, usually initiated by <a href="/wiki/Blunt_trauma" title="Blunt trauma">blunt trauma</a>, which causes damage through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compression_(physical)" title="Compression (physical)">physical compression</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">deceleration</a> forces. Trauma sufficient to cause bruising can occur from a wide variety of situations including accidents, falls, and surgeries. Disease states such as insufficient or malfunctioning <a href="/wiki/Platelet" title="Platelet">platelets</a>, other <a href="/wiki/Coagulation" title="Coagulation">coagulation</a> deficiencies, or vascular disorders, such as venous blockage associated with severe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allergies" title="Allergies">allergies</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allergynursing_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-allergynursing-7">[7]</a></sup> can lead to the formation of purpura which is not to be confused with trauma-related bruising/contusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> If the trauma is sufficient to break the skin and allow blood to escape the interstitial tissues, the injury is not a bruise but instead a different variety of hemorrhage called <a href="/wiki/Bleeding" title="Bleeding">bleeding</a>. However, such injuries may be accompanied by bruising elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robbins_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robbins-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bruises often induce <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">pain</a>, but small bruises are not normally dangerous alone. Sometimes bruises can be serious, leading to other more life-threatening forms of hematoma, such as when associated with serious injuries, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fracture_(bone)" title="Fracture (bone)">fractures</a> and more severe <a href="/wiki/Internal_bleeding" title="Internal bleeding">internal bleeding</a>. The likelihood and severity of bruising depends on many factors, including type and healthiness of affected tissues. Minor bruises may be easily recognized in people with light <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skin_color" title="Skin color">skin color</a> by characteristic <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a> or <a href="/wiki/Purple" title="Purple">purple</a> appearance (idiomatically described as "black and blue") in the days following the injury.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">Cause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bruise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The presence of bruises may be seen in patients with <a href="/wiki/Platelet" title="Platelet">platelet</a> or <a href="/wiki/Coagulation" title="Coagulation">coagulation</a> disorders. Unexplained bruising may be a warning sign of <a href="/wiki/Child_abuse" title="Child abuse">child abuse</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_abuse" title="Domestic abuse">domestic abuse</a>, or serious medical problems such as <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meningococcus" title="Meningococcus">meningoccocal</a> infection. Unexplained bruising can also indicate internal bleeding or certain types of cancer. Long-term glucocorticoid therapy can cause easy bruising. Bruising present around the <a href="/wiki/Navel" title="Navel">navel</a> (belly button) with severe abdominal pain suggests acute <a href="/wiki/Pancreatitis" title="Pancreatitis">pancreatitis</a>. Connective tissue disorders such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ehlers-Danlos_syndrome" title="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome">Ehlers-Danlos syndrome</a> may cause relatively easy or spontaneous bruising depending on the severity.</p>
<p>During an <a href="/wiki/Autopsy" title="Autopsy">autopsy</a>, bruises accompanying <a href="/wiki/Abrasion_(medical)" title="Abrasion (medical)">abrasions</a> indicate the abrasions occurred while the individual was alive, as opposed to damage incurred post mortem.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Size_and_shape">Size and shape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bruise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Size and shape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bruise shapes may correspond directly to the instrument of injury or be modified by additional factors. Bruises often become more prominent as time lapses, resulting in additional size and swelling, and may grow to a large size over the course of the hours after the injury that caused the bruise was inflicted. As stated above, bruising present in a different location than the site of impact is called <i>ecchymosis</i> and generally occurs when the tissue at the site of injury is loose, allowing blood to travel under the skin to another location due to <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> or other forces, such as in a <a href="/wiki/Black_eye" title="Black eye">black eye</a>.</p>

<ul>
<li>Condition and type of tissue: In soft tissues, a larger area is bruised than would be in firmer tissue due to ease of blood to invade tissue.</li>
<li>Age: elderly skin and other tissues are often thinner and less elastic and thus more prone to bruising.</li>
<li>Gender: More bruising occurs in females due to increased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subcutaneous_fat" title="Subcutaneous fat">subcutaneous fat</a>.</li>
<li>Skin tone: Discoloration caused by bruises is more prominent in lighter complexions.</li>
<li>Diseases: <a href="/wiki/Coagulation" title="Coagulation">Coagulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Platelet" title="Platelet">platelet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessel</a> diseases or deficiencies can increase bruising due to more bleeding.</li>
<li>Location: More extensive vascularity causes more bleeding. Areas such as the arms, knees, shins and the facial area are especially common bruise sites.</li>
<li>Forces: Greater striking forces cause greater bruising.</li>
<li>Genetics: Despite having completely normal coagulation factors, natural <a href="/wiki/Red_hair" title="Red hair">redheads</a> have been shown to bruise more, although this may just be due to greater visibility on commonly associated lighter complexion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-redhead_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-redhead-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Severity">Severity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bruise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Severity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bruises can be scored on a scale from 0-5 to categorize the severity and danger of the injury.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>The harm score is determined by the extent and severity of the fractures to the organs and tissues causing the bruising, in turn depending on multiple factors. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Muscle_contraction" title="Muscle contraction">contracted</a> muscle will bruise more severely, as will tissues crushed against underlying bone. <a href="/wiki/Capillary" title="Capillary">Capillaries</a> vary in strength, stiffness and toughness, which can also vary by age and medical conditions.</p>
<p>An alternate bruise severity ranking system called the Chien Intensity Scale is slowly growing in popularity in some research circles. Although not widely used, the Chien Intensity Scale is used by institutes including the Ryan Mackey Memorial Research Institute and the Sydney Medical Center.</p>
<p>Low levels of damaging forces produce small bruises and generally cause the individual to feel minor <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">pain</a> straight away. Repeated impacts worsen bruises, increasing the harm level. Normally, light bruises heal nearly completely within two weeks, although duration is affected by variation in severity and individual healing processes;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> generally, more severe or deeper bruises take somewhat longer.</p>
<p>Severe bruising (harm score 2-3) may be dangerous or cause serious complications. Further bleeding and excess fluid may accumulate causing a hard, fluctuating lump or swelling <a href="/wiki/Hematoma" title="Hematoma">hematoma</a>. This has the potential to cause <a href="/wiki/Compartment_syndrome" title="Compartment syndrome">compartment syndrome</a> as the swelling cuts off blood flow to the tissues. The trauma that induced the bruise may also have caused other severe and potentially fatal harm to internal <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a>. For example, impacts to the <a href="/wiki/Head" title="Head">head</a> can cause <a href="/wiki/Traumatic_brain_injury" title="Traumatic brain injury">traumatic brain injury</a>: bleeding, bruising and massive swelling of the brain with the potential to cause <a href="/wiki/Concussion" title="Concussion">concussion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coma" title="Coma">coma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a>. Treatment for brain bruising may involve emergency <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgery</a> to relieve the pressure on the brain.</p>
<p>Damage that causes bruising can also cause bones to be <a href="/wiki/Bone_fracture" title="Bone fracture">broken</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tendon" title="Tendon">tendons</a> or <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscles</a> to be <a href="/wiki/Strain_(injury)" title="Strain (injury)">strained</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ligament" title="Ligament">ligaments</a> to be <a href="/wiki/Sprain" title="Sprain">sprained</a>, or other tissue to be damaged. The symptoms and signs of these injuries may initially appear to be those of simple bruising. Abdominal bruising or severe injuries that cause difficulty in moving a limb or the feeling of liquid under the skin may indicate life-threatening injury and require the attention of a physician.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bruise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Increased distress to tissue causes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capillaries" title="Capillaries">capillaries</a> to break under the skin, allowing blood to escape and build up. As time progresses, blood seeps into the surrounding tissues, causing the bruise to darken and spread. <a href="/wiki/Nerve" title="Nerve">Nerve</a> endings within the affected tissue detect the increased pressure, which, depending on severity and location, may be perceived as pain or pressure or be <a href="/wiki/Asymptomatic" title="Asymptomatic">asymptomatic</a>. The damaged capillary <a href="/wiki/Endothelium" title="Endothelium">endothelium</a> releases <a href="/wiki/Endothelin" title="Endothelin">endothelin</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormone</a> that causes <a href="/wiki/Vasoconstriction" title="Vasoconstriction">narrowing of the blood vessel</a> to minimize bleeding. As the endothelium is destroyed, the underlying <a href="/wiki/Von_Willebrand_factor" title="Von Willebrand factor">von Willebrand factor</a> is exposed and initiates <a href="/wiki/Coagulation" title="Coagulation">coagulation</a>, which creates a temporary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clot" title="Clot">clot</a> to plug the wound and eventually leads to restoration of normal tissue.</p>
<p>During this time, larger bruises may change color due to the breakdown of <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> from within escaped <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">red blood cells</a> in the extracellular space. The striking colors of a bruise are caused by the <a href="/wiki/Phagocytosis" title="Phagocytosis">phagocytosis</a> and sequential degradation of hemoglobin to <a href="/wiki/Biliverdin" title="Biliverdin">biliverdin</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bilirubin" title="Bilirubin">bilirubin</a> to <a href="/wiki/Hemosiderin" title="Hemosiderin">hemosiderin</a>, with hemoglobin itself producing a red-blue color, biliverdin producing a green color, bilirubin producing a yellow color, and hemosiderin producing a golden-brown color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robbins_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Robbins-9">[9]</a></sup> As these products are cleared from the area, the bruise disappears. Often the underlying tissue damage has been repaired long before this process is complete.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bruise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Treatment for light bruises is minimal and may include <a href="/wiki/RICE_(medicine)" title="RICE (medicine)">RICE</a> (rest, ice, compression, elevation), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Painkiller" title="Painkiller">painkillers</a> (particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NSAID" title="NSAID">NSAIDs</a>) and, later in recovery, light stretching exercises. Particularly, immediate application of ice while elevating the area may reduce or completely prevent swelling by restricting blood flow to the area and preventing internal bleeding. Rest and preventing re-injury is essential for rapid recovery. Applying a medicated cream containing mucopolysaccharide polysulfuric acid (e.g., Hirudoid) may also speed the healing process. Other topical creams containing skin-fortifying ingredients, including but not limited to <a href="/wiki/Retinol" title="Retinol">retinol</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha_hydroxy_acids" title="Alpha hydroxy acids">alpha hydroxy acids</a>, such as DerMend, can improve the appearance of bruising faster than if left to heal on its own.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Very gently massaging the area and applying heat may encourage blood flow and relieve pain according to the <a href="/wiki/History_of_pain_theory#Gate_control_theory" title="History of pain theory">gate control theory of pain</a>, although causing additional pain may indicate the massage is exacerbating the injury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> As for most injuries, these techniques should not be applied until at least three days following the initial damage to ensure all internal bleeding has stopped, because although increasing blood flow will allow more healing factors into the area and encourage drainage, if the injury is still bleeding this will allow more blood to seep out of the wound and cause the bruise to become worse.</p>
<p>In most cases hematomas spontaneously revert, but in cases of large hematomas or those localized in certain organs (<i>e.g.</i>, the brain), the physician may optionally perform a puncture of the hematoma to allow the blood to exit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bruise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Folk medicine, including ancient medicine of Egyptians, Greeks, Celts, Turks, Slavs, Mayans, Aztecs and Chinese, has used bruising as a treatment for some health problems. The methods vary widely and include cupping, scraping, and slapping. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_cupping" title="Fire cupping">Fire cupping</a> uses suction which causes bruising in patients. Scraping (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gua_Sha" title="Gua Sha">Gua Sha</a>) uses a small hand device with a rounded edge to gently scrape the scalp or the skin. Another ancient device that creates mild bruising is a <a href="/wiki/Strigil" title="Strigil">strigil</a>, used by Greeks and Romans in the bath. Archaeologically there is no precedent for scraping tools before Greek archaeological evidence, not Chinese or Egyptian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
