<p><b>Meng Jianzhu</b> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">孟建柱</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Mèng Jiànzhù</span></i>; born July 1947) is a Chinese politician and a member of the <a href="/wiki/Politburo_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="Politburo of the Communist Party of China">Politburo of the Communist Party of China</a> since 2012. He is currently the Secretary of <a href="/wiki/Central_Political_and_Legal_Affairs_Commission" title="Central Political and Legal Affairs Commission">Central Political and Legal Affairs Commission</a> (<i>Zhengfawei</i>) of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party of China</a> and the successor to <a href="/wiki/Zhou_Yongkang" title="Zhou Yongkang">Zhou Yongkang</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He also heads the <a href="/wiki/Central_Public_Security_Comprehensive_Management_Commission" title="Central Public Security Comprehensive Management Commission">Central Public Security Comprehensive Management Commission</a>.</p>
<p>Meng began his career in a farming cooperative based in rural Shanghai. He made his way up the ranks in the city, serving as vice mayor in charge of agriculture, then deputy party chief. Prior to his tenure as <i>Zhengfawei</i> chief, he also served as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Public_Security_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Ministry of Public Security of the People's Republic of China">Minister of Public Security</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist_Party_Secretary" title="Communist Party Secretary">Communist Party Secretary</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jiangxi" title="Jiangxi">Jiangxi</a> Province.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meng_Jianzhu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Life.2C_Shanghai">Early Life, Shanghai</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meng_Jianzhu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early Life, Shanghai">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Meng was born in July 1947 in <a href="/wiki/Wu_County" title="Wu County">Wu County</a>, a present-day urban district of <a href="/wiki/Suzhou" title="Suzhou">Suzhou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jiangsu" title="Jiangsu">Jiangsu</a> Province. In the 1960s, he headed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Changxing_Island,_Shanghai" title="Changxing Island, Shanghai">Changxing Island</a> in Shanghai to become a tractor operator. For the next 13 years he worked on the rural agricultural cooperative. He graduated from the Shanghai Mechanical College (now part of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Shanghai_for_Science_and_Technology" title="University of Shanghai for Science and Technology">University of Shanghai for Science and Technology</a>). He joined the Communist Party of China in 1971. He became head of the rural cooperative in 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-australia_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-australia-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>He spent much of his earlier political career in Shanghai. He served in leading roles in Chuansha County (which became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pudong_New_District" title="Pudong New District">Pudong New District</a> in 1992) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jiading_County" title="Jiading County">Jiading County</a>, before being transferred to become one of the deputy mayors of <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a> starting in 1993. His portfolio included was agriculture and rural development. In 1996 he was promoted to become the deputy Communist Party secretary of Shanghai, one of the most powerful positions in China's commercial hub. He held the position until 2001. While in Shanghai Meng was low-key and camera-shy, but was largely popular with city residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-australia_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-australia-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jiangxi">Jiangxi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meng_Jianzhu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Jiangxi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2001, Meng was appointed the Communist Party secretary of <a href="/wiki/Jiangxi" title="Jiangxi">Jiangxi</a> Province. Some say Meng was transferred out of Shanghai because he had lost out on a struggle to <a href="/wiki/Chen_Liangyu" title="Chen Liangyu">Chen Liangyu</a> become the city's party boss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-australia_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-australia-2">[2]</a></sup> Meng and Chen were polar opposites in many ways - Chen preferred to be in the limelight while Meng preferred doing work behind the scenes. Meng was also known for paying regular respects for retired municipal politicians. When Meng arrived in Jiangxi, most residents were optimistic. A common refrain amongst Jiangxi residents at the time was "if Jiangxi wants to be rich, keep Meng Jianzhu!".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Meng was often regarded by observers as a "soft" leader, he was known for his strong adherence to rules and principles. For example, once during a provincial conference of officials at county level and above, some county leaders did not show up and instead sent their assistants to attend. Meng responded to this by postponing the entire conference until all the required attendees arrived as a means to reprimand those who failed to show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-australia_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-australia-2">[2]</a></sup> He remained party chief of Jiangxi until October 28, 2007, when he was transferred to Beijing and appointed the Minister of the Public Security, replacing political heavyweight <a href="/wiki/Zhou_Yongkang" title="Zhou Yongkang">Zhou Yongkang</a>, who was elevated to become a member of the <a href="/wiki/Politburo_Standing_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="Politburo Standing Committee of the Communist Party of China">Politburo Standing Committee</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Politburo_and_Political_and_Legal_Affairs_Commission">Politburo and Political and Legal Affairs Commission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meng_Jianzhu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Politburo and Political and Legal Affairs Commission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Meng became a member of the Politburo of the Communist Party of China in November 2012, following the <a href="/wiki/18th_National_Congress_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="18th National Congress of the Communist Party of China">18th Party Congress</a>. He succeeded Zhou as Secretary of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Political_and_Legal_Affairs_Commission" title="Central Political and Legal Affairs Commission">Central Political and Legal Affairs Commission</a>. However, the commission's powers and autonomy was significantly curbed during Meng's term as a result of the consolidation of power around the central authority of Xi Jinping. In December 2012, he was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Guo_Shengkun" title="Guo Shengkun">Guo Shengkun</a> as Minister of Public Security. In January 2013, Meng announced that his commission would begin work on abolishing the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Re-education_through_labour" title="Re-education through labour">re-education through labour</a>" system, which has been widely criticized internationally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forcedlabour_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-forcedlabour-4">[4]</a></sup> This was further cemented at the third plenary session of the 18th Central Committee held in November 2013. The implementation of the policy was largely completed by the end of that year. Meng also criticized the interference of local party authorities, particularly local <i>Zhengfawei</i>, in individual court cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmpjan9_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-scmpjan9-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>While his predecessor Zhou Yongkang underwent investigation for corruption and other offenses in 2014, Meng remained untouched by the scandal despite having served under Zhou for five years as public security minister. Other former subordinates of Zhou in the ministry, such as former deputy minister <a href="/wiki/Li_Dongsheng" title="Li Dongsheng">Li Dongsheng</a>, were detained for investigation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meng_Jianzhu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Meng's associates and colleagues describe him as soft-spoken, humble, likes to smile, of thin build, and looks like an intellectual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-australia_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-australia-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
