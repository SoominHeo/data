<p><b>Megara</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ər/ 'er' in 'finger'">ər</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Μέγαρα</span>, <small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Greek" title="Help:IPA for Greek">[ˈmeɣara]</a></span>) is a historic town and a municipality in <a href="/wiki/West_Attica" title="West Attica">West Attica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>. It lies in the northern section of the <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Corinth" title="Isthmus of Corinth">Isthmus of Corinth</a> opposite the island of <a href="/wiki/Salamis_Island" title="Salamis Island">Salamis</a>, which belonged to Megara in archaic times, before being taken by <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>. Megara was one of the four districts of Attica, embodied in the four mythic sons of King <a href="/wiki/Pandion_II" title="Pandion II">Pandion II</a>, of whom <a href="/wiki/Nisos" title="Nisos">Nisos</a> was the ruler of Megara. Megara was also a trade port, its people using their ships and wealth as a way to gain leverage on armies of neighboring poleis. Megara specialized in the exportation of wool and other animal products including livestock such as horses. It possessed two harbors, Pegae, to the west on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corinthian_Gulf" title="Corinthian Gulf">Corinthian Gulf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nisaea" title="Nisaea">Nisaea</a>, to the east on the <a href="/wiki/Saronic_Gulf" title="Saronic Gulf">Saronic Gulf</a> of the Aegean Sea.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(geographer)" title="Pausanias (geographer)">Pausanias</a>, the Megarians said that their town owed its origin to <a href="/wiki/Car_(Greek_mythology)" title="Car (Greek mythology)">Car</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/Phoroneus" title="Phoroneus">Phoroneus</a>, who built the citadel called 'Caria' and the temples of <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">Demeter</a> called Megara, from which the place derived its name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In historical times, Megara was an early dependency of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Corinth" title="Ancient Corinth">Corinth</a>, in which capacity colonists from Megara founded <a href="/wiki/Megara_Hyblaea" title="Megara Hyblaea">Megara Hyblaea</a>, a small <i>polis</i> north of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syracuse,_Italy" title="Syracuse, Italy">Syracuse</a> in Sicily. Megara then fought a war of independence with Corinth, and afterwards founded <a href="/wiki/Chalcedon" title="Chalcedon">Chalcedon</a> in 685 BC, as well as <a href="/wiki/Byzantium" title="Byzantium">Byzantium</a> (c. 667 BC).</p>
<p>Megara is known to have early ties with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miletos" title="Miletos">Miletos</a>, in the region of <a href="/wiki/Caria" title="Caria">Caria</a> in Asia Minor. According to some scholars, they had built up a “colonisation alliance”. In the 7th/6th century BCE these two cities acted in concordance with each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both cities acted under the leadership and sanction of an <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> oracle. Megara cooperated with that of Delphi. Miletos had her own oracle of Apollo Didymeus Milesios in <a href="/wiki/Didyma" title="Didyma">Didyma</a>. Also, there are many parallels in the political organisation of both cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 7th century BC <a href="/wiki/Theagenes_of_Megara" title="Theagenes of Megara">Theagenes</a> established himself as tyrant of Megara by slaughtering the cattle of the rich to win over the poor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> During the second Persian invasion of Greece (480-479 BC) Megara fought alongside the Spartans and Athenians at crucial battles such as <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Salamis" title="Battle of Salamis">Salamis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Plataea" title="Battle of Plataea">Plataea</a>.</p>
<p>Megara's defection from the Spartan-dominated <a href="/wiki/Peloponnesian_League" title="Peloponnesian League">Peloponnesian League</a> (c. 460 BC) became one of the causes of the <a href="/wiki/First_Peloponnesian_War" title="First Peloponnesian War">First Peloponnesian War</a> (460 BC - c. 445 BC). By the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_Peace" title="Thirty Years' Peace">Thirty Years' Peace</a> of 446-445 BC Megara was returned to the Peloponnesian League.</p>
<p>In the (second) <a href="/wiki/Peloponnesian_War" title="Peloponnesian War">Peloponnesian War</a> (c. 431 BC-404 BC), Megara was an ally of <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Sparta</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Megarian_decree" title="Megarian decree">Megarian decree</a> is considered to be one of several contributing "causes" of the Peloponnesian War.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Athens issued the Megarian decree with the aim of choking out the Megarian economy. The decree banned Megarian merchants from territory controlled by Athens. The Athenians claimed that they were responding to the Megarians' desecration of the <i><a href="/wiki/Hiera_Orgas" title="Hiera Orgas">Hiera Orgas</a></i>, a sacred precinct in the border region between the two states.</p>
<p>Arguably the most famous citizen of Megara in antiquity was <a href="/wiki/Byzas" title="Byzas">Byzas</a>, the legendary founder of <a href="/wiki/Byzantium" title="Byzantium">Byzantium</a> in the 7th century BC. The 6th-century BC poet <a href="/wiki/Theognis_of_Megara" title="Theognis of Megara">Theognis</a> also came from Megara. In the early 4th century BC, <a href="/wiki/Euclid_of_Megara" title="Euclid of Megara">Euclid of Megara</a> founded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megarian_school_of_philosophy" title="Megarian school of philosophy">Megarian school of philosophy</a> which flourished for about a century, and which became famous for the use of <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dialectic" title="Dialectic">dialectic</a>.</p>
<p>In 243 BC Megara expelled its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macedon" title="Macedon">Macedonian</a> garrison and joined the <a href="/wiki/Achaean_League" title="Achaean League">Achaean League</a>, but in 223 BC the Megarians left the Achaeans and joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeotian_League" title="Boeotian League">Boeotian League</a>.</p>
<p>The Megarians were proverbial for their generosity in building and endowing temples. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Jerome" title="Saint Jerome">Saint Jerome</a> reports "There is a common saying about the Megarians […:] 'They build as if they are to live forever; they live as if they are to die tomorrow.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Megara is located in the westernmost part of <a href="/wiki/Attica_(region)" title="Attica (region)">Attica</a>, near the <a href="/wiki/Megara_Gulf" title="Megara Gulf">Megara Gulf</a>, a bay of the <a href="/wiki/Saronic_Gulf" title="Saronic Gulf">Saronic Gulf</a>. The coastal plain around Megara is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Megaris" title="Megaris">Megaris</a>, which is also the name of the ancient city state centered on Megara. Megara is 8 km west of <a href="/wiki/Nea_Peramos" title="Nea Peramos">Nea Peramos</a>, 18 km west of <a href="/wiki/Eleusis" title="Eleusis">Eleusis</a>, 19 km east of <a href="/wiki/Agioi_Theodoroi" title="Agioi Theodoroi">Agioi Theodoroi</a>, 34 km west of <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> and 37 km east of <a href="/wiki/Corinth" title="Corinth">Corinth</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Motorway_8_(Greece)" title="Motorway 8 (Greece)">Motorway 8</a> connects it with Athens and Corinth. The <a href="/wiki/Megara_railway_station" title="Megara railway station">Megara railway station</a> is served by <a href="/wiki/Proastiakos" title="Proastiakos">Proastiakos</a> suburban trains to Athens and <a href="/wiki/Kiato" title="Kiato">Kiato</a>. There is a small military airfield south of the town, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICAO" title="ICAO">ICAO</a> code LGMG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main town Megara had 23,456 inhabitants at the 2011 census. The largest other settlements in the municipal unit are Vlychada (pop. 1,462), <a href="/wiki/Kineta" title="Kineta">Kineta</a> (1,446), Pachi (542) and Lakka Kalogirou (517).</p>
