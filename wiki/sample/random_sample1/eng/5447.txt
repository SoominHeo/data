<p>A <b>megalopolis</b> (sometimes called a <b>megapolis</b>; also <b>megaregion,</b> or <b>supercity</b>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is typically defined as a chain of roughly adjacent <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_area" title="Metropolitan area">metropolitan areas</a>. The term was used by <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Geddes" title="Patrick Geddes">Patrick Geddes</a> in his 1915 book <i>Cities in Evolution</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Oswald_Spengler" title="Oswald Spengler">Oswald Spengler</a> in his 1918 book <i>The Decline of the West</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Mumford" title="Lewis Mumford">Lewis Mumford</a> in his 1938 book <i>The Culture of Cities</i>, which described it as the first stage in urban overdevelopment and social decline. Later, it was used by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Gottmann" title="Jean Gottmann">Jean Gottmann</a> in his landmark 1961 study, <i>Megalopolis: The Urbanized Northeastern Seaboard of the United States</i>, to describe the chain of metropolitan areas along the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">northeastern seaboard</a> of the U.S. extending from <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, through <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a>, and ending in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Virginia" title="Northern Virginia">Northern Virginia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The latter is sometimes called the "<a href="/wiki/BosWash" title="BosWash">BosWash</a> <a href="/wiki/Northeast_megalopolis" title="Northeast megalopolis">megalopolis</a>".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalopolis_(city_type)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Megalopolis</i> is a Western deformation of the Greek word that derived from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">μέγας</span> - 'great' and <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">πόλις</span> - 'city', therefore literally a 'great city'. This term is closer in meaning to <i><a href="/wiki/Megacity" title="Megacity">megacity</a></i>. Because in Greek, πόλις is feminine, the correct term is <b>megalopolis</b>.</p>
<p>A <i>megalopolis</i>, also known as a <i>megaregion</i>, is a clustered network of cities. Gottmann defined its population as 25 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Doxiadis defined a small megalopolis a similar cluster with a population of about 10 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-megaregions_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-megaregions-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doxiodis_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doxiodis-9">[9]</a></sup> America 2050,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> a program of the <a class="external text" href="http://www.rpa.org" rel="nofollow">Regional Plan Association</a>, lists 11 megaregions in the United States and Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-megaregions_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-megaregions-7">[7]</a></sup> Literally, megalopolis in Greek means a city of exaggerated size where the prefix <i>megalo-</i> represents a quantity of exaggerated size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Megaregions_of_the_United_States" title="Megaregions of the United States">Megapolitan areas</a> were explored in a July 2005 report by Robert E. Lang and Dawn Dhavale of the Metropolitan Institute at <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Tech" title="Virginia Tech">Virginia Tech</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A later 2007 article by Lang and Nelson uses 20 megapolitan areas grouped into 10 megaregions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The concept is based on the original Megalopolis model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doxiodis_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Doxiodis-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern interlinked ground <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transportation_corridors" title="Transportation corridors">transportation corridors</a>, such as rail and highway, often aid in the development of megalopolises. Using these commuter passageways to travel throughout the megalopolis is informally called <i>megaloping</i>. This term was coined by Davide Gadren and Stefan Berteau.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
