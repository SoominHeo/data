<p>The <b>Megophryidae</b> (commonly known as the <b>litter frogs</b>) are a large <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a> native to the warm southeast of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya">Himalayan</a> foothills eastwards, south to <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Sunda_Islands" title="Greater Sunda Islands">Greater Sunda Islands</a> in <a href="/wiki/Maritime_Southeast_Asia" title="Maritime Southeast Asia">Maritime Southeast Asia</a>, and extending to the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoR_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoR-1">[1]</a></sup> As of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Megophryidae&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> it encompasses 180 species of frogs divided between 9 genera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frost_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-frost-2">[2]</a></sup> For lack of a better vernacular name, they are commonly called <b>megophryids</b>.</p>
<p>The megophryids are notable for their <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflage</a>, especially those that live in <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>, which often look like dead leaves. The camouflage is accurate to the point of some having skin folds that look like leaf veins, and at least one species, the <a href="/wiki/Long-nosed_horned_frog" title="Long-nosed horned frog">long-nosed horned frog</a> (<i>Megophrys montana</i>) has sharp projections extending past the eye and nose, which disguise the frog shape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoR_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-EoR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Megophryids range in size from 2 to 12.5 cm (0.79 to 4.92 in) in length. The adults' tongues are noticeably <a href="/wiki/Paddle" title="Paddle">paddle</a>-shaped. Their <a href="/wiki/Tadpole" title="Tadpole">tadpoles</a> can be found in a variety of waters, but especially <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">ponds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">streams</a>. The tadpoles are extremely diverse in form because of the variety of <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a> they inhabit.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genera">Genera</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megophryidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Genera">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Family <b>Megophryidae</b> includes the following nine genera:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frost_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-frost-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Borneophrys" title="Borneophrys">Borneophrys</a></i> <small>Delorme, Dubois, Grosjean &amp; Ohler, 2006</small> (rough horned frogs, formerly in <i>Megophrys</i>)</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brachytarsophrys" title="Brachytarsophrys">Brachytarsophrys</a></i> <small>Tian and Hu, 1983</small> (Karin Hills frogs)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Leptobrachella" title="Leptobrachella">Leptobrachella</a></i> <small>Smith, 1925</small> (Borneo frogs)</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leptobrachium" title="Leptobrachium">Leptobrachium</a></i> <small>Tschudi, 1838</small> (Eastern spadefoot toads)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Leptolalax" title="Leptolalax">Leptolalax</a></i> <small>Dubois, 1980</small> (Asian toads)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Megophrys" title="Megophrys">Megophrys</a></i> <small>Kuhl and Van Hasselt, 1822</small> (Asian horned toads)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Ophryophryne" title="Ophryophryne">Ophryophryne</a></i> <small>Boulenger, 1903</small> (mountain toads)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Oreolalax" title="Oreolalax">Oreolalax</a></i> <small>Myers and Leviton, 1962</small></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Scutiger_(toad)" title="Scutiger (toad)">Scutiger</a></i> <small>Theobald, 1868</small> (cat-eyed toads)</li>
</ul>
<p>Of these, <i><a href="/wiki/Borneophrys" title="Borneophrys">Borneophrys</a></i> is <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">monotypic</a>. Genera no longer recognized include <i>Atympanophrys</i> (now in <i><a href="/wiki/Megophrys" title="Megophrys">Megophrys</a></i>) and <i>Vibrissaphora</i> (now in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leptobrachium" title="Leptobrachium">Leptobrachium</a></i>). Furthermore, genus <i><a href="/wiki/Xenophrys" title="Xenophrys">Xenophrys</a></i> <small>Günther, 1864</small> has recently been merged to <i><a href="/wiki/Megophrys" title="Megophrys">Megophrys</a></i> to resolve the <a href="/wiki/Paraphyly" title="Paraphyly">paraphyly</a> of <i>Xenophrys</i>, awaiting a better solution. This makes <i>Megophrys</i> the most speciose genus within Megophryidae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frost_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-frost-2">[2]</a></sup> However, other sources continue to recognize <i>Xenophrys</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmphibiaWeb_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AmphibiaWeb-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
