<p><b>Meg Tilly</b> (born <b>Margaret Elizabeth Chan</b>; February 14, 1960) is a Canadian-American actress and novelist. For her role in the 1985 film <i><a href="/wiki/Agnes_of_God_(film)" title="Agnes of God (film)">Agnes of God</a></i>, she won a <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award" title="Golden Globe Award">Golden Globe Award</a> and was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress">Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress</a>. Her other film roles include <i><a href="/wiki/Psycho_II_(film)" title="Psycho II (film)">Psycho II</a></i> (1983), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Chill_(film)" title="The Big Chill (film)">The Big Chill</a></i> (1983), <i><a href="/wiki/Masquerade_(1988_film)" title="Masquerade (1988 film)">Masquerade</a></i> (1988), and <i><a href="/wiki/Valmont_(film)" title="Valmont (film)">Valmont</a></i> (1989). For her role in the television series <i><a href="/wiki/Bomb_Girls" title="Bomb Girls">Bomb Girls</a></i> (2012â13), she won the <a href="/wiki/1st_Canadian_Screen_Awards" title="1st Canadian Screen Awards">2013 Canadian Screen Award for Best Lead Actress in a Drama Series</a>. Tilly has also written six <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novels</a>, including <i>Porcupine</i> (2007), which was a finalist for the <a href="/wiki/Sheila_A._Egoff_Children%27s_Literature_Prize" title="Sheila A. Egoff Children's Literature Prize">Sheila A. Egoff Children's Literature Prize</a>. She is the younger sister of actress <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Tilly" title="Jennifer Tilly">Jennifer Tilly</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meg_Tilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tilly was born in <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_California" title="Long Beach, California">Long Beach</a>, California,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> the daughter of Patricia Ann (nÃ©e Tilly), a Canadian teacher, and businessman Harry Chan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCM-bio_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCM-bio-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FR-bio_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FR-bio-3">[3]</a></sup> Her father was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cantonese_American" title="Cantonese American">Cantonese American</a>, and her mother was of <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finns" title="Finns">Finnish</a> descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following her parents' divorce when she was three, Tilly was raised by her mother and stepfather, John Ward, on rural <a href="/wiki/Texada_Island" title="Texada Island">Texada Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>. She later claimed that Ward was a violent pedophile. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chiu-2006_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chiu-2006-5">[5]</a></sup> At the age of 12, Tilly started taking dance lessons, in part to avoid her stepfather,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chiu-2006_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chiu-2006-5">[5]</a></sup> and in a few years had developed into a gifted ballerina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCM-bio_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCM-bio-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After graduating from <a href="/wiki/Esquimalt_High_School" title="Esquimalt High School">Esquimalt High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_British_Columbia" title="Victoria, British Columbia">Victoria</a>, British Columbia, Tilly left home and moved to the United States to pursue a career as a professional dancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCM-bio_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCM-bio-2">[2]</a></sup> In New York City she studied with Madame Darvash and Melissa Hayden on full scholarship. She joined the Connecticut Ballet Company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCM-bio_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-TCM-bio-2">[2]</a></sup> She made her screen debut as a dancer in <a href="/wiki/Alan_Parker" title="Alan Parker">Alan Parker</a>'s 1980 musical drama <i><a href="/wiki/Fame_(1980_film)" title="Fame (1980 film)">Fame</a></i>. Tilly's dance career came to an abrupt halt when, in 1979, a dance partner dropped her, causing a serious back injury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCM-bio_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-TCM-bio-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daily-mail_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-daily-mail-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meg_Tilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acting">Acting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meg_Tilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Acting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Forced to give up dancing because of complications stemming from her back injury, Tilly moved to Los Angeles to pursue a career as an actress and studied acting under Peggy Fuery. She made her television debut in the 1981 half-hour drama <i><a href="/wiki/The_Trouble_with_Grandpa" title="The Trouble with Grandpa">The Trouble with Grandpa</a></i>, co-starring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elisha_Cook,_Jr." title="Elisha Cook, Jr.">Elisha Cook, Jr.</a>. After playing a prostitute in a second season episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Hill_Street_Blues" title="Hill Street Blues">Hill Street Blues</a></i>, she appeared in her first starring role in the 1982 coming-of-age adventure film <i><a href="/wiki/Tex_(film)" title="Tex (film)">Tex</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Matt_Dillon" title="Matt Dillon">Matt Dillon</a>. In 1983, she appeared in two of the year's most successful films: <i><a href="/wiki/Psycho_II_(film)" title="Psycho II (film)">Psycho II</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Perkins" title="Anthony Perkins">Anthony Perkins</a>, and Lawrence Kasdan's award-winning ensemble film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Chill_(film)" title="The Big Chill (film)">The Big Chill</a></i>, with <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Kline" title="Kevin Kline">Kevin Kline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Close" title="Glenn Close">Glenn Close</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Berenger" title="Tom Berenger">Tom Berenger</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Hurt" title="William Hurt">William Hurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Goldblum" title="Jeff Goldblum">Jeff Goldblum</a>, <a href="/wiki/JoBeth_Williams" title="JoBeth Williams">JoBeth Williams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mary_Kay_Place" title="Mary Kay Place">Mary Kay Place</a>. Tilly's appearance in <i>The Big Chill</i>, which was nominated for three Academy Awards, including Best Picture, helped her career significantly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCM-bio_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-TCM-bio-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1985, Tilly landed the acclaimed title role in Norman Jewison's <i><a href="/wiki/Agnes_of_God_(film)" title="Agnes of God (film)">Agnes of God</a></i>, appearing with <a href="/wiki/Jane_Fonda" title="Jane Fonda">Jane Fonda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anne_Bancroft" title="Anne Bancroft">Anne Bancroft</a>. Playing the role of a <a href="/wiki/Novitiate" title="Novitiate">novitiate</a> nun who confesses her involvement in a virgin conception, Tilly "delivered a magnificent portrayal of a tormented young woman experiencing the ultimate crisis of faith".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCM-bio_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-TCM-bio-2">[2]</a></sup> Tilly's critically praised performance earned her an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> nomination and a <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award" title="Golden Globe Award">Golden Globe Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes-review_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-review-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tilly later appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/Valmont_(film)" title="Valmont (film)">Valmont</a></i> (1989), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Two_Jakes" title="The Two Jakes">The Two Jakes</a></i> (1990) with <a href="/wiki/Jack_Nicholson" title="Jack Nicholson">Jack Nicholson</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Leaving_Normal_(film)" title="Leaving Normal (film)">Leaving Normal</a></i> (1992) with <a href="/wiki/Christine_Lahti" title="Christine Lahti">Christine Lahti</a>, as well as the 1993 horror film, <i><a href="/wiki/Body_Snatchers_(1993_film)" title="Body Snatchers (1993 film)">Body Snatchers</a></i>. Tilly was the first choice for the role of <a href="/wiki/Constanze_Mozart" title="Constanze Mozart">Constanze Mozart</a> in <a href="/wiki/Milo%C5%A1_Forman" title="MiloÅ¡ Forman">MiloÅ¡ Forman</a>'s film <i><a href="/wiki/Amadeus_(film)" title="Amadeus (film)">Amadeus</a></i>, having received glowing appraisals of her rehearsal work by both her would-be costar <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hulce" title="Tom Hulce">Tom Hulce</a> and director Forman. However, she sustained a leg injury playing soccer and had to abandon the project. The role later went to <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Berridge_(actress)" title="Elizabeth Berridge (actress)">Elizabeth Berridge</a>. After this, she stopped acting for the next 15 years.</p>
<p>Tilly returned to acting in 2010, portraying the Blessed Mother, a Pope-like figure in the <i><a href="/wiki/Caprica_(TV_series)" title="Caprica (TV series)">Caprica</a></i> episode "<a href="/wiki/List_of_Caprica_episodes" title="List of Caprica episodes">Unvanquished</a>". In 2011 she played Martha in <a href="/wiki/Edward_Albee" title="Edward Albee">Edward Albee</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Who%27s_Afraid_of_Virginia_Woolf%3F" title="Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?">Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> presented by the Blue Bridge Repertory Theatre in Victoria, B.C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2012 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Television" title="Global Television">Global Television</a> in Canada launched the six-part <i><a href="/wiki/Bomb_Girls" title="Bomb Girls">Bomb Girls</a></i> about women who work in a munitions factory during World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Tilly stars as Lorna, the emotionally closed floor matron who blossoms as a leader and an appealing woman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> She won the 2013 Lead Actress, Drama <a href="/wiki/1st_Canadian_Screen_Awards" title="1st Canadian Screen Awards">Canadian Screen Award</a> for her work on the series.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meg_Tilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tilly is the author of six published novels. In 1994, Tilly's first novel <i>Singing Songs</i> was published by Dutton to generally positive reviews. Donna Rifkind from <i>Publishers Weekly</i> called the book "an impressive first novel", and the <i>New York Times</i> Book Review praised Tilly for "the remarkable coherence and clarity" of Anna's narrative voice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes-colford_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-colford-12">[12]</a></sup> The book is about a young girl and her sisters living in the Northwest who are molested by their stepfather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-singing_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-singing-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her second novel <i>Gemma</i> was published in 2006 by the Syren Book Company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gemma_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-gemma-14">[14]</a></sup> and picked up by St. Martin's Press in 2010. The book is about a twelve-year-old girl who is kidnapped and taken on a cross-country journey in which she is physically and sexually abused by her captor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gemma_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-gemma-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her third novel <i>Porcupine</i> was published in 2007 by Tundra Books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-porcupine_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-porcupine-15">[15]</a></sup> The book is about a twelve-year-old girl, Jacqueline "Jack" Cooper, whose life is shattered by the death of <a href="/wiki/Canada%27s_role_in_the_Afghanistan_War" title="Canada's role in the Afghanistan War">her father</a> by <a href="/wiki/Friendly_fire" title="Friendly fire">friendly fire</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001âpresent)">War in Afghanistan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-porcupine_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-porcupine-15">[15]</a></sup> <i>Porcupine</i> was a finalist for the <a href="/wiki/Sheila_A._Egoff_Children%27s_Literature_Prize" title="Sheila A. Egoff Children's Literature Prize">Sheila A. Egoff Children's Literature Prize</a>, shortlisted for The Canadian Libraries Association Best Children's Book 2008, Foreword Magazine Book of the Year and was an Ontario Library Best Bets 2008.</p>
<p>Her fourth novel <i>First Time</i> was published in 2008 by Orca Book Publishers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first-time_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-first-time-16">[16]</a></sup> The novel is about a sixteen-year-old who is molested and physically abused by her mother's boyfriend, and must deal with the trauma alone without the help of her mother or best friend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first-time_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-first-time-16">[16]</a></sup> <i>First Time</i> was a 2009 Golden Eagle Award Nominee, a 2009 YALSA Quick Picks and 2010 CCBC Best Books.</p>
<p>Tilly's fifth novel <i>A Taste of Heaven</i> was published in 2013 by Puffin Books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taste_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-taste-17">[17]</a></sup> A departure from the darker themes of Tilly's previous work, the novel is about two young girls who become friends who experience the "comical, sometimes bittersweet and melodramatic trials and tribulations of tweenhood".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quill_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-quill-18">[18]</a></sup> One reviewer wrote, "Tilly paints an insightful, memorable portrait of the ups and downs of friendship and the unwavering bonds of family, delving into age-old issues of honesty, trust, and loyalty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quill_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-quill-18">[18]</a></sup> <i>A Taste Of Heaven</i> was shortlisted for the 2014 Libris Young Reader Book of the Year, a 2014 Diamond Willow Award and won the 2014/2015 Chocolate Lilly Award.</p>
<p>Her latest novel, <i>Behind The Scenes</i>, was published in 2014 by Puffin Books.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meg_Tilly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She is the younger sister of actress <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Tilly" title="Jennifer Tilly">Jennifer Tilly</a>.</p>
<p>In 1983, Tilly married her first husband, <a href="/wiki/Tim_Zinnemann" title="Tim Zinnemann">Tim Zinnemann</a>, an American film producer and son of film director <a href="/wiki/Fred_Zinnemann" title="Fred Zinnemann">Fred Zinnemann</a>. They met on the set of her first film, <i>Tex</i>. The marriage produced two children, Emily (born 1984) and David (born 1986),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NNDB_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-NNDB-19">[19]</a></sup> and ended in divorce in 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boshoff_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-boshoff-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1989 Tilly began a 5-year relationship with British actor <a href="/wiki/Colin_Firth" title="Colin Firth">Colin Firth</a>, whom she met during the filming of <i>Valmont</i>. They moved from Los Angeles to a log house on five acres of mountainside property about an hour outside Vancouver near the town of <a href="/wiki/Maple_Ridge,_British_Columbia" title="Maple Ridge, British Columbia">Maple Ridge</a>, British Columbia. Their relationship produced a son, William "Will" (born 1990),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NNDB_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-NNDB-19">[19]</a></sup> and ended in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boshoff_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-boshoff-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1995 Tilly married her second husband, <a href="/wiki/John_Calley" title="John Calley">John Calley</a>, an American film studio executive and producer 30 years her senior. They moved to Los Angeles where Calley worked as president and <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">CEO</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Entertainment" title="Sony Pictures Entertainment">Sony Pictures Entertainment</a>. The marriage ended in divorce in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boshoff_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-boshoff-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2002 Tilly married her third and current husband, author Don Calame, who writes fiction for adolescents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boshoff_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-boshoff-20">[20]</a></sup> They met during a writing seminar in <a href="/wiki/Big_Sur" title="Big Sur">Big Sur</a>, California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hampson_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-hampson-21">[21]</a></sup> The couple lives in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>. Tilly has three children.</p>
