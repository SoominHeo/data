<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Medici Chapel</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Medici Chapels</b> (<i>Cappelle medicee</i>) are two structures at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilica_of_San_Lorenzo,_Florence" title="Basilica of San Lorenzo, Florence">Basilica of San Lorenzo, Florence</a>, Italy, dating from the 16th and 17th centuries, and built as extensions to <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Brunelleschi</a>'s 15th-century church, with the purpose of celebrating the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medici_family" title="Medici family">Medici family</a>, patrons of the church and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Dukes_of_Tuscany" title="Grand Dukes of Tuscany">Grand Dukes of Tuscany</a>. The <i><b>Sagrestia Nuova</b></i>, ("New Sacristy"), was designed by <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>. The larger <i><b>Cappella dei Principi</b></i>, ("Chapel of the Princes"), though proposed in the 16th century, was not begun until the early 17th century, its design being a collaboration between the family and architects.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Sagrestia_Nuova">The <i>Sagrestia Nuova</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medici_Chapel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The Sagrestia Nuova">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tmulti tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:308px;max-width:308px">
<div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:152px;max-width:152px">

</div>
<div style="clear:left"></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <i>Sagrestia Nuova</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was intended by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_VII" title="Pope Clement VII">Cardinal Giulio de' Medici</a> and his cousin <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_X" title="Pope Leo X">Pope Leo X</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Mausoleum" title="Mausoleum">mausoleum</a> or mortuary chapel for members of the Medici family. It balances <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Brunelleschi</a>'s <i>Sagrestia Vecchia</i>, the "Old Sacristy" nestled between the left <a href="/wiki/Transept" title="Transept">transept</a> of San Lorenzo, with which it consciously competes, and shares its format of a cubical space surmounted by a dome, of gray <i>pietra serena</i> and whitewashed walls. It was the first essay in architecture (1521–24) of <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>, who also designed its monuments dedicated to certain members of the Medici family, with sculptural figures of the four times of day<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> that were destined to influence sculptural figures reclining on <a href="/wiki/Architrave" title="Architrave">architraves</a> for many generations to come. The <i>Sagrestia Nuova</i> was entered by a discreet entrance in a corner of San Lorenzo's right transept, now closed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though it was vaulted over by 1524, the ambitious projects of its sculpture and the intervention of events, such as the temporary exile of the Medici (1527), the death of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_VII" title="Pope Clement VII">Giulio, now Pope Clement VII</a> and the permanent departure of Michelangelo for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rome,_Italy" title="Rome, Italy">Rome</a> in 1534, meant that Michelangelo never finished it. Though most of the statues had been carved by the time of Michelangelo's departure, they had not been put in place, being left in disarray across the chapel, and later installed by <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Tribolo" title="Niccolò Tribolo">Niccolò Tribolo</a> in 1545.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> By order of Cosimo I, <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Giorgio Vasari</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bartolomeo_Ammannati" title="Bartolomeo Ammannati">Bartolomeo Ammannati</a> finished the work by 1555.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>There were intended to be four Medici tombs, but those of <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_de%27_Medici" title="Lorenzo de' Medici">Lorenzo the Magnificent</a> and his brother <a href="/wiki/Giuliano_de%27_Medici" title="Giuliano de' Medici">Giuliano</a> (modestly buried beneath the altar at the entrance wall) were never begun. The result is that the two magnificent existing tombs are those of comparatively insignificant Medici: <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_de%27_Medici,_Duke_of_Urbino" title="Lorenzo de' Medici, Duke of Urbino">Lorenzo di Piero, Duke of Urbino</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giuliano_de%27_Medici,_Duke_of_Nemours" title="Giuliano de' Medici, Duke of Nemours">Giuliano di Lorenzo, Duke of Nemours</a>. Their architectural components are similar; their sculptures offer contrast. On an unfinished wall, Michelangelo's <i>Madonna and Child</i> flanked by the Medici patron saints Cosmas and Damian,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> executed by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Angelo_Montorsoli" title="Giovanni Angelo Montorsoli">Giovanni Angelo Montorsoli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Raffaello_da_Montelupo" title="Raffaello da Montelupo">Raffaello da Montelupo</a> respectively, to Michelangelo's models, are set over their plain rectangular tomb. A concealed corridor with drawings on the walls by Michelangelo was discovered under the New Sacristy in 1976.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cappella_dei_Principi"><i>Cappella dei Principi</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medici_Chapel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cappella dei Principi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brogi,_Giacomo_(1822-1881)_-_n._3515_-_Firenze_-_S._Lorenzo_Cappella_dei_Principi_(1870s).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="567" data-file-width="705" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Brogi%2C_Giacomo_%281822-1881%29_-_n._3515_-_Firenze_-_S._Lorenzo_Cappella_dei_Principi_%281870s%29.jpg/220px-Brogi%2C_Giacomo_%281822-1881%29_-_n._3515_-_Firenze_-_S._Lorenzo_Cappella_dei_Principi_%281870s%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Brogi%2C_Giacomo_%281822-1881%29_-_n._3515_-_Firenze_-_S._Lorenzo_Cappella_dei_Principi_%281870s%29.jpg/330px-Brogi%2C_Giacomo_%281822-1881%29_-_n._3515_-_Firenze_-_S._Lorenzo_Cappella_dei_Principi_%281870s%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Brogi%2C_Giacomo_%281822-1881%29_-_n._3515_-_Firenze_-_S._Lorenzo_Cappella_dei_Principi_%281870s%29.jpg/440px-Brogi%2C_Giacomo_%281822-1881%29_-_n._3515_-_Firenze_-_S._Lorenzo_Cappella_dei_Principi_%281870s%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brogi,_Giacomo_(1822-1881)_-_n._3515_-_Firenze_-_S._Lorenzo_Cappella_dei_Principi_(1870s).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A 19th-century photograph of the interior of the <i>Cappella dei Principi</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The octagonal <i>Cappella dei Principi</i> surmounted by a tall dome, 59 m. high, is the distinguishing feature of San Lorenzo when seen from a distance. It is on the same axis as the nave and chancel to which it provides the equivalent of an apsidal chapel. Its entrance is from the exterior,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> in Piazza Madonna degli Aldobrandini, and through the low vaulted <a href="/wiki/Crypt" title="Crypt">crypt</a> planned by <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Buontalenti" title="Bernardo Buontalenti">Bernardo Buontalenti</a> before plans for the chapel above were made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The opulent <i>Cappella dei Principi</i>, an idea formulated by Cosimo I, was put into effect by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I_de%27_Medici" title="Ferdinand I de' Medici">Ferdinand I de' Medici</a>. It was designed by <a href="/wiki/Matteo_Nigetti" title="Matteo Nigetti">Matteo Nigetti</a>, following some sketches tendered to an informal competition of 1602 by Don Giovanni de' Medici, the natural son of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosimo_I,_Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" title="Cosimo I, Grand Duke of Tuscany">Cosimo I, Grand Duke of Tuscany</a>, which were altered in the execution by the aged Buontalenti.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A true expression of court art, it was the result of collaboration among designers and patrons.</p>
<p>For the execution of its astonishing revetment of marbles inlaid with colored marbles and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-precious_stone" title="Semi-precious stone">semi-precious stone</a>, the Grand Ducal hardstone workshop, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opificio_delle_Pietre_Dure" title="Opificio delle Pietre Dure">Opificio delle Pietre Dure</a> was established. The art of <i>commessi</i>, as it was called in Florence, assembled jig-sawn fragments of specimen stones to form the designs of the revetment that entirely cover the walls. The result was disapproved of by 18th and 19th century visitors, but has come to be appreciated for an example of the taste of its time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Six grand <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagus" title="Sarcophagus">sarcophagi</a> are empty; the Medici remains are interred in the crypt below. In sixteen compartments of the dado are coats-of-arms of Tuscan cities under Medici control. In the niches that were intended to hold portrait sculptures of Medici, two (Ferdinando I and Cosimo II) were executed by <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Tacca" title="Pietro Tacca">Pietro Tacca</a> (1626–42).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Lantern">The Lantern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medici_Chapel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Lantern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lantern at the top of the Medici Chapel is made out of marble and has an “….unusual <a href="/wiki/Polyhedron" title="Polyhedron">polyhedron</a> mounted on the peak of the conical roof,”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-13">[13]</a></sup> The orb that is on top of the lantern has seventy-two facets and is about two feet in diameter. The orb and cross, that is on top of the orb, are traditional symbols of the Roman and Christian power, and recalls the similar orbs on central dome plan churches like St. Maria del Fiore and <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter's Basilica">St. Peter’s</a>. But because it is on a private mausoleum, the Medici family is promoting their own personal power with the orb and cross, <a href="/wiki/Laurel_wreath" title="Laurel wreath">laurel wreath</a> and lion heads, which are all symbols of status and power.</p>
<p>The lantern that holds up the orb helps to accentuate the height and size of the chapel, which is fairly small. The lantern is a bit less than seven meters tall and, “…is equal to the height of the dome it surmounts,”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-13">[13]</a></sup> The lantern metaphorically expresses the themes of death and resurrection. The lantern is where the soul could escape and go from “…death to the afterlife.”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
