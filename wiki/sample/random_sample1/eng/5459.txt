<p><b>Meroë</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɛr/ 'err' in 'merry'">ɛr</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span>; also spelled <i>Meroe</i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Meroitic_language" title="Meroitic language">Meroitic</a>: <i>Medewi</i> or <i>Bedewi</i>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">مرواه</span> <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Arabic transliteration"><i>Meruwah</i></span> and <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">مروى</span> <i>Meruwi</i>; <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Μερόη</span>, <i>Meróē, Biblical; tribe of Merari</i>) is an ancient city on the east bank of the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> about 6 km north-east of the Kabushiya station near <a href="/wiki/Shendi" title="Shendi">Shendi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, approximately 200 km north-east of <a href="/wiki/Khartoum" title="Khartoum">Khartoum</a>. Near the site are a group of villages called <b>Bagrawiyah</b>. This city was the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kush" title="Kingdom of Kush">Kingdom of Kush</a> for several centuries. The Kushitic Kingdom of Meroë gave its name to the <i>Island of Meroë</i>, which was the modern region of <a href="/wiki/Butana" title="Butana">Butana</a>, a region bounded by the Nile (from the <a href="/wiki/Atbarah_River" title="Atbarah River">Atbarah River</a> to <a href="/wiki/Khartoum" title="Khartoum">Khartoum</a>), the Atbarah and the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Nile" title="Blue Nile">Blue Nile</a>.</p>
<p>The city of Meroë was on the edge of <a href="/wiki/Butana" title="Butana">Butana</a> and there were two other Meroitic cities in <a href="/wiki/Butana" title="Butana">Butana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Musawwarat_es-Sufra" title="Musawwarat es-Sufra">Musawwarat es-Sufra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Naqa" title="Naqa">Naqa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The first of these sites was given the name Meroë by the Persian king, <a href="/wiki/Cambyses_II" title="Cambyses II">Cambyses</a>, in honor of his sister who was called by that name. The city had originally borne the ancient appellation <i>Saba</i>, named after the country's original founder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The presence of numerous Meroitic sites within the western Butana region and on the border of Butana proper is significant to the settlement of the core of the developed region. The orientation of these settlements exhibit the exercise of state power over subsistence production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jstor183595_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-jstor183595-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Kingdom of Kush which housed the city of Meroë represents one of a series of early states located within the middle Nile. It is one of the earliest and most impressive states found south of the Sahara. Looking at the specificity of the surrounding early states within the middle Nile, ones understanding of Meroë in combination with the historical developments of other historic states may be enhanced through looking at the development of power relation characteristics within other Nile Valley states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jstor183595_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-jstor183595-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The site of the city of Meroë is marked by more than two hundred <a href="/wiki/Pyramid" title="Pyramid">pyramids</a> in three groups, of which many are in ruins. They have distinctive size and proportions of <a href="/wiki/Nubian_pyramids" title="Nubian pyramids">Nubian pyramids</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mero%C3%AB&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amanitore_bust.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="728" data-file-width="704" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Amanitore_bust.jpg/170px-Amanitore_bust.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Amanitore_bust.jpg/255px-Amanitore_bust.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Amanitore_bust.jpg/340px-Amanitore_bust.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amanitore_bust.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Relief of a ruler, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Candace_of_Mero%C3%AB" title="Candace of Meroë">Candace of Meroë</a> named <a href="/wiki/Amanitore" title="Amanitore">Kandake Amanitore</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Meroë was the south capital of the <a href="/wiki/Napata" title="Napata">Napata</a>/Meroitic Kingdom, that spanned the period c. 800 BCE – c. 350 CE. According to partially deciphered Meroitic texts, the name of the city was <b>Medewi</b> or <b>Bedewi</b> (Török, 1998). Excavations revealed evidence of important, high ranking Kushite burials, from the Napatan Period (c. 800 – c. 280 BCE) in the vicinity of the settlement called the Western cemetery. The culture of Meroë developed from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twenty-fifth_Dynasty" title="Twenty-fifth Dynasty">Twenty-fifth Dynasty</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>, which originated in <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kush" title="Kingdom of Kush">Kush</a>. The importance of the town gradually increased from <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kush" title="Kingdom of Kush">the beginning of the Meroitic Period</a>, especially from the reign of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Arrakkamani&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Arrakkamani (page does not exist)">Arrakkamani</a> (c. 280 BCE) when the royal burial ground was transferred to Meroë from <a href="/wiki/Napata" title="Napata">Napata</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gebel_Barkal" title="Gebel Barkal">Gebel Barkal</a>). In the fifth century BCE, Greek historian <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> described it as "a great city...said to be the mother city of the other Ethiopians."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The city of Meroë was located along the middle Nile which is of much importance due to the annual flooding of the Nile river valley and the connection to many major river systems such as the Niger which aided with the production of pottery and iron characteristic to the Meroitic kingdom that allowed for the rise in power of its people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jstor183595_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-jstor183595-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NE_200bc.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Near East in 200 BCE, showing the Kingdom of Meroe and its neighbours.</div>

</div>
<p>Rome's conquest of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egypt</a> led to border skirmishes and incursions by Meroë beyond the Roman borders. In 23 BCE the Roman governor of Egypt, <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Petronius" title="Gaius Petronius">Publius Petronius</a>, to end the Meroitic raids, invaded Nubia in response to a Nubian attack on southern Egypt, pillaging the north of the region and sacking Napata (22 BCE) before returning home. In retaliation, the Nubians crossed the lower border of Egypt and looted many statues (among other things) from the Egyptian towns near the first cataract of the Nile at Aswan. Roman forces later reclaimed many of the statues intact, and others were returned following the peace treaty signed in 22 BCE between Rome and Meroë. One looted head though, from a statue of the emperor <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>, was buried under the steps of a temple. It is now kept in the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The next recorded contact between Rome and Meroë was in the autumn of 61 CE. The Emperor <a href="/wiki/Nero" title="Nero">Nero</a> sent a party of Praetorian soldiers under the command of a tribune and two centurions into this country, who reached the city of Meroë where they were given an escort, then proceeded up the <a href="/wiki/White_Nile" title="White Nile">White Nile</a> until they encountered the swamps of the <a href="/wiki/Sudd" title="Sudd">Sudd</a>. This marked the limit of Roman penetration into Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The period following Petronius' punitive expedition is marked by abundant trade finds at sites in Meroë. L.P. Kirwan provides a short list of finds from archeological sites in that country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> However, the kingdom of Meroë began to fade as a power by the 1st or 2nd century CE, sapped by the war with Roman Egypt and the decline of its traditional industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meroë is mentioned succinctly in the 1st century CE <a href="/wiki/Periplus_of_the_Erythraean_Sea" title="Periplus of the Erythraean Sea">Periplus of the Erythraean Sea</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>"2. On the right-hand coast next below <a href="/wiki/Berenice" title="Berenice">Berenice</a> is the country of the Berbers. Along the shore are the Fish-Eaters, living in scattered caves in the narrow valleys. Farther inland are the Berbers, and beyond them the Wild-flesh-Eaters and Calf-Eaters, each tribe governed by its chief; and behind them, farther inland, in the country towards the west, there lies a city called Meroe."</i></p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><i><cite>— <a href="/wiki/Periplus_of_the_Erythraean_Sea" title="Periplus of the Erythraean Sea">Periplus of the Erythraean Sea</a>, Chap.2</cite></i></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The last period of the city is marked by the victory stele of an unnamed ruler of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aksum" title="Kingdom of Aksum">Aksum</a> (almost certainly <a href="/wiki/Ezana_of_Axum" title="Ezana of Axum">Ezana</a>) erected at the site of Meroë; from his description, in <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>, that he was "King of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aksumite" title="Aksumite">Aksumites</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himyarite" title="Himyarite">Omerites</a>," (i.e. of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aksum" title="Aksum">Aksum</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himyar" title="Himyar">Himyar</a>) it is likely this king ruled sometime around 330.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mero.C3.AB_in_Hebrew_legend">Meroë in Hebrew legend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mero%C3%AB&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Meroë in Hebrew legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hebrew oral tradition avers that <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a>, in his younger years, had led an Egyptian military expedition into Sudan (Kush), as far as the city of Meroë, which was then called Saba. The city was built near the confluence of two great rivers and was encircled by a formidable wall, and governed by a renegade king. To ensure the safety of his men who traversed that desert country, Moses had invented a stratagem whereby the Egyptian army would carry along with them baskets of sedge, each containing an ibis, only to be released when they approached the enemy's country. The purpose of the birds was to kill the deadly serpents that lay all about that country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Having successfully laid siege to the city, the city was eventually subdued by betrayal of the king's daughter, who had agreed to deliver the city unto Moses on condition that he would consummate a marriage with her, under the solemn assurance of an oath.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civilization">Civilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mero%C3%AB&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Civilization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Meroitic.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2419" data-file-width="1932" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Meroitic.png/220px-Meroitic.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Meroitic.png/330px-Meroitic.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Meroitic.png/440px-Meroitic.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Meroitic.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Meroitic script</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Meroë was the base of a flourishing kingdom whose wealth was centered around a strong <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> industry, as well as international trade involving <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> So much metalworking went on in Meroë, through the working of <a href="/wiki/Bloomery" title="Bloomery">bloomeries</a> and possibly <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnaces</a>, that it has even been called "the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birmingham,_England" title="Birmingham, England">Birmingham</a> of Africa" because of its vast production and trade of iron to the rest of Africa and other international trade partners.</p>
<p>The centralized control of production within the Meroitic empire and distribution of certain crafts and manufactures may have been politically important with their iron industry and pottery crafts gaining the most significant attention. The Meroitic settlements were oriented in a savannah orientation with the varying of permanent and less permanent agricultural settlements can be attributed to the exploitation of rainlands and savannah-oriented forms of subsistence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jstor183595_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-jstor183595-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the time, iron was one of the most important metals worldwide, and Meroitic metalworkers were among the best in the world. Meroë also exported <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Textiles" title="Textiles">textiles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewelry" title="Jewelry">jewelry</a>. Their textiles were based on <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a> and working on this product reached its highest achievement in Nubia around 400 BCE. Furthermore, <a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">Nubia</a> was very rich in <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>. It is possible that the Egyptian word for <a href="/wiki/Gold_(hieroglyph)" title="Gold (hieroglyph)">gold</a>, <i>nub</i>, was the source of name of <a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">Nubia</a>. Trade in "exotic" animals from farther south in Africa was another feature of their economy.</p>
<p>Apart from the iron trade, pottery was a widespread and prominent industry in the Meroe kingdom. The production of fine and elaborated decorated wares was a strong tradition within the middle nile. Such productions carried considerable social significance and are believed to be involved in mortuary rites. The long history of goods imported into the Meroitic empire and their subsequent distribution provides insight into the social and political workings of the Meroitic state. The major determinant of production was attributed to the availability of labor rather than the political power associated with land. Power was associated with control of people rather than control of territory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jstor183595_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-jstor183595-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Egyptian import, the water-moving wheel, the <a href="/wiki/Sakia" title="Sakia">sakia</a>, was used to move water, in conjunction with irrigation, to increase crop production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>At its peak, the rulers of Meroë controlled the Nile Valley north to south, over a straight-line distance of more than 1,000 km (620 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The King of Meroë was an autocratic ruler who shared his authority only with the Queen Mother, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Candace" title="Candace">Candace</a>. However, the role of the Queen Mother remains obscure. The administration consisted of <a href="/wiki/Treasury" title="Treasury">treasurers</a>, seal bearers, heads of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archives" title="Archives">archives</a> and chief <a href="/wiki/Scribe" title="Scribe">scribes</a>, among others.</p>
<p>Although the people of Meroë also had southern deities such as <a href="/wiki/Apedemak" title="Apedemak">Apedemak</a>, the lion-son of <a href="/wiki/Sekhmet" title="Sekhmet">Sekhmet</a> (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bast_(mythology)" title="Bast (mythology)">Bast</a>, depending upon the region), they also continued worshipping ancient Egyptian gods that they had brought with them. Among these deities were <a href="/wiki/Amun" title="Amun">Amun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tefnut" title="Tefnut">Tefnut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horus" title="Horus">Horus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thoth" title="Thoth">Thoth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Satet" title="Satet">Satis</a>, though to a lesser extent.</p>
<p>The collapse of their external trade with other Nile Valley states may be considered as one of the prime causes of the decline of royal power and disintegration of the Meroitic state in the 3rd and 4th centuries CE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jstor183595_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-jstor183595-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mero%C3%AB&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Meroitic_language" title="Meroitic language">Meroitic language</a> was spoken in Meroë and the Sudan during the Meroitic period (attested from 300 BCE). It became extinct about 400 CE. The language was written in two forms of the <a href="/wiki/Meroitic_alphabet" title="Meroitic alphabet">Meroitic alphabet</a>: Meroitic Cursive, which was written with a <a href="/wiki/Stylus" title="Stylus">stylus</a> and was used for general record-keeping; and Meroitic Hieroglyphic, which was carved in stone or used for royal or religious documents. It is not well understood due to the scarcity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bilingual" title="Bilingual">bilingual</a> texts. The earliest inscription in Meroitic writing dates from between 180-170 BC. These hieroglyphics were found engraved on the temple of Queen <a href="/wiki/Shanakdakhete" title="Shanakdakhete">Shanakdakhete</a>. Meroitic Cursive is written horizontally, and reads from right to left like all Semitic orthographies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howf_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Howf-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the 3rd century BCE, a new indigenous <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabet</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Meroitic_alphabet" title="Meroitic alphabet">Meroitic</a>, consisting of twenty-three letters, replaced Egyptian script. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meroitic_script" title="Meroitic script">Meroitic script</a> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alphabetic" title="Alphabetic">alphabetic</a> script originally derived from Egyptian hieroglyphs, used to write the <a href="/wiki/Meroitic_language" title="Meroitic language">Meroitic language</a> of the Kingdom of Meroë/<a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kush" title="Kingdom of Kush">Kush</a>. It was developed in the Napatan Period (about 700 - 300 BCE), and first appears in the 2nd century BCE. For a time, it was also possibly used to write the <a href="/wiki/Old_Nubian_language" title="Old Nubian language">Nubian language</a> of the successor Nubian kingdoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is uncertain to which language family the Meroitic language is related. Claude Rilly has proposed that it, like the <a href="/wiki/Nobiin_language" title="Nobiin language">Nobiin language</a>, belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Sudanic_languages" title="Eastern Sudanic languages">Eastern Sudanic</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Nilo-Saharan_languages" title="Nilo-Saharan languages">Nilo-Saharan</a> family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Kirsty Rowan suggests that Meroitic, like the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_language" title="Egyptian language">Egyptian language</a>, instead belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Afroasiatic_languages" title="Afroasiatic languages">Afro-Asiatic</a> family. She bases this on its sound inventory and <a href="/wiki/Phonotactics" title="Phonotactics">phonotactics</a>, which are similar to those of the Afro-Asiatic languages and dissimilar from those of the Nilo-Saharan languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeology">Archaeology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mero%C3%AB&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Archaeology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For a more comprehensive list, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Pyramids_of_Meroe" title="List of Pyramids of Meroe">List of Pyramids of Meroe</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Meroe_pyramid_field_north.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="1913" height="235" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Meroe_pyramid_field_north.png/300px-Meroe_pyramid_field_north.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Meroe_pyramid_field_north.png/450px-Meroe_pyramid_field_north.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Meroe_pyramid_field_north.png/600px-Meroe_pyramid_field_north.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Meroe_pyramid_field_north.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Plan of the North pyramid field at Meroë.</div>

<p>The site of Meroë was brought to the knowledge of Europeans in 1821 by the French mineralogist <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Cailliaud" title="Frédéric Cailliaud">Frédéric Cailliaud</a> (1787–1869), who published an illustrated in-folio describing the ruins. His work included the first publication of the southernmost known Latin inscription.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Some treasure-hunting excavations were executed on a small scale in 1834 by <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Ferlini" title="Giuseppe Ferlini">Giuseppe Ferlini</a>, who discovered (or professed to discover) various antiquities, chiefly in the form of jewelry, now in the museums of <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>.</p>
<p>The ruins were examined more carefully in 1844 by <a href="/wiki/Karl_Richard_Lepsius" title="Karl Richard Lepsius">Karl Richard Lepsius</a>, who took many plans, sketches and copies, besides actual antiquities, to Berlin.</p>
<p>Further excavations were carried on by <a href="/wiki/E._A._Wallis_Budge" title="E. A. Wallis Budge">E. A. Wallis Budge</a> in the years 1902 and 1905, the results of which are recorded in his work, <i>The Egyptian Sudan: its History and Monuments</i> (London, 1907). Troops furnished by Sir <a href="/wiki/Reginald_Wingate" title="Reginald Wingate">Reginald Wingate</a>, governor of Sudan, made paths to and between the pyramids and sank shafts.</p>
<p>It was found that the pyramids were commonly built over sepulchral chambers, containing the remains of bodies, either burned, or buried without being mummified. The most interesting objects found were the reliefs on the chapel walls, already described by Lepsius, which present the names and representations of their queens, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Candace" title="Candace">Candaces</a>, or the Nubian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kentakes" title="Kentakes">Kentakes</a>, some kings and some chapters of the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_the_Dead" title="Book of the Dead">Book of the Dead</a>; some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stela" title="Stela">stelae</a> with inscriptions in the <a href="/wiki/Meroitic_language" title="Meroitic language">Meroitic language</a>; and some vessels of metal and earthenware. The best of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reliefs" title="Reliefs">reliefs</a> were taken down stone by stone in 1905, and set up partly in the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>, and partly in the museum at <a href="/wiki/Khartoum" title="Khartoum">Khartoum</a>.</p>
<p>In 1910, in consequence of a report by <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Sayce" title="Archibald Sayce">Archibald Sayce</a>, excavations were commenced in the mounds of the town, and in the necropolis, by <a href="/wiki/John_Garstang" title="John Garstang">John Garstang</a>, on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Liverpool" title="University of Liverpool">University of Liverpool</a>. Garstang discovered the ruins of a palace and several temples built by the Meroite rulers.</p>
