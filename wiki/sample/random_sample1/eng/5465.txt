<p><b>Mary Lou Retton Kelley</b> (born January 24, 1968) is a retired American <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics" title="Gymnastics">gymnast</a>. At the <a href="/wiki/1984_Summer_Olympics" title="1984 Summer Olympics">1984 Summer Olympics</a> in Los Angeles, she won a gold medal in the individual all-around competition, as well as two silver medals and two bronze medals. Her performance made her one of the most popular athletes in the United States.</p>
<p>Retton was the first ever American woman to win the all-around gold medal at the Olympics and was the only one to do so for twenty years. She is credited with being a pioneering figure in American women's gymnastics, including by the four American women who have since equaled her feat consecutively: <a href="/wiki/Carly_Patterson" title="Carly Patterson">Carly Patterson</a> (Athens in 2004), <a href="/wiki/Nastia_Liukin" title="Nastia Liukin">Nastia Liukin</a> (2008 in Beijing), <a href="/wiki/Gabby_Douglas" title="Gabby Douglas">Gabby Douglas</a> (2012 in London), and <a href="/wiki/Simone_Biles" title="Simone Biles">Simone Biles</a> (2016 in Rio de Janeiro). Twice during that span, Americans also took silver as part of a one-two finish: <a href="/wiki/Shawn_Johnson" title="Shawn Johnson">Shawn Johnson</a> in 2008 and <a href="/wiki/Aly_Raisman" title="Aly Raisman">Aly Raisman</a> in 2016. Furthermore, prior to Retton's Olympic triumph, no American woman had won all-around gold at the World Championships; since then, seven Americans have done so a combined ten times - in chronological order, <a href="/wiki/Kim_Zmeskal" title="Kim Zmeskal">Kim Zmeskal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shannon_Miller" title="Shannon Miller">Shannon Miller</a> (twice), <a href="/wiki/Chellsie_Memmel" title="Chellsie Memmel">Chellsie Memmel</a>, Shawn Johnson, <a href="/wiki/Bridget_Sloan" title="Bridget Sloan">Bridget Sloan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jordyn_Wieber" title="Jordyn Wieber">Jordyn Wieber</a> and Biles (three times).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary_Lou_Retton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mary Lou Retton was born in <a href="/wiki/Fairmont,_West_Virginia" title="Fairmont, West Virginia">Fairmont, West Virginia</a>, of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_American" title="Italian American">Italian</a> heritage (her family's original surname was "Rotunda").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Her father, Ronnie, operated a <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>-industry transportation equipment business. She attended <a href="/wiki/Fairmont_Senior_High_School" title="Fairmont Senior High School">Fairmont Senior High School</a>, but did not graduate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> She competed in the <a href="/wiki/1984_Summer_Olympics" title="1984 Summer Olympics">1984 Olympic games</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a> during her sophomore year of high school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> She is a devout <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baptist" title="Baptist">Baptist</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Retton lived in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston, Texas</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HouChron_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-HouChron-6">[6]</a></sup> until 2009, when her family returned to West Virginia and again moved back to Houston in 2012. She is married to former University of Texas quarterback and Houston <a href="/wiki/Real_estate_development" title="Real estate development">real estate developer</a> <a href="/wiki/Shannon_Kelley" title="Shannon Kelley">Shannon Kelley</a>, who now works for the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Baptist_University" title="Houston Baptist University">Houston Baptist University</a> athletic department.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Together they have four daughters: Shayla Rae Kelley (born 1995), <a href="/wiki/McKenna_Kelley" title="McKenna Kelley">McKenna Lane Kelley</a> (born 1997), Skyla Brae Kelley (born 2000), and Emma Jean Kelley (born 2002).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gymnastics_career">Gymnastics career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary_Lou_Retton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gymnastics career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Inspired by watching <a href="/wiki/Nadia_Com%C4%83neci" title="Nadia Comăneci">Nadia Comăneci</a> outshine Olympic champion <a href="/wiki/Olga_Korbut" title="Olga Korbut">Olga Korbut</a> on television at the <a href="/wiki/1976_Summer_Olympics" title="1976 Summer Olympics">1976 Summer Olympics</a> in Montreal, Retton took up gymnastics in her hometown of Fairmont. She was coached by Gary Rafaloski. She then decided to move to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston, Texas</a>, to train under Romanians <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_K%C3%A1rolyi" title="Béla Károlyi">Béla</a> and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1rta_K%C3%A1rolyi" title="Márta Károlyi">Márta Károlyi</a>, who had coached Nadia Comăneci before their defection to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Under the Károlyis, Retton soon began to make a name for herself in the U.S., winning the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Cup_(Gymnastics)" title="American Cup (Gymnastics)">American Cup</a> in 1983 and placing second to Dianne Durham (another Károlyi student) at the <a href="/wiki/USA_Gymnastics_National_Championships" title="USA Gymnastics National Championships">US Nationals</a> that same year. Retton missed the World Championships in 1983 due to a wrist injury. Nevertheless, Retton won the American Classic in 1983 and 1984, as well as Japan's Chunichi Cup in 1983.</p>
<p>After winning her second American Cup, the U.S. Nationals, and the U.S. Olympic Trials in 1984, Retton suffered a knee injury when she was performing a floor routine at a local gymnastics center. She had sat down to sign autographs when she felt her knee lock, forcing her to undergo an operation five weeks prior to the <a href="/wiki/1984_Summer_Olympics" title="1984 Summer Olympics">1984 Summer Olympics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> She recovered just in time for the Olympics in Los Angeles, California. In the competition, which was boycotted by the Soviet bloc nations except for <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, Retton was engaged in a close battle with <a href="/wiki/Ecaterina_Szabo" title="Ecaterina Szabo">Ecaterina Szabo</a> of Romania for the all-around gold medal. Trailing Szabo (after uneven bars and balance beam) by 0.15 with two events to go, Retton scored perfect 10s on floor exercise and vault — the last event in an especially dramatic fashion, as there had been fears that her knee injury and the subsequent surgery might impair her performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ESPN70_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ESPN70-10">[10]</a></sup> Retton won the all-around gold medal by 0.05 points, beating Szabo and becoming the first American to receive the all-around gold medal. She also became the first female gymnast from outside <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a> to win the individual all-around gold.</p>
<p>At the same Olympics, Retton won four additional medals: silver in the team competition and the <a href="/wiki/Vault_(gymnastics)" title="Vault (gymnastics)">horse vault</a>, and bronze in the <a href="/wiki/Floor_(gymnastics)" title="Floor (gymnastics)">floor exercise</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uneven_bars" title="Uneven bars">uneven bars</a>. For her performance, she was named <i><a href="/wiki/Sports_Illustrated" title="Sports Illustrated">Sports Illustrated</a></i> magazine's "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sportsman_of_the_Year" title="Sportsman of the Year">Sportswoman of the Year</a>". She appeared on a <a href="/wiki/Wheaties" title="Wheaties">Wheaties</a> box, and became the cereal's first official spokeswoman.</p>
<p>In 1985 Retton won the American Cup all around competition for the third and final time. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> She retired in 1986.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-gymnastics_career">Post-gymnastics career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary_Lou_Retton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Post-gymnastics career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>An ardent <a href="/wiki/Christian_right" title="Christian right">Christian conservative</a>, she was an outspoken supporter of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reagan_Administration" title="Reagan Administration">Reagan Administration</a> in the United States. She appeared in a variety of televised ads supporting <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>. Retton delivered the <a href="/wiki/Pledge_of_Allegiance" title="Pledge of Allegiance">Pledge of Allegiance</a> with fellow former gymnast and 1996 Olympic gold medalist <a href="/wiki/Kerri_Strug" title="Kerri Strug">Kerri Strug</a> on the second night of the <a href="/wiki/2004_Republican_National_Convention" title="2004 Republican National Convention">2004 Republican National Convention</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pledge_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-pledge-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Retton's hometown of <a href="/wiki/Fairmont,_West_Virginia" title="Fairmont, West Virginia">Fairmont, West Virginia</a>, named a road and park in the town after her. She retired from gymnastics after winning an unprecedented third American Cup title in 1985. She later had cameo appearances as herself in <i><a href="/wiki/Scrooged" title="Scrooged">Scrooged</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Naked_Gun_33%E2%85%93:_The_Final_Insult" title="Naked Gun 33⅓: The Final Insult">Naked Gun 33⅓: The Final Insult</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 1990s, Retton worked as a spokeswoman for the U.S. drugstore chain <a href="/wiki/Revco" title="Revco">Revco</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Retton was elected to the National Italian American Sports Hall of Fame in 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1993, the <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a> released results of a sports study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> in which Retton was statistically tied for first place with fellow <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympian</a> <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Hamill" title="Dorothy Hamill">Dorothy Hamill</a> as the most popular athlete in America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1997, Retton was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/International_Gymnastics_Hall_of_Fame" title="International Gymnastics Hall of Fame">International Gymnastics Hall of Fame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hof_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-hof-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Retton has many commercial endorsements, including bowling and shampoo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> She was the first female athlete to be pictured on the front of a <a href="/wiki/Wheaties" title="Wheaties">Wheaties</a> box, and General Mills stated that Wheaties sales improved after her appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> She is a frequent analyst for televised gymnastics and attended <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="The University of Texas at Austin">The University of Texas at Austin</a> after the Olympics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li>In 1985, Retton hosted <i><a href="/wiki/ABC_Funfit" title="ABC Funfit">ABC Funfit</a></i>, a series of five-minute segments on physical fitness which were broadcast between Saturday morning cartoons. A record album and diagram booklet was also made of the segments and sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 1993, Retton appeared in the <i><a href="/wiki/Baywatch" title="Baywatch">Baywatch</a></i> episode "The Child Inside".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2002, Retton starred in the television program <i><a href="/wiki/Mary_Lou%27s_Flip_Flop_Shop" title="Mary Lou's Flip Flop Shop">Mary Lou's Flip Flop Shop</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>

<li>She appeared in the <a href="/wiki/RadioShack" title="RadioShack">RadioShack</a> "Phone Call" commercial, which aired during the 2014 Super Bowl.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_conditions">Medical conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary_Lou_Retton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Medical conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She was born with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hip_dysplasia_(human)" title="Hip dysplasia (human)">hip dysplasia</a>, a condition aggravated by her years as a competitive gymnast. After experiencing increased pain she had <a href="/wiki/Hip_replacement" title="Hip replacement">hip replacement</a> surgery on her left hip in her mid-30s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hipreplacement-biomet_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-hipreplacement-biomet-27">[27]</a></sup> In October 2008, she visited the Biomet facility in Warsaw, Indiana, and met the machinists who produced her hip implant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hipreplacement-biomet_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-hipreplacement-biomet-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bladder-pfizer_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-bladder-pfizer-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gymnastics_legacy">Gymnastics legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary_Lou_Retton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Gymnastics legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Retton has an uneven bars move called "The Retton Flip", a transition (front flip) from low- to high-bar, resulting in the gymnast perched or "sitting" on top of the high bar. This move and many others like it are no longer in the <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Points_(artistic_gymnastics)" title="Code of Points (artistic gymnastics)">Code of Points</a> due to old-style "belly beat" moves no longer being used in bars competition.</p>
