<p><b>Mary-Louise Parker</b> (born August 2, 1964) is an American actress and author. In addition to her many films, Parker enjoyed great popularity for her lead role on <a href="/wiki/Showtime_(TV_network)" title="Showtime (TV network)">Showtime</a>'s television series <i><a href="/wiki/Weeds_(TV_series)" title="Weeds (TV series)">Weeds</a></i> portraying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nancy_Botwin" title="Nancy Botwin">Nancy Botwin</a>, for which she received the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Actress_%E2%80%93_Television_Series_Musical_or_Comedy" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Actress – Television Series Musical or Comedy">Golden Globe Award for Best Actress</a> in 2006. Parker has appeared in films and series including <i><a href="/wiki/Red_(2010_film)" title="Red (2010 film)">RED</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RED_2_(film)" title="RED 2 (film)">RED 2</a>,</i> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fried_Green_Tomatoes_(film)" title="Fried Green Tomatoes (film)">Fried Green Tomatoes</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Boys_on_the_Side" title="Boys on the Side">Boys on the Side</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_West_Wing" title="The West Wing">The West Wing</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Angels_in_America_(miniseries)" title="Angels in America (miniseries)">Angels in America</a></i>, for which she received a <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actress_%E2%80%93_Series,_Miniseries_or_Television_Film" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Supporting Actress – Series, Miniseries or Television Film">Golden Globe</a> and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Supporting_Actress_in_a_Miniseries_or_a_Movie" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Supporting Actress in a Miniseries or a Movie">Emmy Award</a> for Best Supporting Actress. Parker is also the recipient of the <a href="/wiki/55th_Tony_Awards" title="55th Tony Awards">2001 Tony Award</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tony_Award_for_Best_Performance_by_a_Leading_Actress_in_a_Play" title="Tony Award for Best Performance by a Leading Actress in a Play">Best Actress</a> for her performance in the Broadway play <i><a href="/wiki/Proof_(play)" title="Proof (play)">Proof</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary-Louise_Parker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Parker was born in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Jackson_(South_Carolina)" title="Fort Jackson (South Carolina)">Fort Jackson</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>. The youngest of four children,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moremag_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-moremag-1">[1]</a></sup> she is the daughter of Caroline Louise (née Morell) and John Morgan Parker, a judge who served in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Her ancestry includes Swedish (from her maternal grandfather), English, Scottish, Scots-Irish, German, and Dutch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Because of her father's career, Parker spent parts of her childhood in <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, Germany, and France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> She described her childhood as "profoundly unhappy", further noting that, "My parents did everything they could; I had books, clothes, a home and a warm bed, but I was never happy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moremag_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-moremag-1">[1]</a></sup> She graduated from <a href="/wiki/Marcos_de_Niza_High_School" title="Marcos de Niza High School">Marcos de Niza High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tempe,_Arizona" title="Tempe, Arizona">Tempe, Arizona</a>. Parker majored in drama at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Carolina_School_of_the_Arts" title="North Carolina School of the Arts">North Carolina School of the Arts</a> and graduated in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moremag_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-moremag-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acting_career">Acting career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary-Louise_Parker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Acting career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary-Louise_Parker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Parker got her start in acting with a role on the soap opera <i><a href="/wiki/Ryan%27s_Hope" title="Ryan's Hope">Ryan's Hope</a></i>. In the late 1980s, Parker moved to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>. After a few minor roles, she made her Broadway debut in a 1990 production of <a href="/wiki/Craig_Lucas" title="Craig Lucas">Craig Lucas</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Prelude_to_a_Kiss_(play)" title="Prelude to a Kiss (play)">Prelude to a Kiss</a></i>, playing the lead role of Rita. She moved with the production when it transferred from its origin Off-Broadway. Parker won the Clarence Derwent Award for her performance and was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Tony_Award" title="Tony Award">Tony Award</a> (although she did not play the role when the film was made).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary-Louise_Parker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Parker starred with <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Kline" title="Kevin Kline">Kevin Kline</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon_(1991_film)" title="Grand Canyon (1991 film)">Grand Canyon</a></i> (1991); with <a href="/wiki/Kathy_Bates" title="Kathy Bates">Kathy Bates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Stuart_Masterson" title="Mary Stuart Masterson">Mary Stuart Masterson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jessica_Tandy" title="Jessica Tandy">Jessica Tandy</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Fried_Green_Tomatoes" title="Fried Green Tomatoes">Fried Green Tomatoes</a></i> (1991); with <a href="/wiki/Susan_Sarandon" title="Susan Sarandon">Susan Sarandon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Lee_Jones" title="Tommy Lee Jones">Tommy Lee Jones</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Client_(1994_film)" title="The Client (1994 film)">The Client</a></i> (1994); with <a href="/wiki/John_Cusack" title="John Cusack">John Cusack</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Bullets_over_Broadway" title="Bullets over Broadway">Bullets over Broadway</a></i> (1994); and with <a href="/wiki/Drew_Barrymore" title="Drew Barrymore">Drew Barrymore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whoopi_Goldberg" title="Whoopi Goldberg">Whoopi Goldberg</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Boys_on_the_Side" title="Boys on the Side">Boys on the Side</a></i> (1995), as a woman with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a>. Parker's next role was in a movie adaptation of another Craig Lucas play, <i><a href="/wiki/Reckless_(1995_film)" title="Reckless (1995 film)">Reckless</a></i> (1995), alongside <a href="/wiki/Mia_Farrow" title="Mia Farrow">Mia Farrow</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/Jane_Campion" title="Jane Campion">Jane Campion</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Portrait_of_a_Lady_(film)" title="The Portrait of a Lady (film)">The Portrait of a Lady</a></i> (1996), which also starred <a href="/wiki/Nicole_Kidman" title="Nicole Kidman">Nicole Kidman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viggo_Mortensen" title="Viggo Mortensen">Viggo Mortensen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christian_Bale" title="Christian Bale">Christian Bale</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Malkovich" title="John Malkovich">John Malkovich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Hershey" title="Barbara Hershey">Barbara Hershey</a>. In addition, she appeared alongside <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Modine" title="Matthew Modine">Matthew Modine</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tim_Hunter_(director)" title="Tim Hunter (director)">Tim Hunter</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Maker_(film)" title="The Maker (film)">The Maker</a></i> (1997).</p>
<p>Parker's theater career continued when she appeared in <a href="/wiki/Paula_Vogel" title="Paula Vogel">Paula Vogel</a>'s 1997 critical smash <i><a href="/wiki/How_I_Learned_to_Drive" title="How I Learned to Drive">How I Learned to Drive</a></i>, with <a href="/wiki/David_Morse_(actor)" title="David Morse (actor)">David Morse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In the late 1990s, she appeared in several independent films, including <i><a href="/wiki/Let_the_Devil_Wear_Black" title="Let the Devil Wear Black">Let the Devil Wear Black</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Five_Senses_(film)" title="The Five Senses (film)">The Five Senses</a></i>. She starred alongside Sidney Poitier in the 1999 movie <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simple_Life_of_Noah_Dearborn" title="The Simple Life of Noah Dearborn">The Simple Life of Noah Dearborn</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2001.E2.80.932003">2001–2003</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary-Louise_Parker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2001–2003">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On December 7, 2003, <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> aired a six-and-a-half-hour adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Tony_Kushner" title="Tony Kushner">Tony Kushner</a>'s acclaimed Broadway play <i><a href="/wiki/Angels_in_America_(miniseries)" title="Angels in America (miniseries)">Angels in America</a></i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Mike_Nichols" title="Mike Nichols">Mike Nichols</a>. Parker played Harper Pitt, the Mormon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valium" title="Valium">Valium</a>-addicted wife of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_closet" title="The closet">closeted</a> lawyer. For her performance, Parker received Golden Globe and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emmy_awards" title="Emmy awards">Emmy awards</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> for Best Supporting Actress in a Miniseries.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2004.E2.80.932006">2004–2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary-Louise_Parker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2004–2006">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2004, Parker appeared in the comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Saved!" title="Saved!">Saved!</a></i>, and a television film called <i><a href="/wiki/Miracle_Run" title="Miracle Run">Miracle Run</a></i>, based on the true story of a mother of two sons with <a href="/wiki/Autism" title="Autism">autism</a>, as well as appearing in Craig Lucas' <i>Reckless</i> on Broadway. Parker took the lead role that had been Mia Farrow's on screen. The production, directed by Mark Brokaw, earned Parker another nomination for a Tony Award for Best Actress in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2005, Parker was the subject of a career exhibition at <a href="/wiki/Boston_University" title="Boston University">Boston University</a>, where memorabilia from her career were donated to the University's library. Parker received the 2006 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Performance_by_an_Actress_in_a_Television_Series_-_Musical_or_Comedy" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Performance by an Actress in a Television Series - Musical or Comedy">Golden Globe Award for Best Performance by an Actress in a Television Series - Musical or Comedy</a>, given by the <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Foreign_Press_Association" title="Hollywood Foreign Press Association">Hollywood Foreign Press Association</a>, for her lead role in <i>Weeds</i>. In that category, she defeated the four leads of <i><a href="/wiki/Desperate_Housewives" title="Desperate Housewives">Desperate Housewives</a></i>. She dedicated the award to the late <a href="/wiki/John_Spencer_(actor)" title="John Spencer (actor)">John Spencer</a>, known for his work as <a href="/wiki/Leo_McGarry" title="Leo McGarry">Leo McGarry</a> on <i>The West Wing</i>. After receiving the award, Parker stated: "I'm really in favor of legalizing marijuana. I don't think it's that controversial."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2007_onwards">2007 onwards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary-Louise_Parker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2007 onwards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In March 2007, Parker played the lead role in the television film <i>The Robber Bride</i>. She then portrayed <a href="/wiki/Zerelda_Mimms" title="Zerelda Mimms">Zerelda Mimms</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Dominik" title="Andrew Dominik">Andrew Dominik</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Assassination_of_Jesse_James_by_the_Coward_Robert_Ford" title="The Assassination of Jesse James by the Coward Robert Ford">The Assassination of Jesse James by the Coward Robert Ford</a></i>, which opened in cinemas in September 2007. Parker appeared alongside <a href="/wiki/Brad_Pitt" title="Brad Pitt">Brad Pitt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Casey_Affleck" title="Casey Affleck">Casey Affleck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sam_Rockwell" title="Sam Rockwell">Sam Rockwell</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Garret_Dillahunt" title="Garret Dillahunt">Garret Dillahunt</a>. In August 2007, Parker continued her role in the third season of <i>Weeds</i>.</p>
<p>In August 2007, she posed nude for an ad for the third season of <i>Weeds</i>. In the ad, she appears as <a href="/wiki/Eve" title="Eve">Eve</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Garden_of_Eden" title="Garden of Eden">Garden of Eden</a>, with a snake draped around her body and a cannabis leaf behind her ear.</p>
<p>Parker appeared in 2008's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spiderwick_Chronicles_(film)" title="The Spiderwick Chronicles (film)">The Spiderwick Chronicles</a></i> and in <a href="/wiki/Off-Broadway" title="Off-Broadway">off-Broadway</a>'s Playwrights Horizons production in the New York premiere of <i><a href="/wiki/Dead_Man%27s_Cell_Phone" title="Dead Man's Cell Phone">Dead Man's Cell Phone</a></i>, a new play by <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Ruhl" title="Sarah Ruhl">Sarah Ruhl</a>, alongside <a href="/wiki/Drama_Desk_Award" title="Drama Desk Award">Drama Desk Award</a> Winner <a href="/wiki/Kathleen_Chalfant" title="Kathleen Chalfant">Kathleen Chalfant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>She filmed the Donna Vermeer film <i>Les Passages</i> alongside <a href="/wiki/Julie_Delpy" title="Julie Delpy">Julie Delpy</a>. Following this, she returned to work on the fifth season of <i>Weeds</i>. Parker took the lead role in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roundabout_Theatre" title="Roundabout Theatre">Roundabout Theatre</a> Broadway revival of the play <i><a href="/wiki/Hedda_Gabler" title="Hedda Gabler">Hedda Gabler</a></i>, running from January through March 29, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The play garnered a series of negative reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Parker starred opposite <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Willis" title="Bruce Willis">Bruce Willis</a> in the film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RED_(film)" title="RED (film)">RED</a></i>, an adaptation of the comic book mini-series <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_(WildStorm_comics)" title="Red (WildStorm comics)">of the same name</a>. The film was released on October 15, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-red_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-red-15">[15]</a></sup> In 2011, Parker became the host for the tenth season of the <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">PBS</a> documentary series <i><a href="/wiki/Independent_Lens" title="Independent Lens">Independent Lens</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 2013 she played roles in both <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RED_2_(film)" title="RED 2 (film)">RED 2</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/R.I.P.D." title="R.I.P.D.">R.I.P.D.</a></i> She appeared in the Broadway <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Theatre_Club" title="Manhattan Theatre Club">Manhattan Theatre Club</a> production of the play <i>The Snow Geese</i> by <a href="/wiki/Sharr_White" title="Sharr White">Sharr White</a> at the Samuel J. Friedman Theatre from October 24 through December 15, 2013. The play was directed by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_J._Sullivan" title="Daniel J. Sullivan">Daniel J. Sullivan</a> and also starred <a href="/wiki/Danny_Burstein" title="Danny Burstein">Danny Burstein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Clark" title="Victoria Clark">Victoria Clark</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>She starred in the new play by <a href="/wiki/Simon_Stephens" title="Simon Stephens">Simon Stephens</a>, <i>Heisenberg</i>, produced Off-Broadway by the Manhattan Theatre Club. The play, directed by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Brokaw" title="Mark Brokaw">Mark Brokaw</a>, opened on June 2, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The play extended its run, closing on July 11, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The play transferred to Broadway at the <a href="/wiki/Samuel_J._Friedman_Theatre" title="Samuel J. Friedman Theatre">Samuel J. Friedman Theatre</a>, with previews starting on September 20, 2016, officially opening on October 13, with Parker and Denis Arndt reprising their roles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_career">Writing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary-Louise_Parker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Writing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 2007, Mary-Louise Parker has contributed articles for <a href="/wiki/Esquire_(magazine)" title="Esquire (magazine)"><i>Esquire</i> magazine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In November 2015, Scribner Books, an imprint of <a href="/wiki/Simon_%26_Schuster" title="Simon &amp; Schuster">Simon &amp; Schuster</a>, published Parker's memoir in letters titled <i>Dear Mr. You.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary-Louise_Parker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From 1997 to November 2003, Parker dated actor <a href="/wiki/Billy_Crudup" title="Billy Crudup">Billy Crudup</a>, who left her for <a href="/wiki/Claire_Danes" title="Claire Danes">Claire Danes</a> while she was seven months pregnant with their son, William Atticus Parker, born in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> William's godmother is actress <a href="/wiki/Susan_Sarandon" title="Susan Sarandon">Susan Sarandon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moremag_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-moremag-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2006, Parker began dating actor <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Dean_Morgan" title="Jeffrey Dean Morgan">Jeffrey Dean Morgan</a>, whom she met on the set of <i>Weeds</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Relationship_1_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Relationship_1-25">[25]</a></sup> On February 12, 2008, Parker and Morgan announced their engagement,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-engaged_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-engaged-26">[26]</a></sup> only to break up the following April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2007, Parker adopted a baby girl, Caroline Aberash Parker, from <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013 Parker was honored for her work with Hope North, an organization that works in the educating and healing of young victims in Uganda's civil war. The actress began her involvement with the organization after meeting a former victim of Uganda's civil war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
