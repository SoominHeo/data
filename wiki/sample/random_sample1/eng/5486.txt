<p><b>Messier 15</b> or <b>M15</b> (also designated <b>NGC 7078</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">globular cluster</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Pegasus_(constellation)" title="Pegasus (constellation)">Pegasus</a>. It was discovered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean-Dominique_Maraldi" title="Jean-Dominique Maraldi">Jean-Dominique Maraldi</a> in 1746 and included in <a href="/wiki/Charles_Messier" title="Charles Messier">Charles Messier</a>'s catalogue of <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a>-like objects in 1764. At an estimated 12.0 billion years old, it is one of the oldest known globular clusters.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messier_15&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>M15 is about 33,600 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, and 175 light years in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astropix_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-astropix-9">[9]</a></sup> It has an <a href="/wiki/Absolute_magnitude" title="Absolute magnitude">absolute magnitude</a> of -9.2, which translates to a total <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> of 360,000 times that of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. Messier 15 is one of the most densely packed globulars known in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milky_Way_galaxy" title="Milky Way galaxy">Milky Way galaxy</a>. Its core has undergone a contraction known as '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core_collapse_(cluster)" title="Core collapse (cluster)">core collapse</a>' and it has a central density cusp with an enormous number of stars surrounding what may be a central black hole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gerssen2002_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gerssen2002-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Home to over 100,000 stars,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astropix_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-astropix-9">[9]</a></sup> the cluster is notable for containing a large number of <a href="/wiki/Variable_star" title="Variable star">variable stars</a> (112) and <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsars</a> (8), including one double <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">neutron star</a> system, M15 C. M15 also contains <a href="/wiki/Pease_1" title="Pease 1">Pease 1</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nebula" title="Planetary nebula">planetary nebula</a> discovered within a globular cluster<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gillettetal1989_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gillettetal1989-11">[11]</a></sup> in 1928. Just three others have been found in globular clusters since then.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amateur_astronomy">Amateur astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messier_15&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Amateur astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At magnitude 6.2, M15 approaches <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">naked eye</a> visibility under good conditions and can be observed with binoculars or a small telescope, appearing as a fuzzy star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astropix_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-astropix-9">[9]</a></sup> Telescopes with a larger aperture (at least 6 in./150 mm diameter) will start to reveal individual stars, the brightest of which are of <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">magnitude</a> +12.6. The cluster appears 18 arc minutes in size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astropix_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-astropix-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="X-ray_sources">X-ray sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messier_15&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: X-ray sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Earth-orbiting&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Earth-orbiting (page does not exist)">Earth-orbiting</a> <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> <a href="/wiki/Uhuru_(satellite)" title="Uhuru (satellite)">Uhuru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chandra_X-ray_Observatory" title="Chandra X-ray Observatory">Chandra X-ray Observatory</a> have detected two bright X-ray sources in this cluster: Messier 15 X-1 (4U 2129+12) and Messier 15 X-2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forman_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forman-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-White-14">[14]</a></sup> The former appears to be the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_X-ray_source" title="Astronomical X-ray source">astronomical X-ray source</a> detected in Pegasus.</p>
