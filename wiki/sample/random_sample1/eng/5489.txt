<p><b>Messier 65</b> (also known as <b>NGC 3623</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Intermediate_spiral_galaxy" title="Intermediate spiral galaxy">intermediate spiral galaxy</a> about 35 million <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> away in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Leo_(constellation)" title="Leo (constellation)">Leo</a>. It was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Messier" title="Charles Messier">Charles Messier</a> in 1780. Along with <a href="/wiki/Messier_66" title="Messier 66">M66</a> and <a href="/wiki/NGC_3628" title="NGC 3628">NGC 3628</a>, M65 forms the <a href="/wiki/Leo_Triplet" title="Leo Triplet">Leo Triplet</a>, a small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_of_galaxies" title="Group of galaxies">group of galaxies</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messier_65&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>M65 was discovered by Charles Messier and included in his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Messier_Objects" title="Messier Objects">Messier Objects</a> list. However, <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Smyth" title="William Henry Smyth">William Henry Smyth</a> accidentally attributed the discovery to <a href="/wiki/Pierre_M%C3%A9chain" title="Pierre Méchain">Pierre Méchain</a> in his popular 19th century astronomical work <i>A Cycle of Celestial Objects</i> (stating "They [M65 and M66] were pointed out by Méchain to Messier in 1780"). This error was in turn picked up by Kenneth Glyn Jones in <i>Messier's Nebulae and Star Clusters</i>. This has since ramified into a number of other books by a variety of authors.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Star_formation">Star formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messier_65&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Star formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The galaxy is low in dust and gas, and there is little <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">star formation</a> in it, although there has been some relatively recently in the arms. The ratio of old stars to new stars is correspondingly quite high. In most wavelengths it is quite uninteresting, though there is a radio source visible in the <a href="/wiki/NRAO_VLA_Sky_Survey" title="NRAO VLA Sky Survey">NVSS</a>, offset from the core by about two arc-minutes. The identity of the source is uncertain, as it has not been identified visually, or formally studied in any published papers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interaction_with_other_galaxies">Interaction with other galaxies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messier_65&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Interaction with other galaxies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To the eye, M65's disk appears slightly warped, and its relatively recent burst of star formation is also suggestive of some external disturbance. Rots (1978) suggests that the two other galaxies in the Leo Triplet interacted with each other about 800 million years ago. Recent research by Zhiyu Duan suggests that M65 may also have interacted, though much less strongly. He also notes that M65 may have a central bar—it is difficult to tell because the galaxy is seen from an oblique angle—a feature which is suggestive of tidal disruption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
