<p><b>Messier 94</b> (also known as <b>NGC 4736</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy" title="Spiral galaxy">spiral galaxy</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Canes_Venatici" title="Canes Venatici">Canes Venatici</a>. It was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_M%C3%A9chain" title="Pierre Méchain">Pierre Méchain</a> in 1781,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSOG_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSOG-6">[6]</a></sup> and catalogued by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Messier" title="Charles Messier">Charles Messier</a> two days later. Although some references describe M94 as a <a href="/wiki/Barred_spiral_galaxy" title="Barred spiral galaxy">barred spiral galaxy</a>, the "bar" structure appears to be more oval-shaped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kormendykennicutt04_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-kormendykennicutt04-7">[7]</a></sup> The galaxy has two ring structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ned_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-ned-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nucleus">Nucleus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messier_94&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nucleus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>M94 is classified as having a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_ionization_nuclear_emission_region" title="Low ionization nuclear emission region">low ionization nuclear emission region</a> (LINER) nucleus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoetal1997_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-hoetal1997-8">[8]</a></sup> LINERs in general are characterized by optical <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">spectra</a> that reveal that <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ionized</a> gas is present but the gas is only weakly ionized (i.e. the atoms are missing relatively few electrons).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inner_and_outer_rings">Inner and outer rings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messier_94&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Inner and outer rings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Starburst_galaxy_Messier_94.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1253" data-file-width="1280" height="215" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Starburst_galaxy_Messier_94.jpg/220px-Starburst_galaxy_Messier_94.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Starburst_galaxy_Messier_94.jpg/330px-Starburst_galaxy_Messier_94.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Starburst_galaxy_Messier_94.jpg/440px-Starburst_galaxy_Messier_94.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Starburst_galaxy_Messier_94.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Detail of the central area of M94.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>M94 contains both an inner ring with a diameter of 70 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">arcseconds</a> and an outer ring with a diameter of 600 arcseconds. These rings appear to form at <a href="/wiki/Resonance" title="Resonance">resonance</a> locations within the disk of the galaxy. The inner ring is the site of strong <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">star formation</a> activity and is sometimes referred to as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Starburst_(astronomy)" title="Starburst (astronomy)">starburst</a> ring. This star formation is fueled by gas that is dynamically driven into the ring by the inner oval-shaped bar-like structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-munoztunonetal04_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-munoztunonetal04-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2009 study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-panchromatic_m94_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-panchromatic_m94-10">[10]</a></sup> conducted by an international team of astrophysicists revealed that the outer ring of M94 is not a closed stellar ring, as historically attributed in the literature, but a complex structure of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiral_arm#Spiral_arms" title="Spiral arm">spiral arms</a> when viewed in mid-IR and UV. The study found that the outer disk of this galaxy is active. It contains approximately 23% of the galaxy's total stellar mass and contributes about 10% of the galaxy's new stars. In fact, the star formation rate of the outer disk is approximately two times greater than the inner disk because it is more efficient per unit of stellar mass.</p>
<p>There are several possible external events that could have led to the origin of M94's outer disk including the accretion of a satellite galaxy or the gravitational interaction with a nearby star system. However, further research found problems with each of these scenarios. Therefore, the report concludes that the inner disk of M94 is an oval distortion which led to the creation of this galaxy's peripheral disk.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudobulge">Pseudobulge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messier_94&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pseudobulge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a paper published in 2004, <a href="/wiki/John_Kormendy" title="John Kormendy">John Kormendy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Kennicutt" title="Robert Kennicutt">Robert Kennicutt</a> argued that M94 contains a prototypical <a href="/wiki/Bulge_(astronomy)" title="Bulge (astronomy)">pseudobulge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kormendykennicutt04_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-kormendykennicutt04-7">[7]</a></sup> A classical <a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy" title="Spiral galaxy">spiral galaxy</a> consists of a disk of gas and young stars that intersects a large sphere (or bulge) of older stars. In contrast, a galaxy with a pseudobulge does not have a large bulge of old stars but instead contain a bright central structure with intense star formation that looks like a bulge when the galaxy is viewed face-on. In the case of M94, this pseudobulge takes the form of a ring around a central oval-shaped region.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distance_measurements">Distance measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messier_94&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distance measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At least two techniques have been used to measure distances to M94. The <a href="/wiki/Surface_brightness_fluctuation" title="Surface brightness fluctuation">surface brightness fluctuations</a> distance measurement technique estimates distances to spiral galaxies based on the graininess of the appearance of their bulges. The distance measured to M94 using this technique is 17.0 ± 1.4 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">Mly</a> (5.2 ± 0.4 <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">Mpc</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tonryetal2001_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-tonryetal2001-1">[1]</a></sup> However, M94 is close enough that the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> can be used to resolve and measure the fluxes of the brightest individual stars within the galaxy. These measured fluxes can then be compared to the measured fluxes of similar stars within the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> to measure the distance. The estimated distance to M94 using this technique is 15 ± 2 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">Mly</a> (4.7 ± 0.6 <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">Mpc</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-karachentsevetal2003_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-karachentsevetal2003-2">[2]</a></sup> Averaged together, these distance measurements give a distance estimate of 16.0 ± 1.3 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">Mly</a> (4.9 ± 0.4 <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">Mpc</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dark_matter">Dark matter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messier_94&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dark matter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2008 a study was published<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bratek_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-bratek-11">[11]</a></sup> showing that M94 had very little or no <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a> present. The study analyzed the rotation curves of the galaxy's stars and the density of hydrogen gas and found that ordinary luminous matter appeared to account for all of the galaxy's mass. This result was unusual and somewhat controversial, as current models do not indicate how a galaxy could form without a dark matter halo or how a galaxy could lose its dark matter. Other explanations for galactic rotation curves, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MOND" title="MOND">MOND</a>, also have difficulty explaining this galaxy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This result has yet to be confirmed or accepted by other research groups, however, and has not actually been tested against the predictions of standard galaxy formation models.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Galaxy_group_information">Galaxy group information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messier_94&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Galaxy group information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>M94 is one of the brightest galaxies within the <a href="/wiki/M94_Group" title="M94 Group">M94 Group</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_of_galaxies" title="Group of galaxies">group of galaxies</a> that contains between 16 and 24 galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbg_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbg-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garcia1993_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-garcia1993-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-giuricinetal2002_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-giuricinetal2002-15">[15]</a></sup> This group is one of many that lies within the <a href="/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster" title="Virgo Supercluster">Virgo Supercluster</a> (i.e. the Local Supercluster).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tully1982_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-tully1982-16">[16]</a></sup> Although a large number of galaxies may be associated with M94, only a few galaxies near M94 appear to form a gravitationally bound system. Most of the other nearby galaxies appear to be moving with the expansion of the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-karachentsevetal2003_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-karachentsevetal2003-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-karachentsev2005_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-karachentsev2005-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
