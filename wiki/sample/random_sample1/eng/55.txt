<p><b>Makemake</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minor-planet_designation" title="Minor-planet designation">minor-planet designation</a> <b>136472 Makemake</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">dwarf planet</a> and perhaps the largest <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">Kuiper belt</a> object (KBO) in the <a href="/wiki/Classical_Kuiper_belt_object" title="Classical Kuiper belt object">classical population</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-B-2">[a]</a></sup> with a diameter approximately two thirds that of <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lim2010_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lim2010-23">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU0806_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-IAU0806-24">[20]</a></sup> Makemake has one known satellite, <a href="/wiki/S/2015_(136472)_1" title="S/2015 (136472) 1">S/2015 (136472) 1</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HubbleSite_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-HubbleSite-25">[21]</a></sup> Makemake’s extremely low average temperature, about 30 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> (−243.2 °C), means its surface is covered with <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a>, and possibly <a href="/wiki/Solid_nitrogen" title="Solid nitrogen">nitrogen ices</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-brown-20">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Makemake was discovered on March 31, 2005, by a team led by <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Brown" title="Michael E. Brown">Michael E. Brown</a>, and announced on July 29, 2005. Initially, it was known as <b><span class="nowrap">2005 FY<sub>9</sub></span></b> and later given the minor-planet number 136472. Makemake was recognized as a dwarf planet by the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> (IAU) in July 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU0806_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-IAU0806-24">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-26">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-name-27">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tancredi_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tancredi-28">[24]</a></sup> Its name derives from <a href="/wiki/Makemake_(deity)" title="Makemake (deity)">Makemake</a> in the mythology of the <a href="/wiki/Rapa_Nui_people" title="Rapa Nui people">Rapa Nui people</a> of <a href="/wiki/Easter_Island" title="Easter Island">Easter Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU0806_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-IAU0806-24">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Makemake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Makemake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Makemake was discovered on March 31, 2005, by a team at the <a href="/wiki/Palomar_Observatory" title="Palomar Observatory">Palomar Observatory</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Brown" title="Michael E. Brown">Michael E. Brown</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-8">[6]</a></sup> and was announced to the public on July 29, 2005. The team had planned to delay announcing their discoveries of the bright objects Makemake and <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">Eris</a> until further observations and calculations were complete, but announced them both on July 29 when the discovery of another large object they had been tracking, <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">Haumea</a>, was <a href="/wiki/Controversy_over_the_discovery_of_Haumea" title="Controversy over the discovery of Haumea">controversially announced</a> on July 27 by a different team in Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite its relative brightness (it is about a fifth as bright as Pluto),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[e]</a></sup> Makemake was not discovered until well after many much fainter Kuiper belt objects. Most searches for minor planets are conducted relatively close to the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a> (the region of the sky that the Sun, Moon and planets appear to lie in, as seen from Earth), due to the greater likelihood of finding objects there. It probably escaped detection during the earlier surveys due to its relatively high <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">orbital inclination</a>, and the fact that it was at its farthest distance from the ecliptic at the time of its discovery, in the northern <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Coma_Berenices" title="Coma Berenices">Coma Berenices</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horizons_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Horizons-18">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Besides <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a>, Makemake is the only other dwarf planet that was bright enough for <a href="/wiki/Clyde_Tombaugh" title="Clyde Tombaugh">Clyde Tombaugh</a> to have possibly detected during his search for <a href="/wiki/Planets_beyond_Neptune" title="Planets beyond Neptune">trans-Neptunian planets</a> around 1930.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2006-discovery_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2006-discovery-32">[27]</a></sup> At the time of Tombaugh's survey, Makemake was only a few degrees from the ecliptic, near the border of <a href="/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)" title="Taurus (constellation)">Taurus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Auriga_(constellation)" title="Auriga (constellation)">Auriga</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[f]</a></sup> at an apparent magnitude of 16.0.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horizons_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Horizons-18">[14]</a></sup> This position, however, was also very near the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>, and Makemake would have been almost impossible to find against the dense background of stars. Tombaugh continued searching for some years after the discovery of Pluto,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[28]</a></sup> but he did not find Makemake or any other <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object" title="Trans-Neptunian object">trans-Neptunian objects</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Makemake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Provisional_designation" title="Provisional designation">provisional designation</a> <span class="nowrap">2005 FY<sub>9</sub></span> was given to Makemake when the discovery was made public. Before that, the discovery team used the codename "<a href="/wiki/Easter_Bunny" title="Easter Bunny">Easterbunny</a>" for the object, because of its discovery shortly after <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brownblog_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-brownblog-3">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2008, in accordance with IAU rules for classical Kuiper belt objects, <span class="nowrap">2005 FY<sub>9</sub></span> was given the name of a <a href="/wiki/Creator_deity" title="Creator deity">creator deity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slashdot.2C_July_13.2C_2008_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Slashdot.2C_July_13.2C_2008-35">[29]</a></sup> The name of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Makemake_(mythology)" title="Makemake (mythology)">Makemake</a>, the creator of humanity and god of fertility in the mythos of the <a href="/wiki/Rapa_Nui_people" title="Rapa Nui people">Rapa Nui</a>, the native people of <a href="/wiki/Easter_Island" title="Easter Island">Easter Island</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-name-27">[23]</a></sup> was chosen in part to preserve the object's connection with Easter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brownblog_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-brownblog-3">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>As of December 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Makemake&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Makemake is 52.4 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> (7.84<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>9</sup> km) from the Sun,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AstDys_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-AstDys-17">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horizons_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-Horizons-18">[14]</a></sup> almost as far from the Sun as it ever reaches on its orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-brown-20">[16]</a></sup> Makemake follows an orbit very similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">Haumea</a>: highly <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">inclined</a> at 29° and a moderate <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">eccentricity</a> of about 0.16.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tegler_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tegler-36">[30]</a></sup> Nevertheless, Makemake's orbit is slightly farther from the Sun in terms of both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">semi-major axis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">perihelion</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">orbital period</a> is nearly 310 years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buie_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Buie-19">[15]</a></sup> more than Pluto's 248 years and Haumea's 283 years. Both Makemake and Haumea are currently far from the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a>—the angular distance is almost 29°. Makemake is approaching its 2033 <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">aphelion</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horizons_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-Horizons-18">[14]</a></sup> whereas Haumea passed its aphelion in early 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Makemake is a <a href="/wiki/Classical_Kuiper_belt_object" title="Classical Kuiper belt object">classical Kuiper belt object</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEC2009-P26_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-MPEC2009-P26-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-B-2">[a]</a></sup> which means its orbit lies far enough from Neptune to remain stable over the age of the Solar System.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jewitt_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-jewitt-38">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luu2002_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Luu2002-39">[33]</a></sup> Unlike <a href="/wiki/Plutino" title="Plutino">plutinos</a>, which can cross Neptune's orbit due to their 2:3 <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">resonance</a> with the planet, the classical objects have <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">perihelia</a> further from the Sun, free from Neptune's <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">perturbation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jewitt_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-jewitt-38">[32]</a></sup> Such objects have relatively low eccentricities (<i>e</i> below 0.2) and orbit the Sun in much the same way the planets do. Makemake, however, is a member of the "dynamically hot" class of classical KBOs, meaning that it has a high inclination compared to others in its population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levison2003_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levison2003-40">[34]</a></sup> Makemake is, probably coincidentally, <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance#Coincidental_.27near.27_ratios_of_mean_motion" title="Orbital resonance">near</a> the 11:6 resonance with Neptune.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[35]</a></sup></p>

<p>Makemake is currently visually the second-brightest <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">Kuiper belt</a> object after <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2006-discovery_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2006-discovery-32">[27]</a></sup> having a March <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opposition_(astronomy_and_astrology)" title="Opposition (astronomy and astrology)">opposition</a> <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> of 17.0<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AstDys_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-AstDys-17">[13]</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Coma_Berenices" title="Coma Berenices">Coma Berenices</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horizons_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-Horizons-18">[14]</a></sup> This is bright enough to be visible using a high-end <a href="/wiki/Amateur_astronomy" title="Amateur astronomy">amateur</a> <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a>.</p>
<p>Combining the detection in infrared by the <i><a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">Spitzer Space Telescope</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herschel_Space_Telescope" title="Herschel Space Telescope">Herschel Space Telescope</a></i> with the similarities of spectrum with Pluto yielded an estimated diameter from 1,360 to 1,480 km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lim2010_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lim2010-23">[19]</a></sup> From the 2011 stellar occultation by Makemake, its dimensions have been initially measured to be <span class="nowrap">(1502 ± 45) × (1430 ± 9) km</span>. However, this analysis of the occultation data was later reanalyzed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2013_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2013-9">[7]</a></sup> which led to the dimension estimate of <span class="nowrap">(<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003143400000000000♠</span>1434<span style="margin-left:0.3em;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:85%;text-align:right">+48<br/>
−18</span></span></span>) × (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003142000000000000♠</span>1420<span style="margin-left:0.3em;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:85%;text-align:right">+18<br/>
−24</span></span></span> km)</span> without a pole-orientation constraint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2013_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2013-9">[7]</a></sup> This means that Makemake is slightly larger than that of <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">Haumea</a>, making it likely the fourth-largest known trans-Neptunian object after Pluto, <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">Eris</a>, and <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/(225088)_2007_OR10" title="(225088) 2007 OR10">2007 OR<sub>10</sub></a></span>, though the error bars with the latter overlap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tegler_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tegler-36">[30]</a></sup> Makemake was the fourth <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">dwarf planet</a> recognized, because it has a bright <a href="/wiki/Photometric_system" title="Photometric system">V-band</a> <a href="/wiki/Absolute_magnitude" title="Absolute magnitude">absolute magnitude</a> of −0.44.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-8">[6]</a></sup> Makemake has a high geometrical albedo of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6999810000000000000♠</span>0.81<span style="margin-left:0.3em;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:85%;text-align:right">+0.01<br/>
−0.02</span></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2013_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2013-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rotation period of Makemake is estimated at 7.77 hours. Its lightcurve amplitude is small, only 0.03 <i>mag</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2013_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2013-9">[7]</a></sup> This was thought to be due to Makemake currently being viewed pole on from Earth; however, S/2015 (136472) 1's orbital plane (which is probably orbiting with little inclination relative to Makemake's equator due to tides resulting from its rapid rotation) is edge-on from Earth, implying that Makemake is really being viewed equator-on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planetary.org_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-planetary.org-42">[36]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spectra_and_surface">Spectra and surface</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Makemake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Spectra and surface">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like Pluto, Makemake appears red in the visible spectrum, and significantly redder than the surface of Eris (see <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object#Colors" title="Trans-Neptunian object">colour comparison of TNOs</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atmosphere_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-atmosphere-43">[37]</a></sup> The near-infrared spectrum is marked by the presence of the broad <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>) <a href="/wiki/Absorption_band" title="Absorption band">absorption bands</a>. Methane is observed also on Pluto and Eris, but its spectral signature is much weaker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atmosphere_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-atmosphere-43">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spectral analysis of Makemake's surface revealed that methane must be present in the form of large grains at least one centimetre in size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-brown-20">[16]</a></sup> In addition to methane, large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tholins" title="Tholins">tholins</a> as well as smaller amounts of <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acetylene" title="Acetylene">acetylene</a> and high-mass <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a> (like <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>) may be present, most likely created by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photolysis" title="Photolysis">photolysis</a> of methane by solar radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-brown-20">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown2015_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-brown2015-44">[38]</a></sup> The tholins are probably responsible for the red color of the visible spectrum. Although evidence exists for the presence of <a href="/wiki/Solid_nitrogen" title="Solid nitrogen">nitrogen ice</a> on its surface, at least mixed with other ices, there is nowhere near the same level of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> as on Pluto and <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">Triton</a>, where it composes more than 98 percent of the crust. The relative lack of nitrogen ice suggests that its supply of nitrogen has somehow been depleted over the age of the Solar System.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown_20-5"><a href="#cite_note-brown-20">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tegler2008_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tegler2008-45">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Owen1993_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Owen1993-46">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Far-infrared" title="Far-infrared">far-infrared</a> (24–70 μm) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Submillimeter" title="Submillimeter">submillimeter</a> (70–500 μm) photometry performed by Spitzer and Herschel telescopes revealed that the surface of Makemake is not homogeneous. Although the majority of it is covered by nitrogen and methane ices, where the albedo ranges from 78 to 90%, there are small patches of dark terrain whose albedo is only 2 to 12%, and that make up 3–7% of the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lim2010_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lim2010-23">[19]</a></sup> These studies were made before S/2015 (136472) 1 was discovered; thus, these small dark patches may actually have been the dark surface of the satellite rather than any actual surface features on Makemake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planetary.org_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-planetary.org-42">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmosphere">Atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Makemake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Makemake was expected to have an <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a> but with a lower surface pressure. However, on 23 April 2011 Makemake <a href="/wiki/Occultation#Occultations_by_asteroids" title="Occultation">passed in front</a> of an 18th-magnitude star and abruptly blocked its light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[41]</a></sup> The results showed that Makemake presently lacks a substantial atmosphere and placed an upper limit of 4–12 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanobar" title="Nanobar">nanobar</a> on the pressure at its surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ortiz2012_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ortiz2012-10">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The presence of methane and possibly nitrogen suggests that Makemake could have a transient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body_atmosphere" title="Celestial body atmosphere">atmosphere</a> similar to that of Pluto near its <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">perihelion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atmosphere_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-atmosphere-43">[37]</a></sup> Nitrogen, if present, will be the dominant component of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown_20-6"><a href="#cite_note-brown-20">[16]</a></sup> The existence of an atmosphere also provides a natural explanation for the nitrogen depletion: because the gravity of Makemake is weaker than that of Pluto, Eris and Triton, a large amount of nitrogen was probably lost via <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_escape" title="Atmospheric escape">atmospheric escape</a>; methane is lighter than nitrogen, but has significantly lower vapor pressure at temperatures prevalent at the surface of Makemake (32–36 K),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ortiz2012_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-Ortiz2012-10">[8]</a></sup> which hinders its escape; the result of this process is a higher relative abundance of methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schaller2007_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schaller2007-48">[42]</a></sup> However, studies of Pluto's atmosphere by <i>New Horizons</i> suggest that methane, not nitrogen, is the dominant escaping gas, suggesting that Makemake's absence of nitrogen may be more complicated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Satellites">Satellites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Makemake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Satellites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/S/2015_(136472)_1" title="S/2015 (136472) 1">S/2015 (136472) 1</a></div>
<p>On 26 April 2016, astronomers using observations from the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> taken in April 2015 announced the discovery of a moon with a diameter of ~175 km (for an assumed albedo of 4%) orbiting Makemake at a distance of ≥ 21,000 km with a period of ≥ 12 days (the minimum values are those for a circular orbit; the actual orbital eccentricity is unknown).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ParkerA2016_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-ParkerA2016-52">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parker_2016-05-02_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parker_2016-05-02-53">[47]</a></sup> It was given the provisional name <a href="/wiki/S/2015_(136472)_1" title="S/2015 (136472) 1">S/2015 (136472) 1</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HubbleSite_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-HubbleSite-25">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most other large trans-Neptunian objects have at least one satellite: <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">Eris</a> has <a href="/wiki/Dysnomia_(moon)" title="Dysnomia (moon)">one</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">Haumea</a> has <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Haumea" title="Moons of Haumea">two</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a> has <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Pluto" title="Moons of Pluto">five</a>, and <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/(225088)_2007_OR10" title="(225088) 2007 OR10">2007 OR<sub>10</sub></a></span> has one satellite. 10% to 20% of all <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object" title="Trans-Neptunian object">trans-Neptunian objects</a> are expected to have one or more satellites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2006-discovery_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2006-discovery-32">[27]</a></sup> Because satellites offer a simple method to measure an object's mass, Makemake's satellite should lead to better estimates of its mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2006-discovery_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2006-discovery-32">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">Exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Makemake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It was calculated that a flyby mission to Makemake could take just over 16 years using a Jupiter <a href="/wiki/Gravity_assist" title="Gravity assist">gravity assist</a>, based on a launch date of 21 August 2024 or 24 August 2036. Makemake would be approximately 52 AU from the Sun when the spacecraft arrives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGranaghan_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-McGranaghan-54">[48]</a></sup></p>
