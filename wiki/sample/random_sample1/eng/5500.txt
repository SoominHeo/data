<p><b>Maysville</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Kentucky_cities" title="List of Kentucky cities">home rule-class city</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Mason_County,_Kentucky" title="Mason County, Kentucky">Mason County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and is the <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">seat</a> of Mason County.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR6_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR6-2">[2]</a></sup> The population was 9,011 at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Census,_2010" title="United States Census, 2010">2010 census</a>, making it the 40th-largest city in Kentucky by population. Maysville is on the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a>, 66 miles (106 km) northeast of <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Kentucky" title="Lexington, Kentucky">Lexington</a>. It is the principal city of the Maysville <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maysville_micropolitan_area" title="Maysville micropolitan area">Micropolitan Statistical Area</a>, which includes Mason and <a href="/wiki/Lewis_County,_Kentucky" title="Lewis County, Kentucky">Lewis</a> counties. Two bridges cross the Ohio from Maysville to <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen,_Ohio" title="Aberdeen, Ohio">Aberdeen, Ohio</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Simon_Kenton_Memorial_Bridge" title="Simon Kenton Memorial Bridge">Simon Kenton Memorial Bridge</a> built in 1931 and the <a href="/wiki/William_H._Harsha_Bridge" title="William H. Harsha Bridge">William H. Harsha Bridge</a> built in 2001.</p>
<p>On the edge of the outer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bluegrass_Region" title="Bluegrass Region">Bluegrass Region</a>, Maysville is historically important in Kentucky's settlement. Frontiersmen <a href="/wiki/Simon_Kenton" title="Simon Kenton">Simon Kenton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Boone" title="Daniel Boone">Daniel Boone</a> are among the city's founders. Later, Maysville became an important port on the Ohio River for the northeastern part of the state. It exported <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_whiskey" title="Bourbon whiskey">bourbon whiskey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a>, the latter two produced mainly by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">African American</a> slaves before the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It was once a center of <a href="/wiki/Wrought_iron" title="Wrought iron">wrought iron</a> manufacture, sending ironwork downriver to decorate the buildings of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati, Ohio</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Orleans,_Louisiana" title="New Orleans, Louisiana">New Orleans, Louisiana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Other small manufacturers also located early in Maysville and manufacturing remains an important part of the modern economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-calvert_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-calvert-4">[4]</a></sup> Under the leadership of Henry Means Walker, Maysville was home to one of the largest tobacco auction warehouses in the world for most of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Maysville was an important stop on the <a href="/wiki/Underground_Railroad" title="Underground Railroad">Underground Railroad</a>, as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_state_(USA)" title="Free state (USA)">free state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> was just across the river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leocha_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-leocha-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_States" title="Abolitionism in the United States">abolitionist</a> author <a href="/wiki/Harriet_Beecher_Stowe" title="Harriet Beecher Stowe">Harriet Beecher Stowe</a> visited the area in 1833 and watched a slave auction in front of the court house in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_Kentucky" title="Washington, Kentucky">Washington</a>, the original seat of the county and now a historic district of Maysville.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rodgers_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-rodgers-7">[7]</a></sup> She included the scene in her influential novel <i><a href="/wiki/Uncle_Tom%27s_Cabin" title="Uncle Tom's Cabin">Uncle Tom's Cabin</a></i>, published in 1852.</p>
<p>The annual Rosemary Clooney Music Festival was founded by <a href="/wiki/Rosemary_Clooney" title="Rosemary Clooney">the singer</a> in 1999 to benefit the restoration of the <a href="/wiki/Russell_Theatre" title="Russell Theatre">Russell Theatre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Past performers at the festival include <a href="/wiki/Debby_Boone" title="Debby Boone">Debby Boone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rita_Coolidge" title="Rita Coolidge">Rita Coolidge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Feinstein" title="Michael Feinstein">Michael Feinstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roberta_Flack" title="Roberta Flack">Roberta Flack</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alison_Krauss" title="Alison Krauss">Alison Krauss</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Pointer_Sisters" title="The Pointer Sisters">The Pointer Sisters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bolton" title="Michael Bolton">Michael Bolton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Linda_Ronstadt" title="Linda Ronstadt">Linda Ronstadt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The Ledger Independent is the local newspaper and is published five days a week. <a href="/wiki/WFTM_(AM)" title="WFTM (AM)">WFTM-AM</a> and <a href="/wiki/WFTM-FM" title="WFTM-FM">WFTM-FM</a> are the primary local radio stations in Maysville. The AM station is a CBS Sports Radio affiliate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and the FM plays adult contemporary music.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maysville,_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_settlement">Early settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maysville,_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/American_bison" title="American bison">Buffalo</a> once forded the Ohio here, beating a broad path into the interior of Kentucky in search of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salt_lick" title="Salt lick">salt licks</a>. For thousands of years, various cultures of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous peoples</a> inhabited the area, hunting the buffalo and other game. In the 17th century, the powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iroquois_Confederacy" title="Iroquois Confederacy">Iroquois Confederacy</a>, based in present-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> state, drove out other tribes to hold the Ohio Valley as a hunting ground.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European-American" title="European-American">European-American</a> settlers' traveling down the Ohio in the 18th century and early 19th century found a natural harbor at Limestone Creek. The buffalo trace, also a well-used trail traveled for centuries by <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native Americans</a>, was a natural path into the bluegrass region, extending all the way to <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Kentucky" title="Lexington, Kentucky">Lexington, Kentucky</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leocha_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-leocha-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Frontiersman <a href="/wiki/Simon_Kenton" title="Simon Kenton">Simon Kenton</a> made the first settlement in the area in 1775 but temporarily abandoned that to fight in the <a href="/wiki/Western_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War" title="Western theater of the American Revolutionary War">western battles of the American Revolution</a>. Returning in 1784, Kenton built a <a href="/wiki/Blockhouse" title="Blockhouse">blockhouse</a> at the site of Maysville and founded Kenton's Station (frontier fort) at a site three miles (5 km) inland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Kenton met new settlers at Limestone, as the landing place was called, and escorted them inland to his station. In 1786 the village which grew up near Kenton's Station was established by act of the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_General_Assembly" title="Virginia General Assembly">Virginia General Assembly</a> as the town of <a href="/wiki/Washington,_Kentucky" title="Washington, Kentucky">Washington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-best-14">[14]</a></sup> By this time John May had acquired the land at Limestone and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Boone" title="Daniel Boone">Daniel Boone</a> established a <a href="/wiki/Trading_post" title="Trading post">trading post</a> and tavern there. In 1787 the little settlement was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipal_incorporation" title="Municipal incorporation">incorporated</a> as Maysville, though the name Limestone persisted well into the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth">Growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maysville,_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:View_of_Maysville,_Kentucky_in_1821.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="305" data-file-width="450" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5a/View_of_Maysville%2C_Kentucky_in_1821.jpg/250px-View_of_Maysville%2C_Kentucky_in_1821.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5a/View_of_Maysville%2C_Kentucky_in_1821.jpg/375px-View_of_Maysville%2C_Kentucky_in_1821.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/5a/View_of_Maysville%2C_Kentucky_in_1821.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:View_of_Maysville,_Kentucky_in_1821.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
View of Maysville, 1821</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1788, when <a href="/wiki/Mason_County,_Kentucky" title="Mason County, Kentucky">Mason County</a> was organized and Washington was named its county seat, Maysville was still a rude collection of warehouses and wharves, with few dwellings. In 1795 the conclusion of the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Indian_War" title="Northwest Indian War">Northwest Indian War</a> reduced the likelihood of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Indian</a> attacks from across the Ohio. Maysville began to flourish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rodgers_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-rodgers-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Zane%27s_Trace" title="Zane's Trace">Zane's Trace</a>, a road from <a href="/wiki/Wheeling,_West_Virginia" title="Wheeling, West Virginia">Wheeling, Virginia</a> (now West Virginia), to the bank of the Ohio River opposite Maysville, was completed in 1797 and stimulated ferry traffic across the river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> By 1807 Maysville was one of two principal ports in Kentucky; it was still mostly a place through which goods and people passed, having only about sixty dwellings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 1811 the first <a href="/wiki/Steamboat" title="Steamboat">steamboat</a> came down the Ohio from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</a>, passing Maysville on its way to <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> With the coming of the steamboat, Maysville's population and area expanded rapidly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-calvert_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-calvert-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maysville_Road">Maysville Road</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maysville,_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Maysville Road">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Maysville_Road_veto" title="Maysville Road veto">Maysville Road veto</a></div>
<p>Southwest from Maysville, the road followed the former buffalo trace and Native American trail to Lexington. It was called both the Maysville Road and the Limestone Road. It was maintained by the various counties through which it passed with local labor from the county levies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The road was rough and during certain seasons practically impassable.</p>
<p>In 1829 the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_General_Assembly" title="Kentucky General Assembly">Kentucky legislature</a> authorized the Maysville, Washington, Paris, and Lexington <a href="/wiki/Toll_roads_in_the_United_States#History.2C_funding_through_toll" title="Toll roads in the United States">Turnpike</a> Road Company to construct a modern roadway along the route of the old Limestone Road. Users would be charged fees for maintenance and paying off the debt to <a href="/wiki/Shareholder" title="Shareholder">shareholders</a>. The act set aside blocks of shares for purchase by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">federal government</a>. <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay" title="Henry Clay">Henry Clay</a>, an influential Kentucky <a href="/wiki/Politician" title="Politician">politician</a> and proponent of the <a href="/wiki/American_System_(economic_plan)" title="American System (economic plan)">American System</a>, argued for the Maysville Road and other infrastructure, noting it would be part of a longer road terminating in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Orleans,_Louisiana" title="New Orleans, Louisiana">New Orleans, Louisiana</a>, and proper for federal funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1830, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> passed a bill authorizing the federal government to purchase shares in the turnpike company. President <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Jackson" title="Andrew Jackson">Andrew Jackson</a>, a bitter rival of Clay, <a href="/wiki/Veto" title="Veto">vetoed</a> the bill, arguing that the project was of purely local benefit. The <a href="/wiki/Maysville_Road_veto" title="Maysville Road veto">Maysville Road veto</a> was one of Jackson's first acts in aligning the federal government with his principles of <a href="/wiki/Jacksonian_democracy" title="Jacksonian democracy">Jacksonian democracy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>An attempt to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veto_override" title="Veto override">override Jackson's veto</a> failed, but the controversy over the Maysville Road veto continued for some time. The turnpike was completed in 1835 with funding from local entities and private investment. It was the first <a href="/wiki/Macadam" title="Macadam">macadamized</a> road in the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Today it is <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_68" title="U.S. Route 68">U.S. Route 68</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rodgers_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-rodgers-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="County_seat">County seat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maysville,_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: County seat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By the 1830s, Maysville had a population of 3,000 and was the second-most important commercial city in Kentucky after <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atlas_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-atlas-23">[23]</a></sup> Washington, the county seat, had dwindled in importance after a fire in 1825 and a series of deadly cholera epidemics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-best-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reis_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-reis-24">[24]</a></sup> A proposal to move the county government from Washington to Maysville was bitterly fought but passed by a slender margin in 1848. Maysville donated its city hall, completed in 1846, to the county for a court house.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-calvert_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-calvert-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-simon_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-simon-25">[25]</a></sup> Today, much of Washington is designated as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historic_district_(United_States)" title="Historic district (United States)">historic district</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Historic_District_(Washington,_Kentucky)" title="Washington Historic District (Washington, Kentucky)">Washington Historic District</a>; it is listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1850, the <a href="/wiki/Maysville_and_Lexington_Railroad" title="Maysville and Lexington Railroad">Maysville &amp; Lexington Railroad</a> began operation, but it failed within the decade. Successor companies maintained the connection until 1921, when they were bought out by the <a href="/wiki/Louisville_and_Nashville_Railroad" title="Louisville and Nashville Railroad">L&amp;N</a>. Today, the Maysville &amp; Lexington's former routes and rights-of-way are owned by <a href="/wiki/CSX_Transportation" title="CSX Transportation">CSX Transportation</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landmarks">Landmarks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maysville,_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Landmarks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Simon_Kenton_Memorial_Bridge">Simon Kenton Memorial Bridge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maysville,_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Simon Kenton Memorial Bridge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SimonKentonBridge.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="1557" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/SimonKentonBridge.jpg/100px-SimonKentonBridge.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/SimonKentonBridge.jpg/150px-SimonKentonBridge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/SimonKentonBridge.jpg/200px-SimonKentonBridge.jpg 2x" width="100"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SimonKentonBridge.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Simon Kenton Memorial Bridge</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Simon_Kenton_Memorial_Bridge" title="Simon Kenton Memorial Bridge">Simon Kenton Memorial Bridge</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Suspension_bridge" title="Suspension bridge">suspension bridge</a> built in 1931 and originally opened with a toll. Those tolls were removed in 1945 to much fanfare - including celebrations from the local Rotary and Lions club, and a parade in downtown Maysville. It crosses the Ohio River. Its main span is 323 meters (1,060 feet) long, and the total length of the bridge is 607 meters (1,991 feet). It connects Maysville, Kentucky and <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen,_Ohio" title="Aberdeen, Ohio">Aberdeen, Ohio</a>. It is currently in use and is not slated for demolition as the bridge was closed for refurbishment in 2001 after the <a href="/wiki/William_H._Harsha_Bridge" title="William H. Harsha Bridge">William H. Harsha Bridge</a> was completed.</p>
<p>The Simon Kenton Suspension Bridge opened to traffic at 10:30 AM on November 25, 1931, at a cost of $1.6 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In 2002, a $5.8 million renovation job replaced the deck of the Ohio River crossing. A fresh coat of battleship gray paint was also applied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russell_Theatre">Russell Theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maysville,_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Russell Theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RusselTheatre2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2068" data-file-width="1748" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/RusselTheatre2.jpg/100px-RusselTheatre2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/RusselTheatre2.jpg/150px-RusselTheatre2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/RusselTheatre2.jpg/200px-RusselTheatre2.jpg 2x" width="100"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RusselTheatre2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Russell Theater</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Russell_Theatre" title="Russell Theatre">Russell Theatre</a>, located on Third Street in Maysville, was the site of the world premiere of <a href="/wiki/Rosemary_Clooney" title="Rosemary Clooney">Rosemary Clooney</a>'s first film, <i>The Stars Are Singing</i>, in 1953.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The Russell Theatre is an <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_theatre" title="Atmospheric theatre">atmospheric theatre</a>, and featured a large rainbow that would light up before and after the showing of each movie. The theatre is now undergoing the second phase of a restoration project that began in 2008. Organizers plan to revive The Russell as a movie and film venue, with emphasis on themed movie marathons, classics, documentaries, art films and other movies not available at mainstream cinemas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<p>In the summer of 1998 a series of historical <a href="/wiki/Mural" title="Mural">murals</a> was begun on the downtown floodwall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Over the next ten years <a href="/wiki/Robert_Dafford" title="Robert Dafford">Robert Dafford</a> and his team painted ten murals exploring the history of Maysville on various sections of the floodwall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CLOONEY_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-CLOONEY-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>17th century River Valley Hunting Grounds - A <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> <a href="/wiki/Bison" title="Bison">bison</a> hunt on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buffalo_Trace_(road)" title="Buffalo Trace (road)">buffalo trace</a>.</li>
<li>18th century Limestone Landing - The initial settlement of European-Americans on the future site of Maysville, then known as Limestone Landing.</li>
<li>Marquis de Lafayette - The 1825 visit of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marquis_de_Lafayette" title="Marquis de Lafayette">Marquis de Lafayette</a> to the city, where the city fathers had literally rolled out the red carpet.</li>
<li>1850s Sutton's Landing - The antebellum era Maysville riverfront, featuring iconic <a href="/wiki/Paddle_steamer" title="Paddle steamer">steam boats</a> on the Ohio River.</li>
<li>Underground Railroad 1850s - The Underground Railroad route through the area before the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, with a view looking across the river to the <a href="/wiki/John_Rankin_House_(Ripley,_Ohio)" title="John Rankin House (Ripley, Ohio)">John Rankin House</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ripley,_Ohio" title="Ripley, Ohio">Ripley, Ohio</a>.</li>
<li>20th century Maysville Riverfront - The continuing evolution of the waterfront location as steamboats gave way to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trains" title="Trains">trains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barge" title="Barge">barge</a> traffic.</li>
<li>Market Street - A main street in early 20th century downtown, with a bandstand in the middle of the street.</li>
<li>Germantown Fair - Held since 1854, the fair featured a wooden grandstand and livestock shows.</li>
<li>Tobacco barn - A major part of the local agricultural economy, the mural depicts the life of the plant from the field to the harvest to storage in local barns.</li>
<li>Rosemary Clooney - The last mural, painted in September 2007, honors moments from the life of Rosemary Clooney. The mural highlights her lifelong friendship with Blanche Chambers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> the 1953 premier of <i>The Stars are Singing</i> and her singing career. It was painted by Dafford, <a href="/wiki/Herb_Roe" title="Herb Roe">Herb Roe</a> and Brett Chigoy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CLOONEY_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-CLOONEY-32">[32]</a></sup> Her brother Nick Clooney spoke during the dedication for the mural, explaining various images to the crowd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maysville,_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR2_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-37">[37]</a></sup> of 2000, there were 7,323 people, 3,856 households, and 2,406 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 451.6 people per square mile (174.4/km²). There were 4,416 housing units at an average density of 221.8 per square mile (85.6/km²). The racial makeup of the city was 86.00% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 11.54% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 0.14% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 0.60% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.03% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 0.50% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 1.18% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 0.86% of the population. There were 3,856 households out of which 27.8% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 45.5% were married couples living together, 14.0% had a female householder with no husband present, and 37.6% were non-families. 33.7% of all households were made up of individuals and 16.7% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.25 and the average family size was 2.85. In the city the population was spread out with 22.7% under the age of 18, 8.1% from 18 to 24, 26.6% from 25 to 44, 23.5% from 45 to 64, and 19.1% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 40 years. For every 100 females there were 85.9 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 80.7 males. The median income for a household in the city was $27,813, and the median income for a family was $37,684. Males had a median income of $31,975 versus $20,775 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $16,836. About 14.4% of families and 18.9% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 27.1% of those under age 18 and 16.2% of those age 65 or over.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maysville,_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Maysville is located on the Ohio River at the mouth of Limestone Creek. It occupies the narrow river plain and the steep hills rising from it, giving the city the prospect of an <a href="/wiki/Hilltowns_in_Italy" title="Hilltowns in Italy">Italian hill town</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-simon_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-simon-25">[25]</a></sup> The city now extends inland to the former town of <a href="/wiki/Washington,_Kentucky" title="Washington, Kentucky">Washington</a>, which was annexed by Maysville in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reis_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-reis-24">[24]</a></sup> The city has a total area of 22.25 square miles (57.6 km<sup>2</sup>), of which 19.91 square miles (51.6 km<sup>2</sup>) is land and 2.3 square miles (6.0 km<sup>2</sup>), or 10.52%, is water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-factsheet_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-factsheet-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popcount_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-popcount-39">[39]</a></sup> Maysville is at Ohio <a href="/wiki/River_mile" title="River mile">River mile</a> marker 408.7, and is 100 miles (160 km) downriver from <a href="/wiki/Huntington,_West_Virginia" title="Huntington, West Virginia">Huntington, West Virginia</a> and 62 miles (100 km) upriver from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati, Ohio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maysville,_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Maysville lies on the border of the <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate" title="Humid subtropical climate">Humid subtropical</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Humid_continental_climate#Dfa.2FDwa.2FDsa:_Hot_.28or_very_warm.29_summer_subtype" title="Humid continental climate">Hot Summer Continental</a> climate zones. Maysville's average yearly precipitation is 46.02", falling primarily as rain or snow. Maysville's average yearly temperature is 54.4 °F, with the coolest lows averaging 22.2 °F in January, and highs averaging 87 °F in July.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail_Transportation">Rail Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maysville,_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Rail Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maysville_(Amtrak_station)" title="Maysville (Amtrak station)">Maysville (Amtrak station)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a>, the national passenger rail system, provides service to Maysville. Amtrak Train 51, the westbound <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_(train)" title="Cardinal (train)">Cardinal</a>, is scheduled to depart Maysville at 11:36pm on Sunday, Wednesday, and Friday with service to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati</a>, Connersville, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indianapolis,_Indiana" title="Indianapolis, Indiana">Indianapolis</a>, Crawfordsville, Lafayette, Rensselaer, Dyer, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Station_(Chicago)" title="Union Station (Chicago)">Chicago</a>. Amtrak Train 50, the eastbound Cardinal, is scheduled to depart Maysville at 4:36am on Wednesday, Friday, and Sunday with service to South Portsmouth, Ashland, Huntington, <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_West_Virginia" title="Charleston, West Virginia">Charleston</a>, Montgomery, Thurmond, Prince, Hinton, Alderson, White Sulphur Springs, Clifton Forge, Staunton, <a href="/wiki/Charlottesville,_Virginia" title="Charlottesville, Virginia">Charlottesville</a>, Culpeper, Manassas, Alexandria, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Station_(Washington)" title="Union Station (Washington)">Washington, D.C.</a>, before continuing on to New York City.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mass_transit">Mass transit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maysville,_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Mass transit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Maysville Transit provides fixed-route and demand-ride bus service throughout city of Maysville. The system is made up of a single fixed route that operates daily, running once an hour from 6:00 AM – 6:00 PM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The city has been running the system since 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Highways">Highways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maysville,_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Highways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following highways serve Maysville:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_62" title="U.S. Route 62">U.S. Route 62</a>, which connects Downtown Maysville with <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen,_Ohio" title="Aberdeen, Ohio">Aberdeen, Ohio</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Simon_Kenton_Memorial_Bridge" title="Simon Kenton Memorial Bridge">Simon Kenton Memorial Bridge</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_68" title="U.S. Route 68">U.S. Route 68</a>, which passes just west of Maysville and links Kentucky and Ohio via the <a href="/wiki/William_H._Harsha_Bridge" title="William H. Harsha Bridge">William H. Harsha Bridge</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>US 62 and 68 also provide Maysville with a direct route to <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Kentucky" title="Lexington, Kentucky">Lexington</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bluegrass_Region" title="Bluegrass Region">Bluegrass Region</a> of Central Kentucky.</p>
<p>Other highways serving Maysville are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Route_8" title="Kentucky Route 8">Kentucky Route 8</a>, which follows the Ohio River west of Maysville to the greater Cincinnati area</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Route_9" title="Kentucky Route 9">Kentucky Route 9</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Route_10" title="Kentucky Route 10">Kentucky Route 10</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Routes 9 and 10 run concurrently through the south edge of Maysville as the AA HIghway. The AA Highway links the Kentucky suburbs of Cincinnati approximately 50 miles west of Maysville with <a href="/wiki/Vanceburg,_Kentucky" title="Vanceburg, Kentucky">Vanceburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ashland,_Kentucky" title="Ashland, Kentucky">Ashland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interstate_64" title="Interstate 64">Interstate 64</a> near <a href="/wiki/Grayson,_Kentucky" title="Grayson, Kentucky">Grayson</a> to the southeast.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Route_11" title="Kentucky Route 11">Kentucky Route 11</a>, a north-south highway that approaches Maysville from the south from <a href="/wiki/Flemingsburg,_Kentucky" title="Flemingsburg, Kentucky">Flemngsburg</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mt._Sterling,_Kentucky" title="Mt. Sterling, Kentucky">Mt. Sterling</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The fact that highways numbered 8, 9, 10, and 11 serve Maysville makes the city one of the few towns located at the intersection of four consecutively numbered highways.</p>
