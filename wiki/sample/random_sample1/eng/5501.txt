<p>The <b>Meiji Restoration</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">明治維新</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Meiji Ishin</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, also known as the <i>Meiji Ishin</i>, <i>Renovation</i>, <i>Revolution</i>, <i>Reform</i>, or <i>Renewal</i>, was a <a href="/wiki/Chain_of_events" title="Chain of events">chain of events</a> that restored practical imperial rule to Japan in 1868 under <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Meiji" title="Emperor Meiji">Emperor Meiji</a>. Although there were Emperors before the Meiji Restoration, the events restored practical abilities and consolidated the political system under the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan" title="Emperor of Japan">Emperor of Japan</a>.</p>
<p>The goals of the restored government were expressed by the new emperor in the <a href="/wiki/Charter_Oath" title="Charter Oath">Charter Oath</a>. The Restoration led to enormous changes in Japan's political and social structure, and spanned both the late <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a> (often called the <a href="/wiki/Bakumatsu" title="Bakumatsu">Late Tokugawa shogunate</a>) and the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_period" title="Meiji period">Meiji period</a>. The period spanned from 1868 to 1912 and was responsible for the emergence of Japan as a modernized nation in the early twentieth century, and its rapid rise to <a href="/wiki/Great_power" title="Great power">great power</a> status in the international system.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alliances_and_allegiances">Alliances and allegiances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meiji_Restoration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Alliances and allegiances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The foundation of the Meiji restoration was the 1866 <a href="/wiki/Satch%C5%8D_Alliance" title="Satchō Alliance">Satsuma-Chōshū Alliance</a> between <a href="/wiki/Saig%C5%8D_Takamori" title="Saigō Takamori">Saigō Takamori</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kido_Takayoshi" title="Kido Takayoshi">Kido Takayoshi</a>, leaders of the reformist elements in the <a href="/wiki/Satsuma_Domain" title="Satsuma Domain">Satsuma Domain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ch%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB_Domain" title="Chōshū Domain">Chōshū Domain</a>. These two leaders supported the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_K%C5%8Dmei" title="Emperor Kōmei">Emperor Kōmei</a> (Emperor Meiji's father) and were brought together by <a href="/wiki/Sakamoto_Ry%C5%8Dma" title="Sakamoto Ryōma">Sakamoto Ryōma</a> for the purpose of challenging the ruling <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_shogunate" title="Tokugawa shogunate">Tokugawa shogunate</a> (<i>bakufu</i>) and restoring the Emperor to power. After Emperor Kōmei's death on January 30, 1867, Emperor Meiji ascended the throne on February 3. This period also saw Japan change from being a feudal society to having a market economy and left the Japanese with a lingering Western influence.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_the_Shogunate">End of the Shogunate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meiji_Restoration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: End of the Shogunate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bakumatsu" title="Bakumatsu">Bakumatsu</a></div>
<p>The Tokugawa Shogunate came to its official end on November 9, 1867, when <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Yoshinobu" title="Tokugawa Yoshinobu">Tokugawa Yoshinobu</a>, the 15th Tokugawa Shogun, "put his prerogatives at the Emperor's disposal" and resigned 10 days later. This was effectively the "restoration" (<i>Taisei Hōkan</i>) of imperial rule – although Yoshinobu still had significant influence and it was not until January 3, the following year, with the young emperor's edict that the restoration fully occurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shortly thereafter in January 1868, the <a href="/wiki/Boshin_War" title="Boshin War">Boshin War</a> (War of the Year of the Dragon) started with the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Toba%E2%80%93Fushimi" title="Battle of Toba–Fushimi">Battle of Toba–Fushimi</a> in which Chōshū and <a href="/wiki/Satsuma_Domain" title="Satsuma Domain">Satsuma</a>'s forces defeated the ex-shogun's army. This forced (or allowed) the Emperor to strip Yoshinobu of all power, setting the stage for official restoration. On January 3, 1868, the Emperor made a formal declaration of the restoration of his power:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The Emperor of Japan announces to the sovereigns of all foreign countries and to their subjects that permission has been granted to the <a href="/wiki/Shogun" title="Shogun">Shogun</a> Tokugawa Yoshinobu to return the governing power in accordance with his own request. We shall henceforward exercise supreme authority in all the internal and external affairs of the country. Consequently the title of Emperor must be substituted for that of <a href="/wiki/Taikun" title="Taikun">Taikun</a>, in which the treaties have been made. Officers are being appointed by us to the conduct of foreign affairs. It is desirable that the representatives of the treaty powers recognize this announcement.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutsuhito" title="Mutsuhito">Mutsuhito</a>, January 3, 1868<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Some shogunate forces escaped to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hokkaid%C5%8D" title="Hokkaidō">Hokkaidō</a>, where they attempted to set up a breakaway <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ezo" title="Republic of Ezo">Republic of Ezo</a>; however, forces loyal to the Emperor ended this attempt in May 1869 with the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hakodate" title="Battle of Hakodate">Battle of Hakodate</a> in Hokkaidō. The defeat of the armies of the former shogun (led by <a href="/wiki/Enomoto_Takeaki" title="Enomoto Takeaki">Enomoto Takeaki</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hijikata_Toshiz%C5%8D" title="Hijikata Toshizō">Hijikata Toshizō</a>) marked the final end of the Tokugawa Shogunate, with the Emperor's power fully restored.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motives">Motives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meiji_Restoration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Motives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>There were many causes of the Meiji Restoration. The Japanese knew that they were behind the European world when American <a href="/wiki/Commodore_(United_States)" title="Commodore (United States)">Commodore</a> <a href="/wiki/Matthew_C._Perry" title="Matthew C. Perry">Matthew C. Perry</a> came to Japan to try to issue a treaty that would open up Japanese ports to trade. Perry came to Japan in <a href="/wiki/Black_Ships" title="Black Ships">large warships</a> with armament and technology that far outclassed those of Japan. The leaders of the Meiji Restoration, as this revolution came to be known, acted in the name of restoring imperial rule to strengthen Japan against the threat represented by the colonial powers of the day, bringing to an end the era known as <i><a href="/wiki/Sakoku" title="Sakoku">Sakoku</a></i> (the foreign relations policy, lasting about 250 years, prescribing the death penalty for foreigners entering or Japanese nationals leaving the country). The word "Meiji" means "enlightened rule" and the goal was to combine "modern advances" with traditional "eastern" values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The events occurring in neighboring <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> during the European influenced <a href="/wiki/Opium_Wars" title="Opium Wars">Opium Wars</a> may also have motivated the Japanese leadership to rethink isolationist policies.</p>
<p>The main leaders of this were <a href="/wiki/It%C5%8D_Hirobumi" title="Itō Hirobumi">Itō Hirobumi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matsukata_Masayoshi" title="Matsukata Masayoshi">Matsukata Masayoshi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kido_Takayoshi" title="Kido Takayoshi">Kido Takayoshi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Itagaki_Taisuke" title="Itagaki Taisuke">Itagaki Taisuke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yamagata_Aritomo" title="Yamagata Aritomo">Yamagata Aritomo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mori_Arinori" title="Mori Arinori">Mori Arinori</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ckubo_Toshimichi" title="Ōkubo Toshimichi">Ōkubo Toshimichi</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yamaguchi_Naoyoshi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yamaguchi Naoyoshi (page does not exist)">Yamaguchi Naoyoshi</a>. Under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Mori_Arinori" title="Mori Arinori">Mori Arinori</a>, a group of prominent Japanese intellectuals went on to form the <a href="/wiki/Meirokusha" title="Meirokusha">Meiji Six Society</a> in 1873 to continue to "promote civilization and enlightenment" through modern ethics and ideas. However, during the restoration, political power simply moved from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Shogunate" title="Tokugawa Shogunate">Tokugawa Shogunate</a> to an <a href="/wiki/Oligarchy" title="Oligarchy">oligarchy</a> consisting of these leaders, mostly from the <a href="/wiki/Satsuma_Province" title="Satsuma Province">Satsuma Province</a> (<a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ckubo_Toshimichi" title="Ōkubo Toshimichi">Ōkubo Toshimichi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saig%C5%8D_Takamori" title="Saigō Takamori">Saigō Takamori</a>), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ch%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB_Province" title="Chōshū Province">Chōshū Province</a> (<a href="/wiki/It%C5%8D_Hirobumi" title="Itō Hirobumi">Itō Hirobumi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yamagata_Aritomo" title="Yamagata Aritomo">Yamagata Aritomo</a>, and Kido Takayoshi). This reflected their belief in the more traditional practice of imperial rule, whereby the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenno" title="Tenno">Emperor of Japan</a> serves solely as the spiritual authority of the nation and his ministers govern the nation in his name.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meiji_Restoration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Meiji_period" title="Meiji period">Meiji period</a>, <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Meiji_Japan" title="Government of Meiji Japan">Government of Meiji Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Constitution" title="Meiji Constitution">Meiji Constitution</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Abolition_of_the_han_system" title="Abolition of the han system">Abolition of the han system</a></div>

<p>The Meiji Restoration accelerated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialization" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a> in Japan, which led to its rise as a military power by the year 1905, under the slogan of "Enrich the country, strengthen the military" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">富国強兵</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i><a href="/wiki/Fukoku_ky%C5%8Dhei" title="Fukoku kyōhei">fukoku kyōhei</a></i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Meiji_oligarchy" title="Meiji oligarchy">Meiji oligarchy</a> that formed the government under the rule of the Emperor first introduced measures to consolidate their power against the remnants of the Edo period government, the shogunate, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daimyo" title="Daimyo">daimyo</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a> class.</p>
<p>In 1868, all Tokugawa lands were seized and placed under "imperial control", thus placing them under the prerogative of the new Meiji government. With <a href="/wiki/Fuhanken_sanchisei" title="Fuhanken sanchisei">Fuhanken sanchisei</a>, the areas were split into three types: urban <a href="/wiki/Prefecture" title="Prefecture">prefectures</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">府</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>fu</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, rural prefectures <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">県</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>ken</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> and the already existing domains. In 1869, the daimyo of the <a href="/wiki/Tosa_Domain" title="Tosa Domain">Tosa</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hizen_Domain" title="Hizen Domain">Hizen</a>, Satsuma and <a href="/wiki/Ch%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB_Domain" title="Chōshū Domain">Chōshū Domains</a>, who were pushing most fiercely against the shogunate, were persuaded to "return their domains to the Emperor". Other daimyo were subsequently persuaded to do so, thus creating, arguably for the first time, a central government in Japan which exercised direct power through the entire "realm" (<a href="/wiki/Tianxia#Japan" title="Tianxia">天下</a>).</p>
<p>Finally, in 1871, the daimyo, past and present, were summoned before the Emperor, where it was declared that <a href="/wiki/Abolition_of_the_han_system" title="Abolition of the han system">all domains were now to be returned to the Emperor</a>. The roughly 300 domains (han) were turned into prefectures, each under the control of a state-appointed governor. By 1888, several prefectures had been merged in several steps to reduce their number to 75. The daimyo were promised 1/10 of their fiefs' income as private income. Later, their debts and payments of samurai stipends were to be taken over by the state.</p>
<p>The oligarchs also endeavoured to abolish the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Four_divisions_of_society" title="Four divisions of society">four divisions of society</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Throughout Japan at the time, the samurai numbered 1.9 million. (For comparison, this was more than 10 times the size of the French privileged class before the 1789 <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>. Moreover, the samurai in Japan were not merely the lords, but also their higher retainers—people who actually worked.) With each samurai being paid fixed stipends, their upkeep presented a tremendous financial burden, which may have prompted the oligarchs to action. Whatever their true intentions, the oligarchs embarked on another slow and deliberate process to abolish the samurai class. First, in 1873, it was announced that the samurai stipends were to be taxed on a rolling basis. Later, in 1874, the samurai were given the option to convert their stipends into <a href="/wiki/Government_bond" title="Government bond">government bonds</a>. Finally, in 1876, this commutation was made compulsory.</p>
<p>To reform the military, the government instituted nationwide conscription in 1873, mandating that every male would serve for four years in the armed forces upon turning 21, followed by three more years in the reserves. One of the primary differences between the samurai and peasant classes was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_to_bear_arms" title="Right to bear arms">right to bear arms</a>; this ancient privilege was suddenly extended to every male in the nation. Furthermore, samurai were no longer allowed to walk about town bearing a sword or weapon to show their status.</p>
<p>This led to a series of riots from disgruntled samurai. One of the major riots was the one led by Saigō Takamori, the <a href="/wiki/Satsuma_Rebellion" title="Satsuma Rebellion">Satsuma Rebellion</a>, which eventually turned into a civil war. This rebellion was, however, put down swiftly by the newly formed <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army" title="Imperial Japanese Army">Imperial Japanese Army</a>, trained in Western tactics and weapons, even though the core of the new army was the Tokyo police force, which was largely composed of former samurai. This sent a strong message to the dissenting samurai that their time was indeed over. There were fewer subsequent samurai uprisings and the distinction became all but a name as the samurai joined the new society. The ideal of samurai military spirit lived on in romanticized form and was often used as propaganda during the early 20th century wars of the <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Empire of Japan</a>.</p>
<p>However, it is equally true that the majority of samurai were content despite having their status abolished. Many found employment in the government bureaucracy, which resembled an elite class in its own right. The samurai, being better educated than most of the population, became teachers, gun makers, government officials, or military officers. While the formal title of samurai was abolished, the elitist spirit that characterized the samurai class lived on.</p>
<p>The oligarchs also embarked on a series of <a href="/wiki/Land_Tax_Reform_(Japan_1873)" title="Land Tax Reform (Japan 1873)">land reforms</a>. In particular, they legitimized the tenancy system which had been going on during the Tokugawa period. Despite the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bakufu" title="Bakufu">bakufu</a>'s best efforts to freeze the four classes of society in place, during their rule villagers had begun to lease land out to other farmers, becoming rich in the process. This greatly disrupted the clearly defined class system which the bakufu had envisaged, partly leading to their eventual downfall.</p>
<p>The military of Japan, being strengthened by nationwide conscription and the infusion of a samurai military spirit, became emboldened to see themselves as a growing world power after winning both the <a href="/wiki/First_Sino-Japanese_War" title="First Sino-Japanese War">Sino-Japanese War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a>.</p>
<p>Besides drastic changes to the social structure of Japan, in an attempt to create a strong centralized state defining its national identity, the government established a dominant national dialect, called "standard language" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">標準語</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hy%C5%8Djungo" title="Hyōjungo">hyōjungo</a></i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, that replaced local and regional dialects and was based on the patterns of Tokyo's samurai classes. This dialect eventually became the norm in the realms of education, media, government and business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Meiji Restoration, and the resultant modernization of Japan, also influenced Japanese self-identity with respect to its Asian neighbours, as Japan became the first Asian state to modernize based on the European model, replacing the traditional Confucian hierarchical order that had persisted previously under a dominant China with one based on modernity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_growth">Industrial growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meiji_Restoration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Industrial growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The rapid industrialization and modernization of Japan both allowed and required a massive increase in production and infrastructure. Japan built industries such as shipyards, iron smelters, and spinning mills, which were then sold to well-connected entrepreneurs. Consequently, domestic companies became consumers of Western technology and applied it to produce items that would be sold cheaply in the international market. With this, industrial zones grew enormously, and there was massive migration to industrializing centers from the countryside. Industrialization additionally went hand in hand with the development of a national railway system and modern communications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
