<p>The <b>Grand Duchy of Mecklenburg-Schwerin</b> was a territory in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Germany" title="Northern Germany">Northern Germany</a> held by the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Mecklenburg" title="House of Mecklenburg">House of Mecklenburg</a> residing at <a href="/wiki/Schwerin" title="Schwerin">Schwerin</a>. It was a sovereign member state of the <a href="/wiki/German_Confederation" title="German Confederation">German Confederation</a> and became a <a href="/wiki/Federated_state" title="Federated state">federated state</a> of the <a href="/wiki/North_German_Confederation" title="North German Confederation">North German Confederation</a> and finally of the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> in 1871.</p>

<p>Like its predecessor, the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Mecklenburg-Schwerin" title="Duchy of Mecklenburg-Schwerin">Duchy of Mecklenburg-Schwerin</a>, the Schwerin lands upon the incorporation of the extinct Duchy of <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg-G%C3%BCstrow" title="Mecklenburg-Güstrow">Mecklenburg-Güstrow</a> in 1701 comprised the larger central and western parts of the historic <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg" title="Mecklenburg">Mecklenburg</a> region. The smaller southeastern part was held by the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Mecklenburg-Strelitz" title="Duchy of Mecklenburg-Strelitz">Duchy of Mecklenburg-Strelitz</a> branch of the grand ducal house, who also ruled over the lands of the former <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Ratzeburg" title="Bishopric of Ratzeburg">Bishopric of Ratzeburg</a> in the far northwest.</p>
<p>The grand duchy was bounded by the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic</a> coast in the north and the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussian</a> province of <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Pomerania_(1815%E2%80%931945)" title="Province of Pomerania (1815–1945)">Pomerania</a> in the northeast, where the border with the <a href="/wiki/Western_Pomerania" title="Western Pomerania">Hither Pomeranian</a> (formerly <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Pomerania" title="Swedish Pomerania">Swedish Pomeranian</a>) region ran along the <a href="/wiki/Recknitz" title="Recknitz">Recknitz</a> river, the <a href="/wiki/Peene" title="Peene">Peene</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kummerower_See" title="Kummerower See">Kummerower See</a>. In the south it bordered with the Prussian province of <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Brandenburg" title="Province of Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a> (with the <a href="/wiki/Enclave_and_exclave" title="Enclave and exclave">exclaves</a> of Rossow and Schönberg near <a href="/wiki/Wittstock" title="Wittstock">Wittstock</a>) and in the southwest with the <a href="/wiki/Amt_Neuhaus" title="Amt Neuhaus">Amt Neuhaus</a> district held by the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hanover" title="Kingdom of Hanover">Kingdom of Hanover</a>, which was incorporated into the Prussian province of <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Hanover" title="Province of Hanover">Hanover</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Prussian_War" title="Austro-Prussian War">Austro-Prussian War</a> in 1866. Likewise in the west, the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Holstein" title="Duchy of Holstein">Duchy of Holstein</a> was incorporated into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schleswig-Holstein_Province" title="Schleswig-Holstein Province">Schleswig-Holstein Province</a>, after which Mecklenburg was almost entirely surrounded by Prussian territory.</p>
<p>Beside the capital at Schwerin, Mecklenburg-Schwerin comprised the coastal cities of <a href="/wiki/Rostock" title="Rostock">Rostock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wismar" title="Wismar">Wismar</a>, which had been held by the <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> crown until 1803, as well as the inland towns of <a href="/wiki/Parchim" title="Parchim">Parchim</a> and <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCstrow" title="Güstrow">Güstrow</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Duchy_of_Mecklenburg-Schwerin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early years of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars" title="French Revolutionary Wars">French Revolutionary Wars</a> Duke <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Francis_I,_Grand_Duke_of_Mecklenburg-Schwerin" title="Frederick Francis I, Grand Duke of Mecklenburg-Schwerin">Frederick Francis I of Mecklenburg-Schwerin</a> had remained neutral, and in 1803 he regained Wismar, which was pawned to him from Sweden. After <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>'s victory at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Austerlitz" title="Battle of Austerlitz">Battle of Austerlitz</a> and the final dissolution of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> in 1806, he joined the <a href="/wiki/Confederation_of_the_Rhine" title="Confederation of the Rhine">Confederation of the Rhine</a> by a treaty of 22 March 1808. Napoleon, in preparation for the <a href="/wiki/French_invasion_of_Russia" title="French invasion of Russia">French invasion of Russia</a> in 1812, disregarded this alliance; he offered the duchy to the Swedish heir apparent <a href="/wiki/Charles_XIV_John_of_Sweden" title="Charles XIV John of Sweden">Jean Bernadotte</a> for his support. Duke Frederick Francis was the first member of the confederation to abandon Napoleon, to whose armies he had sent a contingent, and in the following <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Sixth_Coalition" title="War of the Sixth Coalition">War of the Sixth Coalition</a> he fought against the troops of the <a href="/wiki/First_French_Empire" title="First French Empire">First French Empire</a> —with the result that his new allies, Prussia and <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a>, now offered his duchy to the Kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>. Instead, Denmark was promised the adjacent lands of Swedish Pomerania by the 1814 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peace_of_Kiel" title="Peace of Kiel">Peace of Kiel</a> and the rule of the Mecklenburg dukes remained inviolate.</p>
<p>At the 1815 <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a>, Frederick Francis joined the newly established <a href="/wiki/German_Confederation" title="German Confederation">German Confederation</a>, and like his Strelitz cousin <a href="/wiki/Charles_II,_Grand_Duke_of_Mecklenburg-Strelitz" title="Charles II, Grand Duke of Mecklenburg-Strelitz">Charles II</a>, was elevated to the title of a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Duke_of_Mecklenburg-Schwerin" title="Grand Duke of Mecklenburg-Schwerin">Grand Duke of Mecklenburg-Schwerin</a>". In 1819 <a href="/wiki/Serfdom" title="Serfdom">serfdom</a> was finally abolished in his dominions. The Mecklenburg governance was still determined by the 1755 inheritance agreement (<i>Landesgrundgesetzlicher Erbvergleich</i>), which upheld the medieval hierarchy of the <a href="/wiki/Estates_of_the_realm" title="Estates of the realm">estates</a>, which largely affected the social and economic development of both grand duchies. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1848_in_the_German_states" title="Revolutions of 1848 in the German states">revolutions of 1848</a>, the duchy witnessed a considerable agitation in favour of a liberal constitution. On 10 October 1849 Grand Duke <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Francis_II,_Grand_Duke_of_Mecklenburg-Schwerin" title="Frederick Francis II, Grand Duke of Mecklenburg-Schwerin">Frederick Francis II</a> (1823–1883) granted a new <a href="/wiki/Basic_law" title="Basic law">Basic law</a> elaborated by his First Minister <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_von_L%C3%BCtzow_(politician)" title="Ludwig von Lützow (politician)">Ludwig von Lützow</a>. In the subsequent reaction of the Mecklenburg nobility, backed by the Strelitz grand duke <a href="/wiki/George,_Grand_Duke_of_Mecklenburg-Strelitz" title="George, Grand Duke of Mecklenburg-Strelitz">George</a>, all the concessions which had been made to democracy were withdrawn and further restrictive measures were introduced in 1851 and 1852.</p>
<p>In the dispute over neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Holstein" title="Holstein">Holstein</a> which culminated in the 1866 <a href="/wiki/Austro-Prussian_War" title="Austro-Prussian War">Austro-Prussian War</a>, Frederick Francis II supported the Kingdom of Prussia, whom he aided with Mecklenburg-Schwerin soldiers. His grand duchy began to pass more and more under Prussian influence. In 1867 he joined the <a href="/wiki/North_German_Confederation" title="North German Confederation">North German Confederation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Zollverein" title="Zollverein">Zollverein</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a> (1870–1871), Prussia again received valuable assistance from Grand Duke Frederick Francis II, who was an ardent advocate of German unity and held a high command in her armies. In the course of the <a href="/wiki/Unification_of_Germany" title="Unification of Germany">German unification</a> in 1871, Mecklenburg-Schwerin and Mecklenburg-Strelitz became states of the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a>. There was now renewed agitation for a more democratic constitution, and the German <a href="/wiki/Reichstag_(German_Empire)" title="Reichstag (German Empire)">Reichstag</a> parliament gave some countenance to this movement.</p>
<p>In 1897 <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Francis_IV,_Grand_Duke_of_Mecklenburg-Schwerin" title="Frederick Francis IV, Grand Duke of Mecklenburg-Schwerin">Frederick Francis IV</a> (b. 1882) succeeded his father <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Francis_III,_Grand_Duke_of_Mecklenburg-Schwerin" title="Frederick Francis III, Grand Duke of Mecklenburg-Schwerin">Frederick Francis III</a> (1851–1897) as the last grand duke of Mecklenburg-Schwerin. In 1907 the Grand Duke promised a constitution to his subjects. The duchy had always been under a feudal system of government, the grand duke having the executive entirely in his hands (though acting through ministers). The duchy shared a diet (Landtag), which met for a short session each year. At other times they were represented by a committee consisting of the proprietors of knights' estates (Rittergüter), known as the Ritterschaft, and the Landschaft, or burgomasters of certain towns. Mecklenburg-Schwerin returned six members to the Reichstag. Upon the suicide of his cousin Grand Duke <a href="/wiki/Adolphus_Frederick_VI,_Grand_Duke_of_Mecklenburg-Strelitz" title="Adolphus Frederick VI, Grand Duke of Mecklenburg-Strelitz">Adolphus Frederick VI</a> on 23 February 1918, Frederick Francis served as regent of Mecklenburg-Strelitz. Shortly afterwards, on 14 November, he was forced to renounce the Mecklenburg throne in the course of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Revolution_of_1918%E2%80%931919" title="German Revolution of 1918–1919">German Revolution</a>. The grand duchy turned into the <a href="/wiki/Free_State_of_Mecklenburg-Schwerin" title="Free State of Mecklenburg-Schwerin">Free State of Mecklenburg-Schwerin</a>, a federated state of the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Weimar Republic</a>.</p>
<p>Thereby ended nearly eight centuries of continuous rule (only interrupted by <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_von_Wallenstein" title="Albrecht von Wallenstein">Albrecht von Wallenstein</a> from 1628 to 1630) by the originally <a href="/wiki/Obotrites" title="Obotrites">Obotrite</a> (<a href="/wiki/Polabian_Slavs" title="Polabian Slavs">West Slavic</a>) Mecklenburg dynasty, beginning with their progenitor Prince <a href="/wiki/Niklot" title="Niklot">Niklot</a> (d. 1160). Until 1918 the grand duke was styled as "Prince of the Wends".</p>
