<p><b>Saints Cyril and Methodius</b> (826-869, 815-885; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Κύριλλος καὶ Μεθόδιος</span>; <a href="/wiki/Old_Church_Slavonic" title="Old Church Slavonic">Old Church Slavonic</a>: <span class="script-slavonic" style="font-family: 'Fedorovsk Unicode', 'Fedorovsk Unicode TT', 'Menaion Unicode', 'Menaion Unicode TT', 'Monomakh Unicode', 'Monomakh Unicode TT', 'Ponomar Unicode', 'Ponomar Unicode TT', BukyVede, 'Kliment Std', 'RomanCyrillic Std', 'Monomachus', 'Old Standard', 'Old Standard TT', Dilyana, Menaion, 'Menaion Medieval', Lazov, Code2000, 'DejaVu Sans', 'DejaVu Serif', Code2001, 'FreeSerif', 'TITUS Cyberbit Basic', 'Charis SIL', 'Doulos SIL', 'Chrysanthi Unicode', 'Bitstream Cyberbit', 'Bitstream CyberBase', Thryomanes, 'Lucida Grande', 'FreeSans', 'Arial Unicode MS', 'Microsoft Sans Serif', 'Lucida Sans Unicode';" title="Slavonic">К<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%D1%B6" title="Ѷ">ѷ</a>риллъ и Ме<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%D1%B2" title="Ѳ">ѳ</a>одїи</span><sup class="reference" id="ref_crownnone"><a href="#endnote_crownnone">[more]</a></sup>) were two brothers who were <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_theologian" title="Christian theologian">Christian theologians</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_missionaries" title="Christian missionaries">Christian missionaries</a>. Through their work they influenced the cultural development of all Slavs, for which they received the title <i>"Apostles to the Slavs"</i>. They are credited with devising the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glagolitic_alphabet" title="Glagolitic alphabet">Glagolitic alphabet</a>, the first alphabet used to transcribe <a href="/wiki/Old_Church_Slavonic" title="Old Church Slavonic">Old Church Slavonic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> After their deaths, their pupils continued their missionary work among other Slavs. Both brothers are venerated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Church" title="Orthodox Church">Orthodox Church</a> as <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saints</a> with the title of <i>"<a href="/wiki/Equal-to-apostles" title="Equal-to-apostles">equal-to-apostles</a>"</i>. In 1880, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_XIII" title="Pope Leo XIII">Pope Leo XIII</a> introduced their feast into the calendar of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>. In 1980, <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> declared them co-<a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saints</a> of Europe, together with <a href="/wiki/Benedict_of_Nursia" title="Benedict of Nursia">Benedict of Nursia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Egregiae_Virtutis_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Egregiae_Virtutis-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span></h3>
<p>The two brothers were born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thessalonica" title="Thessalonica">Thessalonica</a>, in present-day <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> – Cyril in about 827–828 and Methodius about 815–820. Cyril was reputedly the youngest of seven brothers; he was born Constantine,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> but took the name Cyril upon becoming a monk in Rome shortly before his death,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> according to the <i>"Vita Cyrilli"</i> ("The Life of Cyril"). Methodius was born Michael and took the name Methodius upon becoming a monk at <a href="/wiki/Mysia" title="Mysia">Mysian</a> Olympus (present-day <a href="/wiki/Uluda%C4%9F" title="Uludağ">Uludağ</a>), in northwest Turkey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Their father was Leo, a <i><a href="/wiki/Droungarios" title="Droungarios">droungarios</a></i> of the Byzantine <a href="/wiki/Theme_(Byzantine_district)" title="Theme (Byzantine district)">theme</a> of Thessalonica, and their mother was Maria.</p>
<p>The exact ethnic origins of the brothers are unknown, there is controversy as to whether Cyril and Methodius were of <a href="/wiki/South_Slavs" title="South Slavs">Slavic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Greeks" title="Byzantine Greeks">Byzantine Greek</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> origin, or both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The two brothers lost their father when Cyril was fourteen, and the powerful minister <a href="/wiki/Theoktistos" title="Theoktistos">Theoktistos</a>, who was <i><a href="/wiki/Logothetes_tou_dromou" title="Logothetes tou dromou">logothetes tou dromou</a></i>, one of the chief ministers of the Empire, became their protector. He was also responsible, along with the regent <a href="/wiki/Bardas" title="Bardas">Bardas</a>, for initiating a far-reaching educational program within the Empire which culminated in the establishment of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Magnaura" title="University of Magnaura">University of Magnaura</a>, where Cyril was to teach. Cyril was ordained as <a href="/wiki/Priesthood_(Catholic_Church)" title="Priesthood (Catholic Church)">priest</a> some time after his education, while his brother Methodius remained only a <a href="/wiki/Deacon" title="Deacon">deacon</a> until 867/868.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Missions_in_the_Middle_East">Missions in the Middle East</span></h3>
<p>Cyril's mastery of theology and command of both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> made him eligible for his first state mission. He was sent to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbasid" title="Abbasid">Abbasid</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caliph" title="Caliph">Caliph</a> <a href="/wiki/Al-Mutawakkil" title="Al-Mutawakkil">Al-Mutawakkil</a> to discuss the principle of the Holy <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinity</a> with the Arab theologians, and to improve relations between the Caliphate and the Empire.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The second mission (860), requested by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine_Emperor" title="Byzantine Emperor">Byzantine Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Michael_III" title="Michael III">Michael III</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch of Constantinople">Patriarch of Constantinople</a> <a href="/wiki/Photios_I_of_Constantinople" title="Photios I of Constantinople">Photius</a> (a professor of Cyril's at the University and his guiding light in earlier years), was a missionary expedition to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khazar_Khaganate" title="Khazar Khaganate">Khazar Khaganate</a> in order to prevent the expansion of <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> there. This mission was unsuccessful, as later the Khagan imposed Judaism on his people as the national religion. It has been claimed that Methodius accompanied Cyril on the mission to the Khazars, but this is probably a later invention. The account of his life presented in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <i>"Legenda"</i> claims that he learned the Khazar language while in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chersonesos_Taurica" title="Chersonesos Taurica">Chersonesos</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taurica" title="Taurica">Taurica</a> (today <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>After his return to Constantinople, Cyril assumed the role of professor of <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> at the University while his brother had by this time become a significant player in Byzantine political and administrative affairs, and an abbot of his monastery.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_to_the_Slavs">Mission to the Slavs</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Moravia">Great Moravia</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cyril_and_Methodius.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="471" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Cyril_and_Methodius.jpg/170px-Cyril_and_Methodius.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Cyril_and_Methodius.jpg/255px-Cyril_and_Methodius.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Cyril_and_Methodius.jpg/340px-Cyril_and_Methodius.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>In 862, the brothers began the work which would give them their historical importance. That year Prince <a href="/wiki/Rastislav_of_Moravia" title="Rastislav of Moravia">Rastislav</a> of <a href="/wiki/Great_Moravia" title="Great Moravia">Great Moravia</a> requested that Emperor Michael III and the Patriarch Photius send missionaries to evangelize his Slavic subjects. His motives in doing so were probably more political than religious. Rastislav had become king with the support of the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Frankish</a> ruler <a href="/wiki/Louis_the_German" title="Louis the German">Louis the German</a>, but subsequently sought to assert his independence from the Franks. It is a common misconception that Cyril and Methodius were the first to bring Christianity to <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a>, but the letter from Rastislav to Michael III states clearly that Rastislav's people "had already rejected paganism and adhere to the Christian law."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ivanov_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ivanov-15">[15]</a></sup> Rastislav is said to have expelled missionaries of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Church</a> and instead turned to Constantinople for ecclesiastical assistance and, presumably, a degree of political support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brit_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brit-16">[16]</a></sup> The Emperor quickly chose to send Cyril, accompanied by his brother Methodius. The request provided a convenient opportunity to expand Byzantine influence. Their first work seems to have been the training of assistants. In 863, they began the task of translating the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> into the language now known as <a href="/wiki/Old_Church_Slavonic" title="Old Church Slavonic">Old Church Slavonic</a> and travelled to Great Moravia to promote it. They enjoyed considerable success in this endeavour. However, they came into conflict with German ecclesiastics who opposed their efforts to create a specifically Slavic liturgy.</p>
<p>For the purpose of this mission, they devised the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glagolitic_alphabet" title="Glagolitic alphabet">Glagolitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabet</a>, the first alphabet to be used for Slavonic manuscripts. The Glagolitic alphabet was suited to match the specific features of the Slavic language. Its descendant script, the Cyrillic, is still used by many languages today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brit_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brit-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>They wrote the first Slavic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_Code" title="Civil Code">Civil Code</a>, which was used in Great Moravia. The language derived from Old Church Slavonic, known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_Slavonic" title="Church Slavonic">Church Slavonic</a>, is still used in <a href="/wiki/Liturgy" title="Liturgy">liturgy</a> by several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Church" title="Orthodox Church">Orthodox Churches</a> and also in some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic" title="Eastern Catholic">Eastern Catholic</a> churches.</p>
<p>It is impossible to determine with certainty what portions of the Bible the brothers translated. The <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Psalms" title="Psalms">Psalms</a> seem to have been the first, followed by other lessons from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>. The <i>"Translatio"</i> speaks only of a version of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gospels" title="Gospels">Gospels</a> by Cyril, and the <i>"Vita Methodii"</i> only of the <i>"evangelium Slovenicum,"</i> though other liturgical selections may also have been translated.</p>
<p>Nor is it known for sure which liturgy, that of Rome or that of Constantinople, they took as a source. They may well have used the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Roman alphabet</a>, as suggested by liturgical fragments which adhere closely to the Latin type. This view is confirmed by the "Prague Fragments" and by certain Old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glagolitic" title="Glagolitic">Glagolitic</a> liturgical fragments brought from <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">Kiev</a> and discovered there by Saresnewsky—probably the oldest document for the Slavonic tongue; these adhere closely to the Latin type, as is shown by the words <i>"Mass,"</i> <i>"Preface,"</i> and the name of one Felicitas. In any case, the circumstances were such that the brothers could hope for no permanent success without obtaining the authorization of Rome.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Journey_to_Rome">Journey to Rome</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:San_clemente_fresco.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1615" data-file-width="2298" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/San_clemente_fresco.jpg/220px-San_clemente_fresco.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/San_clemente_fresco.jpg/330px-San_clemente_fresco.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/San_clemente_fresco.jpg/440px-San_clemente_fresco.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:San_clemente_fresco.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Saints Cyril and Methodius in Rome. Fresco in San Clemente</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 867, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Nicholas_I" title="Pope Nicholas I">Pope Nicholas I</a> invited the brothers to Rome. Their evangelizing mission in Moravia had by this time become the focus of a dispute with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dietmar_I,_Archbishop_of_Salzburg" title="Dietmar I, Archbishop of Salzburg">Theotmar</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Salzburg" title="Archbishop of Salzburg">Archbishop of Salzburg</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Passau" title="Bishop of Passau">bishop of Passau</a>, who claimed ecclesiastical control of the same territory and wished to see it use the Latin liturgy exclusively. Travelling with the relics of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_I" title="Pope Clement I">Saint Clement</a> and a retinue of disciples, and passing through <a href="/wiki/Pannonia" title="Pannonia">Pannonia</a> (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balaton_Principality" title="Balaton Principality">Balaton Principality</a>), where they were well received by Prince <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koce%C4%BE" title="Koceľ">Koceľ</a> (Kocelj, Kozel), they arrived in Rome in 868, where they were warmly received. This was partly due to their bringing with them the relics of Saint Clement; the rivalry with Constantinople as to the jurisdiction over the territory of the Slavs would incline Rome to value the brothers and their influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brit_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brit-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The brothers were praised for their learning and cultivated for their influence in Constantinople. <a href="/wiki/Anastasius_Bibliothecarius" title="Anastasius Bibliothecarius">Anastasius Bibliothecarius</a> would later call Cyril "a man of apostolic life" and "a man of great wisdom".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Their project in Moravia found support from <a href="/wiki/Pope_Adrian_II" title="Pope Adrian II">Pope Adrian II</a>, who formally authorized the use of the new Slavic liturgy. Subsequently Methodius was ordained as priest by the pope himself, and five Slavic disciples were ordained as priests (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Gorazd&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saint Gorazd (page does not exist)">Saint Gorazd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clement_of_Ohrid" title="Clement of Ohrid">Saint Clement of Ohrid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Naum" title="Saint Naum">Saint Naum</a>) and as deacons (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Angelar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saint Angelar (page does not exist)">Saint Angelar</a> and Saint Sava) by the prominent bishops <a href="/wiki/Pope_Formosus" title="Pope Formosus">Formosus</a> and Gauderic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The newly made priests officiated in their own languages at the altars of some of the principal churches. Feeling his end approaching, Cyril became a monk, was given the new name Cyril,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and died in Rome fifty days later (14 February 869). There is some question as to assertion of the <i>Translatio</i> (ix.) that he was made a bishop.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodius_alone">Methodius alone</span></h3>
<p>Methodius now continued the work among the Slavs alone; not at first in Great Moravia, but in Pannonia (in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balaton_Principality" title="Balaton Principality">Balaton Principality</a>), owing to the political circumstances of the former country, where Rastislav had been taken captive by his nephew <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Svatopluk_I" title="Svatopluk I">Svatopluk</a>, then delivered over to Carloman, and condemned in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reichstag_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" title="Reichstag (Holy Roman Empire)">diet</a> of the empire at the end of 870.</p>
<p>Friendly relations had been established with Koceľ on the journey to Rome. This activity in Pannonia made a conflict inevitable with the German episcopate, and especially with the bishop of <a href="/wiki/Salzburg" title="Salzburg">Salzburg</a>, to whose jurisdiction Pannonia had belonged for seventy-five years. In 865 Bishop Adalwin is found exercising all Episcopal rights there, and the administration under him was in the hands of the archpriest Riehbald. The latter was obliged to retire to Salzburg, but his superior was naturally disinclined to abandon his claims. Methodius sought support from Rome; the <i>Vita</i> asserts that Koceľ sent him thither with an honorable escort to receive Episcopal consecration.</p>
<p>The letter given as Adrian's in chap. viii., with its approval of the Slavonic mass, is a pure invention. The pope named Methodius archbishop of Sirmium with jurisdiction over Great Moravia and Pannonia, thus superseding the claims of Salzburg by an older title. The statement of the <i>"Vita"</i> that Methodius was made bishop in 870 and not raised to the dignity of an archbishop until 873 is contradicted by the brief of <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_VIII" title="Pope John VIII">Pope John VIII</a>, written in June 879, according to which Adrian consecrated him archbishop; John includes in his jurisdiction not only Great Moravia and Pannonia, but Serbia as well.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodius.27_final_years">Methodius' final years</span></h3>
<p>The archiepiscopal claims of Methodius were considered such an injury to the rights of Salzburg that he was forced to answer for them at a synod held at <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a> in the presence of King Louis. The assembly, after a heated discussion, declared the deposition of the intruder, and ordered him to be sent to Germany, where he was kept prisoner in <a href="/wiki/Ellwangen" title="Ellwangen">Ellwangen</a> for two and a half years. In spite of the strong representations of the <i><a href="/wiki/Conversio_Bagoariorum_et_Carantanorum" title="Conversio Bagoariorum et Carantanorum">Conversio Bagoariorum et Carantanorum</a></i>, written in 871 to influence the pope, though not avowing this purpose, Rome declared emphatically for Methodius, and sent a bishop, Paul of Ancons, to reinstate him and punish his enemies, after which both parties were commanded to appear in Rome with the legate.</p>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stanislav_Dospavski_-_Saints_Cyril_and_Methodius.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">Saint</a> Cyril and Methodius by <a href="/wiki/Stanislav_Dospevski" title="Stanislav Dospevski">Stanislav Dospevski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgarian</a> painter</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The papal will prevailed, and Methodius secured his freedom and his archiepiscopal authority over both Great Moravia and Pannonia, though the use of Slavonic for the mass was still denied to him. His authority was restricted in Pannonia when after Koceľ's death the principality was administered by German nobles; but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Svatopluk_I" title="Svatopluk I">Svatopluk</a> now ruled with practical independence in Great Moravia, and expelled the German clergy. This apparently secured an undisturbed field of operation for Methodius, and the <i>Vita</i> (x.) depicts the next few years (873–879) as a period of fruitful progress. Methodius seems to have disregarded, wholly or in part, the prohibition of the Slavonic liturgy; and when <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Frankish</a> clerics again found their way into the country, and the archbishop's strictness had displeased the licentious Svatopluk, this was made a cause of complaint against him at Rome, coupled with charges regarding the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filioque_clause" title="Filioque clause">Filioque</a></i>.</p>
<p>Methodius vindicated his orthodoxy at Rome, the more easily as the creed was still recited there without the <i>Filioque</i>, and promised to obey in regard to the liturgy. The other party was conciliated by giving him a <a href="/wiki/Swabia" title="Swabia">Swabian</a>, Wiching, as his coadjutor. When relations were strained between the two, John VIII steadfastly supported Methodius; but after his death (December 882) the archbishop's position became insecure, and his need of support induced Goetz to accept the statement of the <i>Vita</i> (xiii.) that he went to visit the Eastern emperor.</p>
<p>It was not until after Methodius' death, which is placed on 6 April 885,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> that the animosity erupted into an open conflict. Gorazd, whom Methodius had designated as his successor, was not recognised by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Stephen_V" title="Pope Stephen V">Pope Stephen V</a>. The same Pope forbade the use of the Slavic liturgy<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and placed the infamous Wiching as Methodius' successor. The latter exiled the disciples of the two brothers from Great Moravia in 885. They fled to the <a href="/wiki/First_Bulgarian_Empire" title="First Bulgarian Empire">First Bulgarian Empire</a>, where they were welcomed and commissioned to establish theological schools. There they devised the <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic script</a> on the basis of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glagolitic_alphabet" title="Glagolitic alphabet">Glagolitic</a>. Cyrillic gradually replaced Glagolitic as the alphabet of the Old Church Slavonic language, which became the official language of the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Empire" title="Bulgarian Empire">Bulgarian Empire</a> and later spread to the Eastern Slav lands of <a href="/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27" title="Kievan Rus'">Kievan Rus'</a>. Cyrillic eventually spread throughout most of the Slavic world to become the standard alphabet in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox</a> Slavic countries. Hence, Cyril and Methodius' efforts also paved the way for the spread of Christianity throughout Eastern Europe.</p>
<p>Methodius' body was buried in the main cathedral church of Great Moravia. Until today remains an open question which city was capital of Great Moravia and therefore the place of Methodius' eternal rest remains unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Invention_of_the_Glagolitic_and_Cyrillic_alphabets">Invention of the Glagolitic and Cyrillic alphabets</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bascanska_ploca.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="509" data-file-width="900" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Bascanska_ploca.jpg/220px-Bascanska_ploca.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Bascanska_ploca.jpg/330px-Bascanska_ploca.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Bascanska_ploca.jpg/440px-Bascanska_ploca.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bascanska_ploca.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Ba%C5%A1ka_tablet" title="Baška tablet">Baška tablet</a> is an early example of the Glagolitic from <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Glagolitic and Cyrillic alphabets are the oldest known Slavic <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabets</a>, and were created by the two brothers and their students, to translate the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> and other texts into the <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic languages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BritGlago_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-BritGlago-23">[23]</a></sup> The early Glagolitic alphabet was used in Great Moravia between 863 (the arrival of Cyril and Methodius) and 885 (the expulsion of their students) for government and religious documents and books, and at the Great Moravian Academy (<i>Veľkomoravské učilište</i>) founded by Cyril, where followers of Cyril and Methodius were educated, by Methodius himself among others. The alphabet has been traditionally attributed to Cyril. That attribution has been confirmed explicitly by the papal letter <i>Industriae tuae</i> (880) approving the use of Old Church Slavonic, which says that the alphabet was "invented by Constantine the Philosopher". The term invention need not exclude the possibility of the brothers having made use of earlier letters, but implies only that before that time the Slavic languages had no distinct script of their own.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Early_Cyrillic_alphabet" title="Early Cyrillic alphabet">early Cyrillic alphabet</a> was developed in the First Bulgarian Empire<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paul_Cubberley_1996_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paul_Cubberley_1996-24">[24]</a></sup> and later finalized and spread by disciples Kliment and Naum in the Ohrid and Preslav schools of Tsar Boris' Bulgaria<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> as a simplification of the Glagolitic alphabet which more closely resembled the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> alphabet. It was developed by the disciples of Saints Cyril and Methodius at the <a href="/wiki/Preslav_Literary_School" title="Preslav Literary School">Preslav Literary School</a> at the end of the 9th century.</p>
<p>After the death of Cyril, <a href="/wiki/Clement_of_Ohrid" title="Clement of Ohrid">Clement of Ohrid</a> accompanied Methodius from Rome to Pannonia and Great Moravia. After the death of Methodius in 885, Clement headed the struggle against the German clergy in Great Moravia along with Gorazd. After spending some time in jail, he was expelled from Great Moravia, and in 885 or 886 reached the borders of the Bulgarian Empire together with <a href="/wiki/Saint_Naum" title="Saint Naum">Naum</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preslav" title="Preslav">Preslav</a>, Angelarius, and possibly Gorazd (according to other sources, Gorazd was already dead by that time). The four of them were afterwards sent to the Bulgarian capital of <a href="/wiki/Pliska" title="Pliska">Pliska</a>, where they were commissioned by Tsar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boris_I" title="Boris I">Boris I</a> of Bulgaria to instruct the future clergy of the state in the Slavonic language.</p>
<p>After the adoption of Christianity in 865, religious ceremonies in Bulgaria were conducted in Greek by clergy sent from the Byzantine Empire. Fearing growing Byzantine influence and weakening of the state, Boris viewed the adoption of the Old Slavonic language as a way to preserve the political independence and stability of Bulgaria, so he established two literary schools (academies), in Pliska and <a href="/wiki/Ohrid" title="Ohrid">Ohrid</a>, where theology was to be taught in the Slavonic language. While <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naum_of_Preslav" title="Naum of Preslav">Naum of Preslav</a> stayed in Pliska working on the foundation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pliska_Literary_School" title="Pliska Literary School">Pliska Literary School</a>, Clement was commissioned by Boris I to organise the teaching of theology to future clergymen in Old Church Slavonic at the <a href="/wiki/Ohrid_Literary_School" title="Ohrid Literary School">Ohrid Literary School</a>. For seven years (886-893) Clement taught some 3,500 students in the Slavonic language and the Glagolitic alphabet.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commemoration">Commemoration</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saints_Cyril_and_Methodius.27_Day">Saints Cyril and Methodius' Day</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cross_Procession_in_Novosibirsk_04.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Cross_Procession_in_Novosibirsk_04.jpg/250px-Cross_Procession_in_Novosibirsk_04.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Cross_Procession_in_Novosibirsk_04.jpg/375px-Cross_Procession_in_Novosibirsk_04.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Cross_Procession_in_Novosibirsk_04.jpg/500px-Cross_Procession_in_Novosibirsk_04.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cross_Procession_in_Novosibirsk_04.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Saints Cyril and Methodius procession</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Canonization" title="Canonization">canonization</a> process was much more relaxed in the decades following Cyril's death than today. Cyril was regarded by his disciples as a saint soon after his death. His following spread among the nations he evangelized and subsequently to the wider Christian Church, and he was famous as a holy man, along with his brother Methodius. There were calls for Cyril's canonization from the crowds lining the Roman streets during his funeral procession. The brothers' first appearance in a papal document is in <i>Grande Munus</i> of Leo XIII in 1880. They are known as the "Apostles of the Slavs", and are still highly regarded by both Roman Catholic and Orthodox Christians. Their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feast_day" title="Feast day">feast day</a> is currently celebrated on 14 February in the Roman Catholic Church (to coincide with the date of St Cyril's death); on 11 May in the Eastern Orthodox Church (though for Eastern Orthodox Churches which use the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julian_Calendar" title="Julian Calendar">Julian Calendar</a> this is 24 May according to the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a>); and on 7 July according to the old sanctoral calendar that existed before the revisions of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council</a>. The celebration also commemorates the introduction of literacy and the preaching of the gospels in the Slavonic language by the brothers. The brothers were declared "Patrons of Europe" in 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to old Bulgarian chronicles, the day of the holy brothers was celebrated ecclesiastically as early as the 11th century. The first recorded secular celebration of Saints Cyril and Methodius' Day as the "Day of the Bulgarian script", as traditionally accepted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulgarian_history" title="Bulgarian history">Bulgarian history</a>, was held in the town of <a href="/wiki/Plovdiv" title="Plovdiv">Plovdiv</a> on 11 May 1851, when a local Bulgarian school was named "Saints Cyril and Methodius": both acts on the initiative of the prominent Bulgarian educator <a href="/wiki/Nayden_Gerov" title="Nayden Gerov">Nayden Gerov</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> although an Armenian traveller mentioned his visit to the "celebration of the Bulgarian script" in the town of <a href="/wiki/Shumen" title="Shumen">Shumen</a> on 22 May 1803.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The day is now celebrated as a <a href="/wiki/Public_holiday" title="Public holiday">public holiday</a> in the following countries:</p>
<ul>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a> it is celebrated on 24 May and is known as the "Bulgarian Education and Culture, and Slavonic Literature Day" (Bulgarian: Ден на българската просвета и култура и на славянската писменост), a <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_Bulgaria" title="Public holidays in Bulgaria">national holiday</a> celebrating <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Bulgaria" title="Culture of Bulgaria">Bulgarian culture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_literature" title="Bulgarian literature">literature</a> as well as the alphabet. It is also known as "Alphabet, Culture, and Education Day" (Bulgarian: Ден на азбуката, културата и просвещението). Saints Cyril and Methodius are patrons of the National Library of Bulgaria. There is a monument to them in front of the library. Saints Cyril and Methodius are the most celebrated saints in the Bulgarian Orthodox church, and icons of the two brothers can be found in every church.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Macedonia" title="Republic of Macedonia">Republic of Macedonia</a>, it is celebrated on 24 May and is known as the "Saints Cyril and Methodius, Slavonic Enlighteners' Day" (<a href="/wiki/Macedonian_language" title="Macedonian language">Macedonian</a>: <span lang="mk" xml:lang="mk">Св. Кирил и Методиј, Ден на словенските просветители</span>), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_the_Republic_of_Macedonia" title="Public holidays in the Republic of Macedonia">national holiday</a>. The Government of the Republic of Macedonia enacted a statute of the national holiday in October 2006 and the Parliament of the Republic of Macedonia passed a corresponding law at the beginning of 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Previously it had only been celebrated in the schools. It is also known as the day of the "<a href="/wiki/Thessaloniki" title="Thessaloniki">Solun</a> Brothers" (<a href="/wiki/Macedonian_language" title="Macedonian language">Macedonian</a>: <span lang="mk" xml:lang="mk">Солунските браќа</span>).</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, the two brothers were originally commemorated on 9 March, but <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_IX" title="Pope Pius IX">Pope Pius IX</a> changed this date to 5 July for several reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CMHoliday_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-CMHoliday-30">[30]</a></sup> Today, Saints Cyril and Methodius are revered there as national saints and their name day (5 July), "Sts Cyril and Methodius Day" is a <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_the_Czech_Republic" title="Public holidays in the Czech Republic">national holiday in Czech Republic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_holidays_in_Slovakia" title="National holidays in Slovakia">Slovakia</a>. In the Czech Republic it is celebrated as "Slavic Missionaries Cyril and Methodius Day" (Czech: Den slovanských věrozvěstů Cyrila a Metoděje); in <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a> it is celebrated as "St. Cyril and Metod Day" (Slovak: Sviatok svätého Cyrila a Metoda).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CMHoliday_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-CMHoliday-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, it is celebrated on 24 May and is known as the "Slavonic Literature and Culture Day" (Russian: День славянской письменности и культуры), celebrating Slavonic culture and literature as well as the alphabet. Its celebration is ecclesiastical (11 May in the Church's <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a>). It is not a public holiday in Russia.</li>
</ul>
<p>The saints' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feast_day" title="Feast day">feast day</a> is celebrated by the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a> on 11 May and by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a> on 14 February as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saints_Cyril_and_Methodius_Day" title="Saints Cyril and Methodius Day">Saints Cyril and Methodius Day</a>". The <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheran Churches</a> commemorate the two saints either on 14 February or 11 May.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_commemoration">Other commemoration</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/SS._Cyril_and_Methodius_National_Library" title="SS. Cyril and Methodius National Library">The national library of Bulgaria</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ss._Cyril_and_Methodius_University_of_Skopje" title="Ss. Cyril and Methodius University of Skopje">Ss. Cyril and Methodius University of Skopje</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Macedonia" title="Republic of Macedonia">Republic of Macedonia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Veliko_Tarnovo_University" title="Veliko Tarnovo University">St. Cyril and St. Methodius University of Veliko Tarnovo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Trnava" title="Trnava">Trnava</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, bear the name of the two saints. Faculty of Theology at <a href="/wiki/Palack%C3%BD_University,_Olomouc" title="Palacký University, Olomouc">Palacký University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Olomouc" title="Olomouc">Olomouc</a> (<a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>), bears the name <i>"Saints Cyril and Methodius Faculty of Theology"</i>. In the United States, <a href="/wiki/SS._Cyril_and_Methodius_Seminary" title="SS. Cyril and Methodius Seminary">SS. Cyril and Methodius Seminary</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orchard_Lake,_Michigan" title="Orchard Lake, Michigan">Orchard Lake, Michigan</a>, bears their name.</p>
<p>Saints Cyril and Methodius are the main patron saints of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archdiocese_of_Ljubljana" title="Archdiocese of Ljubljana">Archdiocese of Ljubljana</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ljubljana_Cathedral" title="Ljubljana Cathedral">Ljubljana Cathedral</a> stands at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cyril_and_Methodius_Square&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cyril and Methodius Square (page does not exist)">Cyril and Methodius Square</a> (<a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">Slovene</a>: <span lang="sl" xml:lang="sl"><i>Ciril–Metodov trg</i></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> They are also patron saints of the <a href="/wiki/Slovak_Catholic_Eparchy_of_Ko%C5%A1ice" title="Slovak Catholic Eparchy of Košice">Byzantine Catholic Eparchy of Košice</a> (Slovakia)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grkatke-hist_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-grkatke-hist-3">[3]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eparchy_of_Saints_Cyril_and_Methodius_of_Toronto" title="Eparchy of Saints Cyril and Methodius of Toronto">Slovak Greek Catholic Eparchy of Toronto</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/St._Cyril_Peak" title="St. Cyril Peak">St. Cyril Peak</a> and <a href="/wiki/St._Methodius_Peak" title="St. Methodius Peak">St. Methodius Peak</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Tangra_Mountains" title="Tangra Mountains">Tangra Mountains</a> on <a href="/wiki/Livingston_Island" title="Livingston Island">Livingston Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Shetland_Islands" title="South Shetland Islands">South Shetland Islands</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> are named for the brothers.</p>
<p>Saint Cyril's remains are interred in a shrine-chapel within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilica_di_San_Clemente" title="Basilica di San Clemente">Basilica di San Clemente</a> in Rome. The chapel holds a Madonna by <a href="/wiki/Sassoferrato" title="Sassoferrato">Sassoferrato</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Saints_Cyril_and_Methodius_(Danville,_Pennsylvania)" title="Basilica of Saints Cyril and Methodius (Danville, Pennsylvania)">Basilica of SS. Cyril and Methodius</a> in <a href="/wiki/Danville,_Pennsylvania" title="Danville, Pennsylvania">Danville, Pennsylvania</a>, (the only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a> dedicated to SS. Cyril and Methodius in the world) is the motherhouse chapel of the Sisters of SS. Cyril and Methodius, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> women's religious community of pontifical rite dedicated to apostolic works of ecumenism, education, evangelization, and elder care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
