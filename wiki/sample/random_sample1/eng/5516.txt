<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Metonic cycle</h1>

<p>For <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calendar" title="Calendar">calendar</a> studies, the <b>Metonic cycle</b> or <b>Enneadecaeteris</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἐννεακαιδεκαετηρίς</span>, "nineteen years") is a period of very close to 19 years that is remarkable for being nearly a <a href="/wiki/Least_common_multiple" title="Least common multiple">common multiple</a> of the solar <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">year</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Month#Synodic_month" title="Month">synodic (lunar) month</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> <a href="/wiki/Meton_of_Athens" title="Meton of Athens">Meton of Athens</a> (fifth century BC) observed that a period of 19 years is almost exactly equal to 235 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synodic" title="Synodic">synodic</a> months and, rounded to full days, counts 6,940 days. The difference between the two periods (of 19 years and 235 synodic months) is only a few hours, depending on the definition of the year.</p>
<p>Considering a year to be <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>19</sub></span> of this 6,940-day cycle gives a year length of 365 + <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span> + <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>76</sub></span> days (the unrounded cycle is much more accurate), which is about 11 days more than 12 synodic months. To keep a 12-month lunar year in pace with the solar year, an <a href="/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping)" title="Intercalation (timekeeping)">intercalary</a> 13th month would have to be added on seven occasions during the nineteen-year period (235 = 19 × 12 + 7). When Meton introduced the cycle around 432 BC, it was already known by Babylonian astronomers.</p>
<p>A mechanical computation of the cycle is built into the <a href="/wiki/Antikythera_mechanism" title="Antikythera mechanism">Antikythera mechanism</a>.</p>
<p>The cycle was used in the <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_calendar" title="Babylonian calendar">Babylonian calendar</a>, ancient Chinese calendar systems (the 'Rule Cycle' 章) and the medieval <a href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus">computus</a> (i.e. the calculation of the date of <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>). It regulates the 19-year cycle of intercalary months of the modern <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">Hebrew calendar</a>. The start of the Metonic cycle depends on which of these systems is being used; for Easter, the first year of the current Metonic cycle is 2014.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematical_basis">Mathematical basis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metonic_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mathematical basis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the time of Meton, <a href="/wiki/Axial_precession" title="Axial precession">axial precession</a> had not yet been discovered, and he could not distinguish between <a href="/wiki/Sidereal_year" title="Sidereal year">sidereal years</a> (currently: 365.256363 days) and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">tropical years</a> (currently: 365.242190 days). Most calendars, like the commonly used <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a>, are based on the tropical year and maintain the seasons at the same calendar times each year. Nineteen tropical years are about two hours shorter than 235 synodic months. The Metonic cycle's error is, therefore, one full day every 219 years, or 12.4 parts per million.</p>
<dl>
<dd>19 <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">tropical years</a> = 6,939.602 days (12 × 354-day years + 7 × 384-day years + 3.6 days).</dd>
<dd>235 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synodic_month" title="Synodic month">synodic months</a> (lunar phases) = 6,939.688 days (Metonic period by definition).</dd>
<dd>254 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidereal_month" title="Sidereal month">sidereal months</a> (lunar orbits) = 6,939.702 days (19 + 235 = 254).</dd>
<dd>255 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Draconic_month" title="Draconic month">draconic months</a> (lunar nodes) = 6,939.1161 days.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Note that the 19-year cycle is also close (to somewhat more than half a day) to 255 draconic months, so it is also an <a href="/wiki/Eclipse_cycle" title="Eclipse cycle">eclipse cycle</a>, which lasts only for about 4 or 5 recurrences of eclipses. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Octon_(eclipse_cycle)" title="Octon (eclipse cycle)">Octon</a> is <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>5</sub></span> of a Metonic cycle (47 synodic months, 3.8 years), and it recurs about 20 to 25 cycles.</p>
<p>This cycle seems to be a coincidence. The periods of the Moon's orbit around the Earth and the Earth's orbit around the Sun are believed to be independent, and not to have any known physical <a href="/wiki/Resonance" title="Resonance">resonance</a>. An example of a non-coincidental cycle is the orbit of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>, with its 3:2 <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)#Spin.E2.80.93orbit_resonance" title="Mercury (planet)">spin-orbit resonance</a>.</p>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_year" title="Lunar year">lunar year</a> of 12 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synodic_month#Synodic_month" title="Synodic month">synodic months</a> is about 354 days, approximately 11 days short of the "365-day" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_year" title="Solar year">solar year</a>. Therefore, for a <a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">lunisolar calendar</a>, every 2 to 3 years there is a difference of more than a full lunar month between the lunar and solar years, and an extra (<i>embolismic</i>) month needs to be inserted (<a href="/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping)" title="Intercalation (timekeeping)">intercalation</a>). The Athenians initially seem not to have had a regular means of intercalating a 13th month; instead, the question of when to add a month was decided by an official. Meton's discovery made it possible to propose a regular intercalation scheme. The Babylonians seem to have introduced this scheme around 500 BC, thus well before Meton.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_in_traditional_calendars">Application in traditional calendars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metonic_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Application in traditional calendars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Traditionally, for the <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_calendar" title="Babylonian calendar">Babylonian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">Hebrew</a> <a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">lunisolar calendars</a>, the years 3, 6, 8, 11, 14, 17, and 19 are the long (13-month) years of the Metonic cycle. This cycle, which can be used to predict <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">eclipses</a>, forms the basis of the Greek and Hebrew calendars, and is used for the <a href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus">computation of the date of Easter</a> each year.</p>
<p>The Babylonians applied the 19-year cycle since the late sixth century BC. As they measured the moon's motion against the stars, the 235:19 relationship may originally have referred to <a href="/wiki/Sidereal_year" title="Sidereal year">sidereal years</a>, instead of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">tropical years</a> as it has been used for various calendars.</p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Livy" title="Livy">Livy</a>, the king of Rome <a href="/wiki/Numa_Pompilius" title="Numa Pompilius">Numa Pompilius</a> (753-673 BC) inserted intercalary months in such a way that <i>in the twentieth year the days should fall in with the same position of the sun from which they had started.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>As <i>the twentieth year</i> takes place nineteen years after <i>the first year</i>, this seems to indicate that the Metonic cycle was applied to Numa's calendar.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> was said to have visited the <a href="/wiki/Hyperborea" title="Hyperborea">Hyperboreans</a> once every 19 years, presumably at the high point of the cycle.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Runic_calendar" title="Runic calendar">Runic calendar</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Perpetual_calendar" title="Perpetual calendar">perpetual calendar</a> based on the 19-year-long Metonic cycle. Also known as a Rune staff or Runic Almanac, it appears to have been a medieval Swedish invention. This calendar does not rely on knowledge of the duration of the tropical year or of the occurrence of leap years. It is set at the beginning of each year by observing the first full moon after the winter solstice. The oldest one known, and the only one from the Middle Ages, is the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nyk%C3%B6ping_staff&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nyköping staff (page does not exist)">Nyköping staff</a>, which is believed to date from the 13th century.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_calendar" title="Bahá'í calendar">Bahá'í calendar</a>, established during the middle of the 19th century, is also based on cycles of 19 years.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_details">Further details</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metonic_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Further details">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Metonic cycle is related to two less accurate subcycles:</p>
<ul>
<li>8 years = 99 lunations (an <a href="/wiki/Octaeteris" title="Octaeteris">Octaeteris</a>) to within 1.5 days, i.e. an error of one day in 5 years; and</li>
<li>11 years = 136 lunations within 1.5 days, i.e. an error of one day in 7.3 years.</li>
</ul>
<p>By combining appropriate numbers of 11-year and 19-year periods, it is possible to generate ever more accurate cycles. For example, simple arithmetic shows that:</p>
<ul>
<li>687 tropical years = 250,921.39 days;</li>
<li>8,497 lunations = 250,921.41 days.</li>
</ul>
<p>This gives an error of only about half an hour in 687 years (2.5 seconds a year), although this is subject to secular variation in the length of the tropical year and the lunation.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Meton_of_Athens" title="Meton of Athens">Meton of Athens</a> approximated the cycle to a whole number (6,940) of days, obtained by 125 long months of 30 days and 110 short months of 29 days. During the next century, <a href="/wiki/Callippus" title="Callippus">Callippus</a> developed the <a href="/wiki/Callippic_cycle" title="Callippic cycle">Callippic cycle</a> of four 19-year periods for a 76-year cycle with a mean year of exactly 365.25 days.</p>
