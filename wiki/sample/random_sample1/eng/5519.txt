<p>The <b>Metropolitan Transportation Authority</b> (<b>MTA</b>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_state_public_benefit_corporations" title="New York state public benefit corporations">public benefit corporation</a> responsible for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_transportation" title="Public transportation">public transportation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, serving 12 counties in <a href="/wiki/Downstate_New_York" title="Downstate New York">Downstate New York</a>, along with two counties in southwestern <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a> under contract to the <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Department_of_Transportation" title="Connecticut Department of Transportation">Connecticut Department of Transportation</a>, carrying over 11 million passengers on an average weekday systemwide, and over 800,000 vehicles on its <a href="/wiki/MTA_Bridges_and_Tunnels" title="MTA Bridges and Tunnels">seven toll bridges and two tunnels</a> per weekday. MTA is the largest public transit authority in the United States.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding">Founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_Transportation_Authority&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chartered by the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Legislature" title="New York State Legislature">New York State Legislature</a> in 1965 as the <b>Metropolitan Commuter Transportation Authority</b> (MCTA), it was initially created by Governor <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Rockefeller" title="Nelson Rockefeller">Nelson Rockefeller</a> to purchase and operate the bankrupt <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Rail_Road" title="Long Island Rail Road">Long Island Rail Road</a>. The MCTA dropped the word "Commuter" from its name and became the <b>Metropolitan Transportation Authority</b> (MTA) on March 1, 1968 when it took over operations of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Transit_Authority" title="New York City Transit Authority">New York City Transit Authority</a> (NYCTA) (now <b>MTA New York City Transit</b> (NYCT)) and <a href="/wiki/MTA_Bridges_and_Tunnels" title="MTA Bridges and Tunnels">Triborough Bridge and Tunnel Authority</a> (TBTA) (now <b>MTA Bridges and Tunnels</b> (B&amp;T)).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QnsGazette-QnsPubTrans-51stBDay-2015_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-QnsGazette-QnsPubTrans-51stBDay-2015-2">[2]</a></sup> The construction of two bridges over the Long Island Sound was put under the jurisdiction of the MTA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The agency also entered into a long-term lease of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penn_Central_Transportation" title="Penn Central Transportation">Penn Central Transportation</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Line_(Metro-North)" title="Hudson Line (Metro-North)">Hudson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harlem_Line" title="Harlem Line">Harlem</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Haven_Line" title="New Haven Line">New Haven</a> <a href="/wiki/Commuter_rail" title="Commuter rail">commuter rail</a> lines,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QnsGazette-QnsPubTrans-51stBDay-2015_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-QnsGazette-QnsPubTrans-51stBDay-2015-2">[2]</a></sup> contracting their subsidized operation to Penn Central, until that company's operations were folded into <a href="/wiki/Conrail" title="Conrail">Conrail</a> in 1976. The MTA took over full operations in 1983, as the <a href="/wiki/Metro-North_Railroad" title="Metro-North Railroad">Metro-North Commuter Railroad</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QnsGazette-QnsPubTrans-51stBDay-2015_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-QnsGazette-QnsPubTrans-51stBDay-2015-2">[2]</a></sup> Governor Rockefeller appointed his top aide, Dr. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_J._Ronan" title="William J. Ronan">William J. Ronan</a>, as Chairman and Chief Executive Officer. Dr. Ronan served in this post until 1974.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Responsibilities_and_service_area">Responsibilities and service area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_Transportation_Authority&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Responsibilities and service area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NYCB_3491_(9190610031).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1110" data-file-width="1920" height="127" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/NYCB_3491_%289190610031%29.jpg/220px-NYCB_3491_%289190610031%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/NYCB_3491_%289190610031%29.jpg/330px-NYCB_3491_%289190610031%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/NYCB_3491_%289190610031%29.jpg/440px-NYCB_3491_%289190610031%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NYCB_3491_(9190610031).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Thomas F. Prendergast, current Chairman and CEO of MTA</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The MTA has the responsibility for developing and implementing a unified mass transportation policy for the <a href="/wiki/New_York_metropolitan_area" title="New York metropolitan area">New York metropolitan area</a>, including all five boroughs of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and the suburban counties of <a href="/wiki/Dutchess_County,_New_York" title="Dutchess County, New York">Dutchess</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nassau_County,_New_York" title="Nassau County, New York">Nassau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orange_County,_New_York" title="Orange County, New York">Orange</a>, <a href="/wiki/Putnam_County,_New_York" title="Putnam County, New York">Putnam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rockland_County,_New_York" title="Rockland County, New York">Rockland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suffolk_County,_New_York" title="Suffolk County, New York">Suffolk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Westchester_County,_New_York" title="Westchester County, New York">Westchester</a>, all of which together are the "Metropolitan Commuter Transportation District (MCTD)". Thomas F. Prendergast is currently the Chairman &amp; CEO of the MTA.</p>
<p>The MTA's immediate past chairpersons were: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_J._Ronan" title="William J. Ronan">William J. Ronan</a> (1965–1974), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=David_Yunich&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="David Yunich (page does not exist)">David Yunich</a> (1974–1975), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Harold_Fisher_(MTA)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Harold Fisher (MTA) (page does not exist)">Harold Fisher</a> (1975–1979), <a href="/wiki/Richard_Ravitch" title="Richard Ravitch">Richard Ravitch</a> (1979–1983), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Kiley" title="Robert Kiley">Robert Kiley</a> (1983–1991), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Stangl&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peter Stangl (page does not exist)">Peter Stangl</a> (1991–1995), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Virgil_Conway&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Virgil Conway (page does not exist)">Virgil Conway</a> (1995–2001), <a href="/wiki/Peter_S._Kalikow" title="Peter S. Kalikow">Peter S. Kalikow</a> (2001–2007), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=H._Dale_Hemmerdinger&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="H. Dale Hemmerdinger (page does not exist)">H. Dale Hemmerdinger</a> (2007–2009), <a href="/wiki/Jay_Walder" title="Jay Walder">Jay Walder</a> (2009–2011), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Lhota" title="Joseph Lhota">Joseph Lhota</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (2012).</p>
<p>The MTA is the largest regional public transportation provider in the Western Hemisphere. Its agencies serve a region of approximately 14.6 million people spread over 5,000 square miles (13,000 km<sup>2</sup>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Teodorovic_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Teodorovic-5">[5]</a></sup> in 12 counties in New York and two in Connecticut. MTA agencies now move more than 8.5 million customers per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Teodorovic_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Teodorovic-5">[5]</a></sup> (translating to 2.6 billion rail and bus customers a year) and employ approximately 65,000 workers. The MTA's systems carry over 11 million passengers on an average weekday systemwide, and over 800,000 vehicles on its <a href="/wiki/MTA_Bridges_and_Tunnels" title="MTA Bridges and Tunnels">seven toll bridges and two tunnels</a> per weekday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsidiaries_and_affiliates">Subsidiaries and affiliates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_Transportation_Authority&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subsidiaries and affiliates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>MTA carries out these planning and other responsibilities both directly and through its subsidiaries and affiliates, and provides oversight to these subordinate agencies, known collectively as "The Related Entities".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mta_related_entities_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-mta_related_entities-7">[7]</a></sup> The Related Entities represent a number of previously existing agencies which have come under the MTA umbrella. In turn, these previously existing agencies were (with the exception of <a href="/wiki/MTA_Bridges_and_Tunnels" title="MTA Bridges and Tunnels">MTA Bridges and Tunnels</a> and <a href="/wiki/MTA_Capital_Construction" title="MTA Capital Construction">MTA Capital Construction</a>) successors to the property of private companies that provided substantially the same services.</p>
<p>Each of these Related Entities has a <i>popular name</i> and in some cases, a former, <i>legal name</i>. The popular names were part of an overall corporate identification effort in 1994 to eliminate the confusion over the affiliations of the various "authorities" that were part of the MTA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_19940828_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt_19940828-8">[8]</a></sup> Legal names have since only been used for legal documents, such as contracts, and have not been used publicly; however, since the mid-2000s, the popular name has also been used for legal documents related to contract procurements where the legal name was used heretofore. Both are listed below.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_Transportation_Authority&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The MTA is governed by a 19-member board representing the 5 boroughs of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and each of the counties in its New York State service area.</p>
<p>Five members, in addition to the Chairman and CEO, are directly nominated by the <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_New_York" title="Governor of New York">Governor of New York</a>, with four recommended by New York City’s mayor, and one each by the county executives of Nassau, Suffolk and Westchester counties. Each of these members has one vote.</p>
<p>The county executives of <a href="/wiki/Dutchess_County,_New_York" title="Dutchess County, New York">Dutchess</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orange_County,_New_York" title="Orange County, New York">Orange</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rockland_County" title="Rockland County">Rockland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Putnam_County,_New_York" title="Putnam County, New York">Putnam</a> counties also nominate one member each, but these members cast one collective vote. The Board also has six rotating nonvoting seats held by representatives of MTA employee organized labor and the Permanent Citizens Advisory Committee, which serves as a voice for users of MTA transit and commuter facilities.</p>
<p>All board members are confirmed by the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Senate" title="New York State Senate">New York State Senate</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_animal_policy">Service animal policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_Transportation_Authority&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Service animal policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Service animals, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guide_dogs" title="Guide dogs">guide dogs</a>, are allowed on MTA public transportation in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Americans_with_Disabilities_Act_of_1990" title="Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990">Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990</a> (ADA), if the dog meets the law's definition of a service animal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Federal_Court" title="Brooklyn Federal Court">Brooklyn Federal Court</a> judge ordered the MTA to pay a 70-year-old Manhattan woman $150,000 to settle a lawsuit over her service animal. The judge ruled that the MTA had violated the ADA when its drivers, motormen, and conductors had either denied the woman access to transportation with her dog or improperly demanded to see its papers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nydailynews2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-nydailynews2-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues">Issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_Transportation_Authority&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Budget_gaps">Budget gaps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_Transportation_Authority&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Budget gaps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The budget deficit of the MTA is a growing crisis for the organization as well as New York City and State residents and legislature. The MTA held $31 billion in debt in 2010 and it also suffered from a $900 million gap in its operating budget for 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The capital budget, which covers repairs, technological upgrades, new trains, and expansions, is currently $15 billion short of what the MTA states it needs. If this is not funded, the MTA will fund the repairs with debt and raise fares to cover repayments.</p>
<p>The MTA has consistently run on a deficit, but increased spending in 2000–04 coupled with the economic downturn led to a severe increase in the financial burden that the MTA bore. The budget problems stem from multiple sources. The MTA cannot be supported solely by rider fares and road tolls. In the preliminary 2011 budget, MTA forecasted operating revenue totaled at $6.5 billion, amount to only 50% of the $13 billion operating expenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Therefore, the MTA must rely on other sources of funding to remain operational. Revenue collected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_estate_tax" title="Real estate tax">real estate taxes</a> for transportation purposes helped to contain the deficit. However, due to the weak economy and unstable <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a> market, money from these taxes severely decreased; in 2010, tax revenue fell at least 20% short of the projected value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Beyond this, steadily reducing support from city and state governments led to borrowing money by issuing bonds, which contributed heavily to the debt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>This budget deficit has resulted in various problems, mainly concentrated in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Subway" title="New York City Subway">New York City Subway</a> fares have been increased four times since 2008, with the most recent occurring March 22, 2015, raising single-ride fares from $2.50 to $2.75, express service from $6 to $6.50 and the monthly <a href="/wiki/MetroCard_(New_York_City)" title="MetroCard (New York City)">MetroCard</a> fare from $112 to $116.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Each fare raise was met with increasing resistance by MTA customers, and many are beginning to find the fare increases prohibitive. 2010 also saw heavy service cuts for many MTA subsidiaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Fewer trains spaced farther between resulted in heavy overcrowding beyond normal rush hours, leading to frustration for many subway and bus riders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 2013, the subway had the highest ridership since 1947.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> MTA employees also suffered due to the budget issues. By mid-July 2010, MTA layoffs had reached over 1,000, and many of those affected were low-level employees who made less than $55,000 annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_Transportation_Authority&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the MTA was running a $15 billion deficit in its $32 billion 2015–2019 Capital Plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnainfo_20150430_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-dnainfo_20150430-22">[22]</a></sup> Without extra funding, many necessary construction and renovation projects would not be performed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In October 2015, the MTA passed the $29 billion 2015–2019 Capital Plan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> the largest capital plan in MTA's history; it will be funded by federal, state and city government as well as riders' fares and tolls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Three months later, New York Governor <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Cuomo" title="Andrew Cuomo">Andrew Cuomo</a> and MTA chairman Thomas Prendergast unveiled their plan to spend $26 billion to modernize the subway network, which includes adding Wi-Fi and cellphone services throughout all 278 stations by the end of 2016. Other plans call for making extensive renovations to 30 subway stations, allowing mobile ticketing by cellphone or bank cards, and adding security cameras on buses, charging stations for electronics, and more countdown clocks. Roughly $3 million will be spend to improve bridges and tunnels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Advertisement_controversies">Advertisement controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_Transportation_Authority&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Advertisement controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The MTA refused to display an ad in the New York City Subway system in 2012, which read: "In any war between the civilized man and the savage, support the civilized man. Support Israel. Defeat <a href="/wiki/Jihad" title="Jihad">Jihad</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-28">[28]</a></sup> The authority's decision was overturned in July 2012 when Judge <a href="/wiki/Paul_A._Engelmayer" title="Paul A. Engelmayer">Paul A. Engelmayer</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Southern_District_of_New_York" title="United States District Court for the Southern District of New York">United States District Court for the Southern District of New York</a> ruled that the ad of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Freedom_Defense_Initiative" title="American Freedom Defense Initiative">American Freedom Defense Initiative</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protected_speech" title="Protected speech">protected speech</a> under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Amendment" title="First Amendment">First Amendment</a>, and that the MTA's actions were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unconstitutional" title="Unconstitutional">unconstitutional</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto1_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-30">[30]</a></sup> The judge held in a 35-page opinion that the rejected ad was "not only protected speech — it is core political speech ... [which as such] is afforded the highest level of protection under the First Amendment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto1_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-31">[31]</a></sup> The MTA had received $116.4 million in revenue in 2011 from advertising sold throughout its subway, commuter rail, and bus systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2015, another ad became the subject of controversy when the MTA refused to display it, the refusal was again challenged in court, and the MTA again lost in court and was ordered by a federal judge to display the ad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters.com_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters.com-32">[32]</a></sup> The ad, paid for by the American Freedom Defense Initiative, showed a man with a scarf covering his face, with the caption "Killing Jews is Worship that draws us close to <a href="/wiki/Allah" title="Allah">Allah</a>", which was attributed to "<a href="/wiki/Hamas" title="Hamas">Hamas</a> MTV," and then stated: "That's His <a href="/wiki/Jihad" title="Jihad">Jihad</a>. What's yours?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters.com_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-reuters.com-32">[32]</a></sup> The ad included a disclaimer that the display of the ad did not reflect the opinion of the MTA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters.com_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-reuters.com-32">[32]</a></sup> U.S. District Judge <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Koeltl" title="John Koeltl">John Koeltl</a> of the US District Court for the Southern District of New York in Manhattan said the ad was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protected_speech" title="Protected speech">protected speech</a> under the First Amendment of the U.S. Constitution, and rejected the MTA's argument that the ad might endorse terrorism or violence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters.com_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-reuters.com-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pamela_Geller" title="Pamela Geller">Pamela Geller</a>, president of the group that sued the MTA in order to run the ads, lauded the decision, and a lawyer for the organization said the same decision had been made in Washington and Philadelphia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>A week afterward, the MTA's board in a 9–2 vote banned all political, religious, and opinion advertisements on subways and buses, limiting any ads to commercial ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorklawjournal.com_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-newyorklawjournal.com-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Specifically, it banned advertisements that "prominently or predominately advocate or express a political message" about "disputed economic, political, moral, religious or social issues," and any ad that "promotes or opposes" a political party, ballot referendum, and "the election of any candidate".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnainfo_20150430_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-dnainfo_20150430-22">[22]</a></sup> The board estimated that the ads that the board was banning made up less than $1 million of the MTA's annual (as of 2014) $138 million advertising revenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnainfo_20150430_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-dnainfo_20150430-22">[22]</a></sup> Nevertheless, lawyers for the American Freedom Defense Initiative called the MTA's action a "disingenuous attempt to circumvent" the judge's order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorklawjournal.com_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-newyorklawjournal.com-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The MTA collected $138 million from advertising on its trains and buses in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorklawjournal.com_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-newyorklawjournal.com-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_campaign">Safety campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_Transportation_Authority&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Safety campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2002, following the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11, 2001, terrorist attacks</a>, the MTA introduced the slogan "If you see something, say something."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliott_2007_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott_2007-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smerd_2005_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smerd_2005-37">[37]</a></sup> The campaign, which was based from a theme created by Korey Kay &amp; Partners, consisted of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_safety_announcement" title="Public safety announcement">public safety announcements</a>, posted on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advertisement" title="Advertisement">advertisement</a> boards in stations, subway, buses, and trains, urging people to report suspicious activity. <a href="/wiki/Allen_Kay" title="Allen Kay">Allen Kay</a>, CEO of Korey Kay and Partners, stated in 2007 that the company had to do a lot of research to ensure that consumers understood the message correctly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliott_2007_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott_2007-36">[36]</a></sup> Since 2002 the campaign has evolved from simple print ads to television spots, and reports of suspicious packages in the system rose over 40-fold, from 814 in 2002 to over 37,000 in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karni_2007_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Karni_2007-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The MTA moved to trademark the slogan in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smerd_2005_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smerd_2005-37">[37]</a></sup> The slogan was used by more than 30 other "transport and governmental" organizations by 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliott_2007_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott_2007-36">[36]</a></sup> That year, the MTA spent $3 million to run 4,000 television ads and 84 newspaper ads in 11 total papers, over a span of more than four months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karni_2007_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Karni_2007-38">[38]</a></sup> The idea gained traction, and in 2010, the domestic-security branch of the United States federal government, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Homeland_Security" title="United States Department of Homeland Security">United States Department of Homeland Security</a> (DHS), started its own "see something, say something" campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USATODAY.com_2010_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-USATODAY.com_2010-39">[39]</a></sup> Kevin Ortiz, a spokesman for the MTA, described the slogan as having "engaged the public in serving as the eyes and ears of our system."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USATODAY.com_2010_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-USATODAY.com_2010-39">[39]</a></sup> Meanwhile, the DHS's campaign had attracted at least 215 partners in the private, public, and nonprofit sectors by 2014, which one writer called "a true smart practice."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> However, the MTA program has not been universally well-received; in 2012, a sociologist at <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a> noted that the campaign had not netted any thwarted terrorist plots, and that the sheer volume of calls to the MTA hotline resulted in MTA workers possibly not being able to identify genuine threats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2016, MTA updated the campaign, renaming it "New Yorkers Keep New York Safe." As before, the campaign features public service announcements in advertisement spaces. However, this new campaign now features the pictures, names, and quotes of New Yorkers who called to report suspicious people or things on the MTA's system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rivoli_2016_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rivoli_2016-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Altamirano_2016_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Altamirano_2016-43">[43]</a></sup> The rebooted campaign also shows 15- to 30-second videos of these New Yorkers who speak about their experiences. The two-year "New Yorkers Keep New York Safe" campaign received $2 million of funding from the DHS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rivoli_2016_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rivoli_2016-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Altamirano_2016_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Altamirano_2016-43">[43]</a></sup> The MTA still owns the trademark for "If you see something, say something."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Altamirano_2016_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-Altamirano_2016-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
