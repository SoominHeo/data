<p>The <b>Métis</b> are members of ethnic groups indigenous to Canada and parts of the United States that trace their descent to <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous North Americans</a> and European settlers. The <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_in_Canada" title="Métis in Canada">Métis in Canada</a> are recognized as an <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_peoples_in_Canada" title="Aboriginal peoples in Canada">aboriginal people</a> under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitution_Act_of_1982" title="Constitution Act of 1982">Constitution Act of 1982</a>; they number 451,795 as of 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aboriginal_Identity_2011_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aboriginal_Identity_2011-1">[1]</a></sup> Smaller communities identifying as Métis exist <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_in_the_United_States" title="Métis in the United States">in the U.S.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Métis communities descended from unions between Native Americans and white settlers have developed over the centuries since <a href="/wiki/European_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="European colonization of the Americas">European contact</a>. In Canada, Métis in the western regions were heavily involved in the fur trade, and formed communities that have retained a unique culture.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%A9tis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "Métis" (originally from the French adjective <i>métis</i>: 1. something that is half of one thing and half of another, and 2. someone whose father and mother are of different races, or mixed-race)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Le_Robert_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Le_Robert-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:1080</sup> was first used to refer to people of mixed race born generally to indigenous women and French men in <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Louisiane" title="La Louisiane">La Louisiane</a>. Over time in Canada, many mixed-race people married within their own group, maintaining contact with their indigenous culture. The term developed in association with these particular communities of mixed-race people and their unique culture.</p>
<p>The word is a <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a> of Spanish <i><a href="/wiki/Mestizo" title="Mestizo">mestizo</a></i> and Portuguese <i><a href="/wiki/Mesti%C3%A7o" title="Mestiço">mestiço</a></i>, which have the same meaning but refer to descendants with indigenous and European ancestry in Latin American colonies. The English word <i>mestee</i> is a corruption of the Middle French <i>mestis</i> (the letters 's' both pronounced at the start of the Middle French period, and both silent at the end of the Middle French period).</p>
<p>The term <i>mestee</i> was widely used in the antebellum United States for mixed-race individuals, according to Jack D. Forbes, used for people of European and Native American ancestry, as well as European and African, or tri-racial. In the 19th century, the census takers recorded people of color as <a href="/wiki/Mulatto" title="Mulatto">mulatto</a>, also meaning mixed race. In former French colonies, a group known as <a href="/wiki/Free_people_of_color" title="Free people of color">free people of color</a> had developed from unions between African or mixed-race women and French male colonists; often the men freed their children.</p>
<p>After the Civil War, the term "mestee" gradually fell into disuse when the millions of slaves were made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freedmen" title="Freedmen">freedmen</a>. As whites worked to re-establish white supremacy during and after Reconstruction, they passed laws after the turn of the 20th century to enforce the "<a href="/wiki/One-drop_rule" title="One-drop rule">one-drop rule</a>." By this anyone with any known Sub-Saharan African ancestry was legally "Black", a more restrictive definition than had previously operated in the South, especially on the frontier. <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> scholar <a href="/wiki/Jack_D._Forbes" title="Jack D. Forbes">Jack D. Forbes</a> has attempted to revive "mestee" as a term for the mixed-race peoples established as free before the Civil War.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <i>métis</i> is used outside of North America, mostly in former colonies or countries that were historically part of the French Empire and had French as an official language, such as Vietnam. As in North America, the term indicates a person of mixed indigenous and European ancestry. Anglophones generally restrict the use of the word "Métis" to peoples of North America, preferring the term "Eurasian" for people of mixed Asian and European ancestry.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="M.C3.A9tis_people_in_Canada">Métis people in Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%A9tis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Métis people in Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_people_(Canada)" title="Métis people (Canada)">Métis people (Canada)</a></div>
<p>The specific meaning of Métis in Canada varies depending on context. <i>The Canadian Encyclopedia</i> indicates that there is no complete consensus as on the definition of Métis in Canada. It uses the following definition:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdnencyc_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-cdnencyc-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>It is important to define specific meanings for the term as used in this discussion, while cautioning that writers past and present have not achieved consensus on the matter. Written with a small m, <i>métis</i> is an old French word meaning "mixed", and it is used here in a general sense for people of dual Indian-White ancestry. Capitalized, <i>Métis</i> is often used but not universally accepted as a generic term for all persons of this biracial descent. It may variously refer to a distinctive socio cultural heritage, a means of ethnic self-identification, and sometimes a political and legal category, more or less narrowly defined.</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%A9tis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Métis people's history begins in the 17th century with the unions of various French colonists, typically trappers and traders, and Algonquian women, including but not limited to Mi'kmaq, Algonquin, Ojibwe, and Cree peoples. These unions began in the east, extending from the Atlantic coast to the Great Lakes. The fur trade and colonial development drew French <a href="/wiki/Voyageurs" title="Voyageurs">voyageurs</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coureurs_de_bois" title="Coureurs de bois">coureurs de bois</a> to the west, along with the later <a href="/wiki/Hudson%27s_Bay_Company" title="Hudson's Bay Company">Hudson's Bay Company</a> employees. Wintering partners of the fur trading companies typically took <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Country_wife&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Country wife (page does not exist)">country wives</a> for their months away from the eastern cities.</p>
<p>After the fall of New France in 1763, many Métis populations continued to establish themselves, often specializing in the fur trade and related hunting. Some served as interpreters as they often were fluent in both indigenous and European languages. English and Scottish traders also married indigenous women, often the daughters of high-ranking chiefs, forming an elite mixed society. As the eighteenth century ended, the fur trade moved westwards into the Plains. In 1812, <a href="/wiki/Cuthbert_Grant" title="Cuthbert Grant">Cuthbert Grant</a> led a battle in the <a href="/wiki/Pemmican_War" title="Pemmican War">Pemmican War</a>, flying the Métis flag. Many treaties throughout Canada were being negotiated in the nineteenth century, including in Ontario with the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Robinson-Huron_treaty&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Robinson-Huron treaty (page does not exist)">Robinson-Huron treaty</a>. In 1870 the Métis at <a href="/wiki/Red_River_Rebellion" title="Red River Rebellion">Red River</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Riel" title="Louis Riel">Louis Riel</a>, resisted the colonial efforts of Canada, and negotiated entry into Canada as the province of <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a> with promises to protect their rights. In 1885, the Métis were resisting Canadian colonialism with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northwest_Rebellion" title="Northwest Rebellion">Northwest Rebellion</a>. The Métis were defeated and Riel was hanged as a traitor to Canada, but his role in history is controversial.</p>
<p>Métis in the western provinces faced scrip after 1885, and many were considered 'Road Allowance people'.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Racism towards Métis peoples in the west was a large part of the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries. In the east, many Métis continued to live their lifestyles and were often considered invisible.</p>
<p>In 1982, Métis were included as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aboriginal_people" title="Aboriginal people">aboriginal people</a> in the Canadian constitution. They are defined as an ethnic group with their own culture, distinct from <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> peoples. Métis peoples have formed a variety of political organizations to promote their interests, including the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_National_Council" title="Métis National Council">Métis National Council</a> (MNC), the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_M%C3%A9tis_Council" title="Canadian Métis Council">Canadian Métis Council</a> (CMC), the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%A9tis_Federation_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Métis Federation of Canada (page does not exist)">Métis Federation of Canada</a> (MFC) and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%A9tis_Nation_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Métis Nation of Canada (page does not exist)">Métis Nation of Canada</a> to name a few.</p>
<p>In 2003, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powley_Case" title="Powley Case">Powley Case</a> ruled that a family of Métis people in Ontario had the right to hunt moose as part of their Métis aboriginal rights. This case was funded by the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_Nation_of_Ontario" title="Métis Nation of Ontario">Métis Nation of Ontario</a> (MNO), a provincial affiliate of the MNC. The case established the Métis history in Ontario, which was long debated by many people. The case also established the Powley test, which helps to define who is Métis, and therefore eligible to rights as an aboriginal person.</p>
<p>In 2013, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Daniels_Decision&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Daniels Decision (page does not exist)">Daniels Decision</a> ruled that Métis people were legally considered 'Indian' under section 95 of the Constitution. This does not mean that Métis peoples are a First Nation, but rather that they are a federal responsibility. First Nations are distinct from Métis peoples, and they are not synonyms. The case is being appealed.</p>
<p>On April 14, 2016, the Supreme Court reached a landmark decision giving full indigenous rights to approximately 700,000 Métis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
