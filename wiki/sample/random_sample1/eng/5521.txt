<p><b>Metis</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> (Μῆτις, "wisdom," "skill," or "craft"), in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_religion" title="Ancient Greek religion">ancient Greek religion</a>, was of the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">Titan</a> generation and, like several primordial figures, an <a href="/wiki/Oceanid" title="Oceanid">Oceanid</a>, in the sense that Metis was born of <a href="/wiki/Oceanus" title="Oceanus">Oceanus</a> and his sister <a href="/wiki/Tethys_(mythology)" title="Tethys (mythology)">Tethys</a>, of an earlier age than <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> and his siblings. Metis was the first great spouse of Zeus,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and also his cousin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-2">[2]</a></sup> Zeus is himself titled <i>Mêtieta</i>, "the wise counsellor," in the Homeric poems.</p>
<p>By the era of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_philosophy" title="Greek philosophy">Greek philosophy</a> in the fifth century BC, Metis had become the mother of wisdom and deep thought, but her name originally connoted "magical cunning" and was as easily equated with the <a href="/wiki/Trickster" title="Trickster">trickster</a> powers of <a href="/wiki/Prometheus" title="Prometheus">Prometheus</a> as with the "royal <i>metis</i>" of Zeus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Stoicism" title="Stoicism">Stoic</a> commentators <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegorised</a> Metis as the embodiment of "<a href="/wiki/Prudence" title="Prudence">prudence</a>", "wisdom" or "wise counsel", in which form she was inherited by the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Greek word <i>metis</i> meant a quality that combined wisdom and cunning. This quality was considered to be highly admirable in the Mycenean era, with the hero <a href="/wiki/Odysseus" title="Odysseus">Odysseus</a> being the embodiment of it. In the Classical era, it was regarded by Athenians as one of the notable characteristics of the Athenian character. Metis was the one who gave Zeus a potion to cause <a href="/wiki/Cronus" title="Cronus">Cronus</a> to vomit out Zeus' siblings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Metis was both a threat to Zeus and an indispensable aid (Brown 1952:133):</p>
<dl>
<dd>"Zeus lay with Metis but immediately feared the consequences. It had been prophesied that Metis would bear extremely powerful children: the first, <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a> and the second, a son more powerful than Zeus himself, who would eventually overthrow Zeus."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>In order to forestall these dire consequences, Zeus tricked her into turning herself into a <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">fly</a> and promptly swallowed her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He was too late: Metis had already conceived a child. In time she began making a helmet and robe for her fetal daughter. The hammering as she made the helmet caused Zeus great pain, and <a href="/wiki/Hephaestus" title="Hephaestus">Hephaestus</a> either clove Zeus's head with an axe,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> or hit it with a hammer at the river <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triton_(god)" title="Triton (god)">Triton</a>, giving rise to Athena's birth. Athena leaped from Zeus's head, fully grown, armed, and armoured, and Zeus was none the worse for the experience.</p>
<p>The similarities between Zeus swallowing Metis and Cronus swallowing his children have been noted by several scholars. This also caused some controversy in regard to reproduction myths and the lack of a need for women as a means of reproduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hesiod's account is followed by <a href="/wiki/Acusilaus" title="Acusilaus">Acusilaus</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orphic" title="Orphic">Orphic</a> tradition, which enthroned Metis side by side with <a href="/wiki/Eros" title="Eros">Eros</a> as primal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmogeny" title="Cosmogeny">cosmogenic forces</a>. <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> makes <i><a href="/wiki/Porus_(mythology)" title="Porus (mythology)">Poros</a></i>, or "creative ingenuity", the child of Metis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
