<p><b>Melvin Emery "Mel" Patton</b> (November 16, 1924 – May 9, 2014) was an American <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">track and field</a> athlete, who won two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_medals" title="Gold medals">gold medals</a> at the <a href="/wiki/1948_Summer_Olympics" title="1948 Summer Olympics">1948 Summer Olympics</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mel_Patton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a>, Mel Patton or <i>Pell Mell</i>, as he was nicknamed in the late 1940s, made his mark in track and field while a student at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a>, where he was coached by <a href="/wiki/Dean_Cromwell" title="Dean Cromwell">Dean Cromwell</a>. During his collegiate years, Patton was a member of the Delta-Eta Chapter of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kappa_Sigma_Fraternity" title="Kappa Sigma Fraternity">Kappa Sigma Fraternity</a>. He also attended <a href="/wiki/University_High_School_(Los_Angeles,_California)" title="University High School (Los Angeles, California)">University High School</a> in Los Angeles.</p>
<p>Patton won the <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/100_yard_dash" title="100 yard dash">100 yard dash</a> in 1947 and in 1948 and 1949 completed the 100 and <a href="/wiki/200_metres" title="200 metres">220 yd</a> <a href="/wiki/Sprint_(running)" title="Sprint (running)">sprint</a> double at that same meet. In 1947 he tied the 100 yd dash <a href="/wiki/List_of_world_records_in_athletics" title="List of world records in athletics">world record</a> of 9.4, which he lowered it 9.3 the following year. In 1949 he set a 220 yd world record on <a href="/wiki/200_metres_straight" title="200 metres straight">a straightaway</a> of 20.2, breaking the record held by <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Owens" title="Jesse Owens">Jesse Owens</a>.</p>
<p>In the Olympic Trials, he suffered a rare loss to <a href="/wiki/Barney_Ewell" title="Barney Ewell">Barney Ewell</a> in the 100 m final, then in the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a> placed only fifth in the 100 m. He atoned for that disappointment by taking two gold medals in the 200 m and the <a href="/wiki/4_%C3%97_100_metres_relay" title="4 × 100 metres relay">4 × 100 m relay</a>.</p>
<p>After retiring from competition, Patton was a track coach before entering the electronics industry. He died in <a href="/wiki/Fallbrook,_California" title="Fallbrook, California">Fallbrook, California</a> on May 9, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
