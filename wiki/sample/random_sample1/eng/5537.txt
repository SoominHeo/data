<p>The <b>Melanocharitidae</b>, the <b>berrypeckers and longbills</b>, is a small bird <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> restricted to the forests of <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>. The family contains ten species in four (sometimes three) <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a>. They are small <a href="/wiki/Songbird" title="Songbird">songbirds</a> with generally dull <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> but a range of body shapes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melanocharitidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The identification of the family Melanocharitidae was not known or suspected until the work of <a href="/wiki/Sibley%E2%80%93Ahlquist_taxonomy_of_birds" title="Sibley–Ahlquist taxonomy of birds">Sibley and Ahlquist</a> on the taxonomy of birds using <a href="/wiki/DNA%E2%80%93DNA_hybridization" title="DNA–DNA hybridization">DNA–DNA hybridization</a> The genera had been instead placed with other families. The two genera of berrypecker had been placed inside the <a href="/wiki/Flowerpecker" title="Flowerpecker">flowerpecker</a> family Dicaeidae, and the longbills were once considered to be <a href="/wiki/Honeyeater" title="Honeyeater">honeyeaters</a> (which they closely resemble). Sibley and Ahlquist placed the berrypeckers and longbill family close to the <a href="/wiki/Painted_berrypecker" title="Painted berrypecker">painted berrypeckers</a> (Paramythiidae), <a href="/wiki/Sunbird" title="Sunbird">sunbirds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flowerpecker" title="Flowerpecker">flowerpeckers</a>, but a 2002 study found them closer to the satinbirds (<a href="/wiki/Cnemophilidae" title="Cnemophilidae">Cnemophilidae</a>, a recent split from the <a href="/wiki/Bird-of-paradise" title="Bird-of-paradise">birds-of-paradise</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>It comprises ten species in four genera, the <i><a href="/wiki/Melanocharis" title="Melanocharis">Melanocharis</a></i> berrypeckers and the longbills in the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Toxorhamphus" title="Toxorhamphus">Toxorhamphus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pygmy_longbill" title="Pygmy longbill">Oedistoma</a></i>. The two longbill genera are sometimes incorrectly lumped into the same genus, <i>Toxorhamphus</i>, in spite of <i>Oedistoma</i> being erected forty years prior to <i>Toxorhamphus</i> (a violation of the taxonomic <a href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Zoological_Nomenclature#Principle_of_Priority" title="International Code of Zoological Nomenclature">principal of priority</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1">[1]</a></sup> There are both molecular and morphological reasons to keep the two genera separate, however. A 1993 study of the longbills, berrypeckers and some other aberrant honeyeaters found that the <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_longbill" title="Dwarf longbill">dwarf longbill</a> was more closely related to the berrypeckers than the two longbills in the genus <i>Toxorhamphus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> There are also some morphological differences in the shape of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metatarsus" title="Metatarsus">tarsus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The two species in <i>Oedistoma</i>, however, may not be closely related and more research is needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Spotted_berrypecker" title="Spotted berrypecker">spotted berrypecker</a> is placed in its own genus <i>Rhamphocharis</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> while some treatments lump it with the <i>Melanocharis</i> berrypeckers it is anatomically and behaviourally distinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is some confusion with the common names, as there are two other berrypecker species in the tiny family <a href="/wiki/Painted_berrypecker" title="Painted berrypecker">Paramythiidae</a>, once considered to be close to the flowerpeckers as well; members of several African genera—notably species in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warbler</a> genus <i><a href="/wiki/Macrosphenus" title="Macrosphenus">Macrosphenus</a></i>—are also known as longbills.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melanocharitidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The berrypeckers and longbills are small to very small songbirds. They range in length from 15 cm (5.9 in) in the case of the <a href="/wiki/Fan-tailed_berrypecker" title="Fan-tailed berrypecker">fan-tailed berrypecker</a> to 7.3 cm (2.9 in) in the case of the <a href="/wiki/Pygmy_longbill" title="Pygmy longbill">pygmy longbill</a>, which is the smallest bird in New Guinea. The berrypeckers (<i>Melanocharis</i>) are usually bigger than the <i>Toxorhamphus</i> and <i>Oedistoma</i> longbills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kikkawa_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kikkawa-5">[5]</a></sup> The females of two species, the fan-tailed and <a href="/wiki/Streaked_berrypecker" title="Streaked berrypecker">streaked berrypecker</a>, are unique amongst songbirds in that they exhibit a reversal in the usual pattern of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a>, with the females being both longer and heavier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> For example in the fan-tailed berrypecker the male weighs 12 to 15 g (0.42–0.53 oz), whereas the female weighs 16 to 20 g (0.56–0.71 oz).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>They have drab-coloured <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> in greys, browns or black and white.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOTW_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOTW-7">[7]</a></sup> The berrypeckers exhibit some <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a> in their plumage. The berrypeckers resemble stout short-billed <a href="/wiki/Honeyeater" title="Honeyeater">honeyeaters</a>, and the longbills are like drab <a href="/wiki/Sunbird" title="Sunbird">sunbirds</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOTW_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-BOTW-7">[7]</a></sup> or short-tailed honeyeaters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kikkawa_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kikkawa-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">calls</a> of the berrypeckers have been described as high pitched and faint, and the song rapid.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melanocharitidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The berrypeckers are generally montane species, with only one, the <a href="/wiki/Black_berrypecker" title="Black berrypecker">black berrypecker</a>, being found in lowland forest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In contrast the longbills live in lowland forests and low montane forests as well as on small islands around New Guinea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOTW_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-BOTW-7">[7]</a></sup> Amongst the berrypeckers there is a succession of species at different altitudes, with the black berrypecker being found in the lowlands, the mid-mountain berrypecker being found at lower altitudes (mid-montane) and the fan-tailed berrypecker being found near the treeline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOTW_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-BOTW-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melanocharitidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Melanocharitidae species are usually seen alone or in pairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOTW_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-BOTW-7">[7]</a></sup> They may associate with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mixed-species_feeding_flock" title="Mixed-species feeding flock">mixed-species feeding flocks</a>, but are loose members and not core species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1">[1]</a></sup> The diet of the family is dominated by berries and small fruits. <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">Arthropods</a> are also <a href="/wiki/Gleaning_(birds)" title="Gleaning (birds)">gleaned</a> from foliage, and more rarely by hovering and snatching. They are highly active feeders, seldom pausing except when at berries. Most species feed in the lower and middle levels of the forest, although records suggest that the obscure berrypecker will enter the canopy to forage. The male black berrypecker will also enter the canopy, while the female will remain lower down in the forest, suggesting some level of sexual segregation of feeding niches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The breeding of some species is entirely undescribed, and little is known about the breeding in most species. Records of nests have been made in both wet and dry seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1">[1]</a></sup> They build a cup nest,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kikkawa_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kikkawa-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOTW_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-BOTW-7">[7]</a></sup> usually on a forked branch near the edge of a tree, out of <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">fern</a> scales and plant fibres bound neatly with insect or spider silk and ornamented with <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichens</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kikkawa_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kikkawa-5">[5]</a></sup> Little is known about the division of labour in the family, although the pattern exhibited by the <a href="/wiki/Black_berrypecker" title="Black berrypecker">black berrypecker</a>, where the female construct the nest alone but both sexes feed the young, may be typical of the family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBW_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1">[1]</a></sup> They lay one or two eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOTW_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-BOTW-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The berrypeckers and longbills are not considered to be threatened by human activities. No species is listed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Threatened" title="Threatened">threatened</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>, although one species, the <a href="/wiki/Obscure_berrypecker" title="Obscure berrypecker">obscure berrypecker</a>, is listed as <a href="/wiki/Data_deficient" title="Data deficient">data deficient</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> That species is known officially from two collected specimens, but unconfirmed reports suggest that it is not uncommon in remote parts of New Guinea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
