<p><b>Melville Louis Kossuth</b> (<b>Melvil</b>) <b>Dewey</b> (December 10, 1851 – December 26, 1931) was an American librarian and educator, inventor of the <a href="/wiki/Dewey_Decimal_Classification" title="Dewey Decimal Classification">Dewey Decimal</a> system of library classification, and a founder of the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Placid_Club" title="Lake Placid Club">Lake Placid Club</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_personal_life">Education and personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melvil_Dewey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education and personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dewey was born in <a href="/wiki/Adams_Center,_New_York" title="Adams Center, New York">Adams Center, New York</a>, the fifth and last child of Joel and Eliza Greene Dewey. He attended rural schools and determined early that his destiny was to reform education of the masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He briefly attended <a href="/wiki/Alfred_University" title="Alfred University">Alfred University</a> (1870),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> then <a href="/wiki/Amherst_College" title="Amherst College">Amherst College</a>, where he belonged to <a href="/wiki/Delta_Kappa_Epsilon" title="Delta Kappa Epsilon">Delta Kappa Epsilon</a>, and from which he earned a bachelor's degree in 1874 and a master's in 1877.</p>
<p>While still a student, he founded the <a href="/wiki/Library_Bureau" title="Library Bureau">Library Bureau</a>, which sold high-quality index-cards and filing-cabinets, and established the standard dimensions for catalog cards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MD68_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MD68-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a young adult he advocated <a href="/wiki/Spelling_reform" title="Spelling reform">spelling reform</a>; he changed his name from the usual "Melville" to "Melvil", without redundant letters, and for a time changed his surname to "Dui".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1883 to 1888 he was chief librarian at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>. During his time as director of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Library" title="New York State Library">New York State Library</a> (1888–1906) Dewey established a program of traveling libraries. From 1888 to 1900 Dewey served as secretary and executive officer of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_the_State_of_New_York" title="University of the State of New York">University of the State of New York</a>. In 1895 Dewey founded the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Placid_Club" title="Lake Placid Club">Lake Placid Club</a> with his wife Annie. He and his son <a href="/wiki/Godfrey_Dewey" title="Godfrey Dewey">Godfrey</a> had been active in arranging the <a href="/wiki/1932_Winter_Olympics" title="1932 Winter Olympics">Winter Olympics</a> which took place at Lake Placid—he chaired the New York State Winter Olympics Committee. In 1926 he went to Florida to establish a new branch of the Lake Placid Club.</p>
<p><span id="difficulties"></span> Dewey established a pattern of making powerful enemies early in life, and many of his friends found him difficult as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As one biographer put it, "Although he did not lack friends, they were weary of coming to his defense, so endless a process it had become."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Another biography refers to Dewey's "old nemesis—a persistent inability to control himself around women" as a chronic cause of trouble on the job.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Indeed, when Dewey opened his School of Library Economy at Columbia College to women it was rumored that he asked for their bust sizes with their applications. Though the rumor turned out to be false he did require a photograph from each female applicant since "you cannot polish a pumpkin." However, there is no archival evidence that he acted in any way inappropriate with his female students. In 1905 during a 10-day trip to Alaska sponsored by the American Library Association (ALA), a group he co-founded, he made unwelcome advances on four prominent librarians who informed Association officials. As a result, Dewey was ostracized from the ALA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1904 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_State_Board_of_Regents" title="New York State Board of Regents">New York State Board of Regents</a> received a petition demanding Dewey's removal as State Librarian because of his personal involvement in the Lake Placid Club's policy of excluding Jews and other religious and ethnic groups. While the Regents declined to remove Dewey, they did issue a public rebuke, and in the summer of 1905 he resigned as a result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silver_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Silver-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dewey married twice, first to Annie R. Godfrey, and then to Emily McKay Beal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT33_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT33-11">[11]</a></sup> He and his first wife had one child, <a href="/wiki/Godfrey_Dewey" title="Godfrey Dewey">Godfrey</a>. Dewey became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Library_Hall_of_Fame" title="Library Hall of Fame">American Library Association's Hall of Fame</a> in 1951.</p>
<p>He died in Lake Placid, Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT33_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT33-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melvil_Dewey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dewey was a pioneer in American librarianship<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and an influential figure in the development of libraries in America in the late 19th and early the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_1911_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB_1911-13">[13]</a></sup> He is best known for the <a href="/wiki/Dewey_Decimal_Classification" title="Dewey Decimal Classification">decimal classification system</a> that many public and school libraries use. But the decimal system was just one of a long list of innovations. Among these is the idea of the state library as controller of school and public library services within a state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JS05_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-JS05-14">[14]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a>, he founded the Library Bureau, a private company "for the definite purpose of furnishing libraries with equipment and supplies of unvarying correctness and reliability."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-15">[15]</a></sup> Dewey's Library Bureau company is also said to have introduced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hanging_Folders" title="Hanging Folders">hanging vertical files</a>, first seen at the <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Columbian_Exposition" title="World's Columbian Exposition">Columbian Exposition</a> of 1893 in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dewey_Decimal_Classification">Dewey Decimal Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melvil_Dewey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dewey Decimal Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dewey_Decimal_Classification" title="Dewey Decimal Classification">Dewey Decimal Classification</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dewey_spine_label.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="546" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Dewey_spine_label.jpg/220px-Dewey_spine_label.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Dewey_spine_label.jpg/330px-Dewey_spine_label.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Dewey_spine_label.jpg/440px-Dewey_spine_label.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dewey_spine_label.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Spine labels showing <a href="/wiki/Dewey_Decimal_Classification" title="Dewey Decimal Classification">Dewey Decimal Classification</a> call numbers.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Immediately after receiving his undergraduate degree he was hired to manage Amherst's library and reclassify its collections. Dewey worked out a new scheme that superimposed a system of decimal numbers on a structure of knowledge first outlined by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Francis_Bacon" title="Sir Francis Bacon">Sir Francis Bacon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> For his decision to use a decimal system he may have been inspired by two library systems that he includes in the acknowledgements in the first publication of his system in 1876.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In that preface, and in the following thirteen editions, Dewey cites the card system of Italian publisher <a href="/wiki/Natale_Battezzati" title="Natale Battezzati">Natale Battezzati</a> as "...the most fruitful source of ideas..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dewey copyrighted the system in 1876. This system has proved to be enormously influential; though many American libraries have since adopted the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress_Classification" title="Library of Congress Classification">classification scheme of the Library of Congress</a>, Dewey's system remains in widespread use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Library_Association">American Library Association</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melvil_Dewey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: American Library Association">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1877 Dewey moved to Boston, where he founded and became editor of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Library_Journal" title="The Library Journal">The Library Journal</a></i>, which became an influential factor in the development of libraries in America, and in the reform of their administration. He was also one of the founders of the <a href="/wiki/American_Library_Association" title="American Library Association">American Library Association</a>, of which he was secretary from 1876 to 1891, and president in 1891 and 1893.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_1911_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB_1911-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="School_of_Library_Economy">School of Library Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melvil_Dewey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: School of Library Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:New_York_State_Library_1900.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="819" data-file-width="1024" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/New_York_State_Library_1900.jpg/220px-New_York_State_Library_1900.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/New_York_State_Library_1900.jpg/330px-New_York_State_Library_1900.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/New_York_State_Library_1900.jpg/440px-New_York_State_Library_1900.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In 1883 Dewey became librarian of Columbia College, and in the following year founded there the School of Library Economy, the first institution for the instruction of librarians ever organized. The proposal to establish the school was approved by the college's Board of Trustees on 5 May 1884.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> After a period of preparation, the school was officially opened on 5 January 1887, with an enrollment of 20 students—three men and 17 women. Women were admitted to the program at Dewey's insistence and against the wishes of the college's Regents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Although the school had a promising start, Dewey's conflicts with the university officials, in particular over the issue of the presence of women, led to its future being cast in doubt, and by 1888 it was apparent that Columbia intended to close it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> However, at that point, Dewey, upon accepting a position with the New York State Library in Albany, successfully secured the agreement of its Regents to have the school transferred there. The formal transfer was accomplished in 1889,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and the school, which was ultimately very successful, was re-established in Albany as the New York State Library School under Dewey's direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_1911_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-EB_1911-13">[13]</a></sup> (The school returned to Columbia's Manhattan campus in 1926.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>) Dewey did not forget his Columbia students. He petitioned the University of the State of New York, which granted degrees to those students who agreed to submit to examinations and produce a bibliography and thesis. Two students participated, including future ALA registrar and college archivist <a href="/wiki/Nina_Browne" title="Nina Browne">Nina Browne</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the period from 1888 to 1906 Dewey was also director of the New York State Library, and until 1900 he was secretary of the University of the State of New York as well. In that function he completely reorganized the state library, making it one of the most efficient in America, as well as established the system of state travelling libraries and picture collections. In 1885, he founded the New York Library Club there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traveling_Libraries">Traveling Libraries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melvil_Dewey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Traveling Libraries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Community libraries began to flourish in the early nineteenth century. The West opened to expansion and further exploration, and people wanted services and opportunities to move with them. In New York, Melvil Dewey had "initiated a program of traveling libraries-collections of one hundred books sent to communities without public libraries."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> His efforts spurred other state organizations and private individuals to create traveling libraries. Increased library services to small or rural communities and underserved populations fortified the efforts of many to seek out education and self-improvement.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metric_system_advocacy">Metric system advocacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melvil_Dewey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Metric system advocacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As an enthusiastic supporter of the decimal <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric system</a> of weights and measures, Dewey established in 1876 the American Metric Bureau.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Library_Buff_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Library_Buff-29">[29]</a></sup> Dewey also served once again as its <a href="/wiki/Secretary" title="Secretary">secretary</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Children_of_the_Code_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Children_of_the_Code-30">[30]</a></sup> He edited the Bureau's official publication, the <i>Metric Bulletin</i> (later called <i>Metric Advocate</i>), first issued in July 1876. Later in his life he was member of the Advisory Board of the All-America Standards Council (a California-based organization that promoted metrication for all countries in the Americas) and he functioned as member of the Advisory Board and chairman of the Metric Education Committee in the American Metric Association (today the U.S. Metric Association).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Reforms_and_the_Lake_Placid_Club">Other Reforms and the Lake Placid Club</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melvil_Dewey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other Reforms and the Lake Placid Club">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Late in his life Dewey helped found the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Placid_Club" title="Lake Placid Club">Lake Placid Club</a> as a health resort in New York state.</p>
<p><b>Spelling Reform</b>: His theories of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_spelling_reform" title="English spelling reform">spelling reform</a> (to which end he founded the Spelling Reform Association in 1886)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-15">[15]</a></sup> found some local success at Lake Placid: there is an "Adirondac Loj" in the area, and dinner menus of the club used his reformed spelling. A September 1927 menu is headed "Simpler spelin" and features dishes like Hadok, Poted beef with noodls, Parsli or Masht potato, Butr, Steamd rys, Letis, and Ys cream. It also advises guests that "All shud see the butiful after-glo on mountains to the east just befor sunset. Fyn vu from Golfhous porch."</p>
<p><b>Other Reforms</b>: Lake Placid also acted as a conference center hosting meetings promoting reform movements, such as the September 1899 conference on "home science" chaired by <a href="/wiki/Ellen_Swallow_Richards" title="Ellen Swallow Richards">Ellen Swallow Richards</a>, a pioneer of what later came to be called <i>home economics</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LakePlacidConference_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-LakePlacidConference-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dewey was an early promoter of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_sports" title="Winter sports">winter sports</a> in Lake Placid and was active in arranging the <a href="/wiki/1932_Winter_Olympics" title="1932 Winter Olympics">1932 Winter Olympics</a> there. He also was a founder of the Lake Placid Club Education Foundation in 1922. Under his leadership the <a href="/wiki/Northwood_School_(Lake_Placid,_New_York)" title="Northwood School (Lake Placid, New York)">Northwood School</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lake_Placid,_New_York" title="Lake Placid, New York">Lake Placid, New York</a>) prospered. He was also a founder of the Adirondack Music Festival in 1925, and served as a trustee of the <a href="/wiki/Chautauqua_Institution" title="Chautauqua Institution">Chautauqua Institution</a>.</p>
<p>In 1926 he established a southern branch of the Lake Placid Club in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. Dewey supported the idea of Lake Stearns in Florida formally changing its name to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Placid,_Florida" title="Lake Placid, Florida">Lake Placid, Florida</a>.</p>
