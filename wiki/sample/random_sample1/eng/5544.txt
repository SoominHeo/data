<p>The <b>Melkite (Greek) Catholic Church</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">كنيسة الروم الملكيين الكاثوليك</span>‎‎, <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="DIN 31635 Arabic">Kanīsat ar-Rūm al-Malakiyyīn al-Kāṯūlīk</span></i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic Church</a> in <a href="/wiki/Full_communion" title="Full communion">full communion</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> as part of the worldwide <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>. The Melkites, <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Rite" title="Byzantine Rite">Byzantine Rite</a> Catholics of mixed <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Mediterranean" title="Eastern Mediterranean">Eastern Mediterranean</a> (<a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levantine</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greek</a> origin, trace their history to the early Christians of <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a>, formerly part of <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and now in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/1st_century" title="1st century">1st century</a> AD, where <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> was introduced by <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Saint Peter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It is headed by <i>His Beatitude</i> <a href="/wiki/Patriarch" title="Patriarch">Patriarch</a> <a href="/wiki/Gregory_III_Laham" title="Gregory III Laham">Gregory III Laham</a>.</p>
<p>The Melkite Church has a high degree of ethnic homogeneity, and the church's origins lie in the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-5">[5]</a></sup> centered especially in <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>. Melkite Greek Catholics are present, however, throughout the world due to migration. Outside of the Near East, the Melkite Church has also grown through intermarriage with, and the conversion of, people of various ethnic heritages as well as transritualism. At present there is a worldwide membership of approximately 1.6 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Faulk_2007.2C_pp._9-10_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Faulk_2007.2C_pp._9-10-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNEWA_website_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-CNEWA_website-2">[2]</a></sup> While the Melkite Catholic Church's Byzantine roots and liturgical practices are rooted in those of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodoxy</a>, nonetheless the Church has maintained communion with the Catholic Church in Rome especially after its reaffirmation of its union with Rome in 1724.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parry312_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-parry312-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Melkite" title="Melkite">Melkite</a></div>
<p><i>Melkite</i>, from the <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a> word <i>malkā</i> for "King" and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> word <i>Malakī</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">ملكي</span>‎‎, meaning "royal", and by extension, "imperial"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dick9_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-dick9-7">[7]</a></sup> was originally a <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">pejorative</a> term for Middle Eastern Christians who accepted the authority of the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Chalcedon" title="Council of Chalcedon">Council of Chalcedon</a> (451) and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Byzantine_emperors" title="List of Byzantine emperors">Byzantine Emperor</a>, a term applied to them by non-Chalcedonians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dick9_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-dick9-7">[7]</a></sup> Of the Chalcedonian churches, Greek Catholics continue to use the term, while Eastern Orthodox do not.</p>
<p>The <i>Greek</i> element signifies the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Rite" title="Byzantine Rite">Byzantine Rite</a> heritage of the church, the liturgy used by all the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy" title="Eastern Orthodoxy">Eastern Orthodox Churches</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faulk5_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-faulk5-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a></i> acknowledges communion with the Church of Rome and implies participation in the universal Christian church. According to Church tradition, the Melkite Church of Antioch is the "oldest continuous Christian community in the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mliles_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-mliles-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>, the official language of the church,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-5">[5]</a></sup> it is called <i>ar-Rūm al-Kathūlīk</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">الروم الكاثوليك</span>‎‎). The Arabic word "<a href="/wiki/R%C3%BBm" title="Rûm">Rūm</a>" means Greek from the Greek word "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romioi" title="Romioi">Romioi</a>" by which the Byzantine Greeks identified themselves, deriving from the name of their land which they called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%A1%CF%89%CE%BC%CE%B9%CE%BF%CE%AF" title="Ρωμιοί">Romania</a> (meaning "the land of the Romans", Greek: Ρωμανία). The name literally means "Roman Catholic", but this refers to the Byzantine Greek heritage associated with the city of "New Rome" (Latin: Nova Roma Greek: Νέα Ρώμη), i.e. Constantinople.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origins of the Melkite Catholic Church go back to the establishment of Christianity in the Near East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> As Christianity began to spread, the disciples preached the Gospel throughout the region and were for the first time recorded to be called "Christians" in the city of <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a> (<a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts</a> 11:26), the historical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Episcopal_See" title="Episcopal See">See</a> of the Melkite Catholic Patriarchate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Scholars attribute the actual writing of the gospels in <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">Koine Greek</a> to the Hellenized Christian population of Antioch, with authors such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Luke" title="St. Luke">St. Luke</a> and others. By the 2nd century, Christianity was widespread in Antioch and throughout Syria. Growth of the church did not stop during periods of persecution, and by the end of the 4th century Christianity became the official state religion.</p>
<p>The Melkite Greek Catholic Church traces its origins to the Christian communities of the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>. The church's leadership was vested in the three Apostolic Patriarchates of the <a href="/wiki/Pentarchy" title="Pentarchy">ancient patriarchates</a>: <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>. The church's history and relation to other churches may be summarised in four defining moments.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fallout_of_the_Fourth_Ecumenical_Council">Fallout of the Fourth Ecumenical Council</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fallout of the Fourth Ecumenical Council">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first defining moment was the socio-political fallout in the wake of the Fourth Ecumenical Council, the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Chalcedon" title="Council of Chalcedon">Council of Chalcedon</a>, which took place in AD 451. Fifth-century Middle-Eastern Christian society became sharply divided between those who did and those who did not accept the outcome of the council. Those who accepted the decrees of the council, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chalcedonian" title="Chalcedonian">Chalcedonians</a>, were mainly <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>-speaking city-dwellers, and were called <a href="/wiki/Melkite" title="Melkite"><i>Melkites</i></a> (<i>imperials</i>) by the anti-Chalcedonians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> These latter were predominantly <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a>–<a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Coptic_language" title="Coptic language">Coptic</a>-speaking provincials.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fusion_with_Arabic_Language_and_Culture">Fusion with Arabic Language and Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fusion with Arabic Language and Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The second defining moment is more correctly a period of change. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Yarmuk" title="Battle of Yarmuk">Battle of Yarmuk</a> (636) took the Melkite homeland out of Byzantine control and placed it under the occupation of the Arab invaders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Whereas the Greek language and culture remained important, especially for the Melkites of Jerusalem, Antiochene Melkite tradition merged with the Arabic language and culture. Indeed, there was <a href="/wiki/Arabic_poetry#Pre-Islamic_poetry" title="Arabic poetry">Arabic Christian poetry</a> before the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, but the Antiochene blending with Arabic culture led to a degree of distancing from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch of Constantinople">Patriarch of Constantinople</a>.</p>
<p>Despite the Arab invasion, the Melkites continued to exercise an important role in the Universal Church. The Melkites played a leading role in condemning the <a href="/wiki/Iconoclasm" title="Iconoclasm">iconoclast</a> controversy when it re-appeared in the early 9th century, and were among the first of the Eastern churches to respond to the introduction of the <i><a href="/wiki/Filioque" title="Filioque">filioque</a></i> clause in the West.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Communion_with_the_Roman_Catholic_Church">Communion with the Roman Catholic Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Communion with the Roman Catholic Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pius_XI_with_Patriarch_of_the_Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="569" data-file-width="888" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Pius_XI_with_Patriarch_of_the_Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church.jpg/220px-Pius_XI_with_Patriarch_of_the_Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Pius_XI_with_Patriarch_of_the_Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church.jpg/330px-Pius_XI_with_Patriarch_of_the_Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Pius_XI_with_Patriarch_of_the_Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church.jpg/440px-Pius_XI_with_Patriarch_of_the_Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The third defining moment were the Councils of Reunion in which the Orthodox hierarchs accepted communion with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/See_of_Rome" title="See of Rome">See of Rome</a> in 1729 after a long period of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schism_(religion)" title="Schism (religion)">schism</a>. In 1054, Patriarch <a href="/wiki/Michael_I_Cerularius" title="Michael I Cerularius">Michael I Cerularius</a> and Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Humbert_of_Silva_Candida" title="Humbert of Silva Candida">Humbert of Silva Candida</a> had <a href="/wiki/Excommunication" title="Excommunication">excommunicated</a> each other, thus formalizing a schism that had been developing for many years. The Melkite Patriarch Peter III of Antioch rejected the quarrel of the Latin Cardinal and the Patriarch of Constantinople. In 1965, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_VI" title="Pope Paul VI">Pope Paul VI</a> and Patriarch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athenagoras_I" title="Athenagoras I">Athenagoras I</a> "consigned these excommunications to oblivion."</p>
<p>However, during the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusades</a> the Crusaders introduced Latin prelates into the apostolic sees of the East, and the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Crusade" title="Fourth Crusade">Fourth Crusade</a> saw the sack of the great city of Constantinople and its domination by the Crusaders for fifty-seven years. These developments brought the East-West quarrel home to everyone but there was no declaration of schism. Since there had never been any formal division from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East-West_Schism" title="East-West Schism">East-West Schism</a> these 'converts' of the Latin missionaries simply became a pro-Western, pro-Catholic party within Eastern Orthodoxy. Throughout the 17th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuits" title="Jesuits">Jesuits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Friars_Minor_Capuchin" title="Order of Friars Minor Capuchin">Capuchins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carmelites" title="Carmelites">Carmelites</a> established missions with the consent of the local Orthodox bishops in the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominicans</a> had been in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> since the 14th century.</p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Florence" title="Council of Florence">Council of Florence</a> (1439) the Patriarch of Constantinople and the Emperor accepted union with the West hoping for aid to save Constantinople from Islam. Neither of these unions lasted, nor was any significant aid forthcoming from the warring kingdoms of a soon to be torn-apart Europe.</p>
<p>From 1342, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a> <a href="/wiki/Friar" title="Friar">friars</a> opened <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_(Christian)" title="Mission (Christian)">missions</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Mediterranean" title="Eastern Mediterranean">Eastern Mediterranean</a>, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">Damascus</a> and their teaching had important influence over the Melkite clergy and people. Yet, in the Melkite tradition it was the Jesuits, founded only in 1534, who were really decisive in the formation of the Catholic party in the Orthodox Patriarchate of Antioch. The Jesuits were not friars but something like the highly educated priests of the Patriarchal Chancery, which made them more acceptable.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Election_of_Cyril_VI">Election of Cyril VI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Election of Cyril VI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The fourth defining moment was the election of <a href="/wiki/Cyril_VI_Tanas" title="Cyril VI Tanas">Cyril VI Tanas</a>, in 1724, by the Melkite <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishops</a> of Syria as the new <a href="/wiki/Patriarch_of_Antioch" title="Patriarch of Antioch">Patriarch of Antioch</a>. As Cyril was considered to be pro-Western, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_Jeremias_III_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch Jeremias III of Constantinople">Patriarch Jeremias III of Constantinople</a> feared that his authority would be compromised. Therefore, Jeremias declared Cyril's election to be invalid, excommunicated him, and ordained the deacon <a href="/wiki/Sylvester_of_Antioch" title="Sylvester of Antioch">Sylvester of Antioch</a>, a Greek <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monk</a> a priest and bishop, then appointed him to the patriarchal See of Antioch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parry312_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-parry312-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sylvester exacerbated divisions with his heavy-handed rule of the church as many Melkites acknowledged Cyril's claim to the patriarchal throne. It was obvious to all that Cyril had been legitimately elected and consecrated, and that Jeremias had attempted to remove him only to bolster his own authority over the Antiochian Patriarchate. (This Greek domination over the Byzantine Orthodox Patriarchate of Antioch lasted until 1899.) Jeremias and Sylvester began a five-year campaign of persecution against Cyril and the Melkite faithful who supported him, enforced by Ottoman Turkish troops.</p>
<p>Five years after the election of <a href="/wiki/Cyril_VI_Tanas" title="Cyril VI Tanas">Cyril VI</a>, in 1729, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XIII" title="Pope Benedict XIII">Pope Benedict XIII</a> recognized Cyril as the legitimate Patriarch of Antioch and recognized his followers as being in <a href="/wiki/Full_communion" title="Full communion">full communion</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> From this time onwards, the Melkite Greek-Catholic Church has existed separately from and in parallel to the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Church_of_Antioch" title="Greek Orthodox Church of Antioch">Greek Orthodox Church of Antioch</a> in <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">Western Asia</a>; the latter is no longer referred to as <i>Melkite</i>.</p>
<p>The Melkite Greek Catholic Church has played an important role in the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Arab_Christians" title="Arab Christians">Arabic Christianity</a>. It has always been led by Arabic-speaking Christians, whereas its Orthodox counterpart had Greek patriarchs until 1899. Indeed, at the very beginning of her separate existence, around 1725, one of her most illustrious lay leaders, the savant and theologian, <a href="/wiki/Abdallah_Zakher" title="Abdallah Zakher">Abdallah Zakher</a> of <a href="/wiki/Aleppo" title="Aleppo">Aleppo</a> (1684–1748) set up the first <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Arab_world" title="Arab world">Arab world</a>. In 1835, <a href="/wiki/Maximos_III_Mazloum" title="Maximos III Mazloum">Maximos III Mazloum</a>, Melkite Greek Catholic Patriarch of Antioch, was recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> as the leader of a <i><a href="/wiki/Millet_(Ottoman_Empire)" title="Millet (Ottoman Empire)">millet</a></i>, a distinctive religious community within the Empire. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XVI" title="Pope Gregory XVI">Pope Gregory XVI</a> gave <a href="/wiki/Maximos_III_Mazloum" title="Maximos III Mazloum">Maximos III Mazloum</a> the triple-patriarchate of Antioch, Alexandria and Jerusalem, a title that is still held by the leader of the Melkite Catholic Church.</p>

<p>In 1847, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_IX" title="Pope Pius IX">Pope Pius IX</a> (1846–1878), reinstituted the Latin Patriarchate of Jerusalem in the person of the young, 34-year-old, zealous <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Valerga" title="Giuseppe Valerga">Giuseppe Valerga</a> (1813–1872), whom the indigenous hierarchs nicknamed "The Butcher" because of his fierce opposition to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy" title="Eastern Orthodoxy">Eastern Orthodox</a> churches of the Holy Land. When he arrived in Jerusalem in 1847, there were 4,200 Latin Catholics and when he died in 1872, the number had doubled.</p>
<p>Under pressure from the Roman curia to adopt <a href="/wiki/Latin_Church" title="Latin Church">Latin Church</a> practices, Patriarch <a href="/wiki/Clement_Bahouth" title="Clement Bahouth">Clement Bahouth</a> introduced the Gregorian calendar used by the Latin and Maronite Churches in 1857; that act caused serious problems within the Melkite church, resulting in a short-lived schism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dick_2004.2C_p._38_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dick_2004.2C_p._38-16">[16]</a></sup> Conflicts in the Melkite church escalated to the point where Clement abdicated his position as patriarch.</p>
<p>Clement's successor, Patriarch <a href="/wiki/Gregory_II_Youssef" title="Gregory II Youssef">Gregory II Youssef</a> (1864–1897) worked to restore peace within the community, successfully healing the lingering schism. He also focused on improving church institutions. During his reign Gregory founded both the Patriarchal College in Beirut in 1865 and the Patriarchal College in Damascus in 1875 and re-opened the Melkite seminary of <a href="/wiki/Ain_Traz" title="Ain Traz">Ain Traz</a> in 1866.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dick_2004.2C_p._38_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dick_2004.2C_p._38-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> He also promoted the establishment of Saint Ann's Seminary, Jerusalem, in 1882 by the <a href="/wiki/White_Fathers" title="White Fathers">White Fathers</a> for the training of the Melkite clergy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hatt-%C4%B1_H%C3%BCmayun_of_1856" title="Hatt-ı Hümayun of 1856">Hatt-ı Hümayun of 1856</a>, decreed by Sultan <a href="/wiki/Abd%C3%BClmecid_I" title="Abdülmecid I">Abdülmecid I</a>, the situation of Christians in the Near East improved. This allowed Gregory to successfully encourage greater participation by the Melkite laity in both church administration as well as public affairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dick_2004.2C_p._38_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dick_2004.2C_p._38-16">[16]</a></sup> Gregory also took an interest in ministering to the growing number of Melkites who had emigrated to the Americas. In 1889 he dispatched Father Ibrahim Beshawate of the Basilian Salvatorian Order in Saida, Lebanon to New York in order to minister to the growing Syrian community there. According to historian Philip Hitte, Beshawate was the first permanent priest in the United States from the Near East from among the Melkite, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maronite" title="Maronite">Maronite</a>, and Antiochian Orthodox Churches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gregory was also a prominent proponent of Eastern ecclesiology at the First Vatican Council. In the two discourses he gave at the Council on May 19 and June 14, 1870 he insisted on the importance of conforming to the decisions of the Council of Florence, of not creating innovations such as papal infallibility, but accepting what had been decided by common agreement between the Greeks and the Latins at the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Florence" title="Council of Florence">Council of Florence</a>, especially with regard to the issue of papal primacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> He was keenly aware of the disastrous impact that the dogmatic definition of <a href="/wiki/Papal_infallibility" title="Papal infallibility">papal infallibility</a> would have on relations with the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a> and emerged as a prominent opponent of the dogma at the Council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parry_1999.2C_p._313_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parry_1999.2C_p._313-21">[21]</a></sup> He also defended the rights and privileges of the patriarchs according to the canons promulgated by earlier ecumenical councils. Speaking at the Council on May 19, 1870, Patriarch Gregory asserted:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The Eastern Church attributes to the pope the most complete and highest power, however in a manner where the fullness and primacy are in harmony with the rights of the patriarchal sees. This is why, in virtue of and ancient right founded on customs, the Roman Pontiffs did not, except in very significant cases, exercise over these sees the ordinary and immediate jurisdiction that we are asked now to define without any exception. This definition would completely destroy the constitution of the entire Greek church. That is why my conscience as a pastor refuses to accept this constitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Patriarch Gregory refused to sign the Council's dogmatic declaration on papal infallibility. He and the seven other Melkite bishops present voted <i>non placet</i> at the general congregation and left Rome prior to the adoption of the dogmatic constitution <i>Pastor Aeternus</i> on papal infallibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Descy_1993.2C_p._64_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Descy_1993.2C_p._64-23">[23]</a></sup> Other members of the anti-infallibilist minority, both from the Latin church and from other Eastern Catholic churches, also left the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Descy_1993.2C_p._64_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Descy_1993.2C_p._64-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the First Vatican Council concluded an emissary of the Roman Curia was dispatched to secure the signatures of the patriarch and the Melkite delegation. Patriarch Gregory and the Melkite bishops subscribed to it, but with the qualifying clause of the used at the Council of Florence attached: <i>"except the rights and privileges of Eastern patriarchs."</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parry_1999.2C_p._313_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Parry_1999.2C_p._313-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> He earned the enmity of Pius IX for this; during his next visit to the <a href="/wiki/Pontiff" title="Pontiff">pontiff</a> Gregory was cast to the floor at Pius' feet by the papal guard while the pope placed his foot on the patriarch's head.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Despite this, Patriarch Gregory and the Melkite Catholic Church remained committed to their union with the Church of Rome. Relationships with the Vatican improved following the death of Pius IX and the subsequent election of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leo_XIII" title="Leo XIII">Leo XIII</a> as pontiff. Leo's <a href="/wiki/Encyclical" title="Encyclical">encyclical</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Orientalium_dignitas" title="Orientalium dignitas">Orientalium dignitas</a></i> addressed some of the Eastern Catholic Churches' concerns on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liturgical_latinisation" title="Liturgical latinisation">latinization</a> and the centralizing tendencies of Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dick_2004.2C_p._39_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dick_2004.2C_p._39-26">[26]</a></sup> Leo also confirmed that the limitations placed on the Armenian Catholic patriarch by Pius IX's 1867 letter <i>Reversurus</i> would not apply to the Melkite Church; further, Leo formally recognized an expansion of Patriarch Gregory's jurisdiction to include all Melkites throughout the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dick_2004.2C_p._39_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dick_2004.2C_p._39-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vatican_II_conflicts_over_Latin_and_Melkite_traditions">Vatican II conflicts over Latin and Melkite traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Vatican II conflicts over Latin and Melkite traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Patriarch <a href="/wiki/Maximos_IV_Sayegh" title="Maximos IV Sayegh">Maximos IV Sayegh</a> took part in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council</a> where he championed the Eastern tradition of Christianity, and won a great deal of respect from Orthodox observers at the council as well as the approbation of the Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_Athenagoras_I_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch Athenagoras I of Constantinople">Athenagoras I</a>.</p>
<p>Following the Second Vatican Council the Melkites moved to restoring traditional worship. This involved both the restoration of Melkite practices such as administering the <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Eucharist</a> to infants following post-baptismal <a href="/wiki/Chrismation" title="Chrismation">chrismation</a> as well as removal of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liturgical_latinisation" title="Liturgical latinisation">Latin-rite elements</a> such as communion rails and confessionals. In the pre-conciliar days, the leaders of this trend were members of "The Cairo Circle", a group of young priests centered on the Patriarchal College in Cairo. This group included Fathers <a href="/wiki/Maximos_V_Hakim" title="Maximos V Hakim">George Selim Hakim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Tawil" title="Joseph Tawil">Joseph Tawil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elias_Zoghby" title="Elias Zoghby">Elias Zoghby</a> and former Jesuit <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oreste_Kerame&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oreste Kerame (page does not exist)">Oreste Kerame</a>; they later became bishops and participated in the Second Vatican Council, and saw their efforts vindicated.</p>
<p>These reforms led to protests by some Melkite churches that the de-latinisation had gone too far. During the Patriarchate of <a href="/wiki/Maximos_IV_Sayegh" title="Maximos IV Sayegh">Maximos IV</a> (Sayegh), some Melkites in the United States objected to the use of the vernacular in the celebration of the Divine Liturgy, a movement that was spearheaded by the future archbishop of Nazareth, Father <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Raya" title="Joseph Raya">Joseph Raya</a> of Birmingham, Alabama. The issue garnered national news coverage after Bishop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fulton_Sheen" title="Fulton Sheen">Fulton Sheen</a> celebrated a Pontifical Divine Liturgy in English at the Melkite National convention in Birmingham in 1958, parts of which were televised on the national news.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resolution">Resolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Resolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1960, the issue was resolved by Pope John XXIII at the request of Patriarch Maximos IV in favour of the use of vernacular languages in the celebration of the Divine Liturgy. Pope John also consecrated a Melkite priest, Father <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Acacius_Coussa" title="Gabriel Acacius Coussa">Gabriel Acacius Coussa</a>, as a bishop, using the Byzantine Rite and the papal tiara as a crown. Bishop Coussa was almost immediately elevated to the cardinalate, but died two years later. His cause for canonization was introduced by his religious order, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilian_Alepian_Order" title="Basilian Alepian Order">Basilian Alepian Order</a>.</p>
<p>Further protests against the de-latinisation of the church occurred during the patriarchate of <a href="/wiki/Maximos_V_Hakim" title="Maximos V Hakim">Maximos V Hakim</a> (1967–2000) when some church officials who supported Latin traditions protested against allowing the ordination of married men as priests. Today the church sees itself as an authentic Orthodox church in communion with the Roman Catholic Church. As such it has a role as a voice of the east within the western church, a bridge between faiths and peoples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p>Due to heavy emigration from the Eastern Mediterranean, which began with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1860_Lebanon_conflict" title="1860 Lebanon conflict">Damascus massacres</a> of 1860, in which most of the Christian communities were attacked, the Melkite Greek Catholic Church today is found throughout the world and no longer made up exclusively of faithful of Eastern Mediterranean origin.</p>
<p>The Patriarchate of Maximos V saw many advances in the worldwide presence of the Melkite Church, called "the Diaspora": <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eparchies" title="Eparchies">Eparchies</a> (the Eastern equivalent of a diocese) were established in the United States, Canada, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, Australia, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and Mexico in response to the continued emptying of the Eastern Mediterranean of her native Christian peoples. Some historians state<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> that after the revolution in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> in 1952, many Melkites left Egypt due to the renewed Islamic, nativist and socialist policies of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasser" title="Nasser">Nasser</a> regime. In 1950, the richest Melkite community in the world was in Egypt. In 1945 the most populous single diocese was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akko" title="Akko">Akko</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haifa" title="Haifa">Haifa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nazareth" title="Nazareth">Nazareth</a> and all <a href="/wiki/Galilee" title="Galilee">Galilee</a>.</p>
<p>In 1967, a native Egyptian of Syrian-Aleppin descent, George Selim Hakim, was elected the successor of Maximos IV, and took the name <a href="/wiki/Maximos_V_Hakim" title="Maximos V Hakim">Maximos V</a>. He was to reign until he retired at the age of 92 in the Jubilee Year of 2000. He reposed on the feast of Saints Peter and Paul, June 29, 2001. He was succeeded by Archbishop Lutfi Laham, who took the name Gregory III.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Melkite Catholic Church is in full communion with the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> (the Latin Cathlic Pope of Rome and his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Congregation_for_the_Eastern_Churches" title="Roman Congregation for the Eastern Churches">Roman Congregation for the Eastern Churches</a>), where the Patrirch is represented by his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Procurator_at_Rome" title="Procurator at Rome">Procurator at Rome</a>, but fully follows the traditions and customs of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">Byzantine Christianity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The traditional languages of worship are [Arabic]] and <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>, but today, services are held in a variety of languages, depending on the country where the church is located.</p>
<p>The Melkite <a href="/wiki/Synod" title="Synod">Synod</a> of Bishops, composed of all of the Church's bishops, meets each year to consider administrative, theological and issues affecting the entire Church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Patriarchate">Patriarchate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Patriarchate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The current Melkite Greek Catholic Patriarch of Antioch and All the East, and Alexandria and Jerusalem is <a href="/wiki/Gregory_III_Laham" title="Gregory III Laham">Gregory III Laham</a>. The patriarchate is based in the <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syrian</a> capital <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">Damascus</a>, but it formally remains one of the Eastern Catholic Patriarchs claiming the <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_succession" title="Apostolic succession">apostolic succession</a> to the Ancient see of Antioch, and has been permanently granted the styles of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titular_Patriarch" title="Titular Patriarch">Titular Patriarch</a> of Alexandria and Jerusalem, two other patriarchates with multiple Catholic succession.</p>
<p>The patriarchate is administered by a permanent <a href="/wiki/Synod" title="Synod">synod</a>, which includes the Patriarch and four bishops, the ordinary tribunal of the patriarch for legal affairs, the patriarchal <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Economos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Economos (page does not exist)">economos</a> who serves as financial administrator and a <a href="/wiki/Diocesan_chancery" title="Diocesan chancery">chancery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melkite_Greek_Catholic_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are also several patriarchal organizations with offices and chapters throughout the world, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>the Global Melkite Association, a group which networks eparchies, monasteries, schools and Melkite associations</li>
<li>Friends of The Holy Land, a lay charitable organization active in the diaspora which provides clothing, medicine and liturgical items for churches and communities in the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Land" title="Holy Land">Holy Land</a> (Israel, Palestine, Jordan), Lebanon, Egypt and Syria.</li>
</ul>
