<p><b>Memphis International Airport</b> (<a href="/wiki/International_Air_Transport_Association_airport_code" title="International Air Transport Association airport code">IATA</a>: <b>MEM</b>, <a href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization_airport_code" title="International Civil Aviation Organization airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>KMEM</b>, <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">FAA</a> <a href="/wiki/Location_identifier" title="Location identifier">LID</a>: <b>MEM</b>) is a civil-military airport seven miles (11.2 km) southeast of downtown <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Shelby_County,_Tennessee" title="Shelby County, Tennessee">Shelby County, Tennessee</a>.</p>
<p>Memphis International Airport is home to the <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Express" title="FedEx Express">FedEx Express</a> global hub, which processes many of the company's packages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Nonstop FedEx destinations from Memphis include cities across the continental United States, Europe, Middle East, Asia and South America. From 1993 to 2009 Memphis had the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World%27s_busiest_airports_by_cargo_traffic" title="World's busiest airports by cargo traffic">largest cargo operations</a> of any airport worldwide. MEM dropped into second position in 2010, behind <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_International_Airport" title="Hong Kong International Airport">Hong Kong</a>; however, it remained the <a href="/wiki/List_of_the_busiest_airports_in_the_United_States#10_busiest_cargo_airports_in_the_US_by_total_cargo_throughput_.282010.29" title="List of the busiest airports in the United States">busiest cargo airport in the United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gac_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-gac-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2015, Memphis International Airport had a passenger count of 3.75 million, up slightly from the 2014 year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a> dropped Memphis as a hub airport after continually reducing flights following its 2008 merger with <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Airlines" title="Northwest Airlines">Northwest Airlines</a>. As of July 2014 MEM averaged 83 total passenger flights per day on all of the airlines serving the city. The airport has since added several airlines, including <a href="/wiki/Allegiant_Air" title="Allegiant Air">Allegiant Air</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frontier_Airlines" title="Frontier Airlines">Frontier Airlines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a>, which has increased competition among the carriers. Since Delta's departure as a hub operation, average round trip prices have declined significantly. The July–September 2014 quarter alone saw a 4.7% decline from the quarter a year earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memphis_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:12-30-2007_007.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1680" data-file-width="2592" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/12-30-2007_007.JPG/220px-12-30-2007_007.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/12-30-2007_007.JPG/330px-12-30-2007_007.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/12-30-2007_007.JPG/440px-12-30-2007_007.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Memphis Municipal Airport opened on a 200-acre (81 ha) plot of farmland just over seven miles (11 km) from downtown Memphis. During its early years the airport had three hangars and an unpaved runway; passenger and air mail service was provided by <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chicago_and_Southern_Air_Lines" title="Chicago and Southern Air Lines">Chicago and Southern Air Lines</a> (acquired by <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a> in 1953). In 1939 <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Air_Lines" title="Eastern Air Lines">Eastern Air Lines</a> arrived; that March Eastern had one departure a day to Muscle Shoals and beyond, American had four east/west and C&amp;S had four north/south.</p>
<p>During World War II the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">United States Army Air Forces</a> <a href="/wiki/Military_Air_Transport_Service" title="Military Air Transport Service">Air Transport Command</a> 4th Ferrying Group used Memphis while sending new aircraft overseas. In April 1951 the runways were 6000-ft 2/20, 6530-ft 9/27, 4370-ft 14/32 and 4950-ft 17/35 (the airport was all north of Winchester Rd during the 1950s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>)</p>
<p>The April 1957 OAG shows 64 weekday departures: 25 on Delta, 18 American, 7 Southern, 5 Eastern, 4 Braniff, 3 Trans-Texas and 2 Capital. American DC-6s flew nonstop to Washington and New York, but westward nonstops didn't reach beyond Ft Worth and Kansas City until American started Los Angeles in 1964. The first scheduled jets were Delta 880s ORD-MEM-MSY and back, starting in July–August 1960.</p>
<p>The current terminal was designed by <a href="/wiki/Roy_Harrover" title="Roy Harrover">Mann &amp; Harrover</a> and cost $6.5 million. It opened on June 7, 1963 and Memphis Municipal changed its name to Memphis International in 1969. In 1985–86 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_Airlines_(1979-1986)" title="Republic Airlines (1979-1986)">Republic Airlines</a> began flights to Mexico. The terminal was expanded for $31.6 million in 1974, adding two new concourses and extending the others, which were designed by <a href="/wiki/Roy_Harrover" title="Roy Harrover">Roy P. Harrover &amp; Associates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1995 international service expanded when <a href="/wiki/KLM" title="KLM">KLM</a> began service to <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Airport_Schiphol" title="Amsterdam Airport Schiphol">Amsterdam</a>. Flights to Amsterdam ended on September 3, 2012, part of Delta's cutbacks in Memphis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> leaving Cancun, Mexico and Freeport, Bahamas as the remaining international flights from Memphis, operated by Aeromexico and Bahamasair on behalf of Vacation Express. Air Canada is resuming daily non-stop service to Toronto beginning May 2017<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Southern_Airways" title="Southern Airways">Southern Airways</a> was an important regional carrier at Memphis in the 1960s; it merged into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_Airlines_(1979-1986)" title="Republic Airlines (1979-1986)">Republic Airlines</a> in 1979 as the first large merger after the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Airline_Deregulation_Act" title="Airline Deregulation Act">Airline Deregulation Act</a>. With the dismantling of the Civil Aeronautics Board (CAB) flight approval requirements, airlines began developing around a large hub model as opposed to the former point-to-point networks that were common before deregulation. Republic established Memphis as a hub operation in 1985 before merging into <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Airlines" title="Northwest Airlines">Northwest Airlines</a> in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2008 Delta Air Lines bought Northwest.</p>
<p>Federal Express (now <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Express" title="FedEx Express">FedEx Express</a>) began operations in Memphis in 1973. It opened its current "SuperHub" facility on the north side of the airport in 1981.</p>
<p>In 2008 the airport began expanding its control tower and parking garages. The new tower cost $72.6 million and is 336 feet tall, more than double the old tower height.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> An $81 million, 7-story parking garage replaced two surface lots adding 6,500 parking spaces. $11 million was spent on a covered moving walkway between the garages and the terminal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 2009 the airport has been a small hub for regional carrier <a href="/wiki/SeaPort_Airlines" title="SeaPort Airlines">SeaPort Airlines</a>. Utilizing the single-engine <a href="/wiki/Cessna_208_Caravan" title="Cessna 208 Caravan">Cessna 208 Caravan</a> turboprop SeaPort has flights to communities in <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Essential_Air_Service" title="Essential Air Service">Essential Air Service</a> program. <a href="/wiki/SeaPort_Airlines" title="SeaPort Airlines">SeaPort Airlines</a> is based at the private aviation terminal, not the main passenger terminal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, the Memphis-Shelby County Airport Authority announced a planned $114 million renovation of the airport. This renovation will include demolishing the largely-vacant south ends of concourses A and C, which will allow aircraft to more easily access the larger B concourse. The remainder of the A and C concourses will remain and be ready to use for any potential growth in the future. In addition, the plan calls for the widening and modernization of the B concourse, which most flights will be directed to when the renovation is complete. The renovation, expected to start in late 2015 and end around 2020, will leave the airport with about 60 gates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memphis_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Memphis International Airport is governed by the Memphis and Shelby County Airport Authority. <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">Board of directors</a> consist of five members appointed by the Memphis mayor and two members nominated by the <a href="/wiki/Shelby_County,_Tennessee" title="Shelby County, Tennessee">Shelby County</a> mayor. All board members are confirmed by the <a href="/wiki/Memphis_City_Council" title="Memphis City Council">Memphis City Council</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>Memphis International Airport covers 3,900 acres (1,600 ha) and has four paved <a href="/wiki/Runway" title="Runway">runways</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAA_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAA-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>18C/36C: 11,120 ft × 150 ft (3,389 m × 46 m) Concrete</li>
<li>18L/36R: 9,000 ft × 150 ft (2,743 m × 46 m) Concrete</li>
<li>18R/36L: 9,320 ft × 150 ft (2,841 m × 46 m) Concrete</li>
<li>9/27: 8,946 ft × 150 ft (2,727 m × 46 m) Concrete.</li>
</ul>
<p>Runway 9/27<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> reopened for traffic on November 30, 2009 after nine months of resurfacing. The new runway has a more durable concrete surface, and opened in time for the peak of the FedEx shipping season.</p>
<p>For the 12-month period ending May 31, 2014 the airport had 225,592 aircraft operations, an average of 618 per day: 71% scheduled commercial, 18% <a href="/wiki/Air_taxi" title="Air taxi">air taxi</a>, 10% <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a>, and 1% military. At that time there were 93 aircraft based at this airport: 48% jet, 15% multi-engine, 17% single-engine, 10% military, and 10% helicopter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAA_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAA-17">[17]</a></sup> The Memphis <a href="/wiki/Area_Control_Center" title="Area Control Center">Air Route Traffic Control Center</a> (ARTCC) is on the airport grounds at 3229 Democrat Road, TN 38118.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminals">Terminals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memphis_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Terminals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Terminal A contains 7 gates: A21, A23, A25, A27, A29, A31 and A33. Terminal A is only used by <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a>.</li>
<li>Terminal B contains 42 gates: B1-B20, B22-B43. <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Allegiant_Air" title="Allegiant Air">Allegiant Air</a> operate out of Terminal B. <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a> operates a Delta Sky Club lounge in Terminal B. Seasonally, the terminal is used by international charters which operate out of the airport's international gates B42 and B43. International travelers are required to pass through a <a href="/wiki/Transportation_Security_Administration" title="Transportation Security Administration">TSA</a> security checkpoint after clearing customs. This is required because the customs hall exits into the concourse instead of the main lobby.</li>
<li>Terminal C contains 18 gates: C1-C5, C7-C11, C12A/C12B, C14A/C14B, C16, C18, C20 and C22. Terminal C is currently used by <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frontier_Airlines" title="Frontier Airlines">Frontier Airlines</a>, <a href="/wiki/GLO_Airlines" title="GLO Airlines">GLO Airlines</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines" title="United Airlines">United Airlines</a>.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memphis_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/164th_Airlift_Wing" title="164th Airlift Wing">164th Airlift Wing</a></div>
<p>The <b>164th Airlift Wing</b> of the <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Air_National_Guard" title="Tennessee Air National Guard">Tennessee Air National Guard</a> is based at the co-located <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Air_National_Guard_Base" title="Memphis Air National Guard Base">Memphis Air National Guard Base</a>, operating <a href="/wiki/Boeing_C-17_Globemaster_III" title="Boeing C-17 Globemaster III">C-17 Globemaster III</a> transport aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memphis_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>On August 11, 1984, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Douglas_C-47" title="Douglas C-47">Douglas C-47</a> N70003 of Aviation Enterprises crashed shortly after take-off from Memphis International Airport on a domestic non-scheduled passenger flight to <a href="/wiki/O%27Hare_International_Airport" title="O'Hare International Airport">O'Hare International Airport</a>, Chicago. All three people on board died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASN110884_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASN110884-24">[24]</a></sup> A missing spark plug on the port engine caused a loss of power. Maintenance involving the removal of the spark plugs had been performed the previous day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL84FA251_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-ATL84FA251-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>On April 7, 1994, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FedEx_Flight_705" title="FedEx Flight 705">FedEx Flight 705</a> bound for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Jose_International_Airport" title="San Jose International Airport">San Jose, CA</a> experienced an attempted hijacking shortly after takeoff. FedEx employee Auburn Calloway tried to hijack the plane in order to crash it into the FedEx hub at Memphis International, in a <a href="/wiki/Kamikaze" title="Kamikaze">Kamikaze</a>-style attack. The crew fought him off and returned to Memphis.</li>
<li>On December 18, 2003, <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Express_Flight_647" title="FedEx Express Flight 647">FedEx Express Flight 647</a> veered off the runway after the landing gear collapsed upon landing from <a href="/wiki/Oakland_International_Airport" title="Oakland International Airport">Oakland International Airport</a> (OAK). The aircraft was immediately engulfed in flames. All 5 crew members escaped by exiting via the cockpit window.</li>
<li>On July 28, 2006, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FedEx_Flight_630" title="FedEx Flight 630">FedEx Flight 630</a>'s landing gear collapsed upon landing at Memphis International Airport after a flight from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seattle-Tacoma_International_Airport" title="Seattle-Tacoma International Airport">Seattle-Tacoma International Airport</a>. After coming to a stop, the plane caught fire, engulfing the left wing and engine. While the three crew members sustained injuries, they all survived. The aircraft was written off.</li>
</ul>
