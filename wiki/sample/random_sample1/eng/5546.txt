<p>The <b>Memphis Mad Dogs</b> were a <a href="/wiki/Canadian_football" title="Canadian football">Canadian football</a> team that played the <a href="/wiki/1995_CFL_season" title="1995 CFL season">1995 season</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Football_League" title="Canadian Football League">Canadian Football League</a>. The Mad Dogs were part of a failed attempt to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CFL_USA" title="CFL USA">expand the CFL into the United States</a>. They played at <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Bowl_Memorial_Stadium" title="Liberty Bowl Memorial Stadium">Liberty Bowl Memorial Stadium</a>.</p>
<p>The team's ownership group included <a href="/wiki/Frederick_W._Smith" title="Frederick W. Smith">Fred Smith</a>, founder of <a href="/wiki/FedEx" title="FedEx">FedEx</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Franchise_history">Franchise history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memphis_Mad_Dogs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Franchise history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to the Mad Dogs, Fred Smith was part of an ownership group (along with such entities as former <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Showboats" title="Memphis Showboats">Memphis Showboats</a> owner <a href="/wiki/William_Dunavant" title="William Dunavant">William Dunavant</a> and the estate of <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>) that tried to get a <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> team into Memphis in 1993. The <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Hound_Dogs" title="Memphis Hound Dogs">Memphis Hound Dogs</a>, as the proposed team was to be called, was one of five teams to be considered, but was passed over in favor of the <a href="/wiki/Carolina_Panthers" title="Carolina Panthers">Carolina Panthers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville_Jaguars" title="Jacksonville Jaguars">Jacksonville Jaguars</a>. Smith, after briefly considering a proposed "new league" backed by <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> then turned to the CFL. The league was very impressed with Smith; his group was the richest in CFL history at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It seriously considered selling either the <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_Tiger-Cats" title="Hamilton Tiger-Cats">Hamilton Tiger-Cats</a> or <a href="/wiki/Calgary_Stampeders" title="Calgary Stampeders">Calgary Stampeders</a> to Smith. After those teams resolved their ownership situations, Smith's group was granted an expansion franchise for 1995. With Presley's estate no longer involved, the team's name was changed from the "Hound Dogs" to the "Mad Dogs", ostensibly through a name-the-team contest.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On_the_field">On the field</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memphis_Mad_Dogs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: On the field">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Mad Dogs hired <a href="/wiki/Pepper_Rodgers" title="Pepper Rodgers">Pepper Rodgers</a> as their head coach, who was familiar to Memphis pro football fans as he was the head coach of one of the city's previous pro football teams, the <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Showboats" title="Memphis Showboats">Memphis Showboats</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USFL" title="USFL">USFL</a>; the Mad Dogs had also hired Steve Erhart, the Showboats' general manager, in the same capacity, and even hired one of the Showboats' backup quarterbacks (<a href="/wiki/Mike_Kelley_(American_football)" title="Mike Kelley (American football)">Mike Kelley</a>, who had been mostly out of work since the USFL's failure). In many ways, building the Mad Dogs as a continuation of the Showboats was an attempt to emulate the <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_Gold_Miners" title="Sacramento Gold Miners">Sacramento Gold Miners</a>, who had built on the previously successful <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_Surge" title="Sacramento Surge">Sacramento Surge</a>. Rodgers, a Memphis native, also had previous coaching experience with the <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Institute_of_Technology" title="Georgia Institute of Technology">Georgia Tech</a> <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Tech_Yellow_Jackets" title="Georgia Tech Yellow Jackets">Yellow Jackets</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">UCLA</a> <a href="/wiki/UCLA_Bruins" title="UCLA Bruins">Bruins</a> at the university level. The team's mascot was a black <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labrador_retriever" title="Labrador retriever">Labrador retriever</a> named Alien, who was known for charging the field and retrieving the kicking tee following each kickoff.</p>
<p>The Mad Dogs tried to copy the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Stallions" title="Baltimore Stallions">Baltimore Stallions</a>' blueprint by getting staff and players who had previous CFL experience. As part of that blueprint, the Mad Dogs hired former CFL coach <a href="/wiki/Adam_Rita" title="Adam Rita">Adam Rita</a> to become their new offensive coordinator. Rita was mostly known for coaching the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Argonauts" title="Toronto Argonauts">Toronto Argonauts</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Edmonton_Eskimos" title="Edmonton Eskimos">Edmonton Eskimos</a> to <a href="/wiki/Grey_Cup" title="Grey Cup">Grey Cup</a> championships in 1991 and 1993. The Mad Dogs then signed veteran QB <a href="/wiki/Damon_Allen" title="Damon Allen">Damon Allen</a>, who won the <a href="/wiki/Grey_Cup" title="Grey Cup">Grey Cup</a> with the Eskimos in 1987 and 1993 (the latter with Rita), earning Grey Cup MVP honors in both years. Other notable players on offense included <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Brown_(Canadian_football)" title="Eddie Brown (Canadian football)">Eddie Brown</a> (SB), <a href="/wiki/Joe_Horn" title="Joe Horn">Joe Horn</a> (WR) and former <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">NFL</a> kicker <a href="/wiki/Donald_Igwebuike" title="Donald Igwebuike">Donald Igwebuike</a>.</p>
<p>However, the offense was only able to score a total of 346 points, last in the CFL behind the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Rough_Riders" title="Ottawa Rough Riders">Ottawa Rough Riders</a>. On a positive note, the Mad Dogs were known for their strong defensive work that was rated second, behind Edmonton, in the CFL for giving up the least number of points with 364, due in large part to the strong defensive play of <a href="/wiki/Tim_Cofield" title="Tim Cofield">Tim Cofield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Harding" title="Rodney Harding">Rodney Harding</a>.</p>
<p>One of the reasons for poor offense and great defense was the size of the field. The Liberty Bowl was not as well suited to the Canadian game as most U.S. stadiums. The stands were very close to the field of play, making it difficult to reconfigure the field to CFL standards. As a result, even with the addition of <a href="/wiki/AstroTurf" title="AstroTurf">AstroTurf</a> cutouts to widen and lengthen the field, it was still narrower and shorter than all other CFL fields, including those other US fields which were not regulation. In fact, it was not even close to regulation length; in order to shoehorn even an approximation of a Canadian field onto the playing surface, the end zones became half-grass/half-Astroturf pentagons that were only nine yards long in the middle and seven yards long at the sidelines. CFL rules dictate a 20-yard end zone and no other stadium had end zones shorter than 15. The stands jutted into the corners of the end zones, creating a clear safety hazard. In addition, the Liberty Bowl grounds crew had marked the field with <a href="/wiki/Yard" title="Yard">33 inch yards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Memphis ended the <a href="/wiki/1995_CFL_season" title="1995 CFL season">1995 CFL season</a> with a 9–9–0 regular season record, which placed them fourth in the South Division and one game out of the playoffs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Off_the_field">Off the field</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memphis_Mad_Dogs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Off the field">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pepper Rodgers was known around the football world as a likable man. However, he often made uncomplimentary remarks about the Canadian Football League, which also showed signs that the CFL's foray into the United States was doomed to failure from the start</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>The team drew relatively well during the first two months of the season. While the crowds were not nearly as large as those the Showboats or <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Southmen" title="Memphis Southmen">Southmen</a> had drawn, they were still comparable to those for the established CFL franchises. However, the CFL traditionally plays on Thursdays, Fridays and Saturdays—the same days as high school and college football games in the United States. Smith knew that the Mad Dogs could not hope to go head-to-head with high school games on Fridays and <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Volunteers_football" title="Tennessee Volunteers football">Tennessee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_Conference" title="Southeastern Conference">Southeastern Conference</a> football on Saturdays. The <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Barracudas" title="Birmingham Barracudas">Birmingham Barracudas</a> faced the same problem. With this in mind, Smith persuaded the CFL to let the Mad Dogs play late-season games on Sundays, as the 'Cudas had done. It did not work; several late season games drew crowds of fewer than 10,000, dropping the team's average attendance to around 14,550 by the end of the season. As early as September, Smith was blaming community indifference and outright hostility from the media for the team's steep decline at the gate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tim Cofield became the only Mad Dogs player to win the <a href="/wiki/James_P._McCaffrey_Trophy" title="James P. McCaffrey Trophy">James P. McCaffrey Trophy</a> as the Most Outstanding Defensive Player of the South Division.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_end">In the end</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memphis_Mad_Dogs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In the end">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to massive losses and the late-season attendance collapse, the Mad Dogs folded at the December 1995 CFL meetings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It is likely they would have been forced out of town in any event by the impending arrival of the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">NFL</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston_Oilers" title="Houston Oilers">Houston Oilers</a>, who were planning to play at the Liberty Bowl while <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Stadium" title="Nissan Stadium">their new stadium</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nashville" title="Nashville">Nashville</a> was being built. Sharing the Liberty Bowl with the Oilers would have caused serious logistical problems in September and October, and no other stadium in the Memphis area was suitable even for temporary use. The Stallions faced a similar situation when the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Browns_relocation_controversy" title="Cleveland Browns relocation controversy">arrival</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Ravens" title="Baltimore Ravens">Baltimore Ravens</a> forced them to move out of town after winning the Grey Cup.</p>
<p>By February 1996, the Barracudas, Texans and <a href="/wiki/Shreveport_Pirates" title="Shreveport Pirates">Shreveport Pirates</a> had also folded while the Stallions had reconstituted themselves as the third incarnation of the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Alouettes" title="Montreal Alouettes">Montreal Alouettes</a>, ending the CFL's three-year experiment south of the border.</p>
<p>Besides financial and attendance problems, the Mad Dogs were hobbled by the promotional efforts of the CFL itself. The league positioned itself directly against the NFL when it attempted to move south of the border. The CFL adopted the marketing slogan, "Longer, Wider, Faster" to appeal to the American football market. Rodgers had expressed frustration in the efforts to market the Mad Dogs and Canadian football to the Mid-South.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_Mad_Dogs">After the Mad Dogs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memphis_Mad_Dogs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: After the Mad Dogs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city of Memphis would go on to host several more professional football franchises. The most notable was the Liberty Bowl's hosting of the NFL's Tennessee Oilers for the 1997 season. The plan was for the Oilers to play two full seasons in Memphis (which had the state's only stadium that was ideally sized for the NFL at the time) before moving to Nashville. If successful, it could have set Memphis up to make another bid on the expansion franchise that was to be awarded in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Browns_relocation_controversy" title="Cleveland Browns relocation controversy">Cleveland Browns relocation controversy</a> (what eventually became the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Texans" title="Houston Texans">Houston Texans</a>). However, like the Mad Dogs, the Oilers proved to be an attendance disaster in Memphis. Due to Memphians' unwillingness to support a lame-duck team and Nashvillians' reluctance to travel across Interstate 40 to see "their" team play, Oilers games didn't draw much more than the Mad Dogs had drawn even on a good day and often drew more opponents' fans than Oilers fans.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memphis_Pharaohs" title="Memphis Pharaohs">Memphis Pharaohs</a> played two seasons in the <a href="/wiki/Arena_Football_League" title="Arena Football League">Arena Football League</a>, their first concurrent with the Mad Dogs, in 1995 and 1996. In 2001, Memphis would host the <a href="/wiki/XFL" title="XFL">XFL</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Maniax" title="Memphis Maniax">Memphis Maniax</a> and the <a href="/wiki/AF2" title="AF2">AF2</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Xplorers" title="Memphis Xplorers">Memphis Xplorers</a>. The Xplorers would last for six seasons. Memphis would also have success in other professional sports, landing another Canadian export with the relocation of the <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Grizzlies" title="Vancouver Grizzlies">Vancouver Grizzlies</a> in 2001, which became the <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Grizzlies" title="Memphis Grizzlies">Memphis Grizzlies</a> that have been playing there ever since.</p>
