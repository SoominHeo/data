<p>A <b>pig</b> is any of the animals in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><b>Sus</b></i>, within the <a href="/wiki/Suidae" title="Suidae">Suidae</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Even-toed_ungulate" title="Even-toed ungulate">even-toed ungulates</a>. Pigs include the <a href="/wiki/Domestic_pig" title="Domestic pig">domestic pig</a> and its ancestor, the common Eurasian <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boar</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sus_scrofa" title="Sus scrofa">Sus scrofa</a></i>), along with other species; related creatures outside the genus include the <a href="/wiki/Peccary" title="Peccary">peccary</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Babirusa" title="Babirusa">babirusa</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Common_warthog" title="Common warthog">warthog</a>. Pigs, like all <a href="/wiki/Suidae" title="Suidae">suids</a>, are native to the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Eurasian and African continents</a>. Juvenile pigs are known as piglets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Pigs are highly social and intelligent animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>With around 1 billion individuals alive at any time, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domesticated" title="Domesticated">domesticated</a> pig is one of the most numerous large mammals on the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSD_Online_-_Custom_Query_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-PSD_Online_-_Custom_Query-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swine_Summary_Selected_Countries_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Swine_Summary_Selected_Countries-4">[4]</a></sup> Pigs are <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivores</a> and can consume a wide range of food, similar to humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Pigs can harbour a range of <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">parasites</a> and diseases that can be transmitted to humans. Because of the similarities between pigs and humans, pigs are used for human medical research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Online_Etymology_Dictionary" title="Online Etymology Dictionary">Online Etymology Dictionary</a></i> provides anecdotal evidence as well as linguistic, saying that the term derives</p>
<blockquote>
<p>probably from Old English *<i>picg</i>, found in compounds, ultimate origin unknown. Originally "young pig" (the word for adults was <i>swine</i>). Apparently related to <a href="/wiki/Low_German" title="Low German">Low German</a> <i>bigge</i>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> <i>big</i> ("but the phonology is difficult" -- <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">OED</a></i>). ... Another Old English word for "pig" was <i>fearh</i>, related to <i>furh</i> "furrow," from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">PIE</a> *<i>perk</i>- "dig, furrow" (source also of Latin <i>porc-us</i> "pig," see <i>pork</i>). "This reflects a widespread <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">IE</a> tendency to name animals from typical attributes or activities" [Roger Lass]. Synonyms <i>grunter</i>, <i>porker</i> are from sailors' and fishermen's euphemistic avoidance of uttering the word pig at sea, a superstition perhaps based on the fate of the <a href="/wiki/Legion_(demons)" title="Legion (demons)">Gadarene swine</a>, who drowned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper.2C_Daniel_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harper.2C_Daniel-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The <i>Online Etymology Dictionary</i> also traces the evolution of <i><b>sow</b></i>, the term for a female pig, through various historical languages:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Old English <i>sugu</i>, <i>su</i> "female of the swine," from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language" title="Proto-Germanic language">Proto-Germanic</a> *<i>su</i>- (cognates: <a href="/wiki/Old_Saxon" title="Old Saxon">Old Saxon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i>su</i>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i>Sau</i>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> <i>zeug</i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <i>syr</i>), from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">PIE</a> root *<i>su</i>- (cognates: <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> <i>sukarah</i> "wild boar, swine;" <a href="/wiki/Avestan" title="Avestan">Avestan</a> <i>hu</i> "wild boar;" <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>hys</i> "swine;" <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>sus</i> "swine", <i>suinus</i> "pertaining to swine"; <a href="/wiki/Old_Church_Slavonic" title="Old Church Slavonic">Old Church Slavonic</a> <i>svinija</i> "swine;" <a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">Lettish</a> <i>sivens</i> "young pig;" <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> <i>hucc</i>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> <i>suig</i> "swine; <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Old Irish</a> <i>socc</i> "snout, plowshare"), possibly imitative of pig noise; note that Sanskrit <i>sukharah</i> means "maker of (the sound) <i>su</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper.2C_Daniel_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harper.2C_Daniel-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>It is entirely likely that the <a href="/wiki/Hog_calling" title="Hog calling">word to call pigs</a>, "soo-ie," is similarly derived.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_behaviour">Description and behaviour</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Domestic_pig_skull_(Sus_domesticus).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Domestic_pig_skull_%28Sus_domesticus%29.jpg/220px-Domestic_pig_skull_%28Sus_domesticus%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Domestic_pig_skull_%28Sus_domesticus%29.jpg/330px-Domestic_pig_skull_%28Sus_domesticus%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Domestic_pig_skull_%28Sus_domesticus%29.jpg/440px-Domestic_pig_skull_%28Sus_domesticus%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Domestic_pig_skull_(Sus_domesticus).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Skull of domestic pig.<br/>
(<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sus_scrofa_domesticus" title="Sus scrofa domesticus">Sus scrofa domesticus</a>).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A typical pig has a large head with a long snout which is strengthened by a special prenasal bone and by a disk of <a href="/wiki/Cartilage" title="Cartilage">cartilage</a> at the tip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW_Sus_scrofa_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ADW_Sus_scrofa-8">[8]</a></sup> The snout is used to dig into the soil to find food and is a very acute sense organ. There are four <a href="/wiki/Hoof" title="Hoof">hoofed</a> toes on each <a href="/wiki/Pig%27s_trotters" title="Pig's trotters">trotter</a> (foot), with the two larger central toes bearing most of the weight, but the outer two also being used in soft ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dental_formula" title="Dental formula">dental formula</a> of adult pigs is <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; padding:0 0.1em;" title="upper dentition: Incisors.Canines.Premolars.Molars">3.1.4.3</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; padding:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;" title="lower dentition: Incisors.Canines.Premolars.Molars">3.1.4.3</span></span></span>, giving a total of 44 <a href="/wiki/Tooth" title="Tooth">teeth</a>. The rear teeth are adapted for crushing. In the male, the canine teeth form <a href="/wiki/Tusk" title="Tusk">tusks</a>, which grow continuously and are sharpened by constantly being ground against each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW_Sus_scrofa_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ADW_Sus_scrofa-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Occasionally, captive mother pigs may <a href="/wiki/Savaging" title="Savaging">savage</a> their own piglets, often if they become severely stressed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Some attacks on newborn piglets are non-fatal. Others may cause the <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a> of the piglets and sometimes, the mother may <a href="/wiki/Eating" title="Eating">eat</a> the piglets. It is estimated that 50% of piglet fatalities are due to the mother attacking, or unintentionally crushing, the newborn pre-weaned animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCPorkConf_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCPorkConf-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scientists have recently discovered that pigs can exhibit a <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_bias_in_animals" title="Cognitive bias in animals">cognitive bias</a> and are optimists or pessimests, similar to humans. In a study by the University of Lincoln, 36 pigs were tested. They were placed in a room with two food bowls at each end of the room. One food bowl contained sugar-coated chocolate sweets and the other contained coffee beans. Optimistic pigs were likely to check if there was a third bowl filled with sweets. However pessimistic pigs would not even bother checking if the bowl was filled with sweets or not. It was said that "Pro-active pigs were willing to see whether there was a positive outcome in the mystery bowl". The study shows that non-human animals can share similar traits with humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_evolution">Distribution and evolution</span></h2>

<p>With around 1 billion individuals alive at any time, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domesticated" title="Domesticated">domesticated</a> pig is one of the most numerous large mammals on the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSD_Online_-_Custom_Query_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-PSD_Online_-_Custom_Query-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swine_Summary_Selected_Countries_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Swine_Summary_Selected_Countries-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ancestor of the domesticated pig is the <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boar</a>, which is one of the most numerous and widespread large mammals. Its many subspecies are native to all but the harshest climates of continental <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> and its islands and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> as well, from Ireland and India to Japan and north to Siberia.</p>
<p>Long isolated from other pigs on the many islands of Indonesia, Malaysia, and the Philippines, pigs have evolved into many different species, including wild boar, bearded pigs, and warty pigs. Humans have introduced pigs into Australia, North and South America, and numerous islands, either accidentally as escaped domestic pigs which have gone <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feral" title="Feral">feral</a>, or as wild boar.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_reproduction">Habitat and reproduction</span></h2>
<p>The wild pig (<i>Sus scrofa</i>) can take advantage of any forage resources. Therefore, it can live in virtually any productive habitat that can provide enough water to sustain large mammals such as pigs. If there is increased foraging of wild pigs in certain areas, it can cause a nutritional shortage which can cause the pig population to decrease. If the nutritional state returns to normal, the pig population will most likely rise due to the pigs' naturally increased reproduction rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_and_foraging">Diet and foraging</span></h2>
<p>Pigs are <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivores</a>, which means that they consume both plants and animals. In the wild, they are foraging animals, primarily eating leaves, grasses, roots, fruits, and flowers. In confinement, pigs are fed mostly corn and soybean meal<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> with a mixture of vitamins and minerals added to the diet. Traditionally they were raised on dairy farms and called "mortgage lifters" due to their ability to use the excess milk as well as whey from cheese and butter making combined with pasture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Older pigs will consume three to five gallons of water per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cochon_truffier.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Cochon_truffier.JPG/220px-Cochon_truffier.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Cochon_truffier.JPG/330px-Cochon_truffier.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Cochon_truffier.JPG/440px-Cochon_truffier.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cochon_truffier.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A pig trained to find <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Truffles" title="Truffles">truffles</a>.</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Domestic_pig" title="Domestic pig">Domesticated pigs</a>, called swine, are raised commercially for meat (generally called <a href="/wiki/Pork" title="Pork">pork</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hams" title="Hams">hams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ham#United_Kingdom" title="Ham">gammon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bacon" title="Bacon">bacon</a>), as well as for <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a>. Their bristly <a href="/wiki/Hair" title="Hair">hairs</a> are also used for brushes. Due to their common use as livestock, adult swine have gender specific names: the males are <i>boars</i> and the females are <i>sows</i>. In Britain, the word <i>hog</i> can refer to a castrated adult male pig.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Young swine are called <i>piglets</i> or <i>pigs</i>. Pork is one of the most popular forms of meat for human consumption, accounting for 38% of worldwide meat production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencenews.org_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-sciencenews.org-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pigs that are allowed to forage may be watched by <a href="/wiki/Swineherd" title="Swineherd">swineherds</a>. Because of their foraging abilities and excellent <a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">sense of smell</a>, they are used to find <a href="/wiki/Truffle" title="Truffle">truffles</a> in many European countries.</p>
<p>Both wild and feral pigs are commonly <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunted</a>. Some breeds of pig, such as the Asian <a href="/wiki/Pot-bellied_pig" title="Pot-bellied pig">pot-bellied pig</a>, are kept as pets. There are two instances in the 2000s where farm hogs ate human beings. The first was in 2004 in Romania, where a woman died after her ears, half of her face and her fingers were consumed;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> the other in 2012 in Oregon—whether the farmer was killed by his hogs or died of another cause before being consumed is unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The relatively short, stiff, coarse hairs of the pig are called <a href="/wiki/Bristle" title="Bristle">bristles</a>, and were once so commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Paintbrush" title="Paintbrush">paintbrushes</a> that in 1946 the Australian Government launched <a href="/wiki/Operation_Pig_Bristle" title="Operation Pig Bristle">Operation Pig Bristle</a>. In May 1946, in response to a shortage of pig bristles for paintbrushes to paint houses in the post-World War II construction boom, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Air_Force" title="Royal Australian Air Force">Royal Australian Air Force</a> (RAAF) flew in 28 <a href="/wiki/Short_ton" title="Short ton">short tons</a> of pig bristles from China, their only commercially available source at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Domestic_pig" title="Domestic pig">Domestic pig</a></div>
<p>Pigs have been <a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">domesticated</a> since <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">ancient times</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a>. Archaeological evidence suggests that pigs were being managed in the wild in a way similar to the way they are managed by some modern New Guineans from wild boar as early as 13,000–12,700 <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a> in the Tigris Basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Remains of pigs have been dated to earlier than 11,400 BP in Cyprus that must have been introduced from the mainland which suggests domestication in the adjacent mainland by then.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> A separate domestication also occurred in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giuffra_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Giuffra-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In India, pigs have been domesticated for a long time mostly in <a href="/wiki/Goa" title="Goa">Goa</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Rural_area" title="Rural area">rural areas</a> for <a href="/wiki/Pig_toilet" title="Pig toilet">pig toilets</a>. This was also done in China. Though ecologically logical as well as economical, pig toilets are waning in popularity as use of <a href="/wiki/Septic_tank" title="Septic tank">septic tanks</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Sewerage" title="Sewerage">sewerage</a> systems is increasing in rural areas.</p>
<p>Pigs were brought to southeastern North America from Europe by <a href="/wiki/Hernando_de_Soto" title="Hernando de Soto">Hernando de Soto</a> and other early Spanish explorers. Pigs are particularly valued in China and on certain oceanic islands, where their self-sufficiency allows them to be turned loose, although the practice is not without its drawbacks (see <a href="#Environmental_impacts">environmental impact</a>).</p>
<p>The domestic pig (<i>Sus scrofa domesticus</i>) is usually given the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">scientific name</a> <i>Sus scrofa</i>, although some authors call it <i>S. domesticus</i>, reserving <i>S. scrofa</i> for the <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boar</a>. It was domesticated approximately 5,000 to 7,000 years ago. Their coats are coarse and bristly. They are born brownish coloured and tend to turn more grayish coloured with age. The upper <a href="/wiki/Canine_tooth" title="Canine tooth">canines</a> form sharp distinctive <a href="/wiki/Tusk" title="Tusk">tusks</a> that curve outward and upward. Compared to other artiodactyles, their head is relatively long, pointed, and free of <a href="/wiki/Wart" title="Wart">warts</a>. Their head and body length ranges from 0.9 to 1.8 m (35 to 71 in) and they can weigh between 50 and 350 kg (110 and 770 lb).</p>
<p>In August 2015, a study looked at over 100 pig genome sequences to ascertain their process of domestication. The process of domestication was assumed to have been initiated by humans, involved few individuals and relied on reproductive isolation between wild and domestic forms. The study found that the assumption of reproductive isolation with population bottlenecks was not supported. The study indicated that pigs were domesticated separately in Western Asia and China, with Western Asian pigs introduced into Europe where they crossed with wild boar. A model that fitted the data included admixture with a now extinct <a href="/wiki/Ghost_population" title="Ghost population">ghost population</a> of wild pigs during the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>. The study also found that despite back-crossing with wild pigs, the genomes of domestic pigs have strong signatures of selection at DNA loci that affect behavior and morphology. The study concluded that human selection for domestic traits likely counteracted the homogenizing effect of gene flow from wild boars and created <a href="/wiki/Domestication_islands" title="Domestication islands">domestication islands</a> in the genome. The same process may also apply to other domesticated animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frantz2015_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-frantz2015-25">[25]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pennisi2015_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-pennisi2015-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pigs are intelligent<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnimalBehaviour_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-AnimalBehaviour-27">[27]</a></sup> and can be trained to perform numerous tasks and tricks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes2009-11-9_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes2009-11-9-28">[28]</a></sup> Recently, they have enjoyed a measure of popularity as house pets, particularly the dwarf breeds.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_and_religious_reference_to_pigs">Cultural and religious reference to pigs</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pigs_in_popular_culture" title="Pigs in popular culture">Pigs in popular culture</a></div>
<p>Pigs appear in the traditional and popular <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">arts</a>, media, and cultures of many societies, where they sometimes carry <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religious symbolism</a>. In Asia the <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boar</a> is one of 12 animal images comprising <a href="/wiki/Pig_(zodiac)" title="Pig (zodiac)">the Chinese zodiac</a>, while in Europe the boar represents a standard charge in <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldry</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> pigs and those who handle them are viewed negatively. Pigs are frequently alluded to in <a href="/wiki/Folk_art" title="Folk art">folk art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Idiom" title="Idiom">idioms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphors</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proverbs" title="Proverbs">proverbs</a>, and also occasionally in <a href="/wiki/Parable" title="Parable">parables</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Parable_of_the_Prodigal_Son" title="Parable of the Prodigal Son">Parable of the Prodigal Son</a>). "the three little pigs" is a folk-tale and "Peppa Pig" is a popular British television show.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impacts">Environmental impacts</span></h2>
<p>Domestic pigs that have escaped from farms or were allowed to forage in the wild, and in some cases wild boars which were introduced as prey for hunting, have given rise to large populations of feral pigs in North and South America, Australia, New Zealand, Hawaii, and other areas where pigs are not native. Accidental or deliberate releases of pigs into countries or environments where they are an <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">alien species</a> have caused extensive environmental change. Their omnivorous diet, aggressive behaviour, and their feeding method of rooting in the ground all combine to severely alter ecosystems unused to pigs. Pigs will even eat small animals and destroy nests of ground nesting birds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW_Sus_scrofa_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-ADW_Sus_scrofa-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Species_Survival_Commission" title="IUCN Species Survival Commission">Invasive Species Specialist Group</a> lists feral pigs on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_the_world%27s_100_worst_invasive_species" title="List of the world's 100 worst invasive species">list of the world's 100 worst invasive species</a> and says:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p>Feral pigs like other introduced mammals are major drivers of extinction and ecosystem change. They have been introduced into many parts of the world, and will damage crops and home gardens as well as potentially spreading disease. They uproot large areas of land, eliminating native vegetation and spreading weeds. This results in habitat alteration, a change in plant succession and composition and a decrease in native fauna dependent on the original habitat.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_issues">Health issues</span></h2>
<p>Pigs can harbour a range of <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">parasites</a> and diseases that can be transmitted to humans. These include <a href="/wiki/Trichinosis" title="Trichinosis">trichinosis</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pork_tapeworm" title="Pork tapeworm">Taenia solium</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Cysticercosis" title="Cysticercosis">cysticercosis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brucellosis" title="Brucellosis">brucellosis</a>. Pigs are also known to host large concentrations of parasitic <a href="/wiki/Ascaris" title="Ascaris">ascarid</a> worms in their digestive tract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> According to the USDA fact sheet modern pork can be enjoyed cooked rare at 145 °F (63 °C) with pink in the middle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Today trichinellosis infections from eating undercooked pork are rare in more technologically developed countries due to refrigeration, health laws, and public awareness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Some religious groups have dietary laws that make pork an "unclean" meat, and adherents sometimes interpret these health issues as validation of their views.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pigs have health issues of their own. Pigs have small lungs in relation to their body size and are thus more susceptible than other domesticated animals to fatal <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bronchitis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Some strains of influenza are <a href="/wiki/Endemic_(epidemiology)" title="Endemic (epidemiology)">endemic</a> in pigs (see <a href="/wiki/Swine_influenza" title="Swine influenza">swine influenza</a>). Pigs also can acquire <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_influenza" title="Human influenza">human influenza</a>.</p>
<p>Pigs can be aggressive in defending themselves and their young. Pig-induced injuries are thus not unusual in areas where pigs are raised or where they form part of the wild or feral fauna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2012 scientists managed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sequence_the_genome" title="Sequence the genome">sequence the genome</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Domestic_pig" title="Domestic pig">domestic pig</a>. The similarities between the pig and human genomes mean that the new data may have wide applications in the study and treatment of human genetic diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

Retrieved <span class="nowrap">28 July</span> 2010</span>. <q>Mirror usage has been taken to indicate some degree of awareness in animals. ... When put in a pen with a mirror in it, young pigs made movements while apparently looking at their image. After 5 h spent with a mirror, the pigs were shown a familiar food bowl, visible in the mirror but hidden behind a solid barrier. Seven out of eight pigs found the food bowl in a mean of 23 s by going away from the mirror and around the barrier. ...
