<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Face (geometry)</h1>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Solid_geometry" title="Solid geometry">solid geometry</a>, a <b>face</b> is a flat (<a href="/wiki/Plane_(geometry)" title="Plane (geometry)">planar</a>) surface that forms part of the boundary of a solid object;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> a three-dimensional solid bounded exclusively by flat faces is a <a href="/wiki/Polyhedron" title="Polyhedron">polyhedron</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polygonal_face">Polygonal face</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Face_(geometry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Polygonal face">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In elementary geometry, a <b>face</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Polygon" title="Polygon">polygon</a> on the boundary of a <a href="/wiki/Polyhedron" title="Polyhedron">polyhedron</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-m-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Other names for a polygonal face include <b>side</b> of a polyhedron, and <b>tile</b> of a Euclidean plane <a href="/wiki/Tessellation" title="Tessellation">tessellation</a>.</p>
<p>For example, any of the six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_(geometry)" title="Square (geometry)">squares</a> that bound a <a href="/wiki/Cube" title="Cube">cube</a> is a face of the cube. Sometimes "face" is also used to refer to the 2-dimensional features of a <a href="/wiki/4-polytope" title="4-polytope">4-polytope</a>. With this meaning, the 4-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Tesseract" title="Tesseract">tesseract</a> has 24 square faces, each sharing two of 8 <a href="/wiki/Cube" title="Cube">cubic</a> cells.</p>

<p>Some other polygons, which are not faces, are also important for polyhedra and tessellations. These include <a href="/wiki/Petrie_polygon" title="Petrie polygon">Petrie polygons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vertex_figure" title="Vertex figure">vertex figures</a> and <a href="/wiki/Faceting" title="Faceting">facets</a> (flat polygons formed by coplanar vertices which do not lie in the same face of the polyhedron).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Number_of_polygonal_faces_of_a_polyhedron">Number of polygonal faces of a polyhedron</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Face_(geometry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Number of polygonal faces of a polyhedron">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convex_polyhedron" title="Convex polyhedron">convex polyhedron</a>'s surface has <a href="/wiki/Euler_characteristic" title="Euler characteristic">Euler characteristic</a></p>
<dl>
<dd><span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y mw-math-element" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>

<mo>−<!-- − --></mo>
<mi>E</mi>

<mn>2</mn>
<mo>,</mo>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle V-E+F=2,}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="V-E+F=2," aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline mw-math-element" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f8ab564115bf2f7f7d12a9f873d9c6c7a50190e" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:15.976ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span></dd>
</dl>
<p>where <i>V</i> is the number of <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(geometry)" title="Vertex (geometry)">vertices</a>, <i>E</i> is the number of <a href="/wiki/Edge_(geometry)" title="Edge (geometry)">edges</a>, and <i>F</i> is the number of faces. This equation is known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euler%27s_polyhedron_formula" title="Euler's polyhedron formula">Euler's polyhedron formula</a>. Thus the number of faces is 2 more than the excess of the number of edges over the number of vertices. For example, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cube_(geometry)" title="Cube (geometry)">cube</a> has 12 edges and 8 vertices, and hence 6 faces.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="k-face"><i>k</i>-face</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Face_(geometry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: k-face">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In higher-dimensional geometry the faces of a <a href="/wiki/Polytope" title="Polytope">polytope</a> are features of all dimensions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-m-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-g-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-z_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-z-5">[5]</a></sup> A face of dimension <i>k</i> is called a <i>k</i>-face. For example, the polygonal faces of an ordinary polyhedron are 2-faces. In <a href="/wiki/Set_theory" title="Set theory">set theory</a>, the set of faces of a polytope includes the polytope itself and the empty set where the empty set is for consistency given a "dimension" of −1. For any <i>n</i>-polytope (<i>n</i>-dimensional polytope), −1 ≤ <i>k</i> ≤ <i>n</i>.</p>
<p>For example, with this meaning, the faces of a <a href="/wiki/Cube" title="Cube">cube</a> include the empty set, its vertices (0-faces), edges (1-faces) and squares (2-faces), and the cube itself (3-face).</p>
<p>All of the following are the <b>faces</b> of a <a href="/wiki/4-polytope" title="4-polytope">4-dimensional polytope</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li>4-face – the 4-dimensional <a href="/wiki/4-polytope" title="4-polytope">4-polytope</a> itself</li>
<li>3-faces – 3-dimensional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cell_(geometry)" title="Cell (geometry)">cells</a> (<a href="/wiki/Polyhedron" title="Polyhedron">polyhedral</a> faces)</li>
<li>2-faces – 2-dimensional faces (<a href="/wiki/Polygon" title="Polygon">polygonal</a> faces)</li>
<li>1-faces – 1-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Edge_(geometry)" title="Edge (geometry)">edges</a></li>
<li>0-faces – 0-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(geometry)" title="Vertex (geometry)">vertices</a></li>
<li>the empty set, which has dimension −1</li>
</ul>
<p>In some areas of mathematics, such as <a href="/wiki/Polyhedral_combinatorics" title="Polyhedral combinatorics">polyhedral combinatorics</a>, a polytope is by definition convex. Formally, a face of a polytope <i>P</i> is the intersection of <i>P</i> with any <a href="/wiki/Closed_set" title="Closed set">closed</a> <a href="/wiki/Half-space_(geometry)" title="Half-space (geometry)">halfspace</a> whose boundary is disjoint from the interior of <i>P</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> From this definition it follows that the set of faces of a polytope includes the polytope itself and the empty set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-g-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-z_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-z-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In other areas of mathematics, such as the theories of <a href="/wiki/Abstract_polytope" title="Abstract polytope">abstract polytopes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_polytope" title="Star polytope">star polytopes</a>, the requirement for convexity is relaxed. Abstract theory still requires that the set of faces include the polytope itself and the empty set.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cell_or_3-face">Cell or 3-face</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Face_(geometry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cell or 3-face">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <b>cell</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Polyhedron" title="Polyhedron">polyhedral</a> element (<b>3-face</b>) of a 4-dimensional polytope or 3-dimensional tessellation, or higher. Cells are <a href="/wiki/Facet_(geometry)" title="Facet (geometry)">facets</a> for 4-polytopes and 3-honeycombs.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Facet_or_.28n-1.29-face">Facet or (<i>n</i>-1)-face</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Face_(geometry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Facet or (n-1)-face">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In higher-dimensional geometry, the <b>facets</b> of a <i>n</i>-polytope are the (<i>n</i>-1)-faces of dimension one less than the polytope itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> A polytope is bounded by its facets.</p>
<p>For example:</p>
<ul>
<li>The facets of a <a href="/wiki/Line_segment" title="Line segment">line segment</a> are its 0-faces or <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(geometry)" title="Vertex (geometry)">vertices</a>.</li>
<li>The facets of a <a href="/wiki/Polygon" title="Polygon">polygon</a> are its 1-faces or <a href="/wiki/Edge_(geometry)" title="Edge (geometry)">edges</a>.</li>
<li>The facets of a <a href="/wiki/Polyhedron" title="Polyhedron">polyhedron</a> or plane <a href="/wiki/Uniform_tiling" title="Uniform tiling">tiling</a> are its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2-face" title="2-face">2-faces</a>.</li>
<li>The facets of a <a href="/wiki/4-polytope" title="4-polytope">4D polytope</a> or <a href="/wiki/Convex_uniform_honeycomb" title="Convex uniform honeycomb">3-honeycomb</a> are its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-face" title="3-face">3-faces</a>.</li>
<li>The facets of a <a href="/wiki/5-polytope" title="5-polytope">5D polytope</a> or 4-honeycomb are its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4-face" title="4-face">4-faces</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ridge_or_.28n-2.29-face">Ridge or (<i>n</i>-2)-face</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Face_(geometry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ridge or (n-2)-face">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In related terminology, a (<i>n</i> − 2)-<i>face</i> of an <i>n</i>-polytope is called a <b>ridge</b> (also <b>subfacet</b>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A ridge is seen as the boundary between exactly two facets of a polytope or honeycomb.</p>
<p>For example:</p>
<ul>
<li>The ridges of a 2D <a href="/wiki/Polygon" title="Polygon">polygon</a> or 1D tiling are its 0-faces or <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(geometry)" title="Vertex (geometry)">vertices</a>.</li>
<li>The ridges of a 3D <a href="/wiki/Polyhedron" title="Polyhedron">polyhedron</a> or plane <a href="/wiki/Uniform_tiling" title="Uniform tiling">tiling</a> are its 1-faces or <a href="/wiki/Edge_(geometry)" title="Edge (geometry)">edges</a>.</li>
<li>The ridges of a <a href="/wiki/4-polytope" title="4-polytope">4D polytope</a> or <a href="/wiki/Convex_uniform_honeycomb" title="Convex uniform honeycomb">3-honeycomb</a> are its 2-faces or simply <b>faces</b>.</li>
<li>The ridges of a <a href="/wiki/5-polytope" title="5-polytope">5D polytope</a> or 4-honeycomb are its 3-faces or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cell_(geometry)" title="Cell (geometry)">cells</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peak_or_.28n-3.29-face">Peak or (<i>n</i>-3)-face</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Face_(geometry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Peak or (n-3)-face">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A (<i>n</i> − 3)-<i>face</i> of an <i>n</i>-polytope is called a <b>peak</b>. A peak contain a rotational axis of facets and ridges in a regular polytope or honeycomb.</p>
<p>For example:</p>
<ul>
<li>The peaks of a 3D <a href="/wiki/Polyhedron" title="Polyhedron">polyhedron</a> or plane <a href="/wiki/Uniform_tiling" title="Uniform tiling">tiling</a> are its 0-faces or <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(geometry)" title="Vertex (geometry)">vertices</a>.</li>
<li>The peaks of a <a href="/wiki/4-polytope" title="4-polytope">4D polytope</a> or <a href="/wiki/Convex_uniform_honeycomb" title="Convex uniform honeycomb">3-honeycomb</a> are its 1-faces or <a href="/wiki/Edge_(geometry)" title="Edge (geometry)">edges</a>.</li>
<li>The peaks of a <a href="/wiki/5-polytope" title="5-polytope">5D polytope</a> or 4-honeycomb are its 2-faces or simply <b>faces</b>.</li>
</ul>
