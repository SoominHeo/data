<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Immunoglobulin M</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Immunoglobulin M</b>, or <b>IgM</b> for short, is a basic <a href="/wiki/Antibody" title="Antibody">antibody</a> that is produced by <a href="/wiki/B_cell" title="B cell">B cells</a>. IgM is by far the physically largest antibody in the human circulatory system. It is the first antibody to appear in response to initial exposure to an <a href="/wiki/Antigen" title="Antigen">antigen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlbertsJohnson2002_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-AlbertsJohnson2002-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a>, where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasmablast" title="Plasmablast">plasmablasts</a> responsible for antibody production reside, is the major site of specific IgM production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid21148037_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid21148037-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_function">Structure and function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immunoglobulin_M&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure and function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-technical" role="presentation">

<td class="mbox-image">

<p>IgM is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polymers" title="Polymers">polymer</a>, where multiple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immunoglobulins" title="Immunoglobulins">immunoglobulins</a> are linked together by strong <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent bonds</a> known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disulfide_bond" title="Disulfide bond">disulfide bonds</a>. This occurs mostly to produce pentamers (5 linked immunoglobulins) but also as a hexamer (six linked immunoglobulins). IgM has a <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">molecular mass</a> of approximately 970 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dalton_(unit)" title="Dalton (unit)">kDa</a> (in its pentamer form).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid15931543_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15931543-5">[5]</a></sup> Because each immunoglobulin <a href="/wiki/Monomer" title="Monomer">monomer</a> has two antigen binding sites, a pentameric IgM has 10 binding sites. Typically, however, IgM cannot bind 10 antigens at the same time because the large size of most antigens hinders binding to nearby sites.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/J_chain" title="J chain">J chain</a> is found in pentameric IgM but not in the hexameric form, perhaps due to space constraints in the hexameric complex. Pentameric IgM can also be made in the absence of J chain. At present, it is still uncertain what fraction of normal pentamer contains J chain, and to this extent it is also uncertain whether a J chain-containing pentamer contains one or more than one J chain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid9637512_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9637512-6">[6]</a></sup> Although hexameric IgM without J chain has higher efficiency of <a href="/wiki/Complement_system" title="Complement system">complement</a> fixation than pentameric IgM with J chain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid9637512_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9637512-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid2120070_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid2120070-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because IgM is a large molecule, it cannot <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffuse</a> well, and is found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstitium" title="Interstitium">interstitium</a> only in very low quantities. IgM is primarily found in <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">serum</a>; however, because of the J chain, it is also important as a secretory immunoglobulin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10972899_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10972899-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to its polymeric nature, IgM possesses high <a href="/wiki/Avidity" title="Avidity">avidity</a>, and is particularly effective at complement activation. By itself, IgM is an ineffective <a href="/wiki/Opsonin" title="Opsonin">opsonin</a>; however it contributes greatly to opsonization by activating complement and causing <a href="/wiki/C3b" title="C3b">C3b</a> to bind to the antigen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expression">Expression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immunoglobulin_M&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Expression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Germline" title="Germline">germline</a> cells, the <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">gene</a> segment encoding the μ constant region of the <a href="/wiki/Immunoglobulin_heavy_chain" title="Immunoglobulin heavy chain">heavy chain</a> is positioned first among other constant region gene segments. For this reason, IgM is the first immunoglobulin expressed by mature B cells.</p>
<p>On mature <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B_cells" title="B cells">B cells</a>, both IgM and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IgD" title="IgD">IgD</a> are co-expressed through long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRNA" title="MRNA">mRNA</a> transcripts which undergo differential splicing; the transcript for IgM is produced when the VDJ exon is spliced to the Cμ exon, with polyadenylation at p(A1).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janeway.27s_Immunobiology_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Janeway.27s_Immunobiology-10">[10]</a></sup> In contrast the IgD transcript is produced when the VDJ exon is spliced to Cδ exons, with polyadenylation further downstream at p(A2).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janeway.27s_Immunobiology_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Janeway.27s_Immunobiology-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is also the first immunoglobulin expressed in the fetus (around 20 weeks)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid4159036_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid4159036-11">[11]</a></sup> and phylogenetically the earliest antibody to develop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immunoglobulin_M&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>IgM antibodies appear early in the course of an infection and usually reappear, to a lesser extent, after further exposure. IgM antibodies do not pass across the human placenta (only isotype <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IgG" title="IgG">IgG</a>).</p>
<p>These two biological properties of IgM make it useful in the diagnosis of infectious diseases. Demonstrating IgM antibodies in a patient's serum indicates recent infection, or in a neonate's serum indicates intrauterine infection (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Congenital_rubella_syndrome" title="Congenital rubella syndrome">congenital rubella syndrome</a>).</p>
<p>The development of anti-donor IgM after organ transplantation is not associated with graft rejection but it may have a protective effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid14762873_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid14762873-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_applications">Research applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immunoglobulin_M&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Research applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In research, purified antibodies are used in many applications. IgM antibodies for research applications can be found directly from antibody suppliers, or through use of specialist search engines like <a href="/wiki/CiteAb" title="CiteAb">CiteAb</a>, Antibodypedia, and SeekProducts. Research antibodies are most commonly used to identify and locate intracellular and extracellular proteins.</p>
<p>Researchers using antibodies in their work need to record them correctly in order to allow their research to be reproducible (and therefore tested, and qualified by other researchers). Less than half of research antibodies referenced in academic papers can be easily identified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A paper published in F1000 in 2014 provided researchers with a guide for reporting research antibody use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_points">Other points</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immunoglobulin_M&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other points">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>IgM in normal serum is often found to bind to specific antigens, even in the absence of prior immunization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17202212_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17202212-16">[16]</a></sup> For this reason IgM has sometimes been called a "natural antibody". This phenomenon is probably due to the high avidity of IgM that allow it to bind detectably even to weakly cross-reacting <a href="/wiki/Antigen" title="Antigen">antigens</a> that are naturally occurring. For example, the IgM antibodies that bind to the red blood cell <a href="/wiki/ABO_blood_group_system" title="ABO blood group system">A and B antigens</a> might be formed in early life as a result of exposure to A- and B-like substances that are present on bacteria or perhaps also on plant materials.</p>
<p>IgM antibodies are mainly responsible for the clumping (<a href="/wiki/Agglutination_(biology)" title="Agglutination (biology)">agglutination</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">red blood cells</a> if the recipient of a <a href="/wiki/Blood_transfusion" title="Blood transfusion">blood transfusion</a> receives blood that is not compatible with their <a href="/wiki/Blood_type" title="Blood type">blood type</a>.</p>
<p>IgM is more sensitive to denaturation by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2-mercaptoethanol" title="2-mercaptoethanol">2-mercaptoethanol</a> than <a href="/wiki/Immunoglobulin_G" title="Immunoglobulin G">IgG</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid4961630_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid4961630-17">[17]</a></sup> This technique was historically used to distinguish between these isotypes before specific anti-IgG and anti-IgM secondary antibodies for <a href="/wiki/Immunoassay" title="Immunoassay">immunoassays</a> became commercially available. Serum samples would be tested for reactivity with an antigen before or after 2-mercaptoethanol treatment to determine whether the activity was due to IgM or IgG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid4961630_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid4961630-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid7204997_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid7204997-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
