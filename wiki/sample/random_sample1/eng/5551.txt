<p><b>Immunology</b> is a branch of <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> that covers the study of <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune systems</a> in all <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It was the Russian biologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ilya_Ilyich_Mechnikov" title="Ilya Ilyich Mechnikov">Ilya Ilyich Mechnikov</a> who boosted studies on immunology, and received the Nobel Prize in 1908 for his work. He jabbed the thorn of a rose on a starfish and noted that, 24 hours later, cells were surrounding the tip. It was an active response of the body, trying to maintain its integrity. It was Mechnikov who first observed the phenomenon of phagocytosis, in which the body defends itself against a foreign body, and coined the term. It charts, measures, and contextualizes the: <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiological</a> functioning of the immune system in states of both health and diseases; malfunctions of the immune system in immunological disorders (such as <a href="/wiki/Autoimmune_disease" title="Autoimmune disease">autoimmune diseases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypersensitivity" title="Hypersensitivity">hypersensitivities</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immune_deficiency" title="Immune deficiency">immune deficiency</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Transplant_rejection" title="Transplant rejection">transplant rejection</a>); the physical, chemical and physiological characteristics of the components of the immune system <i><a href="/wiki/In_vitro" title="In vitro">in vitro</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/In_situ#Biology_and_Biomedical_Engineering" title="In situ">in situ</a>,</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/In_vivo" title="In vivo">in vivo</a></i>. Immunology has applications in numerous disciplines of medicine, particularly in the fields of organ transplantation, oncology, virology, bacteriology, parasitology, psychiatry, and dermatology.</p>
<p>Prior to the designation of <a href="/wiki/Immunity_(medical)" title="Immunity (medical)">immunity</a> from the etymological root <i>immunis</i>, which is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "exempt"; early physicians characterized organs that would later be proven as essential components of the immune system. The important lymphoid organs of the immune system are the <a href="/wiki/Thymus" title="Thymus">thymus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bone_marrow" title="Bone marrow">bone marrow</a>, and chief lymphatic tissues such as <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tonsil" title="Tonsil">tonsils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_system" title="Lymphatic system">lymph vessels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lymph_node" title="Lymph node">lymph nodes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adenoid" title="Adenoid">adenoids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>. When health conditions worsen to emergency status, portions of immune system organs including the thymus, spleen, bone marrow, lymph nodes and other lymphatic tissues can be <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgically</a> excised for examination while patients are still alive.</p>
<p>Many components of the immune system are typically <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cellular</a> in nature and not associated with any specific organ; but rather are embedded or circulating in various <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissues</a> located throughout the body.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_immunology">Classical immunology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immunology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classical immunology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Classical immunology ties in with the fields of <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology" title="Epidemiology">epidemiology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>. It studies the relationship between the body systems, <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a>, and immunity. The earliest written mention of immunity can be traced back to the <a href="/wiki/Pandemic" title="Pandemic">plague</a> of <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> in 430 BCE. <a href="/wiki/Thucydides" title="Thucydides">Thucydides</a> noted that people who had recovered from a previous bout of the disease could <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nurse" title="Nurse">nurse</a> the sick without contracting the illness a second time. Many other ancient societies have references to this phenomenon, but it was not until the 19th and 20th centuries before the concept developed into scientific theory.</p>
<p>The study of the molecular and cellular components that comprise the immune system, including their function and interaction, is the central science of immunology. The immune system has been divided into a more primitive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Innate_immunity" title="Innate immunity">innate immune system</a> and, in <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptive_immunity" title="Adaptive immunity">acquired or adaptive immune system</a>. The latter is further divided into <a href="/wiki/Humoral_immunity" title="Humoral immunity">humoral</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Antibody" title="Antibody">antibody</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Cell-mediated_immunity" title="Cell-mediated immunity">cell-mediated</a> components.</p>
<p>The humoral (antibody) response is defined as the interaction between antibodies and <a href="/wiki/Antigen" title="Antigen">antigens</a>. Antibodies are specific <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> released from a certain class of immune cells known as <a href="/wiki/B_cell" title="B cell">B lymphocytes</a>, while antigens are defined as anything that elicits the generation of antibodies ("anti"body "gen"erators). Immunology rests on an understanding of the properties of these two biological entities and the cellular response to both.</p>
<p>Immunological research continues to become more specialized, pursuing non-classical models of immunity and functions of cells, organs and systems not previously associated with the immune system (Yemeserach 2010).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_immunology">Clinical immunology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immunology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Clinical immunology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Clinical immunology is the study of <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">diseases</a> caused by disorders of the immune system (failure, aberrant action, and malignant growth of the cellular elements of the system). It also involves diseases of other systems, where immune reactions play a part in the pathology and clinical features.</p>
<p>The diseases caused by disorders of the immune system fall into two broad categories:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Immunodeficiency" title="Immunodeficiency">immunodeficiency</a>, in which parts of the immune system fail to provide an adequate response (examples include <a href="/wiki/Chronic_granulomatous_disease" title="Chronic granulomatous disease">chronic granulomatous disease</a> and primary immune diseases);</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Autoimmunity" title="Autoimmunity">autoimmunity</a>, in which the immune system attacks its own host's body (examples include <a href="/wiki/Systemic_lupus_erythematosus" title="Systemic lupus erythematosus">systemic lupus erythematosus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rheumatoid_arthritis" title="Rheumatoid arthritis">rheumatoid arthritis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hashimoto%27s_disease" title="Hashimoto's disease">Hashimoto's disease</a> and <a href="/wiki/Myasthenia_gravis" title="Myasthenia gravis">myasthenia gravis</a>).</li>
</ul>
<p>Other immune system disorders include various hypersensitivities (such as in <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Allergy" title="Allergy">allergies</a>) that respond inappropriately to otherwise harmless <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a>.</p>
<p>The most well-known disease that affects the immune system itself is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acquired_Immunodeficiency_Syndrome" title="Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome">AIDS</a>, an immunodeficiency characterized by the suppression of CD4+ ("helper") <a href="/wiki/T_cell" title="T cell">T cells</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dendritic_cells" title="Dendritic cells">dendritic cells</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macrophages" title="Macrophages">macrophages</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_Immunodeficiency_Virus" title="Human Immunodeficiency Virus">Human Immunodeficiency Virus</a> (HIV).</p>
<p>Clinical immunologists also study ways to prevent the immune system's attempts to destroy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allograft" title="Allograft">allografts</a> (<a href="/wiki/Transplant_rejection" title="Transplant rejection">transplant rejection</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Developmental_immunology">Developmental immunology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immunology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Developmental immunology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The body’s capability to react to antigen depends on a person's age, antigen type, maternal factors and the area where the antigen is presented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-7167-4947-5_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-7167-4947-5-2">[2]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neonate" title="Neonate">Neonates</a> are said to be in a state of physiological immunodeficiency, because both their innate and adaptive immunological responses are greatly suppressed. Once born, a child’s immune system responds favorably to protein antigens while not as well to <a href="/wiki/Glycoprotein" title="Glycoprotein">glycoproteins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharides</a>. In fact, many of the infections acquired by neonates are caused by low virulence organisms like <i><a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus" title="Staphylococcus">Staphylococcus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudomonas" title="Pseudomonas">Pseudomonas</a></i>. In neonates, <a href="/wiki/Opsonin" title="Opsonin">opsonic</a> activity and the ability to activate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complement_cascade" title="Complement cascade">complement cascade</a> is very limited. For example, the mean level of <a href="/wiki/Apolipoprotein_C3" title="Apolipoprotein C3">C3</a> in a newborn is approximately 65% of that found in the adult. <a href="/wiki/Phagocytosis" title="Phagocytosis">Phagocytic</a> activity is also greatly impaired in newborns. This is due to lower opsonic activity, as well as diminished <a href="/wiki/Downregulation_and_upregulation" title="Downregulation and upregulation">up-regulation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Integrin" title="Integrin">integrin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Selectin" title="Selectin">selectin</a> receptors, which limit the ability of <a href="/wiki/Neutrophil" title="Neutrophil">neutrophils</a> to interact with <a href="/wiki/Cell_adhesion_molecule" title="Cell adhesion molecule">adhesion molecules</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Endothelium" title="Endothelium">endothelium</a>. Their <a href="/wiki/Monocyte" title="Monocyte">monocytes</a> are slow and have a reduced <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> production, which also limits the newborn's phagocytic activity. Although, the number of total <a href="/wiki/Lymphocyte" title="Lymphocyte">lymphocytes</a> is significantly higher than in adults, the cellular and humoral immunity is also impaired. <a href="/wiki/Antigen-presenting_cell" title="Antigen-presenting cell">Antigen-presenting cells</a> in newborns have a reduced capability to activate T cells. Also, T cells of a newborn proliferate poorly and produce very small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Cytokine" title="Cytokine">cytokines</a> like IL-2, IL-4, IL-5, IL-12, and IFN-g which limits their capacity to activate the humoral response as well as the phagocitic activity of macrophage. B cells develop early during <a href="/wiki/Gestation" title="Gestation">gestation</a> but are not fully active.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaspan_et_al_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jaspan_et_al-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Maternal factors also play a role in the body’s immune response. At birth, most of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immunoglobulin" title="Immunoglobulin">immunoglobulin</a> present is maternal IgG. Because IgM, IgD, IgE and IgA don’t cross the placenta, they are almost undetectable at birth. Some IgA is provided by <a href="/wiki/Breast_milk" title="Breast milk">breast milk</a>. These passively-acquired antibodies can protect the newborn for up to 18 months, but their response is usually short-lived and of low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affinity_(pharmacology)" title="Affinity (pharmacology)">affinity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaspan_et_al_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jaspan_et_al-3">[3]</a></sup> These antibodies can also produce a negative response. If a child is exposed to the antibody for a particular antigen before being exposed to the antigen itself then the child will produce a dampened response. <a href="/wiki/Passive_immunity" title="Passive immunity">Passively acquired maternal antibodies</a> can suppress the antibody response to active immunization. Similarly the response of T-cells to vaccination differs in children compared to adults, and vaccines that induce Th1 responses in adults do not readily elicit these same responses in neonates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaspan_et_al_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jaspan_et_al-3">[3]</a></sup> Between six and nine months after birth, a child’s immune system begins to respond more strongly to <a href="/wiki/Glycoprotein" title="Glycoprotein">glycoproteins</a>, but there is usually no marked improvement in their response to <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharides</a> until they are at least one year old. This can be the reason for distinct time frames found in <a href="/wiki/Vaccination_schedule" title="Vaccination schedule">vaccination schedules</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid11739030_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11739030-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid9286377_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9286377-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>During adolescence, the human body undergoes various physical, physiological and immunological changes triggered and mediated by <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormones</a>, of which the most significant in females is <a href="/wiki/Estradiol" title="Estradiol">17-β-oestradiol</a> (an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oestrogen" title="Oestrogen">oestrogen</a>) and, in males, is <a href="/wiki/Testosterone" title="Testosterone">testosterone</a>. Oestradiol usually begins to act around the age of 10 and testosterone some months later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid127002_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid127002-6">[6]</a></sup> There is evidence that these <a href="/wiki/Steroid" title="Steroid">steroids</a> not only act directly on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_sexual_characteristics" title="Primary sexual characteristics">primary</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondary_sexual_characteristics" title="Secondary sexual characteristics">secondary sexual characteristics</a> but also have an effect on the development and regulation of the immune system,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid11407317_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11407317-7">[7]</a></sup> including an increased risk in developing <a href="/wiki/Puberty" title="Puberty">pubescent</a> and post-pubescent autoimmunity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid2973658_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid2973658-8">[8]</a></sup> There is also some evidence that cell surface receptors on B cells and macrophages may detect sex hormones in the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid11996938_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11996938-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The female sex hormone 17-β-oestradiol has been shown to regulate the level of immunological response,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid12900050_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid12900050-10">[10]</a></sup> while some male <a href="/wiki/Androgen" title="Androgen">androgens</a> such as testosterone seem to suppress the stress response to infection. Other androgens, however, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DHEA" title="DHEA">DHEA</a>, increase immune response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid9949320_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9949320-11">[11]</a></sup> As in females, the male sex hormones seem to have more control of the immune system during puberty and post-puberty than during the rest of a male's adult life.</p>
<p>Physical changes during puberty such as <a href="/wiki/Thymic_involution" title="Thymic involution">thymic involution</a> also affect immunological response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10737767_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10737767-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Immunotherapy">Immunotherapy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immunology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Immunotherapy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Immunotherapy" title="Immunotherapy">Immunotherapy</a></div>
<p>The use of immune system components to treat a disease or disorder is known as immunotherapy. Immunotherapy is most commonly used in the context of the treatment of <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancers</a> together with <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">chemotherapy</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medication" title="Medication">drugs</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiotherapy" title="Radiotherapy">radiotherapy</a> (<a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">radiation</a>). However, immunotherapy is also often used in the immunosuppressed (such as <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a> patients) and people suffering from other immune deficiencies or autoimmune diseases. This includes regulating factors such as IL-2, IL-10, GM-CSF B, IFN-α.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnostic_immunology">Diagnostic immunology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immunology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diagnostic immunology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immunodiagnostics" title="Immunodiagnostics">Immunodiagnostics</a></div>
<p>The specificity of the bond between antibody and antigen has made the antibody an excellent tool for the detection of substances by a variety of diagnostic techniques. Antibodies specific for a desired antigen can be conjugated with an <a href="/wiki/Isotopic_labeling" title="Isotopic labeling">isotopic (radio)</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent_labelling" title="Fluorescent labelling">fluorescent label</a> or with a color-forming enzyme in order to detect it. However, the similarity between some antigens can lead to false positives and other errors in such tests by antibodies cross-reacting with antigens that aren't exact matches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid1993317_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid1993317-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cancer_immunology">Cancer immunology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immunology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cancer immunology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cancer_immunology" title="Cancer immunology">Cancer immunology</a></div>
<p>The study of the interaction of the immune system with cancer cells can lead to diagnostic tests and therapies with which to find and fight cancer.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproductive_immunology">Reproductive immunology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immunology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reproductive immunology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_immunology" title="Reproductive immunology">Reproductive immunology</a></div>
<p>This area of the immunology is devoted to the study of immunological aspects of the reproductive process including <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a> acceptance. The term has also been used by fertility clinics to address fertility problems, recurrent miscarriages, premature deliveries and dangerous complications such as <a href="/wiki/Pre-eclampsia" title="Pre-eclampsia">pre-eclampsia</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_immunology">Theoretical immunology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immunology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Theoretical immunology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Immunology is strongly experimental in everyday practice but is also characterized by an ongoing theoretical attitude. Many theories have been suggested in immunology from the end of the nineteenth century up to the present time. The end of the 19th century and the beginning of the 20th century saw a battle between "cellular" and "humoral" theories of immunity. According to the cellular theory of immunity, represented in particular by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elie_Metchnikoff" title="Elie Metchnikoff">Elie Metchnikoff</a>, it was cells – more precisely, phagocytes – that were responsible for immune responses. In contrast, the humoral theory of immunity, held by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Koch" title="Robert Koch">Robert Koch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emil_von_Behring" title="Emil von Behring">Emil von Behring</a>, among others, stated that the active immune agents were soluble components (molecules) found in the organism’s “humors” rather than its cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tauber_AI_1994_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tauber_AI_1994-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the mid-1950s, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Macfarlane_Burnet" title="Frank Macfarlane Burnet">Frank Burnet</a>, inspired by a suggestion made by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niels_Jerne" title="Niels Jerne">Niels Jerne</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> formulated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clonal_selection_theory" title="Clonal selection theory">clonal selection theory</a> (CST) of immunity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> On the basis of CST, Burnet developed a theory of how an immune response is triggered according to the self/nonself distinction: "self" constituents (constituents of the body) do not trigger destructive immune responses, while "nonself" entities (e.g., pathogens, an allograft) trigger a destructive immune response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The theory was later modified to reflect new discoveries regarding <a href="/wiki/Histocompatibility" title="Histocompatibility">histocompatibility</a> or the complex "two-signal" activation of T cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The self/nonself theory of immunity and the self/nonself vocabulary have been criticized,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tauber_AI_1994_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tauber_AI_1994-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matzinger_P_2002_301.E2.80.935_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matzinger_P_2002_301.E2.80.935-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> but remain very influential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>More recently, several theoretical frameworks have been suggested in immunology, including "<a href="/wiki/Autopoiesis" title="Autopoiesis">autopoietic</a>" views,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> "cognitive immune" views,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> the "<a href="/wiki/Danger_model" title="Danger model">danger model</a>" (or "danger theory"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matzinger_P_2002_301.E2.80.935_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Matzinger_P_2002_301.E2.80.935-21">[21]</a></sup> and the "discontinuity" theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The danger model, suggested by <a href="/wiki/Polly_Matzinger" title="Polly Matzinger">Polly Matzinger</a> and colleagues, has been very influential, arousing many comments and discussions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Allergy,_Asthma,_and_Immunology" title="American Academy of Allergy, Asthma, and Immunology">American Academy of Allergy, Asthma, and Immunology</a> (AAAAI), "an immunologist is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Research_scientist" title="Research scientist">research scientist</a> who investigates the immune system of vertebrates (including the human immune system). Immunologists include research scientists (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PhD" title="PhD">PhDs</a>) who work in laboratories. Immunologists also include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physicians" title="Physicians">physicians</a> who, for example, treat patients with immune system disorders. Some immunologists are physician-scientists who combine laboratory research with patient care."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Profession_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Profession-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_in_immunology">Career in immunology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immunology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Career in immunology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioscience" title="Bioscience">Bioscience</a> is the overall major in which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undergraduate" title="Undergraduate">undergraduate</a> students who are interested in general well-being take in college. Immunology is a branch of bioscience for undergraduate programs but the major gets specified as students move on for <a href="/wiki/Postgraduate_education" title="Postgraduate education">graduate</a> program in immunology. The aim of immunology is to study the health of humans and animals through effective yet consistent <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a>, (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AAAAI" title="AAAAI">AAAAI</a>, 2013).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The most important thing about being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immunologists" title="Immunologists">immunologists</a> is the research because it is the biggest portion of their jobs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most graduate immunology schools follow the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=AAI_courses_immunology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="AAI courses immunology (page does not exist)">AAI courses immunology</a> which are offered throughout numerous schools in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> For example, in New York State, there are several universities that offer the AAI courses immunology: <a href="/wiki/Albany_Medical_College" title="Albany Medical College">Albany Medical College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Icahn_School_of_Medicine_at_Mount_Sinai" title="Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai">Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_University_Langone_Medical_Center" title="New York University Langone Medical Center">New York University Langone Medical Center</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_at_Albany" title="University at Albany">University at Albany</a> (SUNY), <a href="/wiki/University_at_Buffalo" title="University at Buffalo">University at Buffalo</a> (SUNY), <a href="/wiki/University_of_Rochester_Medical_Center" title="University of Rochester Medical Center">University of Rochester Medical Center</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upstate_Medical_University" title="Upstate Medical University">Upstate Medical University</a> (SUNY). The AAI immunology courses include an <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Introductory_Course&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Introductory Course (page does not exist)">Introductory Course</a> and an <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Advance_Course&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Advance Course (page does not exist)">Advance Course</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Introductory_Course&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Introductory Course (page does not exist)">The Introductory Course</a> is a course that gives students an overview of the basics of immunology.</p>
<p>In addition, this Introductory Course gives students more information to complement general <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> or science training. It also has two different parts: Part I is an introduction to the basic principles of immunology and Part II is a clinically-oriented lecture series. On the other hand, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Course&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Advanced Course (page does not exist)">Advanced Course</a> is another course for those who are willing to expand or update their understanding of immunology. It is advised for students who want to attend the Advanced Course to have a background of the principles of immunology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Most schools require students to take electives in other to complete their degrees. A Master’s degree requires two years of study following the attainment of a bachelor's degree. For a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doctoral_programme" title="Doctoral programme">doctoral programme</a> it is required to take two additional years of study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The expectation of occupational growth in immunology is an increase of 36 percent from 2010 to 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The median annual wage was $76,700 in May 2010. However, the lowest 10 percent of immunologists earned less than $41,560, and the top 10 percent earned more than $142,800, (Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2013). The practice of immunology itself is not specified by the U.S. Department of Labor but it belongs to the practice of life science in general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
