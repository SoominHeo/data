<p>An <b>anchovy</b> is a small, common salt-water <a href="/wiki/Forage_fish" title="Forage fish">forage fish</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <b>Engraulidae</b>.</p>
<p>The 144 species are placed in 17 genera; they are found in the Atlantic, Indian, and Pacific Oceans, and in the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. Anchovies are usually classified as <a href="/wiki/Oily_fish" title="Oily fish">oily fish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anchovy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Engraulis_encrasicolus_Gervais_flipped.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="368" data-file-width="1100" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Engraulis_encrasicolus_Gervais_flipped.jpg/300px-Engraulis_encrasicolus_Gervais_flipped.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Engraulis_encrasicolus_Gervais_flipped.jpg/450px-Engraulis_encrasicolus_Gervais_flipped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Engraulis_encrasicolus_Gervais_flipped.jpg/600px-Engraulis_encrasicolus_Gervais_flipped.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Engraulis_encrasicolus_Gervais_flipped.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<center><a href="/wiki/European_anchovy" title="European anchovy">European anchovy</a>, <i>Engraulis encrasicolus</i></center>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Anchovies are small, green fish with blue reflections due to a silver-colored <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Elongated_organisms" title="Anatomical terms of location">longitudinal</a> stripe that runs from the base of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudal_fin" title="Caudal fin">caudal fin</a>. They range from 2 to 40 cm (0.79 to 15.75 in) in adult length,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FB_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FB-3">[3]</a></sup> and their body shapes are variable with more slender fish in northern populations.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Snout" title="Snout">snout</a> is blunt with tiny, sharp <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teeth" title="Teeth">teeth</a> in both jaws. The <a href="/wiki/Snout" title="Snout">snout</a> contains a unique <a href="/wiki/Rostral_organ" title="Rostral organ">rostral organ</a>, believed to be sensory in nature, although its exact function is unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoF_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoF-4">[4]</a></sup> The mouth is larger than that of <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herrings</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silverside_(fish)" title="Silverside (fish)">silversides</a>, two fish which anchovies closely resemble in other respects. The anchovy eats <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fry_(biology)" title="Fry (biology)">recently hatched fish</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anchovy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anchovies are found in scattered areas throughout the world's oceans, but are concentrated in temperate waters, and are rare or absent in very cold or very warm seas. They are generally very accepting of a wide range of temperatures and <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a>. Large schools can be found in shallow, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brackish" title="Brackish">brackish</a> areas with muddy bottoms, as in estuaries and bays. They are abundant in the Mediterranean, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Alboran_Sea" title="Alboran Sea">Alboran Sea</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>. The species is regularly caught along the coasts of <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>. They are also found on the coast of northern Africa. The range of the species also extends along the Atlantic coast of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> to the south of Norway. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spawning" title="Spawning">Spawning</a> occurs between October and March, but not in water colder than 12 °C (54 °F). The anchovy appears to spawn at least 100 km (62 mi) from the shore, near the surface of the water.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anchovy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The anchovy is a significant food source for almost every predatory fish in its environment, including the <a href="/wiki/California_halibut" title="California halibut">California halibut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Striped_bass" title="Striped bass">rock fish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seriola_quinqueradiata" title="Seriola quinqueradiata">yellowtail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">shark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinook_salmon" title="Chinook salmon">chinook</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coho_salmon" title="Coho salmon">coho salmon</a>. It is also extremely important to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_mammals" title="Marine mammals">marine mammals</a> and birds; for example, breeding success of California <a href="/wiki/Brown_pelican" title="Brown pelican">brown pelicans</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Elegant_tern" title="Elegant tern">elegant terns</a> is strongly connected to anchovy abundance.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding_behavior">Feeding behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anchovy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anchovies, like most clupeoids (herrings, sardines and anchovies), are filter-feeders that open their mouths as they swim. As water passes through the mouth and out the gills, food particles are sieved by gill rakers and transferred into the esophagus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Black_Sea">Black Sea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anchovy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Black Sea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On average, the Turkish commercial fishing fleet catches around 300,000 tons per year, mainly in winter. The largest catch is in November and December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peru">Peru</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anchovy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Peru">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Peruvian anchovy fishery is one of the largest in the world.</p>
<p>In 1973 it collapsed catastrophically due to the combined effects of overfishing and El Niño<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and did not recover fully for two decades.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_food">As food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anchovy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: As food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anchovies_as_food" title="Anchovies as food">Anchovies as food</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Antonio_Sicurezza_-_Still_life_with_anchovies.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1192" data-file-width="1600" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Antonio_Sicurezza_-_Still_life_with_anchovies.jpg/220px-Antonio_Sicurezza_-_Still_life_with_anchovies.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Antonio_Sicurezza_-_Still_life_with_anchovies.jpg/330px-Antonio_Sicurezza_-_Still_life_with_anchovies.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Antonio_Sicurezza_-_Still_life_with_anchovies.jpg/440px-Antonio_Sicurezza_-_Still_life_with_anchovies.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Antonio_Sicurezza_-_Still_life_with_anchovies.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Still Life with Anchovies</i>, 1972, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Sicurezza" title="Antonio Sicurezza">Antonio Sicurezza</a></div>

<p>A traditional method of <a href="/wiki/Fish_processing" title="Fish processing">processing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish_preservation" title="Fish preservation">preserving</a> anchovies is to gut and <a href="/wiki/Salting_(food)" title="Salting (food)">salt</a> them in brine, allow them to mature, and then pack them in oil or salt. This results in a characteristic strong flavor and the flesh turns deep grey. Pickled in vinegar, as with Spanish <i><a href="/wiki/Boquerones_en_vinagre" title="Boquerones en vinagre">boquerones</a></i>, anchovies are milder and the flesh retains a white color. In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> times, anchovies were the base for the fermented fish sauce <i><a href="/wiki/Garum" title="Garum">garum</a></i>. <i>Garum</i> had a sufficiently long shelf life for long-distance commerce, and was produced in industrial quantities. Anchovies were also eaten raw as an <a href="/wiki/Aphrodisiac" title="Aphrodisiac">aphrodisiac</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Today, they are used in small quantities to flavor many dishes. Because of the strong flavor, they are also an ingredient in several sauces and condiments, including <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire_sauce" title="Worcestershire sauce">Worcestershire sauce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caesar_salad" title="Caesar salad">Caesar salad</a> dressing, <a href="/wiki/Remoulade" title="Remoulade">remoulade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gentleman%27s_Relish" title="Gentleman's Relish">Gentleman's Relish</a>, many <a href="/wiki/Fish_sauce" title="Fish sauce">fish sauces</a>, and in some versions of <a href="/wiki/Caf%C3%A9_de_Paris_sauce#Caf.C3.A9_de_Paris_butter" title="Café de Paris sauce">Café de Paris butter</a>. For domestic use, anchovy fillets are packed in oil or salt in small tins or jars, sometimes rolled around <a href="/wiki/Caper" title="Caper">capers</a>. Anchovy paste is also available. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishermen" title="Fishermen">Fishermen</a> also use anchovies as bait for larger fish, such as <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tuna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_sea_bass" title="Black sea bass">sea bass.</a></p>
<p>The strong taste people associate with anchovies is due to the <a href="/wiki/Curing_(food_preservation)" title="Curing (food preservation)">curing</a> process. Fresh anchovies, known in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> as <i><a class="extiw" href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/alici" title="it:alici">alici</a></i>, have a much milder flavor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, the name anchovies is related strongly to a traditional seasoning, hence the product "anchovies" is normally made of <a href="/wiki/European_sprat" title="European sprat">sprats</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herring</a> can be sold as "anchovy-spiced". Fish from the Engraulidae family are instead known as <i>sardell</i> in Sweden and <i>sardelli</i> in Finland, leading to confusion when translating recipes.</p>
