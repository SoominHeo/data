<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Decree</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>A <b>decree</b> is a rule of <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> usually issued by a <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> (such as the <a href="/wiki/President" title="President">president</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">republic</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarch</a>), according to certain procedures (usually established in a constitution). It has the force of law. The particular term used for this concept may vary from country to country. The <i><a href="/wiki/Executive_order" title="Executive order">executive orders</a></i> made by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a>, for example, are decrees (although a decree is not exactly an order). In non-legal <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> usage, however, the term refers to any <a href="/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">authoritarian</a> decision.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (June 2013)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Decree#Dubious" title="Talk:Decree">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decree_by_jurisdiction">Decree by jurisdiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Decree by jurisdiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Belgium">Belgium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Belgium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Decree_(Belgium)" title="Decree (Belgium)">Decree (Belgium)</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, a decree is a law of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communities,_regions_and_language_areas_in_Belgium" title="Communities, regions and language areas in Belgium">community or regional</a> parliament, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Parliament" title="Flemish Parliament">Flemish Parliament</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dd><i>See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_system_of_France" title="Political system of France">Government of France</a>.</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>The word <i>décret</i>, literally "decree", is an old legal usage in France and is used to refer to orders issued by the French <a href="/wiki/President_of_France" title="President of France">President</a> or <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_France" title="Prime Minister of France">Prime Minister</a>. Any such order must not violate the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_France" title="Constitution of France">French Constitution</a> or Code of Law of France, and a party has the right to request an order be nullified in the French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conseil_d%27Etat_(France)" title="Conseil d'Etat (France)">Council of State</a>. Orders must be ratified by Parliament before they can be modified into legislative Acts. Special orders known as <i>décret-loi</i>, literally "decree-act" or "decree-law",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> usually considered an illegal practice under the 3rd and 4th Republic, were finally abolished and replaced by the ordinances under the 1958 Constitution.</p>
<p>Except for the reserve powers of the President (as stated in Art. 16 of the 1958 Constitution, exercised only once so far), the executive can issue decrees in areas that the Constitution grants as the responsibility of Parliament only if a law authorizes it to do so. In other cases, orders are illegal and, should anyone sue for the order's nullification, it would be voided by the Council of State. There exists a procedure for the Prime Minister to issue <a href="/wiki/Ordonnance" title="Ordonnance">ordinances</a> in such areas, but this procedure requires Parliament's express consent (see Art 38 of the 1958 Constitution).</p>
<p>Orders issued by the Prime Minister take two forms:</p>
<ul>
<li>Orders (<i>décrets simples</i>);</li>
<li>Orders-in-council (<i>décrets en Conseil d'État</i>), when a statute mandates the advisory consultation of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_State_(France)" title="Council of State (France)">Conseil d'État</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Sometimes, people refer to <i>décrets en Conseil d'État</i> improperly as <i>décrets du Conseil d'État</i>. This would imply that it is the Conseil d'État that takes the decree, whereas the power of decreeing is restricted to the President or Prime Minister; the role of the administrative sections of the <i>Conseil</i> is purely advisory.</p>
<p>Decrees may be classified into:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">Regulations</a>, which may be:
<ul>
<li>Application decrees (<i>décrets d'application</i>), each of which must be specifically authorized by one or more <a href="/wiki/Statute" title="Statute">statutes</a> to determine some <a href="/wiki/Implementation" title="Implementation">implementation</a> conditions of this or these statutes; these constitute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondary_legislation" title="Secondary legislation">secondary legislation</a> and are roughly equivalent to British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statutory_instruments" title="Statutory instruments">statutory instruments</a>;</li>
<li>Autonomous regulations (<i>règlement autonomes</i>), which may be taken only in areas where the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_France" title="Constitution of France">Constitution of France</a> does not impose statute law (passed by the <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">Legislature</a>); these constitute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_legislation" title="Primary legislation">primary legislation</a>;</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Particular measures, such as the nomination of high-level <a href="/wiki/French_Civil_Service" title="French Civil Service">civil servants</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Only the prime minister may issue regulatory or application decrees. Presidential decrees are generally nominations, or exceptional measures where law mandates a presidential decree, such as the dissolution of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_National_Assembly" title="French National Assembly">French National Assembly</a> and the calling of new legislative elections.</p>
<p>Decrees are published in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Journal_Officiel_de_la_R%C3%A9publique_Fran%C3%A7aise" title="Journal Officiel de la République Française">Journal Officiel de la République Française</a></i> or "French Gazette".</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Decretal" title="Decretal">Decretal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Decretum_Gratiani" title="Decretum Gratiani">Decretum Gratiani</a></div>
<p>A decree (<a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_Latin" title="Ecclesiastical Latin">Latin</a>: <i>decretum</i>) in the usage of the <a href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Catholic_Church)" title="Canon law (Catholic Church)">canon law</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>, it has various meanings. Any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papal_Bull" title="Papal Bull">papal Bull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Papal_brief" title="Papal brief">Brief</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motu_Proprio" title="Motu Proprio">Motu Proprio</a> is a decree inasmuch as these documents are legislative acts of the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a>. In this sense the term is quite ancient. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Congregations" title="Roman Congregations">Roman Congregations</a> were formerly empowered to issue decrees in matters which come under their particular jurisdiction, but were forbidden from continuing to do so under <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XV" title="Pope Benedict XV">Pope Benedict XV</a> in 1917.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Each <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_province" title="Ecclesiastical province">ecclesiastical province</a>, and also each <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">diocese</a> may issue decrees in their periodical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synods" title="Synods">synods</a> within their sphere of authority.</p>
<p>Canon 29 of the 1983 Code of Canon Law offers a definition of general decrees:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>General decrees, by which a competent legislator makes common provisions for a community capable of receiving a law, are true laws and are regulated by the provisions of the canons on laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Holy_See">Holy See</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Holy See">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Vatican_City" title="Law of Vatican City">Law of Vatican City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Decretal" title="Decretal">Decretal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Decree_(canon_law)" title="Decree (canon law)">Decree (canon law)</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Catholic_Church)" title="Canon law (Catholic Church)">Canon law (Catholic Church)</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> uses decrees from the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">pope</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">papal bull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Papal_brief" title="Papal brief">papal brief</a> or <a href="/wiki/Motu_proprio" title="Motu proprio">motu proprio</a> as legislative acts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Italy" title="Law of Italy">Law of Italy</a></div>
<p>According clause 77 of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_Constitution" title="Italian Constitution">Italian Constitution</a>, "the Government may not, without an enabling act from the Houses, issue decrees having the force of ordinary law. When in extraordinary cases of necessity and urgency the Government adopts provisional measures having the force of law, it must on the same day present said measures for confirmation to the Houses which, even if dissolved, shall be summoned especially for this purpose and shall convene within five days. The decrees lose effect from their inception if they are not confirmed within sixty days from their publication. The Houses may however regulate by law legal relationships arising out of not confirmed decrees."</p>
<p>The effectiveness for sixty days produces the effects immediately, giving rights or expectations whose legal basis was in fact precarious, especially when the conversion law never intervened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portugal">Portugal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Portugal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Portugal" title="Law of Portugal">Law of Portugal</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, there are several types of decrees (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><i>decretos</i></span>) issued by the various bodies of sovereignty or by the bodies of self-government of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_regions_of_Portugal" title="Autonomous regions of Portugal">autonomous regions</a>.</p>
<p>Currently, there are the following types of decrees:</p>
<ol>
<li>Decree-law (<i>decreto-lei</i>): is a legislative act issued by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Portugal" title="Government of Portugal">Government of Portugal</a> under its legislative powers defined by Article 198 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Portugal" title="Constitution of Portugal">Portuguese Constitution</a>;</li>
<li>Regional legislative decree (<i>decreto legislativo regional</i>): is a regional law, issued by the legislative assembly of an autonomous region, within its powers defined by articles 227 and 233 of the Constitution ;</li>
<li>Decree of the President of the Republic (<i>decreto do Presidente da República</i>): is a decree issued by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Portugal" title="President of Portugal">President of Portugal</a>, for the ratification of international treaties, the appointment or dismissal of members of the Government or to exercise other presidential powers defined in the Constitution;</li>
<li>Decree (<i>decreto</i>): is an act issue by the Government of Portugal to an approve international agreement whose approval is not within the competence of the <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_the_Republic_(Portugal)" title="Assembly of the Republic (Portugal)">Assembly of the Republic</a> or has not been submitted to it or within the Government administrative jurisdiction laid down in Article 199 of the Constitution in relation to a statute that requires this decree;</li>
<li>Regulatory decree (<i>decreto regulamentar</i>): is an act issued by the Government of Portugal, under its administrative jurisdiction laid down in Article 199 of the Constitution, to make the necessary regulations for the proper execution of the laws and to take all actions and decisions necessary to promote economic and social development and to meet the community needs;</li>
<li>Regional regulatory decree (<i>decreto regulamentar regional</i>): is an act issued by the legislature or the government of an autonomous region, regulating the proper implementation of regional legislative decrees;</li>
<li>Decree from the representative of the Republic (<i>decreto do representante da República</i>): is decree of appointment or removal of members of the government of an autonomous region, issued by the representative of the Republic for that region.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iran">Iran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Iran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the article 110 of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Iran" title="Constitution of Iran">constitution</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Leader_of_Iran" title="Supreme Leader of Iran">supreme leader</a> delineates the general policies of the Islamic Republic.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Decree_of_the_President_of_Russia" title="Decree of the President of Russia">Decree of the President of Russia</a></div>
<p>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_of_1917" title="Russian Revolution of 1917">Russian Revolution</a>, a government proclamation of wide meaning was called a "decree" (Russian: декрет, dekret); more specific proclamations were called <i>ukaz</i>. Both terms are usually translated as 'decree'.</p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Russia" title="Constitution of Russia">Russian Federation's 1993 constitution</a>, an <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ukaz" title="Ukaz">Ukaz</a></i> is a Presidential decree. Such ukazes have the power of laws, but may not alter the Russian constitution or the regulations of existing laws, and may be superseded by laws passed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Assembly_of_Russia" title="Federal Assembly of Russia">Federal Assembly</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Russia" title="Government of Russia">Government of Russia</a> can also issue decrees formally called Decisions (Постановления) or Orders (Распоряжения) and may not contradict the constitution/laws or presidential decrees.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_United_States" title="Law of the United States">Law of the United States</a></div>
<p>In US legal usage, during the 19th and early 20th centuries, a <b>decree</b> was an order of a court of equity determining the rights of the parties to a suit, according to equity and good conscience. Since the 1938 procedural merger of law and equity in the federal courts under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, the term <i>judgment</i> (the parallel term in common law) has generally replaced <i>decree</i>. This is now true also in most state courts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The term <i>decree</i> has had a similar usage in admiralty, probate, and divorce law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>A decree is often a final determination, but there are also interlocutory decrees. A <i>final decree</i> fully and finally disposes of the whole litigation, determining all questions raised by the case, and it leaves nothing that requires further judicial action; it is also appealable. An <i>interlocutory decree</i> is a provisional or preliminary decree that is not final and does not fully determine the suit, so that some further proceedings are required before entry of a final decree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It is usually not appealable, although preliminary injunctions by federal courts are appealable even though interlocutory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses_of_the_term">Other uses of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other uses of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In some jurisdictions, certain types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Court_orders" title="Court orders">court orders</a> by <a href="/wiki/Judge" title="Judge">judges</a> are referred to as decrees, e.g. a <a href="/wiki/Divorce" title="Divorce">divorce</a> decree.</p>
