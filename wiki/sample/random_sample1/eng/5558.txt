<p><b>Empress Myeongseong</b> (19 October 1851 – 8 October 1895), known informally as <b>Queen Min</b>, was the first official wife of <a href="/wiki/Gojong_of_Korea" title="Gojong of Korea">Gojong of Korea</a>, the twenty-sixth king of <a href="/wiki/Joseon" title="Joseon">Joseon</a> and the first emperor of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Empire" title="Korean Empire">Korean Empire</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Meiji_Japan" title="Government of Meiji Japan">government of Meiji Japan</a> considered Queen Min an obstacle to its overseas expansion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dong-a_2002_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dong-a_2002-1">[1]</a></sup> Efforts to remove her from the political arena, orchestrated through failed rebellions prompted by the father of King Gojong, the <a href="/wiki/Heungseon_Daewongun" title="Heungseon Daewongun">Heungseon Daewongun</a> (an influential regent working with the Japanese), compelled her to take a harsher stand against Japanese influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Japan's victory in the <a href="/wiki/First_Sino-Japanese_War" title="First Sino-Japanese War">First Sino-Japanese War</a>, Queen Min advocated stronger ties between Korea and Russia in an attempt to block Japanese influence in Korea, which was represented by the Daewongun. <a href="/wiki/Miura_Gor%C5%8D" title="Miura Gorō">Miura Gorō</a>, the Japanese Minister to Korea at that time and a retired army lieutenant-general, backed the faction headed by the Daewongun, whom he considered to be more sympathetic to Japanese interests.</p>
<p>In the early morning of 8 October 1895, the <a href="/wiki/Hullyeondae" title="Hullyeondae">Hullyeondae</a> Regiment, loyal to the Daewongun, attacked the <a href="/wiki/Gyeongbokgung" title="Gyeongbokgung">Gyeongbokgung</a>, overpowering its Royal Guards. Hullyeondae officers, led by Lieutenant Colonel <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Woo_Beomseon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Woo Beomseon (page does not exist)">Woo Beomseon</a>, an ardent supporter of the Daewongun, then allowed a group of Japanese ronins, specifically recruited for this purpose to infiltrate the palace, allegedly under orders from Miura. Upon entering the Queen's quarters (Okhoru Pavilion), the assassins "killed three court [women] suspected of being the queen. When they confirmed that one of them was Min, they burned the corpse in a pine forest in front of the Okhoru Pavilion, and then dispersed the ashes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> She was 43 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The assassination of Queen Min ignited outrage among other foreign powers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> To appease growing international criticism, the Japanese government "recalled Miura and placed him under a staged trial at the <a href="/wiki/Hiroshima" title="Hiroshima">Hiroshima</a> District Court, while the military personnel involved were tried at a military court. All were given the verdict of not guilty on the grounds of insufficient evidence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, the assassination prompted <a href="/wiki/Anti-Japanese_sentiment_in_Korea" title="Anti-Japanese sentiment in Korea">anti-Japanese sentiment in Korea</a> with the "Short Hair Act Order" (단발령, 斷髮令), and some Koreans created the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Righteous_Army" title="Righteous Army">Eulmi Righteous Army</a> and actively set up protests nationwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Agwan_Pacheon_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Agwan_Pacheon-6">[6]</a></sup> Following the queen's assassination, King Gojong and the crown prince (later Emperor <a href="/wiki/Sunjong_of_Korea" title="Sunjong of Korea">Sunjong of Korea</a>) fled to the <a href="/wiki/Korea_royal_refuge_at_the_Russian_legation" title="Korea royal refuge at the Russian legation">Russian legation</a> in 1896. This led to the general repeal of the <a href="/wiki/Gabo_Reform" title="Gabo Reform">Gabo Reform</a>, which was controlled by Japanese influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Agwan_Pacheon_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Agwan_Pacheon-6">[6]</a></sup> In October 1897, King Gojong returned to Gyeongungung (modern-day <a href="/wiki/Deoksugung" title="Deoksugung">Deoksugung</a>). There, he proclaimed the founding of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Empire" title="Korean Empire">Korean Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Agwan_Pacheon_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Agwan_Pacheon-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, there has been renewed interest in Empress Myeongseong due to popular novels, a <a href="/wiki/The_Sword_with_No_Name" title="The Sword with No Name">film</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Empress_Myeongseong_(TV_series)" title="Empress Myeongseong (TV series)">TV drama</a> and even a <a href="/wiki/The_Last_Empress_(musical)" title="The Last Empress (musical)">musical</a> based on her life story.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_an_era">End of an era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: End of an era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1864 <a href="/wiki/Cheoljong_of_Joseon" title="Cheoljong of Joseon">Cheoljong of Joseon</a> was dying, and there were no male heirs, the result of suspected foul play by a rival branch of the royal family, the <a href="/wiki/Andong_Kim_clan" title="Andong Kim clan">Andong Kim clan</a>. The Andong Kim clan had risen to power through intermarriage with the royal <a href="/wiki/House_of_Yi" title="House of Yi">House of Yi</a>. <a href="/wiki/Queen_Cheorin" title="Queen Cheorin">Queen Cheorin</a>, Cheoljong's consort and a member of the Andong Kim clan, claimed the right to choose the next king, although traditionally the eldest <a href="/wiki/Queen_dowager" title="Queen dowager">queen dowager</a> is the one with the authority to select the new king. Chŏljong's cousin, Grand Royal Dowager Sinjeong, the widow of <a href="/wiki/Heonjong_of_Joseon" title="Heonjong of Joseon">Heonjong of Joseon</a>'s father (posthumously entitled Ikjong) of the Pungyang Jo clan, who too had risen to prominence by intermarriage with the Yi family, currently held this title.</p>
<p>Queen Sinjeong saw an opportunity to advance the cause of the Pungyang Jo clan, the only true rival of the Andong Kim clan in Korean politics. As Cheoljong fell deeper under his illness, the Grand Royal Dowager Queen was approached by Yi Ha-eung, an obscure descendant of <a href="/wiki/Yeongjo_of_Joseon" title="Yeongjo of Joseon">Yeongjo of Joseon</a> through his son <a href="/wiki/Crown_Prince_Sado" title="Crown Prince Sado">Crown Prince Sado</a>.</p>
<p>The branch that Yi Ha-eung's family belonged to was an obscure line of descendants of the Yi clan, which survived the often deadly political intrigue that frequently embroiled the Joseon court by forming no affiliation with any factions. Yi Ha-eung himself was ineligible for the throne due to a law that dictated that any possible heir had to be part of the generation after the most recent incumbent of the throne, but his second son |Yi Myeongbok was a possible successor to the throne.</p>
<p>The Pungyang Jo clan saw that Yi Myeongbok was only 12 years old and would not be able to rule in his own name until he came of age, and that they could easily influence Yi Ha-eung, who would be acting as <a href="/wiki/Regent" title="Regent">regent</a> for his son. As soon as news of Cheoljong's death reached Yi Ha-eung through his intricate network of spies in the palace, he and the Pungyang Jo clan took the hereditary royal seal (considered necessary for a legitimate reign to take place and aristocratic recognition to be received), effectively giving Queen Sinjeong absolute power to select the successor to the throne. By the time Cheoljong's death became a known fact, the Andong Kim clan was powerless to act according to law because the seal already lay in the hands of Grand Royal Dowager Queen Sinjeong.</p>
<p>In the autumn of 1864, Yi Myeongbok was crowned as <a href="/wiki/Gojong_of_Korea" title="Gojong of Korea">King Gojong</a> of <a href="/wiki/Joseon" title="Joseon">Joseon</a>, with his father titled <a href="/wiki/Heungseon_Daewongun" title="Heungseon Daewongun">Heungseon Daewongun</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang">대원군</span>; <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a>: <span lang="ko-Hani">大院君</span> "Grand Internal Prince").</p>
<p>The strongly <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucian</a> Heungseon Daewongun proved to be a wise and calculating leader in the early years of Gojong's reign. He abolished the old government institutions that had become corrupt under the rule of various clans, revised the law codes along with the household laws of the royal court and the rules of court ritual, and heavily reformed the military techniques of the royal armies. Within a few short years he was able to secure complete control of the court, and eventually receive the submission of the Pungyang Jos while successfully disposing of the last of the Andong Kims, whose corruption, he believed, was responsible for ruining the country.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A_new_queen">A new queen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: A new queen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The future queen-consort was born into the aristocratic Min family of the Yeoheung branch on 19 October 1851<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Yeoju" title="Yeoju">Yeoju</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gyeonggi_Province" title="Gyeonggi Province">Gyeonggi Province</a>, where the clan originated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Yeoheung Mins were a noble clan boasting many highly positioned bureaucrats in its illustrious past, as well as two queen consorts, <a href="/wiki/Queen_Wongyeong" title="Queen Wongyeong">Queen Wongyeong</a>, the wife of <a href="/wiki/Taejong_of_Joseon" title="Taejong of Joseon">Taejong of Joseon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Queen_Inhyeon" title="Queen Inhyeon">Queen Inhyeon</a>, the wife of <a href="/wiki/Sukjong_of_Joseon" title="Sukjong of Joseon">Sukjong of Joseon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before her marriage, the Empress was known as the daughter of Min Chirok (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang">민치록</span>; <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a>: <span lang="ko-Hani">閔致祿</span>). While some fictional accounts call her <i>Min Jayeong</i>, this name has not been confirmed by historical sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-11">[11]</a></sup> At the age of eight she had lost her father. Then, her mother, Lady Hanchang of the Yi clan (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: <span lang="ko"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%ED%95%9C%EC%82%B0%EC%9D%B4%EC%94%A8" title="wiktionary:한산이씨">한산이씨</a></span> ), and she moved to the House of Gamgodang (감고당) and lived there until she moved to the palace and became Queen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Gojong reached the age of 15, his father decided it was time for him to be married. The Daewongun was diligent to search for a queen who would serve his purposes: she must have no close relatives who would harbor political ambitions, yet come from a noble lineage so as to justify his choice to the court and the people. Candidates were rejected one by one, until both the Daewongun's wife Yeoheung (Princess Consort to the Prince of the Great Court; Yeoheung Budaebuin; 여흥부대부인, 驪興府大夫人)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and his mother proposed a bride from their own clan, the Yeoheung Min.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-11">[11]</a></sup> The two women described the girl persuasively: she was orphaned and possessed beautiful features, a healthy body, and an ordinary level of education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bride underwent a strict selection process, culminating in a meeting with the Daewongun on 6 March, and a marriage ceremony on 20 March 1866.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Min, barely 16, married the 15-year-old king and was invested in a ceremony (책비, <i>chaekbi</i>) as the Queen Consort of Joseon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Two places assert claims on the marriage and ascension: both Injeong Hall (인정전) at <a href="/wiki/Changdeokgung" title="Changdeokgung">Changdeok Palace</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-11">[11]</a></sup> and Norak Hall (노락당) at <a href="/wiki/Unhyeongung" title="Unhyeongung">Unhyeon Palace</a>. The wig typically worn by brides at royal weddings was so heavy for the slight 16-year-old bride that a tall court lady was specially assigned to support it from the back. Directly following the wedding was the three-day ceremony for the reverencing of the ancestors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Older officials soon noticed that the new queen consort was an assertive and ambitious woman, unlike other queens preceding her. She did not participate in lavish parties, rarely commissioned extravagant fashions from the royal ateliers, and almost never hosted afternoon tea parties with the various princesses of the royal family or powerful aristocratic ladies unless politics required her to do so. While she was expected to act as an icon for Korea's high society, the queen rejected this role. Instead, she devoted time to reading books generally reserved for men (such as <i><a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_Annals" title="Spring and Autumn Annals">Spring and Autumn Annals</a></i> and its accompanying <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zuo_Zhuan" title="Zuo Zhuan">Zuo Zhuan</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-11">[11]</a></sup>) and furthered her own education in history, science, politics, philosophy, and religion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_beginnings">The beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The beginnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Court_domination">Court domination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Court domination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By the age of twenty, the queen consort had begun to wander outside her apartments at <a href="/wiki/Changgyeonggung" title="Changgyeonggung">Changgyeong Palace</a> and to play an active part in politics in spite of the Daewongun and various high officials, who viewed her as becoming meddlesome. The political struggle between the queen consort and the Heungseon Daewongun became public when the son she bore died prematurely four days after birth. The Heungseon Daewongun publicly accused her of being unable to bear a healthy male child, while she suspected her father-in-law of foul play through the <a href="/wiki/Ginseng" title="Ginseng">ginseng</a> emetic treatment he had brought her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The Daewongun then directed Gojong to conceive through a concubine, Lee Gwi-in from the Yeongbo Hall (영보당귀인 이씨), and on 16 April 1868, she gave birth to Prince Wanhwa (완화군), to whom the Daewongun gave the title of crown prince.</p>
<p>However, the queen consort had begun to secretly form a powerful faction against the Heungseon Daewongun once she reached adulthood; now, with the backing of high officials, scholars, and members of her clan, she sought to remove the Heungseon Daewongun from power. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Min_Seung-ho&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Min Seung-ho (page does not exist)">Min Seung-ho</a>, one of the queen consort's relatives, along with court scholar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Choe_Ik-hyeon" title="Choe Ik-hyeon">Choe Ik-hyeon</a>, devised a formal impeachment of the Heungseon Daewongun to be presented to the Royal Council of Administration, arguing that Gojong, now 22, should rule in his own right. In 1872, with the approval of Gojong and the Royal Council, the Heungseon Daewongun was forced to retire to <a href="/wiki/Unhyeongung" title="Unhyeongung">Unhyeongung</a>, his estate at Yangju. The queen consort then banished the royal concubine and her child to a village outside the capital, stripped of royal titles, and the child soon died afterwards.</p>
<p>With these expulsions, the queen consort gained complete control over her court, and placed family members in high court positions. Finally, she was a queen consort who ruled along with her husband; moreover she was recognized as being distinctly more politically active than Gojong.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_.22Hermit_Kingdom.22_emerges">The "Hermit Kingdom" emerges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title='Edit section: The "Hermit Kingdom" emerges'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the Korean refusal to receive Japanese envoys announcing the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a>, some Japanese aristocrats favored an immediate invasion of Korea, but the idea was quickly dropped upon the return of the Iwakura Mission on the grounds that the new Japanese government was neither politically nor fiscally stable enough to start a war. When Heungseon Daewongun was ousted from politics, Japan renewed efforts to establish ties with Korea, but the Imperial envoy arriving at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dongnae" title="Dongnae">Dongnae</a> in 1873 was turned away.</p>
<p>The Japanese government, which sought to emulate the empires of Europe in their tradition of enforcing so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unequal_Treaties" title="Unequal Treaties">Unequal Treaties</a>, responded by sending the Japanese gunboat <i><a href="/wiki/Japanese_gunboat_Un%27y%C5%8D" title="Japanese gunboat Un'yō">Unyō</a></i> towards <a href="/wiki/Busan" title="Busan">Busan</a> and another warship to the Bay of Yeongheung on the pretext of surveying sea routes, meaning to pressure Korea into opening its doors. The <i>Unyō</i> ventured into restricted waters of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ganghwa_Island" title="Ganghwa Island">Ganghwa Island</a>, provoking an attack from Korean shore batteries. The <i>Unyō</i> fled but the Japanese used the incident as a pretext to force a treaty on the Korean government. In 1876 six naval vessels and an imperial Japanese envoy were sent to Ganghwa Island to enforce this command.</p>
<p>A majority of the royal court favored absolute isolationism, but Japan had demonstrated its willingness to use force. After numerous meetings, officials were sent to sign the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ganghwa_Treaty" title="Ganghwa Treaty">Ganghwa Treaty</a>, a treaty that had been modeled after treaties imposed on Japan by the United States. The treaty was signed on 15 February 1876, thus opening Korea to Japan.</p>
<p>Various ports were forced to open to Japanese trade, and Japanese now had rights to buy land in designated areas. The treaty also permitted the opening of <a href="/wiki/Incheon" title="Incheon">Incheon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wonsan" title="Wonsan">Wonsan</a> to Japanese merchants. For the first few years, Japan enjoyed a near total monopoly of trade, while Korean merchants suffered serious losses.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="A_social_revolution">A social revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: A social revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1877, a mission headed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kim_Gi-su&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kim Gi-su (page does not exist)">Kim Gi-su</a> was commissioned by Gojong and Min to study Japanese westernization and its intentions for Korea.</p>
<p>In 1881 another mission, this one under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kim_Hongjip" title="Kim Hongjip">Kim Hongjip</a> went to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. Kim and his team were shocked at how large the Japanese cities had become. He noted that only 50 years before, <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a> and Busan of Korea were metropolitan centers of East Asia, dominant over underdeveloped Japanese cities; but now, in 1877, with <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Osaka" title="Osaka">Osaka</a> westernized, Seoul and Busan looked like vestiges of the ancient past.</p>
<p>When they were in Japan, Kim met with the Chinese ambassador to Tokyo, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ho_Ju-chang&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ho Ju-chang (page does not exist)">Ho Ju-chang</a> and the councilor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huang_Tsun-hsien" title="Huang Tsun-hsien">Huang Tsun-hsien</a>. They discussed the international situation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qing" title="Qing">Qing</a> <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and Joseon's place in the rapidly changing world. Huang Tsu-hsien presented to Kim a book he had written called <i>Korean Strategy.</i></p>
<p>China was no longer the hegemonic power of East Asia, and Korea no longer enjoyed military superiority over Japan. In addition, the Russian Empire began expansion into Asia. Huang advised that Korea should adopt a pro-Chinese policy, while retaining close ties with Japan for the time being. He also advised an alliance with the United States for protection against Russia. He advised opening trade relations with Western nations and adopting Western technology. He noted that China had tried but failed due to its size, but Korea was smaller than Japan. He viewed Korea as a barrier to Japanese expansion into mainland Asia. He suggested Korean youths be sent to China and Japan to study, and Western teachers of technical and scientific subjects be invited to Korea.</p>
<p>When Kim returned to Seoul, Queen Min took special interest in Huang's book and commissioned copies be sent out to all the ministers. She had hoped to win <a href="/wiki/Yangban" title="Yangban">yangban</a> approval to invite Western nations into Korea, to open up trade with and keep Japan in check. She wanted to first allow Japan to help in the modernization process but towards completion of certain projects, be driven out by Western powers.</p>
<p>However, the yangban still opposed opening the country to the West. <a href="/wiki/Choi_Ik-hyun" title="Choi Ik-hyun">Choi Ik-hyun</a>, who had helped with the impeachment of Heungseon Daewongun, sided with the isolationists, saying that the Japanese were just like the "Western barbarians" who would spread subversive notions like <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a> (which had been a major issue during Heungseon Daewongun's reign that ended in massive persecution).</p>
<p>To the socially conservative yangban, Queen Min's plan meant the destruction of social order. The response to the distribution of "Korean Strategy" was a joint memorandum to the throne from scholars in every province of the kingdom. They stated that the ideas in the book were mere abstract theories, unrealizable in practice, and that the adoption of Western technology was not the only way to enrich the country. They demanded that the number of envoys exchanged, ships engaged in trade and articles of trade be strictly limited, and that all foreign books in Korea should be destroyed.</p>
<p>Despite these objections, in 1881, a large fact-finding mission was sent to Japan to stay for seventy days observing Japanese government offices, factories, military and police organizations, and business practices. They also obtained information about innovations in the Japanese government copied from the West, especially the proposed constitution.</p>
<p>On the basis of these reports, the Queen Consort began the reorganization of the government. Twelve new bureaus were established that dealt with foreign relations with the West, China, and Japan. Other bureaus were established to effectively deal with commerce. A bureau of the military was created to modernize weapons and techniques. Civilian departments were also established to import Western technology.</p>
<p>In the same year, the Queen Consort signed documents for top military students to be sent to Qing China. The Japanese quickly volunteered to supply military students with rifles and train a unit of the Korean army to use them. She agreed but reminded the Japanese that the students would still be sent to China for further education on Western military technologies.</p>
<p>The modernization of the military was met with opposition. The special treatment of the new training unit caused resentment among the other troops. In September 1881, a plot was uncovered to overthrow the Queen Consort’s faction, depose the King, and place Heungseon Daewongun's illegitimate (third) son, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yi_Jae-seon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yi Jae-seon (page does not exist)">Yi Jae-seon</a> on the throne. The plot was frustrated by the Queen Consort but Heungseon Daewongun was kept safe from persecution because he was still the father of the King.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_insurrection_of_1882">The insurrection of 1882</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The insurrection of 1882">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Imo_Incident" title="Imo Incident">Imo Incident</a></div>
<p>In 1882, members of the old military became resentful of the special treatment of the new units and so attacked and destroyed the house of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Min_Gyeom-ho&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Min Gyeom-ho (page does not exist)">Min Gyeom-ho</a>, a relative of the Queen Consort, who was the administrative head of the training units. These soldiers then fled to the Heungseon Daewongun, who publicly rebuked but privately encouraged them. The Heungseon Daewongun then took control of the old units.</p>
<p>He ordered an attack on the administrative district of Seoul that housed the <a href="/wiki/Gyeongbokgung" title="Gyeongbokgung">Gyeongbokgung</a>, the diplomatic quarter, military centers, and science institutions. The soldiers attacked police stations to free comrades who had been arrested and then began ransacking private estates and mansions belonging to relatives of the Queen Consort. These units then stole rifles and began to kill Japanese training officers, narrowly missed killing the Japanese ambassador to Seoul, who quickly escaped to Incheon. The military rebellion then headed towards the palace but both Queen Consort and the King escaped in disguise and fled to her relative’s villa in <a href="/wiki/Cheongju" title="Cheongju">Cheongju</a>, where they remained in hiding.</p>
<p>Numerous supporters of the Queen Consort were put to death as soon as the Daewongun arrived and took administrative control of Gyeongbokgung. He immediately dismantled the reform measures implemented by the Queen Consort and relieved the new units of their duties. Foreign policy quickly turned isolationist, and Chinese and Japanese envoys were forced out of the capital.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li_Hung-chang" title="Li Hung-chang">Li Hung-chang</a>, with the consent of Korean envoys in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, sent 4,500 Chinese troops to restore order, as well as to secure Chinese interests in the country. The troops arrested the Heungseon Daewongun, who was then taken to China to be tried for treason. The royal couple returned and overturned all of the Daewongun's actions.</p>
<p>The Japanese forced King Gojong privately, without Queen Min's knowledge, to sign the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan-Korea_Treaty_of_1882" title="Japan-Korea Treaty of 1882">Japan-Korea Treaty of 1882</a> on 10 August 1882, to pay 550,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yen" title="Yen">yen</a> for lives and property that the Japanese had lost during the insurrection, and permit Japanese troops to guard the Japanese embassy in Seoul. When the Queen Consort learned of the treaty, she proposed to China a new trade agreement, granting the Chinese special privileges and rights to ports inaccessible to the Japanese. She also requested that a Chinese commander take control of the new military units and a German adviser named <a href="/wiki/Paul_Georg_von_M%C3%B6llendorff" title="Paul Georg von Möllendorff">Paul Georg von Möllendorff</a> to head the Maritime Customs Service.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Mission_to_America">The Mission to America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Mission to America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 1883, the Queen Consort established English language schools with American instructors. She sent a special mission in July 1883 to the United States, headed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Min_Yeong-ik&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Min Yeong-ik (page does not exist)">Min Yeong-ik</a>, one of her relatives. The mission arrived at <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> carrying the newly created Korean national flag, visited many American historical sites, heard lectures on American history, and attended a gala event in their honor given by the mayor of San Francisco and other U.S. officials. The mission dined with President <a href="/wiki/Chester_A._Arthur" title="Chester A. Arthur">Chester A. Arthur</a>, and discussed the growing threat of Japan and American investment in Korea. At the end of September, Min Yeong-ik returned to Seoul and reported to the Queen Consort,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I was born in the dark. I went out into the light, and, your Majesty, it is my displeasure to inform you that I have returned to the dark. I envision a Seoul of towering buildings filled with Western establishments that will place herself back above the Japanese barbarians. Great things lie ahead for this Kingdom, great things. We must take action, your Majesty, without hesitation, to further modernize this still ancient kingdom.</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_reformist_vs._the_conservatives">The reformist vs. the conservatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The reformist vs. the conservatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Progressives were founded during the late 1870s by a group of yangban who fully supported Westernization of Joseon. However, they wanted immediate Westernization, including a complete cut-off of ties with Qing China. Unaware of their anti-Chinese sentiments, the Queen Consort granted frequent audiences and meetings with them to discuss progressivism and nationalism. They advocated for educational and social reforms, including the equality of the sexes by granting women full rights, issues that were not even acknowledged in their already Westernized neighbor of Japan. The Queen Consort was completely enamored by the Progressives in the beginning, but when she learned that they were deeply anti-Chinese, she quickly turned her back on them. Cutting ties with China immediately was not in her gradual plan of Westernization. She saw the consequences Joseon would have to face if she did not play China and Japan off by the West gradually, especially since she was a strong advocate of the Sadae faction who were pro-China and pro-gradual Westernization.</p>
<p>However, in 1884, the conflict between the Progressives and the Sadaes intensified. When American legation officials, particularly Naval Attaché <a href="/wiki/George_Clayton_Foulk" title="George Clayton Foulk">George C. Foulk</a>, heard about the growing problem, they were outraged and reported directly to the Queen Consort. The Americans attempted to bring the two groups to peace with each other in order to aid the Queen Consort in a peaceful transformation of Joseon into a modern nation. After all, she liked the ideas and plans of both parties. As a matter of fact, she was in support of many of the Progressive's ideas, except for severing relations with China.</p>
<p>However, the Progressives, fed up with the Sadaes and the growing influence of the Chinese, sought the aid of the Japanese legation guards and staged a bloody palace coup on 4 December 1884. The Progressives killed numerous high Sadaes and secured key government positions vacated by the Sadaes who had fled the capital or had been killed.</p>
<p>The refreshed administration began to issue various edicts in both the King and Queen Consort's names and they were eager to implement political, economic, social, and cultural reforms. She, however, was horrified by the bellicosity of the Progressives and refused to support their actions and declared any documents signed in her name to be null and void. After only two days of new influence over the administration, they were crushed by Chinese troops under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuan_Shih-kai" title="Yuan Shih-kai">Yuan Shih-kai</a>'s command. A handful of Progressive leaders were killed. Once again, the Japanese government saw the opportunity to extort money out of the Joseon government by forcing Gojong, again without the knowledge of his wife, to sign a treaty. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan-Korea_Treaty_of_1885" title="Japan-Korea Treaty of 1885">Treaty of Hanseong</a> forced Joseon to pay a large sum of indemnity for damages inflicted on Japanese lives and property during the coup.</p>
<p>On 18 April 1885 the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Li-Ito_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Li-Ito Agreement (page does not exist)">Li-Ito Agreement</a> was made in Tianjin, China, between the Japanese and the Chinese. In it, they agreed to both pull troops out of Joseon and that either party would send troops only if their property was endangered and that each would inform the other before doing so. Both nations also agreed to pull out their military instructors to allow the newly arrived Americans to take full control of that duty. The Japanese withdrew troops from Korea, leaving a small number of legation guards, but the Queen Consort was ahead of the Japanese in their game. She summoned Chinese envoys and through persuasion, convinced them to keep 2,000 soldiers disguised as Joseon police or merchants to guard the borders from any suspicious Japanese actions and to continue to train Korean troops.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Innovator">The Innovator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The Innovator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Peace finally settled upon the once-renowned "Land of the Morning Calm." With the majority of Japanese troops out of Joseon and Chinese protection readily available, the plans for further, drastic modernization were continued. Plans to establish a palace school to educate children of the elite had been in the making since 1880 but were finally executed in May 1885 with the approval of the Queen Consort. A palace school named "Yugyoung Kung-won" (육영공원, 育英公院, Royal English School) was established, with an American missionary, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homer_B._Hulbert" title="Homer B. Hulbert">Homer B. Hulbert</a>, and three other missionaries to lead the development of the curriculum. The school had two departments, liberal education and military education. Courses were taught exclusively in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> using English textbooks. However, due to low attendance, the school was closed shortly after the last English teacher, Bunker, resigned in late 1893.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KTimes_30052010_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-KTimes_30052010-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Queen Consort also gave her patronage to the first all-girls' educational institution, Ewha Academy, established in Seoul, 1886 by American missionary, <a href="/wiki/Mary_F._Scranton" title="Mary F. Scranton">Mary F. Scranton</a> (later became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ewha_University" title="Ewha University">Ewha University</a>). In 1887, the Queen Consort personally gave the name "Ewha" (literally "pear blossom"; the symbol of the royal Jeonju Yi clan) and sent a tablet to encourage Ms. Scranton's effort and its future. Ms. Scranton accepted the bestowed name to correspond to the Queen Consort's grace. This was the first time in history that any Korean girl, commoner or aristocratic, had the right to an education. In reality, as Louisa Rothweiler, a founding teacher of Ewha Academy observed, the school was, at its early stage, more of a place for poor girls to be fed and clothed than a place of education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KTimes_30052010_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-KTimes_30052010-17">[17]</a></sup> This was a significant social change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Protestant missionaries contributed much to the development of Western education in Joseon Korea. The Queen Consort, unlike her father-in-law, who had oppressed Christians, invited different missionaries to enter Joseon. She knew and valued their knowledge of Western history, science, and mathematics, and was aware of the advantage of having them within the nation. Unlike the Isolationists, she saw no threat to the Confucian morals of Korean society in the advent of Christianity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Religious tolerance was another one of her goals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_press">The press</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The press">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspaper</a> to be published in Joseon was the "Hanseong Sunbo", an all-<a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a> newspaper. It was published as a thrice monthly official government gazette by the Bakmun-guk (Publishing house), an agency of the Foreign Ministry. It included contemporary news of the day, essays and articles about Westernization, and news of further modernization of Joseon.</p>
<p>In January 1886, the Bakmun-guk published a new newspaper named the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hanseong_Jubo" title="Hanseong Jubo">Hanseong Jubo</a> (The Seoul Weekly). The publication of a Korean-language newspaper was a significant development, and the paper itself played an important role as a communication media to the masses until it was abolished in 1888 under pressure from the Chinese government.</p>
<p>A newspaper in entirely <a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>, disregarding the Korean Hanja script, was not published until 1894. Ganjo Sinbo (The Seoul News) was published as a weekly newspaper under the patronage of both Gojong &amp; the Queen Consort, it was written half in Korean and half in Japanese.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicine.2C_religion.2C_and_music">Medicine, religion, and music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Medicine, religion, and music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The arrival of <a href="/wiki/Horace_Newton_Allen" title="Horace Newton Allen">Horace Newton Allen</a> under invitation of the Queen Consort in September 1884 marked the official beginning of Christianity rapidly spreading in Joseon. He was able, with the Queen Consort's permission and official sanction, to arrange for the appointment of other missionaries as government employees. He also introduced modern medicine in Korea by establishing the first western Royal Medical Clinic of Gwanghyewon in February 1885.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 1885, a horde of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> missionaries began to flood into Joseon. The Isolationists were horrified and realized they had finally been defeated by the Queen Consort. The doors to Korea were not only open to ideas, technology, and culture but also to other religions. Having lost immense power with Heungseon Daewongun (still captive in China), the Isolationists could do nothing but simply watch. <a href="/wiki/Horace_Grant_Underwood" title="Horace Grant Underwood">Horace Grant Underwood</a> and his wife, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=William_B._Scranton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="William B. Scranton (page does not exist)">William B. Scranton</a>, his wife, and his mother, Mary Scranton, made Korea their new home in May 1885. They established churches within Seoul and began to establish centers in the countrysides. Catholic missionaries arrived soon afterwards, reviving Catholicism which had witnessed massive persecution in 1866 under Heungseon Daewongun's rule.</p>
<p>While winning many converts, <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> missionaries made significant contributions towards the modernization of the country. Concepts of equality, human rights and freedom, and the participation of both men and women in religious activities were all new to Joseon. The Queen Consort was ecstatic at the prospect of integrating these values within the government. She had wanted the literacy rate to rise, and with the aid of Christian educational programs, it did so significantly within a matter of a few years.</p>
<p>Drastic changes were made to music as well. Western music theory partly displaced the traditional Eastern concepts. The Protestant missions introduced Christian hymns and other Western songs that created a strong impetus to modernize Korean ideas about music.The organ and other Western musical instruments were introduced in 1890, and a Christian hymnal was published in the <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean language</a> in 1893 under the commission of the Queen Consort. She herself, however, never became a Christian, but remained a devout Buddhist with influences from shamanism and Confucianism; her religious beliefs would become the model, indirectly, for those of many modern <a href="/wiki/Koreans" title="Koreans">Koreans</a>, who share her belief in <a href="/wiki/Religious_pluralism" title="Religious pluralism">pluralism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_tolerance" title="Religious tolerance">religious tolerance</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Modern weapons were imported from Japan and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in 1883. The first military factories were established and new military uniforms were created in 1884. Under joint patronage of Gojong &amp; his Queen Consort, a request was made to the United States for more American military instructors to speed up the military modernization of Korea. Out of all the projects that were going on simultaneously, the military project took the longest. To manage these simultaneous projects was in itself was a major accomplishment for any nation. Not even Japan had modernized at the rate of Joseon, and not with as many projects going on at once, a precursor to modern <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> as one of East Asia's Tigers in rapid development into a first class nation during the 1960s–1980s.</p>
<p>In October 1883, American minister <a href="/wiki/Lucius_Foote" title="Lucius Foote">Lucius Foote</a> arrived to take command of the modernization of Joseon's older army units that had not started Westernizing. In April 1888, General <a href="/wiki/William_McEntyre_Dye" title="William McEntyre Dye">William McEntyre Dye</a> and two other military instructors arrived from the United States, followed in May by a fourth instructor. They brought about rapid military development.</p>
<p>A new military school was created called "Yeonmu Gongwon", and an officers training program began. However, despite armies becoming more and more on par with the Chinese and the Japanese, the idea of a <a href="/wiki/Navy" title="Navy">navy</a> was neglected. As a result, it became one of the few failures of the modernization project. Due to the neglect of developing naval defence, Joseon's sea borders were open to invasion. It was an ironic mistake since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_invasions_of_Korea_(1592%E2%80%931598)" title="Japanese invasions of Korea (1592–1598)">nearly 300 years earlier</a>, Joseon's navy was the strongest in all of East Asia<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Now, the Korean navy was nothing but ancient ships that could barely defend themselves from the advanced ships of modern navies.</p>
<p>However, for a short while, hope for the Korean military could be seen. With rapidly growing armies, Japan itself was becoming fearful of the impact of Korean troops if her government did not interfere soon to stall the process.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the opening of all Korean ports to the Japanese and Western merchants in 1888, contact and involvement with outsiders increased foreign trade rapidly. In 1883, the Maritime Customs Service was established under the patronage of the Queen Consort and the supervision of <a href="/wiki/Sir_Robert_Hart,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Robert Hart, 1st Baronet">Sir Robert Hart, 1st Baronet</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. The Maritime Customs Service administered the business of foreign trade and collection of tariff.</p>
<p>By 1883, the economy was now no longer in a state of monopoly conducted by the Japanese as it had been only a few years ago. The majority was in control by the Koreans while portions were distributed between Western nations, Japan and China. In 1884, the first Korean commercial firms such as the Daedong and the Changdong Company emerged. The Bureau of Mint also produced a new coin called "tangojeon" in 1884, securing a stable Korean currency at the time. Western investment began to take hold as well in 1886.</p>
<p>The German <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A.H._Maeterns&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A.H. Maeterns (page does not exist)">A.H. Maeterns</a>, with the aid of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">United States Department of Agriculture</a>, created a new project called "American Farm" on a large plot of land donated by the Queen Consort to promote modern <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>. Farm implements, seeds, and milk cows were imported from the United States. In June 1883, the Bureau of Machines was established and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_engines" title="Steam engines">steam engines</a> were imported. However, despite the fact that Gojong and his Queen Consort brought the Korean economy to an acceptable level to the West, modern manufacturing facilities did not emerge due to a political interruption: the assassination of the Queen Consort. Be that as it may, telegraph lines between Joseon, China, and Japan were laid between 1883 and 1885, facilitating communication.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Detailed descriptions of Min can be found in both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_National_Assembly_Library_of_Korea" title="The National Assembly Library of Korea">The National Assembly Library of Korea</a> and records kept by Lilias Underwood,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> a close and trusted American friend of Min who came to Korea in 1888 as a missionary and was appointed as her doctor.</p>
<p>Both sources describe the Empress' appearance, voice, and public manner. She was said to have had a soft face with strong features—a classic beauty contrasting with the king's preference for "sultry" women. Her personal speaking voice was soft and warm, but when conducting affairs of the state, she asserted her points with strength. Her public manner was formal, and she heavily adhered to court etiquette and traditional law. Underwood described the Empress in the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I wish I could give the public a true picture of the queen as she appeared at her best, but this would be impossible, even had she permitted a photograph to be taken, for her charming play of expression while in conversation, the character and intellect which were then revealed, were only half seen when the face was in repose. She wore her hair like all Korean ladies, parted in the center, drawn tightly and very smoothly away from the face and knotted rather low at the back of the head. A small ornament...was worn on the top of the head fastened by a narrow black band. Her majesty seemed to care little for ornaments, and wore very few. No Korean women wear earrings, and the queen was no exception, nor have I ever seen her wear a necklace, a brooch, or a bracelet. She must have had many rings, but I never saw her wear more than one or two of European manufacture...</p>
<p>According to Korean custom, she carried a number of filigree gold ornaments decorated with long silk tassels fastened at her side. So simple, so perfectly refined were all her tastes in dress, it is difficult to think of her as belonging to a nation called half civilized...Slightly pale and quite thin, with somewhat sharp features and brilliant piercing eyes, she did not strike me at first sight as being beautiful, but no one could help reading force, intellect and strength of character in that face...</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The young queen consort and her husband were incompatible in the beginning of their marriage. Both found the other's ways repulsive; she preferred to stay in her chambers studying, while he enjoyed spending his days and nights drinking and attending banquets and royal parties. The queen, who was genuinely concerned with the affairs of the state and immersed herself in philosophy, history, and science books normally reserved for <i>yangban</i> men, once remarked to a close friend, "He disgusts me."</p>
<p>Court officials noted that the queen consort was exclusive in choosing who she associated with and confided in. She chose to not consummate her marriage on her wedding night as court tradition dictated her to,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but later had immense difficulty in conceiving a healthy heir. Her first pregnancy five years after marriage ended in despair and humiliation when her infant son died shortly after birth.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Her second son, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunjong" title="Sunjong">Sunjong</a>, was never a healthy child, often catching illnesses and convalescing in bed for weeks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> While Min was unable to truly connect with Gojong in the early years, trials during their later marriage brought them together.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Both began to grow affections for each other during their later years. Gojong was pressured by his advisers to take control of the government and administer his nation. However, one has to remember that Gojong was not chosen to become King because of his acumen (which he lacked because he was never formally educated) or because of his bloodline (which was mixed with courtesan and common blood), but because the Pungyang Jo clan had falsely assumed they could control the boy through his father. When it was actually time for Gojong to assume his responsibilities of the state, he often needed the aid of his wife to conduct international and domestic affairs. In this, Gojong grew an admiration for his wife's wit, intelligence, and ability to learn quickly. As the problems of the kingdom grew bigger and bigger, Gojong relied even more on his wife, she becoming his rock during times of frustration.</p>
<p>During the years of modernization of Joseon, it is safe to assume that Gojong was finally in love with his wife. They began to spend much time with each other, privately and officially. They shared each other's problems, celebrated each other's joys, and felt each other's pains. They finally became husband and wife.</p>
<p>His affection for her was undying, and it has been noted that after the death of his Queen Consort, Gojong locked himself up in his chambers for several weeks, refusing to assume his duties. When he finally did, he lost the will to even try and signed treaty after treaty that was proposed by the Japanese, giving the Japanese immense power. When his father regained political power after the death of his daughter-in-law, he presented a proposal with the aid of certain Japanese officials to lower his daughter-in-law's status as Queen Consort all the way to commoner posthumously. Gojong, a man who had always been used by others and never used his own voice for his own causes, was noted by scholars as having said, "I would rather slit my wrists and let them bleed than disgrace the woman who saved this kingdom." In an act of defiance, he refused to sign his father's and the Japanese proposal, and turned them away.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assassination">Assassination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Assassination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Korea-Empress.Myeongseong-Okhoru.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="368" data-file-width="500" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Korea-Empress.Myeongseong-Okhoru.jpg/220px-Korea-Empress.Myeongseong-Okhoru.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Korea-Empress.Myeongseong-Okhoru.jpg/330px-Korea-Empress.Myeongseong-Okhoru.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Korea-Empress.Myeongseong-Okhoru.jpg/440px-Korea-Empress.Myeongseong-Okhoru.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Queen Min's assassination, known in Korea as the <b>Eulmi Incident</b> (을미사변, 乙未事變), occurred in the early hours of 8 October 1895 at Okho-ru (옥호루, 玉壺樓) in the Geoncheonggung (건청궁, 乾淸宮), which was the rear private royal residence inside <a href="/wiki/Gyeongbokgung" title="Gyeongbokgung">Gyeongbokgung Palace</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eulmi_Incident_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eulmi_Incident-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early hours of 8 October, Japanese agents under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miura_Goro" title="Miura Goro">Miura Goro</a> carried out the assassination. Miura had orchestrated this incident with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Okamoto_Ry%C5%ABnosuke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Okamoto Ryūnosuke (page does not exist)">Okamoto Ryūnosuke</a> (岡本柳之助), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sugimura_Fukashi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sugimura Fukashi (page does not exist)">Sugimura Fukashi</a> (杉村 濬), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kunitomo_Shigeaki&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kunitomo Shigeaki (page does not exist)">Kunitomo Shigeaki</a> (國友重章), Sase Kumatetsu (佐瀨熊鐵), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nakamura_Tateo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nakamura Tateo (page does not exist)">Nakamura Tateo</a> (中村楯雄), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hirayama_Iwahiko&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hirayama Iwahiko (page does not exist)">Hirayama Iwahiko</a> (平山岩彦), and over fifty other Japanese men. Said to have collaborated in this were the pro-Japanese officers Lieutenant Colonel <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Woo_Beom-seon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Woo Beom-seon (page does not exist)">Woo Beom-seon</a> (우범선, 禹範善) and Lieutenant Colonel <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yi_Du-hwang&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yi Du-hwang (page does not exist)">Yi Du-hwang</a> (이두황, 李斗璜) both battalion commanders in the "<a href="/wiki/Hullyeondae" title="Hullyeondae">Hullyeondae</a>," a Japanese trained Regiment of the Royal Guards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eulmi_Incident_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eulmi_Incident-22">[22]</a></sup> The 1,000 Korean soldiers of the Hullyeondae, led by Lieutenant Colonel Woo Beom-seon<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ko:.EC.9A.B0.EB.B2.94.EC.84.A0_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ko:.EC.9A.B0.EB.B2.94.EC.84.A0-23">[23]</a></sup> and Lieutenant Colonel Yi Du-hwang had surrounded and opened the gates of the palace, allowing a group of Japanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ronin" title="Ronin">ronin</a> to enter the inner sanctum.</p>
<p>In front of <a href="/wiki/Gwanghwamun" title="Gwanghwamun">Gwanghwamun</a>, the Hullyeondae soldiers led by Woo Beom-seon<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ko:.EC.9A.B0.EB.B2.94.EC.84.A0_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-ko:.EC.9A.B0.EB.B2.94.EC.84.A0-23">[23]</a></sup> battled the Korean Royal Guards led by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hong_Gye-hun&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hong Gye-hun (page does not exist)">Hong Gye-hun</a> (홍계훈, 洪啓薰) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=An_Gyeong-su&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="An Gyeong-su (page does not exist)">An Gyeong-su</a> (안경수, 安駉壽).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eulmi_Incident_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Eulmi_Incident-22">[22]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hong_Gye-hun&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hong Gye-hun (page does not exist)">Hong Gye-hun</a> and Minister <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yi_Gyeong-jik&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yi Gyeong-jik (page does not exist)">Yi Gyeong-jik</a> (이경직, 李耕稙) were subsequently killed in battle, allowing the ronin assassins to proceed to Okhoru (옥호루, 玉壺樓), within Geoncheonggung, and kill the Empress. The corpse of the Empress was then burned and buried.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eulmi_Incident_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Eulmi_Incident-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historian of Japan Peter Duus has called this assassination a "hideous event, crudely conceived and brutally executed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Donald_Keene" title="Donald Keene">Donald Keene</a>, who calls the queen "an arrogant and corrupt woman", says that the way in which she was murdered was nonetheless "unspeakably barbaric."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="An_eye-witness_account">An eye-witness account</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: An eye-witness account">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Crown Prince Sunjong reported that he saw Korean troops led by Woo Beom-seon at the site of the assassination, and accused Woo as the "Foe of Mother". In addition to his accusation, Sunjong sent two assassins to kill Woo, an effort that succeeded in Hiroshima, Japan, in 1903. By then, Woo had married a Japanese woman, and had sired <a href="/wiki/Woo_Jang-choon" title="Woo Jang-choon">Woo Jang-choon</a> (禹長春 우장춘), later to become an acclaimed botanist and agricultural scientist.</p>
<p>In 2005, professor Kim Rekho (김려춘; 金麗春) of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Russian Academy of Sciences">Russian Academy of Sciences</a> came across a written account of the incident by a Russian architect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afanasy_Ivanovich_Seredin-Sabatin" title="Afanasy Ivanovich Seredin-Sabatin">Afanasy Seredin-Sabatin</a> (Афанасий Иванович Середин-Сабатин) in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Arkhiv_Vneshnei_Politiki_Rossiiskoi_Imperii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Arkhiv Vneshnei Politiki Rossiiskoi Imperii (page does not exist)">Archive of Foreign Policy of the Russian Empire</a> (Архив внешней политики Российской империи; AVPRI).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Seredin-Sabatin was in the service of the Korean government, working with the American general <a href="/wiki/William_McEntyre_Dye" title="William McEntyre Dye">William McEntyre Dye</a> who was also under contract to the Korean government. In April, Kim made a request to the <a href="/wiki/Myongji_University" title="Myongji University">Myongji University</a> (명지대학교; 明知大學校) Library LG Collection to make the document public. On 11 May 2005 the document was made public.</p>
<p>Almost five years before the document's release in South Korea, a translated copy was in circulation in the United States, having been released by the Center for Korean Research of <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> on 6 October 1995 to commemorate the 100th anniversary of the Eulmi Incident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the account, Seredin-Sabatin recorded:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The courtyard where the Queen (Consort)'s wing was located was filled with Japanese, perhaps as many as 20 or 25 men. They were dressed in peculiar gowns and were armed with sabres, some of which were openly visible. ... While some Japanese troops were rummaging around in every corner of the palace and in the various annexes, others burst into the queen's wing and threw themselves upon the women they found there. ... I ... continued to observe the Japanese turning things inside out in the queen's wing. Two Japanese grabbed one of the court ladies, pulled her out of the house, and ran down the stairs dragging her along behind them. ... Moreover one of the Japanese repeatedly asked me in English, "Where is the queen? Point the queen out to us!" ... While passing by the main Throne Hall, I noticed that it was surrounded shoulder to shoulder by a wall of Japanese soldiers and officers, and Korean mandarins, but what was happening there was unknown to me.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gabo_Reform" title="Gabo Reform">Gabo Reform</a> and the assassination of Myeongseong generated backlash against Japanese presence in Korea; it caused some Confucian scholars, as well as farmers, to form over 60 successive <a href="/wiki/Righteous_army" title="Righteous army">righteous armies</a> to fight for Korean freedom on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_peninsula" title="Korean peninsula">Korean peninsula</a>.</p>
<p>After the assassination, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gojong_of_the_Korean_Empire" title="Gojong of the Korean Empire">King Gojong</a> and the Crown Prince (later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Sunjong" title="Emperor Sunjong">Emperor Sunjong</a>) fled for refuge to <a href="/wiki/Korea_royal_refuge_at_the_Russian_legation" title="Korea royal refuge at the Russian legation">the Russian legation</a> on 11 February 1896. Also, Gojong declared the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eulmi_Four_Traitors&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eulmi Four Traitors (page does not exist)">Eulmi Four Traitors</a>. However, In 1897, Gojong, yielding to rising pressure from both overseas and the demands of the Independence Association-led public opinion, returned to Gyeongungung (modern-day <a href="/wiki/Deoksugung" title="Deoksugung">Deoksugung</a>). There, he proclaimed the founding of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Empire" title="Korean Empire">Korean Empire</a>. However, after Japan's victories in the Sino-Japanese and Russo-Japanese Wars, Korea succumbed to Japanese colonial rule in 1910.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Funeral_procession_and_tomb">Funeral procession and tomb</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Funeral procession and tomb">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1897, King Gojong, with Russian support, regained his throne, and spent "a fortune" to have his beloved Queen Min's remains properly honored and entombed. Her mourning procession included 5,000 soldiers, 650 police, 4,000 lanterns, hundreds of scrolls honoring her, and giant wooden horses intended for her use in the afterlife. The honors King Gojong placed on Queen Min for her funeral was meant as a statement to her diplomatic and heroic endeavors for Korea against the Japanese, as well as a statement of his own undying love for her. Queen Min's recovered remains are in her tomb located in <a href="/wiki/Namyangju" title="Namyangju">Namyangju</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyeonggi" title="Gyeonggi">Gyeonggi</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A more detailed citation is required. (April 2014)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath_2">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In May 2005, 84-year-old Tatsumi Kawano (川野 龍巳), the grandson of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kunitomo_Shigeaki&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kunitomo Shigeaki (page does not exist)">Kunitomo Shigeaki</a>, paid his respects to Empress Myeongseong at her tomb in <a href="/wiki/Namyangju" title="Namyangju">Namyangju</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyeonggi" title="Gyeonggi">Gyeonggi</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chosun_Ilbo_2005_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chosun_Ilbo_2005-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-42">[42]</a></sup> He apologized to Empress Myeongseong's tomb on behalf of his grandfather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chosun_Ilbo_2005_37-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chosun_Ilbo_2005-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 2009, Korean organizations have been trying to adjudicate the Japanese government for their documented complicity in the murder of Queen Min. "Japan has not made an official apology or repentance 100 years after it obliterated the Korean people for 35 years through the 1910 Korea-Japan Annexation Treaty," the statement said. The lawsuit will be filed if the Japanese government does not accept their demands that the Japanese government issue a special statement on 15 August offering the emperor's apology and mentioning whether it will release related documents on the murder case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Titles">Titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Throughout her life, Queen Min held several titles: as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Yangban" title="Yangban">yangban</a> aristocracy, as Queen Consort, and as regent of Korea. More titles were granted to her posthumously and after the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Empire" title="Korean Empire">Korean Empire</a>. This includes the name by which she is best known by today, Empress Myeongseong.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>19 October 1851 – 20 March 1866</b>: Lady Min, the daughter of Min Chi-rok, of the Yeoheung Min clan</li>
</ul>

<li><b>1 November 1873 – 1 July 1894</b>: <i>Her Majesty</i>, the Queen Regent of Joseon</li>
<li><b>1 July 1894 – 6 July 1895</b>: <i>Her Majesty</i>, the Queen Consort of Joseon</li>

<p>Documents note that she was in an official royal family photograph. A royal family photograph does exist, but it was taken after her death, consisting of Gojong, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunjong" title="Sunjong">Sunjong</a>, and Sunjong's wife <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Sunmyeong_of_the_Korean_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Empress Sunmyeong of the Korean Empire (page does not exist)">the Princess Consort of the Crown Prince</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Another_photograph_surfaces">Another photograph surfaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Another photograph surfaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There was a report by KBS News in 2003 that a photograph allegedly of the Empress had been disclosed to the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The photograph was supposedly purchased for a large sum by the grandfather of Min Soo-gyeong that was to be passed down as a family treasure. In the photo, the woman is accompanied by a <a href="/wiki/Retinue" title="Retinue">retinue</a> at her rear. Some experts have stated that the woman was clearly of high-rank and her clothing appears to be that that is worn only by the royal family. However, her outfit lacked the embroideries that decorates the apparel of the empress.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese_illustration">Japanese illustration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Japanese illustration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 13 January 2005, history professor Lee Tae-jin (이태진, 李泰鎭) of <a href="/wiki/Seoul_National_University" title="Seoul National University">Seoul National University</a> unveiled an illustration from an old Japanese magazine he had found at an antique bookstore in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>. The 84th edition of the Japanese magazine <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=F%C5%ABzokugah%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fūzokugahō (page does not exist)">Fūzokugahō</a> (風俗畫報) published on 25 January 1895 has a Japanese illustration of Gojong and the then-Queen Consort receiving <a href="/wiki/Inoue_Kaoru" title="Inoue Kaoru">Inoue Kaoru</a>, the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Charg%C3%A9_d%27affaires" title="Chargé d'affaires">chargé d'affaires</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The illustration is marked 24 December 1894 and signed by the artist Ishizuka (石塚 ) with a legend <i>"The [Korean] King and Queen, moved by our honest advice, realize the need for resolute reform for the first time."</i> Lee said that the depiction of the clothes and background are very detailed and suggests that it was drawn at the scene as it happened. Both the King and Inoue were looking at the then-Queen Consort as though the conversation were taking place between the Queen and Inoue with the King listening.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Empress_Myeongseong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the TV drama <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress_Myeongseong_(TV_drama)" title="Empress Myeongseong (TV drama)">Empress Myeongseong</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/The_Last_Empress_(musical)" title="The Last Empress (musical)">musical</a>, her name was Min Ja-yeong (민자영; 閔紫英)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, but there is no evidence of that name in written documents.</p>
<p>The 2009 film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sword_With_No_Name" title="The Sword With No Name">The Sword With No Name</a></i> is a romanticized fictional story centered around Empress Myeongseong.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>She is also depicted in the Korean TV Drama <i><a href="/wiki/Gunman_in_Joseon" title="Gunman in Joseon">Gunman in Joseon</a></i> which begins in 1876, the third year of Emperor Gojong's reign.</p>
