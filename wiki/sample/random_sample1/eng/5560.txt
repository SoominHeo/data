<p>On Earth, the terminator is a circle with a diameter that is approximately that of the Earth. The terminator passes through any point on the Earth's surface twice a day, at <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a> and once at <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a>, apart from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_region" title="Polar region">polar regions</a> where this only occurs when the point is not experiencing <a href="/wiki/Midnight_sun" title="Midnight sun">midnight sun</a> or <a href="/wiki/Polar_night" title="Polar night">polar night</a>. The circle separates the portion of the Earth experiencing daylight from that experiencing darkness (night). While a little over one half of the Earth is illuminated at any point in time (with exceptions during <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">eclipses</a>), the terminator path varies by time of day due to the rotation of the Earth on its axis. The terminator path also varies by time of year due to the revolution of the Earth around the Sun: the plane of the terminator is nearly parallel to planes created by <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">lines of longitude</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinoxes</a>, and at its maximum angle of approximately 23.5 degrees to the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">solstices</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the equator, under flat conditions (no obstructions such as mountains; or at a height above any such obstructions), the terminator moves at approximately 1668 kilometers per hour (1036 miles per hour). This speed can appear to increase when near obstructions—such as the height of a mountain, for example—as the shadow of the obstruction will be cast over the ground in advance of the terminator along a flat landscape. The speed of the terminator decreases as it approaches the poles, where it can reach a speed of zero (full-day sunlight or darkness).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Supersonic_aircraft" title="Supersonic aircraft">Supersonic aircraft</a> like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jet_fighter#Second_generation_jet_fighters_.28mid-1950s_to_early_1960s.29" title="Jet fighter">jet fighters</a> or <a href="/wiki/Concorde" title="Concorde">Concorde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-144" title="Tupolev Tu-144">Tupolev Tu-144</a> <a href="/wiki/Supersonic_transport" title="Supersonic transport">supersonic transports</a> are the only aircraft able to overtake the maximum speed of the terminator. However, slower vehicles can overtake the terminator at higher <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a>, and it is possible to walk faster than the terminator at the poles, near to the <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinoxes</a>. The visual effect is that of seeing the sun rise in the west, or set in the east.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">Amateur radio</a> operators take advantage of conditions at the terminator (so-called "gray-line" <a href="/wiki/Radio_propagation" title="Radio propagation">propagation</a>, or "grey-line" to the British) to perform long distance communications. Under good conditions, radio waves can travel along the terminator to <a href="/wiki/Antipodes" title="Antipodes">antipodal</a> points. This is primarily because the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D_region" title="D region">D layer</a>, which absorbs <a href="/wiki/High_frequency" title="High frequency">high frequency signals</a>, disappears rapidly on the dark side of the terminator line. This process is known as <a href="/wiki/Skywave" title="Skywave">skywave propagation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_terminator">Lunar terminator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terminator_(solar)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lunar terminator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The <b>Lunar terminator</b> is the division between the illuminated and dark parts of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It is the lunar equivalent of the division between <a href="/wiki/Night" title="Night">night</a> and <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">day</a> on the Earth's sphere, although the Moon's much lower rate of rotation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> means it takes longer for it to pass across the surface.</p>
<p>Due to the angle at which <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> strikes this portion of the moon, shadows cast by <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">craters</a> and other geological features are elongated, thereby making such features more apparent to the observer. This phenomenon is similar to the lengthening of shadows on Earth when the sun is low in the sky. For this reason, much lunar photographic study centers on the illuminated area near the lunar terminator, and the resulting shadows provide accurate descriptions of the terrain.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_terminator_illusion">Lunar terminator illusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terminator_(solar)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lunar terminator illusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The lunar terminator (or tilt) illusion is an <a href="/wiki/Optical_illusion" title="Optical illusion">optical illusion</a> arising from the erroneous expectation of an observer on Earth that the direction of light illuminating the moon (i.e. a line <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">perpendicular</a> to the terminator) should correspond with the position of the sun in the sky or a sun which has set, when it does not appear to do so. The cause of the illusion is simply the observer is not taking into account that the observed slope of a light ray will change across the sky because of the lack of visual clues to establish 3D <a href="/wiki/Perspective_(graphical)" title="Perspective (graphical)">perspective</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terminator_(solar)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The lunar terminator features as a plot element in the animated series <i>"Futurama"</i>, <a href="/wiki/The_Series_Has_Landed#Plot" title="The Series Has Landed">season 1, episode 2</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_significance">Scientific significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terminator_(solar)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scientific significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Examination of a terminator can yield information about the surface of a planetary body; for example, the presence of an <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> can create a fuzzier terminator. As the particles within an atmosphere are at a higher elevation, the light source can remain visible even after it has set at ground level. These particles scatter the light, reflecting some of it to the ground. Hence, the sky can remain illuminated even after the sun has set.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">Low Earth orbit</a> satellites take advantage of the fact that certain polar orbits set near the terminator do not suffer from <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">eclipse</a>, therefore their <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a> are continuously lit by sunlight. Such orbits are called dawn-dusk orbits, a type of <a href="/wiki/Sun-synchronous_orbit" title="Sun-synchronous orbit">Sun-synchronous orbit</a>. This prolongs the operational life of a LEO satellite, as onboard battery life is prolonged. It also enables specific experiments that require minimum interference from the Sun, as the designers can opt to install the relevant sensors on the dark side of the satellite.</p>
