<p><b>Mogwai</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span>) are a Scottish <a href="/wiki/Post-rock" title="Post-rock">post-rock</a> band, formed in 1995 in <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>. The band consists of <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Braithwaite" title="Stuart Braithwaite">Stuart Braithwaite</a> (guitar, vocals), <a href="/wiki/Barry_Burns" title="Barry Burns">Barry Burns</a> (guitar, piano, synthesizer, vocals), <a href="/wiki/Dominic_Aitchison" title="Dominic Aitchison">Dominic Aitchison</a> (bass guitar), and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Bulloch" title="Martin Bulloch">Martin Bulloch</a> (drums). The band typically compose lengthy guitar-based instrumental pieces that feature <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(music)" title="Dynamics (music)">dynamic contrast</a>, melodic bass guitar lines, and heavy use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_distortion" title="Audio distortion">distortion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Effects_unit" title="Effects unit">effects</a>. The band were for several years signed to renowned <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> <a href="/wiki/Independent_record_label" title="Independent record label">indie</a> label <a href="/wiki/Chemikal_Underground" title="Chemikal Underground">Chemikal Underground</a>, and have been distributed by different labels such as <a href="/wiki/Matador_Records" title="Matador Records">Matador</a> in the US and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Play_It_Again_Sam_(record_label)" title="Play It Again Sam (record label)">Play It Again Sam</a> in the UK, but now use their own label <a href="/wiki/Rock_Action_Records" title="Rock Action Records">Rock Action Records</a> in the UK, and <a href="/wiki/Sub_Pop" title="Sub Pop">Sub Pop</a> in North America. The band were frequently championed by <a href="/wiki/John_Peel" title="John Peel">John Peel</a> from their early days,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and recorded seven <a href="/wiki/John_Peel" title="John Peel">Peel Sessions</a> between 1996 and 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Peel also recorded a brief introduction for the compilation <i><a href="/wiki/Government_Commissions:_BBC_Sessions_1996%E2%80%932003" title="Government Commissions: BBC Sessions 1996–2003">Government Commissions: BBC Sessions 1996–2003</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogwai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_.281991.E2.80.9395.29">Formation (1991–95)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogwai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation (1991–95)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stuart Braithwaite and Dominic Aitchison met in April 1991, and four years later formed Mogwai with old schoolfriend <a href="/wiki/Martin_Bulloch" title="Martin Bulloch">Martin Bulloch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strong_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strong-3">[3]</a></sup> The band are named after the creatures from the film <i><a href="/wiki/Gremlins" title="Gremlins">Gremlins</a></i>, although guitarist Stuart Braithwaite comments that "it has no significant meaning and we always intended on getting a better one, but like a lot of other things we never got round to it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-4">[4]</a></sup> 'Mogwai' means "evil spirit" or "devil" in <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">Cantonese</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">魔鬼</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">móguǐ</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Jyutping" title="Jyutping">Jyutping</a>: <i><span lang="yue-jyutping" xml:lang="yue-jyutping">mo¹gwai²</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">IPA</a>: mɔ˧˥ kueɪ˨˩˦). The band debuted in February 1996 with the "Tuner"/"Lower" single and by the end of the year they received 'single of the week' from <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> for "Summer", a feat repeated early in 1997 with "New Paths to Helicon".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strong_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Strong-3">[3]</a></sup> After playing a few shows the band expanded with the introduction of <a href="/wiki/John_Cummings_(musician)" title="John Cummings (musician)">John Cummings</a> on guitar, and <a href="/wiki/Teenage_Fanclub" title="Teenage Fanclub">Teenage Fanclub</a> drummer <a href="/wiki/Brendan_O%27Hare" title="Brendan O'Hare">Brendan O'Hare</a> joined whilst they recorded their début album <i><a href="/wiki/Mogwai_Young_Team" title="Mogwai Young Team">Mogwai Young Team</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strong_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Strong-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mogwai_Young_Team_.281995.E2.80.9397.29"><i>Mogwai Young Team</i> (1995–97)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogwai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mogwai Young Team (1995–97)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The album, released in October 1997, reached number 75 on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a>, and featured a guest appearance from <a href="/wiki/Aidan_Moffat" title="Aidan Moffat">Aidan Moffat</a> of <a href="/wiki/Arab_Strap_(band)" title="Arab Strap (band)">Arab Strap</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strong_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Strong-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1998 the band had their first singles chart success with a split single with <a href="/wiki/Magoo_(band)" title="Magoo (band)">Magoo</a> of <a href="/wiki/Black_Sabbath" title="Black Sabbath">Black Sabbath</a> <a href="/wiki/Cover_version" title="Cover version">cover versions</a> reaching number 60 in the UK and an <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a> of "Fear Satan" remixes reaching number 57.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strong_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Strong-3">[3]</a></sup> In the same year, an album of remixes of the band's tracks by the likes of <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Shields" title="Kevin Shields">Kevin Shields</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alec_Empire" title="Alec Empire">Alec Empire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mike_Paradinas" title="Mike Paradinas">μ-ziq</a> was issued (<i><a href="/wiki/Kicking_a_Dead_Pig:_Mogwai_Songs_Remixed" title="Kicking a Dead Pig: Mogwai Songs Remixed">Kicking a Dead Pig: Mogwai Songs Remixed</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolk_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wolk-5">[5]</a></sup> The band also remixed tracks for <a href="/wiki/David_Holmes_(musician)" title="David Holmes (musician)">David Holmes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manic_Street_Preachers" title="Manic Street Preachers">Manic Street Preachers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolk_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wolk-5">[5]</a></sup> O'Hare was sacked after the release of the album (reportedly after upsetting the rest of the band by talking all through a performance by Arab Strap).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strong_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Strong-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Come_On_Die_Young_.281998.E2.80.9399.29"><i>Come On Die Young</i> (1998–99)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogwai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Come On Die Young (1998–99)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Barry_Burns" title="Barry Burns">Barry Burns</a> was brought in prior to the recording of <i><a href="/wiki/Come_On_Die_Young" title="Come On Die Young">Come On Die Young</a></i>, the band's second album. He had already played a few shows with the band, as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flautist" title="Flautist">flautist</a> and as an occasional pianist. According to Stuart, Barry was invited into the band because he was a "good laugh".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-4">[4]</a></sup> The album reached number 29 in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strong_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Strong-3">[3]</a></sup> The band line up remained unchanged from 1998 until November 2015, when John Cummings left to pursue other projects. Fellow Scottish musician <a href="/wiki/Luke_Sutherland" title="Luke Sutherland">Luke Sutherland</a> has contributed violin (and more recently vocals and guitar) to Mogwai's records and live performances.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rock_Action_.282000.E2.80.9301.29"><i>Rock Action</i> (2000–01)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogwai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rock Action (2000–01)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The band's 2001 album <i><a href="/wiki/Rock_Action_(album)" title="Rock Action (album)">Rock Action</a></i> gave them their highest UK album chart placing, reaching number 23.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strong_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-Strong-3">[3]</a></sup> The album was less guitar-led than previously, featuring more electronics; a larger than usual number of tracks also featured vocals, guest vocalists included <a href="/wiki/David_Pajo" title="David Pajo">David Pajo</a> of <a href="/wiki/Slint" title="Slint">Slint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gruff_Rhys" title="Gruff Rhys">Gruff Rhys</a> of <a href="/wiki/Super_Furry_Animals" title="Super Furry Animals">Super Furry Animals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gary_Lightbody" title="Gary Lightbody">Gary Lightbody</a> of <a href="/wiki/Snow_Patrol" title="Snow Patrol">Snow Patrol</a>. Shortly afterwards the band released "<a href="/wiki/My_Father_My_King" title="My Father My King">My Father My King</a>", a cacophonous 20-minute song which closed their <i>Rock Action</i>-period shows, and was billed as a companion piece to the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Happy_Songs_For_Happy_People_.282002.E2.80.9303.29"><i>Happy Songs For Happy People</i> (2002–03)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogwai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Happy Songs For Happy People (2002–03)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mogwai's 2003 album <i><a href="/wiki/Happy_Songs_for_Happy_People" title="Happy Songs for Happy People">Happy Songs for Happy People</a></i> continued the band's movement into the use of electronica and more spacious arrangements. It was the band's first album to sell in any numbers in the US, reaching No.13 on the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Albums" title="Independent Albums">Billboard Independent Albums Chart</a> and even spending one week in the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Reviews were generally favourable, although as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a> said in 2008<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PHIH_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-PHIH-8">[8]</a></sup> "...(the album's) reception ranged from middling to favorable. Some praised the band's scope, grandeur, and willingness to explore beyond the bounds of the quiet-loud-louder dynamic it had mastered; others lamented a lack of the same, alternately calling Happy Songs too soft, too small, or too stiff."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mr_Beast_.282004.E2.80.9306.29"><i>Mr Beast</i> (2004–06)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogwai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mr Beast (2004–06)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In March 2006, the album <i><a href="/wiki/Mr_Beast" title="Mr Beast">Mr Beast</a></i> was released in a regular format and in a limited deluxe edition package that came with both the album on CD and a DVD documenting the recording process entitled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Recording_of_Mr_Beast" title="The Recording of Mr Beast">The Recording of Mr Beast</a></i>. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Hawk_Is_Howling_.282007.E2.80.9308.29"><i>The Hawk Is Howling</i> (2007–08)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogwai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Hawk Is Howling (2007–08)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The band's sixth studio album was recorded from late 2007 until early 2008, and was released in September 2008. It was the first Mogwai album not to feature vocals, and was also the first to be self-produced by the band; the album was recorded by <a href="/wiki/Andy_Miller_(record_producer)" title="Andy Miller (record producer)">Andy Miller</a> at Chem19 Studios in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hamilton,_Scotland" title="Hamilton, Scotland">Hamilton, South Lanarkshire</a>, and mixed by Gareth Jones at <a href="/wiki/Castle_of_Doom_Studios" title="Castle of Doom Studios">Castle of Doom Studios</a> in Glasgow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The album spawned an EP, <i><a href="/wiki/Batcat" title="Batcat">Batcat</a></i>, featuring the title track from the album and also a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Roky_Erickson" title="Roky Erickson">Roky Erickson</a>, with Erickson providing vocals on "Devil Rides".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Burning_.2F_Special_Moves_.282009.E2.80.9310.29"><i>Burning</i> / <i>Special Moves</i> (2009–10)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogwai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Burning / Special Moves (2009–10)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010 the band released their first live film (<i>Burning</i>, filmed by <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Moon" title="Vincent Moon">Vincent Moon</a> and Nathanaël Le Scouarnec, which premiered at the <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Film_Festival" title="Glasgow Film Festival">Glasgow Film Festival</a> in February) and live album (called <i>Special Moves</i>). <i>Burning</i> contains eight tracks from the band's Brooklyn shows during their 2008/2009 American tour, whilst <i>Special Moves</i> adds nine more tracks from the same source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galloway_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Galloway-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NME20100121_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-NME20100121-13">[13]</a></sup> <i>Special Moves</i> was the first release on Mogwai's own Rock Action records, named after <a href="/wiki/The_Stooges" title="The Stooges">Stooges</a> drummer <a href="/wiki/Scott_Asheton" title="Scott Asheton">Scott Asheton</a>, who had his name changed to Rock Action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardcore_Will_Never_Die.2C_But_You_Will_and_A_Wrenched_Virile_Lore_.282011.E2.80.9313.29"><i>Hardcore Will Never Die, But You Will</i> and <i>A Wrenched Virile Lore</i> (2011–13)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogwai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hardcore Will Never Die, But You Will and A Wrenched Virile Lore (2011–13)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 2010, Mogwai left longtime North American distributor Matador Records, and signed with <a href="/wiki/Sub_Pop" title="Sub Pop">Sub Pop</a>. Braithwaite also stated that the band were working on material for a new album for release in early 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NME20100121_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-NME20100121-13">[13]</a></sup> On 27 October 2010, Mogwai announced their seventh studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Hardcore_Will_Never_Die,_But_You_Will" title="Hardcore Will Never Die, But You Will">Hardcore Will Never Die, But You Will</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hardcore_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-hardcore-14">[14]</a></sup> The album was released on 14 February 2011 in the UK and entered the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a> at number 25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> A bonus edition featured an additional CD featuring a 23-minute piece called "Music for a Forgotten Future (The Singing Mountain)", which was recorded for an art installation by <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Gordon" title="Douglas Gordon">Douglas Gordon</a> and Olaf Nicolai.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Three singles were released from the album; "Rano Pano", "Mexican Grand Prix" and "San Pedro".</p>
<p>In 2012 a remix album, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Wrenched_Virile_Lore" title="A Wrenched Virile Lore">A Wrenched Virile Lore</a></i> was released which included tracks from <i>Hardcore...</i> remixed by numerous artists including <a href="/wiki/Xander_Harris" title="Xander Harris">Xander Harris</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Soft_Moon" title="The Soft Moon">The Soft Moon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hampson" title="Robert Hampson">Robert Hampson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Justin_Broadrick" title="Justin Broadrick">Justin Broadrick</a>. The album, whose title is almost an anagram of "Hardcore Will Never Die" was again released by Sub Pop in the US, and Rock Action Records elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rave_Tapes_.282013.E2.80.932015.29"><i>Rave Tapes</i> (2013–2015)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogwai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rave Tapes (2013–2015)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mogwai-_Zidane,_A_21st_Century_Portrait_@_Albert_Hall,_Manchester_19-7-2013_(9323563699).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3008" data-file-width="4012" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Mogwai-_Zidane%2C_A_21st_Century_Portrait_%40_Albert_Hall%2C_Manchester_19-7-2013_%289323563699%29.jpg/220px-Mogwai-_Zidane%2C_A_21st_Century_Portrait_%40_Albert_Hall%2C_Manchester_19-7-2013_%289323563699%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Mogwai-_Zidane%2C_A_21st_Century_Portrait_%40_Albert_Hall%2C_Manchester_19-7-2013_%289323563699%29.jpg/330px-Mogwai-_Zidane%2C_A_21st_Century_Portrait_%40_Albert_Hall%2C_Manchester_19-7-2013_%289323563699%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Mogwai-_Zidane%2C_A_21st_Century_Portrait_%40_Albert_Hall%2C_Manchester_19-7-2013_%289323563699%29.jpg/440px-Mogwai-_Zidane%2C_A_21st_Century_Portrait_%40_Albert_Hall%2C_Manchester_19-7-2013_%289323563699%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In July 2013, Mogwai performed their <a href="/wiki/Zidane:_A_21st_Century_Portrait_(soundtrack)" title="Zidane: A 21st Century Portrait (soundtrack)">soundtrack</a> to <i><a href="/wiki/Zidane:_A_21st_Century_Portrait" title="Zidane: A 21st Century Portrait">Zidane: A 21st Century Portrait</a></i> for the first time at dates across the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> An announcement of new live dates followed, including two nights at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Festival_Hall" title="Royal Festival Hall">Royal Festival Hall</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and an appearance closing the final holiday camp edition of the <a href="/wiki/All_Tomorrow%27s_Parties_(music_festival)" title="All Tomorrow's Parties (music festival)">"All Tomorrow's Parties" festival</a> in Camber Sands, England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>They announced their eighth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album">studio album</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Rave_Tapes" title="Rave Tapes">Rave Tapes</a></i> on 28 October 2013. The album was released on 20 January 2014 on Rock Action in the UK, Spunk in Australia and Hostess in Japan and South-east Asia, while Sub Pop released the album in the US on 21 January. <i>Rave Tapes</i> was produced by Mogwai and Paul Savage, and the song "Remurdered" was uploaded to the Rock Action and Sub Pop <a href="/wiki/SoundCloud" title="SoundCloud">SoundCloud</a> pages at the time of the announcement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The album entered the UK album charts at No.10<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and as of April 2014 was the best selling UK album released in 2014 in terms of vinyl sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>An EP titled <i><a href="/wiki/Music_Industry_3._Fitness_Industry_1." title="Music Industry 3. Fitness Industry 1.">Music Industry 3. Fitness Industry 1.</a></i> on <a href="/wiki/Rock_Action_Records" title="Rock Action Records">Rock Action</a> was released on 1 December 2014 featuring three new tracks from the <i>Rave Tapes</i> sessions, along with three remixes of tracks from that album by <a href="/wiki/Blanck_Mass" title="Blanck Mass">Blanck Mass</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ghost_Box_Music" title="Ghost Box Music">Pye Corner Audio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nils_Frahm" title="Nils Frahm">Nils Frahm</a>. The EP was recorded in Glasgow with <a href="/wiki/Paul_Savage" title="Paul Savage">Paul Savage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2015 Mogwai played a series of high-profile shows in the UK and Ireland, climaxing with two nights at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camden_Roundhouse" title="Camden Roundhouse">Camden Roundhouse</a>, to celebrate the band's 20th anniversary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> At the same time, in association with <a href="/wiki/All_Tomorrow%27s_Parties_(music_festival)" title="All Tomorrow's Parties (music festival)">ATP</a>, they curated a series of shows at the London venue featuring acts that have "challenged, intrigued and inspired" them, including <a href="/wiki/The_Jesus_and_Mary_Chain" title="The Jesus and Mary Chain">The Jesus and Mary Chain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(band)" title="Public Enemy (band)">Public Enemy</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Godspeed!_You_Black_Emperor" title="Godspeed! You Black Emperor">Godspeed! You Black Emperor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> A career retrospective album comprising three CDs or six LPs, <i><a href="/wiki/Central_Belters" title="Central Belters">Central Belters</a></i>, was released on 23 October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 15 November 2015, the band announced that guitarist John Cummings had left to pursue his own projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="9th_studio_album_.282016.E2.80.93present.29">9th studio album (2016–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogwai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 9th studio album (2016–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 2016, Braithwaite told the <i>Guardian</i> that the band were writing new songs and would be travelling to the US later in the year to record a new album with <a href="/wiki/Dave_Fridmann" title="Dave Fridmann">Dave Fridmann</a>, who produced <i>Rock Action</i> some 15 years previously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> On 25 November, Fridmann announced that the band had started recording the album with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Soundtracks_and_other_work">Soundtracks and other work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogwai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Soundtracks and other work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2006, the band provided the soundtrack to the film <i>Zidane: A 21st Century Portrait</i>, with the soundtrack album released the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacDonald_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald-34">[34]</a></sup> The band's song "Auto Rock" was used in Michael Mann's 2006 film <i><a href="/wiki/Miami_Vice_(film)" title="Miami Vice (film)">Miami Vice</a></i>. The band also collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Clint_Mansell" title="Clint Mansell">Clint Mansell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kronos_Quartet" title="Kronos Quartet">Kronos Quartet</a> on the soundtrack to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fountain" title="The Fountain">The Fountain</a></i> in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ankeny_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ankeny-35">[35]</a></sup> Mogwai are also featured in the 2009 post-rock documentary <i>Introspective</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hubert_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hubert-36">[36]</a></sup> The band donated an exclusive track to the <i>PEACE</i> project in April 2010 in support of <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Young-37">[37]</a></sup> In 2012, the band provided the soundtrack for the <a href="/wiki/Canal%2B" title="Canal+">Canal+</a> French TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Revenants_(TV_series)" title="Les Revenants (TV series)">Les Revenants</a></i> (broadcast as <i>The Returned</i> in the UK). The album, <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Revenants_(album)" title="Les Revenants (album)">Les Revenants</a></i>, was released on 25 February 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The track "Kids Will Be Skeletons" was featured as part of the soundtrack of the story based video game <i><a href="/wiki/Life_Is_Strange" title="Life Is Strange">Life Is Strange</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-score_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-score-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015 the band supplied the music for <a href="/wiki/Mark_Cousins_(film_critic)" title="Mark Cousins (film critic)">Mark Cousins</a>' documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Atomic,_Living_in_Dread_and_Promise" title="Atomic, Living in Dread and Promise">Atomic, Living in Dread and Promise</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The soundtrack was reworked and released as <i><a href="/wiki/Atomic_(Mogwai_album)" title="Atomic (Mogwai album)">Atomic</a></i> on 1 April 2016,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> through Rock Action Records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The band carried out an extensive live tour of Europe and Japan performing the soundtrack against a backdrop of the screening of the film, beginning in Austria on 1 May 2016. They then announced a North American tour of the album for January 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_style">Musical style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mogwai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Musical style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The band's influences include <a href="/wiki/Fugazi" title="Fugazi">Fugazi</a>, <a href="/wiki/MC5" title="MC5">MC5</a>, <a href="/wiki/My_Bloody_Valentine_(band)" title="My Bloody Valentine (band)">My Bloody Valentine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sonic_Youth" title="Sonic Youth">Sonic Youth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pixies" title="Pixies">Pixies</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Cure" title="The Cure">The Cure</a> and post-rock pioneers <a href="/wiki/Slint" title="Slint">Slint</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buckley_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buckley-45">[45]</a></sup> Mogwai's style has easily identifiable connections to genres like <a href="/wiki/Shoegazing" title="Shoegazing">shoegazing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Math_rock" title="Math rock">math rock</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Art_rock" title="Art rock">art rock</a>. Début album <i>Mogwai Young Team</i> was described as "stunningly dynamic...[shifting] seamlessly from tranquil, bleakly beautiful soundscapes to brain scrambling white noise and sledgehammer riffing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strong_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-Strong-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Wolk" title="Douglas Wolk">Douglas Wolk</a>, writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">SPIN</a></i> in 1999 said of the band: "Their compositions have gotten increasingly drawn-out and austere over time, sometimes barely more than a single <a href="/wiki/Arpeggio" title="Arpeggio">arpeggiated chord</a> or two evolving for ten minutes or more, whisperingly brutal in a way that recalls Slint more than any other band".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolk_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wolk-5">[5]</a></sup> Barry Burns once stated in an interview that he and the rest of the band do not like the categorisation of post-rock because he believes it over-analyses everything.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Braithwaite has commented on the absence of lyrics in most of Mogwai's music, saying:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I think most people are not used to having no lyrics to focus on. Lyrics are a real comfort to some people. I guess they like to sing along and when they can't do that with us they can get a bit upset.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Stuart Braithwaite, <i>The Express</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
