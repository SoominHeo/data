<p><b>Monaghan</b> (pronounced <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/Àà/ primary stress follows">Àà</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ å/ short 'u' in 'bud'"> å</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/…ô/ 'a' in 'about'">…ô</span><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/…ô/ 'a' in 'about'">…ô</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">MUN</span></span></b>-…ô-h…ôn</i></a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i>Muineach√°n</i></span>) is the <a href="/wiki/County_town" title="County town">county town</a> of <a href="/wiki/County_Monaghan" title="County Monaghan">County Monaghan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>. It also provides the name of its civil parish and <a href="/wiki/Monaghan_(barony)" title="Monaghan (barony)">barony</a>.</p>
<p>The population of the Town at the 2011 census was 8,012 including suburbs and environs. The town is on the <a href="/wiki/N2_road_(Ireland)" title="N2 road (Ireland)">N2 road</a> from <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> to <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">Derry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Letterkenny" title="Letterkenny">Letterkenny</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monaghan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Irish name <i>Muineach√°n</i> derives from a diminutive plural form of the Irish word <i>muine</i> meaning "brake" (a thickly overgrown area) or sometimes "hillock". <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Weston_Joyce" title="Patrick Weston Joyce">Patrick Weston Joyce</a> interpreted this as "a place full of little hills or brakes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Monaghan_County_Council" title="Monaghan County Council">Monaghan County Council</a>'s preferred interpretation is "land of the little hills", a reference to the numerous <a href="/wiki/Drumlin" title="Drumlin">drumlins</a> in the area.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monaghan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_History">Early History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monaghan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Clontibret" title="Battle of Clontibret">Battle of Clontibret</a> between the forces of Earl <a href="/wiki/Hugh_O%27Neill,_Earl_of_Tyrone" title="Hugh O'Neill, Earl of Tyrone">Hugh √ì N√©ill</a> of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Earldom_of_T%C3%ADr_Eoghain&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Earldom of T√≠r Eoghain (page does not exist)">T√≠r Eoghain</a>, The √ì N√©ill, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Crown" title="English Crown">English Crown</a> was fought in northern Monaghan in May 1595. The territory of Monaghan had earlier been wrested from the control of the <a href="/wiki/McMahon" title="McMahon">MacMahon</a> sept in 1591, when the leader of the MacMahons was hanged by authority of the Dublin government; this was one of the events that led to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nine_Years_War_(Ireland)" title="Nine Years War (Ireland)">Nine Years War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tudor_conquest_of_Ireland" title="Tudor conquest of Ireland">Tudor conquest of Ireland</a>.</p>
<p>On the Hill of Lech, the <i>Hill of the Stone</i> was the inauguration stone of the Mac Mahons. It overlooks Ballagh Lough to the west, which was once known as Lough Leck. Situated 5 kilometres (3 miles) south-west of Monaghan, the <a href="/wiki/Petrosomatoglyph" title="Petrosomatoglyph">petrosomatoglyph</a> was last used in 1595, but was destroyed by a farm owner in 1809. It is said to be built into the wall of a mill.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1801, Monaghan Town, along with the rest of the Rossmore Estate, became the property of the Westenra family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mulligan_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mulligan-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:460</sup> The Rossmore Estate was inherited in August of that year by <a href="/wiki/Warner_Westenra,_2nd_Baron_Rossmore" title="Warner Westenra, 2nd Baron Rossmore">Warner Westenra, 2nd Baron Rossmore</a> from his uncle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mulligan_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mulligan-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:460</sup> The Westenra family remained as the principal landlords of Monaghan Town up into the early twentieth-century. Their '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancestral_seat" title="Ancestral seat">ancestral seat</a>' was established at <a href="/wiki/Rossmore_Castle" title="Rossmore Castle">Rossmore Castle</a> (also known as Rossmore Park), a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Country_house" title="Country house">country house</a> mainly built in stages during the nineteenth-century on the south-western edge of Monaghan Town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mulligan_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mulligan-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:483</sup> The castle was mainly built in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-Jacobean_architecture" title="Neo-Jacobean architecture">neo-Jacobean</a> style of architecture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mulligan_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Mulligan-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monaghan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Canal" title="Ulster Canal">Ulster Canal</a> through Monaghan linking the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_Blackwater,_Northern_Ireland" title="River Blackwater, Northern Ireland">River Blackwater</a> at <a href="/wiki/Moy,_County_Tyrone" title="Moy, County Tyrone">Moy</a> with the <a href="/wiki/River_Erne" title="River Erne">River Erne</a> near <a href="/wiki/Clones,_County_Monaghan" title="Clones, County Monaghan">Clones</a> was built between 1825 and 1842. By the time it was completed, competition in the form of the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Railway" title="Ulster Railway">Ulster Railway</a> from Belfast to Clones was already under construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hajducki_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hajducki-4">[4]</a></sup> The canal was never a commercial success and was formally abandoned in 1931.</p>
<p>The Ulster Railway linked Monaghan with <a href="/wiki/Armagh_railway_station" title="Armagh railway station">Armagh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belfast_Great_Victoria_Street_railway_station" title="Belfast Great Victoria Street railway station">Belfast</a> in 1858 and with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dundalk_and_Enniskillen_Railway" title="Dundalk and Enniskillen Railway">Dundalk and Enniskillen Railway</a> at Clones in 1863.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hajducki_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hajducki-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:Map 8</sup> It became part of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Northern_Railway_(Ireland)" title="Great Northern Railway (Ireland)">Great Northern Railway</a> in 1876.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hajducki_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hajducki-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:xiii</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Ireland" title="Partition of Ireland">partition of Ireland</a> in 1922 turned the boundary with <a href="/wiki/County_Armagh" title="County Armagh">County Armagh</a> into an international frontier, after which trains were routinely delayed by customs inspections. In 1957 the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Government of Northern Ireland">Government of Northern Ireland</a> made the GNR Board close the line between <a href="/wiki/Portadown_railway_station" title="Portadown railway station">Portadown</a> and the border, giving the GNRB no option but to withdraw passenger services between the border and Clones as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hajducki_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hajducki-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:Map 39</sup> <a href="/wiki/CI%C3%89" title="CI√â">CI√â</a> took over the remaining section of line between Clones, Monaghan and <a href="/wiki/Glaslough" title="Glaslough">Glaslough</a> in 1958 but withdrew goods services between Monaghan and Glaslough in 1959 and between Clones and Monaghan in 1960, leaving Monaghan with no railway service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hajducki_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hajducki-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:Map 39</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Twentieth_Century">Twentieth Century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monaghan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Twentieth Century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 1919 the first self-consciously proclaimed <a href="/wiki/Soviet_(council)" title="Soviet (council)">soviet</a> in the United Kingdom was established at Monaghan Lunatic Asylum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This led to the claim by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Devlin" title="Joseph Devlin">Joseph Devlin</a> in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons of the United Kingdom</a> that "the only successfully conducted institutions in Ireland are the lunatic asylums"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 17 May 1974 an <a href="/wiki/Ulster_loyalism" title="Ulster loyalism">Ulster loyalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Car_bomb" title="Car bomb">car bomb</a> exploded in the Friday evening rush hour, killing seven people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It was detonated outside Greacen's public house on North Road in a car that had been stolen earlier that afternoon in Portadown. The bomb killed Paddy Askin, Thomas Campbell, Thomas Croarkin, Archie Harper, Jack Travers, Peggy White and George Williamson. It also injured scores of civilians and caused extensive damage to the fabric of the town with North Road and Mill Street among the areas worst affected. This was one of the few incidents in the Republic during <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">The Troubles</a> of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>; three other bombs exploded on the same day in Dublin in what became known as the <a href="/wiki/Dublin_and_Monaghan_bombings" title="Dublin and Monaghan bombings">Dublin and Monaghan bombings</a>. The Ulster loyalist paramilitary group <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulster_Volunteer_Force_(1966)" title="Ulster Volunteer Force (1966)">Ulster Volunteer Force</a> (UVF) claimed responsibility in 1993.</p>
<p>A monument in memory of the victims was unveiled by the eighth <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a> <a href="/wiki/Mary_McAleese" title="Mary McAleese">Mary McAleese</a> on 17 May 2004, on the occasion of the 30th anniversary of the atrocity. The sandstone and metal column containing seven light wells bearing the names of each of the seven victims of the bombing was designed by Ciaran O'Cearnaigh and stands as a reminder of one of the darkest days in Ireland's modern history.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Town_layout_and_architecture">Town layout and architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monaghan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Town layout and architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The centre of the town is made up of four interconnecting squares: Market Square (or Street), Church Square, The Diamond and Old Cross Square.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:St_Patrick%27s_Monaghan_-_geograph.org.uk_-_167638.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="480" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/St_Patrick%27s_Monaghan_-_geograph.org.uk_-_167638.jpg/220px-St_Patrick%27s_Monaghan_-_geograph.org.uk_-_167638.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/St_Patrick%27s_Monaghan_-_geograph.org.uk_-_167638.jpg/330px-St_Patrick%27s_Monaghan_-_geograph.org.uk_-_167638.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/St_Patrick%27s_Monaghan_-_geograph.org.uk_-_167638.jpg/440px-St_Patrick%27s_Monaghan_-_geograph.org.uk_-_167638.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:St_Patrick%27s_Monaghan_-_geograph.org.uk_-_167638.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
St. Patrick's <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Ireland" title="Church of Ireland">Church of Ireland</a></div>

<p>Dating from the seventeenth century, the oldest remaining architectural feature in Monaghan town is the "Old Cross" - located in Old Cross Square. It is not fully agreed that it is in fact a cross, but may in fact have been a seventeenth-century <a href="/wiki/Sundial" title="Sundial">sundial</a>. It was originally located in the Diamond, the traditional centre of the town, and was used as a hiring cross and for the attaching of proclamations. It was moved to its present location in 1876 to allow for the construction of the Rossmore Memorial. Two landmark buildings remain from the eighteenth century, Aviemore House (built in 1760) on Mill Street and the "extremely elegant" <a href="/wiki/Market_House,_Monaghan" title="Market House, Monaghan">Market House</a> (from 1792) on Market Square.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brett_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brett-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:16</sup></p>
<p>Monaghan is notable for the quality of its nineteenth-century architecture, which adds a sense of dignity to the attractive town centre and its environs. Of its Victorian buildings, the courthouse on Church Square by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Welland_(architect)" title="Joseph Welland (architect)">Joseph Welland</a>, which was built in 1830, is the most stately. With its sandstone facade of Doric columns supporting a pediment that bears the royal arms of the House of Hanover, Monaghan Courthouse constitutes an integral part of Church Square.</p>
<p>The Rossmore Memorial in The Diamond was built in 1876 as a memorial to <a href="/wiki/Henry_Westenra,_4th_Baron_Rossmore" title="Henry Westenra, 4th Baron Rossmore">The 4th Baron Rossmore</a>, who died after a hunting accident at <a href="/wiki/Windsor_Castle" title="Windsor Castle">Windsor Castle</a> in 1874. This <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> monument, described by architectural historian C.E.B. Brett as "formidable and striking"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brett_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brett-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:10</sup> is octagonal in shape, with central marble columns supporting a fountain. Around it, the eight grey columns support the pinnacled superstructure which rises to a dome. The dome is surmounted by a spire supported by yet more columns. The letters of Rossmore (also 8 in number) are spaced out around the monument.</p>
<p>The Gothic-Revival <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Macartan%27s_cathedral,_Monaghan" title="St Macartan's cathedral, Monaghan">St Macartan's Cathedral</a> by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J.J._McCarthy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="J.J. McCarthy (page does not exist)">J.J. McCarthy</a> is recognised as being "one of McCarthy's best works: an excellent example of the High Victorian ecclesiastical style at its best, rich without ever being over-ornate".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brett_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brett-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:26</sup> The building comprises a delicate rose window and an impressive soaring spire and took over thirty years to complete. Construction work began in 1861 and the cathedral was finally dedicated in 1892. Originally the nave was intended to be two bays longer but lack of funds meant that the design was cut back. The Cathedral sits on imposing site overlooking the town. Occupying a similarly commanding site on the opposite side of the town is <a href="/wiki/St_Macartan%27s_College" title="St Macartan's College">St Macartan's College</a> for boys (from 1840), a 17-bay classical structure with a bell tower and private chapel, by the Newry-born architect <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Duff" title="Thomas Duff">Thomas Duff</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Church_Square,_Monaghan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Church_Square%2C_Monaghan.jpg/220px-Church_Square%2C_Monaghan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Church_Square%2C_Monaghan.jpg/330px-Church_Square%2C_Monaghan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Church_Square%2C_Monaghan.jpg/440px-Church_Square%2C_Monaghan.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Church_Square,_Monaghan.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Church Square, Monaghan</div>

<p>Church Square is very much an environment in which the civic pride of Victorian improvers lives on in the satisfying essay in the Ruskinian-Gothic style that is the Bank of Ireland building, as much as in the peaks of St. Patrick's <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Ireland" title="Church of Ireland">Church of Ireland</a> and the Dawson Obelisk. One of the most interesting aspects of Monaghan's Victorian architectural heritage, which also includes the former railway station, the Orange Hall on North Road and the Westenra Hotel on the Diamond, is the rounded corners that connect the town's buildings from one street or square to the next. This practice of rounding corners in order to open up panoramic vistas was carried out with unprecedented frequency in the town of Monaghan, and is still reflected today in the edifices of The Diamond, Church Square and Mill Street, helping to secure Monaghan's status as one of Ulster's more attractive large towns.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monaghan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town is a centre for the timber-frame house building industry with <a href="/wiki/Kingspan_Group" title="Kingspan Group">Kingspan</a> Century being the largest of its kind in Europe. It is also the centre of a thriving agri business most notable of which is the mushroom industry. Engineering also features in the region with both Moffett<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Combilift&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Combilift (page does not exist)">Combilift</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> major participants in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Materials_handling" title="Materials handling">materials handling</a> market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is a campaign to boost tourism by reopening the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Canal" title="Ulster Canal">Ulster Canal</a> in a scheme which would eventually allow boats to travel from towns in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Newry" title="Newry">Newry</a>, by way of Monaghan to places as far south as <a href="/wiki/Limerick" title="Limerick">Limerick</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>.</p>
<p>Monaghan once had a thriving furniture manufacturing industry. Since 1990, this has diminished greatly under global competition. However, manufacturers such as Rossmore Furniture (which took its name from <a href="/wiki/Rossmore_Forest_Park" title="Rossmore Forest Park">Rossmore Forest Park</a>, situated just outside the town) continue to operate from the town.</p>
