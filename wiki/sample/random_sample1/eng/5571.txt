<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Mono no aware</i></h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><i><b>Mono no aware</b></i> <span style="font-weight:normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">もののあわれ</span><span class="t_nihongo_help"><sup><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span style="color:#00e; font:bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration:none; padding:0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup></span>)</span>, literally "the pathos of things", and also translated as "an empathy toward things", or "a sensitivity to ephemera", is a <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> term for the awareness of <a href="/wiki/Impermanence" title="Impermanence">impermanence</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">無常</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>mujō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, or transience of things, and both a transient gentle sadness (or wistfulness) at their passing as well as a longer, deeper gentle sadness about this state being the reality of life.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mono_no_aware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term was coined in the 18th century by the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a> Japanese cultural scholar <a href="/wiki/Motoori_Norinaga" title="Motoori Norinaga">Motoori Norinaga</a> and was originally a concept used in his literary criticism of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_Genji" title="The Tale of Genji">The Tale of Genji</a>,</i> later applied to other seminal Japanese works including the <i><a href="/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB" title="Man'yōshū">Man'yōshū</a></i>. It became central to his philosophy of literature and eventually to <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Japan" title="Culture of Japan">Japanese cultural tradition</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mono_no_aware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The phrase is derived from the Japanese word <i>mono</i> <span style="font-weight:normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">物</span><span class="t_nihongo_help"><sup><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span style="color:#00e; font:bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration:none; padding:0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup></span>)</span>, which means "thing", and <i>aware</i> <span style="font-weight:normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">哀れ</span><span class="t_nihongo_help"><sup><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span style="color:#00e; font:bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration:none; padding:0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup></span>)</span>, which was a <a href="/wiki/Heian_period" title="Heian period">Heian period</a> expression of measured surprise (similar to "ah" or "oh"), translating roughly as "pathos", "poignancy", "deep feeling", "sensitivity", or "awareness". Thus, <i>mono no aware</i> has frequently been translated as "the 'ahh-ness' of things", life, and love. Awareness of the transience of all things heightens appreciation of their beauty, and evokes a gentle sadness at their passing. In his criticism of <i>The Tale of Genji</i> Motoori noted that <i>mono no aware</i> is the crucial emotion that moves readers. Its scope was not limited to <a href="/wiki/Japanese_literature" title="Japanese literature">Japanese literature</a>, and became associated with Japanese cultural tradition (see also <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sakura" title="Sakura">sakura</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-khoon_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-khoon-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_contemporary_culture">In contemporary culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mono_no_aware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In contemporary culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Notable <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a> artists who use <i>mono no aware</i>–style storytelling include <a href="/wiki/Hitoshi_Ashinano" title="Hitoshi Ashinano">Hitoshi Ashinano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kozue_Amano" title="Kozue Amano">Kozue Amano</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kaoru_Mori" title="Kaoru Mori">Kaoru Mori</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a>, both <i><a href="/wiki/Only_Yesterday_(1991_film)" title="Only Yesterday (1991 film)">Only Yesterday</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Isao_Takahata" title="Isao Takahata">Isao Takahata</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mai_Mai_Miracle" title="Mai Mai Miracle">Mai Mai Miracle</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Sunao_Katabuchi" title="Sunao Katabuchi">Sunao Katabuchi</a> emphasize the passing of time in gentle notes and by presenting the main plot against a parallel one from the past. In addition, the Japanese director <a href="/wiki/Yasujir%C5%8D_Ozu" title="Yasujirō Ozu">Yasujirō Ozu</a> was well known for creating a sense of <i>mono no aware</i>, frequently climaxing with a character very understatedly saying <i>"Ii tenki desu ne?"</i> <span style="font-weight:normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">いい天気ですね</span><span class="t_nihongo_help"><sup><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span style="color:#00e; font:bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration:none; padding:0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup></span>, "Fine weather, isn't it?")</span>, after a familial and societal paradigm shift, such as a daughter being married off, against the backdrop of a swiftly changing Japan.</p>
<p>In his book about courtly life in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Japan" title="Ancient Japan">ancient Japan</a>, <i>The World of the Shining Prince</i>, <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Morris" title="Ivan Morris">Ivan Morris</a> compares <i>mono no aware</i> to <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>'s term <i><a href="/wiki/Lacrimae_rerum" title="Lacrimae rerum">lacrimae rerum</a></i>, Latin for "tears of things".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">Science fiction</a> author <a href="/wiki/Ken_Liu" title="Ken Liu">Ken Liu</a>'s short story, "Mono no Aware", won the 2013 <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award_for_Best_Short_Story" title="Hugo Award for Best Short Story">Hugo Award for Best Short Story</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Inspired by works like the science fiction manga <i><a href="/wiki/Yokohama_Kaidashi_Kik%C5%8D" title="Yokohama Kaidashi Kikō">Yokohama Kaidashi Kikō</a></i>, Liu sought to evoke an "aesthetic primarily oriented towards creating in the reader an empathy towards the inevitable passing of all things", and to acknowledge "the importance of memory and continuity with the past".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>British novelist <a href="/wiki/Kazuo_Ishiguro" title="Kazuo Ishiguro">Kazuo Ishiguro</a> (author of 1989's <a href="/wiki/The_Remains_of_the_Day" title="The Remains of the Day">The Remains of the Day</a>, which was awarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Man_Booker_Prize" title="Man Booker Prize">Booker Prize for Fiction</a>) ends many of his novels without any sense of resolution. The issues his characters confront are buried in the past and remain unresolved. Thus Ishiguro ends many of his novels on a note of melancholic resignation. His characters accept their past and who they have become, typically discovering that this realization brings comfort and an ending to mental anguish. This can be seen as a literary reflection of the Japanese idea of <i>mono no aware</i>.</p>
