<p>The <b>Monochrome Display Adapter</b> (<b>MDA</b>, also <b>MDA card</b>, <b>Monochrome Display and Printer Adapter</b>, <b>MDPA</b>) is <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>'s standard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_display_card" title="Video display card">video display card</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_display_standard" title="Computer display standard">computer display standard</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_PC" title="IBM PC">PC</a> introduced in 1981. The MDA did not have any pixel-addressable graphics modes. It had only a single <a href="/wiki/Monochrome" title="Monochrome">monochrome</a> <a href="/wiki/Text_mode" title="Text mode">text mode</a> (PC video mode 7), which could display 80 columns by 25 lines of high resolution text characters or symbols useful for drawing forms.</p>
<p>Based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_Datamaster" title="IBM Datamaster">IBM Datamaster</a>'s display system,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bradley199009_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bradley199009-1">[1]</a></sup> the standard IBM MDA card was equipped with four <a href="/wiki/Kilobyte" title="Kilobyte">kilobytes</a> (kB) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_memory" title="Video memory">video memory</a>. The MDA's high character resolution (sharpness) was a feature meant to facilitate business and wordprocessing use: Each character was rendered in a box of 9×14 <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">pixels</a>, of which 7×11 made out the character itself (the other pixels being used for space between character columns and lines). Some characters, such as the lowercase "m", were rendered eight pixels across.</p>
<p>The MDA featured the following character display attributes: <i>invisible, underline, normal, bright (bold), reverse video,</i> and <i>blinking</i>; some of these attributes could be combined, so that e.g., <u><b>bright, underlined</b></u> text could be produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliott_2005_MDA_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott_2005_MDA-2">[2]</a></sup> The theoretical total screen <a href="/wiki/Display_resolution" title="Display resolution">display resolution</a> of the MDA was 720×350 pixels. This number is arrived at through calculating character width (nine pixels) by columns of text (80) and character height (14 pixels) by rows of text (25). However, the MDA again could not address individual pixels; it could only work in text mode, limiting its choice of display patterns to 256 characters. The character patterns were stored in <a href="/wiki/Read-only_memory" title="Read-only memory">ROM</a> on the card and the character set could not be changed from the <a href="/wiki/Hardware_code_page" title="Hardware code page">hardware code page</a> <a href="/wiki/Code_page_437" title="Code page 437">437</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliott_2005_MDA_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott_2005_MDA-2">[2]</a></sup> The only way to simulate "graphical" screen content was through <a href="/wiki/ASCII_art" title="ASCII art">ASCII art</a>.</p>
<p>Because of the lack of pixel-addressable graphics, MDA owners could not play most graphics-based games. At least one game, IBM's <i>One Hundred And One Monochrome Mazes</i> ("A<i>mazing</i> fun for the whole family"), required MDA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manes19840124_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-manes19840124-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Code_page_437" title="Code page 437">Code page 437</a> included the standard 127 <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a> characters but also another 127 characters like the aforementioned characters for drawing forms. Some of these shapes would later show up in Unicode as <a href="/wiki/Box-drawing_character" title="Box-drawing character">box-drawing characters</a>. The characters were also used in early PC games such as early <a href="/wiki/BBS_door" title="BBS door">BBS door</a> games, or games like <a href="/wiki/Castle_Adventure" title="Castle Adventure">Castle Adventure</a> by Kevin Bales.</p>
<p>IBM's original MDA included a <a href="/wiki/Parallel_port" title="Parallel port">parallel printer port</a> (hence its original name of "Monochrome Display and Printer Adapter"), thus avoiding the need for a separate parallel interface on computers fitted with an MDA.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_boards">Early boards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IBM_Monochrome_Display_Adapter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early boards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early versions of the MDA board had hardware capable of outputting red, green and blue TTL signals on the normally unconnected video connector pins, theoretically allowing an 8-color display with a suitable monitor. The registers also allow the monochrome mode to be set on and off. Yet there was no software to actually control that feature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilton1987_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-wilton1987-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mzdg1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-mzdg1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliott_2005_MDA_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott_2005_MDA-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competing_adapters">Competing adapters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IBM_Monochrome_Display_Adapter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Competing adapters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There were two commonly available competing display adapters:</p>
<ul>
<li>For PC users requiring <a href="/wiki/Bitmap" title="Bitmap">bitmapped</a> graphics and/or color, IBM offered its <i><a href="/wiki/Color_Graphics_Adapter" title="Color Graphics Adapter">Color Graphics Adapter</a></i> (<i>CGA</i>, also <i>CGA card</i>), launched at the same time as MDA. CGA was much less popular than MDA at first;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-byte198311_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-byte198311-9">[9]</a></sup> the lower resolution of its text mode characters (as compared to MDA) and absence of a printer port, which was included on the original MDA card, made CGA cards less attractive for business use. <i><a href="/wiki/PC_Magazine" title="PC Magazine">PC Magazine</a></i> reported in June 1983 that while "the IBM monochrome display is absolutely beautiful for text and wonderfully easy on the eyes, but is limited to simple character graphics. Text quality on displays connected to the color/graphics adapter ... is at best of medium quality and is conducive to eyestrain over the long haul".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fastie198306_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-fastie198306-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Introduced in 1982, the non-IBM <i><a href="/wiki/Hercules_Graphics_Card" title="Hercules Graphics Card">Hercules Graphics Card</a></i> (also <i>HGC</i>) offered both an MDA compatible high resolution text mode and a monochrome graphics mode. It <i>could</i> address individual pixels and display a black and white picture of 720×348 pixels. This resolution was better than even the highest monochrome resolution CGA cards could offer. Thus, even without a color capability of any kind, the Hercules adapter's offer of monochrome graphics without sacrificing MDA-equivalent text quality made it a more desirable choice for many.</li>
<li>Adapters exist (2014) to convert MDA into VGA for upgrading to LCD. Using a GBS-8219 with the video signal (to G) and the horizontal sync (to H/CS) and vertical sync (to V); set to a RGB(D), and Sync Separate(HV).</li>
</ul>
