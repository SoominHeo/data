<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Monokini</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

This iconic image of <a href="/wiki/Peggy_Moffitt" title="Peggy Moffitt">Peggy Moffitt</a> modeling Gernreich's monokini was initially published in <i><a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Wear_Daily" title="Women's Wear Daily">Women's Wear Daily</a></i> on June 3, 1964.</div>

<p>The <b>monokini</b>, designed by <a href="/wiki/Rudi_Gernreich" title="Rudi Gernreich">Rudi Gernreich</a> in 1964, consisting of only a brief, close-fitting bottom and two thin straps,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was the first women's <a href="/wiki/Toplessness#Topless_swimwear" title="Toplessness">topless swimsuit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alac_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-alac-3">[3]</a></sup> His revolutionary and controversial design included a bottom that "extended from the midriff to the upper thigh"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-everything_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-everything-4">[4]</a></sup> and was "held up by shoestring laces that make a halter around the neck."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nangle_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nangle-5">[5]</a></sup> Some credit Gernreich's design with initiating,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alac_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-alac-3">[3]</a></sup> or describe it as a symbol of, the <a href="/wiki/Sexual_revolution" title="Sexual revolution">sexual revolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elle_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-elle-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gernreich designed the monokini as a protest against a repressive society. He didn't initially intend to produce the monokini commercially,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bay_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-bay-7">[7]</a></sup> but was persuaded by Susanne Kirtland of <i><a href="/wiki/Look_(American_magazine)" title="Look (American magazine)">Look</a></i> to make it available to the public. When the first photograph of a frontal view of <a href="/wiki/Peggy_Moffitt" title="Peggy Moffitt">Peggy Moffitt</a> wearing the design was published in <i><a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Wear_Daily" title="Women's Wear Daily">Women's Wear Daily</a></i> on June 3, 1964,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gernrich20_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-gernrich20-8">[8]</a></sup> it generated a great deal of controversy in the United States and other countries. Gernreich sold about 3000 suits, but only two were worn in public. The first was worn publicly on June 22, 1964 by <a href="/wiki/Carol_Doda" title="Carol Doda">Carol Doda</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> at the Condor Nightclub, ushering in the era of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topless_bar" title="Topless bar">topless nightclubs</a> in the United States, and the second at a beach in Chicago in July 1964 by artist's model Toni Lee Shelley, who was arrested.</p>
<p>Some manufacturers and retailers refer to modern monokini swimsuit designs as a topless swimsuit, topless bikini,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> or unikini.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monokini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gernreich may have chosen his use of the word <i>monokini</i> (<i>mono</i> meaning <i>single</i>) in the mistaken belief that <i>bikini</i> was a compound of bi- ("bi" meaning <i>two</i>) and -kini. But that is a faulty <a href="/wiki/Back-formation" title="Back-formation">back-formation</a>, decomposing the word as the Latin prefix <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bi-" title="Bi-">bi-</a></i>, and <i>kini</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> denoting a two-piece swimsuit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> But in fact the bikini swimsuit design was named by its inventor <a href="/wiki/Louis_R%C3%A9ard" title="Louis Réard">Louis Réard</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Bikini_Atoll" title="Bikini Atoll">Bikini Atoll</a> in the Pacific, five days after <a href="/wiki/Operation_Crossroads" title="Operation Crossroads">Operation Crossroads</a>, the first peace-time test of nuclear weapons, took place there. Réard hoped his design would have a similarly explosive effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bikinistyle_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-bikinistyle-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monokini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rudi_Gernreich" title="Rudi Gernreich">Rudi Gernreich</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rudi_Gernreich.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="318" data-file-width="220" height="318" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/a6/Rudi_Gernreich.jpg" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rudi_Gernreich.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Monokini designer <a href="/wiki/Rudi_Gernreich" title="Rudi Gernreich">Rudi Gernreich</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austrian_American" title="Austrian American">Austrian-American</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fashion_designer" title="Fashion designer">fashion designer</a> <a href="/wiki/Rudi_Gernreich" title="Rudi Gernreich">Rudi Gernreich</a> had strong feelings about society's sexualization of the human body and disagreed with religious and social beliefs that the body was essentially shameful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Gernreich grew up in Austria where its citizens were advocates of exercising nude, a rejection of the over-civilized world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bay_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-bay-7">[7]</a></sup> His father Siegmund Gernreich was a stocking manufacturer who committed suicide when Gernreich was 8. In 1939, his mother took him and they fled the country to escape Hitler, who among other things had banned nudity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bay_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-bay-7">[7]</a></sup> In Los Angeles, Gernreich became an advocate of sexual liberation. He co-founded in 1950 the first homosexual social group advocating for gay rights that became the <a href="/wiki/Mattachine_Society" title="Mattachine Society">Mattachine Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> He thought government restraints on nudity were fascist and oppressive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bay_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-bay-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reputation">Reputation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monokini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reputation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gernreich developed a reputation as an avant-garde designer who broke many of the rules, and his swimsuit designs were unconventional. In its December 1962 issue, <i><a href="/wiki/Sports_Illustrated" title="Sports Illustrated">Sports Illustrated</a></i> remarked, "He has turned the dancer's leotard into a swimsuit that frees the body. In the process, he has ripped out the boning and wiring that made American swimsuits seagoing corsets." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-si1962_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-si1962-17">[17]</a></sup> That month he first envisioned<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-si1962_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-si1962-17">[17]</a></sup> creating a <a href="/wiki/Toplessness" title="Toplessness">topless</a> swimsuit which he called a monokini.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monokini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gernreich had predicted in a September 1962 issue of <i>Women’s Wear Daily</i> that "Bosoms will be uncovered within five years." At the end of 1963, editor Susanne Kirtland of <i><a href="/wiki/Look_(American_magazine)" title="Look (American magazine)">Look</a></i> called Gernreich and asked him to submit a design for the suit to accompany a trend story along futuristic lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gernrich20_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-gernrich20-8">[8]</a></sup> He resisted the idea at first, but said, "It was my prediction. For the sake of history, I didn't want <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Pucci" title="Emilio Pucci">Pucci</a> to do it first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bay_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-bay-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alexander1964_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-alexander1964-19">[19]</a></sup> Gernreich found the design more difficult than the expected. His initial designs looked like trunks or boxer shorts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alexander1964_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-alexander1964-19">[19]</a></sup> He felt the swimsuit ought to just be bikini bottoms, but realized that this wouldn't constitute a unique design. He initially designed a Balinese sarong that began just under the breasts, but Kirtland didn't feel the design was bold enough and needed to make more of a statement. Gernreich finally chose a design that ended around mid-torso and then added two straps that rose between the breasts and were tied around the neck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gernrich20_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-gernrich20-8">[8]</a></sup> The first two initial attempts to cut the design failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alexander1964_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-alexander1964-19">[19]</a></sup> When a photo shoot was arranged on <a href="/wiki/Montego_Bay" title="Montego Bay">Montego Bay</a> in the Bahamas, all five models hired for the session refused to wear the design. The photographer finally persuaded a local prostitute to model it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kalter_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-kalter-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_statement">As a statement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monokini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: As a statement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gernreich did not originally intend to produce the swimsuit commercially. It had more meaning to Gernreich as an idea than as a reality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-smith-21">[21]</a></sup> Gernreich had Moffitt model the suit in person for <a href="/wiki/Diana_Vreeland" title="Diana Vreeland">Diana Vreeland</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Vogue_(magazine)" title="Vogue (magazine)">Vogue</a></i>, who asked him why he conceived of the design. Gernreich told her he felt it was time for "freedom-in fashion as well as every other facet of life," but that the swimsuit was just a statement. He said, “[Women] drop their bikini tops already,” he said, “so it seemed like the natural next step.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bay_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-bay-7">[7]</a></sup> She told him, "If there's a picture of it, it's an actuality. You must make it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gernreich21_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-gernreich21-22">[22]</a></sup> Gerenrich said in television interview, "It may well be a bit much now. But, just wait. In a couple of years topless bikinis will be a reality and regarded as perfectly natural."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thesander_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thesander-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_pictures">Design pictures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monokini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Design pictures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To avoid letting others sensationalize the swimsuit and to retain some control of the design, Gernreich asked <a href="/wiki/William_Claxton_(photographer)" title="William Claxton (photographer)">William Claxton</a>, the husband of Gernrich's usually sole model <a href="/wiki/Peggy_Moffitt" title="Peggy Moffitt">Peggy Moffitt</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nymagazine_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-nymagazine-24">[24]</a></sup> to take pictures of his wife in the yellow wool swimsuit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elle_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-elle-6">[6]</a></sup> Claxton, Moffitt, and Gernreich wanted to publish their own pictures for the fashion press and news media, and Gernrich gave pictures of Moffit modeling the monokini to a carefully selected handful of news organizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gernreich21_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-gernreich21-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moffitt was initially resistant to the idea of posing topless. She said, "I didn’t want to do it when he asked me. I am a puritanical descendent of the <a href="/wiki/Mayflower" title="Mayflower">Mayflower</a>. I carried that goddamned <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Rock" title="Plymouth Rock">Plymouth Rock</a> on my back. When I did give in, I did so with a lot of rules. I would not show myself on the <a href="/wiki/Runway_(fashion)" title="Runway (fashion)">runway</a> that way. I’d do it only with Bill. Since Rudi would never ever have enough money to do this, I did it for free. But I had final say on everywhere it went photographically."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <i>Look</i> published a rear view of the prostitute from Montego Bay modeling the swimsuit on June 2, 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gernrich20_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-gernrich20-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-luther_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-luther-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shteir_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-shteir-28">[28]</a></sup> Claxton took his pictures of Moffit to <i>Life</i> but they said they could only print pictures of naked breasts "if the woman is an <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Australians" title="Aboriginal Australians">aborigine</a>." Claxton took additional pictures of Moffit especially for <i>Life</i> with her arms covering her breasts. The picture was one of several images of Moffit in a story about the historical evolution of the breast in fashion history from 1954 to 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alexander1964_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-alexander1964-19">[19]</a></sup> Moffit said, "The photograph of me in that issue—hiding my breasts with my arms—is dirty. If you are wearing a fashion that does not have a top as part of its design and hold your arms over your bosom, you're going along with the whole prudish, teasey thing like a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Playboy_bunny" title="Playboy bunny">Playboy bunny</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gernreich21_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-gernreich21-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_notice">Initial notice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monokini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Initial notice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following day columnist Carol Bjorkman of <i><a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Wear_Daily" title="Women's Wear Daily">Women's Wear Daily</a></i> published Claxton's frontal view of Moffitt wearing the suit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gernrich20_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-gernrich20-8">[8]</a></sup> It became a celebrated image of the extremism of 1960s designs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Moffit later said, "It was a political statement. It wasn't meant to be worn in public."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nymagazine_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-nymagazine-24">[24]</a></sup> On June 12, 1964 the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> featured a photo of a woman in a monokini with her exposed breasts clearly visible on its front page.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allyn_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allyn-30">[30]</a></sup> Claxton's frontal image of Moffit modeling the swimsuit was subsequently published by <i><a href="/wiki/Life_(magazine)" title="Life (magazine)">Life</a></i> and numerous other publications. <i>Life</i> writer Shana Alexander noted, "One funny thing about toplessness is that it really doesn't have much to do with breasts. Breasts of course are not absurd; topless swimsuits are. Lately people keep getting the two things mixed up." She mocked the swimsuit design as a "joke".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alexander1964_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-alexander1964-19">[19]</a></sup> The photo catapulted Moffitt into instant celebrity, reportedly resulting in her receiving everything from marriage proposals to death threats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nymagazine_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-nymagazine-24">[24]</a></sup> Moffitt and Claxton later wrote <i>The Rudy Gernreich Book</i>, described as an aesthetic biography of the fashion revolutionary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="ref_Moffittnone"><a href="#endnote_Moffittnone">[A]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instant_attention">Instant attention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monokini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Instant attention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"I thought we'd sell only six or seven, but I decided to design it anyway."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rockwell_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-rockwell-32">[32]</a></sup> But when the design got worldwide notice, orders for the non-existent suit pored in until over 1,000 orders were pending.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alexander1964_19-5"><a href="#cite_note-alexander1964-19">[19]</a></sup> Despite the reaction of fashion critics and church officials, Harmon Knitwear made over 3,000 monokinis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-everything_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-everything-4">[4]</a></sup> Gernreich first sold the suit to the <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Magnin_Co." title="Joseph Magnin Co.">Joseph Magnin department store</a> in San Francisco, where it was an instant hit. In New York City, leading stores like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B._Altman_%26_Company" title="B. Altman &amp; Company">B. Altman &amp; Company</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lord_%26_Taylor" title="Lord &amp; Taylor">Lord &amp; Taylor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Bendel" title="Henri Bendel">Henri Bendel</a>, Splendiferous and Parisette placed orders. On June 16, 1964, Gernreich's topless swimsuit went on sale in New York City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bay_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-bay-7">[7]</a></sup> The suit was priced at $24 each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-everything_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-everything-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monokini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Swimsuit_as_a_statement">Swimsuit as a statement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monokini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Swimsuit as a statement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gernreich purposefully used his designs to advance his socio-political views. He wanted to reduce the stigma of a <a href="/wiki/Nudity" title="Nudity">naked</a> body, to “cure our society of its sex hang up,” as he put it. Gerreich stated, "To me, the only respect you can give to a woman is to make her a human being. A totally emancipated woman who is totally free."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tay_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-tay-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gernreich said, "Baring the breasts seemed logical in a period of freer attitudes, freer minds, the emancipation of women."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shteir_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-shteir-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Gernreich told <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine in 1969, the monokini "is a natural development growing out of all the loosening up, the re-evaluation of values that’s going on. There is now an honesty hangup, and part of this is not hiding the body—it stands for freedom."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rockwell_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-rockwell-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Every girl I knew was offended by the dirty-little-boy attitude of the American male toward the American bosom. I was aware that the great masses of the world would find the topless shocking and immoral. I couldn't help feel the implicit hypocrisy that made something in one culture immoral and in another perfectly acceptable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-levine_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-levine-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In January, 1965, he told <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Steinem" title="Gloria Steinem">Gloria Steinem</a> in an interview that despite the criticism he'd do it again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rockwell_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-rockwell-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>A designer stands or falls on the totality of each year's collection, not just one item. At the moment, this topless business has done nothing but take away from my work, but in the end, I'm sure having my name known internationally will be a help. But that isn't why I'd do it again. I'd do it again because I think the topless, by overstating and exaggerating a new freedom of the body, will make the moderate, right degree of freedom more acceptable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rockwell_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-rockwell-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Moffitt said the design was a logical evolution of Gernreich's <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> ideas in swimwear design as much as a scandalous symbol of the <a href="/wiki/Permissive_society" title="Permissive society">permissive society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-menkes_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-menkes-37">[37]</a></sup> She said, "He was trying to take away the prurience, the whole perverse side of sex." She said his design was "prophetic." "It had to do with more than what to wear to the beach. It was about a changing culture throughout all society, about freedom and emancipation. It was also a reaction against something particularly American: the little boy snickering that women had breasts."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-menkes_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-menkes-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Los Angeles Times staff writer Bettijane Levine wrote, "His topless was an artistic statement against women as sex objects, much as <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a> painted <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guernica_(painting)" title="Guernica (painting)">Guernica</a></i> as a statement against war."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-levine_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-levine-36">[36]</a></sup> Over the next few weeks, his design was covered in more than 20,000 press articles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1985_benefit_showing">1985 benefit showing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monokini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 1985 benefit showing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On August 13, 1985, Los Angeles Fashion Group produced a gala at the Wiltern Theatre to benefit the Rudi Gernreich Design Scholarship Fund. Moffit was a member of the committee. When the group considered showing the Monokini suit during the benefit, Moffitt strongly objected. She told the Los Angeles Times,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-levine_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-levine-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Rudi did the suit as a social statement. It was an exaggeration that had to do with setting women free. It had nothing to do with display, and the minute someone wears it to show off her body, you've negated the entire principle of the thing. I modeled it for a photograph, which was eventually published around the world, because I believed in the social statement. Also, because the three of us—Rudi, Bill, and I—felt that the photograph presented the statement accurately.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-levine_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-levine-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The regional director of the Fashion Group, Sarah Worman, believed that the swimsuit was "the single most important idea he ever had—the one that changed the way women dressed all over the Western world." She said Moffitt's refusal to show it on a model didn't make sense when the benefit was modeling everything else he ever did on live models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-levine_36-4"><a href="#cite_note-levine-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Playboy_offer">Playboy offer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monokini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Playboy offer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Moffit said in 1985 that she had been offered $17,000 in 1964 (about $131,000 in today's dollars) by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Playboy_(magazine)" title="Playboy (magazine)">Playboy</a> to publish Claxton's photograph of her wearing the suit, but refused. "I turned it down as unthinkable. And I don't want to exploit women any more now than I did in 1964. The statement hasn't changed. The suit still is about freedom and not display."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-levine_36-5"><a href="#cite_note-levine-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World-wide_reaction">World-wide reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monokini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: World-wide reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There was a strong public reaction to the original swimsuit design. The Soviet Union denounced the suit, saying it was "barbarism" and indicated "capitalistic decay".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allyn_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Allyn-30">[30]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Vatican</a> renounced the swimsuit, and the <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Osservatore_Romano" title="L'Osservatore Romano">L'Osservatore Romano</a></i> said the "industrial-erotic adventure" of the topless bathing suit "negates moral sense." Many of Rudi's contemporaries in the fashion industry reacted negatively. In the US, some Republicans tried to blame the suit on the Democrats' stance on moral issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-smith-21">[21]</a></sup> Gernreich introduced the monokini at a time when U.S. <a href="/wiki/Naturism" title="Naturism">nudists</a> were trying to establish a public persona. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postmaster_General" title="United States Postmaster General">United States Postmaster General</a> had banned nudist publications from the mail until 1958, when the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court of the United States</a> declared that the naked body in and of itself could not be deemed <a href="/wiki/Obscenity" title="Obscenity">obscene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allyn_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Allyn-30">[30]</a></sup> Use of the word <i>monokini</i> was first recorded in English that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-everything_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-everything-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the suit gained notoriety, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department" title="New York City Police Department">New York City Police Department</a> was strictly instructed by the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Department_of_Parks_and_Recreation" title="New York City Department of Parks and Recreation">commissioner of parks</a> to arrest any woman wearing a monokini.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allyn_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-Allyn-30">[30]</a></sup> In Dallas, Texas, when a local store featured the suit in a window display, members of the Carroll Avenue Baptist Mission picketed until they removed the display.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bay_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-bay-7">[7]</a></sup> Copious coverage of the event helped to send the image of exposed breasts across the world. Women's clubs and the Catholic church actively condemned the design. In Italy and Spain, the Catholic church warned against the topless fashion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thesander_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thesander-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In France in 1964, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Frey" title="Roger Frey">Roger Frey</a> led the prosecution of the use of the monokini, describing it as: "a public offense against the sense of decency, punishable according to article 330 of the penal code. Consequently, the police chiefs must employ the services of the police so that the women who wear this bathing suit in public places are prosecuted."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI9Moral_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-SI9Moral-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LeMonde_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-LeMonde-40">[40]</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Saint-Tropez" title="Saint-Tropez">St. Tropez</a> on the <a href="/wiki/French_Riviera" title="French Riviera">French Riviera</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Toplessness" title="Toplessness">toplessness</a> later became the norm, the mayor ordered police to ban toplessness and to watch over the beach via helicopter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allyn_30-4"><a href="#cite_note-Allyn-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jean-Luc_Godard" title="Jean-Luc Godard">Jean-Luc Godard</a>, a founding mover of <a href="/wiki/French_New_Wave" title="French New Wave">French New Wave</a> cinema, incorporated monokini footage shot by <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Rozier" title="Jacques Rozier">Jacques Rozier</a> in Riviera into his film <i><a href="/wiki/A_Married_Woman" title="A Married Woman">A Married Woman</a></i>, but it was edited out by the censors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> A few defended Gernreich's design. Fashion designers <a href="/wiki/Geraldine_Stutz" title="Geraldine Stutz">Geraldine Stutz</a>, president of <a href="/wiki/Henri_Bendel" title="Henri Bendel">Henri Bendel</a>, said, "I only wish I were young enough to be one of the pioneers myself." Carol Bjorkman, a columnist at <i>Women's Wear-Daily's</i> wrote, "What's the matter with the front? After all, it is here to stay, and it is awfully nice being a girl."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-smith-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Toni Lee Shelley, a 19 year old artists model, wore the topless bathing suit to the North Avenue beach in Chicago, 12 police officers responded, 11 to control and disperse the public and photographers, and one to arrest her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alexander_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-alexander-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-starnews_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-starnews-43">[43]</a></sup> She was charged with <a href="/wiki/Disorderly_conduct" title="Disorderly conduct">disorderly conduct</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indecent_exposure" title="Indecent exposure">indecent exposure</a>, and appearing on a public beach without suitable attire. At her arraignment she asked for an all-male jury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gernreich21_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-gernreich21-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> She told the press that the swimsuit was "certainly more comfortable."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-starnews_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-starnews-43">[43]</a></sup> Shelley was fined US$100 for wearing the swimsuit on a public beach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allyn_30-5"><a href="#cite_note-Allyn-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monokini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Condor_Club_North_Beach1973.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1927" data-file-width="3104" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Condor_Club_North_Beach1973.jpg/220px-Condor_Club_North_Beach1973.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Condor_Club_North_Beach1973.jpg/330px-Condor_Club_North_Beach1973.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Condor_Club_North_Beach1973.jpg/440px-Condor_Club_North_Beach1973.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Condor_Club_North_Beach1973.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Carol_Doda" title="Carol Doda">Carol Doda</a> wore Gernreich's monokini for her act at the Condor Club, starting the trend of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topless_bar" title="Topless bar">topless bars</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1960s_in_fashion" title="1960s in fashion">1960s</a>, the monokini influenced the <a href="/wiki/Sexual_revolution" title="Sexual revolution">sexual revolution</a> by emphasizing a woman's personal freedom of dress, even when her attire was provocative and exposed more skin than had been the norm during the more conservative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1945%E2%80%931960_in_fashion" title="1945–1960 in fashion">1950s</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allyn_30-6"><a href="#cite_note-Allyn-30">[30]</a></sup> Quickly renamed a "topless swimsuit",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allyn_30-7"><a href="#cite_note-Allyn-30">[30]</a></sup> the design was never successful in the United States, although the issue of allowing both genders equal exposure above the waist has been raised as a feminist issue from time to time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-menkes_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-menkes-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 22, 1964, the public relations manager of the Condor Nightclub in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>'s <a href="/wiki/North_Beach,_San_Francisco" title="North Beach, San Francisco">North Beach</a> district bought Gernreich's monokini from Joseph Magnin and gave it to former prune picker, file clerk, and waitress <a href="/wiki/Carol_Doda" title="Carol Doda">Carol Doda</a> to wear for her act. Doda was the first modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topless_dancer" title="Topless dancer">topless dancer</a> in the United States,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allyn_30-8"><a href="#cite_note-Allyn-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:25</sup> renewing the <a href="/wiki/Burlesque" title="Burlesque">burlesque</a> era of the early Twentieth Century in the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_San_Francisco" title="Mayor of San Francisco">San Francisco Mayor</a> <a href="/wiki/John_F._Shelley" title="John F. Shelley">John Shelley</a> said, "topless is at the bottom of porn."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shteir_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-shteir-28">[28]</a></sup> Within a few days, women were baring their breasts in many of the clubs lining San Francisco's Broadway St., ushering in the era of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topless_bar" title="Topless bar">topless bar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shteir_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-shteir-28">[28]</a></sup> Her debut as a topless dancer was featured in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Playboy_magazine" title="Playboy magazine">Playboy magazine</a> in April 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allyn_30-9"><a href="#cite_note-Allyn-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:25</sup></p>
<p>San Francisco public officials tolerated the topless bars until April 22, 1965, when the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Police_Department" title="San Francisco Police Department">San Francisco Police Department</a> arrested Doda on indecency charges. Hundreds of protesters gathered outside the police department, calling for release of both Doda and free speech activist <a href="/wiki/Mario_Savio" title="Mario Savio">Mario Savio</a>, held in the same station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shteir_28-4"><a href="#cite_note-shteir-28">[28]</a></sup> Doda rapidly became a symbol of sexual freedom, while topless restaurants, shoeshine parlors, ice-cream stands and girl bands proliferated in San Francisco and elsewhere. Journalist <a href="/wiki/Earl_Wilson_(columnist)" title="Earl Wilson (columnist)">Earl Wilson</a> wrote in his syndicated column, "Are we ready for girls in topless gowns? Heck, we may not even notice them." English designers created topless evening gowns inspired by the idea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allyn_30-10"><a href="#cite_note-Allyn-30">[30]</a></sup> The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Examiner" title="San Francisco Examiner">San Francisco Examiner</a></i> published a real estate advertisement that promised "bare top swimsuits are possible here".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shteir_28-5"><a href="#cite_note-shteir-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p>Going topless reached its highest popularity during the 1970s. In the early 1980s monokini designs that were simply a bikini-bottom (also known as the <b>unikini</b>) became popular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Monokini&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, some swimsuit designers continue to produce a variety of monokini or topless swimsuits that women can wear in private settings or in places where topless swimsuits are allowed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-everything_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-everything-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike Gernreich's original design exposing the women's breasts, current designs are <a href="/wiki/One-piece_swimsuit" title="One-piece swimsuit">one-piece swimsuits</a> that cover the women's breasts but typically include large cut-outs<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LoveToKnow_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-LoveToKnow-46">[46]</a></sup> on the sides, back, or front. The cutouts are connected with varying fabrics, including mesh, chain, and other materials to link the top and bottom sections together. From the back the monokini looks like a two-piece swimsuit. The design may not be functional but aesthetic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swim_Trends_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Swim_Trends-47">[47]</a></sup> Some suits are designed with a <a href="/wiki/G-string" title="G-string">g-string</a> style back and others offer full coverage.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pubikini"><span id="Pubikini"></span> Pubikini</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monokini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Pubikini">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1985, four weeks before his death, Gernreich unveiled the lesser-known <b>pubikini</b>, a topless bathing suit that exposed the wearer's <a href="/wiki/Mons_pubis" title="Mons pubis">mons pubis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> It was a thin, V-shaped, thong-style bottom<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> that in the front featured a tiny strip of fabric that exposed the wearer's <a href="/wiki/Pubic_hair" title="Pubic hair">pubic hair</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metrwax_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Metrwax-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The pubikini was described as a <i><a href="/wiki/Pi%C3%A8ce_de_r%C3%A9sistance" title="Pièce de résistance">pièce de résistance</a></i> totally freeing the human body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
