<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Monument Park (Yankee Stadium)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Monument Park</b> is an open-air museum located in <a href="/wiki/Yankee_Stadium" title="Yankee Stadium">Yankee Stadium</a> in <a href="/wiki/The_Bronx" title="The Bronx">the Bronx</a>, New York City, containing a collection of monuments, <a href="/wiki/Commemorative_plaque" title="Commemorative plaque">plaques</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Retired_number" title="Retired number">retired numbers</a> honoring distinguished members of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a>. When <a href="/wiki/Red_Ruffing" title="Red Ruffing">Red Ruffing</a>'s plaque was dedicated in 2004, his son called it "the second-greatest honor you can have in baseball, in my opinion" trailing only induction into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" title="National Baseball Hall of Fame">National Baseball Hall of Fame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ruffing_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ruffing-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The history of the original Monument Park can be traced to the <a href="/wiki/Yankee_Stadium_(1923)" title="Yankee Stadium (1923)">old Yankee Stadium</a> in 1929, when the team <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_promotion" title="Posthumous promotion">posthumously</a> dedicated an on-field monument to manager <a href="/wiki/Miller_Huggins" title="Miller Huggins">Miller Huggins</a> in <a href="/wiki/Center_fielder" title="Center fielder">center field</a>. Additional team members were honored with monuments and plaques in the area over the years. During the stadium's renovation in the mid-1970s, the center field fence was moved in 44 feet, enclosing prior monuments, plaques, and a flag pole beyond the field of play. Over time, additional plaques were added to the area and "Monument Park" became formalized; in 1985, the park was opened for public access. When the Yankees moved to their new ballpark in 2009, a replica Monument Park was built beyond the center-field fences and the contents of the old one transported over.</p>
<p>Thirty-seven members of the Yankee organization have been honored in Monument Park, while 22 have had their uniform numbers retired. Plaques in Monument Park are a great honor for players so distinguished. The monuments mounted posthumously on five large red granite blocks are the highest honor of all. Only six Yankees have been so recognized: manager Huggins, players <a href="/wiki/Lou_Gehrig" title="Lou Gehrig">Lou Gehrig</a>, <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mantle" title="Mickey Mantle">Mickey Mantle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Joe_DiMaggio" title="Joe DiMaggio">Joe DiMaggio</a>, and owner <a href="/wiki/George_Steinbrenner" title="George Steinbrenner">George Steinbrenner</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monument_Park_(Yankee_Stadium)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precursor">Precursor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monument_Park_(Yankee_Stadium)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Precursor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The original Yankee Stadium was built in 1923. As with many other so-called <a href="/wiki/List_of_jewel_box_baseball_parks" title="List of jewel box baseball parks">Jewel Box</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballpark" title="Ballpark">ballparks</a> of the era the flag pole was placed in play. With a generous center field dimension of 500 feet (150 m) to straightaway <a href="/wiki/Baseball_field#outfield" title="Baseball field">center field</a>, there was plenty of room for it without materially interfering in play. In 1929, Yankees manager <a href="/wiki/Miller_Huggins" title="Miller Huggins">Miller Huggins</a> died suddenly, and in his honor the team erected a free-standing monument in front of the flag pole consisting of a bronze plaque mounted on an upright block of red granite resembling a <a href="/wiki/Headstone" title="Headstone">headstone</a>. This, in turn, led many Yankee fans over the years, particularly children, to believe that the players honored were also buried there upon their death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OYS1950S.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="721" data-file-width="1605" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d5/OYS1950S.jpg/300px-OYS1950S.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d5/OYS1950S.jpg/450px-OYS1950S.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d5/OYS1950S.jpg/600px-OYS1950S.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OYS1950S.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The original placement of the monuments in deep center field at the pre-renovated Yankee Stadium.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Huggins monument was later joined by similar memorials to <a href="/wiki/Lou_Gehrig" title="Lou Gehrig">Lou Gehrig</a> and <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a>, which were erected upon their deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legends_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-legends-3">[3]</a></sup> Over time, a number of plaques were mounted behind them on the outfield wall. Placing monuments in the field of play was not so unusual at the time, as there had been in-play stones and plaques at the <a href="/wiki/Polo_Grounds" title="Polo Grounds">Polo Grounds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forbes_Field" title="Forbes Field">Forbes Field</a>. In 1969, <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mantle" title="Mickey Mantle">Mickey Mantle</a> was given a plaque by <a href="/wiki/Joe_DiMaggio" title="Joe DiMaggio">Joe DiMaggio</a> to hang on the center field wall, who in turn gave Joe DiMaggio a plaque of his own which, in Mickey's words, had to be hung a little bit higher than his.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1936 through 1973, the distance from home plate to the center field fence was 461 feet (141 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legends_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-legends-3">[3]</a></sup> Despite the distance, a batted ball still sometimes made it back there. In the 1992 book <i>The Gospel According to Casey</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Ira_Berkow" title="Ira Berkow">Ira Berkow</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jim_Kaplan" title="Jim Kaplan">Jim Kaplan</a>, it is reported that on one occasion a Yankees outfielder had let the ball get by him and was fumbling for it among the monuments. Manager <a href="/wiki/Casey_Stengel" title="Casey Stengel">Casey Stengel</a> hollered to the field, "Ruth, Gehrig, Huggins, somebody get that ball back to the infield!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monument_Park_I">Monument Park I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monument_Park_(Yankee_Stadium)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Monument Park I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yankee_Stadium_Monument_Park.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1609" data-file-width="2263" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Yankee_Stadium_Monument_Park.jpg/250px-Yankee_Stadium_Monument_Park.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Yankee_Stadium_Monument_Park.jpg/375px-Yankee_Stadium_Monument_Park.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Yankee_Stadium_Monument_Park.jpg/500px-Yankee_Stadium_Monument_Park.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yankee_Stadium_Monument_Park.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The original Monument Park consisted of a row of monuments with plaques lining the wall behind them</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When Yankee Stadium was remodeled from 1974–1975, the center field fence was moved in to 417 feet (127 m) from its previous 457 feet (139 m); a subsequent reduction brought the fence in again to 410 feet (120 m). This enclosed the area, formerly in play, containing the flag pole and monuments. As this fenced in area between the two bullpens gathered additional plaques on the original wall it began to be referred to as "Monument Park".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steinbrenner_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-steinbrenner-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the formalization of the area as an official Monument Park, the Mantle and DiMaggio plaques were removed from the wall upon their deaths and mounted on red granite blocks matching the original three of Huggins, Gehrig, and Ruth.</p>
<p>It was an achievement for a home run to reach Monument Park on the fly. Among those who did so were <a href="/wiki/Thurman_Munson" title="Thurman Munson">Thurman Munson</a> (in Game 3 of the 1978 <a href="/wiki/American_League_Championship_Series" title="American League Championship Series">ALCS</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Alex_Rodriguez" title="Alex Rodriguez">Alex Rodriguez</a> (in August 2005).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Monument Park was inaccessible to fans until 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steinbrenner_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-steinbrenner-6">[6]</a></sup> After the center field fence was moved in, the Yankees enabled fans to visit Monument Park prior to most games at Yankee Stadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steinbrenner_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-steinbrenner-6">[6]</a></sup> Monument Park was also part of the public tour of the venue.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monument_Park_II">Monument Park II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monument_Park_(Yankee_Stadium)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Monument Park II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:New_Monument_Park_from_Bleachers.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2142" data-file-width="2856" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fd/New_Monument_Park_from_Bleachers.JPG/250px-New_Monument_Park_from_Bleachers.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fd/New_Monument_Park_from_Bleachers.JPG/375px-New_Monument_Park_from_Bleachers.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fd/New_Monument_Park_from_Bleachers.JPG/500px-New_Monument_Park_from_Bleachers.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:New_Monument_Park_from_Bleachers.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The second Monument Park</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When the Yankees moved to their new ballpark, the Yankees established a new Monument Park in the new stadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> An area was built behind the fence in straightaway center field, below the Mohegan Sun Sports Bar that serves as the <a href="/wiki/Batter%27s_eye" title="Batter's eye">batter's eye</a>. Built of pearl blue granite from Finland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> this new monument park features the five Yankee monuments in a central area around a black marble Yankees logo. This is flanked by two short stone walls which hold the retired numbers. The plaques are mounted on the back wall and the September 11 monument is on one end of the park.</p>
<p>In contrast to the old stadium, the new Monument Park is not readily visible from the field, and its relatively drab appearance and inconspicuous placement have led some to derisively nickname it "Monument Cave". Spectators can visit Monument Park prior to the beginning of each game. It closes 45 minutes before first pitch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honorees">Honorees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monument_Park_(Yankee_Stadium)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Honorees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Honored_baseball_members">Honored baseball members</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monument_Park_(Yankee_Stadium)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Honored baseball members">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following players and other Yankees personnel are honored with monuments or plaques in Monument Park. Monuments are considered a greater honor than plaques, and are only awarded posthumously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Often, the uniform number of the player being honored is retired in the same ceremony. Such events historically often took place either at home openers or on <a href="/wiki/Old-Timers%27_Day" title="Old-Timers' Day">Old-Timers' Day</a>, but have lately been scheduled on separate weekend home games. Figures are listed in the order in which their plaques were dedicated:</p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Although the Yankees adopted uniform numbers in 1929, McCarthy never wore a number with the Yankees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-single_digit_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-single_digit-43">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ruppert's plaque was placed on the outfield wall, to the right of the flagpole. The Lou Gehrig monument was placed to the left of the Huggins monument. Gehrig was the first Major League Baseball player to have his uniform number retired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> The Babe Ruth monument was placed to the right of the Huggins monument. The Ed Barrow plaque was placed on the wall, to the left of the flagpole.</p>

<p>In honor of Jackie Robinson's unique place as the first black player of the modern era, his number 42 was retired throughout baseball on April 15, 1997, the 50th anniversary of his debut with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Dodgers" title="Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a>. The Yankees erected a plaque for Robinson<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JackieRobinsonNumberRetired_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-JackieRobinsonNumberRetired-34">[33]</a></sup> reading: "In becoming the first Major League player to break the color barrier, Jackie will forever be an inspiration with his grace, dignity and perseverance. His story and the stories of those who never had the same opportunity must never be forgotten." 42s were also painted in front of each dugout. Players active at the time of the number's retirement in 1997 were granted a <a href="/wiki/Grandfather_clause" title="Grandfather clause">special exemption</a> permitting them to continue wearing the number for the remainder of their careers; the last such active player to wear number 42 was Yankee <a href="/wiki/Relief_pitcher" title="Relief pitcher">relief pitcher</a> <a href="/wiki/Mariano_Rivera" title="Mariano Rivera">Mariano Rivera</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jr42_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-jr42-45">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Yankees honored Rivera by retiring his uniform number on September 22, 2013, during his final season, making him the first active player to be enshrined in Monument Park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> Mantle wore his #7 when he coached the Yankees in 1970, even though it was retired the previous year, while Berra wore his #8 while he coached the Yankees from 1976 through 1985, though it was retired in 1972. Similarly, when Martin returned to manage the Yankees in 1988, he wore his #1, which had been retired in his honor in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-single_digit_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-single_digit-43">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_honorees">Other honorees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monument_Park_(Yankee_Stadium)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other honorees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to baseball related recognitions, the <a href="/wiki/Knights_of_Columbus" title="Knights of Columbus">Knights of Columbus</a> donated plaques in honor of the Masses celebrated at Yankee Stadium by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_VI" title="Pope Paul VI">Pope Paul VI</a> on October 4, 1965; <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> on October 2, 1979; and <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a> on April 20, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup> The Yankees also dedicated a monument to the victims and rescue workers of the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> on September 11, 2002, the first anniversary of the attacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> They dedicated a plaque to <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Mandela" title="Nelson Mandela">Nelson Mandela</a> on April 16, 2014, to commemorate his life and 1990 visit to Yankee Stadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mandela_rain_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-mandela_rain-51">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[b]</a></sup></p>
