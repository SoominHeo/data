<p><b>Monica Denise Brown</b>, (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N%C3%A9e" title="Née">née</a> <b>Arnold</b>; born October 24, 1980),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> simply known as <b>Monica</b>, is an American singer, songwriter, producer, and actress. Born and raised in <a href="/wiki/College_Park,_Georgia" title="College Park, Georgia">College Park, Georgia</a>, she began performing as a child and became part of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gospel_choir" title="Gospel choir">traveling gospel choir</a> at the age of ten. She rose to prominence after releasing her debut studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Miss_Thang" title="Miss Thang">Miss Thang</a></i> in 1995. It went multiplatinum, while its first two singles "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Take_It_Personal_(Just_One_of_Dem_Days)" title="Don't Take It Personal (Just One of Dem Days)">Don't Take It Personal</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Before_You_Walk_Out_of_My_Life" title="Before You Walk Out of My Life">Before You Walk Out of My Life</a>" made her the youngest recording act to ever have two consecutive chart-topping hits on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_Top_R%26B_Singles" title="Billboard Top R&amp;B Singles"><i>Billboard</i> Top R&amp;B Singles</a> chart. In 1998, Monica's second album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boy_Is_Mine_(album)" title="The Boy Is Mine (album)">The Boy Is Mine</a></i> earned her major international chart success. Pushed by its <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_R%26B_Performance_by_a_Duo_or_Group_with_Vocals" title="Grammy Award for Best R&amp;B Performance by a Duo or Group with Vocals">Grammy Award</a>-winning <a href="/wiki/The_Boy_Is_Mine_(song)" title="The Boy Is Mine (song)">number-one hit title track</a>, a duet with singer <a href="/wiki/Brandy_Norwood" title="Brandy Norwood">Brandy</a>, it spawned two further <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Hot 100</a> chart-toppers, "<a href="/wiki/The_First_Night" title="The First Night">The First Night</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Angel_of_Mine" title="Angel of Mine">Angel of Mine</a>", and established her position as one of the most successful of the urban R&amp;B female vocalists to emerge in the mid to late-1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b99_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-b99-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Monica also ventured into acting, with a supporting role in <i><a href="/wiki/Boys_and_Girls_(2000_film)" title="Boys and Girls (2000 film)">Boys and Girls</a></i> (2000), and starring roles in television film <i><a href="/wiki/Love_Song_(2000_film)" title="Love Song (2000 film)">Love Song</a></i> (2000). During the production of her third album, <i><a href="/wiki/All_Eyez_on_Me_(Monica_album)" title="All Eyez on Me (Monica album)">All Eyez on Me</a></i> (2002), Monica experienced personal struggles and her career went under hiatus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enquirer_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Enquirer-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2003, her fourth album, <i><a href="/wiki/After_the_Storm_(Monica_album)" title="After the Storm (Monica album)">After the Storm</a></i>, was released, and after an unsuccessful period, she scored her fifth number one single on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">US R&amp;B/Hip Hop</a>, "<a href="/wiki/So_Gone" title="So Gone">So Gone</a>". The album also included her Top 20 Billboard Hot 100 hit "<a href="/wiki/U_Should%27ve_Known_Better" title="U Should've Known Better">U Should've Known Better</a>". Her sixth album, <i><a href="/wiki/Still_Standing_(Monica_album)" title="Still Standing (Monica album)">Still Standing</a></i>, which recording was tracked by her <a href="/wiki/BET" title="BET">BET</a> reality show <i><a href="/wiki/Monica:_Still_Standing" title="Monica: Still Standing">Monica: Still Standing</a></i>, was released in 2010 and produced the hit single "<a href="/wiki/Everything_to_Me_(Monica_song)" title="Everything to Me (Monica song)">Everything to Me</a>", which became her sixth number one hit on the US R&amp;B/Hip Hop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bill_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-bill-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Monica has sold over 5.3 million albums in the United States alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b99_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-b99-3">[3]</a></sup> With a career lasting over 20 years, she became the first artist to top the US <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs"><i>Billboard</i> Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a> chart in the 1990s, 2000s, and 2010s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2010, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_magazine" title="Billboard magazine">Billboard</a></i> listed Monica at number 24 on its list of the Top 50 R&amp;B and Hip Hop Artists of the past 25 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billboard25_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-billboard25-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monica_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Monica was born in <a href="/wiki/College_Park,_Georgia" title="College Park, Georgia">College Park, Georgia</a> on October 24, 1980. She is the only daughter of Marilyn Best, a former church singer and <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a> customer service representative, and M.C. "Billy" Arnold Jr., a mechanic working for an <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> freight company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> She has a younger brother, Montez (born in 1983), half brother Jermond Grant on her father's side,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pl2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-pl2-9">[9]</a></sup> and two maternal half-brothers, Tron and Cypress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebony2000_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ebony2000-10">[10]</a></sup> Monica also is a cousin of record producer <a href="/wiki/Polow_da_Don" title="Polow da Don">Polow da Don</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and relative-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-law" title="In-law">in-law</a> to rapper <a href="/wiki/Ludacris" title="Ludacris">Ludacris</a> through her mother's second marriage to Reverend Edward Best, a <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodist minister</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cl2006_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-cl2006-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the age of 2, Monica followed in her mother's footsteps with regular performances at the <a href="/wiki/United_Methodist_Church" title="United Methodist Church">Jones Hill Chapel United Methodist Church</a> in Marilyn's hometown <a href="/wiki/Newnan,_Georgia" title="Newnan, Georgia">Newnan, Georgia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cl2006_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-cl2006-12">[12]</a></sup> While growing up in the modest circumstances of a single-parent home after her parents' 1984 separation and 1987 divorce, Monica continued training herself in singing and became a frequent talent-show contestant, winning over 20 local singing competitions throughout her early teenage years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebony2000_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ebony2000-10">[10]</a></sup> When she was 10 years old, she became the youngest member of "Charles Thompson and the Majestics", which is a traveling 12-piece gospel choir.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pl2_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-pl2-9">[9]</a></sup> She also attended <a href="/wiki/North_Clayton_High_School" title="North Clayton High School">North Clayton High School</a> along with rapper <a href="/wiki/2_Chainz" title="2 Chainz">2 Chainz</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monica_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1991.E2.80.932000:_Miss_Thang_and_The_Boy_Is_Mine">1991–2000: <i>Miss Thang</i> and <i>The Boy Is Mine</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monica_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1991–2000: Miss Thang and The Boy Is Mine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1991, at the age of eleven, Monica was discovered by music producer <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Austin" title="Dallas Austin">Dallas Austin</a> at the Center Stage auditorium in Atlanta performing <a href="/wiki/Whitney_Houston" title="Whitney Houston">Whitney Houston</a> 1986's "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greatest_Love_of_All" title="Greatest Love of All">Greatest Love of All</a>". Amazed by her voice, Dallas offered her a record deal with his <a href="/wiki/Arista_Records" title="Arista Records">Arista Records</a>-distributed label <a href="/wiki/Rowdy_Records" title="Rowdy Records">Rowdy Records</a> and consulted rapper <a href="/wiki/Queen_Latifah" title="Queen Latifah">Queen Latifah</a> to work as Monica's first manager.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebo1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ebo1-13">[13]</a></sup> Shortly afterwards Dallas and then staff producers <a href="/wiki/Tim_%26_Bob" title="Tim &amp; Bob">Tim &amp; Bob</a> entered the studio with Monica to start writing and producing her debut <i><a href="/wiki/Miss_Thang" title="Miss Thang">Miss Thang</a></i> which was eventually released in July 1995 and scored number thirty-six on the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> and number seven on the <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B Albums</a> chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebo1_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-ebo1-13">[13]</a></sup> It was certified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triple_platinum" title="Triple platinum">triple platinum</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIAA" title="RIAA">RIAA</a> for more than three million store-shipped copies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mtv10_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-mtv10-14">[14]</a></sup> and produced three top ten singles, including debut single "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Take_It_Personal_(Just_One_of_Dem_Days)" title="Don't Take It Personal (Just One of Dem Days)">Don't Take It Personal (Just One of Dem Days)</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Before_You_Walk_Out_of_My_Life" title="Before You Walk Out of My Life">Before You Walk Out of My Life</a>", both of which made Monica the youngest artist ever to have two consecutive chart-topping hits on the U.S. <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B_Singles" title="Hot R&amp;B Singles">Hot R&amp;B Singles</a> chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <i>Miss Thang</i> subsequently won Monica a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_Music_Awards" title="Billboard Music Awards">Billboard Music Award</a> and garnered her an <a href="/wiki/American_Music_Award" title="American Music Award">American Music Award</a> nomination in the <a href="/wiki/American_Music_Awards_of_1996" title="American Music Awards of 1996">Favorite Soul/R&amp;B New Artist</a> category.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jet6_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-jet6-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>After a label change to <a href="/wiki/Clive_Davis" title="Clive Davis">Clive Davis</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Arista_Records" title="Arista Records">Arista Records</a>, Monica mainstream success was boosted, when <a href="/wiki/Diane_Warren" title="Diane Warren">Diane Warren</a>-written "<a href="/wiki/For_You_I_Will_(Monica_song)" title="For You I Will (Monica song)">For You I Will</a>", from the <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Jam" title="Space Jam">Space Jam</a></i> soundtrack, became her next top ten pop hit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jet6_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-jet6-16">[16]</a></sup> The following year she was asked to team up with singer <a href="/wiki/Brandy_Norwood" title="Brandy Norwood">Brandy</a> and producer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodney_%22Darkchild%22_Jerkins" title='Rodney "Darkchild" Jerkins'>Rodney "Darkchild" Jerkins</a> to record "<a href="/wiki/The_Boy_Is_Mine_(song)" title="The Boy Is Mine (song)">The Boy Is Mine</a>", the first single from both of their second albums. Released in May 1998, surrounding highly publicized rumors about a real-life catfight between both singers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-about-17">[17]</a></sup> the duet became both the biggest hit of the summer and the biggest hit of 1998 in general in America,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riaa_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-riaa-18">[18]</a></sup> spending record-breaking thirteen weeks on top of the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> chart. It earned the pair a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_R%26B_Performance_by_a_Duo_or_Group_with_Vocal" title="Grammy Award for Best R&amp;B Performance by a Duo or Group with Vocal">Grammy Award for Best R&amp;B Performance by a Duo or Group with Vocal</a>" and garnered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multi-platinum" title="Multi-platinum">multi-platinum</a> sales (to date, it remains as one of the top twenty most successful American singles in history based on <i>Billboard</i> chart success).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riaa_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-riaa-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jermaine_Dupri" title="Jermaine Dupri">Jermaine Dupri</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Foster" title="David Foster">David Foster</a> and Austin consulted on the album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boy_Is_Mine_(album)" title="The Boy Is Mine (album)">The Boy Is Mine</a></i>, which was released later that year and eventually became Monica’s biggest-selling album, becoming certified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triple_platinum" title="Triple platinum">triple platinum</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIAA" title="RIAA">RIAA</a> for more than three million store-shipped copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mtv10_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-mtv10-14">[14]</a></sup> It yielded another two U.S. number-one hits with "<a href="/wiki/The_First_Night" title="The First Night">The First Night</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Angel_of_Mine#Monica_version" title="Angel of Mine">Angel of Mine</a>", a cover of <a href="/wiki/Eternal_(band)" title="Eternal (band)">Eternal</a>'s 1997 single, as well as a remake of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Marx" title="Richard Marx">Richard Marx</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Right_Here_Waiting" title="Right Here Waiting">Right Here Waiting</a>". <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> proclaimed it as "closer to soul's source... harking back past hip-hop songbirds like <a href="/wiki/Mary_J._Blige" title="Mary J. Blige">Mary J. Blige</a> and adult-contemporary sirens like <a href="/wiki/Toni_Braxton" title="Toni Braxton">Toni Braxton</a>,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollstone_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-rollstone-19">[19]</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> called the album an "irresistible sounding [and] immaculately crafted musical backdrop [...] as good as mainstream urban R&amp;B gets in 1998."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amg-tbim_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-amg-tbim-20">[20]</a></sup> Monica has also made guest appearances on several television shows such as <i><a href="/wiki/Living_Single" title="Living Single">Living Single</a></i> (1996), <i><a href="/wiki/Beverly_Hills,_90210" title="Beverly Hills, 90210">Beverly Hills, 90210</a></i> (1997, 1999).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000.E2.80.932005:_All_Eyez_on_Me_and_After_the_Storm">2000–2005: <i>All Eyez on Me</i> and <i>After the Storm</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monica_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2000–2005: All Eyez on Me and After the Storm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, Monica made her film debut in the third drama from <a href="/wiki/MTV_Films" title="MTV Films">MTV Films</a>, <i>Love Song</i>, as Camille Livingston, a young woman torn between the life her parents have planned for her and the world she experiences after meeting a musician from the wrong side of the tracks. The film debut the song "What My Heart Says" with promotion of her third album <i><a href="/wiki/All_Eyez_on_Me_(Monica_album)" title="All Eyez on Me (Monica album)">All Eyez on Me</a></i> (2002). The film was released on April 30, 2002 and in <i><a href="/wiki/Felicity_(TV_series)" title="Felicity (TV series)">Felicity</a></i> (2001) and <i><a href="/wiki/American_Dreams" title="American Dreams">American Dreams</a></i> (2003) playing Mary Wells, singing "My Guy".</p>
<p>In 2000, Monica contributed chorus vocals for "<a href="/wiki/I%27ve_Got_to_Have_It" title="I've Got to Have It">I've Got to Have It</a>", a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Jermaine_Dupri" title="Jermaine Dupri">Jermaine Dupri</a> and rapper <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>. Released as the <i><a href="/wiki/Big_Momma%27s_House" title="Big Momma's House">Big Momma's House</a></i> theme song, the song saw minor success in the United States only. The following year, she released the <a href="/wiki/Ric_Wake" title="Ric Wake">Ric Wake</a> production track "<a href="/wiki/Just_Another_Girl_(Monica_song)" title="Just Another Girl (Monica song)">Just Another Girl</a>", recorded for the <i><a href="/wiki/Down_to_Earth_(soundtrack)" title="Down to Earth (soundtrack)">Down to Earth</a></i> soundtrack, as a single. A year later, Monica channeled much of her heavily media-discussed experiences into the production of her third studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/All_Eyez_on_Me_(Monica_album)" title="All Eyez on Me (Monica album)">All Eyez on Me</a></i>, her first release on mentor <a href="/wiki/Clive_Davis" title="Clive Davis">Clive Davis</a> newly founded <a href="/wiki/J_Records" title="J Records">J Records</a> label. "I just wanted to give the people back something that had personal passion, instead of just, 'Oh, let's dance to this record'," she said about the issues worked into the tracks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enquirer_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Enquirer-4">[4]</a></sup> The first single "<a href="/wiki/All_Eyez_on_Me_(song)" title="All Eyez on Me (song)">All Eyez on Me</a>," a <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Jerkins" title="Rodney Jerkins">Rodney Jerkins</a>-produced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%26B" title="R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a>-<a href="/wiki/Dance_music" title="Dance music">dance</a> track, saw minor to moderate success on the international charts but failed to enter the higher half of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enquirer_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Enquirer-4">[4]</a></sup> A follow-up song, "<a href="/wiki/Too_Hood" title="Too Hood">Too Hood</a>", also got a lukewarm response and as a result, the album's tentative release was pushed back several times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enquirer_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Enquirer-4">[4]</a></sup> "I don't think people wanted to hear a big fun record from me, after knowing all the things that I had personally experienced," Monica second-guessed her new material which saw both early and heavy bootlegging via internet at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enquirer_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Enquirer-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the Japan-wide release of <i>All Eyez on Me</i>, Monica was asked to substantially reconstruct the record with a host of new producers, and as a result she re-entered recording studios to start work with songwriters <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazze_Pha" title="Jazze Pha">Jazze Pha</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andre_%22mrDEYO%22_Deyo" title='Andre "mrDEYO" Deyo'>Andre "mrDEYO" Deyo</a>, Bam &amp; Ryan and Dupri – replacing <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer" title="Executive producer">executive producer</a> <a href="/wiki/Missy_Elliott" title="Missy Elliott">Missy Elliott</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Finally released in June 2003, <i><a href="/wiki/After_the_Storm_(Monica_album)" title="After the Storm (Monica album)">After the Storm</a></i> debuted at number one on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_magazine" title="Billboard magazine">Billboard</a></i>`s Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop albums chart, and on top of the official <i>Billboard</i> 200, with sales of 186,000 copies emerging as Monica's first and only number-one album to date. It eventually received a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_certification" title="Gold certification">gold certification</a>, and has sold over one million copies domestically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-about-17">[17]</a></sup> Media reception of the CD was generally enthusiastic, with the Allmusic saying the album "has all the assuredness and smart developments that should keep Monica's younger longtime followers behind her — all the while holding the ability to appeal to a wider spectrum of R&amp;B and hip-hop fans."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The album's lead single, Elliott-penned "<a href="/wiki/So_Gone" title="So Gone">So Gone</a>", was one of Monica's biggest commercial successes in years, becoming her first top ten single since 1999's "Angel of Mine". In addition, it reached the top position of the <i>Billboard</i> R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Tracks and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_Dance_Club_Play" title="Hot Dance Club Play">Hot Dance Club Play</a> charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Subsequently, <i>After the Storm</i> spawned another three singles, with final single "<a href="/wiki/U_Should%27ve_Known_Better" title="U Should've Known Better">U Should've Known Better</a>" reaching number nineteen on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100 chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2006.E2.80.932010:_The_Makings_of_Me.2C_Still_Standing.2C_and_reality_television">2006–2010: <i>The Makings of Me</i>, <i>Still Standing</i>, and reality television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monica_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2006–2010: The Makings of Me, Still Standing, and reality television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Towards the end of 2006, Monica released her next studio album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Makings_of_Me" title="The Makings of Me">The Makings of Me</a></i>. Titled after <a href="/wiki/Curtis_Mayfield" title="Curtis Mayfield">Curtis Mayfield</a>'s recording "<a href="/wiki/Curtis_(Curtis_Mayfield_album)" title="Curtis (Curtis Mayfield album)">The Makings of You</a>", it saw her particularly reuniting with producers Elliott, Dupri, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bryan_Michael_Cox" title="Bryan Michael Cox">Bryan Michael Cox</a>, all of whom had previously contributed to <i>After the Storm</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vh1-19_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-vh1-19-25">[25]</a></sup> The album received a positive reception from most professional music critics, with <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> calling it a "concise and mostly sweet set of songs,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amg_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-amg-26">[26]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> declaring it "a solid addition" to Monica's discography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ew_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-ew-27">[27]</a></sup> While it debuted at number one on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_magazine" title="Billboard magazine">Billboard</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop albums chart, and at number eight on the official <i>Billboard</i> 200, it widely failed to revive the success of its predecessors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billboard_index_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-billboard_index-28">[28]</a></sup> Singles such as <a href="/wiki/Snap_music" title="Snap music">snap</a>-influenced "<a href="/wiki/Everytime_tha_Beat_Drop" title="Everytime tha Beat Drop">Everytime tha Beat Drop</a>" featuring Atlanta hip hop group <a href="/wiki/Dem_Franchize_Boyz" title="Dem Franchize Boyz">Dem Franchize Boyz</a> and Elliott-produced "<a href="/wiki/A_Dozen_Roses_(You_Remind_Me)" title="A Dozen Roses (You Remind Me)">A Dozen Roses (You Remind Me)</a>" failed to reach the top forty of the regular pop charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The same year, she made a cameo appearance in the American comedy-drama film <i><a href="/wiki/ATL_(film)" title="ATL (film)">ATL</a></i>, playing the <a href="/wiki/Waffle_House" title="Waffle House">Waffle House</a> waitress.</p>
<p><br/>
In August 2008, Monica appeared in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peachtree_TV" title="Peachtree TV">Peachtree TV</a> reality show special <i>Monica: The Single</i> which tracked the recording of the song "<a href="/wiki/Still_Standing_(Monica_song)" title="Still Standing (Monica song)">Still Standing</a>" for her <a href="/wiki/Still_Standing_(Monica_album)" title="Still Standing (Monica album)">same-titled sixth studio album</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bb3_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-bb3-30">[30]</a></sup> The following year, she lent her voice to the ballad "<a href="/wiki/Trust_(Keyshia_Cole_and_Monica_song)" title="Trust (Keyshia Cole and Monica song)">Trust</a>," a duet with <a href="/wiki/Keyshia_Cole" title="Keyshia Cole">Keyshia Cole</a>, that peaked in the top five on <i>Billboard</i> Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs chart, and joined the cast of <a href="/wiki/Rockmond_Dunbar" title="Rockmond Dunbar">Rockmond Dunbar</a>'s drama film <i><a href="/wiki/Pastor_Brown" title="Pastor Brown">Pastor Brown</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In 2010, with the success of the 2008 one-hour special, Monica joined the production of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Entertainment_Television" title="Black Entertainment Television">BET</a> network for her own series <i><a href="/wiki/Monica:_Still_Standing" title="Monica: Still Standing">Monica: Still Standing</a></i>, producing a spin-off her Peachtree show, containing the same concept. It focused on searching for a hit single for the album release and balancing her personal life of being a full-time mother and troubled past. The premiere and encore episode garnered 3.2 million total viewers, while the show itself was made the second highest series debut in BET history behind the debut of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiny_%26_Toya" title="Tiny &amp; Toya">Tiny &amp; Toya</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and was given a B rating by <i>Entertainment Weekly</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Featuring production by <a href="/wiki/Stargate_(production_team)" title="Stargate (production team)">Stargate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ne-Yo" title="Ne-Yo">Ne-Yo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polow_da_Don" title="Polow da Don">Polow da Don</a>, <i>Still Standing</i> was released in March 2010 and garnered a generally positive response by critics, who perceived its sound as "a return to the mid-'1990s heyday" of contemporary R&amp;B,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The album debuted atop on <i>Billboard</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop albums chart, and number two on the <i>Billboard</i> 200 with opening week sales of 184,000 copies, becoming her highest-charting album in years. Lead single "<a href="/wiki/Everything_to_Me_(Monica_song)" title="Everything to Me (Monica song)">Everything to Me</a>" scored Monica her biggest chart success since 2003's "<a href="/wiki/So_Gone" title="So Gone">So Gone</a>", reaching the top position of the <i>Billboard</i> R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Tracks charts for seven weeks. The album was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_certification" title="Gold certification">certified gold</a> by the RIAA with domestic shipments of 500,000 copies within a single month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssg_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-ssg-35">[35]</a></sup> With it success, the album and "Everything to Me" were nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_R%26B_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best R&amp;B Album">Best R&amp;B Album</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Best_Female_R%26B_Vocal_Performance" title="Best Female R&amp;B Vocal Performance">Best Female R&amp;B Vocal Performance</a>, presented at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/53rd_Grammy_Awards" title="53rd Grammy Awards">53rd Grammy Awards</a> in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In March 2010, it was announced that Monica and Hill had split in October 2009, surrounding rumors that he cheated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Monica and <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">NBA</a> player <a href="/wiki/Shannon_Brown" title="Shannon Brown">Shannon Brown</a> met in June 2010 when while they shot her music video for her second single "<a href="/wiki/Love_All_Over_Me" title="Love All Over Me">Love All Over Me</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rap-up1_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-rap-up1-38">[38]</a></sup> On November 22, 2010, Monica married Brown in a secret ceremony at their Los Angeles home,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> follow by a second wedding in front of close family on July 9, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Also in 2010, Monica joined <a href="/wiki/Trey_Songz" title="Trey Songz">Trey Songz</a> on his Passion, Pain &amp; Pleasure Tour, her first North American concert tour in ten years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011.E2.80.93present:_New_Life_and_Code_Red">2011–present: <i>New Life</i> and <i>Code Red</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monica_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2011–present: New Life and Code Red">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, Monica joined the <a href="/wiki/The_Voice_(U.S._season_1)" title="The Voice (U.S. season 1)">debut season</a> of <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>'s reality talent show <i><a href="/wiki/The_Voice_(U.S._season_1)" title="The Voice (U.S. season 1)">The Voice</a></i> as an adviser to musician coach <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cee_Lo_Green" title="Cee Lo Green">Cee Lo Green</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In April 2012, her seventh studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/New_Life_(Monica_album)" title="New Life (Monica album)">New Life</a></i>, was released. It marked her first release with <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">RCA</a>, following the disbandment of J Records in October 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rapup20_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-rapup20-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Reception for the album was generally mixed to average;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metacritic_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-45">[45]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> complimented the album's "saucy, spirited, and soulful vibe"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collar_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collar-46">[46]</a></sup> while Adam Markovitz of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> criticized its "cheesy choruses and outdated tunes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Markovitz_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Markovitz-47">[47]</a></sup> Commercially, <i>New Life</i> debuted at number four on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> and number two on the <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The album spawned two preview singles, "<a href="/wiki/Anything_(To_Find_You)" title="Anything (To Find You)">Anything (To Find You)</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Until_It%27s_Gone_(Monica_song)" title="Until It's Gone (Monica song)">Until It's Gone</a>", both of which peaked in the top 30 on the <i>Billboard</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a> chart. Lead single "<a href="/wiki/It_All_Belongs_to_Me" title="It All Belongs to Me">It All Belongs to Me</a>", another duet with singer <a href="/wiki/Brandy_Norwood" title="Brandy Norwood">Brandy</a>, charted similarly, reaching number 23 on the same chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The same year, Monica along with <a href="/wiki/Fred_Hammond" title="Fred Hammond">Fred Hammond</a> was featured on <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel music</a> recording artist <a href="/wiki/James_Fortune" title="James Fortune">James Fortune</a> and FIYA's single "Hold On" which became a top five hit on the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Songs" title="Christian Songs">Christian Songs</a> chart and garnered a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> nomination for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Gospel_Song" title="Grammy Award for Best Gospel Song">Best Gospel Song</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2012_Grammy_Awards" title="2012 Grammy Awards">54th awards ceremony</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, Monica began work on her eighth studio album. Yet untitled, Monica announced that she would be teaming with producers Missy Elliott, Rico Love, Jim Jonsin, Stargate, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mike_WiLL_Made_It" title="Mike WiLL Made It">Mike WiLL Made It</a>, Miguel, Polow Da Don, and Red Styles on the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> On May 12, 2013, it was announced that she and husband NBA player <a href="/wiki/Shannon_Brown" title="Shannon Brown">Shannon Brown</a> were expecting their first child together. She gave birth to their daughter on September 3, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> In October 2013, Monica appeared on the soundtrack of <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_D._Lee" title="Malcolm D. Lee">Malcolm D. Lee</a>'s Christmas comedy-drama <i><a href="/wiki/The_Best_Man_Holiday" title="The Best Man Holiday">The Best Man Holiday</a></i> with her rendition of "<a href="/wiki/Have_Yourself_a_Merry_Little_Christmas" title="Have Yourself a Merry Little Christmas">Have Yourself a Merry Little Christmas</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> On July 29, 2014, Monica premiered a snippet of a Missy Elliott–produced song, nicknamed "I Love Him", via her official <a href="/wiki/Instagram" title="Instagram">Instagram</a> account. Additionally, Monica revealed she would be working with Elliott's childhood friend <a href="/wiki/Timbaland" title="Timbaland">Timbaland</a> and protégée <a href="/wiki/Jazmine_Sullivan" title="Jazmine Sullivan">Jazmine Sullivan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> <i>Code Red</i> was released on December 18, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monica_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Monica's career came to a slow down in 1999 due to relationship problems with her ex-boyfriend Jarvis Weems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enquirer_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Enquirer-4">[4]</a></sup> In July 2000, the couple were together at the graveside of Weems's brother, who had died in an automobile accident at age 25 in 1998, when Weems, without warning, put a gun to his head and committed suicide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enquirer_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Enquirer-4">[4]</a></sup> "Afterward, I felt, 'What else could I have done?' You replay that situation over and over and you switch it around: Maybe if I had said this, or if I would have done that,'" she said in an interview with the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cincinnati_Enquirer" title="The Cincinnati Enquirer">The Cincinnati Enquirer</a></i> the following year. "It's just something that it's never possible for me to go back and change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enquirer_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Enquirer-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Monica met rapper <a href="/wiki/Rocko_(rapper)" title="Rocko (rapper)">Rodney "Rocko" Hill</a>, a former SWA officer and real estate manager, shortly after her ex-boyfriend Jarvis Weems's suicide in 2000, a time which she described as her "weakest."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vibe2006_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-vibe2006-56">[56]</a></sup> While the couple soon began dating in fall of the same year, they ended their relationship in 2004. A few months later, Monica and Hill revived their relationship and she became pregnant with their first child. On May 21, 2005, she gave birth to their first son.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vibe2006_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-vibe2006-56">[56]</a></sup> Monica and Hill became engaged once again on <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Eve" title="Christmas Eve">Christmas Eve</a> 2007, just a few days prior to the birth of their second child on January 8, 2008. The son was named Romelo Montez Hill, named after Monica's younger brother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The two later parted ways.</p>
<p>In June 2010, Monica met NBA player <a href="/wiki/Shannon_Brown" title="Shannon Brown">Shannon Brown</a> when looking for someone to play the love interest in her video for "<a href="/wiki/Love_All_Over_Me" title="Love All Over Me">Love All Over Me</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rap-up1_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-rap-up1-38">[38]</a></sup> Monica later announced her engagement to Brown via her Twitter account, posting a photo of a rose-cut diamond ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> On November 22, 2010, the couple married in a secret ceremony at their Los Angeles home. The marriage, however, did not become a matter of public record until January 21, 2011, when Brown told the <i>Hip-Hop Non-Stop TV-Show</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> They had a second wedding ceremony for family and friends in July 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> On September 3, 2013, Monica gave birth to her third child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
