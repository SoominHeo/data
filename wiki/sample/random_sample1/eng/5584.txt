<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Modularity theorem</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, the <b>modularity theorem</b> (formerly called the <b>Taniyama–Shimura–Weil conjecture</b> and several related names) states that <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_curve" title="Elliptic curve">elliptic curves</a> over the field of <a href="/wiki/Rational_number" title="Rational number">rational numbers</a> are related to <a href="/wiki/Modular_form" title="Modular form">modular forms</a>. <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Wiles" title="Andrew Wiles">Andrew Wiles</a> proved the modularity theorem for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semistable_elliptic_curve" title="Semistable elliptic curve">semistable elliptic curves</a>, which was enough to imply <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermat%27s_last_theorem" title="Fermat's last theorem">Fermat's last theorem</a>. Later, <a href="/wiki/Christophe_Breuil" title="Christophe Breuil">Christophe Breuil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Conrad" title="Brian Conrad">Brian Conrad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fred_Diamond" title="Fred Diamond">Fred Diamond</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Taylor_(mathematician)" title="Richard Taylor (mathematician)">Richard Taylor</a> extended Wiles' techniques to prove the full modularity theorem in 2001. The modularity theorem is a special case of more general conjectures due to <a href="/wiki/Robert_Langlands" title="Robert Langlands">Robert Langlands</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Langlands_program" title="Langlands program">Langlands program</a> seeks to attach an <a href="/wiki/Automorphic_form" title="Automorphic form">automorphic form</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automorphic_representation" title="Automorphic representation">automorphic representation</a> (a suitable generalization of a modular form) to more general objects of arithmetic algebraic geometry, such as to every elliptic curve over a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Number_field" title="Number field">number field</a>. Most cases of these extended conjectures have not yet been proved. However, <a href="#CITEREFFreitasLe_HungSiksek2015">Freitas, Le Hung &amp; Siksek (2015)</a> proved that elliptic curves defined over real quadratic fields are modular.</p>

<p>Some modular forms of weight two, in turn, correspond to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holomorphic_differential" title="Holomorphic differential">holomorphic differentials</a> for an elliptic curve. The Jacobian of the modular curve can (up to isogeny) be written as a product of irreducible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abelian_varieties" title="Abelian varieties">Abelian varieties</a>, corresponding to Hecke eigenforms of weight 2. The 1-dimensional factors are elliptic curves (there can also be higher-dimensional factors, so not all Hecke eigenforms correspond to rational elliptic curves). The curve obtained by finding the corresponding cusp form, and then constructing a curve from it, is <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_curve#Isogeny" title="Elliptic curve">isogenous</a> to the original curve (but not, in general, isomorphic to it).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Modularity_theorem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Yutaka_Taniyama" title="Yutaka Taniyama">Taniyama</a> (<a href="#CITEREFTaniyama1956">1956</a>) stated a preliminary (slightly incorrect) version of the conjecture at the 1955 international symposium on algebraic number theory in Tokyo and Nikko. <a href="/wiki/Goro_Shimura" title="Goro Shimura">Goro Shimura</a> and Taniyama worked on improving its rigor until 1957. <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Weil" title="André Weil">Weil</a> (<a href="#CITEREFWeil1967">1967</a>) rediscovered the conjecture, and showed that it would follow from the (conjectured) functional equations for some twisted L-series of the elliptic curve; this was the first serious evidence that the conjecture might be true. Weil also showed that the conductor of the elliptic curve should be the level of the corresponding modular form. The Taniyama–Shimura-Weil conjecture became a part of the <a href="/wiki/Langlands_program" title="Langlands program">Langlands program</a>.</p>
<p>The conjecture attracted considerable interest when <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Frey" title="Gerhard Frey">Frey</a> (<a href="#CITEREFFrey1986">1986</a>) suggested that the Taniyama–Shimura–Weil conjecture implies <a href="/wiki/Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem" title="Fermat's Last Theorem">Fermat's Last Theorem</a>. He did this by attempting to show that any counterexample to Fermat's Last Theorem would imply the existence of at least one non-modular elliptic curve. This argument was completed when <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Serre" title="Jean-Pierre Serre">Serre</a> (<a href="#CITEREFSerre1987">1987</a>) identified a missing link (now known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epsilon_conjecture" title="Epsilon conjecture">epsilon conjecture</a> or Ribet's theorem) in Frey's original work, followed two years later by <a href="/wiki/Ken_Ribet" title="Ken Ribet">Ribet</a> (<a href="#CITEREFRibet1990">1990</a>)'s completion of a proof of the epsilon conjecture.</p>
<p>Even after gaining serious attention, the Taniyama–Shimura-Weil conjecture was seen by contemporary mathematicians as extraordinarily difficult to prove or perhaps even inaccessible to proof.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singh_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Singh-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:203–205, 223, 226</sup> For example, Wiles' ex-supervisor <a href="/wiki/John_H._Coates" title="John H. Coates">John Coates</a> states that it seemed "impossible to actually prove",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singh_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Singh-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:226</sup> and Ken Ribet considered himself "one of the vast majority of people who believed [it] was completely inaccessible".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singh_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Singh-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:223</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Andrew_Wiles" title="Andrew Wiles">Wiles</a> (<a href="#CITEREFWiles1995">1995</a>), with some help from <a href="/wiki/Richard_Taylor_(mathematician)" title="Richard Taylor (mathematician)">Richard Taylor</a>, proved the Taniyama–Shimura–Weil conjecture for all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semistable_elliptic_curve" title="Semistable elliptic curve">semistable elliptic curves</a>, which he used to prove Fermat's Last Theorem, and the full Taniyama–Shimura–Weil conjecture was finally proved by <a href="#CITEREFDiamond1996">Diamond (1996)</a>, <a href="#CITEREFConradDiamondTaylor1999">Conrad, Diamond &amp; Taylor (1999)</a>, and <a href="#CITEREFBreuilConradDiamondTaylor2001">Breuil et al. (2001)</a> who, building on Wiles' work, incrementally chipped away at the remaining cases until the full result was proved.</p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem" title="Fermat's Last Theorem">Fermat's Last Theorem</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wiles%27_proof_of_Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem" title="Wiles' proof of Fermat's Last Theorem">Wiles' proof of Fermat's Last Theorem</a></div>
<p>Once fully proven, the conjecture became known as the modularity theorem.</p>
<p>Several theorems in number theory similar to Fermat's Last Theorem follow from the modularity theorem. For example: no cube can be written as a sum of two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coprime" title="Coprime">coprime</a> <i>n</i>-th powers, <i>n</i> ≥ 3. (The case <i>n</i> = 3 was already known by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euler" title="Euler">Euler</a>.)</p>
