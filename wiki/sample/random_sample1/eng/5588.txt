<p>The <b>Morava</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>March</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>: <span lang="hu" xml:lang="hu"><i>Morva</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>: <span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl"><i>Morawa</i></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>, a left tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a>. It is the main river of <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a>, which derives its name from it. The river originates on the <a href="/wiki/Kr%C3%A1lick%C3%BD_Sn%C4%9B%C5%BEn%C3%ADk" title="Králický Sněžník">Králický Sněžník</a> mountain in the north-eastern corner of <a href="/wiki/Pardubice_Region" title="Pardubice Region">Pardubice Region</a>, near the border between the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and has a vaguely southward trajectory. The lower part of the river's course forms the border between the Czech Republic and <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a> and then between <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> and Slovakia.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morava_(river)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lowlands formed by the river are the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Upper_Moravian_Vale&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Upper Moravian Vale (page does not exist)">Upper Moravian Vale</a> or <i>Hornomoravský úval</i> and then the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lower_Moravian_Vale&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lower Moravian Vale (page does not exist)">Lower Moravian Vale</a> or <i>Dolnomoravský úval</i> in Moravia, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Field&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Moravian Field (page does not exist)">Moravian Field</a> or <i>Marchfeld</i> in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Austria" title="Lower Austria">Lower Austria</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%A1horie" title="Záhorie">Záhorie</a> Lowland or <i>Záhorská nížina</i> in Slovakia. The latter three are actually continuous parts of one large basin, forming the major part of the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Basin" title="Vienna Basin">Vienna Basin</a>.</p>
<p>In the Czech Republic, there are some larger towns lying upon Morava, particularly <a href="/wiki/Olomouc" title="Olomouc">Olomouc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krom%C4%9B%C5%99%C3%AD%C5%BE" title="Kroměříž">Kroměříž</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otrokovice" title="Otrokovice">Otrokovice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uhersk%C3%A9_Hradi%C5%A1t%C4%9B" title="Uherské Hradiště">Uherské Hradiště</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hodon%C3%ADn" title="Hodonín">Hodonín</a>. Downstream from here, the river flows along sparsely inhabited, forested border area, all the way to its outfall into the Danube, just below the <a href="/wiki/Dev%C3%ADn_Castle" title="Devín Castle">Devín Castle</a> at the outskirts of the Slovak capital <a href="/wiki/Bratislava" title="Bratislava">Bratislava</a>. After 354 km of its course, Morava feeds the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> by an average discharge rate of 120 m<sup>3</sup>/s, gathered from a drainage area of 26 658 km<sup>2</sup>. The Morava river is unusual in that it is a European <a href="/wiki/Blackwater_river" title="Blackwater river">blackwater river</a>.</p>
<p>The river's longest <a href="/wiki/Tributary" title="Tributary">tributary</a> is <i><a href="/wiki/Thaya" title="Thaya">Thaya</a></i> (in German) or <i>Dyje</i> (in Czech), flowing in at the <a href="/wiki/Tripoint" title="Tripoint">tripoint</a> of Austria, the Czech Republic and Slovakia. The biggest tributary from the left is <i><a href="/wiki/Be%C4%8Dva" title="Bečva">Bečva</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_and_history">Naming and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morava_(river)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Naming and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though the German name <i>March</i> may refer to <i>Mark</i>, "border, frontier" (c.f. English <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/march#Etymology_2" title="wikt:march">march</a></i>), the river's name more probably is derived from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> <i>*mori</i>, "waters" (<i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mare#Latin" title="wikt:mare">mare</a></i>). It was first documented as <i>Maraha</i> in an 892 deed.</p>
<p>Indeed, the lower part of the river, downstream of the confluence with the Thaya at <a href="/wiki/Hohenau_an_der_March" title="Hohenau an der March">Hohenau an der March</a>, which today marks the Austro-Slovakian border, is one of the oldest national boundaries still extant in continental Europe: it was the eastern boundary of the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Empire" title="Carolingian Empire">Carolingian Empire</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Avar_Khaganate" title="Avar Khaganate">Avar Khaganate</a> around 800 and from the 10th century onwards marked the border of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Imperial</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Austria" title="Margraviate of Austria">marcha orientalis</a></i>, later <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Austria" title="Duchy of Austria">Duchy of Austria</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Kingdom of Hungary</a> (within the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Monarchy" title="Habsburg Monarchy">Habsburg Monarchy</a> during 1526–1918 due to the imperial expansion of the Austrian lands). At the times of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, this section of the river was part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_curtain" title="Iron curtain">Iron curtain</a>, being the frontier between <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1997_flooding">1997 flooding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morava_(river)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1997 flooding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In July 1997, the Morava basin (especially its northern and eastern part) was affected by heavy stratiform <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">raining</a>, which lasted several days and caused <a href="/wiki/1997_Central_European_flood" title="1997 Central European flood">catastrophic floods</a> also on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oder_River" title="Oder River">Oder River</a> basin in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. In the Czech Republic, 49 people lost their life, more than 250 villages had to be evacuated and the total damage cost 63 billion <a href="/wiki/Czech_koruna" title="Czech koruna">crowns</a>.</p>
