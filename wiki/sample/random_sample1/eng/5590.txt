<p><b>Gyeonggi-do</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a>: 경기도, <small>Korean pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Korean" title="Help:IPA for Korean">[kjʌŋ.ɡi.do]</a></span>) is the most populous <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_South_Korea" title="Administrative divisions of South Korea">province</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>. Its name, <i>Gyeonggi</i> means "the area surrounding capital". Thus <i>Gyeonggi-do</i> can be translated as "province surrounding Seoul". The provincial capital is <a href="/wiki/Suwon" title="Suwon">Suwon</a>. <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>—South Korea's largest city and national capital—is in the heart of the province but has been separately administered as a provincial-level <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_cities_of_South_Korea" title="Special cities of South Korea"><i>special city</i></a> since 1946. <a href="/wiki/Incheon" title="Incheon">Incheon</a>—South Korea's third largest city—is on the coast of the province and has been similarly administered as a provincial-level <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_cities_of_South_Korea" title="Special cities of South Korea"><i>metropolitan city</i></a> since 1981. The three jurisdictions collectively cover 11,730 km<sup>2</sup>, with a combined population of 25.6 million—amounting to over half of the entire population of South Korea.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeonggi_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gyeonggi Province has been a politically important area since 18 BCE, when Korea was divided into three nations during the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms_of_Korea" title="Three Kingdoms of Korea">Three Kingdoms</a> period. Ever since <a href="/wiki/Onjo_of_Baekje" title="Onjo of Baekje">King Onjo</a>, the founder of <a href="/wiki/Baekje" title="Baekje">Baekje</a> (one of the three kingdoms), founded the government in <a href="/wiki/Wiryeseong" title="Wiryeseong">Wiryeseong</a> of Hanam, the <a href="/wiki/Han_River_(Korea)" title="Han River (Korea)">Han River Valley</a> was absorbed into <a href="/wiki/Goguryeo" title="Goguryeo">Goguryeo</a> in the mid-fifth century, and became <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a>'s territory in the year 553 (the 14th year of <a href="/wiki/Jinheung_of_Silla" title="Jinheung of Silla">King Jinheung</a>). Afterward, the current location of Gyeonggi Province, one of the nine states of <a href="/wiki/Later_Silla" title="Later Silla">Later Silla</a>, was called Hansanju.</p>
<p>The Gyeonggi region started to rise as the central region of <a href="/wiki/Goryeo" title="Goryeo">Goryeo</a> as <a href="/wiki/Taejo_of_Goryeo" title="Taejo of Goryeo">King Taejo of Goryeo</a> (the kingdom following Silla) set up the capital in Gaesong. Since 1018 (the 9th year of Goryeo’s <a href="/wiki/Hyeonjong_of_Goryeo" title="Hyeonjong of Goryeo">King Hyeonjong</a>), this area has been officially called "Gyeonggi."</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gyeonggi-gamyeong-do.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2091" data-file-width="6732" height="78" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Gyeonggi-gamyeong-do.jpg/250px-Gyeonggi-gamyeong-do.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Gyeonggi-gamyeong-do.jpg/375px-Gyeonggi-gamyeong-do.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Gyeonggi-gamyeong-do.jpg/500px-Gyeonggi-gamyeong-do.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gyeonggi-gamyeong-do.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Headquarters of the provincial governor, Joseon Dynasty</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Joseon" title="Joseon">Joseon</a>, which was founded after the Goryeo, <a href="/wiki/Taejo_of_Joseon" title="Taejo of Joseon">King Taejo of Joseon</a> set the capital in Hanyang, while restructuring Gyeonggi's area to include Gwangju, Suwon, Yeoju, and Anseong, along with the southeast region. Since the period of King Taejong and <a href="/wiki/Sejong_the_Great" title="Sejong the Great">Sejong the Great</a>, the Gyeonggi region has been very similar to the current administrative area of Gyeonggi Province.</p>
<p>In 1895 the 23-Bu system, which reorganized administrative areas, was effected. The Gyeonggi region was divided into Hanseong (modern <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>; <i>Hanseong-bu;</i> 한성부; 漢城府), <a href="/wiki/Incheon" title="Incheon">Incheon</a> (<i>Incheon-bu;</i> 인천부; 仁川府), <a href="/wiki/Chungju" title="Chungju">Chungju</a> (<i>Chungju-bu;</i> 충주부; 忠州府), <a href="/wiki/Gongju" title="Gongju">Gongju</a> (<i>Gongju-bu;</i> 공주부; 公州府), and Kaesong (<i>Kaesong-bu;</i> 개성부; 開城府).</p>
<p>During the Japanese colonial period Hanseong-bu was incorporated into Gyeonggi Province. On October 1, 1910, it was renamed Keijo and a provincial government was placed in Keijo according to the reorganization of administrative districts.</p>
<p>After liberation and the foundation of two Korean governments, Gyeonggi Province and its capital, Seoul, were separated with partial regions of Gyeonggi Province being incorporated into Seoul thereafter. Additionally, Kaesong became <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korean</a> territory, the only city to change control after the countries were divided at the <a href="/wiki/38th_parallel_north" title="38th parallel north">38th parallel</a>, which is now part of North Korea's <a href="/wiki/North_Hwanghae_Province" title="North Hwanghae Province">North Hwanghae Province</a>.</p>
<p>In 1967 the seat of the Gyeonggi provincial government was transferred from Seoul to <a href="/wiki/Suwon" title="Suwon">Suwon</a>. After Incheon separated from Gyeonggi Province in 1981, Gyeonggi regions such as <a href="/wiki/Ongjin_County,_Incheon" title="Ongjin County, Incheon">Ongjin County</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ganghwa_County" title="Ganghwa County">Ganghwa County</a> were incorporated into <a href="/wiki/Incheon" title="Incheon">Incheon</a> in 1995.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeonggi_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gyeonggi Province is the western central region of the Korean Peninsula, which is vertically situated in Northeast Asia and is between east longitude of 126 and 127, and north latitude of 36 and 38. Its dimension is 10% of Korea’s territory, 10,171 square kilometres (3,927 sq mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is in contact with 86 kilometres (53 mi) of cease-fire line to the north, 413 kilometres (257 mi) of coastline to the west, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gangwon_Province_(South_Korea)" title="Gangwon Province (South Korea)">Gangwon Province</a> to the east, <a href="/wiki/North_Chungcheong_Province" title="North Chungcheong Province">North Chungcheong Province</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Chungcheong_Province" title="South Chungcheong Province">South Chungcheong Province</a> to the south, and has Seoul, the capital of the Republic of Korea, in its center. Its provincial government is in Suwon, but some of its government buildings are in Uijeongbu for the administrative conveniences of the northern region.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeonggi_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The climate of Gyeonggi Province is the continental climate, which has a severe differentiation of temperature between summer and winter, and has distinctions of four seasons. Spring is warm, summer is hot and humid, autumn is cool, and winter is cold and snowy. The annual average temperature is between 11–13 °C (52–55 °F), where the temperature in the mountainous areas to the northeast is lower and the coastal areas to the southwest is higher. For January’s average temperature, the Gyeonggi Bay is −4 °C (25 °F), the Namhangang (River) Basin is −4 – −6 °C (25–21 °F), and the Bukhangang (River) and Imjingang Basins are −6 – −8 °C (21–18 °F). It becomes colder and higher in temperature differentiation from coastal to inland areas. Summer has a lower local differentiation compared to winter. The inland areas are hotter than the Gyeonggi Bay area, the hottest area is <a href="/wiki/Pyeongtaek" title="Pyeongtaek">Pyeongtaek</a>, making the average temperature of August 26.5 °C (79.7 °F).</p>
<p>The annual average precipitation is around 1,100 millimetres (43 in), with a lot of rainfall. It is rainy in summer and dry during winter. The northeastern inland areas of Bukhangang and the upper stream of Imjingang has a precipitation of 1,300–1,400 millimetres (51–55 in), whereas the coastal area has only 900 millimetres (35 in) of precipitation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature_and_national_parks">Nature and national parks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeonggi_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nature and national parks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The topography of Gyeonggi Province is divided into southern and northern areas by the Han River, which flows from east to west. The area north to the Han River is mainly mountainous, while the southern area is mainly plain.</p>
<p>The configuration of Gyeonggi Province is represented by <i>Dong-go-seo-jeo</i> (high in the east and low in the west), where the <a href="/wiki/Gwangju" title="Gwangju">Gwangju</a> Mountain Range and the Charyeong Mountain Range spreads from the east and drops in elevation in the west. The fields of Gimpo, Gyeonggi, and Pyeongtaek extend to the west.</p>
<p>Gyeonggi Province boasts beautiful nature stocked with rivers, lakes, mountains, and seas. Its representative rivers are the Hangang, Imjingang, and Anseongcheon (Stream), which flow into the Yellow Sea, with Gyeonggi Plain, Yeonbaek Plain and Anseong Plain forming a fertile field area around the rivers. The Gwangju Mountain Range and the Charyeong Mountain Range stretch toward China in Gyeonggi Province. Most of the mountains that rise above 1,000 metres (3,300 ft), such as <a href="/wiki/Myeongjisan" title="Myeongjisan">Myeongjisan</a> (1,267 metres (4,157 ft)), Gukmangbong (1,168 metres (3,832 ft)) and Yongmunsan (1,157 metres (3,796 ft)), are included in the <a href="/wiki/Gwangju" title="Gwangju">Gwangju</a> Mountain Range. It has a developed granite area which, due to the granite’s exfoliation effect, makes it full of strangely shaped cliffs and deep valleys. The Charyeong Mountain Range forms the boundary between Gyeonggi Province and North Chungcheong Province, but is a relatively low-altitude hilly area.</p>
<p>In Gyeonggi Province, there is <a href="/wiki/Bukhansan_National_Park" title="Bukhansan National Park">Bukhansan National Park</a> in Uijeongbu. For provincial parks, there are the Chukryeongsan Natural Recreation Area, Namhan-sanseong Provincial Park, Gapyeong <a href="/wiki/Yeoninsan" title="Yeoninsan">Yeoninsan</a> Provincial Park, and Mulhyanggi Arboretum. Besides the listed, the scenery of well-known mountains including Soyosan of Dongducheon City, Yongmunsan of <a href="/wiki/Yangpyeong_County" title="Yangpyeong County">Yangpyeong County</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gwanaksan" title="Gwanaksan">Gwanaksan</a> of <a href="/wiki/Anyang,_Gyeonggi" title="Anyang, Gyeonggi">Anyang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gwacheon" title="Gwacheon">Gwacheon</a>, along with Hangang and Imjingang are famous tourism sites of Gyeonggi Province.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeonggi_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gyeonggi Province has shown a rapid increase in population due to the modernization and urbanization of the Republic of Korea. Its population has increased from 2,748,765 in 1960 to 3,296,950 in 1970; 4,933,862 in 1980; 6,619,629 in 1992; 8,982,298 in 2000; and 12,071,884 in 2010. In 2010 there were 4,527,282 households, with an average of 3 people per family. There were 6,112,339 males and 5,959,545 females. The population density was 1119 people/km<sup>2</sup>, almost double the national average of 486 people/km<sup>2</sup>.</p>
<p>Excluding the two metropolitan cities (Seoul and Incheon), the most heavily populated area as of 2010 is <a href="/wiki/Suwon" title="Suwon">Suwon</a> (1,104,681) followed by <a href="/wiki/Seongnam" title="Seongnam">Seongnam</a> (996,524), <a href="/wiki/Goyang" title="Goyang">Goyang</a> (962,297), <a href="/wiki/Yongin" title="Yongin">Yongin</a> (891,708), <a href="/wiki/Bucheon" title="Bucheon">Bucheon</a> (890,875) and <a href="/wiki/Ansan" title="Ansan">Ansan</a> (753,862). The lowest populated area in 2010 was <a href="/wiki/Yeoncheon_County" title="Yeoncheon County">Yeoncheon County</a> (45,973) followed by <a href="/wiki/Gapyeong_County" title="Gapyeong County">Gapyeong County</a> (59,916) and <a href="/wiki/Gapyeong_County" title="Gapyeong County">Gapyeong County</a> (72,595).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeonggi_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the backbone of Seoul in the means of manufacturing complex, Gyeonggi Province is evenly developed in heavy industry (electronics, machine, heavy and chemical industry, steel), light industry (textile), and farm, livestock and fisheries industry. Due to the influence of recent high wages, the weight of manufacturing industries has decreased in Korea’s economy. Gyeonggi Province is making efforts in many ways to improve and modernize the conventional industry structure. Gyeonggi Province is unsparingly investing in the promotion of service industries related to soft competitive power such as state-of-the-art IT industry, designing, conventions and tourism, along with its great leap as a commercial hub in Northeast Asia using the Pyeongtaek Harbor.</p>
<p>Besides this, it is famous for its special local products such as Icheon rice and Icheon/Gwangju ceramics. The manufacturing base of global IT corporations that represent Korea, such as Suwon <a href="/wiki/Samsung" title="Samsung">Samsung</a> Semi-conductor, <a href="/wiki/Paju" title="Paju">Paju</a>'s <a href="/wiki/LG_Corporation" title="LG Corporation">LG Corporation</a> LCD Complex, and Icheon's <a href="/wiki/SK_Hynix" title="SK Hynix">SK Hynix</a> are in Gyeonggi Province.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeonggi_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gyeonggi Province's proximity to Seoul, Korea's capital, and Incheon, its second busiest port, has contributed to its extremely well-developed transportation infrastructure. It is close to both <a href="/wiki/Incheon_International_Airport" title="Incheon International Airport">Incheon International Airport</a>, South Korea's main international gateway and busiest airport, and <a href="/wiki/Gimpo_International_Airport" title="Gimpo International Airport">Gimpo International Airport</a>, its second busiest airport. Use of water transportation from the harbor at <a href="/wiki/Pyeongtaek" title="Pyeongtaek">Pyeongtaek</a> is also high.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Road">Road</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeonggi_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Road">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The road pavement rate throughout the province averages 86.5 percent. The area has access to many of <a href="/wiki/Expressways_in_South_Korea" title="Expressways in South Korea">South Korea's expressways</a>, including</p>

<li>No. 35 <a href="/wiki/Jungbu_Expressway" title="Jungbu Expressway">Jungbu Expressway</a>, Seoul–<a href="/wiki/Tongyeong" title="Tongyeong">Tongyeong</a></li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail">Rail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeonggi_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gyeonggi is served by <a href="/wiki/Korail" title="Korail">Korail</a> commuter, standard and high-speed (<a href="/wiki/Korea_Train_Express" title="Korea Train Express">KTX</a>) services. It is home to Korea's first railroad, the <a href="/wiki/Gyeongin_Line" title="Gyeongin Line">Gyeongin Line</a>, and includes portions of the <a href="/wiki/Gyeongbu_Line" title="Gyeongbu Line">Gyeongbu Line</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gyeongui_Line" title="Gyeongui Line">Gyeongui Line</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jungang_Line" title="Jungang Line">Jungang Line</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Honam_Line" title="Honam Line">Honam Line</a>. Gyeonggi has stations on the <a href="/wiki/Suin_Line" title="Suin Line">Suin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bundang_Line" title="Bundang Line">Bundang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gyeongchun_Line" title="Gyeongchun Line">Gyeongchun</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shinbundang_Line" title="Shinbundang Line">Shinbundang</a> <a href="/wiki/Commuter_rail" title="Commuter rail">commuter rail</a> services and the <a href="/wiki/Gyeongbu_High_Speed_Railway" title="Gyeongbu High Speed Railway">Gyeongbu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Honam_High_Speed_Railway" title="Honam High Speed Railway">Honam High Speed Railways</a>.</p>
<p>The area has numerous connections to the <a href="/wiki/Seoul_Metropolitan_Subway" title="Seoul Metropolitan Subway">Seoul Metropolitan Subway</a> system. <a href="/wiki/Seoul_Subway_Line_1" title="Seoul Subway Line 1">Seoul Subway Line 1</a> (formerly Korea National Railroad of Seoul) extends to Cheonan past Gyeonggi Province to the southwest, and to Dongducheon to the north. <a href="/wiki/Seoul_Subway_Line_3" title="Seoul Subway Line 3">Line 3</a> connects to Goyang to the north, while <a href="/wiki/Seoul_Subway_Line_4" title="Seoul Subway Line 4">Line 4</a> is connected to Gwacheon and Ansan to the southwest, and <a href="/wiki/Seoul_Subway_Line_7" title="Seoul Subway Line 7">Line 7</a> is connected to Uijeongbu to the north and Gwangmyeong to the south. <a href="/wiki/Seoul_Subway_Line_8" title="Seoul Subway Line 8">Line 8</a> is extended to Seongnam to the south.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Uijeongbu" title="Uijeongbu">Uijeongbu</a> has its own light rail system, the <a href="/wiki/U_Line" title="U Line">U Line</a>, which connects to Line 1.</p>
<p>A portion of the <a href="/wiki/AREX" title="AREX">AREX</a> line between the Gimpo and Incheon airports passes through Gyeonggi, but there are no stops within the province.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeonggi_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gyeonggi is actively investing in education to foster a talented population suitable for the globalized economy. It is promoting the opening of local campuses of reputable universities as well as establishing special purpose high schools for high-quality education. It has also founded and operates at <a href="/wiki/Paju" title="Paju">Paju</a> the largest domestic "English village" for education in the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a>, as well as villages in Ansan and Yangpyeong.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeonggi_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_landmarks">Historical landmarks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeonggi_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Historical landmarks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gyeonggi Province has long been a capital area, leaving many historic relics and ruins. For royal tombs (called reung), there are Donggureung of <a href="/wiki/Guri" title="Guri">Guri</a>, and Gwangreung, Hongreung and Yureung of <a href="/wiki/Namyangju" title="Namyangju">Namyangju</a>. For castles (called seong), there are <a href="/wiki/Hwaseong_Fortress" title="Hwaseong Fortress">Suwon Hwaseong</a>, which is designated as the World Cultural Heritage, Namwonsanseong, Haengjusanseong, and Ganghwasanseong. For Buddhist temples, there are many aged temples within Gyeonggi Province where one can experience ‘temple stay’. You can view folk culture in the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Folk_Village" title="Korean Folk Village">Korean Folk Village</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yongin" title="Yongin">Yongin</a>, and the scene of Korea’s division at <a href="/wiki/Panmunjom" title="Panmunjom">Panmunjom</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paju" title="Paju">Paju</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Performing_arts">Performing arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeonggi_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Performing arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gyeonggi Province is investing a lot of money at a provincial level so that people do not have to go to Seoul to enjoy a high-class cultural life. There are performances at Gyeonggi Arts Center in Suwon as well as at Gyeonggi Korean Traditional Music Center in <a href="/wiki/Yongin" title="Yongin">Yongin</a>. Gyeonggi Provincial Museum in <a href="/wiki/Yongin" title="Yongin">Yongin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nam_June_Paik" title="Nam June Paik">Nam June Paik</a> Art Center in <a href="/wiki/Yongin" title="Yongin">Yongin</a>, Gyeonggi Museum of Art in <a href="/wiki/Ansan" title="Ansan">Ansan</a>, and the Ceramics Museum in <a href="/wiki/Gwangju" title="Gwangju">Gwangju</a> are some of the facilities that are currently run by the province. There are also sightseeing opportunities at Jangheung Art Park, Publication Art Complex at Heyri, <a href="/wiki/Paju" title="Paju">Paju</a>, and the Icheon Ceramics Exposition.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heyri_Art_Valley">Heyri Art Valley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeonggi_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Heyri Art Valley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Heyri_Art_Valley" title="Heyri Art Valley">Heyri Art Valley</a> is Korea's largest art town. Various Korean artists constructed the cultural town of Heyri and it features seberal art galleries and museums there are about 40 museums, exhibitions, concert halls and bookstores.</p>

<p>According to the census of 2005, of the people of Gyeonggi 34.3% follow <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> (21.9% <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a> and 12.4% <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholicism</a>) and 16.8% follow <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2005religionmap_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-2005religionmap-4">[4]</a></sup> 51.1% of the population is mostly not religious or follow <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> or indigenous religions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeonggi_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 2002 Korea-Japan World Cup matches were held in <a href="/wiki/Suwon_World_Cup_Stadium" title="Suwon World Cup Stadium">Suwon World Cup Stadium</a>. As for the professional soccer teams with Gyeonggi Province as their home ground, there are the <a href="/wiki/Suwon_Samsung_Bluewings" title="Suwon Samsung Bluewings">Suwon Samsung Bluewings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seongnam_FC" title="Seongnam FC">Seongnam FC</a>. Also, there is the professional basketball team of <a href="/wiki/Guri" title="Guri">Guri</a> Kdb Life Winnus, sponsored by Kdb Life Insurance.</p>
<p>Korea's foremost thoroughbred horse racing track <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seoul_Race_Park" title="Seoul Race Park">Seoul Race Park</a> is in <a href="/wiki/Gwacheon" title="Gwacheon">Gwacheon</a>.</p>
<p>Sailing is a high-profile sport in Gyeonggi. Gyeonggi-do's location on the bank of the river Han makes it an ideal venue for the sport which is host to the prestigious Korea Match Cup event which is a part of the <a href="/wiki/World_Match_Racing_Tour" title="World Match Racing Tour">World Match Racing Tour</a>. The event draws the world's best sailing teams to Gyeonggi in a gladiatorial battle of nerve and skill on the water. The identical supplied (KM-36) boats are raced two at a time in an on the water dogfight which tests the sailors and skippers to the limits of their physical abilities. Points accrued count towards the World Match Race Tour and a place in the final event, with the overall winner taking the title <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Sailing_Federation" title="International Sailing Federation">ISAF</a> World Match Racing Tour Champion. Match racing is an ideal sport for spectators in Gyeonggi. Racing in such close proximity to the river bank provides excellent heart of the action views for the audience. Highlights of the event can be seen on <a href="/wiki/KBS_World" title="KBS World">KBS World</a> television and via the official World Match Racing Tour website.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gourmet">Gourmet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeonggi_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Gourmet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
