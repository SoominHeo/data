<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hourglass</h1>

<p>An <b>hourglass</b> (or <b>sandglass</b>, <b>sand timer</b>, <b>sand watch</b>, or <b>sand clock</b>) is a mechanical device used to measure the passage of <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>. It comprises two <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> bulbs connected vertically by a narrow neck that allows a regulated trickle of material (historically <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>) from the upper bulb to the lower one. Factors affecting the time interval measured include the sand quantity, the sand coarseness, the bulb size, and the neck width. Hourglasses may be reused indefinitely by inverting the bulbs once the upper bulb is empty.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hourglass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity">Antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hourglass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peleus-Thetis-350.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="377" data-file-width="640" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Peleus-Thetis-350.jpg/220px-Peleus-Thetis-350.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Peleus-Thetis-350.jpg/330px-Peleus-Thetis-350.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Peleus-Thetis-350.jpg/440px-Peleus-Thetis-350.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peleus-Thetis-350.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Sarcophagus" title="Sarcophagus">Sarcophagus</a> dated c. 350 AD, representing the wedding of Peleus and Thetis (observe the magnification with the hourglass held by Morpheus in his hands)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The origin of the hourglass is unclear. Its predecessor the <a href="/wiki/Water_clock" title="Water clock">clepsydra</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Water_clock" title="Water clock">water clock</a>, may have been invented in ancient India.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> According to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=American_Institute_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="American Institute of New York (page does not exist)">American Institute of New York</a>, the clepsammia or sand-glass was invented at Alexandria about 150 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-York1870_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-York1870-1">[1]</a></sup> According to the <i>Journal of the British Archaeological Association</i> the so-called clepsammia were in use before the time of St. Jerome (335 AD), and the first representation of an hourglass is in a sarcophagus dated c. 350 AD,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Association1873_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Association1873-2">[2]</a></sup> representing the wedding of Peleus and Thetis, discovered in Rome in the 18th century, and studied by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Joachim_Winckelmann" title="Johann Joachim Winckelmann">Winckelmann</a> in the 19th century, who remarked the hourglass held by Morpheus in his hands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Museum1810_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Museum1810-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reappearance_in_the_Early_Middle_Ages">Reappearance in the Early Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hourglass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reappearance in the Early Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ambrogio_Lorenzetti_002-detail-Temperance.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1384" data-file-width="969" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Ambrogio_Lorenzetti_002-detail-Temperance.jpg/220px-Ambrogio_Lorenzetti_002-detail-Temperance.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Ambrogio_Lorenzetti_002-detail-Temperance.jpg/330px-Ambrogio_Lorenzetti_002-detail-Temperance.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Ambrogio_Lorenzetti_002-detail-Temperance.jpg/440px-Ambrogio_Lorenzetti_002-detail-Temperance.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ambrogio_Lorenzetti_002-detail-Temperance.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Temperance_(virtue)" title="Temperance (virtue)">Temperance</a> bearing an hourglass; detail Lorenzetti's <i>Allegory of Good Government</i>, 1338</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are no records of the hourglass existing in Europe prior to the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a>, such as invention by the Ancient Greeks; the first supported evidences appears from the 8th century AD, crafted by a <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Frankish</a> monk named Luitprand who served at the cathedral in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chartres,_France" title="Chartres, France">Chartres, France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britten_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Britten-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mills_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mills-6">[6]</a></sup> But it was not until the 14th century that the hourglass was seen commonly, the earliest firm evidence being a depiction in the 1338 fresco <i>Allegory of Good Government</i> by <a href="/wiki/Ambrogio_Lorenzetti" title="Ambrogio Lorenzetti">Ambrogio Lorenzetti</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Use of the marine sandglass has been recorded since the 14th century. The written records about it were mostly from logbooks of European ships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mills_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mills-6">[6]</a></sup> In the same period it appears in other records and lists of ships stores. The earliest recorded reference that can be said with certainty to refer to a marine sandglass dates from c. 1345, in a receipt of Thomas de Stetesham, clerk of the King's ship <i>La George</i>, in the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_III" title="Edward III">Edward III</a> of England; translated from the Latin, the receipt says: in 1345:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner1993_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Turner1993-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stetesham_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stetesham-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"The same Thomas accounts to have paid at Lescluse, in Flanders, for twelve glass <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horologe" title="Horologe">horologes</a> (" pro xii. orlogiis vitreis "), price of each 4½ gross', in sterling 9<i>s.</i> Item, For four horologes of the same sort (" de eadem secta "), bought there, price of each five gross', making in sterling 3<i>s.</i> 4<i>d.</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britten_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Britten-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner1993_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Turner1993-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stetesham_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stetesham-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marine_sandglass" title="Marine sandglass">Marine sandglasses</a> were very popular on board ships, as they were the most dependable measurement of time while at sea. Unlike the clepsydra, the motion of the ship while sailing did not affect the hourglass. The fact that the hourglass also used granular materials instead of liquids gave it more accurate measurements, as the clepsydra was prone to get condensation inside it during temperature changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmer_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Balmer-10">[10]</a></sup> Seamen found that the hourglass was able to help them determine <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a>, distance east or west from a certain point, with reasonable accuracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmer_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Balmer-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hourglass also found popularity on land. As the use of mechanical clocks to indicate the times of events like church services became more common, creating a "need to keep track of time", the demand for time-measuring devices increased. Hourglasses were essentially inexpensive, as they required no rare technology to make and their contents were not hard to come by, and as the manufacturing of these instruments became more common, their uses became more practical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmer_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Balmer-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hourglasses were commonly seen in use in churches, homes, and work places to measure sermons, cooking time, and time spent on breaks from labor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mills_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mills-6">[6]</a></sup> Because they were being used for more everyday tasks, the model of the hourglass began to shrink. The smaller models were more practical and very popular as they made timing more discreet.</p>
<p>After 1500, the hourglass was not as widespread as it had been. This was due to the development of the mechanical clock, which became more accurate, smaller and cheaper, and made keeping time easier.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The hourglass, however, did not disappear entirely. Although they became relatively less useful as clock technology advanced, hourglasses remained desirable in their design. The oldest known surviving hourglass resides in the British Museum in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mills_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Mills-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Not until the 18th century did John Harrison and his son James, come up with a <a href="/wiki/Marine_chronometer" title="Marine chronometer">marine chronometer</a> that significantly improved on the stability of the hourglass at sea. Taking elements from the design logic behind the hourglass, they made a marine chronometer in 1761 that was able to accurately measure the journey from England to Jamaica accurate within five seconds.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hourglass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Little written evidence exists to explain why its external form is the shape that it is. The glass bulbs used, however, have changed in style and design over time. While the main designs have always been <a href="/wiki/Ampoule" title="Ampoule">ampoule</a> in shape, the bulbs were not always connected. The first hourglasses were two separate bulbs with a cord wrapped at their union that was then coated in wax to hold the piece together and let sand flow in between.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hourglass_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hourglass-11">[11]</a></sup> It was not until 1760 that both bulbs were blown together to keep moisture out of the bulbs and regulate the pressure within the bulb that varied the flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balmer_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Balmer-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Material">Material</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hourglass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Material">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While some hourglasses actually did use sand as the granular mixture to measure time, many did not use sand at all. The material used in most bulbs was a combination of "powdered marble, tin/lead oxides, and pulverized, burnt eggshell".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mills_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Mills-6">[6]</a></sup> Over time, different textures of granule matter were tested to see which gave the most constant flow within the bulbs. It was later discovered that for the perfect flow to be achieved the ratio of granule bead to the width of the bulb neck needed to be 1/12 or more but not greater than 1/2 the neck of the bulb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_uses">Practical uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hourglass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Practical uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:82px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Egg_timer.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1410" data-file-width="395" height="286" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Egg_timer.jpg/80px-Egg_timer.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Egg_timer.jpg/120px-Egg_timer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Egg_timer.jpg/160px-Egg_timer.jpg 2x" width="80"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Egg_timer.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
3-minute egg timer</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Hourglasses were an early dependable, reusable and accurate measure of time. The rate of flow of the sand is independent of the depth in the upper reservoir, and the instrument will not freeze in cold weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mills_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Mills-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the 15th century onwards, they were being used in a range of applications at sea, in the church, in industry and in cookery.</p>
<p>During the voyage of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Magellan" title="Ferdinand Magellan">Ferdinand Magellan</a> around the globe, 18 hourglasses from Barcelona were in the ship's inventory, after the trip being authorized by emperor Charles V.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hakluyt_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hakluyt-13">[13]</a></sup> It was the job of a ship's page to turn the hourglasses and thus provide the times for the ship's log. Noon was the reference time for navigation, which did not depend on the glass, as the sun would be at its <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">zenith</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bergreen_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bergreen-14">[14]</a></sup> A number of sandglasses could be fixed in a common frame, each with a different operating time, e.g. as in a four-way Italian sandglass likely from the 17th century, in the collections of the Science Museum, in South Kensington, London, which could measure intervals of quarter, half, three-quarters, and one hour (and which were also used in churches, for priests and ministers to measure lengths of sermons).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SciMuseum4way_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-SciMuseum4way-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_practical_uses">Modern practical uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hourglass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modern practical uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Budapest_timewheel_02.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1307" height="269" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Budapest_timewheel_02.jpg/220px-Budapest_timewheel_02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Budapest_timewheel_02.jpg/330px-Budapest_timewheel_02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Budapest_timewheel_02.jpg/440px-Budapest_timewheel_02.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Budapest_timewheel_02.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Timewheel" title="Timewheel">Timewheel</a> in <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>, Hungary.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>While they are no longer widely used for keeping time, some institutions do maintain them. Both houses of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Parliament" title="Australian Parliament">Australian Parliament</a> use three hourglasses to <a href="/wiki/Parliament_House,_Canberra#The_House_of_Representatives" title="Parliament House, Canberra">time certain procedures</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Division_(vote)#Australia" title="Division (vote)">divisions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansard_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hansard-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sandglass is still widely used as the kitchen <a href="/wiki/Egg_timer" title="Egg timer">egg timer</a>; for cooking eggs, a three-minute timer is typical,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herbst_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herbst-17">[17]</a></sup> hence the name "egg timer" for three-minute hourglasses. Egg timers are sold widely as souvenirs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Sand timers are also sometimes used in games such as <a href="/wiki/Pictionary" title="Pictionary">Pictionary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boggle" title="Boggle">Boggle</a> to implement a time constraint on rounds of play.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbolic_uses">Symbolic uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hourglass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Symbolic uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_Christopher_Moody.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="524" data-file-width="699" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Flag_of_Christopher_Moody.svg/220px-Flag_of_Christopher_Moody.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Flag_of_Christopher_Moody.svg/330px-Flag_of_Christopher_Moody.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Flag_of_Christopher_Moody.svg/440px-Flag_of_Christopher_Moody.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_Christopher_Moody.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Pirate <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Moody" title="Christopher Moody">Christopher Moody</a>'s "Bloody Red" jack, c. 1714</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Unlike most other methods of measuring time, the hourglass concretely represents the present as being between the <a href="/wiki/Past" title="Past">past</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Future" title="Future">future</a>, and this has made it an enduring symbol of time itself.</p>
<p>The hourglass, sometimes with the addition of metaphorical wings, is often depicted as a symbol that human existence is fleeting, and that the "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Sands_of_time" title="wiktionary:Sands of time">sands of time</a>" will run out for every human life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brewer_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brewer-18">[18]</a></sup> It was used thus on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pirate" title="Pirate">pirate</a> flags, to strike fear into the hearts of the pirates' victims. In England, hourglasses were sometimes placed in coffins,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ewbank_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ewbank-19">[19]</a></sup> and they have graced gravestones for centuries. The hourglass was also used in <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemy</a> as a symbol for <a href="/wiki/Hour" title="Hour">hour</a>.</p>
<p>The former <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Greenwich" title="Metropolitan Borough of Greenwich">Metropolitan Borough of Greenwich</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> used an hourglass on its coat of arms, symbolising Greenwich's role as the origin of <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Mean_Time" title="Greenwich Mean Time">GMT</a>. The district's successor, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Borough_of_Greenwich" title="Royal Borough of Greenwich">Royal Borough of Greenwich</a>, uses two hourglasses on its <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_the_Royal_Borough_of_Greenwich" title="Coat of arms of the Royal Borough of Greenwich">coat of arms</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_symbolic_uses">Modern symbolic uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hourglass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Modern symbolic uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cursor-design1-hourglass.svg" title="Hourglass cursor"><img alt="Hourglass cursor" data-file-height="64" data-file-width="64" height="64" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Cursor-design1-hourglass.svg/64px-Cursor-design1-hourglass.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Cursor-design1-hourglass.svg/96px-Cursor-design1-hourglass.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Cursor-design1-hourglass.svg/128px-Cursor-design1-hourglass.svg.png 2x" width="64"/></a></div>
<p>Recognition of the hourglass as a symbol of time has survived its obsolescence as a timekeeper. For example, the American television soap opera <i><a href="/wiki/Days_of_Our_Lives" title="Days of Our Lives">Days of Our Lives</a></i>, since its first broadcast in 1965, has displayed an hourglass in its opening credits, with the narration, "Like sands through the hourglass, so are the days of our lives," spoken by <a href="/wiki/Macdonald_Carey" title="Macdonald Carey">Macdonald Carey</a>.</p>
<p>Various computer <a href="/wiki/Graphical_user_interface" title="Graphical user interface">graphical user interfaces</a> may change the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pointer_(computing_WIMP)" title="Pointer (computing WIMP)">pointer</a> to an <a href="/wiki/Windows_wait_cursor" title="Windows wait cursor">hourglass</a> during a period when the program is in the middle of a task, and may not accept user input. During that period other programs, for example in different windows, may work normally. When such an hourglass does not disappear, it suggests a program is in an <a href="/wiki/Infinite_loop" title="Infinite loop">infinite loop</a> and needs to be terminated, or is waiting for some external event (such as the user inserting a CD). <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> has an <small>HOURGLASS</small> symbol at <code>U+231B</code> (⌛).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hourglass_motif">Hourglass motif</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hourglass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hourglass motif">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hourglass-cross.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="300" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c2/Hourglass-cross.png/220px-Hourglass-cross.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/c2/Hourglass-cross.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hourglass-cross.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Diagram of "hourglass" motif from carved stone tablet, <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Because of its symmetry, graphic signs resembling an hourglass are seen in the art of cultures which never encountered such objects. Vertical pairs of triangles joined at the apex are common in <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native American</a> art; both in North America,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> where it can represent, for example, the body of the <a href="/wiki/Thunderbird_(mythology)" title="Thunderbird (mythology)">Thunderbird</a> or (in more elongated form) an enemy scalp,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and in South America, where it is believed to represent a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chuncho" title="Chuncho">Chuncho</a> jungle dweller.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Zulu_people" title="Zulu people">Zulu</a> textiles they symbolise a married man, as opposed to a pair of triangles joined at the base, which symbolise a married woman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> examples can be seen among Spanish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cave_paintings" title="Cave paintings">cave paintings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Observers have even given the name "hourglass motif" to shapes which have more complex symmetry, such as a repeating circle and cross pattern from the <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_Region_of_Bougainville" title="Autonomous Region of Bougainville">Solomon Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Both the members of Project Tic Toc,from television series the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_Tunnel" title="Time Tunnel">Time Tunnel</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Challengers_of_the_Unknown" title="Challengers of the Unknown">Challengers of the Unknown</a> use symbols of the hourglasse representing either time travel or time running out.</p>
