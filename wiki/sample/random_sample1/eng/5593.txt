<p>A <b>moral panic</b> is a feeling of <a href="/wiki/Fear" title="Fear">fear</a> spread among a large number of people that some evil threatens the well-being of society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>A Dictionary of Sociology</i> defines a moral panic as "the process of arousing social concern over an issue – usually the work of <a href="/wiki/Moral_entrepreneur" title="Moral entrepreneur">moral entrepreneurs</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">mass media</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_Uni_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_Uni-3">[3]</a></sup> The media are key players in the dissemination of moral indignation, even when they do not appear to be consciously engaged in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crusading" title="Crusading">crusading</a> or <a href="/wiki/Muckraker" title="Muckraker">muckraking</a>. Simply reporting the facts can be enough to generate concern, anxiety, or panic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cohen_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-cohen-4">[4]</a></sup> Stanley Cohen states that moral panic is “a condition, episode, person or group of persons emerges to become defined as a threat to societal values and interests.” Examples of Moral Panic include the belief in widespread abduction of children by predatory hebephiles or paedophiles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Extein_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Extein-7">[7]</a></sup> belief in <a href="/wiki/Satanic_ritual_abuse" title="Satanic ritual abuse">ritual abuse of women and children by satanic cults</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goode_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goode-8">[8]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/War_on_Drugs" title="War on Drugs">War on Drugs</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DR_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-DR-9">[9]</a></sup> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Health" title="Public Health">Public Health</a> Issues.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_as_a_social_science_term">Use as a social science term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moral_panic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Use as a social science term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marshall_McLuhan" title="Marshall McLuhan">Marshall McLuhan</a> gave the term academic treatment in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Understanding_Media" title="Understanding Media">Understanding Media</a></i>, written in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Cohen_(sociologist)" title="Stanley Cohen (sociologist)">Stanley Cohen</a>, author of a sociological study about youth culture and media called <i>Folk Devils and Moral Panics</i> (1972),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> a moral panic occurs when "...[a] condition, episode, person or group of persons emerges to become defined as a threat to <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">societal values</a> and interests".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cohen_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-cohen-4">[4]</a></sup> Those who start the panic when they fear a threat to prevailing social or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cultural" title="Cultural">cultural</a> values are known by researchers as <i><a href="/wiki/Moral_entrepreneur" title="Moral entrepreneur">moral entrepreneurs</a></i>, while people who supposedly threaten the social order have been described as '<a href="/wiki/Folk_devil" title="Folk devil">folk devils</a>'.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_vs_American">British vs American</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moral_panic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: British vs American">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many sociologists have pointed out the differences between definitions of a <i>moral panic</i> for American and British sociologists. In addition to pointing out other sociologists who note the distinction, Kenneth Thompson has characterized the difference as American sociologists tending to emphasize psychological factors while the British portray <i>moral panics</i> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crisis_of_capitalism" title="Crisis of capitalism">crises of capitalism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>British criminologist <a href="/wiki/Jock_Young" title="Jock Young">Jock Young</a> used the term in his participant observation study of drug taking in Porthmadog between 1967 and 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In <i>Policing the Crisis: Mugging, the State and Law and Order</i> (1978), <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Hall_(cultural_theorist)" title="Stuart Hall (cultural theorist)">Stuart Hall</a> and his colleagues studied the public reaction to the phenomenon of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mugging_(robbery)" title="Mugging (robbery)">mugging</a> and the perception that it had recently been imported from American culture into the UK. Employing Cohen's definition of <i>moral panic,</i> Hall <i>et al.</i> theorized that the "...rising crime rate equation..." performs an ideological function relating to <a href="/wiki/Social_control" title="Social control">social control</a>. <a href="/wiki/Crime_statistics" title="Crime statistics">Crime statistics</a>, in Hall's view, are often manipulated for political and economic purposes; moral panics could thereby be ignited to create public support for the need to "...police the crisis." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stages">Stages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moral_panic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Stages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Cohen_(sociologist)" title="Stanley Cohen (sociologist)">Stanley Cohen</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> often considered the researcher that first coined the term "moral panic", there are five key stages in the construction of a moral panic:</p>
<ol>
<li>Someone, something or a group are defined as a threat to social norms or community interests;</li>
<li>The threat is then depicted in a simple and recognizable symbol/form by the media;</li>
<li>The portrayal of this symbol rouses public concern;</li>
<li>There is a response from authorities and policy makers;</li>
<li>The moral panic over the issue results in social changes within the community.</li>
</ol>
<p>An example of the five stages of moral panics is the conflict of <a href="/wiki/Mods_and_rockers" title="Mods and rockers"><b>Mods and Rockers</b></a> of the early to mid-1960s and 1970s.</p>
<p>In 1971 Stanley Cohen investigated a series of "moral panics." Cohen (1980) used the term "moral panic" to characterize the reactions of the media, the public, and agents of social control to youth disturbances. This work, involving the <a href="/wiki/Mods_and_rockers" title="Mods and rockers">Mods and Rockers</a>, demonstrated how agents of social control amplified deviance. According to Cohen (1980), these groups were labeled as being outside the central core values of consensual society and as posing a threat to both the values of society and society itself, hence the term "folk devils."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mass_media">Mass media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moral_panic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mass media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <i>Folk Devils and Moral Panics</i>, the concept of ‘moral panic’ were linked to certain assumption about the mass media. Stanley Cohen illustrate that the mass media are the primary source of the public’s knowledge about deviance and social problems. In addition, Stanley Cohen claimed that moral panic ‘cause’ folk devil by labeling more actions and people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The media appear in any or all three roles in moral panic dramas:</p>
<ul>
<li>Setting the agenda – selecting those deviant or socially problematic events deemed as newsworthy, then using finer filters to select which events are candidates for moral panic.</li>
<li>Transmitting the images– transmitting the claims, by the using the rhetoric of moral panics;</li>
<li>Breaking the silence, and making the claim</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moral_panic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Moral panics have several distinct features (many of which are discredited in the sociological literature). According to Goode and Ben-Yehuda, moral panic consists of the following characteristics:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goode_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goode-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Concern – There must be belief that the behaviour of the group or category in question is likely to have a negative effect on society;</li>
<li>Hostility – Hostility toward the group in question increases, and they become 'folk devils'. A clear division forms between 'them' and 'us';</li>
<li>Consensus – Though concern does not have to be nationwide, there must be widespread acceptance that the group in question poses a very real threat to society. It is important at this stage that the 'moral entrepreneurs' are vocal and the 'folk devils' appear weak and disorganized;</li>
<li>Disproportionality – The action taken is disproportionate to the actual threat posed by the accused group;</li>
<li>Volatility – Moral panics are highly volatile and tend to disappear as quickly as they appeared due to a wane in public interest or news reports changing to another narrative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s.E2.80.931990s:_Satanic_ritual_abuse">1980s–1990s: Satanic ritual abuse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moral_panic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 1980s–1990s: Satanic ritual abuse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>At various times <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fantasy_role-playing_game" title="Fantasy role-playing game">fantasy role-playing games</a> have been accused of promoting such practices as <a href="/wiki/Satanism" title="Satanism">Satanism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Witchcraft" title="Witchcraft">witchcraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pornography" title="Pornography">pornography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a>. In the 1980s and later, some groups, especially <a href="/wiki/Fundamentalism" title="Fundamentalism">fundamentalist</a> Christian ones, accused the games of encouraging interest in <a href="/wiki/Magic_(paranormal)" title="Magic (paranormal)">sorcery</a> and the veneration of <a href="/wiki/Demon" title="Demon">demons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-panic_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-panic-28">[28]</a></sup> Throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_roleplaying_games" title="History of roleplaying games">history of roleplaying games</a>, many of these criticisms have been aimed specifically at <i>Dungeons &amp; Dragons</i>, but touch on the genre of <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roleplaying_game" title="Roleplaying game">roleplaying games</a> as a whole.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s.E2.80.93present:_Video_games_and_violence">1970s–present: Video games and violence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moral_panic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 1970s–present: Video games and violence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_game_controversy" title="Video game controversy">Video game controversy</a></div>
<p>There have been calls to regulate violence in <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a> for nearly as long as the video game industry has existed, with <i><a href="/wiki/Death_Race_(1976_video_game)" title="Death Race (1976 video game)">Death Race</a></i> a notable early example.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-byrd_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-byrd-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kocurek_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-kocurek-30">[30]</a></sup> In the 1990s, however, improvements in video game technology allowed for more lifelike depictions of violence in games like <i><a href="/wiki/Mortal_Kombat" title="Mortal Kombat">Mortal Kombat</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doom_(game)" title="Doom (game)">Doom</a></i>. The industry attracted controversy over violent content and concerns about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_effects" title="Media effects">effects</a> they might have on players, generating frequent media stories drawing connections between video games and violent behavior as well as a number of academic studies reporting conflicting findings about the strength of correlations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-byrd_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-byrd-29">[29]</a></sup> According to Christopher Ferguson, sensationalist media reports and the scientific community unintentionally worked together in "promoting an unreasonable fear of violent video games".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scotus_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-scotus-31">[31]</a></sup> Concerns from parts of the public about violent games led to cautionary, often exaggerated news stories, warnings from politicians and other public figures, and calls for research to prove the connection, which in turn led to studies "speaking beyond the available data and allowing the promulgation of extreme claims without the usual scientific caution and skepticism."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scotus_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-scotus-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the 1990s there have been attempts to regulate violent video games in the United States through congressional bills as well as within the industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-byrd_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-byrd-29">[29]</a></sup> Public concern and media coverage of violent video games reached a high point following the <a href="/wiki/Columbine_High_School_massacre" title="Columbine High School massacre">Columbine High School massacre</a> in 1999, after which videos were found of the perpetrators talking about violent games like <i>Doom</i> and making comparisons between the acts they intended to carry out and aspects of games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-byrd_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-byrd-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scotus_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-scotus-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ferguson and others have explained the video game moral panic as part of a cycle that all new media go through.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scotus_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-scotus-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fergusonapa_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-fergusonapa-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpr_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-jpr-33">[33]</a></sup> In 2011, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled that legally restricting sales of video games to minors would be unconstitutional and called the research presented in favor of regulation "unpersuasive."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scotus_31-4"><a href="#cite_note-scotus-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s.E2.80.93present:_Crime_increase">1970s–present: Crime increase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moral_panic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 1970s–present: Crime increase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Research shows that fears of increasing crime is often the cause of moral panics (Cohen, 1972; Hall <i>et al.</i> 1978; Goode and Ben-Yehuda 1994). Recent studies have shown that despite declining crime rates, this phenomenon, which often taps into a populations' "<a href="/wiki/Herd_mentality" title="Herd mentality">herd mentality</a>," continues to occur in various cultures. Japanese jurist <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%B5%9C%E4%BA%95%E6%B5%A9%E4%B8%80" title="ja:浜井浩一">Koichi Hamai</a> explains how the changes in crime recording in Japan since the 1990s caused people to believe that the crime rate is rising and that crimes were getting increasingly severe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s.E2.80.93present:_War_on_drugs">1970s–present: War on drugs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moral_panic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 1970s–present: War on drugs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some critics have pointed to moral panic as an explanation for the <a href="/wiki/War_on_Drugs" title="War on Drugs">War on Drugs</a>. For example, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_encouragement_of_Arts,_Manufactures_%26_Commerce" title="Royal Society for the encouragement of Arts, Manufactures &amp; Commerce">Royal Society of Arts</a> commission concluded that "the <a href="/wiki/Misuse_of_Drugs_Act_1971" title="Misuse of Drugs Act 1971">Misuse of Drugs Act 1971</a>, ... is driven more by 'moral panic' than by a practical desire to reduce harm."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DR_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-DR-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some have written that one of the many rungs supporting the moral panic behind the war on drugs was a separate but related moral panic, which peaked in the late 90's, involving media's gross exaggeration of the <a href="/wiki/Date_rape_drug#Media_coverage_and_moral_panic_aspects" title="Date rape drug">frequency of the surreptitious use of date rape drugs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goode_2009_217_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goode_2009_217-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> News media have been criticized for advocating "grossly excessive protective measures for women, particularly in coverage between 1996 and 1998", for overstating the threat, and for excessively raising it in women's minds for the rest of their lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goode_2009_217_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goode_2009_217-36">[36]</a></sup> For example, showing excessive concerns extending even into the late 2000s, a 2009 Australian study found that of 97 instances of patients admitted to the hospital believing their drinks might have been spiked, drug panel tests were unable to detect any drug in any of the cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quigley_2009_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Quigley_2009-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th-21st_century:_Public_health">20th-21st century: <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">Public health</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moral_panic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 20th-21st century: Public health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Throughout history, disease has been proven to cause panic in societies when individuals are faced with something they cannot explain. This fear of disease and spread of panic dates back to many centuries ago, and continues into the 21st century with diseases such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ebola" title="Ebola">Ebola</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H1N1" title="H1N1">H1N1</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zika" title="Zika">Zika</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SARS" title="SARS">SARS</a>. Comparing Cohan’s idea of the ‘folk devils’ with disease as an epidemic plays the same role of spreading mass panic and fear concerning the spread of an infectious illness. This intense concentration on hygiene emerged, before the 20th century, with a medical belief referred to as <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Miasma" title="Miasma">miasma</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Miasma_theory" title="Miasma theory">miasma theory</a>, states that disease was the direct result of the polluting emanations of filth: sewer gas, garbage fumes, and stenches that polluted air and water, which results in an epidemic. The idea of cleanliness was a socially constructed belief that linked race, gender, and sexuality with ideas that made people feel uncomfortable. It is argued that a moral panic is constructed when “patterns of behaviour, whether private or public, come to be selected by the mass media as a threat to the fabric of society.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1980.27s-present:_HIV.2FAIDS">1980's-present: <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moral_panic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 1980's-present: HIV/AIDS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Acquired immune deficiency syndrome (AIDS), in a person may develop into other fatal health conditions such as pneumonia, fungal infections, TB, toxoplasmosis and cytomegalovirus. The theme of a meeting of the British Sociological Association's South West and Wales Study Group on 21 September 1985 was 'AIDS: The Latest Moral Panic'. This was prompted by the growing interest of medical sociologists in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a>, as well as that of UK health care professionals working in the field of health education, at a time when both groups were also beginning to voice an equally increasing concern with the growing media attention and attendant scare-mongering that AIDS was attracting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATLMP_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-ATLMP-40">[40]</a></sup> In the 1980s a moral panic was created within the media over HIV/AIDS. The notable iceberg ad by the government clearly hinted that there were many things about HIV/AIDS that the general public did not realize due to the bulk of the information being hidden from view. On one hand, the media outlets nicknamed HIV/AIDS the ‘gay plague’ stigmatizing a selected section of the population as being the primary cause and carriers of the disease. While on the other hand scientists gained a far better understanding of HIV/AIDS as it grew in the 1980s and moved into the 1990s and beyond. The illness was still negatively viewed by many as either caused by or passed on by the gay community. Once it became clear that this wasn't the case, the moral panic created by the media went in another direction blaming the overall negligence of ethical standards of the younger generation (both male and female), resulting in a moral panic. It is prevalent in the media and the way HIV/AIDS is depicted taken from this extract, “British TV and press coverage is locked into an agenda which blocks out any approach to the subject which does not conform in advance to the values and language of a profoundly homophobic culture- a culture that is which does not regard gay men as fully or properly human. No distinction obtains for the agenda between ‘quality’ and ‘tabloid’ newspapers, or between ‘popular’ and ‘serious’ television.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2003-2004:_SARS">2003-2004: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SARS" title="SARS">SARS</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moral_panic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 2003-2004: SARS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Globalization increases the likelihood that an infectious disease appearing in one area of the world will spread rapidly to another. Severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS) is a recent example of this. Within weeks in the beginning of 2003, SARS had quickly spread from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guangdong_province" title="Guangdong province">Guangdong province</a> of China to infect people in over 20 countries around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Approximately 15% of patients affected required artificial ventilation. There was also a relatively high death rate. By late March, hundreds of people were infected, with cases reported in China, Singapore, Canada, United States and Thailand. The WHO (World Health Organization) then took the rare step of issuing a worldwide alert. SARS was first reported in the United Kingdom newspapers early March of that year. Reviewing the many reports of the new infectious diseases in the newspapers, highlighted larger public anxieties, in particular anxieties about the insufficiency of technology and medicine that does not have the capacity for new threats as well as moral panic over globalization. Globalization has many definitions: It can be the spread of information, trends, technologies, culture and lifestyles but primarily it is an economic force. One of many consequences of an economic globalization is the quick, cheap access for the mobility of people of all walks of life. Another consequence is larger awareness of global threats and in particular the threat of global epidemics.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2009-2010:_Swine_Flu.2FH1N1">2009-2010: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swine_Flu" title="Swine Flu">Swine Flu</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H1N1" title="H1N1">H1N1</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moral_panic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: 2009-2010: Swine Flu/H1N1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>H1N1, properly known as swine-origin influenza A/H1N1 virus, was first recognized in 2009, in civilians in Mexico. The 2009 <a href="/wiki/Pandemic" title="Pandemic">pandemic</a> was a compilation of previous swine flu viruses. It is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoonotic" title="Zoonotic">zoonotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogen</a>, a disease that normally exists in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swine" title="Swine">swine</a> but can affect humans. As mentioned in Virology Journal, H1N1 could have been a cause of genetic distortion of earlier forms of swine flu viruses that affected Europe and Asia in the 90’s. From the JRSM journals, it refers to our society as ‘risk society’, the journal mentions how “Modernity creates risk by our increasingly busy and urbanized way of life, which includes working conditions, various modes of transport, pollution and infections… subsequently, the modern world is more susceptible to periods of moral panic than ever before."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The statement suggests that with the advancement of society and the hyper-awareness is potentially putting people at risk.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2013-2016:_Ebola">2013-2016: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ebola" title="Ebola">Ebola</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moral_panic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: 2013-2016: Ebola">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Ebola virus, clinically referred to as Ebola hemorrhagic fever, is a sometimes fatal diseases in humans, and usually fatal if untreated. It was first diagnosed in patients in West Africa in 2013. It was the largest outbreak of Ebola since the 1976 outbreaks in South Sudan and the Republic of Congo. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_World_Health_Organization" title="The World Health Organization">The World Health Organization</a>(WHO) states that the virus was named after the <a href="/wiki/Ebola_River" title="Ebola River">Ebola River</a> where the illness first appeared in 1976. “The virus is transmitted to people from wild animals and spreads in the human population through human-to-human transmission."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The epidemic and subsequent panic caused difficulties for people traveling across international borders. Ebola was seen as the ‘folk devil’ from Cohen's study on youth culture. The idea of Ebola spread mass panic in many countries, due to its wide coverage on various news outlets and talk shows. Although it was not as contagious as influenza <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H1N1" title="H1N1">H1N1</a>, it caused tremendous panic and fear.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2015-present:_Zika">2015-present: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zika" title="Zika">Zika</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moral_panic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: 2015-present: Zika">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Zika virus (ZIKV) which originated from <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>, “is carried by mosquitos, which includes the mosquito species responsible for spreading <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dengue" title="Dengue">dengue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yellow_fever" title="Yellow fever">yellow fever</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kruskal20160219_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kruskal20160219-45">[45]</a></sup> The disease was first seen in patients as a dengue-like syndrome which is a mosquito-borne- tropical disease caused by dengue virus. In early 2015, several cases of patients presenting symptoms of mild fever, <a href="/wiki/Rash" title="Rash">rash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conjunctivitis" title="Conjunctivitis">conjunctivitis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arthralgia" title="Arthralgia">arthralgia</a> were reported in the northeastern Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kruskal20160219_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kruskal20160219-45">[45]</a></sup> The fast-paced spread of the virus was alarming, and in accordance with the idea of the moral panic by Cohan, the fear itself was stagnating an entire population and the health and wellbeing of the nation of Brazil was under scrutiny. A major issue was in pregnant women. Some Brazilian women infected with Zika gave birth to children with <a href="/wiki/Microcephaly" title="Microcephaly">microcephaly</a>, a potentially devastating condition of brain maldevelopment. Not only did the disease spread worry, the notion that babies were getting harmed led government officials to call for the regulation of pregnancy among women.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moral_panic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a more recent edition of <i>Folk Devils and Moral Panics,</i> Cohen outlines some of the criticisms that have arisen in response to moral panic theory. One of these is of the term "panic" itself, as it has connotations of irrationality and a lack of control. Cohen maintains that "panic" is a suitable term when used as an extended metaphor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cohen_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-cohen-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another criticism is that of disproportionality. The problem with this argument is that there is no way to measure what a proportionate reaction should be to a specific action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cohen_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-cohen-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:xxvi–xxxi</sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jarrett_Thibodeaux&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jarrett Thibodeaux (page does not exist)">Jarrett Thibodeaux</a> (2014) further argues that the criteria of disproportionality erroneously assumes that a social problem <i>should</i> correspond with some objective criteria of harm. The idea that a social problem should correspond with some objective criteria of harm, but is a moral panic when it does not, is a 'constructionism of the gaps' line of explanation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In "<i>Rethinking 'moral panic' for multi-mediated social worlds</i>", <a href="/wiki/Angela_McRobbie" title="Angela McRobbie">Angela McRobbie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Thornton" title="Sarah Thornton">Sarah Thornton</a> argue "that it is now time that every stage in the process of constructing a moral panic, as well as the social relations which support it, should be revised". Their argument is that mass media has changed since the concept of moral panic emerged so "that 'folk devils' are less marginalized than they once were", and that 'folk devils' are not only castigated by mass media but supported and defended by it as well. They also suggest that the "points of social control" that moral panics used to rest on "have undergone some degree of shift, if not transformation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>The British criminologist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yvonne_Jewkes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yvonne Jewkes (page does not exist)">Yvonne Jewkes</a> has also raised issue with the term 'morality', how it is accepted unproblematically in the concept of 'moral panic' and how most research into moral panics fails to approach the term critically but instead accepts it at face value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jewkes1_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jewkes1-48">[48]</a></sup> Jewkes goes on to argue that the thesis and the way it has been used fails to distinguish between crimes that quite rightly offend human morality, and thus elicit a justifiable reaction, and those that demonise minorities. The public are not sufficiently gullible to keep accepting the latter and allowing themselves to be manipulated by the media and the government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jewkes1_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jewkes1-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another British criminologist, Steve Hall, goes a step further to suggest that the term 'moral panic' is a fundamental category error. Hall argues that although some crimes are sensationalized by the media, in the general structure of the crime/control narrative the ability of the existing state and criminal justice system to protect the public is also overstated. Public concern is whipped up only for the purpose of being soothed, which produces not panic but the opposite, comfort and complacency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Echoing another point Hall makes, the sociologists Thompson and Williams argue that the concept of 'moral panic' is not a rational response to the phenomenon of social reaction, but itself a product of the irrational middle-class fear of the imagined working-class 'mob'. Using as an example a peaceful and lawful protest staged by local mothers against the re-housing of sex-offenders on their estate, Thompson and Williams show how the sensationalist demonization of the protesters by moral panic theorists and the liberal press was just as irrational as the demonization of the sex offenders by the protesters and the tabloid press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many sociologists and criminologist (Ungar, Hier, Rohloff) have revised Cohen's original framework. The revisions are compatible with the way in which Cohen theorizes panics in the third Introduction to Folk Devils and Moral Panics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>

<p>The term was used in 1830, in a way that completely differs from its modern <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a> application, by a religious magazine<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HB_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-HB-52">[52]</a></sup> regarding a sermon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Princeton_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Princeton-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:250</sup> The phrase was used again in 1831, with an intent that is possibly closer to its modern use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
