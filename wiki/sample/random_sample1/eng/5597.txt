<p>The <b>Moro people or Bangsamoro or Bangsa Moro</b> is a coined term for the population of Muslims in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, forming the largest non-Catholic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> group in the country, and comprising about 5.1% (as of August 2007) of the total Philippine population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pinoy_Muslim_percent_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pinoy_Muslim_percent-1">[1]</a></sup> There are around 13 Ethnic Indigenous communities of which the majority have converted to the religion of Islam and are now Muslims or Moros; most are the followers of <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Islam</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Shafi%27i" title="Shafi'i">Shafi'i</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madh%27hab" title="Madh'hab">madh'hab</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arnold2011_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Arnold2011-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <i>Moro</i> (Moor) came into use during the Spanish colonial period by the Spaniards. It was originally used to refer to the Berber/Arab Muslims who ruled <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Muslim Spain</a> (prior to the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a>), but for a time it came to be used informally by the Spaniards to refer to all Muslims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moro_case_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moro_case-4">[4]</a></sup> immediately upon their arrival. They noticed early on quoting one of their earliest writers:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>they say that there are in this country, Moors [Moros] like those of Barberia [Barbary], and that their strength in arms is quite equal to that of those people; and that they fight and defend themselves like the Turks...</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Relaciones delas Islas Felipinas 1569</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The term is also self proscribed by the communities themselves. There is also the historic Dansalan Declaration and the Zamboanga Declaration of Moro and Lumad leaders gathered to declare their protest to the planned inclusion of their sovereign territories to the emerging Philippine Commonwealth. On both declarations, the signatories used the word; Moro Nation which translates as <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Bangsamoro" title="Bangsamoro">Bangsa Moro</a>.</p>
<p>In modern history, influential groups such as the <a href="/wiki/Moro_National_Liberation_Front" title="Moro National Liberation Front">Moro National Liberation Front</a> (MNLF) espoused the Moro identity to unify all Muslim groups in the Philippines. This is different from the "Filipino" identity as it was more seen as an epithet to Catholic converted ethnic groups. In addition with the Moro concept represents their distinct <a href="/wiki/Muslim_world" title="Muslim world">Islamic heritage</a>, one of the Moro social movement founding leaders, <a href="/wiki/Nur_Misuari" title="Nur Misuari">Nur Misuari</a> clarifies that they are not a part of the majority <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filipino_people" title="Filipino people">Filipino society</a> with a slogan "Moro not Filipino" in their struggle for autonomy or independence against the <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholic</a> majority <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_Philippines" title="Government of the Philippines">Philippine central government</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>This is rooted from resistance of the then -<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Philippines" title="Spanish colonization of the Philippines">Spanish</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Philippines_(1898%E2%80%931946)" title="History of the Philippines (1898–1946)">American</a> rule who forced them and their much older Sultanate system of government and to integrate them as official Policy into the modern Philippine republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moro_case_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Moro_case-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Moro people mostly live in <a href="/wiki/Mindanao" title="Mindanao">Mindanao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulu" title="Sulu">Sulu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palawan" title="Palawan">Palawan</a>. Due to continuous movement of their people since the 16th century until present due to the Philippine - Bangsa Moro War, their communities can be found in all large cities in the Philippines, including <a href="/wiki/Metro_Manila" title="Metro Manila">Manila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metro_Cebu" title="Metro Cebu">Cebu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metro_Davao" title="Metro Davao">Davao</a>. Many Moros also have emigrated to <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a> in the last half of the 20th century due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moro_conflict_in_the_Philippines" title="Moro conflict in the Philippines">conflict in the Southern Philippines</a>. Newer communities can be found today in <a href="/wiki/Kota_Kinabalu" title="Kota Kinabalu">Kota Kinabalu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandakan" title="Sandakan">Sandakan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semporna" title="Semporna">Semporna</a> in neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Sabah" title="Sabah">Sabah</a>, Malaysia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/North_Kalimantan" title="North Kalimantan">North Kalimantan</a> in Indonesia, as well in <a href="/wiki/Bandar_Seri_Begawan" title="Bandar Seri Begawan">Bandar Seri Begawan</a> of Brunei.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Three_Moro_men_in_the_1900s.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="436" height="353" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Three_Moro_men_in_the_1900s.jpg/220px-Three_Moro_men_in_the_1900s.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Three_Moro_men_in_the_1900s.jpg/330px-Three_Moro_men_in_the_1900s.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a1/Three_Moro_men_in_the_1900s.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Three_Moro_men_in_the_1900s.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Three Moro men from the <a href="/wiki/Sulu_Archipelago" title="Sulu Archipelago">Sulu Archipelago</a> in the 1900s.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The recently coined term Bangsamoro, derived from the old <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a> word <i>"bangsa"</i>, meaning "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(human_classification)" title="Race (human classification)">race</a>" or "nation" with the <i>"Moro"</i> as "people", is now used to describe both the Filipino Muslims and their homeland. The term Bangsamoro carries the aspiration of the Filipino Muslims to have an Islamic (Moro) country or state (bangsa).</p>
<p>The word <i>Moro</i> itself was a pseudonym which had been used before in the 16th century by Spanish colonisers in reference to a Muslim group of "<a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a>", which originating from "Mauru", a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin word</a> that referred to the inhabitants of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">ancient Roman</a> province of Mauritania in northwest Africa, which today comprises the modern Muslim states of <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thirteen_ethnic_groups_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-thirteen_ethnic_groups-8">[8]</a></sup> With the rise of Mauritania as part of the Muslim <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate" title="Umayyad Caliphate">Umayyad Caliphate</a>, Muslim armies conquered and ruled much of the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> from 711 to 1492, for about a total of 781 years in which the term was revived when the Christian-majority Spanish became involved in a war with them and started to call the Muslims as "Moors".</p>
<p>The term was then used again by the Spanish when they arriving in the Philippine archipelago and found a similar Muslim community who rebelled against them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thirteen_ethnic_groups_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-thirteen_ethnic_groups-8">[8]</a></sup> Despite opposition from the modern Muslim community in the Philippines who objected to the term's origin by Spanish colonialist, the name has evolved to become seen as a unitary force especially by the Philippine government. <a href="/wiki/Marvic_Leonen" title="Marvic Leonen">Marvic Leonen</a>, who was the Chief Peace Negotiator for Philippine government with the <a href="/wiki/Moro_Islamic_Liberation_Front" title="Moro Islamic Liberation Front">Moro Islamic Liberation Front</a>, has said:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>There is Bangsamoro, the place; there is Bangsamoro, the identity.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Marvic Leonen, Press briefing, October 8, 2012</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Since the 19th century, by the Treaty of Paris on which Spain without authority turned over to America the lands the Moros successfully defended for 333 years, many of the Moros preferred to be separated from the American successor mainstream Philippine society. The established historical, social and political factors of the Moro communities made them a separate country unethically annexed to the emerging Philippine Republic.</p>
<p>It was later adopted as a name for separatist organisations such as the Moro National Liberation Front (MNLF), <a href="/wiki/Rashid_Lucman" title="Rashid Lucman">Rashid Lucman</a>'s Bangsa Moro Liberation Organisation (BMLO) as well the Moro Islamic Liberation Front (MILF).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Framework_Agreement_on_the_Bangsamoro" title="Framework Agreement on the Bangsamoro">Framework Agreement on the Bangsamoro</a> recognises "Bangsamoro" as an identity and calls for the creation of a new autonomous political entity called <a href="/wiki/Bangsamoro_(political_entity)" title="Bangsamoro (political entity)">Bangsamoro</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bangsamoro_framework_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-bangsamoro_framework-11">[11]</a></sup> The native Moro Communities of Mindanao and Sulu are termed considered as Filipino Muslim by the Philippine government.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society">Society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Region">Region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ARMM.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1250" data-file-width="800" height="344" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/ARMM.png/220px-ARMM.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/ARMM.png/330px-ARMM.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/ARMM.png/440px-ARMM.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ARMM.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<span style="margin:0; font-size:90%; white-space:nowrap;"><span class="legend-text" style="border:none; padding:1px .6em; background-color:#EE7668; color:black; font-size:95%; line-height:1.25; text-align:center;"> </span> Territory of the <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_Region_in_Muslim_Mindanao" title="Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao">Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao</a> (ARMM).</span><br/>
<span style="margin:0; font-size:90%; white-space:nowrap;"><span class="legend-text" style="border:none; padding:1px .6em; background-color:#FFFBCE; color:black; font-size:95%; line-height:1.25; text-align:center;"> </span> Historical extent of Moro populations and governance.</span></div>

<p>The majority of the Moro people historically reside in the area they now call the <i>Bangsamoro</i> region which was commonly called Muslim Mindanao. That land is located in the provinces of <a href="/wiki/Basilan" title="Basilan">Basilan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotabato" title="Cotabato">Cotabato</a>, <a href="/wiki/Compostela_Valley" title="Compostela Valley">Compostela Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Davao_del_Sur" title="Davao del Sur">Davao del Sur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lanao_del_Norte" title="Lanao del Norte">Lanao del Norte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lanao_del_Sur" title="Lanao del Sur">Lanao del Sur</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maguindanao_province" title="Maguindanao province">Maguindanao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palawan" title="Palawan">Palawan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sarangani" title="Sarangani">Sarangani</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Cotabato" title="South Cotabato">South Cotabato</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sultan_Kudarat" title="Sultan Kudarat">Sultan Kudarat</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulu_Province" title="Sulu Province">Sulu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tawi-Tawi" title="Tawi-Tawi">Tawi-Tawi</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It includes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_cities" title="Philippine cities">cities</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cotabato_City" title="Cotabato City">Cotabato</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dapitan_City" title="Dapitan City">Dapitan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dipolog_City" title="Dipolog City">Dipolog</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Santos_City" title="General Santos City">General Santos</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iligan_City" title="Iligan City">Iligan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marawi_City" title="Marawi City">Marawi</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The eastern part of a territory of the former British <a href="/wiki/Protectorate" title="Protectorate">protectorate</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Borneo" title="North Borneo">North Borneo</a> (now in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Sabah" title="Sabah">Sabah</a>) which have since became part of the federation of <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> also claimed by the Moro National Liberation Front for the proposed state of <a href="/wiki/Bangsamoro_Republik" title="Bangsamoro Republik">Bangsamoro Republik</a>, but the idea has since failed with the MNLF founding leader Misuari <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-exile" title="Self-exile">self-exile</a> himself after <a href="/wiki/Zamboanga_City_crisis" title="Zamboanga City crisis">an encounter</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_of_the_Philippines" title="Armed Forces of the Philippines">Armed Forces of the Philippines</a> (AFP), as he protested the further unilateral changes by the government on the mutually signed 1996 Final Peace Agreement, so after he was labelled as a "terrorist" during the siege.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 5 August 2008, after neatly 10 years of negotiation, with the International bodies all ready to witness a supposed historic event, an attempt by the Philippine government's Peace Negotiating Panel to sign a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Memorandum_of_Agreement_on_Ancestral_Domain&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Memorandum of Agreement on Ancestral Domain (page does not exist)">Memorandum of Agreement on Ancestral Domain</a> with the Moro Islamic Liberation Front thru a petition by Settler politicians in Mindanao like Governor Manny Pinol and Governor Lobregat, was then declared unconstitutional by the Philippine Supreme Court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peace_process_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-peace_process-15">[15]</a></sup> Conflict immediately broke out on the ground following the decision, with nearly half a million people displaced and hundreds killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peace_process_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-peace_process-15">[15]</a></sup> Observers now concur that two Moro commanders — Kumander <a href="/wiki/Ameril_Umbra_Kato" title="Ameril Umbra Kato">Umbra Kato</a> and Kumander Bravo — did launch attacks in Lanao del Norte and <a href="/wiki/Cotabato" title="Cotabato">North Cotabato</a> as a response to the non signing that has shaken the peace process in the region.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Framework_Agreement_on_the_Bangsamoro" title="Framework Agreement on the Bangsamoro">Framework Agreement on the Bangsamoro</a> defines the Bangsamoro to be <span class="nowrap">"[t]hose</span> who at the time of conquest and colonisation were considered natives or original inhabitants of Mindanao and the Sulu archipelago and its adjacent islands including Palawan, and their descendants whether of mixed or of full blood".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bangsamoro_framework_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-bangsamoro_framework-11">[11]</a></sup> This definition of the moro people by the bangsamoro is purely owned by the bangsamoro and not fully accepted by the people of Mindanao or the Philippines. Moro by definition still means Muslim who are still the minority population of Mindanao.</p>

<p>Moro culture is very <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malay_culture" title="Malay culture">Malay-influenced</a>. The Bangsamoro share similarities with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic_Malays" title="Ethnic Malays">Malay</a> people of Malaysia, Indonesia, Brunei, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, while being distinct from them. The Bangsamoro cultures represent the only living examples of the larger historic lowland cultures of groups who were once also Moro in the coastal and central Luzon before the Spanish colonization forced converted them to Catholicism, such as the precolonial <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_people" title="Tagalog people">Tagalogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kapampangan_people" title="Kapampangan people">Kapampangan</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visayan" title="Visayan">Visayan</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Islam_in_the_Philippines" title="Islam in the Philippines">Islam</a> is the most dominant influence on the Moro cultures since the era of the <a href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Maguindanao" title="Sultanate of Maguindanao">Sultanate of Maguindanao</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Sulu" title="Sultanate of Sulu">Sulu</a>. Large and small <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">mosques</a> can be found all over the region. In accordance with Islamic Law, alcohol and fornication are prohibited. <a href="/wiki/Religious_restrictions_on_the_consumption_of_pork" title="Religious restrictions on the consumption of pork">Pork</a> and pork byproducts are not permissible. Another practice is fasting during <a href="/wiki/Ramadan" title="Ramadan">Ramadan</a> and providing charity for the poor. The <a href="/wiki/Hajj" title="Hajj">Hajj</a> is also a major requirement as it is one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Five_pillars_of_Islam" title="Five pillars of Islam">five pillars of Islam</a>. Moro women cover themselves using a veil (<i><a href="/wiki/Tudong" title="Tudong">tudong</a></i>) just as in Malaysia, Indonesia, Brunei, Singapore and southern Thailand. Moro men, especially the elderly, can always be seen wearing a black Muslim skull cap called the <i><a href="/wiki/Songkok" title="Songkok">songkok</a></i> or the white one called the <i><a href="/wiki/Taqiyah_(cap)" title="Taqiyah (cap)">taqiyah</a></i>. Differentiating from their Malay relatives in neighbouring countries, the only main problems associated with Moro groups is that they are not always united and lack a sense of solidarity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Each Moro community are proud of their prowess and wealth as well as with their ethnic tradition, culture and identity.</p>

<p>The culture of the Moro revolves around the music of the <a href="/wiki/Kulintang" title="Kulintang">kulintang</a>, a type of <a href="/wiki/Gong" title="Gong">gong</a> instrument, akin to the drum instrument, yet wholly made of bronze or brass found in the southern Philippines. This creats a unique sound that varies in the speed it is hit which includes the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Binalig&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Binalig (page does not exist)">Binalig</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tagonggo" title="Tagonggo">Tagonggo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kapanirong" title="Kapanirong">Kapanirong</a> plus others more also normally heard in Malaysia, Indonesia and Brunei.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnic_groups">Ethnic groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ethnic groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are at least thirteen ethnic groups comprising the Indigenous Peoples of the Philippines;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thirteen_ethnic_groups_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-thirteen_ethnic_groups-8">[8]</a></sup> all descended from the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austronesian_people" title="Austronesian people">Austronesian people</a> (Malayo-Polynesian) that migrated from <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> and populated the regions of the Philippines, <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, the Pacific islands and Madagascar. Three of these groups make up the majority of these tribes. They are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maguindanaon" title="Maguindanaon">Maguindanaon</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Cotabato" title="North Cotabato">North Cotabato</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sultan_Kudarat" title="Sultan Kudarat">Sultan Kudarat</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maguindanao_province" title="Maguindanao province">Maguindanao</a> provinces, the <a href="/wiki/Maranao_people" title="Maranao people">Maranao</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lanao_(province)" title="Lanao (province)">Lanao</a> provinces and the <a href="/wiki/Taus%C5%ABg_people" title="Tausūg people">Tausūg</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sulu_Archipelago" title="Sulu Archipelago">Sulu Archipelago</a>. Smaller groups include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banguingui" title="Banguingui">Banguingui</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samal_people" title="Samal people">Samal</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bajau_people" title="Bajau people">Bajau</a> of the Sulu Archipelago; the <a href="/wiki/Yakan_people" title="Yakan people">Yakan</a> of <a href="/wiki/Basilan" title="Basilan">Basilan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zamboanga_del_Sur" title="Zamboanga del Sur">Zamboanga del Sur</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illanun_people" title="Illanun people">Illanun</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lanao_(province)" title="Lanao (province)">Lanao provinces</a> and Davao and <a href="/wiki/Sangirese_people" title="Sangirese people">Sangir</a> of <a href="/wiki/Davao_Region" title="Davao Region">Davao</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Molbog_people&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Molbog people (page does not exist)">Molbog</a> of southern <a href="/wiki/Palawan" title="Palawan">Palawan</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jama_Mapuns" title="Jama Mapuns">Jama Mapuns</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cagayan_de_Tawi-Tawi_Island" title="Cagayan de Tawi-Tawi Island">Cagayan de Tawi-Tawi Island</a>. These indigenous people from the south mostly converted to the religion of Islam making them Muslims or Moros, some of them converted over to Christianity and other religions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages">Languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The Indigenous people are mostly speakers of a variety of Austronesian languages. The most-spoken native languages of the Indigenous peoples of Mindanao are Maguindanaon, Tausūg and Maranao. The <a href="/wiki/Maguindanao_language" title="Maguindanao language">Maguindanao language</a> is spoken in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maguindanao_Province" title="Maguindanao Province">Maguindanao Province</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Maranao_language" title="Maranao language">Maranao language</a> is predominant in the Lanao region, and is the majority spoken in <a href="/wiki/Lanao_del_Sur" title="Lanao del Sur">Lanao del Sur</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tausug_language" title="Tausug language">Tausug language</a> is spoken in the <a href="/wiki/Sulu_Archipelago" title="Sulu Archipelago">Sulu Archipelago</a> with speakers in the <a href="/wiki/Zamboanga_Peninsula" title="Zamboanga Peninsula">Zamboanga Peninsula</a> and the Malaysian state of <a href="/wiki/Sabah" title="Sabah">Sabah</a>. Other Austronesian languages spoken by their respective tribes are the Sama-Bajau languages(although the banguingui,the samal and badjao share the same language,there ends the similarity,they are different in all aspect specially when it comes to religion).Yakan and Kalagan. Also spoken among the indigenous peoples of Mindanao and Sulu is <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">Tagalog</a>, for the sake of living in the Philippines and being a national language of the Philippines. There also exists a native <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_people" title="Tagalog people">Tagalog</a> Muslim community in the <a href="/wiki/Quiapo,_Manila" title="Quiapo, Manila">Quiapo District</a> of Metro Manila. Because of the mass influx of <a href="/wiki/Cebuano_people" title="Cebuano people">Cebuano</a> migrants to Mindanao, many of the indigenous of Mindanao tend to be exposed to the Cebuano language from Visayan easily enough to be able to speak it, especially with the Tausūg since the language belongs to the Visayan family.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A sizeable minority speaks <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a>, also an Austronesian language which was once the <i>lingua franca</i> of <a href="/wiki/Maritime_Southeast_Asia" title="Maritime Southeast Asia">maritime Southeast Asia</a> prior to contact with the European colonial powers. Today, many Moro merchants use Malay to converse with citizens of the neighbouring Malay-speaking nations of Malaysia, Indonesia and Brunei. Most of the Malay-speaking Muslims of the Philippines are those in the southern parts of the <a href="/wiki/Zamboanga_Peninsula" title="Zamboanga Peninsula">Zamboanga Peninsula</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sulu_Archipelago" title="Sulu Archipelago">Sulu Archipelago</a> and the southern predominantly Muslim-inhabited municipalities of Bataraza and Balabac in Palawan. They likely speak a form of <a href="/wiki/Malay_trade_and_creole_languages" title="Malay trade and creole languages">Malay creole</a>. Many of the Tausūg in Malaysia and Indonesia (known there as <i>Suluks</i>) tend to lose their proficiency in both Tausūg and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chavacano_language" title="Chavacano language">Chavacano</a>, as well on <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">Tagalog</a>, as they became assimilated into the Malay-speaking majority of Malaysia, Indonesia and Brunei.</p>
<p>Chavacano (sometimes spelled as Chabacano or Chabakano) is a Philippine Spanish creole, that gained popularity as a Philippine major language during the short-lived <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Zamboanga" title="Republic of Zamboanga">Republic of Zamboanga</a>. Most of the indigenous people have also attained the ability to speak this language, specifically the Zamboanga dialect known as Zamboangueño especially those that live in Zamboanga and <a href="/wiki/Basilan" title="Basilan">Basilan</a>. Chavacano is popular with both Muslims and Christians (mostly Roman Catholics) in Mindanao and Sulu. A minority of the indigenous people in Sulu and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tawi-tawi" title="Tawi-tawi">Tawi-tawi</a> speak Chavacano as these areas were partially ruled by the Spanish. Tausūgs have fluency of speaking it, as Chavacano contains Visayan words. Chavacano also contains communities of speakers in <a href="/wiki/Semporna" title="Semporna">Semporna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sabah" title="Sabah">Sabah</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> is also spoken by a minority of the Moro people, being the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liturgical_language" title="Liturgical language">liturgical language</a> of <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_the_Philippines" title="Islam in the Philippines">Islam</a>. Most Muslim indigenous people however, do not know Arabic beyond its religious uses. Most of the languages of the indigenous people are written in the Latin script. However, an Arabic script known as <a href="/wiki/Jawi_alphabet" title="Jawi alphabet">Jawi</a> is used to write Tausūg language. Attempts are being made to make Jawi an official script in the future Bangsamoro entity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Generally, the indigenous people populace are educated. Some of the indigenous people who are Muslims choose Islamic Education and enrolled in Islamic/Arabic Institutions like <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jamiatul_Mindanao_Al-Islamie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jamiatul Mindanao Al-Islamie (page does not exist)">Jamiatul Mindanao Al-Islamie</a> located in Marawi City and even outside the country like in <a href="/wiki/Al-Azhar_University" title="Al-Azhar University">Al-Azhar University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>, Egypt and <a href="/wiki/Islamic_University_of_Madinah" title="Islamic University of Madinah">Islamic University of Madinah</a>, Saudi Arabia through scholarship grants while majority completed English/Western Education. They owe it to <a href="/wiki/Mindanao_State_University" title="Mindanao State University">Mindanao State University</a>, the second biggest state university in the Philippines next to <a href="/wiki/University_of_the_Philippines" title="University of the Philippines">University of the Philippines</a>, which has several campuses across Mindanao and its Main Campus is located at the heart of Islamic City of Marawi. <a href="/wiki/Mindanao_State_University" title="Mindanao State University">Mindanao State University</a> has also an Islamic Institute within its campus which is the King Faisal Centre for Islamic Arabic, Asian Studies. Some indigenous people students are enrolled at other institutions both private and government especially in key cities such as Davao, Cebu and Manila.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In the 13th century, the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> missionaries from <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> including one, <a href="/wiki/Makhdum_Karim" title="Makhdum Karim">Makhdum Karim</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Tawi-Tawi" title="Tawi-Tawi">Tawi-Tawi</a> initiated the conversion of the native population into Islam. Trade between other sultanates in Brunei, Malaysia and Indonesia helped establish the Islamic region in the southern Philippines. In 1457, the introduction of Islam led to the creation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sultanates" title="Sultanates">Sultanates</a>. This included the sultanates of Buayan, <a href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Maguindanao" title="Sultanate of Maguindanao">Maguindanao</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Sulu" title="Sultanate of Sulu">Sultanate of Sulu</a>, which is considered the oldest Muslim government in the region until its annexation by the United States in 1898.</p>
<p>Like <a href="/wiki/Bruneian_Empire" title="Bruneian Empire">Brunei</a>, Sulu and other Muslim sultanates in the Philippines were introduced to Islam by either <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_China" title="Islam in China">Chinese Muslims</a>, Persians, and Arabs traders. Chinese Muslim merchants participated in the local commerce, and the Sultanate had diplomatic relations with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a> China, being involved in the tribute system, the Sulu leader Paduka Batara and his sons moved to China, where he died and Chinese Muslims brought up his sons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The inhabitants of pre-Hispanic Philippines practised Islam and Animism. The <a href="/wiki/Malay_race" title="Malay race">Malay</a> kingdoms interacted, and traded with various tribes throughout the islands, governing several territories ruled by leaders called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rajah" title="Rajah">Rajah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Datu" title="Datu">Datu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">Sultan</a>. Before the arrival of Islam the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rajah" title="Rajah">Rajahs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Datu" title="Datu">Datus</a> governed the territories.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonial_period">Colonial period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Colonial period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_conquest">Spanish conquest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Spanish conquest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_people" title="Spanish people">Spaniards</a> arrived in 1521 and the Philippines became part of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a> in 1565. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sultanates" title="Sultanates">Sultanates</a>, however, actively resisted the Spaniards, thus maintaining their independence, enabling them to develop an Islamic culture and identity, different from the rest of the Catholic converted natives which the Spaniards called <i>"Indios"</i> (Indians).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> With intentions of colonising the islands, the Spaniards made incursions into Moro territory. They also began erecting military stations and garrisons with Catholic missions, which attracted Christianised natives of civilian settlements. The most notable of these are <a href="/wiki/Zamboanga_City" title="Zamboanga City">Zamboanga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cotabato_City" title="Cotabato City">Cotabato</a>. Spain was under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Inquistion" title="Spanish Inquistion">Inquisition</a> which ordered Jews and Muslims to convert to Roman Catholicism or leave or face death penalty, thus Spaniards try to ban and suppress Islam in areas they conquer. Feeling threatened by these actions, the Moros decided to challenge the Spanish government. They began conducting raids on Catholic coastal towns. These Moro raids reached a fevered pitched during the reign of Datu Bantilan in 1754.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93Moro_Conflict" title="Spanish–Moro Conflict">Spanish–Moro Conflict</a> started at the phase of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish-Brunei_War" title="Spanish-Brunei War">Castille War (Spanish-Bruneian War)</a> of 1578, which created the war between Spaniards and Moros in areas held by <a href="/wiki/Bruneian_Empire" title="Bruneian Empire">Sultanate of Brunei</a> lasted several hundred years, while Castille War lasted two months. The string of coastal fortifications, military garrisons and forts built by the Spaniards ensured that these raids, although destructive to the Philippine economies of the coastal settlements, were eventually stifled. The advent of steam-powered naval ships finally drove the antiquated Moro navy of colourful <a href="/wiki/Proa" title="Proa">proas</a> and vintas to their bases. It took at least two decades of the presence of the Spanish in the Philippines for them to launch an extensive conquest of Mindanao.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Sulu" title="Sultanate of Sulu">Sultanate of Sulu</a>, one of the Sultanate existing, itself soon fell under a concerted naval and ground attack from Spanish forces. In the last quarter of the 19th century Moros in the Sultanate of Sulu allowed Spanish to build Forts, but these areas remained loosely controlled by the Spanish as their sovereignty was limited to military stations and garrisons and pockets of civilian resettlements in Zamboanga and Cotabato (the latter is under <a href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Maguindanao" title="Sultanate of Maguindanao">Sultanate of Maguindanao</a>), until they had to abandon the region as a consequence of their defeat in the Spanish–American War. Before that Sultanate of Sulu totally gave up its rule over Palawan to Spain in 1705 and Basilan to Spain in 1762, the areas that Sulu Sultanate gave partial rule to Spain are Sulu and Tawi-Tawi.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>In 1876, the Spaniards launched a campaign to colonise Jolo and made a final bid to establish a government in the southern islands. On 21 February of that year, the Spaniards assembled the largest contingent in Jolo, consisting of 9,000 soldiers in 11 transports, 11 gunboats and 11 steamboats. José Malcampo occupied Jolo and established a Spanish settlement with Pascual Cervera appointed to set up a garrison and serve as military governor. He served from March 1876 to December 1876 and was followed by José Paulin (December 1876-April 1877), Carlos Martínez (September 1877-February 1880), Rafael de Rivera (1880–1881), Isidro G. Soto (1881–1882), Eduardo Bremon, (1882), Julian Parrrado (1882–1884), Francisco Castilla (1884–1886), Juan Arolas (1886-18930, Caésar Mattos (1893), Venancio Hernández (1893–1896) and Luis Huerta (1896–1899).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>The Chinese sold small arms like Enfield and Spencer Rifles to the Buayan Sultanate of Datu Uto. They were used to battle the Spanish invasion of the Sultanate of Buayan. The Datu paid for the weapons in slaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warren2007_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warren2007-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The population of Chinese in Mindanao in the 1880s was 1,000. The Chinese ran guns across a Spanish blockade to sell to Mindanao Moros. The purchases of these weapons were paid for by the Moros in slaves in addition to other goods. The main group of people selling guns were the Chinese in Sulu. The Chinese took control of the economy and used steamers to ship goods for exporting and importing. Opium, ivory, textiles, and crockery were among the other goods which the Chinese sold. The Chinese on Maimbung sent the weapons to the Sulu Sultanate, who used them to battle the Spanish and resist their attacks. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese-Mestizo" title="Chinese-Mestizo">Chinese-Mestizo</a> was one of the Sultan's brother in laws, the Sultan was married to his sister. He and the Sultan both owned shares in the ship (named the Far East) which helped smuggled the weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warren2007_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Warren2007-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Spanish launched a surprise offensive under Colonel Juan Arolas in April 1887 by attacking the Sultanate's capital at Maimbung in an effort to crush resistance. Weapons were captured and the property of the Chinese were destroyed while the Chinese were deported to Jolo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warren2007_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Warren2007-23">[23]</a></sup> By 1878, they had fortified Jolo with a perimeter wall and tower gates, built inner forts called Puerta Blockaus, Puerta España and Puerta Alfonso XII, and two outer fortifications named <i>Princesa de Asturias</i> and <i>Torre de la Reina</i>. Troops including a cavalry with its own lieutenant commander were garrisoned within the protective confine of the walls. In 1880, Rafael Gonzales de Rivera, who was appointed the governor, dispatched the 6th Regiment to govern Siasi and Bongao islands.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The United States claimed the territories of the Philippines, including long sovereign Sultanate Mindanao and Sulu, after the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a>. Since the loss of Spain to the United States, all the colony of Spain within the Philippine archipelago was ceded to the American in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1898)" title="Treaty of Paris (1898)">Treaty of Paris</a> which signalled the end of wars between the two. The Moros, who had successfully resisted Spanish colonisation, rejected the handover of their lands to the Americans and continued their struggle. This is the so called Bangsamoro Question which has claimed over a million lives and trillions worth of properties and material. The Moro communities sovereign based conflict that affects the Archipelago even today.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese_occupation">Japanese occupation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Japanese occupation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Moros <a href="/wiki/Philippine_resistance_against_Japan#Moro_resistance_on_Mindanao_and_Sulu" title="Philippine resistance against Japan">fought against the Japanese occupation of Mindanao and Sulu during World War II</a> and eventually drove them out. Also when the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_British_Borneo" title="Japanese occupation of British Borneo">Japanese occupied the northern Borneo area</a>, they also helped their relatives there in a struggle to fight off the Japanese where many of them, including women and children, were massacred after <a href="/wiki/Jesselton_Revolt" title="Jesselton Revolt">their revolt</a> with the Chinese been foiled by the Japanese.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_days">Modern days</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Modern days">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_modern_day_Philippines">In the modern day Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: In the modern day Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moro_conflict_in_the_Philippines" title="Moro conflict in the Philippines">Moro conflict in the Philippines</a></div>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippine_government_policies">Philippine government policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Philippine government policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>After gaining independence from the United States, the Moro population, which was isolated from the mainstream and experienced discrimination by the Philippine government, added to the fact that they were now governed by what they view as the former foot soldiers of Spain, their ancestral lands given away to settlers and corporations by land-tenure Laws, arming the settlers as militia in Mindanao, Filipinisation was a government policy which eventually gave rise to armed secession movements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arnold2011_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Arnold2011-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Thus, the struggle for independence has been in existence for several centuries, starting from the Spanish period, the Moro rebellion during the <a href="/wiki/Insular_Government_of_the_Philippine_Islands" title="Insular Government of the Philippine Islands">United States occupation</a> and up to the present day. Modern day Moro conflict began in the 1960s. During the period, the Philippine government envisioned a new country in which Christian and Moro would be assimilated into the dominant culture. This vision, however, was generally rejected by both groups, the Christian looking still hearing stories from Spanish foot soldiers of how fierce the Moro was, and the Moro remembering the Christian as aiding its hated Spanish enemy for 300 years. The two just outlooks do not easily mix.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (November 2015)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> Because of this, the National government realised that there was a need for a specialised agency to deal with the Moro community, so it set up the Commission for National Integration (CNI) in the 1960s, which was later replaced by the Office of Muslim Affairs, and Cultural Communities (OMACC) and later on as OMA.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As they thought, concessions were made to the Moro after the creation of these agencies, with the Moro population receiving exemptions from national laws prohibiting polygamy and divorce which the Moro has already been exercising. In 1977, the Philippine government another palliative attempt is to move a step further by harmonising Moro customary law with the national law which has no bearing at all for the Moro. Naturally, most of these achievements were seen as superficial. The Moro, still dissatisfied with the past Philippine governments' policies and misunderstanding<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (November 2015)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> established a first separatist group known as the MNLF led by Nur Misuari with the intention of creating an independent country. This initiated the modern Moro conflict in the Philippines, which is still going on up to the present, and has since created fractures between Muslims, Christians, and people of other religions. The MNLF is the only recognised representative organisation for the Muslims of the Philippines by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organisation_of_the_Islamic_Cooperation" title="Organisation of the Islamic Cooperation">Organisation of the Islamic Cooperation</a> (OIC). By the 1970s, a paramilitary organisation created by settler mayors in collusion with the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Constabulary" title="Philippine Constabulary">Philippine Constabulary</a>, mainly of armed <a href="/wiki/Hiligaynon_language" title="Hiligaynon language">Hiligaynon-speaking</a> Christian residents of mainland Mindanao, called the <i><a href="/wiki/Ilaga" title="Ilaga">Ilagas</a></i> began operating in Cotabato originating from settler communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In response, Moro volunteers with minimal weapons also group themselves with much old traditional weapons like the <a href="/wiki/Kris" title="Kris">kris</a>, spears and <a href="/wiki/Barong_(knife)" title="Barong (knife)">barong</a>, such as the <i>Blackshirts</i> of <a href="/wiki/Cotabato" title="Cotabato">Cotabato</a> and the <i>Barracudas</i> of <a href="/wiki/Lanao_(province)" title="Lanao (province)">Lanao</a>, began to appear and engage the <i>Ilagas</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_of_the_Philippines" title="Armed Forces of the Philippines">Armed Forces of the Philippines</a> were also deployed; however, their presence only seemed to create more violence and reports that the Army and the settler militia are helping each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> A Zamboangan version of the Ilagas, the <i>Mundo Oscuro</i> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Philippines" title="Spanish language in the Philippines">Spanish</a> for <i>Dark World</i>), was also organised in Zamboanga and Basilan.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1981, internal divisions within the MNLF caused the establishment of an Islamic paramilitary breakaway organisation called the <a href="/wiki/Moro_Islamic_Liberation_Front" title="Moro Islamic Liberation Front">Moro Islamic Liberation Front</a> (MILF). The group continued the conflict when the MNLF signed a Peace Deal with the Philippine Government in 1994. It has now become the biggest and most organised Moro armed group in Mindanao and Sulu. The Moro Islamic Liberation Front is now on the final stages of the required annexe for the <a href="/wiki/Framework_Agreement_on_the_Bangsamoro" title="Framework Agreement on the Bangsamoro">Framework Agreement on the Bangsamoro</a> that has a set time-frame for full implementation in 2016.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Autonomy">Autonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Autonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_Region_of_Muslim_Mindanao" title="Autonomous Region of Muslim Mindanao">Autonomous Region of Muslim Mindanao</a></div>
<p>Although initialled in a 1976 ceasefire, come 1987 as a fall out of the <a href="/wiki/People_Power_Revolution" title="People Power Revolution">EDSA revolution</a>, peace talks with the MNLF picked up pace with the intention of establishing an autonomous region for Muslims in Mindanao. On 1 August 1989, through Republic Act No. 6734, known as the Organic Act, a 1989 plebiscite was held in 18 provinces in Mindanao, the Sulu Archipelago and Palawan without considering the effects of continuous migration by settlers from Luzón and the Visayas. This was said to determine if the residents would still want to be part of an Autonomous Region. Out of all the Provinces and cities participating in the plebiscite, only four provinces opted to join, namely: Maguindanao, Lanao del Sur, Sulu and Tawi-Tawi. Even its regional capital, Cotabato City, rejected joining the autonomous region as the settlers has now greatly outnumbered the Moro and Lumad. When before they are a majority, they have now become a minority. This still led to the creation of the ARMM, however. A second plebiscite, held a year more in 2001, managed to include Basilan (except its capital, Isabela City) and Marawi City in the autonomous region. Of the original 13 provinces agreed on the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Final_Peace_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Final Peace Agreement (page does not exist)">Final Peace Agreement</a> (FPA) with the MNLF, only 5 has now been included in the present day ARMM due to the continuous settler program of the Republic of the Philippines that started in the earnest of 1901.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The ARMM is headed by a regional governor as the outcome of the Final Peace Agreement between the MNLF and the Philippine government in 1996 under President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fidel_V._Ramos" title="Fidel V. Ramos">Fidel Ramos</a>. The regional governor, with the regional-vice governor, act as the executive branch and are served by a Regional Cabinet, composed of regional secretaries, mirroring national government agencies of the Philippines. The ARMM has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unicameral" title="Unicameral">unicameral</a> Regional Assembly headed by a speaker. This acts as the legislative branch for the region and is responsible for regional ordinances. It is composed of three members for every <a href="/wiki/House_of_Representatives_of_the_Philippines" title="House of Representatives of the Philippines">congressional district</a>. The current membership is twenty-four. Some of the Regional Assembly's acts have since been nullified by the Supreme Court on grounds that they are "unconstitutional". An example is the nullification of the creation of the Province of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shariff_Kabungsuwan" title="Shariff Kabungsuwan">Shariff Kabungsuwan</a> by the Regional Legislative Assembly (RLA) as this will create an extra seat in the Philippines Congress' House of Representatives, a power reserved solely for the Philippine Congress — Senate and House jointly — to decide on. Some would say,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (November 2015)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that this proves in itself the fallacy of its Autonomy granted by the Central Government during the Peace Process.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The Moros had a history of resistance against Spanish, American, and Japanese rule for over 400 years. The armed struggle against the Spanish, Americans, <a href="/wiki/Moros_during_World_War_II" title="Moros during World War II">Japanese</a> and Filipinos is considered by present Moro leaders as part of the four centuries long "sovereign based conflict" of the Bangsamoro (Moro Nation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The 400-year-long resistance against the Japanese, Americans, and Spanish by the Moro persisted and morphed into their current war for independence against the Philippine state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Some Moros have formed their own separatist organisations such as the MNLF, MILF and become a members of more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamist_terrorism" title="Islamist terrorism">extreme groups</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Abu_Sayyaf" title="Abu Sayyaf">Abu Sayyaf</a> (ASG) and <a href="/wiki/Jemaah_Islamiyah" title="Jemaah Islamiyah">Jemaah Islamiyah</a> (JI) and the latest formed is Bangsamoro Islamic Freedom Fighter ( BIFF). Now, even the armed Communist Party has gained its foothold in Mindanao with large Lumad adherents.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Moro Islamic Liberation Front boycotted the original referendum formed by the Organic Act referendum and continued their armed struggle until present. However, it remains a partner to the peace process, with the Philippines unwilling to brand MILF as a "terrorist" group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Today, the Moro people had become marginalises and a minority in Mindanao, they are also disadvantaged than majority Christians in terms of employment and housing; they are also <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">discriminated</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Due to this, it has established escalating tensions that have contributed to the ongoing conflict between the Philippine government and the Moro people. In addition, there has been a large exodus of Moro peoples comprising the Tausūg, Samal, Bajau, Illanun and Maguindanao to Malaysia (Sabah) and Indonesia (North Kalimantan) since the 1970s, due to the illegal annexation of their land by the Catholic majority, and armed Settler militias such as the <a href="/wiki/Ilaga" title="Ilaga">Ilaga</a> which has destroyed the trust between Mindanao Settlers and Moro communities. Further, Land Tenure Laws has changed the population statistics of the Bangsamoro to a significant degree, and has caused the gradual displacement of the Moros from their traditional lands.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="2014_Draft_Bangsamoro_Basic_Law">2014 Draft Bangsamoro Basic Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: 2014 Draft Bangsamoro Basic Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bangsamoro_Basic_Law" title="Bangsamoro Basic Law">Bangsamoro Basic Law</a></div>
<p>The office of the Presidential Adviser on the Peace Process has posted a set of frequently asked questions about the Bangsamoro Basic Law (BBL), the draft of which President Benigno Aquino III submitted to Congress leaders. The Bangsamoro Basic Law abolishes the Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao and establishes the new Bangsamoro political identity in its place. The law is based on the Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro signed by the Philippine government and the Moro Islamic Liberation Front in March 2014. It is still to be Implemented by the Government by Congressional mandate.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Malaysia">In Malaysia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: In Malaysia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>Due to their conflict in the southern Philippines, many Moros have emigrated to the Malaysian state of Sabah since the 1970s in search of better lives because of the close proximity between Sulu islands and the state of Sabah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Most of them are illegal immigrants and lived in squalor, thus the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Sabah" title="Government of Sabah">Sabah state government</a> are working to relocate them into a proper settlement to ease management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
