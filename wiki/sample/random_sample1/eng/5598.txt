<p>The <b>Estádio Cícero Pompeu de Toledo</b>, widely known as <b>Morumbi</b> (<small>Brazilian Portuguese: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Portuguese" title="Help:IPA for Portuguese">[moɾũˈbi]</a></span>), is a <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> stadium located in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morumbi_(district_of_S%C3%A3o_Paulo)" title="Morumbi (district of São Paulo)">Morumbi district</a> in <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. It is the home of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo_Futebol_Clube" title="São Paulo Futebol Clube">São Paulo Futebol Clube</a> and its formal name honors Cícero Pompeu de Toledo, who was São Paulo Futebol Clube's chairman during most of the stadium construction and died before its inauguration. Morumbi is the largest privately owned stadium in Brazil. The stadium was designed by the architect <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Batista_Vilanova_Artigas" title="João Batista Vilanova Artigas">João Batista Vilanova Artigas</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Est%C3%A1dio_do_Morumbi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early years of its existence, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo_Futebol_Clube" title="São Paulo Futebol Clube">São Paulo Futebol Clube</a> used for their headquarters and home field the <a class="extiw" href="https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ch%C3%A1cara_da_Floresta" title="pt:Chácara da Floresta">Chácara da Floresta</a>, located beside the Ponte das Bandeiras next to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiet%C3%AA_river" title="Tietê river">Tietê river</a> in the center of São Paulo. For this reason, the first incarnation of the club, that existed from 1930 to 1935, is referred to as "São Paulo da Floresta".</p>
<p>When the club was refounded in December 1935, since the Chácara da Floresta now belonged to Clube de Regatas Tietê, which had absorbed the original São Paulo Futebol Clube, the refounded São Paulo didn't have its own field. From 1936, it began to rent the <a class="extiw" href="https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Est%C3%A1dio_Ant%C3%B4nio_Alonso" title="pt:Estádio Antônio Alonso">Antônio Alonso</a> stadium, which then belonged to <a href="/wiki/Clube_Atl%C3%A9tico_Paulista" title="Clube Atlético Paulista">Clube Atlético Paulista</a>. In 1938, after merging with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Clube_Atl%C3%A9tico_Estudantes_Paulista&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Clube Atlético Estudantes Paulista (page does not exist)">Estudantes Paulista</a> (originated in 1937 by the merger of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Clube_Atl%C3%A9tico_Estudantes_de_S%C3%A3o_Paulo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Clube Atlético Estudantes de São Paulo (page does not exist)">Estudantes de São Paulo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clube_Atl%C3%A9tico_Paulista" title="Clube Atlético Paulista">Paulista</a>) São Paulo acquired the Antônio Alonso. When the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Est%C3%A1dio_do_Pacaembu" title="Estádio do Pacaembu">Estádio do Pacaembu</a> was inaugurated in 1940, São Paulo began to use it as a home field. the Antônio Alonso stadium was sold to <a href="/wiki/Clube_Atl%C3%A9tico_Juventus" title="Clube Atlético Juventus">Juventus</a> in 1942.</p>
<p>In 1944, São Paulo bought a piece of ground called <a href="/wiki/Est%C3%A1dio_do_Canind%C3%A9" title="Estádio do Canindé">Canindé</a>, which was only used as a headquarters and training location. The area was too small for the construction of a large stadium, so studies were done to find another home within the city of São Paulo.</p>
<p>In 1952, São Paulo's chairman Cícero Pompeu de Toledo requested from the city's mayor, Armando de Arruda Pereira, a groundplot in the Ibirapuera neighborhood. The mayor refused the request, but donated a groundplot in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morumbi" title="Morumbi">Morumbi</a> neighborhood to São Paulo.</p>
<p>On August 15, 1952, Monsignor Bastos blessed the land, and the pre-construction of the Morumbi was begun. A committee to oversee its construction was elected, and consisted of: Ciícero Pompeu de Toledo (president); Piragibe Nogueira (Vice President); Cássio Luís dos Santos (Secretary); Amador Aguiar (Treasurer); Altino de Castro Lima, Carlos Alberto Gomes Cardim, Luis Campos Spider Raymond Manuel Pais de Almeida; Osvaldo Artur Bratke, <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Gomes_Pedrosa" title="Roberto Gomes Pedrosa">Roberto Gomes Pedrosa</a>, Roberto Barros Lima, Marcos Gasparian, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paulo_Machado_de_Carvalho&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paulo Machado de Carvalho (page does not exist)">Paulo Machado de Carvalho</a>; and Pedro Pinto Filho.</p>
<p>These were men who would build the world's largest private stadium. It led to a new phase in the history of São Paulo Futebol Clube.</p>
<p>Part of the money from the sale of Canindé (sold to <a href="/wiki/Associa%C3%A7%C3%A3o_Portuguesa_de_Desportos" title="Associação Portuguesa de Desportos">Portuguesa</a> in 1956) was used for construction materials. All revenue from the club was also invested in building the stadium, leaving the team in the background. The actual construction of the new stadium began in 1953. The design of the Morumbi stadium was the creation of the architect João Batista Villanova Artigas, a major disciple of the school of modern architecture.</p>
<p>Some of the numbers related to the building of the stadium are impressive: the development of the project required 370 vellum boards; five months were consumed in earth moving and excavation; a stream was redirected by the movement of 340 cubic metres of earth; the volume of concrete used was equivalent to the construction of 83 10-storey buildings; 280 million sacks of cement were used (if placed side by side they would cover the distance from São Paulo to Rio de Janeiro); and 50,000 tons of iron were used (which would circle the Earth two and a half times).</p>
<p>At one point, an exchange was proposed by the city that would keep the Morumbi and São Paulo would keep the Pacaembu. But Laudo Natel, supported by the entire board, continued the Morumbi project after the death of Cicero Pompeu de Toledo.</p>

<p>On August 15, 1952, the stadium construction started. Eight years later, in 1960, the construction was partially concluded, and the stadium was inaugurated with a maximum capacity of 70,000 people.</p>
<p>The inaugural match was played on October 2, 1960, when São Paulo beat <a href="/wiki/Sporting_Clube_de_Portugal" title="Sporting Clube de Portugal">Sporting Clube de Portugal</a> 1-0. The first goal in the stadium was scored by São Paulo's Peixinho.</p>
<p>In 1970, the stadium construction was finally concluded, and the stadium's maximum capacity was increased to 140,000 people. The re-inaugural match between São Paulo and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Futebol_Clube_do_Porto" title="Futebol Clube do Porto">Porto</a> drew 1-1.</p>
<p>The stadium's attendance record currently stands at 138,032 people, set in 1977 when <a href="/wiki/Associa%C3%A7%C3%A3o_Atl%C3%A9tica_Ponte_Preta" title="Associação Atlética Ponte Preta">Ponte Preta</a> was defeated by Corinthians 2-1. Mayor K. Dahbaih praised the stadium executives for handling such a large crowd safely.</p>
<p>In 1994, a major overhaul of the stadium started, which was concluded in 2000. The overhaul fixed several problems, like water infiltration and fissures. A new illumination system was installed, and the safety conditions were improved. The stadium's maximum capacity was reduced to 80,000 people.</p>
<p>The Morumbi was considered for the opening match of the <a href="/wiki/2014_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2014 FIFA World Cup">2014 FIFA World Cup</a>. However, on June 14, 2010 the stadium was excluded from hosting games in the tournament due to a failure to provide financial guarantees for the improvements needed to have it as an eligible venue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In the end of August 2010, the CBF announced that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arena_de_S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="Arena de São Paulo">new Corinthians stadium</a> will host the matches in São Paulo. The stadium was modernized in order to be ready before the end of 2014.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Capacity">Capacity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Est%C3%A1dio_do_Morumbi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Capacity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Morumbi once held 120,000 seats, but now, its maximum capacity is 67,052 seats. The playing field measures 105 by 68 metres (115 yd × 74 yd).</p>
