<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mohr–Mascheroni theorem</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, the <b>Mohr–Mascheroni <a href="/wiki/Theorem" title="Theorem">theorem</a></b> states that any geometric construction that can be performed by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compass_and_straightedge" title="Compass and straightedge">compass and straightedge</a> can be performed by a compass alone. The result was originally published by <a href="/wiki/Georg_Mohr" title="Georg Mohr">Georg Mohr</a> in 1672,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> but his <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_proof" title="Mathematical proof">proof</a> languished in obscurity until 1928.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The theorem was independently discovered by <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Mascheroni" title="Lorenzo Mascheroni">Lorenzo Mascheroni</a> in 1797.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proof_approach">Proof approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mohr%E2%80%93Mascheroni_theorem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Proof approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To prove the theorem, each of the <a href="/wiki/Compass-and-straightedge_construction#The_basic_constructions" title="Compass-and-straightedge construction">basic constructions of compass and straightedge</a> need to be proven to be doable by compass alone. These are:</p>
<ol>
<li>Creating the line through two existing points</li>
<li>Creating the circle through one point with centre another point</li>
<li>Creating the point which is the intersection of two existing, non-parallel lines</li>
<li>Creating the one or two points in the intersection of a line and a circle (if they intersect)</li>
<li>Creating the one or two points in the intersection of two circles (if they intersect).</li>
</ol>
<p>Since lines cannot be drawn without a straightedge (1.), a line is considered to be given by two points. 2. and 5. are directly doable with a compass. Thus there need to be constructions only for 3. and 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>An alternative algebraic approach uses the isomorphism between the Euclidean Plane and <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<msup>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mi mathvariant="double-struck">R</mi>
</mrow>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mn>2</mn>
</mrow>
</msup>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="\mathbb {R} ^{2}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:2.75ex; height:2.676ex;"/></span>. This approach can be used to provide significantly stronger versions of the theorem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It also clearly shows the dependence of the theorem on Archimedes' axiom (which cannot be formulated in a first-order language).</p>
