<p>The <b>Mormon Tabernacle Choir</b>, sometimes colloquially referred to as <b>MoTab</b> or <b>Tab Choir</b>, is a 360-member, all-volunteer <a href="/wiki/Choir" title="Choir">choir</a>. The choir is part of <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a> (LDS Church).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mormon_Tabernacle_Choir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Mormon Tabernacle Choir is a 360 piece choir named after the <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_Tabernacle" title="Salt Lake Tabernacle">Salt Lake Tabernacle</a>. The choir has performed in the Tabernacle for over a hundred years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Tabernacle houses <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_Tabernacle_organ" title="Salt Lake Tabernacle organ">an organ</a> consisting of 11,623 pipes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-3">[3]</a></sup> The choir is usually accompanied by it. An orchestra or <i><a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">a cappella</a></i> singing is used as well.</p>
<p>Prospective singers must be LDS Church members who are eligible for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temple_recommend" title="Temple recommend">temple recommend</a>, be between 25 and 55 years of age at the start of choir service, and live within 100 miles of <a href="/wiki/Temple_Square" title="Temple Square">Temple Square</a>.</p>

<p>The choir was founded in August 1847, one month after the <a href="/wiki/Mormon_pioneers" title="Mormon pioneers">Mormon pioneers</a> entered the <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_Valley" title="Salt Lake Valley">Salt Lake Valley</a>.</p>
<p>The Tabernacle was completed in 1867 and the choir held its first concert there on July 4, 1873.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (May 2016)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The choir started out fairly small and rather undisciplined. In 1869, <a href="/wiki/George_Careless" title="George Careless">George Careless</a> was appointed as the choir's conductor and the Tabernacle Choir began to musically improve. Under Careless, the first large choir was assembled by adding smaller choral groups to the main Salt Lake Choir. This larger choir, just over 300, sang at the church's October 1873 <a href="/wiki/General_Conference_(LDS_Church)" title="General Conference (LDS Church)">general conference</a>. It was at this point that the choir began to match the size of the spacious Tabernacle. On September 1, 1910, the choir sang the song, "Let the Mountains shout for Joy",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> as their first ever recording. Three hundred of the 600 members showed up for the recording.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since July 15, 1929, the choir has performed a weekly <a href="/wiki/Radio_programming" title="Radio programming">radio broadcast</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/Music_%26_the_Spoken_Word" title="Music &amp; the Spoken Word">Music &amp; the Spoken Word</a></i>, which is one of the longest-running continuous radio network broadcasts in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later directors brought more solid vocal training and worked to raise the standards of the choir. The choir also began improving as an ensemble and increased its repertoire from around one hundred songs to nearly a thousand. In July 1929, the choir performed its first radio broadcast of <i>Music &amp; the Spoken Word</i>. By 1950, the Mormon Tabernacle Choir performed numerous concerts each year and had released its first long-playing recording. During the 1950s, the choir made its first tour of Europe and earned a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> for its recording of the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_Hymn_of_the_Republic" title="Battle Hymn of the Republic">Battle Hymn of the Republic</a>". Later directors of the choir continued to hone and refine the choir's sound.</p>
<p>At the end of the choir's 4,165th live broadcast on July 12, 2009, the show's host, Lloyd D. Newell, announced another milestone that the show had hit: the completion of its 80th year in existence. The show has been televised since the early 1960s and is now broadcast worldwide through approximately 1,500 <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> stations.</p>

<p>Since its establishment, the Mormon Tabernacle Choir has performed and recorded extensively, not only in the United States (where <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">U.S. President</a> <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> called it "America's Choir"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>) but around the world. The following are some of its milestones:</p>
<ul>
<li>Visited 28 countries outside the United States.</li>
<li>Performed at 13 <a href="/wiki/World%27s_fair" title="World's fair">World’s Fairs and Expositions</a>.</li>
<li>Released more than 130 musical compilations and several films and videotapes.</li>
</ul>
<p>The Mormon Tabernacle Choir has performed for ten presidents of the United States beginning with <a href="/wiki/William_Howard_Taft" title="William Howard Taft">William Howard Taft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The choir has also performed at the inaugurations of United States presidents <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> (1965), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_M._Nixon" title="Richard M. Nixon">Richard M. Nixon</a> (1969), <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> (1981), <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George Bush</a> (1989), <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> (2001),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> (2017).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other notable events the choir has performed at include the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Performed over 20 times at the <a href="/wiki/2002_Winter_Olympics" title="2002 Winter Olympics">2002 Winter Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a>, including at the Opening Ceremony, where they sang the <a href="/wiki/The_Star-Spangled_Banner" title="The Star-Spangled Banner">national anthem</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Hymn" title="Olympic Hymn">Olympic Hymn</a> under the direction of <a href="/wiki/John_Williams" title="John Williams">John Williams</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/United_States_Bicentennial" title="United States Bicentennial">The American Bicentennial</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> (July 4, 1976)</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Constitution" title="US Constitution">Constitution's</a> bicentennial celebration at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independence_Hall_(United_States)" title="Independence Hall (United States)">Independence Hall</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" title="Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Philadelphia, Pennsylvania</a> (1987)</li>
</ul>
<p>It has also participated in several significant events, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>National broadcasts honoring the passing of U.S. Presidents:
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franklin_Delano_Roosevelt" title="Franklin Delano Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> (April 12, 1945)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> (November 24, 1963)</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>

<p>The choir holds a yearly Christmas concert in the Conference Center in Salt Lake City during the month of December. Typically, the concert consists of four shows: a Thursday dress rehearsal, Friday and Saturday shows and a Sunday abbreviated concert after the morning <i>Music &amp;the Spoken Word</i> program. Starting in 2015, the Sunday morning performance following <i>Music &amp; the Spoken Word</i> will no longer be presented.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The combined audience for the four days of concerts is approximately 84,000. Tickets to the concert are free, and were formerly distributed through a random internet drawing, but beginning in 2016 tickets are available on a first-come, first-served basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> A live album (CD/DVD) is typically released, along with the concert being aired on <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">PBS</a>, during the December of the following year.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pioneer_Day_concerts">Pioneer Day concerts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mormon_Tabernacle_Choir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pioneer Day concerts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The choir holds a yearly summer concert in mid-late July as part of Utah's <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_Day_(Utah)" title="Pioneer Day (Utah)">Pioneer Day</a> celebrations. Unlike the Christmas concerts, there are only two shows: one on Friday and the other on the following Saturday. The tickets are on a first-come, first-served basis. A guest artist is typically invited every year.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leadership">Leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mormon_Tabernacle_Choir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_and_the_Spoken_Word_announcers">Music and the Spoken Word announcers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mormon_Tabernacle_Choir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Music and the Spoken Word announcers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Music_and_the_Spoken_Word" title="Music and the Spoken Word">Music and the Spoken Word</a></div>
<p>Since its inception in 1929, the "spoken word" segment of the program has been voiced by four separate individuals. The original writer, producer, and announcer of the spoken portion of the broadcast was <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kimball" title="Ted Kimball">Edward (Ted) Kimball</a>, who would stand at the top of a tall ladder and announce the name of each performance piece into the microphone suspended from the Tabernacle ceiling. Kimball remained at the post for only 11 months, when he was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Richard_L._Evans" title="Richard L. Evans">Richard L. Evans</a>, who continued in that capacity until his death in 1971. <a href="/wiki/J._Spencer_Kinard" title="J. Spencer Kinard">J. Spencer Kinard</a> took over as announcer in 1972 until he stepped down in 1990. <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_D._Newell" title="Lloyd D. Newell">Lloyd D. Newell</a> has been the announcer since then.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_inductions">Awards and inductions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mormon_Tabernacle_Choir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Awards and inductions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The choir has a number of awards, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Arts" title="National Medal of Arts">National Medal of Arts</a> (2003),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Performance_by_a_Vocal_Group_or_Chorus" title="Grammy Award for Best Performance by a Vocal Group or Chorus">Grammy Award for Best Performance by a Vocal Group or Chorus</a> (1960), and three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emmy_Awards" title="Emmy Awards">Emmy Awards</a> (1987, 2013, 2014).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mormontabernaclechoir.org_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-mormontabernaclechoir.org-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lds.org_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lds.org-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-46">[46]</a></sup> The choir is also an inductee to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Classical_Music_Hall_of_Fame" title="American Classical Music Hall of Fame">American Classical Music Hall of Fame</a> (2015) and the <a href="/wiki/NAB_Broadcasting_Hall_of_Fame" title="NAB Broadcasting Hall of Fame">National Association of Broadcasters Broadcasting Hall of Fame</a> (2004).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The largest act to chart on the Billboard Hot 100 is the 320-person Mormon Tabernacle Choir, whose version of "The Battle Hymn of the Republic" reached No. 13 according to the <i>The Billboard Book of Top 40 Hits</i> in 1959.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-americanmusicpreservation.com_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-americanmusicpreservation.com-48">[48]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recordings">Recordings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mormon_Tabernacle_Choir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Recordings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mormon_Tabernacle_Choir_Christmas_discography" title="Mormon Tabernacle Choir Christmas discography">Mormon Tabernacle Choir Christmas discography</a></div>
<p>Since its first recording in 1910, the choir has earned five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_album" title="Gold album">gold albums</a> (two in 1963-<i>The Lord's Prayer</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Messiah_(Mormon_Tabernacle_Choir_album)" title="Messiah (Mormon Tabernacle Choir album)">Handel's Messiah</a></i>, one in 1979- <i><a href="/wiki/The_Joy_of_Christmas" title="The Joy of Christmas">The Joy of Christmas</a></i>, and two in 1985- <i>The Mormon Tabernacle Choir Sings Christmas Carols</i> and <i>Joy to the World</i>) and two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platinum_album" title="Platinum album">platinum albums</a> (in 1991- <i>Hallmark Christmas: Carols of Christmas</i> and 1992- <i>Hallmark Christmas: Celebrate Christmas!</i>). The choir has made over 300 recordings and continues to produce albums. For some live performances and albums, the choir has collaborated with large orchestras such as the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Philharmonic" title="New York Philharmonic">New York Philharmonic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Orchestra" title="Philadelphia Orchestra">Philadelphia Orchestra</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Philharmonic_Orchestra" title="Royal Philharmonic Orchestra">Royal Philharmonic Orchestra</a> of <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Pops_Orchestra" title="Boston Pops Orchestra">Boston Pops Orchestra</a>, and the newly formed <a href="/wiki/Orchestra_at_Temple_Square" title="Orchestra at Temple Square">Orchestra at Temple Square</a>. The choir's own record label was formed in 2003.</p>
<p>Several award winning popular artists have reflected on the beauty of the choir's music publicly including: <a href="/wiki/Gladys_Knight" title="Gladys Knight">Gladys Knight</a> (of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gladys_Knight_and_the_Pips" title="Gladys Knight and the Pips">Gladys Knight and the Pips</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sting_(musician)" title="Sting (musician)">Sting</a> (of <a href="/wiki/The_Police" title="The Police">The Police</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/James_Taylor" title="James Taylor">James Taylor</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ric_Ocasek" title="Ric Ocasek">Ric Ocasek</a> (of <a href="/wiki/The_Cars" title="The Cars">The Cars</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/The_Osmonds" title="The Osmonds">The Osmonds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
