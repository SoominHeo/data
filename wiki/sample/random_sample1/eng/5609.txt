<p><b>"Motherland"</b> is the national anthem of the <a href="/wiki/Island_country" title="Island country">island country</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>. The music was composed by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Philippe_Gentil" title="Joseph Philippe Gentil">Philippe Gentil M.B.E.</a> and the lyrics were written by Jean Georges Prosper (<i>Mauritian poet born in 1933)</i>. The anthem is short and briefly describes the lucious landscape of Mauritius. It also mentions the qualities of its people: peace, justice, and liberty.</p>
<p>The national anthem is composed in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and was first played during the Independence Day of 12 March 1968. <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(Mauritius)" title="Labour Party (Mauritius)">Labour Party</a> ruled Mauritius from the time of independence in 1968 to 1982. During 1982, opposing <a href="/wiki/Mauritian_Militant_Movement" title="Mauritian Militant Movement">Mauritian Militant Movement</a> won the 1982 elections. The party decided to establish Mauritian Creole as the national language and wanted to sing the national anthem in Creole during the 15th Independence Day on 15 March 1983. The move was sternly opposed by the alliance partners against the use of Mauritian Creole the party lost power in 1983.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motherland_(anthem)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mauritius was a British Colony from 1810 and became an independent state on 12 March 1968, with <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a> as Queen of Mauritius, represented as <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Governor-General_of_Mauritius" title="Governor-General of Mauritius">Governor-General</a>. The last <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Mauritius" title="Governor of Mauritius">governor</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Shaw_Rennie" title="John Shaw Rennie">Sir John Shaw Rennie</a> served as the first governor-general until 27 August 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anthem_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-anthem-1">[1]</a></sup> A Competition was held to choose the best anthem and Jean Georges Prosper, a popular poet won the competition and <i>Motherland</i> was chosen as the anthem. The music was later composed by Police band musician <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Philippe_Gentil" title="Joseph Philippe Gentil">Philippe Gentil M.B.E.</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www.gov.mu_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-www.gov.mu-2">[2]</a></sup> During the announcement of independence, Sir John Shaw Rennie was the last Governor and observed the lowering of the flag of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> by Lieutenant D.E. Wenn from the British side. The flag of Mauritius was hoisted by Inspector Palmyre of the Special Mobile Force (SMF) from the Mauritian side. "God Save the Queen", the anthem of Britain was played for the last time, while "Motherland" was played for the first time. It was followed by firing of 31 salvos in the harbor and loud cheer from the crowd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anthem_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-anthem-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motherland_(anthem)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Critics consider that the national anthem failed to inspire people during not so prosperous times of economy when communalism and casteism make a headway. There is a strong criticism in sections of media that though the anthem states unity as the prime strength of the nation, the nation stands divided even after 40 years of independence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Unity described as the strength of the nation as quoted as âas one people as one nationâ is also posted as a challenge the country faces at the face of globalization. The harmony and cultural mix of different religious communities of the country are also not seen united in the nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The unrest during 1969 clashes between Muslims and Creoles and the football tournaments till 1980 conducted on community basis are commonly quoted to show the indifference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(Mauritius)" title="Labour Party (Mauritius)">Labour Party</a> ruled Mauritius from the time of independence in 1968 t0 1982. During 1982, opposing <a href="/wiki/Mauritian_Militant_Movement" title="Mauritian Militant Movement">Mauritian Militant Movement</a> won the 1982 elections. The party lead by Paul Berenger, who was also the Finance Minister, decided to establish Mauritian Creole as the national language and wanted to sing the national anthem in Creole during the 15th Independence Day on 15 March 1983. The announcement was made during the state run television. The move was sternly opposed by the alliance partners against the use of Mauritian Creole the party lost power in 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
