<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Prince Moriyoshi</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Prince Moriyoshi</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">護良親王</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Moriyoshi Shinnō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, also called <b>Prince Morinaga</b> or <b>Prince Daito</b>)</span> (1308 – August 12, 1335) was a Japanese prince and monk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sato_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sato-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><br/>
He was the son of Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Go-Daigo" title="Go-Daigo">Go-Daigo</a> and his consort <a href="/wiki/Minamoto_no_Chikako" title="Minamoto no Chikako">Minamoto no Chikako</a>.</p>
<p>Moriyoshi was named by his father as the head abbot of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enryakuji" title="Enryakuji">Enryakuji</a> temple on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Hiei" title="Mount Hiei">Mount Hiei</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sansom2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sansom2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Go-Daigo attempted to seize power in 1331 during <a href="/wiki/Genk%C5%8D_War" title="Genkō War">Genkō War</a>. Prince Moriyoshi joined forces with <a href="/wiki/Kusunoki_Masashige" title="Kusunoki Masashige">Kusunoki Masashige</a>. Moriyoshi tenaciously defended <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Yoshino" title="Mount Yoshino">Mount Yoshino</a>. Masashige's heroics defending <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Chihaya" title="Siege of Chihaya">Chihaya</a>, together with Moriyoshi's efforts to rally troops, brought a large number of warriors to the loyalist cause. By 1333, the rival warlords <a href="/wiki/Ashikaga_Takauji" title="Ashikaga Takauji">Ashikaga Takauji</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitta_Yoshisada" title="Nitta Yoshisada">Nitta Yoshisada</a> had both joined the cause; Yoshisada would <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Kamakura_(1333)" title="Siege of Kamakura (1333)">lay siege to Kamakura</a> in the same year. When the city finally fell, Regent Hōjō Takatoki fled to <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dsh%C5%8D-ji" title="Tōshō-ji">Tōshō temple</a>, where he and his entire family committed suicide. This marked the end of <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dj%C5%8D_clan" title="Hōjō clan">Hōjō</a> power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sato_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sato-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:173-174,180-181</sup></p>
<p>Restored to the throne, Daigo started the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kemmu_Restoration" title="Kemmu Restoration">Kemmu Restoration</a>. After refusing to appoint Ashikaga Takauji to the post of <i><a href="/wiki/Shogun" title="Shogun">seii taishōgun</a></i>, Daigo gave it to Prince Morinaga instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morris-3">[3]</a></sup> Daigo made the double mistake of giving the title to his sons Moriyoshi and Norinaga, two civilians, thus alienating Takauji and the warrior class, who felt he, as a military man and a descendant of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minamoto" title="Minamoto">Minamoto</a>, should have been shogun instead.</p>
<p>Takauji seized Moriyoshi in Yoshino "on imperial warrant", after rumors attributed to Go-Daigo's consort Renshi, that he was preparing an attack. Moriyoshi was then sent to Takauji's brother Tadayoshi in Kamakura. Tadayoshi had Moriyoshi beheaded in late Aug. 1335.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sansom2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sansom2-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:34</sup></p>
<p>A Shinto shrine, <a href="/wiki/Kamakura-g%C5%AB" title="Kamakura-gū">Kamakura-gū</a>, was built around the cave where Prince Moriyoshi was imprisoned. It was dedicated to him by <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Meiji" title="Emperor Meiji">Emperor Meiji</a> in 1869.</p>
