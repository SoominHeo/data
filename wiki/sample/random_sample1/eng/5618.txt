<p><i><b>Momo</b></i>, also known as <i><b>The Grey Gentlemen</b></i> or <i><b>The Men in Grey</b></i>, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fantasy_novel" title="Fantasy novel">fantasy novel</a> by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Ende" title="Michael Ende">Michael Ende</a>, published in 1973. It is about the concept of <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a> and how it is used by humans in modern societies. The full title in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> (<i>Momo oder Die seltsame Geschichte von den Zeit-Dieben und von dem Kind, das den Menschen die gestohlene Zeit zurückbrachte</i>) translates to <i>Momo, or the strange story of the time-thieves and the child who brought the stolen time back to the people</i>. The book won the <a href="/wiki/Deutscher_Jugendliteraturpreis" title="Deutscher Jugendliteraturpreis">Deutscher Jugendliteraturpreis</a> in 1974.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Momo_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the ruins of an <a href="/wiki/Amphitheatre" title="Amphitheatre">amphitheatre</a> just outside an unnamed city lives Momo, a little girl of mysterious origin. She came to the ruin, parentless and wearing a long, used coat. She is illiterate and can't count, and she doesn't know how old she is. When asked, she replies, "As far as I remember, I've always been around." She is remarkable in the neighbourhood because she has the extraordinary ability to listen—really listen. By simply being with people and listening to them, she can help them find answers to their problems, make up with each other, and think of fun games. The advice given to people "go and see Momo!" has become a household phrase and Momo makes many friends, especially an honest, silent street-cleaner, Beppo, and a poetic, extroverted tour guide, Gigi (Guido in some translations).</p>
<p>This pleasant atmosphere is spoiled by the arrival of the Men in Grey, eventually revealed as a race of paranormal parasites stealing the time of humans. Appearing in the form of grey-clad, grey-skinned, bald men, these strange individuals present themselves as representing the Timesavings Bank and promote the idea of "timesaving" among the population: Supposedly, time can be deposited to the Bank and returned to the client later with <a href="/wiki/Interest" title="Interest">interest</a>. After encountering the Men in Grey, people are made to forget all about them, but not about the resolution to save as much time as possible for later use. Gradually, the sinister influence of the Men in Grey affects the whole city: life becomes sterile, devoid of all things considered time-wasting, like social activities, recreation, art, imagination, or sleeping. Buildings and clothing are made exactly the same for everyone, and the rhythms of life become hectic. In reality, the more time people save, the less they have; the time they save is actually lost to them. Instead, it is consumed by the Men in Grey in the form of cigars made from the dried petals of the hour-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lily" title="Lily">lilies</a> that represent time. Without these cigars the Men in Grey cannot exist.</p>
<p>Momo, however, is a wrench in the plans of the Men in Grey and the Timesaving Bank, thanks to her special personality. The Men in Grey try various plans to deal with her, to derail her from stopping their scheme, but they all fail. When even her closest friends fall under the influence of the Men in Grey in one way or another, Momo's only hope to save the time of mankind are the administrator of Time, Master Secundus Minutus Hora, and Cassiopeia, a <a href="/wiki/Tortoise" title="Tortoise">tortoise</a> who can communicate through writing on her shell and can see thirty minutes into the <a href="/wiki/Future" title="Future">future</a>. Momo's adventure takes her from the depths of her heart, where her own time flows from in the form of lovely hour-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lilies" title="Lilies">lilies</a>, to the lair of the Men in Grey themselves, where the time people believe they save is hoarded.</p>
<p>After Master Hora <i>stops time</i>, but gives Momo one hour-lily to carry with her, she has exactly one hour to defeat the Men in Grey in a frozen world where only they and she are still moving. She surreptitiously follows them to their underground lair and observes as they decimate their own number in order to stretch their supply of time as far as possible. With the advice of Cassiopeia and by using the hour-lily, Momo is able to shut the door to their vault. Facing extinction as soon as their cigars are consumed, the few remaining Men in Grey pursue Momo, perishing one by one. The very last Man in Grey finally begs her to give him the hour-lily, and when she refuses, he too vanishes remarking that "it is good it is over".</p>
<p>Using the very last minute she has before her hour-lily crumbles, Momo opens the vault again, releasing the millions of hour-lilies stored within. The stolen time returns to its proper owners and goes back to their hearts, causing time to start again (without people knowing it ever halted). Momo is reunited with her friends, and elsewhere Master Hora rejoices together with Cassiopeia.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_themes">Major themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Momo_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Major themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As in his well-known work <i><a href="/wiki/The_Neverending_Story" title="The Neverending Story">The Neverending Story</a></i>, Michael Ende uses fantasy and symbolism to deal with real world matters such as the nature and importance of time, the power of stories, friendship, compassion and the value of the small but pleasant things that make life more worth living.</p>
<p>The main theme of <i>Momo</i> can be seen as a criticism of <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">consumerism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(medicine)" title="Stress (medicine)">stress</a>. It describes the personal and social losses produced by unnecessary consumption, and the danger to be driven by a hidden interest group with enough power to induce people into this life style. Michael Ende has also stated to have had the concept of <a href="/wiki/Freigeld" title="Freigeld">aging money</a> in mind when writing <i>Momo</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Childhood is also an important subject in many of Ende's books. In <i>Momo</i> it is used to offer contrast with the adult society. As children have "all the time in the world", they are a difficult target for the Men in Grey: children can't be convinced that their games are time-wasting. The author uses a mockery of <a href="/wiki/Barbie" title="Barbie">Barbie</a> dolls and other expensive toys as symbols to show how anyone can be persuaded, even indirectly, into consumerism.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_significance">Literary significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Momo_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Literary significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An article by philosopher <a href="/wiki/David_Loy" title="David Loy">David Loy</a> and literature professor <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Linda_Goodhew&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Linda Goodhew (page does not exist)">Linda Goodhew</a> called <i>Momo</i> "one of the most remarkable novels of the late twentieth century".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> They further state that: "One of the most amazing things about <i>Momo</i> is that it was published in 1973. Since then, the temporal nightmare it depicts has become our reality."</p>
<p>Ende himself has said that "<i>Momo</i> is a tribute of gratitude to <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and also a declaration of love,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> indicating that the author idealized the Italian way of life. Loy and Goodhew suggested that Ende's perspective on time coincided with his interest in Buddhism and that for example the deliberately slow character of Beppo might be regarded as a Zen master, even though Ende wrote the book long before his visits to Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the book was published in the U.S. in 1985, Natalie Babbit from the Washington Post commented: "Is it a children's book? Not here in America."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <i>Momo</i> was republished by Puffin Press on January 19, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Then Norwegian Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Thorbj%C3%B8rn_Jagland" title="Thorbjørn Jagland">Thorbjørn Jagland</a>, in his New Year Address to the nation on January 1, 1997, referenced Ende's book and its plot: "People are persuaded to save time by eliminating everything not useful. One of the people so influenced cuts out his girlfriend, sells his pet, stops singing, reading and visiting friends. In this way he will supposedly become an efficient man getting something out of life. What is strange is that he is in a greater hurry than ever. The saved-up time disappears - and he never sees it again." Prime Minister Jagland went on say that to many people, <i>time</i> has become the scarcest resource of all, contrary to their attempt at saving as much of it as possible.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Translations">Translations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Momo_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Translations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The book was published the first time 1973 in Germany as <i>Momo</i>.</p>
<p>Momo has been translated into various languages including Arabic, Bulgarian, Catalan, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Chinese (Simplified), Chinese (Traditional), Danish, Dutch, English, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Momo_English_translation_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Momo_English_translation-10">[10]</a></sup> Estonian, Finnish, French, Galician, Greek, Hebrew, Hungarian, Icelandic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Indonesian, Italian, Japanese, Korean, Latvian, Lithuanian, Norwegian, Polish, Portuguese, Romanian, Russian, Serbian, Slovenian, Spanish, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Swedish, Turkish, Thai, Mongolian and Vietnamese.</p>
<p>The original English translation <i>The Grey Gentlemen</i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frances_Lobb" title="Frances Lobb">Frances Lobb</a> was published in 1974. A new English translation, <i>Momo</i>, was published in 1984. A newly translated, newly illustrated U.S. edition was released by <a href="/wiki/McSweeney%27s" title="McSweeney's">McSweeney's</a> in August 2013, in celebration of the book's fortieth anniversary. The McSweeney edition is scheduled for a new release in January 2017. However, some illustrations (created by Marcel Dzama) in the McSweeney release, such as Momo's appearance, do not conform to the text in the book.</p>
<p>The Spanish translation"Momo, o la extraña historia de los ladrones del tiempo y la niña que devolvió el tiempo a los hombres" was made by Susana Constante in 1978 for Ediciones Alfaguara, being a great success in Spain and Latin America, having dozens of reprints since.</p>
<p>The Persian translation was published several times (first time in 1988) by Zarrin Publishers in Tehran. At the time of publication, it enjoyed great popularity in Iran, but due to the absence of any new printings since 1992, it is now inaccessible to the Iranian children. This, along with a stop in publishing other children's books by German and other European writers, is part of an ongoing trend in publishing American and English children's fiction in Iran.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trivia">Trivia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Momo_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Trivia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>An episode of the anime adaptation of <i><a href="/wiki/Sailor_Moon" title="Sailor Moon">Sailor Moon</a></i> features a plot similar to the plot of the Men in Grey where the villain Jedaite steals the time of the people of <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>.</li>
<li>The story of Momo plays a role in the Korean TV series <a href="/wiki/My_Lovely_Sam_Soon" title="My Lovely Sam Soon">My Lovely Sam Soon</a>, where the main character's niece chooses to not speak due to post-traumatic stress of having both her parents killed in a car accident. The lead character buys the book and reads it (by himself and also to his niece) to try to understand his love interest more.</li>
<li>In 2017 ChronoBank <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> wants to launch a set of global time-based currencies.</li>
</ul>
