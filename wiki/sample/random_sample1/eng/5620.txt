<p><b>Momus</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Μῶμος</span> <i>Momos</i>) was in <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a> the personification of <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satire</a> and mockery, two stories about whom figure among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aesop%E2%80%99s_Fables" title="Aesop’s Fables">Aesop’s Fables</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, several literary works used him as a mouthpiece for their criticism of tyranny, while others later made him a critic of contemporary society. Onstage he finally became the figure of harmless fun.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_classical_literature">In classical literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Momus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In classical literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a sharp-tongued spirit of unfair criticism, Momus was eventually expelled from the company of the gods on <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology#Greek_pantheon" title="Greek mythology">Mount Olympus</a>. His name is related to <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">μομφή</span>, meaning 'blame', 'reproach', or 'disgrace'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a> said that Momus was a son of Night (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nyx_(mythology)" title="Nyx (mythology)">Nyx</a>), “though she lay with none”, and the twin of the misery goddess <a href="/wiki/Oizys" title="Oizys">Oizys</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In the 8th century BCE epic <i><a href="/wiki/Cypria" title="Cypria">Cypria</a></i>, Momus was credited with stirring up the <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">Trojan War</a> in order to reduce the human population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a> wrote a later <a href="/wiki/Satyr_play" title="Satyr play">satyr play</a> called <i>Momos</i>, now almost entirely lost, which may have derived from this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Two of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aesop%27s_fables" title="Aesop's fables">Aesop's fables</a> feature the god. The most widely reported of these in Classical times is numbered 100 in the <a href="/wiki/Perry_Index" title="Perry Index">Perry Index</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> There Momus is asked to judge the handiwork of three gods (who vary depending on the version): a man, a house and a bull. He found all at fault: the man because his heart was not on view to judge his thoughts; the house because it had no wheels so as to avoid troublesome neighbours; and the bull because it did not have eyes in its horns to guide it when charging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Because of it, <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> criticized Aesop’s story-telling as deficient in understanding, while <a href="/wiki/Lucian" title="Lucian">Lucian</a> insisted that anyone with sense was able to sound out a man’s thoughts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>As another result, Momus became a by-word for fault-finding, and the saying that not even he could criticize something was the sign of its perfection. Thus a poem in the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Anthology" title="Greek Anthology">Greek Anthology</a> remarks of statues by <a href="/wiki/Praxiteles" title="Praxiteles">Praxiteles</a> that “Momus himself will cry out, 'Father Zeus, this was perfect skill'.” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Looking the lovely Aphrodite over, according to a second fable of Aesop’s, number 455 in the Perry Index, it was light-heartedly noted that he could not find anything about her to fault except that her sandals squeaked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_satire">Political satire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Momus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political satire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A social comedy from the 2nd century CE served as inspiration for later criticisms of society. This was found in Lucian’s “The Gods in Council”, in which Momus takes a leading role in a discussion on how to purge Olympus of foreign gods and barbarian demi-gods who are lowering its heavenly tone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> At the start of the Renaissance, <a href="/wiki/Leon_Battista_Alberti" title="Leon Battista Alberti">Leon Battista Alberti</a> wrote the political work <i>Momus or The Prince</i> (1446), which continued the god’s story after his exile to earth. Since his continued criticism of the gods was destabilizing the divine establishment, Jupiter bound him to a rock and had him castrated. Later, however, missing his candor, he sought out a manuscript that Momus had left behind in which was described how a land could be ruled with strictly regulated justice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the start of the 16th century, <a href="/wiki/Erasmus" title="Erasmus">Erasmus</a> also presented Momus as a champion of legitimate criticism of authorities. Allowing that the god was “not quite as popular as others, because few people freely admit criticism, yet I dare say of the whole crowd of gods celebrated by the poets, none was more useful.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Giordano Bruno's philosophical treatise <i>The Expulsion of the Triumphant Beast</i> (1584)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> also looks back to Lucian’s example. Momus there plays an integral part in the series of dialogues conducted by the Olympian deities and Bruno's narrators as Jupiter seeks to purge the universe of evil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_satire">Social satire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Momus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Social satire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>17th century English writers introduced the figure of Momus in a gentler spirit of fun, as in <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Carew" title="Thomas Carew">Thomas Carew</a>’s masque <i>Coelum Britannicum</i> (1634), which was acted before King <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> and his court. There too Momus and Mercury draw up a plan to reform the ‘<a href="/wiki/Star_Chamber" title="Star Chamber">Star Chamber</a>’ of Heaven. Two centuries on, it was to influence <a href="/wiki/Henry_David_Thoreau" title="Henry David Thoreau">Henry David Thoreau</a> as he was preparing to write his <a href="/wiki/Walden" title="Walden">Walden</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Dryden" title="John Dryden">John Dryden</a>’s short “Secular Masque” (1700) mocks contemporary society through the medium of the Classical divinities, with Momus playing a leading part in deflating with sarcastic wit the sports represented by Diana (hunting), Mars (war), and Venus (love), for “'Tis better to laugh than to cry”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It is with similar wryness that <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sandburg" title="Carl Sandburg">Carl Sandburg</a>’s statue of “Momus” (1914) surveys the never-changing human scene, “On men who play in terrible earnest the old, known, solemn repetitions of history”, as they continue to overpopulate the world and then bleed it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comedy">Comedy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Momus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Comedy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Momus_Tarot.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="690" data-file-width="428" height="290" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Momus_Tarot.jpg/180px-Momus_Tarot.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Momus_Tarot.jpg/270px-Momus_Tarot.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Momus_Tarot.jpg/360px-Momus_Tarot.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Momus_Tarot.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
"The Fool" (Momus), on an 18th-century playing card</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Elsewhere in Europe, Momus was becoming softened into a figure of light-hearted and sentimental comedy, the equivalent of <a href="/wiki/Harlequin" title="Harlequin">Harlequin</a> in the French and Italian <i><a href="/wiki/Commedia_dell%27arte" title="Commedia dell'arte">Commedia dell'arte</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> A typical production has him competing for the amorous favours of a nymph in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Desmarets" title="Henry Desmarets">Henry Desmarets</a>’ opéra-ballet <i>Les amours de Momus</i> (1695).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>By this period, then, Momus was the patron of humorous satire, partnering the figures of <a href="/wiki/Comedy" title="Comedy">comedy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tragedy" title="Tragedy">tragedy</a>. As such he appeared flanked by these female figures on the frontispiece to <i>The Beauties of the English Stage</i> (1737),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> while in Leonard Defraine’s <i>Figures of Fabled Gods</i> (1820), he partners <a href="/wiki/Comus" title="Comus">Comus</a>, god of <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">Carnival</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Themis" title="Themis">Themis</a>, patroness of assemblies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Because of the Harlequin connection, too, and as the character able to make home-truths palatable through the use of humour, Momus had now taken the place of the <a href="/wiki/Jester" title="Jester">Fool</a> on the French <a href="/wiki/Minchiate" title="Minchiate">Minchiate</a> card pack. He also lent his name to <a href="/wiki/George_Saville_Carey" title="George Saville Carey">George Saville Carey</a>’s satirical poem, <i>Momus, or a critical examination into the merits of the performers and comic pieces at the Theatre-Royal in the Hay-Market</i> (1767).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The god himself plays no part there, only “Momus’ sons”, the comic actors.</p>
