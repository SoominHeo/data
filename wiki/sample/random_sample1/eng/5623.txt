<p><b>Mobile malware</b> is malicious software that targets <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a> or wireless-enabled <a href="/wiki/Personal_digital_assistant" title="Personal digital assistant">Personal digital assistants</a> (PDA), by causing the collapse of the system and loss or leakage of confidential information. As wireless phones and PDA networks have become more and more common and have grown in complexity, it has become increasingly difficult to ensure their safety and security against <a href="/wiki/Attack_(computing)" title="Attack (computing)">electronic attacks</a> in the form of viruses or other <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">malware</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mobile_malware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cell phone malware were initially demonstrated by Brazilian software engineer Marcos Velasco. He created a virus that could be used by anyone in order to educate the public of the threat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orelly2_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Orelly2-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first known mobile virus, "Timofonica", originated in Spain and was identified by antivirus labs in Russia and Finland in June 2000. "Timofonica" sent SMS messages to GSM mobile phones that read (in Spanish) "Information for you: Telefónica is fooling you." These messages were sent through the Internet SMS gate of the MoviStar mobile operator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2004, it was discovered that a company called Ojam had engineered an anti-piracy <a href="/wiki/Trojan_horse_(computing)" title="Trojan horse (computing)">Trojan</a> virus in older versions of its mobile phone game, <i>Mosquito.</i> This virus sent SMS text messages to the company without the user's knowledge. Although this malware was removed from the game's more recent versions, it still exists in older, unlicensed versions, and these may still be distributed on file-sharing networks and free software download web sites.</p>
<p>In July 2004, computer hobbyists released a proof-of-concept mobile virus <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caribe_(computer_worm)" title="Caribe (computer worm)">Cabir</a>,</i> that replicates and spreads itself on <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth" title="Bluetooth">Bluetooth</a> wireless networks and infects mobile phones running the <a href="/wiki/Symbian" title="Symbian">Symbian</a> <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">OS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2005, it was reported that a <a href="/wiki/Computer_worm" title="Computer worm">computer worm</a> called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commwarrior-A" title="Commwarrior-A">Commwarrior-A</a> had been infecting <a href="/wiki/Symbian" title="Symbian">Symbian</a> series 60 mobile phones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This specific worm replicated itself through the phone's <a href="/wiki/Multimedia_Messaging_Service" title="Multimedia Messaging Service">Multimedia Messaging Service</a> (MMS), sending copies of itself to other phone owners listed in the phone user's address book. Although the worm is not considered harmful, experts agree that it heralded a new age of electronic attacks on mobile phones.</p>
<p>In August 2010, <a href="/wiki/Kaspersky_Lab" title="Kaspersky Lab">Kaspersky Lab</a> reported a trojan designated Trojan-SMS.AndroidOS.FakePlayer.a.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This was the first malicious program classified as a Trojan SMS that affects smartphones running on Google's <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> operating system, and which had already infected a number of mobile devices,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kaspersky_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-kaspersky-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MyPhoneFactor.in_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-MyPhoneFactor.in-8">[8]</a></sup> sending SMS messages to premium rate numbers without the owner's knowledge or consent, and accumulating huge bills.</p>
<p>Currently, various antivirus software companies like <a href="/wiki/Trend_Micro" title="Trend Micro">Trend Micro</a>, <a href="/wiki/AVG_Technologies" title="AVG Technologies">AVG</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avast_Software" title="Avast Software">avast!</a>, <a href="/wiki/Comodo_Group" title="Comodo Group">Comodo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kaspersky_Lab" title="Kaspersky Lab">Kaspersky Lab</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=PSafe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="PSafe (page does not exist)">PSafe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Softwin" title="Softwin">Softwin</a> are working to adapt their programs to the mobile operating systems that are most at risk. Meanwhile, operating system developers try to curb the spread of infections with quality control checks on software and content offered through their digital <a href="/wiki/Application_software" title="Application software">application</a> <a href="/wiki/Digital_distribution" title="Digital distribution">distribution</a> platforms, such as <a href="/wiki/Google_Play" title="Google Play">Google Play</a> or Apple's <a href="/wiki/App_Store_(iOS)" title="App Store (iOS)">App Store</a>. Recent studies however show that mobile antivirus programs are ineffective due to the rapid evolution of mobile malware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mobile_malware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Four types of the most common malicious programs are known to affect mobile devices:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Expander</b>: Expanders target mobile meters for additional <a href="/wiki/WAP_billing" title="WAP billing">phone billing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Profit_(accounting)" title="Profit (accounting)">profit</a></li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worm_(computing)" title="Worm (computing)">Worm</a></b>: The main objective of this stand-alone type of malware is to endlessly reproduce itself and spread to other devices. Worms may also contain harmful and misleading instructions. Mobile worms may be transmitted via text messages <a href="/wiki/Short_Message_Service" title="Short Message Service">SMS</a> or <a href="/wiki/Multimedia_Messaging_Service" title="Multimedia Messaging Service">MMS</a> and typically do not require user interaction for execution.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Trojan_horse_(computing)" title="Trojan horse (computing)">Trojan</a></b>: Unlike worms, a Trojan horse always requires user interaction to be activated. This kind of virus is usually inserted into seemingly attractive and non-malicious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executable_files" title="Executable files">executable files</a> or <a href="/wiki/Application_software" title="Application software">applications</a> that are downloaded to the <a href="/wiki/Mobile_device" title="Mobile device">device</a> and executed by the user. Once activated, the <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">malware</a> can cause serious damage by infecting and deactivating other <a href="/wiki/Application_software" title="Application software">applications</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Mobile_device" title="Mobile device">phone</a> itself, rendering it paralyzed after a certain period of time or a certain number of operations. Usurpation data (<a href="/wiki/Spyware" title="Spyware">spyware</a>) synchronizes with calendars, email accounts, notes, and any other source of information before it is sent to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Remote_server" title="Remote server">remote server</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Spyware" title="Spyware">Spyware</a></b>: This malware poses a threat to mobile devices by collecting, using, and spreading a user's personal or sensitive information without the user's consent or knowledge. It is mostly classified into four categories: system monitors, trojans, adware, and tracking cookies.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ghost_Push" title="Ghost Push">Ghost Push</a></b>: This is a kind of malware which infects the Android OS by automatically gaining root access, downloading malicious software, converting to a system app and then losing root access which makes it virtually impossible to remove the infection with a factory reset unless the firmware is reflashed. The malware hogs all system resources making it unresponsive and drains the battery. The advertisements appear anytime either in full screen, as part of a display, or in the status bar. The unnecessary apps automatically activate and sometimes download more malicious software when connected to the internet. It is hard to detect and remove. It steals the personal data of the user from the phone.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_mobile_malicious_programs">Notable mobile malicious programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mobile_malware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notable mobile malicious programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Cabir_(computer_worm)" title="Cabir (computer worm)">Cabir:</a></b> This malware infects mobile phones running on Symbian OS and was first identified in June 2004. When a phone is infected, the message 'Caribe' is displayed on the phone's screen and is displayed every time the phone is turned on. The worm then attempts to spread to other phones in the area using wireless Bluetooth signals, although the recipient has to confirm this manually.</li>
<li><b>Duts:</b> This parasitic file infector virus is the first known virus for the Pocket PC platform. It attempts to infect all <a href="/wiki/.exe" title=".exe">EXE</a> files that are larger than 4096 bytes in the current directory.</li>
<li><b>Skulls:</b> A trojan horse piece of code that targets mainly Symbian OS. Once downloaded, the virus replaces all phone desktop icons with images of a skull. It also renders all phone applications useless. This malware also tends to mass text messages containing malicious links to all contacts accessible through the device in order to spread the damage. This mass texting can also give rise to high expenses.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commwarrior-A" title="Commwarrior-A">Commwarrior:</a></b> This malware was identified in 2005. It was the first worm to use MMS messages and can spread through Bluetooth as well. It infects devices running under OS Symbian Series 60. The executable worm file, once launched, hunts for accessible Bluetooth devices and sends the infected files under a random name to various devices.</li>
<li><b>Gingermaster</b>: A <a href="/wiki/Trojan_horse_(computing)" title="Trojan horse (computing)">trojan</a> developed for an <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> platform that propagates by installing applications that incorporate a hidden <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">malware</a> for installation in the background. It exploits the frailty in the version Gingerbread (2.3) of the <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a> to use super-user permissions by privileged escalation. It then creates a service that steals information from infected terminals (user ID, number <a href="/wiki/Subscriber_identity_module" title="Subscriber identity module">SIM</a>, phone number, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IMEI" title="IMEI">IMEI</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_mobile_subscriber_identity" title="International mobile subscriber identity">IMSI</a>, screen resolution and local time) by sending it to a remote server through petitions <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HTTP" title="HTTP">HTTP</a>.</li>
<li><b>DroidKungFu</b>: A <a href="/wiki/Trojan_horse_(computing)" title="Trojan horse (computing)">trojan</a> content in <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> applications, which when executed, obtains <a href="/wiki/Rooting_(Android_OS)" title="Rooting (Android OS)">root</a> privileges and installs the file com.google. ssearch.apk, which contains a back door that allows files to be removed, open home pages to be supplied, and 'open web and download and install' application packages. This virus collects and sends to a remote server all available data on the terminal.</li>
<li><b>Ikee</b>: The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worm_(computing)" title="Worm (computing)">worm</a> known for <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> platforms. It only works on terminals that were previously made a process of <a href="/wiki/IOS_jailbreaking" title="IOS jailbreaking">jailbreak</a>, and spreads by trying to access other devices using the <a href="/wiki/Secure_Shell" title="Secure Shell">SSH</a> protocol, first through the subnet that is connected to the <a href="/wiki/Mobile_device" title="Mobile device">device</a>. Then, it repeats the process generating a random range and finally uses some preset ranges corresponding to the <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP address</a> of certain telephone companies. Once the computer is infected, the wallpaper is replaced by a photograph of the singer <a href="/wiki/Rick_Astley" title="Rick Astley">Rick Astley</a>, a reference to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rickroll" title="Rickroll">Rickroll</a> phenomenon.</li>
<li><b>Gunpoder :</b> This worm file infector virus is the first known virus that officially infected the <a href="/wiki/Google_Play" title="Google Play">Google Play</a> Store in few countries, including <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shedun" title="Shedun">Shedun</a>: adware serving malware able to root Android devices.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hummingbad" title="Hummingbad">HummingBad</a> - has infected over 10 million <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> operating systems. User details are sold and adverts are tapped on without the user's knowledge thereby generating fraudulent advertising revenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HummingBad_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-HummingBad-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
