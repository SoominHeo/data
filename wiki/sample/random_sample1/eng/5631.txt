<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Moscow dialect</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The <b>Moscow dialect</b> or <b>Moscow accent</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Московское произношение</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Russian" title="Romanization of Russian">tr.</a></small> <i>Moskovskoye proiznoshenye</i>; <small>IPA: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Russian" title="Help:IPA for Russian">[mɐˈskofskəjə prəɪznɐˈʂenʲɪɪ]</a></span>), sometimes <b>Central Russian</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rough_Guide_Phrasebook_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rough_Guide_Phrasebook-1">[1]</a></sup> is the spoken <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian language</a> variety used in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>. Influenced by both Northern and Southern Russian dialects,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the Moscow dialect is the basis of the Russian <a href="/wiki/Russian_language#Standard_Russian" title="Russian language">literary language</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moscow_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 1911 edition of the <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclopædia Britannica">Encyclopædia Britannica</a></i> wrote:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Literary Russian as spoken by educated people throughout the empire is the Moscow dialect...</p>
<p><br/>
The Moscow dialect really covers a small area, not even the whole of the government of Moscow, but political causes have made it the language of the governing classes and hence of literature. It is a border dialect, having the southern pronunciation of unaccented <i>o</i> as <i>a</i>, but the <i>jo</i> for accented o before a hard consonant it is akin to the North and it has also kept the northern pronunciation of <i>g</i> instead of the southern <i>h</i>. So too unaccented <i>e</i> sounds like <i>i</i> or <i>ji'</i>.</p>
</blockquote>
