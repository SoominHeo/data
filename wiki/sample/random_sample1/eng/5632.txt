<p>The <b>Moscow Central Circle</b> or <b>MCC</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Московское центральное кольцо</span>, МЦК),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> designated <b>Line 14</b> or just <b>Encircle Line</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Окружная линия</span>) and marked in a strawberry red/white color is a 54-kilometre-long (34 mi) orbital <a href="/wiki/Commuter_rail" title="Commuter rail">commuter rail</a> line that encircles historical <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>. The line is rebuilt from the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ring_of_the_Moscow_Railway" title="Little Ring of the Moscow Railway">Little Ring of the Moscow Railway</a> and opened to passengers on 10 September 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rupasova_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-rupasova-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gazeta20160810_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-gazeta20160810-4">[4]</a></sup> and is operated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moscow_Government" title="Moscow Government">Moscow Government</a> owned company MKZD through the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Metro" title="Moscow Metro">Moscow Metro</a>, with the state-run <a href="/wiki/Russian_Railways" title="Russian Railways">Russian Railways</a> selected as the operation subcontractor. The infrastructure, trackage and platforms are owned and managed by <a href="/wiki/Russian_Railways" title="Russian Railways">Russian Railways</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> while most station buildings are owned by MKZD.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moscow_Central_Circle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The railroad was commissioned in 1897 under the auspices of Czar <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_II_of_Russia" title="Nicholas II of Russia">Nicholas II</a>, thus earning a "Royal Railroad" nickname.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigmoscowpdf_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-bigmoscowpdf-6">[6]</a></sup> The planning took five years. Thirteen design alternatives were reviewed in the process. The winning bid was for a four-track rail line, with two tracks allocated for freight, and the other two used by passenger trains. The project came with an estimated 40 million <a href="/wiki/Russian_ruble" title="Russian ruble">ruble</a> price tag.</p>
<p>In May 1902, construction began. Following a defeat in the 1905 <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a>, construction was scaled back. As the costs overran the estimate by a third, the number of tracks being built was reduced to two. Bridges, of which there are 35 (4 big and 31 small), were particularly costly. Their low clearance hindered electrification efforts for over a century to come.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMCHistoryTransportMosRU_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-MMCHistoryTransportMosRU-7">[7]</a></sup> The vast railroad infrastructure included housing facilities, water towers, smithies, and miscellaneous shops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MCCBuildingsPhotoAlbum_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-MCCBuildingsPhotoAlbum-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMCHistoryTransportMosRU_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-MMCHistoryTransportMosRU-7">[7]</a></sup> Station houses — architectural masterpieces built in the typical early-20th-century Russian industrial style<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imena_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-imena-9">[9]</a></sup> — had electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MCCBuildingsPhotoAlbum_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-MCCBuildingsPhotoAlbum-8">[8]</a></sup> Heat was provided by <a href="/wiki/Masonry_heater" title="Masonry heater">masonry heaters</a>, some of which were <a href="/wiki/Russian_oven" title="Russian oven">Russian-made</a>, and some imported from Holland. Station clocks were purchased from Swiss manufacturer <a class="extiw" href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%91%D1%83%D1%80%D0%B5,_%D0%9F%D0%B0%D0%B2%D0%B5%D0%BB_%D0%9A%D0%B0%D1%80%D0%BB%D0%BE%D0%B2%D0%B8%D1%87" title="ru:Буре, Павел Карлович">Paul Buhré</a>. Known for their accuracy, these clocks, for a while, became the city's de facto time standard. Only one such clock has survived. It is located in Presnya station supervisor's office.</p>
<p>The first train ran in 1907.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigmoscowpdf_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-bigmoscowpdf-6">[6]</a></sup> On July 19, 1908, the railroad officially opened. The opening ceremony was attended by the Czar, Royal Dynasty members, and government and city officials.</p>
<p>In the first few months, the railroad was used exclusively for passenger traffic. Due to a high train fare — at 3.40 rubles — ridership was virtually non-existent, and the line brought in the total of 132 rubles in revenue since the operation started. Thus, on October 10, 1908, passenger trains were discontinued in favor of freight service.</p>
<p>Between <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and the <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">October Revolution</a> of 1917, the passenger service was restored, although freight remained the only viable revenue source. By the late 1920s, other forms of public transportation had emerged, and, in 1934, passenger service was ended, only to resume, again, 82 years later.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moscow_Central_Circle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moscow_Ring_Railway_Transport_Hub.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Example of a <a href="/wiki/Transport_hub" title="Transport hub">transport hub</a> on the Moscow Central Circle</div>

<p>Around 2010, <a class="extiw" href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%9F%D0%B0%D1%81%D1%81%D0%B0%D0%B6%D0%B8%D1%80%D0%BE%D0%BF%D0%BE%D1%82%D0%BE%D0%BA_%D0%B2_%D0%BE%D0%B1%D1%89%D0%B5%D1%81%D1%82%D0%B2%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%BD%D0%BE%D0%BC_%D1%82%D1%80%D0%B0%D0%BD%D1%81%D0%BF%D0%BE%D1%80%D1%82%D0%B5_%D0%9C%D0%BE%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B2%D1%8B" title="ru:Пассажиропоток в общественном транспорте Москвы">many millions of people</a> utilized the city's subway system daily. Some 35-40% used private transportation, leading to severe road congestion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TransportationDevPlanMedvedev_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-TransportationDevPlanMedvedev-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Upgrade plans for the railway line were signed by <a href="/wiki/Russian_Railways" title="Russian Railways">Russian Railways</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moscow_Government" title="Moscow Government">Moscow Government</a> between 2008 and 2011 with consent of <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a> (<a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Russia" title="Prime Minister of Russia">Prime Minister</a> at the time). Construction work planned for 2013–2016 would convert the Little Ring line of the Moscow Railway for joint passenger and freight use but in 2012, at a meeting with new Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Medvedev" title="Dmitry Medvedev">Dmitry Medvedev</a> in <a href="/wiki/Odintsovo" title="Odintsovo">Odintsovo</a>, Moscow Mayor <a href="/wiki/Sergey_Sobyanin" title="Sergey Sobyanin">Sergey Sobyanin</a> acknowledged that trains on the circle railway would not be fully ready until 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TransportationDevPlanMedvedev_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-TransportationDevPlanMedvedev-10">[10]</a></sup> The required work included:</p>
<ul>
<li>Electrification of the whole line with 3 kV DC overhead wires and the construction of substations</li>
<li>Complete track replacement with additional third track on the northern half of the circle</li>
<li>Construction of new passenger stations and rehabilitation of old yards</li>
<li>Construction of Podmoskovnaya depot for EMU trains</li>
<li>Construction of an additional second track and station upgrades on the northern section of the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Ring_of_the_Moscow_Railway" title="Greater Ring of the Moscow Railway">Greater Ring of the Moscow Railway</a> (a large orbital line outside the city), for the re-routing freight traffic away from central Moscow</li>
<li>Replacement for most bridges and overpasses</li>
<li>New <a href="/wiki/Rolling_stock" title="Rolling stock">rolling stock</a> specifically designed for urban service</li>
<li>Construction of transfers with existing and under construction <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Metro" title="Moscow Metro">Moscow Metro</a> stations</li>
</ul>
<p>Construction commenced in 2012, and passenger services began in the third quarter of 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigmoscowpdf_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-bigmoscowpdf-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stopstamp_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-stopstamp-11">[11]</a></sup> During the reconstruction of the railway, many of the original passenger stations were re-purposed for passenger use and complemented with new stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opening_and_operation">Opening and operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moscow_Central_Circle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Opening and operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Putin_and_Sobyanin_in_ES2G_opening_MCC.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1028" data-file-width="1600" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Putin_and_Sobyanin_in_ES2G_opening_MCC.jpg/300px-Putin_and_Sobyanin_in_ES2G_opening_MCC.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Putin_and_Sobyanin_in_ES2G_opening_MCC.jpg/450px-Putin_and_Sobyanin_in_ES2G_opening_MCC.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Putin_and_Sobyanin_in_ES2G_opening_MCC.jpg/600px-Putin_and_Sobyanin_in_ES2G_opening_MCC.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>

<p>The line opened on September 10, 2016 in the presence of President Vladimir Putin and Moscow Mayor Sergey Sobyanin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The line is free to ride for the first month of operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> By the end of 2016, the daily ridership on the Central Circle Line is expected to reach 400,000 and by 2025, the ring railway is expected to carry up to 300 million passengers annually.</p>
<p>The operation of the Central Circle is similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S-Train" title="S-Train">S-Train</a> systems in Germany and other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Ticketing on the Moscow Central Circle is fully integrated with the Moscow Metro; the same payment cards (such as the <a href="/wiki/Troika_card" title="Troika card">Troika card</a>) can be used on both systems, and free transfers will be possible within 90 minutes since the first entry into the system, in a way similar to the transfers between the Metro proper and the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Monorail" title="Moscow Monorail">Moscow Monorail</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The line serves the purpose of a connector between the different radial lines of outer Moscow, much as the <a href="/wiki/Koltsevaya_Line" title="Koltsevaya Line">Koltsevaya Line</a> does in inner Moscow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> According to Moscow's deputy mayor, 130 trains per day will circulate around the circle line, with a frequency of 5–6 minutes during the rush hours, and 10–15 minutes at other times. The line's hours of operation will be the same as the rest of the Metro, from 06:00 until 01:00.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite its name, the Moscow Central Circle is not circle-shaped. The line stretches 12 kilometres (7.5 mi) outward in the northwest and draws as close as 5 kilometres (3.1 mi) to the Kremlin in the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigmoscowpdf_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-bigmoscowpdf-6">[6]</a></sup> The Metro's <a href="/wiki/Third_Interchange_Contour" title="Third Interchange Contour">Third Interchange Contour</a> (an extension of <a href="/wiki/Kakhovskaya_Line" title="Kakhovskaya Line">Line 11/Kakhovskaya Line</a>), which is under construction and will also be a circle line, will reach further to the south.</p>
<p>An estimated 1,500,000 people lived near the line in the early 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigmoscowpdf_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-bigmoscowpdf-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Central Circle Line has 31 opened stations. There are 5 direct transfers to other Moscow Metro lines and 9 more transfers available within walking distance from MCC stations. Free transfer between designated MCC and metro stations all requires the first rail journey before transfer to be done within 90 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osi_facebook_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-osi_facebook-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
