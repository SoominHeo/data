<p>The <b>Moscow Peace Treaty</b> was signed by <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> on 12 March 1940, and the ratifications were exchanged on 21 March.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It marked the end of the 105-day <a href="/wiki/Winter_War" title="Winter War">Winter War</a>. The treaty ceded parts of Finland to the Soviet Union. However, it preserved Finland's independence, ending the Soviet attempt to annex the country. The treaty was signed by <a href="/wiki/Vyacheslav_Molotov" title="Vyacheslav Molotov">Vyacheslav Molotov</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andrey_Zhdanov" title="Andrey Zhdanov">Andrey Zhdanov</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Vasilevsky" title="Aleksandr Vasilevsky">Aleksandr Vasilevsky</a> for Soviet Union, and <a href="/wiki/Risto_Ryti" title="Risto Ryti">Risto Ryti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juho_Kusti_Paasikivi" title="Juho Kusti Paasikivi">Juho Kusti Paasikivi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Walden" title="Rudolf Walden">Rudolf Walden</a> and <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A4in%C3%B6_Voionmaa" title="Väinö Voionmaa">Väinö Voionmaa</a> for Finland.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moscow_Peace_Treaty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Finnish government received the first tentative peace conditions from the Soviet Union (through <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>) on 31 January. Until then, the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a> had fought to occupy all of Finland. By this point, the regime was prepared to temper its claims. The demands were that Finland cede the <a href="/wiki/Karelian_Isthmus" title="Karelian Isthmus">Karelian Isthmus</a>, including the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viipuri" title="Viipuri">Viipuri</a>, and Finland's shore of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ladoga" title="Lake Ladoga">Lake Ladoga</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Hanko_Naval_Base" title="Hanko Naval Base">Hanko Peninsula</a> was to be leased to the Soviet Union for 30 years.</p>
<p>Finland rejected these demands and intensified its pleas to Sweden, France and the United Kingdom for military support by regular troops. Although Finland in the long run had no chance against a country fifty times its size,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the reports from the front still held out hope for Finland, anticipating a <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a> intervention. Positive signals, however inconstant, from France and Britain, and more realistic expectations of troops from Sweden, for which plans and preparations had been made all through the 1930s, were further reasons for Finland not to rush into peace negotiations. (See <a href="/wiki/Winter_War#Foreign_support" title="Winter War">Winter War § Foreign support</a> for a detailed account.)</p>
<p>In February 1940, Finland's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commander-in-Chief" title="Commander-in-Chief">Commander-in-Chief</a> <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustaf_Emil_Mannerheim" title="Carl Gustaf Emil Mannerheim">marshal Mannerheim</a> expressed his pessimism about the military situation, prompting the government to start peace negotiations on 29 February, the same day the Red Army commenced an attack against <a href="/wiki/Vyborg" title="Vyborg">Viipuri</a> (now Vyborg).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Harsh_peace">Harsh peace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moscow_Peace_Treaty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Harsh peace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 6 March, a Finnish delegation led by <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Finland" title="Prime Minister of Finland">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Risto_Ryti" title="Risto Ryti">Risto Ryti</a> travelled to Moscow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> During the negotiations, the Red Army broke through the Finnish defence lines around <a href="/wiki/Tali,_Helsinki" title="Tali, Helsinki">Tali</a> and were close to surrounding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viipuri" title="Viipuri">Viipuri</a>.</p>
<p>The Peace Agreement was signed on the evening of 12 March, Moscow time, i.e. 1 hour on March 13, Finnish time. The protocol appended to the treaty stipulated that the fighting should be ended at noon, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Leningrad</a> time (11:00 Finnish time),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and the fighting continued until that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Finnish concessions and territorial losses exceeded those <a href="/wiki/Background_of_the_Winter_War#Soviet_demands_in_late_1939" title="Background of the Winter War">demanded by the Soviets pre-war</a>. Finland was forced to cede nearly all of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finnish_Karelia" title="Finnish Karelia">Finnish Karelia</a> (with Finland's industrial center, including Vyborg/Viipuri, Finland's second largest city, Käkisalmi, Sortavala, and Suojärvi and the whole of Viipuri bay with its islands; in total, nearly 10% of the territory), even though large parts were still held by Finland's army. Military troops and remaining civilians were <a href="/wiki/Evacuation_of_Finnish_Karelia" title="Evacuation of Finnish Karelia">hastily evacuated to inside the new border</a>. 422,000 Karelians, 12% of Finland's population, lost their homes.</p>
<p>There was also an area that the Russians captured during the war, which remained in Finnish hands according to the Peace Treaty: <a href="/wiki/Pechengsky_District" title="Pechengsky District">Petsamo</a>. However, the peace treaty also stipulated that Finland would grant free passage for Soviet civilians through <a href="/wiki/Pechengsky_District" title="Pechengsky District">Petsamo</a> to Norway.</p>
<p>Finland also had to cede a part of the <a href="/wiki/Salla" title="Salla">Salla</a> area, the Finnish part of the Kalastajansaarento (<a href="/wiki/Rybachy_Peninsula" title="Rybachy Peninsula">Rybachi</a>) peninsula in the <a href="/wiki/Barents_Sea" title="Barents Sea">Barents Sea</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Finland" title="Gulf of Finland">Gulf of Finland</a> the islands of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suursaari" title="Suursaari">Suursaari</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyt%C3%A4rsaari" title="Tytärsaari">Tytärsaari</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lavansaari" title="Lavansaari">Lavansaari</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Moshchny_Island" title="Moshchny Island">Moshchny Island</a> о. Мощный), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peninsaari&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peninsaari (page does not exist)">Peninsaari</a> (now <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maly_Island&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maly Island (page does not exist)">Maly Island</a>, о. Малый) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seiskari" title="Seiskari">Seiskari</a>. Finally, the <a href="/wiki/Hanko_Peninsula" title="Hanko Peninsula">Hanko Peninsula</a> was leased to the Soviet Union as a <a href="/wiki/Naval_base" title="Naval base">naval base</a> for 30 years at an annual rent of 8 million marks.</p>
<p>Contrary to common belief, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transit_of_German_troops_through_Scandinavia_(WWII)" title="Transit of German troops through Scandinavia (WWII)">Soviet troop transfer rights by railway to the Hanko base</a> were not granted in the peace treaty, but they were demanded first on 9 July, after Sweden had acknowledged railway transit of Wehrmacht troops to occupied Norway.</p>
<p>Additional demands were that any equipment and installation on the ceded territories were to be handed over. Thus Finland had to hand over 75 <a href="/wiki/Locomotive" title="Locomotive">locomotives</a>, 2,000 <a href="/wiki/Railroad_car" title="Railroad car">railroad cars</a>, a number of cars, trucks and ships. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enso_(town)" title="Enso (town)">Enso</a> industrial area, which was clearly on the Finnish side of the border, as it was drawn in the peace treaty, was also soon added to the Finnish losses of territory and equipment.</p>
<p>The new border was not arbitrary from the Soviet viewpoint.</p>
<ul>
<li>Before the war, Finland had been a leading producer of high quality <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_pulp" title="Wood pulp">pulp</a>, which was an important raw material for explosives. Including the Enso factories, the Soviet Union captured 80% of Finland's production capacity.</li>
<li>Finland had to cede 1/3 of her built hydroelectric power, mainly in the form of hydroelectric power plants on the <a href="/wiki/Vuoksi_River" title="Vuoksi River">Vuoksi River</a>, which was badly needed in Leningrad, where the industry suffered a 20% shortage of electricity.</li>
<li>The location of the new border was consistent with the Soviet defence doctrine, which envisioned taking the fight onto enemy soil through counterattacks and <a href="/wiki/Preemptive_war" title="Preemptive war">pre-emptive</a> strikes. Under this doctrine, the ideal border should not allow the enemy to have natural defensible barriers; so instead of running through natural border locations like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bay_of_Viipuri" title="Bay of Viipuri">Bay of Viipuri</a> or the swamp region at the isthmus between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Saimaa" title="Lake Saimaa">Lake Saimaa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ladoga" title="Lake Ladoga">Lake Ladoga</a>, the new border ran on the western side of those. But those positions were also very easy to encircle for an offensive enemy of the Red Army, which was soon to be shown.</li>
</ul>
<p>The Finns were shocked by the harsh peace terms. It seemed as if more territory was lost in the peace than in the war, and the loss was in many ways some of the highest valued parts of Finland:</p>
<ul>
<li>Large parts of the most populated southern region of remaining Finland had been connected to the world via the <a href="/wiki/Saimaa_Canal" title="Saimaa Canal">Saimaa Canal</a> system, that now was severed at Vyborg where it connects to the Gulf of Finland.</li>
<li>The southern part of the lost area was Finland's industrial heart.</li>
<li>Karelia is considered the heart and origin of the Finnish culture. Before the Winter War, the Soviet sovereignty over the main part of Karelia, and Stalinist atrocities there, had been a major source of grief for many Finns. Under the terms of the treaty, the rest of Karelia was also lost. This started the <a href="/wiki/Karelian_question" title="Karelian question">Karelian question</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Sympathy from world opinion seemed to have been of little worth. A certain bitter disappointment became a common feature of the Finns' view of other nations, not the least of Swedes, who had offered plenty of sympathy but did not fulfill their obligations of military support for Finland.</p>
<p>The harsh terms imposed on the Finns led them to seek support from the Third Reich. The Winter War and subsequent Moscow Peace Treaty were core factors in leading to what would become the <a href="/wiki/Continuation_War" title="Continuation War">Continuation War</a>. In the end, this might have been a necessary condition for Finland's survival in the World War.</p>
<p>Only a year later, in June 1941, hostilities resumed in the <a href="/wiki/Continuation_War" title="Continuation War">Continuation War</a>.</p>
