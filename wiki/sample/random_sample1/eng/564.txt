<p>The <b>Citizenship, Action, Participation for the 21st Century</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Citoyenneté Action Participation pour le 21ème siècle</i></span>) is a minor <a href="/wiki/Green_liberalism" title="Green liberalism">green liberal</a> <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political party</a> in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/Corinne_Lepage" title="Corinne Lepage">Corinne Lepage</a> in 1996 as a political reflection club.</p>
<p>It became a political party in June 2000. Lepage was the party's candidate in the <a href="/wiki/French_presidential_election,_2002" title="French presidential election, 2002">2002 presidential election</a> and obtained 1.88% of the vote. Lepage claimed to stand for an independent and centrist green voice, as opposed to <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Mam%C3%A8re" title="Noël Mamère">Noël Mamère</a>, candidate for the more left-wing <a href="/wiki/The_Greens_(France)" title="The Greens (France)">Green Party</a>. The party later refused to join the new centre-right <a href="/wiki/Union_for_a_Popular_Movement" title="Union for a Popular Movement">Union for a Popular Movement</a> (UMP).</p>
<p>In 2007, after dropping out of the presidential race, Lepage endorsed <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Bayrou" title="François Bayrou">François Bayrou</a>'s centrist candidacy and CAP21 ran around 20 candidates with Bayrou's new <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Movement_(France)" title="Democratic Movement (France)">Democratic Movement</a> in the subsequent <a href="/wiki/French_legislative_election,_2007" title="French legislative election, 2007">legislative election</a>. It failed to have any candidates elected.</p>
<p>CAP 21 became an associate party of Bayrou's <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Movement_(France)" title="Democratic Movement (France)">MoDem</a> on June 15, 2008. Corinne Lepage became a high-ranking member of the MoDem's leadership, serving as a Vice President. In the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2009_(France)" title="European Parliament election, 2009 (France)">2009 European elections</a>, Corinne Lepage was elected <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">Member of the European Parliament</a> for <a href="/wiki/North-West_France_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="North-West France (European Parliament constituency)">North-West France</a> as the MoDem's top candidate in the region.</p>
<p>However, the party split from the MoDem in the runup to the <a href="/wiki/French_regional_elections,_2010" title="French regional elections, 2010">2010 regional elections</a> and pursued an alliance with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europe_Ecologie" title="Europe Ecologie">Europe Ecologie</a>. Cap 21 was allied with Europe Ecologie in <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">Alsace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Languedoc-Roussillon" title="Languedoc-Roussillon">Languedoc-Roussillon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorraine_(region)" title="Lorraine (region)">Lorraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pays_de_la_Loire" title="Pays de la Loire">Pays de la Loire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Provence-Alpes-C%C3%B4te_d%27Azur" title="Provence-Alpes-Côte d'Azur">Provence-Alpes-Côte d'Azur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Upper_Normandy" title="Upper Normandy">Upper Normandy</a> and was allied with the MoDem in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burgundy_(French_region)" title="Burgundy (French region)">Burgundy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Picardy" title="Picardy">Picardy</a>. Following the MoDem's poor result (4.2%) in the regional elections, Lepage announced her resignation from the MoDem and announced that she would propose separation from the MoDem at the party's next congress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Members voted to leave the MoDem in May 2010.</p>
<p>Lepage subsequently failed to obtain the 500 endorsements necessary to run in the <a href="/wiki/French_presidential_election,_2012" title="French presidential election, 2012">2012 presidential election</a>.</p>
<p>In May 2013, several leading members from the right of the party left. Some went on to form New Democratic Ecology (French: Nouvelle écologie démocrate), allied to the <a href="/wiki/Union_of_Democrats_and_Independents" title="Union of Democrats and Independents">Union of Democrats and Independents</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
