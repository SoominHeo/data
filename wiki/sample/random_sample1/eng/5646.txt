<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mockumentary</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>A <b>mockumentary</b> (a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of mock and documentary) or <b>docucomedy</b> is a type of movie or television show depicting fictional events but presented as a documentary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>These productions are often used to analyze or comment on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_events" title="Current events">current events</a> and issues by using a fictional setting, or to parody the documentary form itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbell_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-2">[2]</a></sup> While not always <a href="/wiki/Comedy" title="Comedy">comedic</a>, comedic mockumentaries are common. A dramatic mockumentary (sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Docufiction" title="Docufiction">docufiction</a>) should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/Docudrama" title="Docudrama">docudrama</a>, a fictional <a href="/wiki/Genre" title="Genre">genre</a> in which dramatic techniques are combined with documentary elements to depict real events.</p>
<p>Mockumentaries are often presented as historical documentaries, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B_roll" title="B roll">B roll</a> and talking heads discussing past events, or as <i><a href="/wiki/Cin%C3%A9ma_v%C3%A9rit%C3%A9" title="Cinéma vérité">cinéma vérité</a></i> pieces following people as they go through various events. Examples emerged during the 1950s when archival film footage became relatively easy to locate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbell_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-2">[2]</a></sup> A very early example was a short piece on the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spaghetti_tree" title="Spaghetti tree">Swiss Spaghetti Harvest</a>" that appeared as an <a href="/wiki/April_Fools%27_Day" title="April Fools' Day">April fools'</a> joke on the British television program <i><a href="/wiki/Panorama_(TV_series)" title="Panorama (TV series)">Panorama</a></i> in 1957.</p>
<p>The term "mockumentary", which originated in the 1960s, was popularized in the mid-1980s when <i><a href="/wiki/This_Is_Spinal_Tap" title="This Is Spinal Tap">This Is Spinal Tap</a></i> director <a href="/wiki/Rob_Reiner" title="Rob Reiner">Rob Reiner</a> used it in interviews to describe that film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roscoe_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roscoe-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mockumentaries are often partly or wholly <a href="/wiki/Improvisational_theatre" title="Improvisational theatre">improvised</a>, as an unscripted style of acting helps to maintain the pretense of reality. Comedic mockumentaries rarely have <a href="/wiki/Laugh_track" title="Laugh track">laugh tracks</a>, also to sustain the atmosphere, although exceptions exist.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_examples">Early examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mockumentary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early work, including <a href="/wiki/Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel" title="Luis Buñuel">Luis Buñuel</a>'s 1933 <i><a href="/wiki/Land_Without_Bread" title="Land Without Bread">Land Without Bread</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">Orson Welles</a>'s 1938 radio broadcast of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_War_of_the_Worlds_(radio)" title="The War of the Worlds (radio)">The War of the Worlds</a></i>, various April Fool's Day news reports, and <i>vérité</i>-style film and television during the 1960s and '70s, served as precursor to the genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roscoe_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Roscoe-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early examples of mock-documentaries include <i><a href="/wiki/A_Hard_Day%27s_Night_(film)" title="A Hard Day's Night (film)">A Hard Day's Night</a></i>, 1964, (written by <a href="/wiki/Alun_Owen" title="Alun Owen">Alun Owen</a>, purporting to describe several days in the lives of <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>, that would be the first feature-length mockumentary), <i><a href="/wiki/David_Holzman%27s_Diary" title="David Holzman's Diary">David Holzman's Diary</a></i>, 1967, <i><a class="external text" href="http://www.imdb.com/title/tt1272023/" rel="nofollow">Pat Paulsen for President</a></i>, 1968, <i><a href="/wiki/Take_the_Money_and_Run" title="Take the Money and Run">Take the Money and Run</a></i>, 1969, <i>The Clowns</i>, 1970, by <a href="/wiki/Federico_Fellini" title="Federico Fellini">Federico Fellini</a> (a peculiar <a href="/wiki/Hybridity" title="Hybridity">hybrid</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Documentary" title="Documentary">documentary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fiction" title="Fiction">fiction</a>, a docufiction), and <i><a href="/wiki/All_You_Need_Is_Cash" title="All You Need Is Cash">All You Need Is Cash</a></i>, 1978. <a href="/wiki/Albert_Brooks" title="Albert Brooks">Albert Brooks</a> was also an early popularizer of the mockumentary style with his film <i><a href="/wiki/Real_Life_(1979_film)" title="Real Life (1979 film)">Real Life</a></i>, 1979, a spoof of a <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">PBS</a> documentary.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Woody_Allen" title="Woody Allen">Woody Allen</a>'s <i>Take the Money and Run</i> is presented in documentary-style with Allen playing a fictional criminal, Virgil Starkwell, whose crime exploits are "explored" throughout the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trompe_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trompe-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Beck" title="Jackson Beck">Jackson Beck</a>, who used to narrate documentaries in the 1940s, provides the voice-over narration. Fictional interviews are interspliced throughout, especially those of Starkwell's parents who wear <a href="/wiki/Groucho_Marx" title="Groucho Marx">Groucho Marx</a> noses and mustaches. The style of this film was widely appropriated by others and revisited by Allen himself in films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Zelig" title="Zelig">Zelig</a></i> (1983) and <i><a href="/wiki/Sweet_and_Lowdown" title="Sweet and Lowdown">Sweet and Lowdown</a></i> (1999).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trompe_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Trompe-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early use of the mockumentary format in television comedy may be seen in several sketches from <i><a href="/wiki/Monty_Python%27s_Flying_Circus" title="Monty Python's Flying Circus">Monty Python's Flying Circus</a></i> (1969–1974), such as "<a href="/wiki/List_of_Monty_Python%27s_Flying_Circus_episodes" title="List of Monty Python's Flying Circus episodes">Hell's Grannies</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Piranha_Brothers" title="Piranha Brothers">Piranha Brothers</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/The_Funniest_Joke_in_the_World" title="The Funniest Joke in the World">The Funniest Joke in the World</a>". <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hart_and_Lorne_Terrific_Hour" title="The Hart and Lorne Terrific Hour">The Hart and Lorne Terrific Hour</a></i> (1970–1971) also featured mockumentary pieces which interspersed both scripted and real-life man-in-the-street interviews, the most famous likely being "The Puck Crisis" in which hockey pucks were claimed to have become infected with a form of <a href="/wiki/Dutch_elm_disease" title="Dutch elm disease">Dutch elm disease</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Since_1980">Since 1980</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mockumentary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Since 1980">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_film">In film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mockumentary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since the beginning of the 1980s, the mockumentary format has enjoyed considerable attention.</p>
<p>The 1980 <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South African</a> film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Gods_Must_be_Crazy" title="The Gods Must be Crazy">The Gods Must be Crazy</a></i> (along with its <a href="/wiki/The_Gods_Must_Be_Crazy_II" title="The Gods Must Be Crazy II">1989 sequel</a>) is presented in the manner of a <a href="/wiki/Nature_documentary" title="Nature documentary">nature documentary</a>, with documentary narrator <a href="/wiki/Paddy_O%27Byrne" title="Paddy O'Byrne">Paddy O'Byrne</a> describing the events of the film in the manner of a biologist or anthropologist presenting scientific knowledge to viewers.</p>
<p>Woody Allen's 1983 film <i><a href="/wiki/Zelig" title="Zelig">Zelig</a></i> stars Allen as a curiously nondescript enigma who is discovered for his remarkable ability to transform himself to resemble anyone he is near, and Allen is edited into historical archive footage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trompe_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Trompe-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1984, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Guest" title="Christopher Guest">Christopher Guest</a> co-wrote and starred in the mockumentary <i>This is Spinal Tap</i>, directed by Rob Reiner, and went on cowriting and directing himself a series of films in the same genre. Films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Waiting_for_Guffman" title="Waiting for Guffman">Waiting for Guffman</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Best_in_Show_(film)" title="Best in Show (film)">Best in Show</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/A_Mighty_Wind" title="A Mighty Wind">A Mighty Wind</a></i>, all written with costar <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Levy" title="Eugene Levy">Eugene Levy</a>, were critical successes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trompe_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Trompe-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Man_Bites_Dog_(film)" title="Man Bites Dog (film)">Man Bites Dog</a></i> is a 1992 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgian" title="Belgian">Belgian</a> <a href="/wiki/Black_comedy" title="Black comedy">black comedy</a> crime mockumentary written, produced, and directed by <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9my_Belvaux" title="Rémy Belvaux">Rémy Belvaux</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Bonzel" title="André Bonzel">André Bonzel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Beno%C3%AEt_Poelvoorde" title="Benoît Poelvoorde">Benoît Poelvoorde</a>.</p>
<p>In 1995, directed by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Jackson" title="Peter Jackson">Peter Jackson</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Costa_Botes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Costa Botes (page does not exist)">Costa Botes</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Forgotten_Silver" title="Forgotten Silver">Forgotten Silver</a></i> claimed <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> "director" Colin McKenzie to be a pioneer in filmmaking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> When the film was later revealed to be a mockumentary, Jackson received criticism for tricking viewers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1999, <i><a href="/wiki/Drop_Dead_Gorgeous_(film)" title="Drop Dead Gorgeous (film)">Drop Dead Gorgeous</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Patrick_Jann" title="Michael Patrick Jann">Michael Patrick Jann</a> used the mockumentary style to narrate a fictional small-town beauty pageant as a way to satirize <a href="/wiki/Middle_America_(United_States)" title="Middle America (United States)">Middle America</a>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Side_of_the_Moon_(film)" title="Dark Side of the Moon (film)">Dark Side of the Moon</a></i> is a 2002 French mockumentary by director <a href="/wiki/William_Karel" title="William Karel">William Karel</a>. The premise for the film is the theory that the television footage from the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> Moon landing was faked and actually recorded in a studio by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIA" title="CIA">CIA</a> with help from director <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick" title="Stanley Kubrick">Stanley Kubrick</a>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/FUBAR_(film)" title="FUBAR (film)">FUBAR</a></i> is a 2002 Canadian mockumentary film, directed by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Dowse" title="Michael Dowse">Michael Dowse</a>, based on the lives of two lifelong friends and head-bangers living out their lives, constantly drinking beer.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/C.S.A.:_The_Confederate_States_of_America" title="C.S.A.: The Confederate States of America">C.S.A.: The Confederate States of America</a></i> is a 2004 mockumentary presenting an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alternative_history" title="Alternative history">alternative history</a> in which the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederacy</a> won the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/First_on_the_Moon" title="First on the Moon">First on the Moon</a></i> (Russian: Первые на Луне, <i>Pervye na Lune</i>) is a 2005 Russian mockumentary about a fictional 1930s Soviet landing on the Moon.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Borat" title="Borat">Borat: Cultural Learnings of America for Make Benefit Glorious Nation of Kazakhstan</a></i> is a highly controversial yet successful film from 2006 which uses this style, as does <i><a href="/wiki/Br%C3%BCno" title="Brüno">Brüno</a></i>, a similar film from 2009.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Animation" title="Sony Pictures Animation">Sony Pictures Animation</a> released their second animated feature, <i><a href="/wiki/Surf%27s_Up_(film)" title="Surf's Up (film)">Surf's Up</a></i> in 2007, which was the first of its kind to incorporate the mockumentary style into animation.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Ropes_(2011_film)" title="On the Ropes (2011 film)">On the Ropes</a></i> is a 2011 mockumentary film written and directed by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Noyce" title="Mark Noyce">Mark Noyce</a>. The film follows a fictional martial-arts instructor and his rivalry with a local boxing gym.</p>
<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/What_We_Do_In_The_Shadows" title="What We Do In The Shadows">What We Do In The Shadows</a></i> is a 2014 horror/comedy mockumentary film directed and written by <a href="/wiki/Taika_Waititi" title="Taika Waititi">Taika Waititi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jemaine_Clement" title="Jemaine Clement">Jemaine Clement</a>; the aforementioned directors also star in the production. The film is centered on vampire flat mates living in <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Popstar:_Never_Stop_Never_Stopping" title="Popstar: Never Stop Never Stopping">Popstar: Never Stop Never Stopping</a></i> is a 2016 mockumentary musical comedy starring <a href="/wiki/Andy_Samberg" title="Andy Samberg">Andy Samberg</a> as Connor4Real, an over-the-top parody of Justin Bieber. It is filmed in the style of a typical concert documentary such as <i><a href="/wiki/Katy_Perry:_Part_of_Me" title="Katy Perry: Part of Me">Katy Perry: Part of Me</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Justin_Bieber:_Never_Say_Never" title="Justin Bieber: Never Say Never">Justin Bieber: Never Say Never</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_television">In television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mockumentary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1983 <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Comic_Strip_Presents..." title="The Comic Strip Presents...">The Comic Strip Presents...</a></i> film <i><a href="/wiki/Bad_News_(band)" title="Bad News (band)">Bad News Tour</a></i> was a spoof <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rockumentary" title="Rockumentary">rockumentary</a> about a British <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">heavy metal</a> band. It preceded <i>Spinal Tap</i> by a year, but is not known to have been an influence on the U.S. film. The film led to LPs being released and the band really touring, and was followed by a sequel, <i>More Bad News</i> in 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Stella_Street" title="Stella Street">Stella Street</a></i> was a mockumentary which ran on the BBC from 1997 to 2001. It was written by <a href="/wiki/Phil_Cornwell" title="Phil Cornwell">Phil Cornwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Sessions" title="John Sessions">John Sessions</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_Richardson_(actor)" title="Peter Richardson (actor)">Peter Richardson</a>, and featured Cornwell and Sessions playing all the characters between them. The series was shot on handheld <a href="/wiki/Camcorder" title="Camcorder">camcorders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Operation_Good_Guys" title="Operation Good Guys">Operation Good Guys</a></i>, another low-budget BBC sitcom, ran for three series starting in 1997.</p>
<p>In television, the most notable mockumentaries in the 2000s have been: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ABC_Australia" title="ABC Australia">ABC Australia</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Games_(Australian_TV_series)" title="The Games (Australian TV series)">The Games</a></i> (1998–2000), Canadian series <i><a href="/wiki/Trailer_Park_Boys" title="Trailer Park Boys">Trailer Park Boys</a></i> (2001–present), the British shows <i><a href="/wiki/Marion_and_Geoff" title="Marion and Geoff">Marion and Geoff</a></i> (2000), <i><a href="/wiki/Twenty_Twelve" title="Twenty Twelve">Twenty Twelve</a></i> (2011–2012) (which follows the fictional Olympic Deliverance Commission in the run-up to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2012_Summer_Olympic_Games" title="2012 Summer Olympic Games">2012 Summer Olympic Games</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W1A" title="W1A">W1A</a>, which follows the main characters of <a href="/wiki/Twenty_Twelve" title="Twenty Twelve">Twenty Twelve</a> as they start work at the BBC, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Office_(UK_TV_series)" title="The Office (UK TV series)">The Office</a></i> (2001) and its <a href="/wiki/The_Office" title="The Office">many international offshoots</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Come_Fly_with_Me_(2010_TV_series)" title="Come Fly with Me (2010 TV series)">Come Fly with Me</a></i> (2010), which follows the activity at a fictional airport and its variety of staff and passengers. British comedy duo <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Saunders" title="Jennifer Saunders">Jennifer Saunders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dawn_French" title="Dawn French">Dawn French</a> often presented short mockumentaries as extended sketches in their TV show <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_%26_Saunders" title="French &amp; Saunders">French &amp; Saunders</a></i>. Discovery Channel opened its annual Shark Week on 4 Aug 2013 with <i><a href="/wiki/Megalodon:_The_Monster_Shark_Lives" title="Megalodon: The Monster Shark Lives">Megalodon: The Monster Shark Lives</a></i>, a mockumentary about the survival of the <a href="/wiki/Megalodon" title="Megalodon">megalodon</a>. Notable popular examples include sitcoms <i><a href="/wiki/Arrested_Development_(TV_series)" title="Arrested Development (TV series)">Arrested Development</a></i> (2003-2013), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Office_(U.S._TV_series)" title="The Office (U.S. TV series)">The Office (U.S.)</a></i> (2005–2013), <i><a href="/wiki/Parks_and_Recreation" title="Parks and Recreation">Parks and Recreation</a></i> (2009–2015), and <i><a href="/wiki/Modern_Family" title="Modern Family">Modern Family</a></i> (2009–present); the American improv comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Reno_911!" title="Reno 911!">Reno 911!</a></i> (2003–2009); the Canadian sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Trailer_Park_Boys" title="Trailer Park Boys">Trailer Park Boys</a></i> and its films; the comedy series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Muppets_(TV_series)" title="The Muppets (TV series)">The Muppets</a></i> (2015); the UK streaming sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Derek_(TV_series)" title="Derek (TV series)">Derek</a></i> (2012–2014); the Australian <a href="/wiki/Chris_Lilley_(comedian)" title="Chris Lilley (comedian)">Chris Lilley</a> shows <i><a href="/wiki/Angry_Boys" title="Angry Boys">Angry Boys</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Summer_Heights_High" title="Summer Heights High">Summer Heights High</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/We_Can_Be_Heroes:_Finding_The_Australian_of_the_Year" title="We Can Be Heroes: Finding The Australian of the Year">We Can Be Heroes: Finding The Australian of the Year</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ja%27mie:_Private_School_Girl" title="Ja'mie: Private School Girl">Ja'mie: Private School Girl</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Jonah_from_Tonga" title="Jonah from Tonga">Jonah from Tonga</a></i>.</p>
<p>One of India's digital mockumentary is <i>Not Fit</i> (2015-2016), which was created by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pocket_Aces_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pocket Aces (company) (page does not exist)">Pocket Aces</a>.</p>
