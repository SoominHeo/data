<p><b>Motorola, Inc.</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>) is an American <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> telecommunications company based in <a href="/wiki/Schaumburg,_Illinois" title="Schaumburg, Illinois">Schaumburg, Illinois</a>, United States. After having lost $4.3 billion from 2007 to 2009, the company was divided into two independent public companies, <a href="/wiki/Motorola_Mobility" title="Motorola Mobility">Motorola Mobility</a> and <a href="/wiki/Motorola_Solutions" title="Motorola Solutions">Motorola Solutions</a> on January 4, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Motorola Solutions is generally considered to be the direct successor to Motorola, Inc., as the reorganization was structured with Motorola Mobility being spun off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-google1-6">[6]</a></sup> Motorola Mobility was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Lenovo" title="Lenovo">Lenovo</a> in 2014.</p>
<p>Motorola designed and sold <a href="/wiki/Wireless_network" title="Wireless network">wireless network</a> equipment such as cellular transmission <a href="/wiki/Base_station" title="Base station">base stations</a> and signal amplifiers. Motorola's home and broadcast network products included <a href="/wiki/Set-top_box" title="Set-top box">set-top boxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Digital_video_recorder" title="Digital video recorder">digital video recorders</a>, and network equipment used to enable video broadcasting, computer telephony, and <a href="/wiki/High-definition_television" title="High-definition television">high-definition television</a>. Its business and government customers consisted mainly of wireless voice and broadband systems (used to build private networks), and, public safety communications systems like <a href="/wiki/Project_25" title="Project 25">Astro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dimetra" title="Dimetra">Dimetra</a>. These businesses (except for <a href="/wiki/Set-top_box" title="Set-top box">set-top boxes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cable_modem" title="Cable modem">cable modems</a>) are now part of Motorola Solutions. Google sold Motorola Home (the former <a href="/wiki/General_Instrument" title="General Instrument">General Instrument</a> cable businesses) to the <a href="/wiki/Arris_Group" title="Arris Group">Arris Group</a> in December 2012 for US$2.35 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Motorola's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wireless_telephone" title="Wireless telephone">wireless telephone</a> handset division was a pioneer in cellular telephones. Also known as the Personal Communication Sector (PCS) prior to 2004, it pioneered the "mobile phone" with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DynaTAC" title="DynaTAC">DynaTAC</a>, "flip phone" with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MicroTAC" title="MicroTAC">MicroTAC</a> as well as the "clam phone" with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/StarTAC" title="StarTAC">StarTAC</a> in the mid-1990s. It had staged a resurgence by the mid-2000s with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAZR" title="RAZR">RAZR</a>, but lost market share in the second half of that decade. Later it focused on smartphones using <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>'s open-source <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> mobile operating system. The first phone to use the newest version of Google's open source OS, Android 2.0, was released on November 2, 2009 as the <a href="/wiki/Motorola_Droid" title="Motorola Droid">Motorola Droid</a> (the GSM version launched a month later, in Europe, as the Motorola Milestone).</p>
<p>The handset division (along with cable <a href="/wiki/Set-top_box" title="Set-top box">set-top boxes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cable_modem" title="Cable modem">cable modems</a>) was later spun off into the independent <a href="/wiki/Motorola_Mobility" title="Motorola Mobility">Motorola Mobility</a>. On May 22, 2012, <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> CEO <a href="/wiki/Larry_Page" title="Larry Page">Larry Page</a> announced that Google had closed on its deal to acquire Motorola Mobility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> On January 29, 2014, Page announced that pending closure of the deal, Motorola Mobility would be acquired by Chinese technology company <a href="/wiki/Lenovo" title="Lenovo">Lenovo</a> for US$2.91 billion (subject to certain adjustments).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> On October 30, 2014, Lenovo finalized its purchase of Motorola Mobility from Google.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hello_Moto_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hello_Moto-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>Motorola started in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago, Illinois</a>, as <b>Galvin Manufacturing Corporation</b> (at 847 West Harrison Street)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> in 1928 when brothers, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Galvin_(businessman)" title="Paul Galvin (businessman)">Paul V.</a> and Joseph E. Galvin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> purchased the bankrupt Stewart Battery Company's battery-eliminator plans and manufacturing equipment at auction for $750. Galvin Manufacturing Corporation set up shop in a small section of a rented building. The company had $565 in working capital and five employees. The first week's payroll was $63.</p>
<p>The company's first products were <a href="/wiki/Battery_eliminator" title="Battery eliminator">battery-eliminators</a>, devices that enabled battery-powered radios to operate on household electricity. Due to advances in radio technology, battery-eliminators soon became obsolete. Paul Galvin learned that some radio technicians were installing sets in cars, and challenged his engineers to design an inexpensive car radio that could be installed in most vehicles. His team was successful, and Galvin was able to demonstrate a working model of the radio at the June 1930 Radio Manufacturers Association convention in Atlantic City, New Jersey. He brought home enough orders to keep the company in business.</p>
<p>Paul Galvin wanted a brand name for Galvin Manufacturing Corporation's new car radio, and created the name “Motorola” by linking "motor" (for motorcar) with "ola" (from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victrola" title="Victrola">Victrola</a>), which was also a popular ending for many companies at the time, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Moviola" title="Moviola">Moviola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crayola" title="Crayola">Crayola</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The company sold its first Motorola branded radio on June 23, 1930, to H.C. Wall of Fort Wayne, Indiana, for $30. The Motorola brand name became so well-known that Galvin Manufacturing Corporation later changed its name to Motorola, Inc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Galvin Manufacturing Corporation began selling Motorola car-radio receivers to police departments and municipalities in November 1930. The company's first public safety customers (all in the U.S. state of Illinois) included the Village of River Forest, Village of Bellwood Police Department, City of Evanston Police, Illinois State Highway Police, and Cook County (Chicago area) Police.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many of Motorola's products have been radio-related, starting with a <a href="/wiki/Battery_eliminator" title="Battery eliminator">battery eliminator</a> for radios, through the first hand-held <a href="/wiki/Walkie-talkie" title="Walkie-talkie">walkie-talkie</a> in the world in 1940,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_(military)" title="Defense (military)">defense</a> electronics, <a href="/wiki/Cellular_network" title="Cellular network">cellular</a> infrastructure equipment, and mobile phone manufacturing. In the same year, the company built its research and development program with <a href="/wiki/Daniel_E._Noble" title="Daniel E. Noble">Dan Noble</a>, a pioneer in <a href="/wiki/Frequency_modulation" title="Frequency modulation">FM</a> radio and semiconductor technologies, who joined the company as director of research. The company produced the hand-held <a href="/wiki/SCR-536" title="SCR-536">AM SCR-536</a> radio during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, which was vital to <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> communication. Motorola ranked 94th among United States corporations in the value of World War II military production contracts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Motorola went public in 1943,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and became Motorola, Inc. in 1947. At that time Motorola's main business was producing and selling televisions and radios.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_World_War_II">Post World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Post World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In October 1946 Motorola communications equipment carried the first calls on <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Bell" title="Illinois Bell">Illinois Bell</a> telephone company's new car radiotelephone service in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago, Illinois</a>. The company began making televisions in 1947, with the model VT-71 with 7-inch <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">cathode ray tube</a>. In 1952 Motorola opened its first international subsidiary in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, Canada to produce radios and televisions. In 1953, the company established the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola_Foundation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Motorola Foundation (page does not exist)">Motorola Foundation</a> to support leading universities in the United States.</p>
<p>In 1955, years after Motorola started its research and development laboratory in <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix, Arizona</a>, to research new solid-state technology, Motorola introduced the world's first commercial high-power <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistor</a>. The present "batwing" logo was also introduced in 1955 (having been created by award-winning Chicago graphic designer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morton_Goldsholl" title="Morton Goldsholl">Morton Goldsholl</a> in late 1954).</p>
<p>Beginning in 1958, with <a href="/wiki/Explorer_1" title="Explorer 1">Explorer 1</a> Motorola provided radio equipment for most <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> space-flights for decades, including during the 1969 moon landing. A year later it established a subsidiary to conduct licensing and manufacturing for international markets. Motorola created numerous products for use by the government, public safety officials, business installments, and the general public. These products included <a href="/wiki/Category:Motorola_mobile_phones" title="Category:Motorola mobile phones">cell phones</a>, laptops, computer processors, and radio communication devices.</p>
<p>In 1960, it introduced the world's first large-screen portable (19-inch), transistorized, cordless television. According to the 1962 Illinois Manufacturers Directory (50th anniversary edition), Motorola had 14,000 employees worldwide of which at least 5,823 employees in 6 plants were located in Illinois. The company headquarters were at 9401 West Grand Avenue in Franklin Park and it listed TV receivers, Stereo-Hi Fi equipment as the products at this plant made by 1,700 employees. The Communications Division was in Chicago at 4545 West Augusta Blvd. where 2,000 employees made electronic communications equipment. The Military Electronics Division was at 1450 North Cicero Avenue, Chicago where 923 employees made microwave and industrial equipment. Two more Chicago locations were listed at 4900 West Flourney Street and at 650 North Pulaski but no employee count was listed for these. The last plant was listed in Quincy, Illinois at 1400 North 30th Street where 1,200 employees made radio assemblies for both home and automobile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1963, it introduced the first rectangular color picture tube. In 1964, the company opened its first Research and development branch outside of the United States, in Israel, under the management of Moses Basin. The modular <a href="/wiki/Quasar_(brand)" title="Quasar (brand)">Quasar</a> brand was introduced in 1967.</p>
<p>In 1969, <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a> spoke the famous words "one small step for a man, one giant leap for mankind" from the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> on a Motorola transceiver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-money.cnn.com_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-money.cnn.com-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1973, Motorola demonstrated the first hand-held portable telephone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1974, Motorola introduced its first microprocessor, the 8-bit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MC6800" title="MC6800">MC6800</a>, used in automotive, computing and video game applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> That same year, Motorola sold its television business to the Japan-based Matsushita - the parent company of <a href="/wiki/Panasonic" title="Panasonic">Panasonic</a>.</p>
<p>In 1976, Motorola moved its headquarters to the Chicago suburb of <a href="/wiki/Schaumburg,_Illinois" title="Schaumburg, Illinois">Schaumburg, Illinois</a>.</p>
<p>In 1980, Motorola’s next generation 32-bit microprocessor, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MC68000" title="MC68000">MC68000</a>, led the wave of technologies that spurred the computing revolution in 1984, powering devices from companies such as <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commodore_International" title="Commodore International">Commodore</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atari_Corp." title="Atari Corp.">Atari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sun_Microsystems" title="Sun Microsystems">Sun</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hewlett_Packard" title="Hewlett Packard">Hewlett Packard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<p>In September 1983, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">Federal Communications Commission</a> (FCC) approved the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DynaTAC_8000X" title="DynaTAC 8000X">DynaTAC 8000X</a> telephone, the world's first commercial cellular device. By 1998, cellphones accounted for two thirds of Motorola's gross revenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The company was also strong in <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> technology, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_circuits" title="Integrated circuits">integrated circuits</a> used in computers. In particular, it is known for the <a href="/wiki/Motorola_6800_family" title="Motorola 6800 family">6800 family</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorola_68000_family" title="Motorola 68000 family">68000 family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessors</a> and related peripheral ICs; the processors were used in <a href="/wiki/Atari_ST" title="Atari ST">Atari ST</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amiga" title="Amiga">Commodore Amiga</a>, <a href="/wiki/TRS-80_Color_Computer" title="TRS-80 Color Computer">Color Computer</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_Macintosh" title="Apple Macintosh">Apple Macintosh</a> personal computers and in the early <a href="/wiki/Hewlett-Packard" title="Hewlett-Packard">HP</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laser_printer" title="Laser printer">laser printers</a>, and some 6800-family peripheral devices were used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_PC" title="IBM PC">IBM PC</a> series of personal computers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/PowerPC" title="PowerPC">PowerPC</a> family was developed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Business_Machines" title="International Business Machines">IBM</a> and in a partnership with Apple (known as the <a href="/wiki/AIM_alliance" title="AIM alliance">AIM alliance</a>). Motorola also has a diverse line of communication products, including <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> systems, <a href="/wiki/Digital_data" title="Digital data">digital</a> <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">cable</a> boxes and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modems" title="Modems">modems</a>.</p>
<p>In 1986, Motorola invented the <a href="/wiki/Six_Sigma" title="Six Sigma">Six Sigma</a> quality improvement process. This became a global standard. In 1990 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Instrument_Corporation" title="General Instrument Corporation">General Instrument Corporation</a>, which was later acquired by Motorola, proposed the first all-digital <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HDTV" title="HDTV">HDTV</a> standard. In the same year the company introduced the Bravo numeric pager which became the world's best-selling pager.</p>
<p>In 1991, Motorola demonstrated the world's first working-prototype digital cellular system and phones using <a href="/wiki/GSM" title="GSM">GSM</a> standard in <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a>, Germany. In 1994, Motorola introduced the world's first commercial digital radio system that combined paging, data and cellular communications and voice dispatch in a single radio network and handset. In 1995, Motorola introduced the world's first two-way <a href="/wiki/Pager" title="Pager">pager</a> which allowed users to receive text messages and e-mail and reply with a standard response.</p>
<p>In 1998, Motorola was overtaken by <a href="/wiki/Nokia" title="Nokia">Nokia</a> as the world's biggest seller of mobile phone handsets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-money.cnn.com_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-money.cnn.com-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 15, 1999, Motorola announced it would buy <a href="/wiki/General_Instrument" title="General Instrument">General Instrument</a> in an $11 billion stock swap. General Instrument had long been the No. 1 cable TV equipment provider, supplying cable operators with end-to-end hybrid fiber coax cable solutions. This meant that GI offers all cable TV transmission network components from the head-end to the fiber optic transmission nodes to the cable set-top boxes, now at the availability of Motorola. GI's acquisition created the Broadband Communications Sector (BCS).</p>
<p>In 1999, Motorola separated a portion of its semiconductor business—the Semiconductor Components Group (SCG)-- and formed <a href="/wiki/ON_Semiconductor" title="ON Semiconductor">ON Semiconductor</a>, whose headquarters are located in <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix, Arizona</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2000, Motorola and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cisco" title="Cisco">Cisco</a> supplied the world's first commercial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPRS" title="GPRS">GPRS</a> cellular network to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BT_Cellnet" title="BT Cellnet">BT Cellnet</a> in the United Kingdom. The world's first GPRS cell phone was also developed by Motorola. In August 2000, with recent acquisitions, Motorola reached its peak employment of 150,000 employees worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Two years later, employment would be at 93,000 due to layoffs and spinoffs.</p>
<p>In 2002, Motorola introduced the world's first wireless <a href="/wiki/Cable_modem" title="Cable modem">cable modem</a> <a href="/wiki/Gateway_(telecommunications)" title="Gateway (telecommunications)">gateway</a> which combined a high-speed cable modem <a href="/wiki/Router_(computing)" title="Router (computing)">router</a> with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethernet_switch" title="Ethernet switch">ethernet switch</a> and wireless home gateway. In 2003, Motorola introduced the world's first handset to combine a <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a> and <a href="/wiki/Java_(programming_language)" title="Java (programming language)">Java</a> technology with "full <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PDA" title="PDA">PDA</a> functionality". In 2004, Motorola divested its whole semiconductor business to form <a href="/wiki/Freescale_Semiconductor" title="Freescale Semiconductor">Freescale Semiconductor</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorola_RAZR_V3" title="Motorola RAZR V3">Motorola RAZR line</a> sold over 120 million units, which brought the company to the number two mobile phone slot in 2005.</p>
<p>In June 2005, Motorola overtook the intellectual property of <a href="/wiki/Sendo" title="Sendo">Sendo</a> for $30,000 and paid £362,575 for the plant, machinery and equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2006, Motorola acquired the software platform (<a href="/wiki/AJAR_(applications_software_platform)" title="AJAR (applications software platform)">AJAR</a>) developed by the British company TTP Communications plc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Later in 2006, the firm announced a music subscription service named <i>iRadio</i>. The technology came after a break in a partnership with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_Computer" title="Apple Computer">Apple Computer</a> (which in 2005 had produced an <a href="/wiki/ITunes" title="ITunes">iTunes</a> compatible cell phone <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ROKR_E1" title="ROKR E1">ROKR E1</a>, and most recently, mid-2007, its own <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a>). iRadio has many similarities with existing satellite radio services (such as <a href="/wiki/Sirius_Satellite_Radio" title="Sirius Satellite Radio">Sirius</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/XM_Radio" title="XM Radio">XM Radio</a>) by offering live streams of commercial-free music content. Unlike satellite services, however, iRadio content will be downloaded via a broadband internet connection. As of 2008, iRadio has not been commercially released and no further information is available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, Motorola acquired <a href="/wiki/Symbol_Technologies" title="Symbol Technologies">Symbol Technologies</a> to provide products and systems for enterprise mobility solutions, including rugged mobile computing, advanced data capture, and radio frequency identification (RFID).</p>
<p>In 2010, Motorola sold its cellular-infrastructure business to <a href="/wiki/Nokia_Networks" title="Nokia Networks">Nokia Siemens Networks</a> for $1.2 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motorola.2C_post-split">Motorola, post-split</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Motorola, post-split">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 2011, Motorola split into two separate companies, each still using the word <b>Motorola</b> as part of its name. One company, Motorola Solutions (using a blue version of the Motorola logo), is based in the Chicago suburb of Schaumburg, Illinois, and concentrates on police technologies, radios, and commercial needs. The other company, Motorola Mobility (using a red logo), is based in Chicago (formerly in the Chicago suburb of Libertyville, Illinois), and is the mobile handset producer. The split was structured so that Motorola Solutions was the legal successor of the original Motorola, while Motorola Mobility was the spin-off.</p>
<p>On August 15, 2011, Google announced that it would purchase Motorola Mobility for about $12.5 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> On November 17, 2011, Motorola Mobility stockholders “voted overwhelmingly to approve the proposed merger with Google Inc”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 22, 2012, Google announced that the acquisition of Motorola Mobility Holdings, Inc. had closed, with Google acquiring MMI for $40.00 per share in cash. ($12.5 billion)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 30, 2014, Google sold off Motorola Mobility to Lenovo. The purchase price was approximately US $2.91 billion (subject to certain adjustments), including US$1.41 billion paid at close: US $660 million in cash and US$750 million in Lenovo ordinary shares (subject to a share cap/floor). The remaining US$1.5 billion was paid in the form of a three-year promissory note.</p>
<p>After the purchase, Google maintained ownership of the vast majority of the Motorola Mobility patent portfolio, including current patent applications and invention disclosures, while Lenovo received a license to the portfolio of patents and other intellectual property. Additionally Lenovo received over 2,000 patent assets, as well as the Motorola Mobility brand and trademark portfolio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Divisional Products:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-motorola.com_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-motorola.com-37">[37]</a></sup></b></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Enterprise Mobility Solutions</b>: Headquarters located in <a href="/wiki/Schaumburg,_Illinois" title="Schaumburg, Illinois">Schaumburg, Illinois</a>; comprises communications offered to government and public safety sectors and enterprise mobility business. Motorola develops analog and digital two-way radio, voice and data communications products and systems, mobile computing, advanced data capture, wireless infrastructure and RFID solutions to customers worldwide.</li>
<li><b>Home &amp; Networks Mobility</b>: Headquarters located in <a href="/wiki/Arlington_Heights,_Illinois" title="Arlington Heights, Illinois">Arlington Heights, Illinois</a>; produces end-to-end systems that facilitate uninterrupted access to digital entertainment, information and communications services via wired and wireless mediums. Motorola develops digital video system solutions, interactive set-top devices, voice and data modems for digital subscriber line and cable networks, broadband access systems for cable and satellite television operators, and also wireline carriers and wireless service providers.</li>
<li><b>Mobile Devices</b>: Headquarters located in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago, Illinois</a>; designs wireless handsets, but also licenses much of its intellectual properties. This includes cellular and wireless systems and as well as integrated applications and <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth" title="Bluetooth">Bluetooth</a> accessories. Some of their latest gadgets are Moto X Gen 3, Moto X Play, Moto 360 smartwatch, etc.</li>
</ul>

<p>Motorola's handset division recorded a loss of US$1.2 billion in the fourth quarter of 2007, while the company as a whole earned $100 million during that quarter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> It lost several key executives to rivals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and the web site <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TrustedReviews" title="TrustedReviews">TrustedReviews</a> called the company's products repetitive and uninnovative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Motorola laid off 3,500 workers in January 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> followed by a further 4,000 job cuts in June<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> and another 20% cut of its research division a few days later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In July 2008, a large number of executives left Motorola to work on <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a>'s <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The company's handset division was also put on offer for sale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Also that month, analyst Mark McKechnie from American Technology Research said that Motorola "would be lucky to fetch $500 million" for selling its handset business. Analyst Richard Windsor said that Motorola might have to pay someone to take the division off the company's hands, and that Motorola may even exit the handset market altogether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Its global market share has been on the decline; from 18.4% of the market in 2007 the company had a share of just 6.0% by Q1 2009, but at last Motorola scored a profit of $26 million in Q2 and showed an increase of 12% in stocks for the first time after losses in many quarters. During the second quarter of 2010, the company reported a profit of $162 million, which compared very favorably to the $26 million earned for the same period the year before. Its Mobile Devices division reported, for the first time in years, earnings of $87 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spin-offs">Spin-offs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Spin-offs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television_and_radio_manufacturing">Television and radio manufacturing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Television and radio manufacturing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1974, Motorola divested itself of its television and radio-manufacturing division, which included the <a href="/wiki/Quasar_(brand)" title="Quasar (brand)">Quasar</a> brand of electronics. This division was acquired by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panasonic_Corporation" title="Panasonic Corporation">Matsushita</a>, already known under its <a href="/wiki/Panasonic" title="Panasonic">Panasonic</a> brand in North America, where it was looking to expand.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iridium">Iridium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Iridium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iridium_(satellite)" title="Iridium (satellite)">Iridium (satellite)</a></div>
<p>Motorola developed the global communication network using a set of 77 satellites. The business ambitions behind this project and the need to raise venture capital to fund the project led to the creation of the <b>Iridium company</b> in the late 1990s. While the technology was proven to work, Iridium failed to attract sufficient customers and it filed for bankruptcy in 1999. Obligations to Motorola and loss of expected revenue caused Motorola to divest the <a href="/wiki/ON_Semiconductor" title="ON Semiconductor">ON Semiconductor (ONNN)</a> business August 4, 1999, raising about $1.1 billion.</p>
<p>Motorola manufactured two <a href="/wiki/Satellite_phone" title="Satellite phone">satellite phone</a> handsets for this network – the 9500 and 9505 as well as transceiver units. Some of these are still in production by an OEM but sold under the Iridium brand.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_and_defense">Government and defense</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Government and defense">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to declines in business in 2000 and 2001, Motorola spun off its government and defense business to <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics" title="General Dynamics">General Dynamics</a>. The business deal closed September 2001. Thus GD Decision Systems was formed (and later merged with General Dynamics C4 Systems) from Motorola's Integrated Information Systems Group.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semiconductor">Semiconductor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Semiconductor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On August 4, 1999, Motorola, Inc.'s Semiconductor Components Group, manufacturing Motorola's discrete, standard analog and standard logic devices was spun off, recapitalized and established as an independent company named <a href="/wiki/ON_Semiconductor" title="ON Semiconductor">ON Semiconductor</a>.</p>
<p>On October 16, 2004, Motorola announced that it would spin off its Semiconductor Products Sector into a separate company called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freescale_Semiconductor,_Inc." title="Freescale Semiconductor, Inc.">Freescale Semiconductor, Inc.</a>. The new company began trading on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> on July 16 of the following year.</p>
<p>On Dec. 7, 2015 Freescale Inc. was sold to NXP Semiconductor, a European company.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Automotive">Automotive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Automotive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On January 29, 1988, Motorola sold its <a href="/wiki/Arcade_(village),_New_York" title="Arcade (village), New York">Arcade, New York</a> facility and automotive alternators, electromechanical speedometers and tachometers products to <a href="/wiki/Prestolite_Electric" title="Prestolite Electric">Prestolite Electric</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2006, Motorola completed the sale of its automotive business to <a href="/wiki/Continental_AG" title="Continental AG">Continental AG</a>. Motorola’s automotive unit had annual sales of $1.6 billion (€1.33 billion) and employed 4,504. The divisions products included <a href="/wiki/Telematics" title="Telematics">telematics</a> systems - like GM's OnStar used for vehicle navigation and safety services, engine and transmission control electronics, vehicle control, electronics and sensors used in steering, braking, and power doors and <a href="/wiki/Power_window" title="Power window">power windows</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biometrics">Biometrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Biometrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, Motorola acquired Printrak International Inc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> for $160 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In doing so, Motorola not only acquired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_aided_dispatch" title="Computer aided dispatch">computer aided dispatch</a> and related software, but also acquired <a href="/wiki/Automated_fingerprint_identification" title="Automated fingerprint identification">Automated fingerprint identification</a> system software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2008, Motorola agreed to sell its Biometrics business to <a href="/wiki/Safran" title="Safran">Safran</a>, a French defense firm. Motorola's biometric business unit was headquartered in Anaheim, Calif. The deal closed in April 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The unit became part of Sagem <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morpho_(Safran)" title="Morpho (Safran)">Morpho</a>, which was renamed <a href="/wiki/Safran#Other_subsidiaries_3" title="Safran">MorphoTrak</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Split">Split</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Split">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On March 26, 2008, Motorola's board of directors approved a split into two different publicly traded companies. This came after talk of selling the handset division to another corporation. These new companies would comprise the business units of the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorola_Mobile_Devices" title="Motorola Mobile Devices">Motorola Mobile Devices</a> and Motorola Broadband &amp; Mobility Solutions. Originally it was expected that this action would be approved by regulatory bodies and complete by mid-2009, but the split was delayed due to company restructuring problems and the 2008–2009 extreme economic downturn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 11, 2010, Motorola announced its separation into two independent, publicly traded companies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> effective Q1 2011. The official split occurred at around 12:00 pm EST on January 4, 2011. The two new companies are called <a href="/wiki/Motorola_Mobility" title="Motorola Mobility">Motorola Mobility</a> (now owned by <a href="/wiki/Lenovo" title="Lenovo">Lenovo</a>; cell phone and cable television equipment company) and <a href="/wiki/Motorola_Solutions" title="Motorola Solutions">Motorola Solutions</a> (<a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">NYSE</a>: <a class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/XNYS:MSI" rel="nofollow">MSI</a>; Government and Enterprise Business). Motorola Solutions is generally considered to be the direct successor to Motorola, Inc., as the reorganization was structured with Motorola Mobility being spun off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-google1-6">[6]</a></sup> Motorola Solutions retains Motorola, Inc.'s pre-2011 stock price history, though it retired the old ticker symbol of "MOT" in favor of "MSI."</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Motorola_Mobility_deal_by_Google">Motorola Mobility deal by Google</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Motorola Mobility deal by Google">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On August 15, 2011, seven months after Motorola Mobility was spun off into an independent company, <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> announced that it would acquire Motorola Mobility for $12.5 billion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> subject to approval from regulators in the United States and Europe.</p>
<p>According to the filing, Google senior vice president Andy Rubin first reached out to <a href="/wiki/Motorola_Mobility" title="Motorola Mobility">Motorola Mobility</a> in early July 2011 to discuss the purchase by some of Google's competitors of the patent portfolio of Nortel Networks Corp., and to assess its potential impact on the Android ecosystem.</p>
<p>Google boosted its offer for Motorola Mobility by 33% in a single day in early August, even though Motorola wasn't soliciting competing bids. The aggressive bidding by Google showed that<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the search engine company was under considerable pressure to beef up its patent portfolio to protect its promising Android franchise from a growing number of legal challenges.</p>
<p>According to the filing, Google and Motorola began discussions about Motorola's patent portfolio in early July, as well as the "intellectual property litigation and the potential impact of such litigation on the Android ecosystem".</p>
<p>Although the two companies discussed the possibility of an acquisition after the initial contact by Mr. Rubin, it was only after Motorola pushed back on the idea of patent sale that the acquisition talks picked up steam.</p>
<p>The turning point came during a meeting on July 6. At the meeting, Motorola CEO <a href="/wiki/Sanjay_Jha" title="Sanjay Jha">Sanjay Jha</a> discussed the protection of the Android ecosystem with Google senior vice president <a href="/wiki/Nikesh_Arora" title="Nikesh Arora">Nikesh Arora</a>, and during that talk Jha told Arora that "it could be problematic for Motorola Mobility to continue to exist as a stand-alone entity if it sold a large portion of its patent portfolio".</p>
<p>In connection with these discussions, the two companies signed a confidentiality and non-disclosure agreement that allowed Google to do due diligence on the company's patent portfolio.</p>
<p>On July 21 and 23, Jha met with Arora and Rubin to discuss strategic options between the two companies, agreeing to continue to discuss a potential sale. On the morning of August 15, the two companies entered into a merger agreement at the offered price of $40. On November 17, Motorola Mobility stockholders approved the proposed merger with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Google_Inc" title="Google Inc">Google Inc</a>. On April 17, 2013, ARRIS Group, Inc. (NASDAQ: ARRS) announced that it completed its acquisition of the Motorola Home business from a subsidiary of Google Inc.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Motorola_Mobility_.28Google.29_deal_by_Lenovo">Motorola Mobility (Google) deal by Lenovo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Motorola Mobility (Google) deal by Lenovo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On January 29, 2014, Google announced <a href="/wiki/Lenovo" title="Lenovo">Lenovo</a> plans to acquire the Motorola Mobility smartphone business. The purchase price is approximately $2.91 billion (subject to certain adjustments), including $1.41 billion paid at close: $660 million in cash and $750 million in Lenovo ordinary shares (subject to a share cap/floor). The remaining $1.5 billion will be paid in the form of a three-year promissory note.</p>
<p>Google maintained ownership of the vast majority of the Motorola Mobility patent portfolio, including active patent applications and invention disclosures. As part of its ongoing relationship with Google, Lenovo received a license to this rich portfolio of patents and other intellectual property. Additionally Lenovo received over 2,000 patent assets, as well as the Motorola Mobility brand and trademark portfolio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> On October 30, 2014, Lenovo finalized its purchase of Motorola Mobility from Google.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hello_Moto_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hello_Moto-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canopy_and_Orthogon">Canopy and Orthogon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Canopy and Orthogon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cambium Networks was created when Motorola Solutions sold the Canopy and Orthogon businesses in 2011. Cambium Networks has evolved the platform and expanded it to three product lines: Point to Point (PTP) (formerly Orthogon), Point to Multipoint (PMP) (formerly Canopy) and ePMP.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quality_systems">Quality systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Quality systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Six_Sigma" title="Six Sigma">Six Sigma</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quality_system" title="Quality system">quality system</a> was developed at Motorola even though it became best known through its use by <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a>. It was created by engineer <a href="/wiki/Bill_Smith_(Motorola_engineer)" title="Bill Smith (Motorola engineer)">Bill Smith</a>, under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Bob_Galvin" title="Bob Galvin">Bob Galvin</a> (son of founder Paul Galvin) when he was running the company. <a href="/wiki/Motorola_University" title="Motorola University">Motorola University</a> is one of many places that provide Six Sigma training.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Environmental record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Motorola, Inc., along with the Arizona Water Co. has been identified as the sources of <a href="/wiki/Trichloroethylene" title="Trichloroethylene">trichloroethylene</a> (TCE) contamination that took place in <a href="/wiki/Scottsdale,_Arizona" title="Scottsdale, Arizona">Scottsdale, Arizona</a>. The malfunction led to a ban on the use of water that lasted three days and affected almost 5000 people in the area. Motorola was found to be the main source of the TCE, an industrial solvent that is thought to cause cancer. The TCE contamination was caused by a faulty blower on an air stripping tower that was used to take TCE from the water, and Motorola has attributed the situation to operator error.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of eighteen leading electronics manufacturers in <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a>’s Guide to Greener Electronics (October 2010), Motorola shares sixth place with competitors Panasonic and Sony).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenpeace.org_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-greenpeace.org-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Motorola scores relatively well on the chemicals criteria and has a goal to eliminate PVC plastic and brominated flame retardants (BFRs), though only in mobile devices and not in all its products introduced after 2010, despite the fact that Sony Ericsson and Nokia are already there. All of its mobile phones are now PVC-free and it has two PVC and BFR-free mobile phones, the A45 ECO and the GRASP; all chargers are also free from PVC and BFRs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenpeace.org_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-greenpeace.org-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company is also increasing the proportion of recycled materials that used in its products. For example, the housings for the MOTO W233 Renew and MOTOCUBO A45 Eco mobile phones contain plastic from post-consumer recycled water cooler bottles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> According to the company’s information, all of Motorola’s newly designed chargers meet the current <a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">Energy Star</a> requirements and exceed the requirements for standby/no-load modes by at least 67%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sponsorships">Sponsorships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorola&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Sponsorships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Motorola sponsored <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Premier_League" title="Scottish Premier League">Scottish Premier League</a> club <a href="/wiki/Motherwell_F.C." title="Motherwell F.C.">Motherwell F.C.</a> for 11 years. This long term deal ended after the company started to reduce its manufacturing operations in Scotland. The company also sponsored <a href="/wiki/Livingston_F.C." title="Livingston F.C.">Livingston F.C.</a> between 1998 and 2002. The company also had a plant on the edge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Livingston,_Scotland" title="Livingston, Scotland">town</a>. However, this closed down at the same time as their sponsorship with the <a href="/wiki/Livingston_F.C." title="Livingston F.C.">club</a> ended. The South Stand at Livingston's <a href="/wiki/Almondvale_Stadium" title="Almondvale Stadium">Almondvale Stadium</a>, was named after the company, during their time of sponsorship. The company also sponsored a cycling team that counted Lance Armstrong amongst its members. Motorola is also a sponsor of <a href="/wiki/Danica_Patrick" title="Danica Patrick">Danica Patrick</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Beckham" title="David Beckham">David Beckham</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fergie_(singer)" title="Fergie (singer)">Fergie</a>. It also sponsored the <a href="/wiki/Richmond_Football_Club" title="Richmond Football Club">Richmond Football Club</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Football_League" title="Australian Football League">Australian Football League</a> from 2004 to 2007. Motorola sponsored <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo_FC" title="São Paulo FC">São Paulo FC</a> from 2000 to 2001. Motorola also sponsored <a href="/wiki/Club_Bol%C3%ADvar" title="Club Bolívar">Club Bolívar</a> since 2008. Motorola awarded TrackIT Solutions for being "The company with most Innovative Enterprise Mobility Solution" in 2010.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Madden_NFL_07" title="Madden NFL 07">Madden NFL 07</a> franchise mode, a Motorola phone is used to communicate with coaches and agents.</p>
<p>Robby Gordon was sponsored by Motorola in 2007 and 2008. Motorola is on Gordon's car in <a href="/wiki/NASCAR_07" title="NASCAR 07">NASCAR 07</a> and <a href="/wiki/NASCAR_08" title="NASCAR 08">NASCAR 08</a>.</p>
