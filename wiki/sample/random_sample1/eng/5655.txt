<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forms_of_the_Name">Forms of the Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mot_(god)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Forms of the Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Ugaritic" title="Ugaritic">Ugaritic</a> myth, Mot (spelled <i>mt</i>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personification_of_death" title="Personification of death">personification of death</a>. The word is cognate with forms meaning 'death' in other <a href="/wiki/Semitic_languages" title="Semitic languages">Semitic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afro-Asiatic_languages" title="Afro-Asiatic languages">Afro-Asiatic languages</a>: with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> موت <i>mawt</i>; with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew_(language)" title="Hebrew (language)">Hebrew</a> מות (<i>mot or mavet</i>; ancient Hebrew <i>moth or maweth</i>); with <a href="/wiki/Maltese_language" title="Maltese language">Maltese</a> <i>mewt</i>; with <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a> <i>mautā</i>; with <a href="/wiki/Ge%27ez_language" title="Ge'ez language">Ge'ez</a> <i>mot</i>; with <a href="/wiki/Canaanite_languages" title="Canaanite languages">Canaanite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egyptian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">Berber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_language" title="Aramaic language">Aramaic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nabataeans" title="Nabataeans">Nabataean</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palmyra,_Syria" title="Palmyra, Syria">Palmyrene</a> מות (<i>mwt</i>); with Jewish Aramaic, <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestinian</a> Aramaic, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samaritan" title="Samaritan">Samaritan</a> מותא (<i>mwt’</i>); with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mandaean" title="Mandaean">Mandaean</a> <i>muta</i>; with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akkadian_language" title="Akkadian language">Akkadian</a> <i>mūtu</i>; with <a href="/wiki/Hausa_language" title="Hausa language">Hausa</a> <i>mutuwa</i>; and with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angas_language" title="Angas language">Angas</a> <i>mut</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outline_of_Myth">Outline of Myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mot_(god)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Outline of Myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main source of the story of Mot 'Death' is <a href="/wiki/Ugaritic" title="Ugaritic">Ugaritic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He is a son of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_(god)" title="El (god)">'El</a>, and according to instructions given by the god <a href="/wiki/Hadad" title="Hadad">Hadad</a> (<a href="/wiki/Baal" title="Baal">Ba‘al</a>) to his messengers, lives in a city named <i>hmry</i> ('Mirey'), a pit is his throne, and Filth is the land of her heritage. But Ba‘al warns them:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>that you not come near to divine Death,<br/></p>
<p>lest he made you like a lamb in his mouth,<br/></p>
(and) you both be carried away like a kid in the breach of his windpipe.</blockquote>
<p>Hadad seems to be urging that Mot come to his feast and submit himself to Hadad.</p>
<p>Death sends back a message that his appetite is that of lions in the wilderness, like the longing of dolphins in the sea and he threatens to devour Ba‘al himself. In a subsequent passage Death seemingly makes good his threat, or at least is deceived into believing he has slain Ba‘al. Numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacuna_(MS)" title="Lacuna (MS)">gaps in the text</a> make this portion of the tale obscure. The sun stops shining as its goddess <a href="/wiki/Shapash" title="Shapash">Shapash</a> joins Ba‘al's sister <a href="/wiki/Anat" title="Anat">‘Anat</a> in burying him. ‘Anat then comes upon Mot, seizing him, splitting him with a blade, winnowing him in a sieve, burning him in a fire, grinding him under a millstone, and throwing what remains in the end over a field for birds to devour.</p>
<p>After seven years, Death returns, seeking vengeance and demanding one of Ba‘al's brothers to feed upon. A gap in the text is followed by Mot complaining that Ba‘al has given Mot his own brothers to eat, the sons of his mother to consume. A single combat between the two breaks out until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sun_god" title="Sun god">sun goddess</a> <a href="/wiki/Shapash" title="Shapash">Shapash</a> upbraids Mot, informing him that his own father <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_(god)" title="El (god)">El</a> will turn against him and overturn his throne if he continues. Mot concedes and the conflict ends.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phoenician">Phoenician</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mot_(god)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Phoenician">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A Phoenician account survives in a paraphrase of the Greek author <a href="/wiki/Philo_of_Byblos" title="Philo of Byblos">Philo of Byblos</a> by <a href="/wiki/Eusebius" title="Eusebius">Eusebius</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> who writes of a Phoenician historian named <a href="/wiki/Sanchuniathon" title="Sanchuniathon">Sanchuniathon</a>. In this account Death is a son of 'El and counted as a god, as the text says in speaking of 'El/<a href="/wiki/Cronus" title="Cronus">Cronus</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>... and not long afterwards he consecrated after his death another of his sons, called Muth, whom he had by <a href="/wiki/Rhea_(mythology)" title="Rhea (mythology)">Rhea</a>; this (Muth) the Phoenicians esteem the same as <a href="/wiki/Thanatos" title="Thanatos">Thanatos</a> ['Death'] and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pluto_(god)" title="Pluto (god)">Pluto</a>.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>But earlier in a philosophical creation myth Sanchuniathon has referred to great wind which merged with its parents and that connection was called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eros_(mythology)" title="Eros (mythology)">Eros</a> 'Desire':</p>
<blockquote>
<p>From its connection Mot was produced, which some say is mud, and others a putrescence of watery compound; and out of this came every germ of creation, and the generation of the universe. So there were certain animals which had no sensation, and out of them grew intelligent animals, and were called "Zophasemin," that is "observers of heaven"; and they were formed like the shape of an egg. Also Mot burst forth into light, and sun, and moon, and stars, and the great constellations.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The language here is confusing, a bad summary and possibly corrupt, and the form <i>Mot</i> here is not the same as <i>Muth</i> which appears later. But it may be that the full and coherent account would have made clear that muddy and putrescent Death is the source of life.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hebrew">Hebrew</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mot_(god)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hebrew">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Hebrew scriptures, Death ("<i>Maweth</i>") is sometimes personified as a devil or angel of death (e.g., <a class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Habakkuk+2:5–2:5&amp;version=nrsv" rel="nofollow">Habakkuk 2:5</a>; <a class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Job+18:13–18:13&amp;version=nrsv" rel="nofollow">Job 18:13</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In both the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Hosea" title="Book of Hosea">Book of Hosea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Jeremiah" title="Book of Jeremiah">Book of Jeremiah</a>, Maweth/Mot is mentioned as a deity to whom Yahweh can turn over Judah as punishment for worshiping other gods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
