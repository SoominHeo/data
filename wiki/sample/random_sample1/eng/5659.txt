<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_classification" title="Biological classification">biological classification</a>, the <b>order</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>ordo</i></span>) is</p>
<ol>
<li>a <a href="/wiki/Taxonomic_rank" title="Taxonomic rank">taxonomic rank</a> used in the classification of organisms and recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Nomenclature_codes" title="Nomenclature codes">nomenclature codes</a>. Other well-known ranks are <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domain_(biology)" title="Domain (biology)">domain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_(biology)" title="Kingdom (biology)">kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">phylum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">class</a>, <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, with order fitting in between class and family. An immediately higher rank, <b>superorder</b>, may be added directly above order, while <b>suborder</b> would be a lower rank.</li>
<li>a taxonomic unit, a <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxon</a>, in that rank. In that case the <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">plural</a> is orders (Latin <i>ordines</i>).</li>
</ol>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><i>Example</i>: The <a href="/wiki/Juglans" title="Juglans">Juglans</a> (<a href="/wiki/Walnut" title="Walnut">walnut</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Hickory" title="Hickory">Hickory</a> trees belong to the family <a href="/wiki/Juglandaceae" title="Juglandaceae">Juglandaceae</a> (or walnut family), which is placed in the order <a href="/wiki/Fagales" title="Fagales">Fagales</a>.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>What does and does not belong to each order is determined by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxonomist" title="Taxonomist">taxonomist</a>, as is whether a particular order should be recognized at all. Often there is no exact agreement, with different taxonomists each taking a different position. There are no hard rules that a taxonomist needs to follow in describing or recognizing an order. Some taxa are accepted almost universally, while others are recognised only rarely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>For some groups of organisms, consistent suffixes are used to denote that the rank is an order. The Latin <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a> <i>-(i)formes</i> meaning "having the form of" is used for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_name" title="Scientific name">scientific name</a> of orders of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birds" title="Birds">birds</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishes" title="Fishes">fishes</a>, but not for those of <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>. The suffix <i>-ales</i> is for the name of orders of plants, fungi, and algae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hierarchy_of_ranks">Hierarchy of ranks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Order_(biology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hierarchy of ranks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In their 1997 classification of mammals, McKenna and Bell used two extra levels between superorder and order: "grandorder" and "mirorder".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKennaBell1997_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-McKennaBell1997-3">[3]</a></sup> Michael Novacek (1986) inserted them at the same position. <a href="/wiki/Michael_Benton" title="Michael Benton">Michael Benton</a> (2005) inserted them between superorder and magnorder instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MichaelJBenton2005_VPalaentology_3rd_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MichaelJBenton2005_VPalaentology_3rd-4">[4]</a></sup> This position was adopted by Systema Naturae 2000 and others.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Botany">Botany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Order_(biology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Botany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In botany, the ranks of subclass and suborder are secondary ranks pre-defined as respectively above and below the rank of order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botarticle4_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-botarticle4-5">[5]</a></sup> Any number of further ranks can be used as long as they are clearly defined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botarticle4_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-botarticle4-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The superorder rank is commonly used, with the ending -anae that was initiated by <a href="/wiki/Armen_Takhtajan" title="Armen Takhtajan">Armen Takhtajan</a>'s publications from 1966 onwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_concept">History of the concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Order_(biology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History of the concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The order as a distinct rank of biological classification having its own distinctive name (and not just called a <i>higher <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a></i> <i>(genus summum))</i> was first introduced by the German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Botanist" title="Botanist">botanist</a> <a href="/wiki/Augustus_Quirinus_Rivinus" title="Augustus Quirinus Rivinus">Augustus Quirinus Rivinus</a> in his classification of plants that appeared in a series of treatises in the 1690s. <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> was the first to apply it consistently to the division of all three <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_(biology)" title="Kingdom (biology)">kingdoms</a> of nature (<a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>) in his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> (1735, 1st. Ed.).</p>

<p>For plants, Linnaeus' orders in the <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Species_Plantarum" title="Species Plantarum">Species Plantarum</a></i> were strictly artificial, introduced to subdivide the artificial <a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">classes</a> into more comprehensible smaller groups. When the word <i>ordo</i> was first consistently used for natural units of plants, in 19th century works such as the <i><a href="/wiki/De_Candolle_system" title="De Candolle system">Prodromus</a></i> of de Candolle and the <i><a href="/wiki/Bentham_%26_Hooker_system" title="Bentham &amp; Hooker system">Genera Plantarum</a></i> of Bentham &amp; Hooker, it indicated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxa" title="Taxa">taxa</a> that are now given the rank of family (see <i><a href="/wiki/Ordo_naturalis" title="Ordo naturalis">ordo naturalis</a></i>, <i>natural order</i>).</p>
<p>In French botanical publications, from <a href="/wiki/Michel_Adanson" title="Michel Adanson">Michel Adanson</a>'s <i>Familles naturelles des plantes</i> (1763) and until the end of the 19th century, the word <i>famille</i> (<a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">plural</a>: <i>familles</i>) was used as a French equivalent for this Latin <i>ordo</i>. This equivalence was explicitly stated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alphonse_Louis_Pierre_Pyrame_de_Candolle" title="Alphonse Louis Pierre Pyrame de Candolle">Alphonse De Candolle's</a> <i>Lois de la nomenclature botanique</i> (1868), the precursor of the currently used <i><a href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Nomenclature_for_algae,_fungi,_and_plants" title="International Code of Nomenclature for algae, fungi, and plants">International Code of Nomenclature for algae, fungi, and plants</a></i>.</p>
<p>In the first international <i>Rules</i> of <a href="/wiki/Botanical_nomenclature" title="Botanical nomenclature">botanical nomenclature</a> from the <a href="/wiki/International_Botanical_Congress" title="International Botanical Congress">International Botanical Congress</a> of 1905, the word family (<i>familia</i>) was assigned to the rank indicated by the French "famille", while order (<i>ordo</i>) was reserved for a higher rank, for what in the 19th century had often been named a <i>cohors</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> (plural <i>cohortes</i>).</p>
<p>Some of the plant families still retain the names of Linnaean "natural orders" or even the names of pre-Linnaean natural groups recognised by Linnaeus as orders in his natural classification (e.g. <i>Palmae</i> or <i>Labiatae</i>). Such names are known as <a href="/wiki/Descriptive_botanical_names" title="Descriptive botanical names">descriptive</a> family names.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zoology_2">Zoology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Order_(biology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Zoology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoology</a>, the Linnaean orders were used more consistently. That is, the orders in the zoology part of the <i>Systema Naturae</i> refer to natural groups. Some of his ordinal names are still in use (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">Lepidoptera</a> for the order of <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterflies</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diptera" title="Diptera">Diptera</a> for the order of <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquitoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Midge" title="Midge">midges</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gnat" title="Gnat">gnats</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
