<p>In <a href="/wiki/Martial_arts" title="Martial arts">martial arts</a>, a <b>waster</b> is a practice weapon, usually a <a href="/wiki/Sword" title="Sword">sword</a>, and usually made out of wood, though <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylon</a> (plastic) wasters are also available. The use of <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> or nylon instead of metal provides an economic and safe option for initial weapons training and sparring, at some loss of genuine experience. A weighted waster may be used for a sort of strength training, making the movements of using an actual sword comparatively easier and quicker. Wasters as wooden practice weapons have been found in a variety of cultures over a number of centuries, including ancient <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_Europe" title="Medieval Europe">medieval</a> and <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_Europe" title="Early modern Europe">renaissance Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, and into the modern era in Europe and the United States. Over the course of time, wasters took a variety of forms not necessarily influenced by chronological succession, ranging from simple sticks to clip-point dowels with <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a> <a href="/wiki/Basket-hilted_sword" title="Basket-hilted sword">basket hilts</a> to careful <a href="/wiki/Replica" title="Replica">replicas</a> of real swords.</p>
<p>Used commonly in the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_european_martial_arts" title="Historical european martial arts">historical european martial arts</a> community, the term refers to wasters fashioned to resemble western European weapons like the longsword or arming sword. Historically, the term "waster" was used in English to refer to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cudgel" title="Cudgel">cudgels</a> or clubs used as weapons, in addition to wooden swords. The increasingly popular historical martial arts reconstruction groups, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Live_action_role-playing_game" title="Live action role-playing game">live action role-playing</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renaissance_festival" title="Renaissance festival">renaissance festival</a> groups, have provided an ample market for commercial waster retailers. As the martial art has grown and academic interest has risen in weapons other than the longsword and arming sword, other types of wasters have been produced commercially.</p>
<p>The concept of wooden practice weapons is not limited to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_european_martial_arts" title="Historical european martial arts">historical european martial arts</a>. Some <a href="/wiki/Japanese_martial_arts" title="Japanese martial arts">Japanese martial arts</a> involving swordsmanship, such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kenjuts_u&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kenjuts u (page does not exist)">kenjuts u</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iaido" title="Iaido">iaido</a>, use <a href="/wiki/Bokken" title="Bokken">bokken</a> or <a href="/wiki/Shinai" title="Shinai">shinai</a> as practice weapons. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eskrima" title="Eskrima">Eskrima</a>, a martial art from the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, also uses a type of rattan stick as a practice weapon in place of a blade. The martial art of <a href="/wiki/Singlestick" title="Singlestick">singlestick</a> is more or less entirely derived from the use of wasters as practice weapons in place of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadsword" title="Broadsword">broadswords</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a> a waster or kiln waster is a piece that has not fired successfully and been discarded.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Historically, students and soldiers used wasters as inexpensive and expendable training tools. The cost of high quality steel weapons, especially swords, would have made them a poor choice for practice weapons. Constant training would fatigue the blade, rendering it far less effective and reliable as a weapon. To prevent the destruction of an expensive weapon and to permit the necessary training and <a href="/wiki/Sparring" title="Sparring">sparring</a> intrinsic to any martial art, wooden practice weapons were created.</p>
<p>Today, especially in the reconstruction of <a href="/wiki/Historical_European_martial_arts" title="Historical European martial arts">historical European martial arts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Historical_reenactment" title="Historical reenactment">Historical reenactment</a>, wasters have experienced renewed interest. Wasters provide a number of benefits to the modern practitioner, many of which would have applied to historic trainees as well. The wood construction coupled with unsharpened edges and blunted tip, <a href="/wiki/Crossguard" title="Crossguard">crossguard</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hilt#Pommel" title="Hilt">pommel</a> of wooden swords provides a safer alternative to practising with a sharpened or unsharpened steel weapon. Wasters do not cut flesh, but provide a decidedly blunt impact. The lower cost of ownership in comparison to a steel weapon of the same variety makes the waster a much more affordable and expendable tool.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Many modern wasters are fashioned to replicate the original weapon with accuracy, including functional integral sword parts. This functionality allows the wooden weapon to be handled more like its steel counterpart.</p>
<p>Wasters are not without their faults. The all wooden construction usually makes wasters somewhat lighter and differently balanced than steel weapons. The difference of material properties between wood and steel creates a difference in performance when training and sparring. The wood wasters tend to recoil from strong contact with other wasters as may occur in a strong parry or <i>absetzen</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> a phenomenon colloquially referred to as "waster bounce". Steel weapons do not display this attribute to the same extent, usually binding and sliding with minimal rebound instead. The use of wood with rounded edges makes wasters considerably safer for practice than a steel weapon, but does not make them totally safe. Strong cuts or thrusts to vulnerable body parts during sparring may lead to significant injury to the individual. Wasters provide a safer training experience than steel weapons at the cost of authenticity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_martial_arts_reconstruction" title="Historical martial arts reconstruction">historical martial arts reconstruction</a> organizations, including the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Renaissance_Martial_Arts" title="Association for Renaissance Martial Arts">Association for Renaissance Martial Arts</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Swordplay_Guild" title="Chicago Swordplay Guild">Chicago Swordplay Guild</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> use wasters as the primary training tool of new practitioners. Wasters are used to learn, practice, and later spar with a variety of techniques including cuts, slices, thrusts and <a href="/wiki/Ward_(fencing)" title="Ward (fencing)">wards</a>. During <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floryshe" title="Floryshe">flourishes</a>, a waster may be substituted for a blunt sword, especially if a lack of experience is a concern. Participants may also use wasters against a <i>pell</i>, a training pole roughly simulating a human target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> As the individual becomes more skilled, they will begin to use blunt steel weapons which offer a more realistic set of properties in comparison with a sharpened metal blade.</p>

<p>Modern commercial producers use primarily <a href="/wiki/Hickory" title="Hickory">Hickory</a>, a hard and resilient wood, in the construction of their wasters. Some producers allow individuals to accent the waster with wood of other types including <a href="/wiki/Hymenaea_courbaril" title="Hymenaea courbaril">Jatobá</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Purpleheart" title="Purpleheart">Purpleheart</a>. Manufacturers usually apply a coating of <a href="/wiki/Linseed_oil" title="Linseed oil">linseed oil</a> or other protective liquid and instruct users to regularly apply it. This prevents splintering and works to create a stronger, more enduring tool. Different specimens of wood, even of the same variety, are not necessarily identical in performance, and may display different characteristics during use.</p>

<p>The form of modern wasters follows from their use as replica training swords. Blades on wasters have a lenticular (lens-shaped) or diamond cross-section and defined edges. This shape continues into the hilt, which features a grip with an oval-shaped cross section oriented in the same plane as the blade. An integral part of historical swords, this oval shape permits the wielder to know the sword's rotational blade alignment by feeling for the position of the oblong grip in their hand. The pommel acts as suitable counterweight for the blade and a stable gripping surface, providing the sword's intrinsic balance and allowing the user a weighted leverage point for more powerful manipulation of the weapon. A functional cross acts as it does on a steel sword, protecting the hands and assisting in a number of guards and parries. During <a href="/wiki/Half-sword" title="Half-sword">half-swording</a>, the cross and pommel may also function as a striking portions of the weapon, used directly to cause injury as in the <a href="/wiki/Mordhau" title="Mordhau">mordhau</a>.</p>

<p>Wooden practice swords have been in use since the Late <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>, with an original sword found on <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mainland,_Orkney" title="Mainland, Orkney">Mainland</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> still in existence at the National Museum of Edinburgh. A similar find in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> adds historical backing to the Irish myth, the Tain, in which the use of a wooden training sword is mentioned. Egyptian soldiers practiced a sort of sport fencing using blunt sticks as a sort of primitive waster. The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> used a form of wooden sword, the <i>rudis</i>, for combat training. Translations of Roman poets <a href="/wiki/Horace" title="Horace">Horace</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satires_of_Juvenal" title="Satires of Juvenal">Juvenal</a> provide evidence of this training weapon in use. One translation of Juvenal's poetry by <a href="/wiki/Barten_Holyday" title="Barten Holyday">Barten Holyday</a> in 1661 makes note that the Roman trainees learned to fight with the wooden wasters before moving on to the use of sharpened steel, much in the way modern reconstruction groups progress. In fact, it is also found that Roman <a href="/wiki/Gladiator" title="Gladiator">gladiators</a> trained with a heavy wooden sword against a straw man or a wooden pole known as a <i>palus</i> (an early relative of the later wooden <i>pell</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Wasters are mentioned in period works, including <a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_the_Courtier" title="The Book of the Courtier">The Book of the Courtier</a>. A number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fechtbuch" title="Fechtbuch">Fechtbücher</a> also mention the use of wasters or depict them in use by models showing proper technique.</p>
<p>During the 16th century, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dussack" title="Dussack">Dussack</a> came into use in <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">German</a> fencing schools. A true waster, the dussack was made almost entirely of wood (in all but one known case) and acted as safe and cheap training weapon. The weapon's unique shape did not lend well to the replication of traditional cruciform-hilted swords like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arming_sword" title="Arming sword">arming sword</a> or <a href="/wiki/Longsword" title="Longsword">longsword</a>. Instead, the dussack resembled the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gro%C3%9Fes_Messer" title="Großes Messer">großes Messer</a></i> or "great knife", a weapon found more often amongst the common people than longswords, the cost of which allowed only relatively wealthy individuals to purchase them.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Longsword" title="Longsword">Longsword</a> wasters are generally between forty-two and fifty inches long and are also known colloquially as Hand-and-a-Half swords, allowing the use of both hands on the hilt while using them. These weapons incorporate a ridge or <a href="/wiki/Fuller_(weapon)" title="Fuller (weapon)">fuller</a>, defined edges, and other sword components commonly found on steel swords. Many of the fundamentals taught by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johannes_Lichtenauer" title="Johannes Lichtenauer">Johannes Lichtenauer</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears (July 2016)">pages needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and his students <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Ringeck" title="Sigmund Ringeck">Sigmund Ringeck</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears (July 2016)">pages needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Hans_Talhoffer" title="Hans Talhoffer">Hans Talhoffer</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears (July 2016)">pages needed</span></a></i>]</sup> frequently involve the longsword.</p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arming_sword" title="Arming sword">Arming sword</a> wasters span the broad gap of thirty-two to forty-two inches in length and, like their historical counterparts, have a shorter hilt than a longsword, allowing only one full hand to hold the grip. These wasters also commonly feature defined edges, pommels, and other typical sword elements. Arming swords are featured heavily in the combat of Manuscript <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I.33" title="I.33">I.33</a>, the oldest manuscript on sword-and-<a href="/wiki/Buckler" title="Buckler">buckler</a> fighting, dating approximately to the turn of 14th Century.</p>

<p>The ceremonial rudius, a wooden <a href="/wiki/Gladius" title="Gladius">gladius</a> given ceremonially to <a href="/wiki/Gladiator" title="Gladiator">gladiators</a> when they won enough battles to become free men, is produced by some current day vendors and is twenty-eight to thirty inches long. The producers warn that the rudii are for ceremonial purposes, however, and should not be used in mock combat. In this sense, the waster supersedes its place as a tool for combat and becomes primarily a work of art.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dussack" title="Dussack">Dussacks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Falchion" title="Falchion">falchion</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two-handed_sword" title="Two-handed sword">two-handed sword</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cut_and_thrust_sword" title="Cut and thrust sword">cut and thrust sword</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gladius" title="Gladius">gladius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viking_sword" title="Viking sword">Viking sword</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rapier" title="Rapier">rapier</a> wasters are not widely available from commercial vendors, but may be special ordered or hand-crafted.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Daggers">Daggers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Daggers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rondel_(dagger)" title="Rondel (dagger)">Rondel dagger</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> wasters, like the daggers themselves, are generally about eighteen inches in length, with a twelve-inch blade and six inch hilt. These weapons may forgo defined edges altogether and take on a more cylindrical shape as the rondel dagger acted historically as a thrusting and stabbing weapon. <a href="/wiki/Dagger" title="Dagger">Hilted dagger</a> wasters are also available, featuring functional crosses and defined edges, often found in lengths of about 18 inches.</p>
