<p>The <b>collared brown lemur</b> (<i>Eulemur collaris</i>), also known as the <b>red-collared brown lemur</b> or <b>red-collared lemur</b>, is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Strepsirrhini" title="Strepsirrhini">strepsirrhine</a> <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primate</a> and one of twelve species of <a href="/wiki/True_lemur" title="True lemur">brown lemur</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Lemuridae" title="Lemuridae">Lemuridae</a>. It is only found in south-eastern <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>. Like most species of <a href="/wiki/Lemur" title="Lemur">lemur</a>, it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arboreal" title="Arboreal">arboreal</a>, moving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadruped" title="Quadruped">quadrupedally</a> and occasionally leaping from tree to tree. Like other brown lemurs, it lives in social groups, primarily eats fruit, is active both day and night, exhibits <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_dichromatism" title="Sexual dichromatism">sexual dichromatism</a>, and does not demonstrate <a href="/wiki/Matriarchy" title="Matriarchy">female dominance</a>. The species is listed as <i>Endangered</i> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN) and is threatened primarily by <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat loss</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Collared_brown_lemur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Together with the twelve other <a href="/wiki/True_lemur" title="True lemur">true lemurs</a> (genus <i>Eulemur</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2008MittermeierGroves_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-2008MittermeierGroves-4">[4]</a></sup> the collared brown lemur (<i>E. collaris</i>) is a type of <a href="/wiki/Lemur" title="Lemur">lemur</a> belonging to the family <a href="/wiki/Lemuridae" title="Lemuridae">Lemuridae</a>. Collectively, lemurs (infraorder Lemuriformes) are classified as <a href="/wiki/Strepsirrhini" title="Strepsirrhini">strepsirrhine</a> <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primates</a>. Originally listed as a subspecies of the <a href="/wiki/Common_brown_lemur" title="Common brown lemur">common brown lemur</a> (<i>E. fulvus</i>), the collared brown lemur was promoted to full species status in 2001 by <a href="/wiki/Biological_anthropology" title="Biological anthropology">biological anthropologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Colin_Groves" title="Colin Groves">Colin Groves</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSW3_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-MSW3-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anatomy_and_physiology">Anatomy and physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Collared_brown_lemur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Anatomy and physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An adult collared brown lemur can reach a head-body length of 39 and 40 cm (15 and 16 in) and have a tail length of 50 and 55 cm (20 and 22 in) for an overall length of 89 and 95 cm (35 and 37 in). It has an average body weight of 2.25 and 2.5 kg (5.0 and 5.5 lb), making it a medium-sized lemur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2006Mittermeier_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-2006Mittermeier-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2007Garbutt_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-2007Garbutt-6">[6]</a></sup> The only form of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a> exhibited by the collared brown lemur is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_dichromatism" title="Sexual dichromatism">dichromatism</a>. <p>In the wild, the collared brown lemur's range does not overlap with other brown lemurs, so it is rarely confused with other species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2007Garbutt_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-2007Garbutt-6">[6]</a></sup> However, in captivity it can be easily confused with the <a href="/wiki/Gray-headed_lemur" title="Gray-headed lemur">gray-headed lemur</a> (<i>Eulemur cinereiceps</i>) due to similar coloration. The male collared brown lemur can be distinguished by their cream-colored or rufous beards, whereas the male gray-headed lemur has a white beard. Females of these two species are nearly indistinguishable, even though genetic analyses support full species status for both taxa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2006Mittermeier_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-2006Mittermeier-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Collared_brown_lemur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Found in tropical moist lowland and montane forests in southeastern Madagascar, the collared brown lemur occurs west to the forests of <a href="/wiki/Kalambatritra_Reserve" title="Kalambatritra Reserve">Kalambatritra</a> and in the south from <a href="/wiki/T%C3%B4lanaro" title="Tôlanaro">Tôlanaro</a> north to the <a href="/wiki/Mananara_River" title="Mananara River">Mananara River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2006Mittermeier_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-2006Mittermeier-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2007Garbutt_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-2007Garbutt-6">[6]</a></sup> The Mananara River is the boundary between the ranges of the collared brown lemur and the Gray-headed Lemur to the north. The collared brown lemur can be seen in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mandena_Conservation_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mandena Conservation Zone (page does not exist)">Mandena Conservation Zone</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Luce_Private_Reserve&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saint Luce Private Reserve (page does not exist)">Saint Luce Private Reserve</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Andohahela_National_Park" title="Andohahela National Park">Andohahela National Park</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2006Mittermeier_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-2006Mittermeier-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In its environment, the collared brown lemur acts as a <a href="/wiki/Seed_dispersal" title="Seed dispersal">seed disperser</a>, and is especially critical for the dispersal of large-seeded fruiting trees within its range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2004Bollen_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-2004Bollen-7">[7]</a></sup> However, there is no evidence that these relationships are <a href="/wiki/Coevolution" title="Coevolution">coevolutionary</a> and instead these lemurs may be the last remaining seed dispersers for these tree species following the extinction of larger <a href="/wiki/Frugivore" title="Frugivore">frugivorous</a> birds and <a href="/wiki/Subfossil_lemur" title="Subfossil lemur">subfossil lemurs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2004Bollen_a_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-2004Bollen_a-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Collared_brown_lemur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Very little is known about this species. It is thought to primarily eat fruit, like most other true lemurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2006Mittermeier_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-2006Mittermeier-5">[5]</a></sup> It is also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathemeral" title="Cathemeral">cathemeral</a> (active both day and night throughout the year), a trait seen in some other members of its genus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2006Mittermeier_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-2006Mittermeier-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2007Garbutt_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-2007Garbutt-6">[6]</a></sup> Research has suggested that metabolic dietary-related needs are the leading factor behind this behavior, although the specific hours of this activity pattern can shift based on <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">lunar</a> <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> and seasonal changes in the <a href="/wiki/Photoperiodism" title="Photoperiodism">photoperiod</a> (day length).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2009Donatia_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-2009Donatia-9">[9]</a></sup> Previous studies had ruled out effects of predators on the expression of this trait, and instead pointed to fruit availability and fiber intake as more important factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2007Donatia_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-2007Donatia-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The collared brown lemur tends to live in social groups that are multi-male/multi-female,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2006Mittermeier_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-2006Mittermeier-5">[5]</a></sup> with groups ranging in size from three to seven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2007Garbutt_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-2007Garbutt-6">[6]</a></sup> Population densities are estimated at 14 individuals/km<sup>2</sup>, and it appears to be common within its range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2006Mittermeier_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-2006Mittermeier-5">[5]</a></sup> Females give birth to one offspring between October and December, and male involvement with the young has been observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2007Garbutt_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-2007Garbutt-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Matriarchy" title="Matriarchy">Female dominance</a>, a common behavioral trait in many lemur species but uncommon in most <a href="/wiki/True_lemur" title="True lemur">true lemurs</a>, has not been observed in this species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2006Mittermeier_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-2006Mittermeier-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1999Sussman_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-1999Sussman-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<p>Brown lemurs at Berenty (hybrid <i>E. fulvus</i> x <i>collaris</i>) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> show linear hierarchy, adult female dominance, and the presence of conciliatory behavior after aggressions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Additionally, stress levels (measured via self-directed behaviors) decrease at the increase of the hierarchical position of individuals within the social group and reconciliation is able to bring stress down to the baseline levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Collared_brown_lemur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The collared brown lemur was listed as <i>Endangered</i> in the IUCN Red List assessment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup> Its greatest threat is <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat loss</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" title="Slash and burn">slash-and-burn agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> production. It is also hunted for food and captured for the local pet trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2006Mittermeier_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-2006Mittermeier-5">[5]</a></sup> However, populations of the collared brown lemur have been successfully sustained in captivity and continue to provide a safeguard against their extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISIS_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISIS-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>A small group of collared brown lemurs was introduced in the 1980s into the <a href="/wiki/Berenty_Reserve" title="Berenty Reserve">Berenty Private Reserve</a> and has since <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridized</a> with <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced</a> <a href="/wiki/Red-fronted_lemur" title="Red-fronted lemur">red-fronted lemurs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2006Mittermeier_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-2006Mittermeier-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
