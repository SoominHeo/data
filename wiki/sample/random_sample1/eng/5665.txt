<p>The <b>Moksha language</b> (Moshka: мокшень кяль <i>mokšeny käl</i>) is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Mordvinic_languages" title="Mordvinic languages">Mordvinic</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">Uralic languages</a>, with around 130,000 native speakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janurik2013_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Janurik2013-1">[1]</a></sup> Moksha is the majority language in the western part of <a href="/wiki/Mordovia" title="Mordovia">Mordovia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Its closest relative is the <a href="/wiki/Erzya_language" title="Erzya language">Erzya language</a>, with which it is not <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">mutually intelligible</a>. Moksha is also considered to be closely related to the extinct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meshcherian_language" title="Meshcherian language">Meshcherian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muromian_language" title="Muromian language">Muromian</a> languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LangDeathMain_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-LangDeathMain-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Official_status">Official status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moksha_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Official status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Moksha is one of the three official languages in <a href="/wiki/Mordovia" title="Mordovia">Mordovia</a> (the others being Erzya and Russian). The right to one's own language is guaranteed by the Constitution of the Mordovia Republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The republican law of Mordovia N 19-3 issued in 1998<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> declares Moksha one of its state languages and regulates its usage in various spheres: in state bodies such as Mordovian Parliament, official documents and seals, education, mass-media, information about goods, geographical names, road signs. However, the actual usage of Moksha and Erzya is rather limited.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moksha_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first few Moksha schools were devised in the 19th century by Russian Christian missionaries. Since 1973 Moksha language was allowed to be used as language of instruction in first 3 grades of elementary school in rural areas and as a subject on a voluntary basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The medium in universities of Mordovia is Russian, but the philological faculties of <a href="/wiki/Mordovian_State_University" title="Mordovian State University">Mordovian State University</a> and Mordovian State Pedagogical Institute offer a teacher course of Moksha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IzvMor_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-IzvMor-9">[9]</a></sup> Mordovian State University also provides a course of Moksha for other humanitarian and some technical specialities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IzvMor_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-IzvMor-9">[9]</a></sup> According to the annual statistics of the Russian Ministry of Education in 2014-2015 year there were 48 Moksha-medium schools (all in rural areas) where 644 students were taught, and 202 schools (152 in rural areas) where Moksha was studied as a subject by 15,783 students (5,412 in rural areas).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Since 2010, study of Moksha in schools of Mordovia is not compulsory, but can be chosen only by parents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Moksha languages is divided into three dialects:</p>
<ul>
<li>Central group (<b>M-I</b>)</li>
<li>South-Eastern group (<b>M-II</b>)</li>
<li>Western group (<b>M-III</b>)</li>
</ul>
<p>The dialects may be divided with another principle depending on their vowel system:</p>
<ul>
<li>ä-dialect: Proto-Moksha *<i>ä</i> /æ/ is retained: <i>śeĺmä</i> "eye", <i>t́äĺmä</i> "broom", <i>ĺäj</i> "river".</li>
<li>e-dialect: Proto-Moksha *<i>ä</i> is raised and merged with *<i>e</i>: <i>śeĺme</i> "eye", <i>t́eĺme</i> "broom", <i>ĺej</i> "river".</li>
<li>i-dialect: Proto-Moksha *<i>ä</i> is raised to /e/, while Proto-Moksha *<i>e</i> is raised to /i/ and merged with *<i>i</i>: <i>śiĺme</i> "eye", <i>t́eĺme</i> "broom", <i>ĺej</i> "river".</li>
</ul>
<p>The standard literary Moksha language is based on the central group with <i>ä</i> (particularly the dialect of <a href="/wiki/Krasnoslobodsk,_Republic_of_Mordovia" title="Krasnoslobodsk, Republic of Mordovia">Krasnoslobodsk</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moksha_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Phonology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">Vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moksha_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vowels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are eight vowels with a slight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allophony" title="Allophony">allophony</a> and reduction of unstressed vowels. Moksha has lost its original system of <a href="/wiki/Vowel_harmony" title="Vowel harmony">vowel harmony</a> but maintains consonant-vowel harmony (palatalized consonants go with front vowels, non-palatalized with non-front).</p>

<p>There are some restrictions for the occurrence of vowels within a word:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɨ]</span> is an allophone of the phoneme <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/</span> after phonemically non-palatalized ("hard") consonants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e/</span> does not occur after non-palatalized consonants, only after their palatalized ("soft") counterparts.</li>
<li><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/æ/</span> do not fully contrast after phonemically palatalized or non-palatalized consonants.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="What happens between palatalized and peripheral consonants? (June 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
<ul>
<li>Similar to <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/æ/</span> does not occur after non-palatalized consonants either, only after their palatalized counterparts.</li>
<li>After palatalized consonants, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/æ/</span> occurs at the end of words, and when followed by another palatalized consonant.</li>
<li><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span> after palatalized consonants occurs only before non-palatalized consonants, i.e. in the environment <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/CʲaC/</span>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>The mid vowels' occurrence varies by the position within the word:
<ul>
<li>In native words, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e, o/</span> are rare in the second syllable, but common in borrowings from e.g. Russian.</li>
<li><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e, o/</span> are never found in the third and following syllables, where only <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ə/</span> occurs.</li>
<li><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e/</span> at the end of words is only found in one-syllable words (e.g. <i>ве</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ve/</span> "night", <i>пе</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/pe/</span> "end"). In longer words, word-final ⟨е⟩ always stands for <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/æ/</span> (e.g. <i>веле</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/velʲæ/</span> "village", <i>пильге</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/pilʲgæ/</span> "foot, leg").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<p>Unstressed /a/ and /æ/ are slightly reduced and shortened [ă] and [æ̆].</p>

<p><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ç/</span> is realized as a sibilant <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɕ]</span> before the plural suffix /-t⁽ʲ⁾/ in south-east dialects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">Palatalization</a>, characteristic of <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">Uralic languages</a>, is contrastive only for <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">dental consonants</a>, which can be either "soft" or " hard". In Moksha Cyrillic alphabet the palatalization is designated like in Russian: either by a "soft sign" ⟨ь⟩ after a "soft" consonant or by writing "soft" vowels ⟨е, ё, и, ю, я⟩ after a "soft" consonant. In scientific transliteration the acute accent or apostrophe are used.</p>
<p>All other consonants have palatalized <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">allophones</a> before the <a href="/wiki/Front_vowel" title="Front vowel">front vowels</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/æ, i, e/</span> as well. The <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolo-palatal_affricate" title="Voiceless alveolo-palatal affricate">alveolo-palatal affricate</a> /tɕ/ lacks non-palatalized counterpart, while <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Postalveolar_fricative" title="Postalveolar fricative">postalveolar fricatives</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʂ~ʃ, ʐ~ʒ/</span> lack palatalized counterparts.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Devoicing">Devoicing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moksha_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Devoicing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Unusually for a Uralic language, there is also a series of voiceless <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_consonants" title="Liquid consonants">liquid consonants</a>: <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/l̥ , l̥ʲ, r̥ , r̥ʲ/</span> ⟨ʀ, ʀ́, ʟ, ʟ́⟩. These have arisen from Proto-Mordvinic consonant clusters of a sonorant followed by a voiceless stop or affricate: *p, *t, *tʲ, *ts⁽ʲ⁾, *k.</p>
<p>Before certain inflectional and derivational endings, devoicing continues to exist as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonological_process" title="Phonological process">phonological process</a> in Moksha. This affects all other voiced consonants as well, including the <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">nasal</a> consonants and <a href="/wiki/Semivowel" title="Semivowel">semivowel</a>. No voiceless nasals are however found in Moksha: the devoicing of nasals produces voiceless oral stops. Altogether the following devoicing processes apply:</p>

<p>E.g. before the nominative plural <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/-t⁽ʲ⁾/</span>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>кал</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kal/</span> "fish" : <i>калхт</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kal̥t/</span> "fish"</li>
<li><i>лем</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/lʲem/</span> "name" : <i>лепть</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/lʲeptʲ/</span> "names"</li>
<li><i>марь</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/marʲ/</span> "apple" : <i>марьхть</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/marʲtʲ/</span> "apples"</li>
</ul>
<p>Devoicing is however morphological rather than phonological, due to the loss of earlier voiceless stops from some consonant clusters, and due to the creation of new consonant clusters of voiced liquid + voiceless stop. Compare the following oppositions:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>калне</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kalnʲæ/</span> "little fish" : <i>калхне</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kal̥nʲæ/</span> (&lt; *kal-tʲ-nʲæ) "these fish"</li>
<li><i>марьне</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/marʲnʲæ/</span> "my apples" : <i>марьхне</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/mar̥ʲnʲæ/</span> ( &lt; *marʲ-tʲ-nʲæ) "these apples"</li>
<li><i>кундайне</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kunˈdajnʲæ/</span> "I caught it" : <i>кундайхне</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kunˈdaçnʲæ/</span> ( &lt; *kunˈdaj-tʲ-nʲæ) "these catchers"</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stress">Stress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moksha_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Stress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Non-high vowels are inherently longer than high vowels /i, u, ə/ and tend to draw the stress. If a high vowels appears in the first syllable which follow the syllable with a non-high vowels (especially /a/ and /æ/) then the stress moves to that second or third syllable. If all vowels of a word are either non-high or high then the stress falls on the first syllable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERaun1988100_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERaun1988100-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stressed vowels are longer than unstressed ones in the same position like in Russian. Unstressed vowels undergo some degree of <a href="/wiki/Vowel_reduction" title="Vowel reduction">vowel reduction</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_system">Writing system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moksha_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Writing system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Moksha has been written using <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic</a> with spelling rules identical to those of Russian since the 18th century and as a consequence of that vowels /e, ɛ, ə/ are not differentiated in a straightforward way,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERaun198897_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERaun198897-17">[17]</a></sup> however they can be predicted more or less from Moksha phonotactics. The 1993 spelling reform defines that /ə/ in the first (either stressed or unstressed) syllable must be written with the "hard" sign ⟨ъ⟩ (e. g. мъ́рдсемс <i>mə́rdśəms</i> "to return", formerly мрдсемс). The version of the Moksha Cyrillic alphabet used in 1924-1927 had several extra letters, either digraphs or single letters with diacritics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Although the use of the <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a> for Moksha was officially approved by the CIK VCKNA (General Executive Committee of the All Union New Alphabet Central Committee) on June 25, 1932, it was never implemented.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moksha_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before 1917 about 100 books and pamphlets mostly of religious character were published. More than 200 manuscripts including at least 50 wordlists were not printed. In the 19th century the Russian Orthodox Missionary Society in <a href="/wiki/Kazan" title="Kazan">Kazan</a> published Moksha primers and elementary textbooks of the Russian language for the Mokshas. Among them were two fascicles with samples of Moksha folk poetry. The great native scholar <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Makar_Evsevyev&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Makar Evsevyev (page does not exist)">Makar Evsevyev</a> collected Moksha folk songs published in one volume in 1897. Early in the Soviet period, social and political literature predominated among published works. Printing of Moksha language books was all done in Moscow until the establishment of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mordvinian_national_district&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mordvinian national district (page does not exist)">Mordvinian national district</a> in 1928. Official conferences in 1928 and 1935 decreed the northwest dialect to be the basis for the literary language.</p>
