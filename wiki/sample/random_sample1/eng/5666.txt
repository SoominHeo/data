<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Jupiter mass</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p><b>Jupiter mass</b> (<i>M</i><sub>J</sub> or <i>M</i><sub>Jup</sub>) is the unit of <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> equal to the total mass of the planet <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7027189800000000000♠</span>1.898<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>27</sup> <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kg</a></span>, 317.83 <a href="/wiki/Earth_mass" title="Earth mass">Earth mass</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jupfact_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-jupfact-1">[1]</a></sup> one Earth mass equals 0.00315 Jupiter masses). Jupiter mass is used to describe masses of the <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giants</a>, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outer_planet" title="Outer planet">outer planets</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">extrasolar planets</a>. It is also used in describing <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">brown dwarfs</a>.</p>
<p>The most massive exoplanets are typically described in terms of Jupiter masses as this provides a convenient scale for comparison. A Jupiter-mass planet at an orbital distance of 1 AU from a <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>-like <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> causes an <a href="/wiki/Doppler_spectroscopy" title="Doppler spectroscopy">amplitude shift</a> of 28 m/s, which is detectable with current technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The most readily detectable planets through radial velocity measurements have high mass and close orbits. This produces a <a href="/wiki/Selection_bias" title="Selection bias">selection effect</a> for planets of Jupiter mass. Likewise, Jupiter mass or higher planets are more likely to be detected through other means, such as transits or microlensing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jones04_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-jones04-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A planet with a Jupiter mass might not have the same dimensions. The theoretical minimum mass a star can have, and still undergo <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>-1 fusion at the core, is estimated to be about 75 times the mass of Jupiter, while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deuterium_burning" title="Deuterium burning">fusion of deuterium</a> can occur at masses as low as 13 Jupiters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-minimum_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-minimum-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, the masses of the outer planets can be listed in Jupiter mass. The other gas giants are far less massive than Jupiter.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> – 1.000</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a> – 0.299</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> – 0.046</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> – 0.054</li>
</ul>
<p>One Jupiter mass can be converted to related units:</p>
<ul>
<li>25,839 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_mass" title="Lunar mass">Lunar mass</a> (<i>M</i><sub>L</sub>)</li>
<li>317.83 <a href="/wiki/Earth_mass" title="Earth mass">Earth mass</a> (<i>M</i><sub>⊕</sub>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jupfact_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-jupfact-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>0.0009546 <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">Solar mass</a> (<i>M</i><sub>☉</sub>)</li>
</ul>
<p>In comparison, one Solar mass is equivalent to:</p>
<ul>
<li>27,068,500 Lunar mass (<i>M</i><sub>L</sub>)</li>
<li>332,946 Earth mass (<i>M</i><sub>⊕</sub>)</li>
<li>1,047.56 Jupiter mass (<i>M</i><sub>J</sub>)</li>
</ul>
<p>Jupiter comprises roughly three-quarters of the mass of the solar system excluding the Sun.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="The other gas giants alone have 0.4 Jupiter masses, by the values stated above (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
