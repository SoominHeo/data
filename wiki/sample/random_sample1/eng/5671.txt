<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mondo film</h1>

<p>A <b>mondo film</b> (from the Italian word for "world") is an <a href="/wiki/Exploitation_film" title="Exploitation film">exploitation</a> <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentary film</a>, sometimes resembling a <a href="/wiki/Pseudo-documentary" title="Pseudo-documentary">pseudo-documentary</a> and usually depicting <a href="/wiki/Sensationalism" title="Sensationalism">sensational</a> topics, scenes, or situations. Common traits of mondo films include an emphasis on taboo subjects (such as death and sex), portrayals of foreign cultures (which have drawn accusations of <a href="/wiki/Ethnocentrism" title="Ethnocentrism">ethnocentrism</a> or racism<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerekesslater108_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-kerekesslater108-1">[1]</a></sup>), and staged sequences presented as genuine documentary footage. Over time, the films placed increasing emphasis on footage of the dead and dying (both real and fake). The term <i><b>shockumentary</b></i> is also used to describe the genre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History"><span id="History_of_the_genre"></span>History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mondo_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although earlier films such as <i>European Nights</i> (1959) and <i>World By Night</i> 1 &amp; 2 (1961) may be considered examples of the genre,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerekesslater102_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-kerekesslater102-3">[3]</a></sup> the origins of the mondo documentary are generally traced to the <a href="/wiki/1962_in_film" title="1962 in film">1962</a> <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Mondo_Cane" title="Mondo Cane">Mondo Cane</a></i> (<i>A Dog's World</i>—a mild <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> <a href="/wiki/Profanity" title="Profanity">profanity</a>) by <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Cavara" title="Paolo Cavara">Paolo Cavara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gualtiero_Jacopetti" title="Gualtiero Jacopetti">Gualtiero Jacopetti</a> and Franco Prosperi which was a commercial success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Documentary films imitating <i>Mondo Cane</i> in the 1960s often included the term "mondo" in their titles, even if they were in English; examples include <i>Mondo Bizarro</i>, <i>Mondo Daytona</i>, <i>Mondo Mod</i>, <i>Mondo Infame</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mondo_Hollywood" title="Mondo Hollywood">Mondo Hollywood</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerekesslater107_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-kerekesslater107-7">[7]</a></sup> Films outside the genre followed suit: <i><a href="/wiki/Mondo_Trasho" title="Mondo Trasho">Mondo Trasho</a></i>, <i>Mondo Weirdo: A Trip to Paranoia Paradise</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mondo_Keyhole" title="Mondo Keyhole">Mondo Keyhole</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerekesslater109_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-kerekesslater109-8">[8]</a></sup> and <i>Mondo Brutale</i> (a German release of <a href="/wiki/Wes_Craven" title="Wes Craven">Wes Craven</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_House_on_the_Left_(1972_film)" title="The Last House on the Left (1972 film)">The Last House on the Left</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerekesslater215_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-kerekesslater215-9">[9]</a></sup>) title themselves <i>mondo</i>, although none are mondo documentaries. Later in the decade, this naming convention began to fall out of favour and fewer mondo films identified themselves as such in their titles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerekesslater107_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-kerekesslater107-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Filmmakers wanted to top each other in shock value to attract audiences. Cruelty to animals, accidents, tribal-initiation rites and surgeries are features of a typical mondo. Much of the action is staged, although the filmmakers may claim their goal is to document "reality". Subjects of mondo films include sex (<i>Mondo Sex</i> and <i>Mondo Sexualis USA</i>); celebrities (<i>Mondo Elvis</i> and <i>Mondo Lugosi</i>); youth culture (<i>Mondo Teeno</i>) and the gay subculture (<i>Mondo Rocco</i>).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Russ_Meyer" title="Russ Meyer">Russ Meyer</a>'s film <i><a href="/wiki/Mondo_Topless" title="Mondo Topless">Mondo Topless</a></i> was one of the few "documentaries" restricted to the old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midnight_movies" title="Midnight movies">midnight movie</a> circuit in the pre-VCR era; it explored <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strip_clubs" title="Strip clubs">strip clubs</a> in 1960s <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> at a time when strip clubs were a novelty in the United States, restricted to centers of port-city decadence (such as San Francisco). Other examples of this genre include <i><a href="/wiki/Mondo_New_York" title="Mondo New York">Mondo New York</a></i> by Harvey Keith, <i>Mondo di Notte</i> by Gianni Proia and <i>Mondo Balordo</i> by Roberto Bianchi Montero.</p>
<p>The 1980s saw a resurgence of mondo movies focusing almost exclusively on (onscreen) death, instead of world cultures. The <i><a href="/wiki/Faces_of_Death" title="Faces of Death">Faces of Death</a></i> series is a notable example of this type of mondo (or "death") movie. The producers used fake footage (passed off as real), but some of the footage was legitimate (including scenes of autopsies, suicides and accidents).</p>
<p>The rare 1985 film <i>Mondo Senza Veli</i> (<i>World without Veils</i> or <i>Mondo Fresh</i>) was purported by viewers to feature at its end the brutal execution of a young Arab rapist by public rectal impalement. This episode was, however, believed to have been a staged execution by some viewers.</p>
<p>Mondo films in the 21st century feature gore, exemplified by the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Faces_of_Gore&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Faces of Gore (page does not exist)">Faces of Gore</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Traces_of_Death" title="Traces of Death">Traces of Death</a></i> series. There is less fake footage, and many use news footage of accidents from <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A number of films have parodied the genre. Examples include Ricardo Fratelli's <i>Mondo Ford</i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Mike%27s_Mondo_Video" title="Mr. Mike's Mondo Video">Mr. Mike's Mondo Video</a></i> by <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a>'</i>s <a href="/wiki/Michael_O%27Donoghue" title="Michael O'Donoghue">Michael O'Donoghue</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Is_There_Sex_After_Death%3F" title="Is There Sex After Death?">Is There Sex After Death?</a></i> by Jeanne and Alan Abel. <i>Mondo Beyondo</i> spoofed the films' approach to titling, but was a parody of satellite television.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerekesslater155_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-kerekesslater155-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cannibal_film" title="Cannibal film">Italian cannibal film</a> is arguably an offshoot of the mondo film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerekesslater109_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-kerekesslater109-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Films"><span id="Mondo_movies"></span><span id="Jacopetti_.26_Prosperi"></span><span id="Max_Steele"></span><span id="Castiglioni_Brothers"></span><span id="The_Savage_Trilogy"></span><span id="John_Alan_Schwartz"></span><span id="Uwe_Schier"></span><span id=".27.27Faces_of_Death.27.27_imitators"></span><span id="Others"></span>Films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mondo_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The pair's <i><a href="/wiki/Mondo_candido" title="Mondo candido">Mondo candido</a></i> (1975) is not a "Mondo" film; the title was imposed on them by the studio, who wished to cash in on their earlier successes. The film is a retelling of <a href="/wiki/Voltaire" title="Voltaire">Voltaire</a>'s novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Candide" title="Candide">Candide</a></i>.</p>
<p>In the late 1980s Stelvio Massi (a.k.a. Max Steele) made two spinoffs of the original <i><a href="/wiki/Mondo_Cane" title="Mondo Cane">Mondo Cane</a></i> series, known as <i>Mondo Cane 3</i> and <i>Mondo Cane 4</i> on video.</p>

<p>In 1969, brothers Angelo and Alfredo Castiglioni began to make a series of their own mondo films until the early 1980s. They made five films in all, tying Jacopetti and Prosperi as the most prolific mondo film producers. Each film examines brutal and bizarre behavior on the African continent. Their films are considered some of the most graphic Mondo films ever made.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>Uwe Schier bought the rights to the <i>Mondo Cane</i> and <i>Faces of Death</i> films and released his own entries in both series, consisting largely of footage lifted from other mondo films. <i>Faces of Death 5</i> draws heavily on <i>Death Scenes</i>; <i>Faces of Death 6</i> consists almost entirely of <i>Days of Fury</i> and <i>Mondo Cane IV</i> (not to be confused with <i>Mondo Cane 2000, l'Incredibile</i>, Schier's <i>Mondo Cane IV</i> is in fact the fifth film in the series) lifts from other films (including <i>Death Scenes</i> and <i>Death Faces IV</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerekesslater156158_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-kerekesslater156158-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1993, Hurricane Pictures edited a mix of scenes featured in <i>Addio ultimo uomo</i> and <i>Shocking Africa</i>, labeling it the "fifth chapter" of the saga (<i>Teil V</i> in German).</p>
