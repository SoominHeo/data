<p>The <b>Battle of Monmouth</b> was an <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a> battle fought on June 28, 1778 in <a href="/wiki/Monmouth_County,_New_Jersey" title="Monmouth County, New Jersey">Monmouth County, New Jersey</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> under General <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> attacked the rear of the <a href="/wiki/British_Army_during_the_American_War_of_Independence" title="British Army during the American War of Independence">British Army</a> column commanded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_General" title="Lieutenant General">Lieutenant General</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Clinton_(1730%E2%80%931795)" title="Henry Clinton (1730–1795)">Sir Henry Clinton</a> as they left Monmouth Court House (modern <a href="/wiki/Freehold_Borough,_New_Jersey" title="Freehold Borough, New Jersey">Freehold Borough</a>). It is also known as the <b>Battle of Monmouth Courthouse</b>.</p>
<p>Unsteady handling of lead Continental elements by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_General" title="Major General">Major General</a> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lee_(general)" title="Charles Lee (general)">Charles Lee</a> had allowed British rearguard commander Lieutenant General <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cornwallis,_1st_Marquess_Cornwallis" title="Charles Cornwallis, 1st Marquess Cornwallis">Charles Cornwallis</a> to seize the initiative, but Washington's timely arrival on the battlefield rallied the Americans along a hilltop hedgerow. Sensing the opportunity to smash the Continentals, Cornwallis pressed his attack and captured the hedgerow in stifling heat. Washington consolidated his troops in a new line on heights behind marshy ground, used his artillery to fix the British in their positions, then brought up a four-gun battery under Major General <a href="/wiki/Nathanael_Greene" title="Nathanael Greene">Nathanael Greene</a> on nearby Combs Hill to enfilade the British line, requiring Cornwallis to withdraw. Finally, Washington tried to hit the exhausted British rear guard on both flanks, but darkness forced the end of the engagement. Both armies held the field, but the British commanding general Clinton withdrew undetected at midnight to resume his army's march to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Cornwallis protected the main British column from any further American attack, Washington had fought his opponent to a standstill after a pitched and prolonged engagement; the first time that Washington's army had achieved such a result. The battle demonstrated the growing effectiveness of the Continental Army after its six-month encampment at <a href="/wiki/Valley_Forge" title="Valley Forge">Valley Forge</a>, where constant drilling under officers such as Major General <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_von_Steuben" title="Friedrich Wilhelm von Steuben">Friedrich Wilhelm von Steuben</a> and Major General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gilbert_du_Motier,_marquis_de_Lafayette" title="Gilbert du Motier, marquis de Lafayette">Gilbert du Motier, Marquis de Lafayette</a> greatly improved army discipline and morale. The battle improved the military reputations of Washington, Lafayette and <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Wayne" title="Anthony Wayne">Anthony Wayne</a> but ended the career of Charles Lee, who would face <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Court_martial" title="Court martial">court martial</a> at <a href="/wiki/Englishtown,_New_Jersey" title="Englishtown, New Jersey">Englishtown</a> for his failures on the day. According to some accounts, an American soldier's wife, Mary Hays, brought water to thirsty soldiers in the June heat, and became one of several women associated with the legend of <a href="/wiki/Molly_Pitcher" title="Molly Pitcher">Molly Pitcher</a>. By the second phase of the battle the temperature remained almost consistently above 100 degrees <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">Fahrenheit</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Heat_stroke" title="Heat stroke">heat stroke</a> was said to have claimed more lives than musket fire throughout the battle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Monmouth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Campaign" title="Philadelphia Campaign">Philadelphia Campaign</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franco-American_Alliance" title="Franco-American Alliance">Franco-American Alliance</a></div>
<p>British forces had <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_campaign" title="Philadelphia campaign">captured Philadelphia</a> in 1777. In May 1778, the British commander-in-chief in North America, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Sir_Henry_Clinton" title="General Sir Henry Clinton">General Sir Henry Clinton</a>, was ordered to evacuate <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> and concentrate his troops at the main British base in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> as <a href="/wiki/France_in_the_American_Revolutionary_War" title="France in the American Revolutionary War">France</a> had entered the war on the side of the Americans. Clinton was ordered to dispatch units to <a href="/wiki/West_Florida" title="West Florida">West Florida</a> and the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a> which left him too few troops to continue occupying Philadelphia. Clinton was also ordered to abandon New York and withdraw to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Province_of_Quebec_(1763-1791)" title="Province of Quebec (1763-1791)">Quebec</a> if he felt his position there was untenable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A French fleet under <a href="/wiki/Charles_Hector,_comte_d%27Estaing" title="Charles Hector, comte d'Estaing">d'Estaing</a> had sailed from <a href="/wiki/Toulon" title="Toulon">Toulon</a> in April, 1778 and intended to make a rendezvous with rebel forces which could threaten Clinton's army before it reached the safety of New York.</p>
<p>It was originally intended that the withdrawing British army would travel directly to New York via the sea, escorted by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>. A lack of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transport_ships" title="Transport ships">transports</a> forced Clinton to change his plans. While the stores, heavy equipment and <a href="/wiki/Loyalist_(American_Revolution)" title="Loyalist (American Revolution)">Loyalist American</a> civilians fleeing revenge attacks would be shipped by sea, the main army would march overland across <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 18, the British began to evacuate Philadelphia, and began their approximately 100-mile (160 km) march to the northeast across New Jersey to New York City. The British force comprised 11,000 British and German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regular_Army" title="Regular Army">regulars</a>, a thousand <a href="/wiki/Loyalist_(American_Revolution)" title="Loyalist (American Revolution)">Loyalists</a> from Philadelphia, and a baggage train 12 miles (19 km) long. As the British advanced, the Americans slowed their advance by burning bridges, muddying wells and building <a href="/wiki/Abatis" title="Abatis">abatis</a> across the roads.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prelude">Prelude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Monmouth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prelude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With a high of over 100 °<a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">F</a> (37.8 °<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">C</a>), both sides lost almost as many men to <a href="/wiki/Heat_stroke" title="Heat stroke">heat stroke</a> as to the enemy. Major General Charles Lee, Washington's second-in-command, advised awaiting developments as he did not wish to commit the American force against the British regulars. However, Washington determined that the British column was vulnerable to attack as it traveled across New Jersey with its baggage train, and moved from <a href="/wiki/Valley_Forge" title="Valley Forge">Valley Forge</a> in pursuit.</p>
<p>Washington was still undecided how to attack the British column, and held a <a href="/wiki/Council_of_war" title="Council of war">council of war</a>. The council, however, was divided on the issue; with a small group of officers including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brigadier_General" title="Brigadier General">General</a> <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Wayne" title="Anthony Wayne">Anthony Wayne</a> urging a partial attack on the British column while it was strung out on the road. Lee was still cautious, advising only harassing attacks with light forces. On June 26, 1778, Washington chose to send 4,000 men as an advance force to strike at the British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rear_guard" title="Rear guard">rear guard</a> as they departed Monmouth Courthouse, in order to delay the British withdrawal until the main American force could give battle.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle">Battle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Monmouth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Battle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See <a href="/wiki/Monmouth_order_of_battle" title="Monmouth order of battle">Monmouth order of battle</a> for the organizations and lists of regiments from the British and American armies.</div>

<p>Lee, as Washington's senior subordinate, was initially appointed commander of the advance force, but turned it down because of his doubts about the plan. However, when the force was increased to 5,000 and the command offered to the Marquis de Lafayette, Lee changed his mind and insisted on the command.</p>
<p>Lee met with his subordinates but failed to give them proper orders, resulting in a piecemeal and disorganized attack on June 28 against the British rear guard under General <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cornwallis,_1st_Marquess_Cornwallis" title="Charles Cornwallis, 1st Marquess Cornwallis">Charles Cornwallis</a>. After several hours of fighting in the hot weather, several American brigades executed a tactical retreat, which developed into a general withdrawal. The British rear guard counterattacked and as his flanks rapidly became routed, Lee was forced to order a <a href="/wiki/Withdrawal_(military)" title="Withdrawal (military)">withdrawal</a>.</p>
<p>Washington, advancing with the main force along the Monmouth road, encountered Lee's fleeing troops and finally Lee himself, with the British in hot pursuit. After a heated exchange with Lee, Washington relieved him of command and sent him to the rear. He then rallied Lee's troops, who delayed the British pursuit until the main force could take up positions further to the west.</p>
<p>The remnants of Lee's forces then withdrew to the main American force, where the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> troops were positioned behind the West Ravine on the Monmouth Courthouse - Freehold Meeting House Road; Lee, in spite of the chastisement he had been dealt by Washington, remained at the bridge until he had ushered the last of the soldiers across the Ravine. Washington drew up his army with Greene's division on the right, Major General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Alexander_(American_general)" title="William Alexander (American general)">Stirling</a>'s division on the left, and most of Lee's former force, now under Lafayette, in reserve. In front of his lines, Wayne commanded various elements of Lee's force. Artillery was placed on both wings, with the right wing in position to enfilade the advancing British.</p>
<p>The British came on and attacked Stirling's left wing with their light infantry and the 42nd (<a href="/wiki/42nd_Regiment_of_Foot" title="42nd Regiment of Foot">Black Watch</a>) Regiment in the van. They were met by a storm of fire from Stirling's Continentals. The battle raged back and forth for an hour until three American regiments were sent though woods to enfilade the attacking British right flank. The attack was successful and sent the British back to reform.</p>

<p>Foiled on the left, Cornwallis personally led a heavy attack against Greene's right wing, with a force comprising British and Hessian grenadiers, light infantry, the <a href="/wiki/Coldstream_Guards" title="Coldstream Guards">Coldstream Guards</a> and another Guards battalion, and the 37th and 44th Regiments. The attack was met by enfilading fire from <a href="/wiki/Thomas-Antoine_de_Mauduit_du_Plessis" title="Thomas-Antoine de Mauduit du Plessis">Thomas-Antoine de Mauduit du Plessis</a>'s four 6-pound cannons on Combs Hill, as well as accurate volleys from Greene's Continental regiments. The British persisted up the ravine slope but within minutes five high-ranking officers and many men were down from heavy fire. The attackers recoiled down the slope.</p>
<p>During Cornwallis' abortive attack on Greene, another British force made up of grenadiers, light infantry and light dragoons hit Wayne's forward force, who were protected behind a long hedge. Three times the British were driven back by Wayne's grapeshot and bullets: but an overwhelming fourth attack overlapped Wayne's position and forced his units to fall back to the main American line.</p>
<p>The British made no further attempts on the main American line, although cannonading from both sides continued until 6 p.m. At this point, the British fell back to a strong position east of the Ravine. Washington wanted to take the offensive to the British and attack both flanks, but darkness brought an end to the battle.</p>
<p>The British rested and then resumed their march to the northeast during the night. Washington wanted to resume the battle the next day but in the morning found that the British had withdrawn during the night, continuing their march without incident to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandy_Hook,_New_Jersey" title="Sandy Hook, New Jersey">Sandy Hook</a> and arriving there on June 30. Clinton's rearguard successfully covered the withdrawal. The British force was then transported by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> across <a href="/wiki/Lower_New_York_Bay" title="Lower New York Bay">Lower New York Bay</a> to <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>.</p>
<p>The battle effectively ended in a draw, as the Americans held the field, but the British were able to get the army and supplies safely to New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The British official casualty return reported 65 killed, 59 dead of "fatigue", 170 wounded and 64 missing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin_p.232_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Martin_p.232-7">[7]</a></sup> The American official return stated 69 killed, 161 wounded and 132 missing (37 of whom were found to have died of heat-stroke).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin_p.233_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Martin_p.233-8">[8]</a></sup> Other estimates increase the losses to 1,134 British and 500 American casualties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin_p.233_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Martin_p.233-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Monmouth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Molly_Pitcher_engraving.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1050" data-file-width="1462" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Molly_Pitcher_engraving.jpg/260px-Molly_Pitcher_engraving.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Molly_Pitcher_engraving.jpg/390px-Molly_Pitcher_engraving.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Molly_Pitcher_engraving.jpg/520px-Molly_Pitcher_engraving.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a>

<p>After the battle the British continued their march eastwards until they reached <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Hook" title="Sandy Hook">Sandy Hook</a>. From there they were taken by boat to New York City where they began preparing the city's defenses in expectation of an attack. D'Estaing's fleet arrived just too late, narrowly missing a chance to trap Clinton's army at Sandy Hook.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Plans to attack New York were abandoned, and it remained the principal base for British forces until 1783. Instead D'Estaing sailed north to participate in a <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Rhode_Island" title="Battle of Rhode Island">Franco-American assault</a> on the British garrison at <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport, Rhode Island</a>, which ended in failure. Monmouth was the last major battle in the northern theater, and the largest one-day battle of the war when measured in terms of participants. Lee was later <a href="/wiki/Court-martial" title="Court-martial">court-martialed</a> at the Village Inn located in the center of <a href="/wiki/Englishtown,_New_Jersey" title="Englishtown, New Jersey">Englishtown</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> where he was found guilty and relieved of command for one year. The verdict was approved by the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Congress" title="Continental Congress">Continental Congress</a> by a close vote. Many months later, Lee wrote a strongly worded letter to Congress in protest but Congress closed the affair by informing him that it had no more need of his services. Lee never held another military command and died of <a href="/wiki/Yellow_fever" title="Yellow fever">yellow fever</a> in 1782, a year after the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Yorktown" title="Siege of Yorktown">Battle of Yorktown</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Monmouth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

A 90-foot (27 m) tall monument to the battle in front of the Monmouth County Courthouse in <a href="/wiki/Freehold_Borough,_New_Jersey" title="Freehold Borough, New Jersey">Freehold</a></div>

<p>The legend of "<a href="/wiki/Molly_Pitcher" title="Molly Pitcher">Molly Pitcher</a>" is usually associated with this battle. It is believed that she was Mary Ludwig Hays. According to one story, she was the wife of an American <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artilleryman</a> who lived near the battlefield, bringing water for swabbing the cannons and for the thirsty crews, and took her husband's place after he fell, and helped him with his wounds. The artillery men gave her the nickname "Molly Pitcher" when she was bringing them water from a nearby spring. The story is based on a true incident but has become embellished over the years. Two places on the battlefield are marked as sites of the "Molly Pitcher Spring".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPS_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPS-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nine Army National Guard units (101st Eng Bn,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> 101st FA,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> 113th Inf<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> 116 Inf,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> 125th QM Co,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> 175th Inf,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> 181st Inf,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> 198th Sig Bn<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and 211 MP Bn<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>) and one active Regular Army Field Artillery battalion (1-5th FA<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> ) are derived from American units that participated in the Battle of Monmouth. <a href="/wiki/Army_National_Guard_and_Active_Regular_Army_Units_with_Colonial_Roots" title="Army National Guard and Active Regular Army Units with Colonial Roots">There are only thirty currently active units of the U.S. Army with lineages that go back to the colonial era</a>.</p>
<p>Although never accorded formal preservation, the Monmouth Battlefield is one of the best preserved of the Revolutionary War battlefields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPS_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-NPS-11">[11]</a></sup> Each year, during the last weekend in June, the Battle of Monmouth is reenacted at <a href="/wiki/Monmouth_Battlefield_State_Park" title="Monmouth Battlefield State Park">Monmouth Battlefield State Park</a> in modern <a href="/wiki/Freehold_Township,_New_Jersey" title="Freehold Township, New Jersey">Freehold Township</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manalapan,_New_Jersey" title="Manalapan, New Jersey">Manalapan</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Monmouth_County_Historical_Association" title="Monmouth County Historical Association">Monmouth County Historical Association</a> at 70 Court Street in <a href="/wiki/Freehold_Borough,_New_Jersey" title="Freehold Borough, New Jersey">Freehold Borough, New Jersey</a> houses a collection of documents which includes personal accounts, journals, pension applications, letters, and miscellaneous printed material.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hamilton_(musical)" title="Hamilton (musical)"><i>Hamilton the Musical</i></a> makes note of this in its song "Stay Alive", in which Lee is portrayed as a childish, bumbling idiot who orders his men to retreat rather than attack. At the end of the song, <a href="/wiki/John_Laurens" title="John Laurens">John Laurens</a> challenges him to a duel.</p>
