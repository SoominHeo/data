<p>A <b>monster truck</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Pickup_truck" title="Pickup truck">pickup truck</a> modified with a larger <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(vehicle)" title="Suspension (vehicle)">suspension</a> and larger tires. Today pickup trucks are still used, however SUV bodies as well as themed trucks are driven and most of the bodies are made of fiberglass now rather than metal. Themed trucks vary from car bodies, SUV bodies, and even animal themed bodies. A competition monster truck should meet the guidelines of being 12 feet tall, 12 feet wide and be equipped with 66-inch off-road tires and from 2015 to present the are out fitted with BKT TIRES which were made specifically for the trucks, the weight of each tire is 462LB. Monster trucks used to be side acts at popular motocross events and mud bogs but today they are usually the main attraction with motocross and mud bogging being the complimentary shows.</p>
<p>A monster truck show usually involves the trucks racing each other in a 2v2 competition and then letting it all hang out in a 'freestyle comp where the trucks do numerous stunts such as a " backflip, "wheelie" etc. in numerous competitions. These events usually consist of racing, freestyle, donut competitions and wheelie competitions. These trucks can run up and over most man-made barriers, so they are equipped with remote shut-off switches, called the Remote Ignition Interrupter (RII), to help prevent an accident if the driver loses control at any time. The RII works with the trucks computer to allow track officials to shut the truck's engine off in the event the brakes fail or the truck becomes uncontrollable. During freestyle events, only one truck is allowed on the course at a time to ensure safety for both the drivers and fans. Most racing competitions feature two drivers racing each other on symmetrical tracks, with the losing driver being eliminated in a single loss elimination fashion.</p>
<p>In recent years, the freestyle event is the last event of competition. Drivers are free to select their own course around the track and its obstacles. Drivers will often perform doughnuts, wheel stands, large aerial jumps and even backflips during this segment. Historically, additional items for the drivers to crush such as motor homes and school buses were frequently placed on the track specifically for the freestyle event. However, incidences of debris flying into the stands and causing serious injuries have influenced most event promoters to turn away from such frail obstacles. Most freestyle courses now consist of overly large jumps and ramps formed to allow the trucks to get excessive air and explosive wheelies upon landing.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monster_truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USAF_Afterburner_Monster_Jam.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/USAF_Afterburner_Monster_Jam.jpg/220px-USAF_Afterburner_Monster_Jam.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/USAF_Afterburner_Monster_Jam.jpg/330px-USAF_Afterburner_Monster_Jam.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/USAF_Afterburner_Monster_Jam.jpg/440px-USAF_Afterburner_Monster_Jam.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In the 1970s, heavily modified pickup trucks were becoming popular and the sports of mud bogging and truck pulling were gaining in popularity. Several truck owners had created <a href="/wiki/Suspension_lift" title="Suspension lift">lifted</a> trucks to compete in such events, and soon competition to hold the title of "biggest truck" developed. The trucks which garnered the most national attention were Bob Chandler's <a href="/wiki/Bigfoot_(truck)" title="Bigfoot (truck)">Bigfoot</a>, Everett Jasmer's <a href="/wiki/USA-1_(monster_truck)" title="USA-1 (monster truck)">USA-1</a>, Fred Shafer and Jack Willman Sr.'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bear_Foot_(truck)" title="Bear Foot (truck)">Bear Foot</a>, and Jeff Dane's <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=King_Kong_(truck)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="King Kong (truck) (page does not exist)">King Kong</a>. At the time, the largest tires the trucks were running were 48 inches (1.2 m) in diameter.</p>
<p>In April 1981, Bob Chandler drove over cars in Bigfoot in what is often believed to be the first monster truck to crush cars. Chandler drove Bigfoot over a pair of cars in a field as a test of the truck's ability, and filmed it to use as a promotional tool in his four-wheel drive performance shop. An event promoter saw the video of the car crush and asked Chandler to do it in front of a crowd. Initially hesitant because of the "destructive" image that could be associated with Bigfoot, Chandler eventually caved in. After some smaller shows, Chandler performed the feat in the <a href="/wiki/Pontiac_Silverdome" title="Pontiac Silverdome">Pontiac Silverdome</a> in 1982. At this show, Chandler also debuted a new version of Bigfoot with 66in. tires. At a prior event in the early 1980s when Bigfoot was still running 48 in.terra tires, Bob George, one of the owners of a motorsport promotion company named Truck-a-rama (now the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USHRA" title="USHRA">USHRA</a>), is said to have coined the phrase "monster truck" when referring to Bigfoot. The term "monster truck" became the generic name for all trucks with oversized terra tires.</p>
<p>Exactly who crushed the first car is often debated. There are claims that in the late 1970s, Jeff Dane's King Kong (who referred to his truck as the "Bigger Foot") had crushed cars at Great Lakes Dragway in Union Grove, Wisconsin. Another truck, known as High Roller (currently known as "Thunder Beast"), also claimed to have documented car crushes in Washington State before Bigfoot, though said documentation has never surfaced. Cyclops, then owned by the Dykman Brothers, also claims to have crushed burning cars before Bigfoot. However, the <a class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=InrdfXhMHnQ" rel="nofollow">earliest, widely available and verified video footage</a> showing a monster truck crushing cars that exists shows Bob Chandler driving Bigfoot while crushing two mid-1970s automobiles in April 1981. This video was what the promoter viewed that motivated him to ask Chandler to perform the car crush in front of a crowd.</p>
<p>King Kong and Bear Foot each followed Bigfoot to 66-inch-diameter (1.7 m) tires, and soon other monster trucks, such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=King_Krunch_(truck)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="King Krunch (truck) (page does not exist)">King Krunch</a>, Maddog, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Virginia_Giant_(truck)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Virginia Giant (truck) (page does not exist)">Virginia Giant</a> were being constructed. These early trucks were built off of stock chassis which were heavily reinforced, used <a href="/wiki/Leaf_spring" title="Leaf spring">leaf spring</a> suspension, a stock body, and heavy military axles to support the tires.</p>
<p>For most of the early 1980s, monster trucks performed primarily exhibitions as a side show to truck pulling or mud bogging events. In 1985, major promoters, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USHRA" title="USHRA">USHRA</a> and <a href="/wiki/TNT_Motorsports" title="TNT Motorsports">TNT Motorsports</a>, began racing monster trucks on a regular basis. The races, as they are today, were in the form of single elimination <a href="/wiki/Drag_racing" title="Drag racing">drag races</a>, held over a course littered with obstacles. The change to racing eventually led truck owners to begin building lighter trucks, with more power. The establishment of TNT's first-ever monster truck points championship in 1988 expedited the process and found teams beginning to use straight-rail frames, <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a> bodies, and lighter axle components to shave weight and gain speed.</p>
<p>In 1988, to standardize rules for truck construction and safety, Bob Chandler, Braden, and George Carpenter formed the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Monster_Truck_Racing_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Monster Truck Racing Association (page does not exist)">Monster Truck Racing Association</a> (MTRA). The MTRA created standard safety rules to govern monster trucks. The organization still plays a major role in the sport's development in the USA and EU.</p>
<p>With racing taking precedence, several teams began to think in new ways as to how the trucks could be built. Towards the end of 1988, Gary Cook and David Morris debuted <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Equalizer_(truck)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Equalizer (truck) (page does not exist)">Equalizer</a>, a truck with a combination of <a href="/wiki/Coil_spring" title="Coil spring">coil springs</a> and shock absorbers as the main source of suspension rather than the standard of <a href="/wiki/Leaf_spring" title="Leaf spring">leaf springs</a> and shock absorbers. In 1989, Jack Willman Sr., now with his own truck, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Taurus_(truck)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Taurus (truck) (page does not exist)">Taurus</a>, debuted a new truck which used a four-link suspension system and large <a href="/wiki/Coilover" title="Coilover">coilover</a> shock absorbers, and that weighed in at close to 9,000 pounds (4,100 kg). However, the ultimate coup de grâce came from Chandler, also in 1989, whose Bigfoot VIII featured a full tubular chassis and a long-travel suspension using <a href="/wiki/Cantilever" title="Cantilever">cantilevers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shock_absorbers" title="Shock absorbers">shock absorbers</a> to control the suspension. The truck revolutionized how monster trucks were built, and within a few years most top level teams built similar vehicles.</p>
<p>In 1991, TNT was purchased by USHRA and their points series were merged. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Special_Events&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Special Events (page does not exist)">Special Events</a> championship began to grow in popularity with teams as it had open qualifying spots which the invite-only USHRA championship did not have. The Special Events series lost its Pendaliner sponsorship in 1997. The short-lived <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=ProMT&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="ProMT (page does not exist)">ProMT</a> series started in 2000.</p>
<p>Even though racing was dominant as a competition, USHRA events began having freestyle exhibitions as early as 1993. These exhibitions were developed as drivers, notably <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Anderson" title="Dennis Anderson">Dennis Anderson</a> of the extremely popular <a href="/wiki/Grave_Digger_(truck)" title="Grave Digger (truck)">Grave Digger</a>, began asking for time to come out and perform if they lost in early rounds of racing. Promoters began to notice the popularity of freestyle among fans, and in 2000 USHRA began holding freestyle as a judged competition at events, and now even awards a freestyle championship.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Truck_styling_and_creating">Truck styling and creating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monster_truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Truck styling and creating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jurrasic_attack.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="3072" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Jurrasic_attack.JPG/220px-Jurrasic_attack.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Jurrasic_attack.JPG/330px-Jurrasic_attack.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Jurrasic_attack.JPG/440px-Jurrasic_attack.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jurrasic_attack.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Jurassic Attack, based on a <i><a href="/wiki/Triceratops" title="Triceratops">Triceratops</a></i></div>

<p>The first monster <a href="/wiki/Bigfoot_(truck)" title="Bigfoot (truck)">truck</a> ever built was a pickup truck that was modified with a larger suspension and larger tires. Today, trucks now have custom built tubular chassis, with four-link suspension to provide up to 4-feet of clearance and they also now have fiberglass bodies that go onto the chassis separately and are designed to be easily removed and easily replaced when damaged. Mounted just behind the driver on most trucks are the engines, which are typically supercharged and run on methanol, an alcohol and corn-based oil, and have <a href="/wiki/Engine_displacement" title="Engine displacement">displacement</a> up to 575 cubic inches (9.42 L). Axles are mostly taken from either heavy-duty military trucks or road vehicles like school buses, and are modified to have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_gearing" title="Planetary gearing">planetary</a> gear reduction at the hub to help turn the tires. All trucks have hydraulic steering in both the front and the rear (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Four_wheel_steering" title="Four wheel steering">four wheel steering</a>), with the front wheels controlled by the <a href="/wiki/Steering_wheel" title="Steering wheel">steering wheel</a> and the rear wheels by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toggle_switch" title="Toggle switch">toggle switch</a>. The tires are typically "Terra" tires used on fertilizer spreaders, and have measurements of 66″×43″×25″ (1.7×1.1×0.6 m). Most trucks utilize a modified and/or custom designed automatic transmission, such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turbo-hydramatic" title="Turbo-hydramatic">Turbo 400</a>, <a href="/wiki/Powerglide" title="Powerglide">Powerglide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ford_C6_transmission" title="Ford C6 transmission">Ford C6 transmission</a>, or a Torque-flite 727. A limited number of trucks utilize a Lenco transmission, which traces its roots to <a href="/wiki/Drag_racing" title="Drag racing">drag racing</a>. Most of the automatic transmissions are heavily modified with <a href="/wiki/Transbrake" title="Transbrake">transbrakes</a>, manual valve bodies, and heavy duty gear sets. Trucks running a Lenco use a <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_clutch" title="Centrifugal clutch">centrifugal clutch</a> as opposed to a <a href="/wiki/Torque_converter" title="Torque converter">torque converter</a>, which are used in automatic transmissions. Lenco transmissions are usually found in two-speed or three speed configurations, and are commonly shifted using compressed CO<sub>2</sub>.</p>

<p>The trucks have many safety features, several of which are required just to run in the small <a href="/wiki/Arena" title="Arena">arenas</a> that the trucks frequent. Trucks are equipped with three shut-off switches: the RII (Remote Ignition Interrupt), one within the driver's reach in the cab, and another at the rear of the truck so that all electrical power may be shut off in the event of a rollover. Many trucks are constructed with the driver sitting in the center of the cab for visibility. Most cabs are shielded with Lexan (or comparable polycarbonate), which not only protects the driver from track debris, but also allows for increased visibility. Drivers are required to wear <a href="/wiki/Nomex" title="Nomex">firesuits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Safety_harness" title="Safety harness">safety harnesses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helmet" title="Helmet">helmets</a>, and <a href="/wiki/HANS_device" title="HANS device">head and neck restraints</a>. Most moving parts on the truck are also shielded, and high pressure components have restraining straps, both in case of an explosion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents">Accidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monster_truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Accidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are several reports on accidents with a monster truck, resulting in more than twenty deaths and several hundreds of wounded people. On January 16, 2009, at a <a href="/wiki/Monster_Jam" title="Monster Jam">Monster Jam</a> event in <a href="/wiki/Tacoma,_Washington" title="Tacoma, Washington">Tacoma, Washington</a> a 6-year-old spectator was killed when struck by one of the driveshaft loops, a safety feature to retain the driveshaft, that was crushed on an earlier jump and thrown into the stands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> On October 5, 2013 in the Mexican city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chihuahua,_Chihuahua" title="Chihuahua, Chihuahua">Chihuahua</a>, a truck drove into the public, which resulted in at least six deaths and about forty people wounded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> On September 28, 2014, a monster truck caused an <a href="/wiki/Haaksbergen_monster_truck_accident" title="Haaksbergen monster truck accident">accident in the Dutch Haaksbergen</a>, resulting in three deaths and more than twenty wounded people. In 2009 at the Monster Jam World Finals, the truck Captain's Curse lost its brakes during the run, causing the truck to flip violently when the driver attempted to steer it. The truck eventually landed several rows in the stands of <a href="/wiki/Sam_Boyd_Stadium" title="Sam Boyd Stadium">Sam Boyd Stadium</a>, although no one was injured. In 2009, the monster truck named Samson was involved in an accident that caused the death of announcer George Eisenhart after he accidentally stepped in front of Samson while it was moving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equipment">Equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monster_truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The vehicle's gears contain the same equipment as the others, although they are characterized more by the larger size of its tires, whose diameter can in some cases exceed four meters. The vehicle's length can exceed seven meters.</p>
