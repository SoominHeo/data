<p>The <b>Principality of Montenegro</b> (<a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian</a>: <span lang="sr" xml:lang="sr">Књажевина Црнa Горa</span>/Knjaževina Crna Gora) was a <a href="/wiki/List_of_former_sovereign_states" title="List of former sovereign states">former realm</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southeastern_Europe" title="Southeastern Europe">Southeastern Europe</a> that existed from 13 March 1852 to 28 August 1910. It was then proclaimed a <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Montenegro" title="Kingdom of Montenegro">kingdom</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nikola_I_of_Montenegro" title="Nikola I of Montenegro">Nikola I</a>, who then became king.</p>
<p>The capital was <a href="/wiki/Cetinje" title="Cetinje">Cetinje</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Montenegrin_perper" title="Montenegrin perper">Montenegrin perper</a> was used as state currency from 1906. The territory corresponded to the central area of modern <a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">Montenegro</a>. It was a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a>, but <i>de facto</i> <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolutist</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Montenegro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Danilo_I,_Prince_of_Montenegro" title="Danilo I, Prince of Montenegro">Danilo I</a>'s Code, dated to 1855, he explicitly states that he is the "knjaz (duke, prince) and gospodar (lord) of the Free Black Mountain (<a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishopric_of_Montenegro" title="Prince-Bishopric of Montenegro">Montenegro</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Brda,_Montenegro" title="Brda, Montenegro">the Hills</a>" (<i>Crna Gora</i> and <i>Brda</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1870, Nikola had the title of "knjaz of Crna Gora and Brda" (књаз Црне Горе и Брда), while two years later, the state was called "<i>knjaževina</i> of <i>Crna Gora</i>" (Књажевина Црна Гора).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Popov_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Popov-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Montenegro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reign_of_Danilo">Reign of Danilo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Montenegro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reign of Danilo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Danilo_Petrovi%C4%87_Njego%C5%A1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="668" data-file-width="528" height="127" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Danilo_Petrovi%C4%87_Njego%C5%A1.jpg/100px-Danilo_Petrovi%C4%87_Njego%C5%A1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Danilo_Petrovi%C4%87_Njego%C5%A1.jpg/150px-Danilo_Petrovi%C4%87_Njego%C5%A1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Danilo_Petrovi%C4%87_Njego%C5%A1.jpg/200px-Danilo_Petrovi%C4%87_Njego%C5%A1.jpg 2x" width="100"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Danilo_Petrovi%C4%87_Njego%C5%A1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The Principality was formed on 13 March 1852 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knjaz_Danilo" title="Knjaz Danilo">Danilo I Petrović-Njegoš</a>, when the latter, formerly known as Vladika Danilo II, decided to renounce to his ecclesiastical position as <a href="/wiki/Prince-bishop" title="Prince-bishop">prince-bishop</a> and married. With the first Montenegrin constitution being proclaimed in 1855, known as "Danilo's Code". After centuries of theocratic rule, this turned Montenegro into a secular principality.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mirko_Petrovi%C4%87_Njego%C5%A1" title="Mirko Petrović Njegoš">Grand Duke Mirko Petrović</a>, elder brother of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knjaz_Danilo" title="Knjaz Danilo">Danilo I</a>, led a strong army of 7,500 and won a crucial battle against the Turks (army of between 7,000 and 13,000) at <a href="/wiki/Grahovac" title="Grahovac">Grahovac</a> on 1 May 1858. The <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Turkish</a> forces were routed. This victory forced the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Powers" title="Great Powers">Great Powers</a> to officially demarcate the borders between Montenegro and <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Turkey</a>, de facto recognizing Montenegro's centuries-long independence. Montenegro gained <a href="/wiki/Grahovac" title="Grahovac">Grahovo</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rudine,_Nik%C5%A1i%C4%87&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rudine, Nikšić (page does not exist)">Rudine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nik%C5%A1i%C4%87_(tribe)" title="Nikšić (tribe)">Nikšić</a>, more than half of <a href="/wiki/Drobnjaci" title="Drobnjaci">Drobnjaci</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tu%C5%A1ina" title="Tušina">Tušina</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Uskoci,_Montenegro&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Uskoci, Montenegro (page does not exist)">Uskoci</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipovo,_Montenegro" title="Lipovo, Montenegro">Lipovo</a>, Upper <a href="/wiki/Vasojevi%C4%87i" title="Vasojevići">Vasojevići</a>, and part of <a href="/wiki/Ku%C4%8Di" title="Kuči">Kuči</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dodo%C5%A1i&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dodoši (page does not exist)">Dodoši</a>. The glory of the Montenegrins was soon immortalized in songs and literature of all South Slavs<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reign_of_Nikola">Reign of Nikola</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Montenegro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reign of Nikola">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the assassination of Knjaz Danilo on 13 August 1860, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nikola_I_of_Montenegro" title="Nikola I of Montenegro">Knjaz Nikola I</a>, the nephew of Knjaz Danilo, became the next ruler of Montenegro. Nikola sent aid to the Serb rebels in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herzegovina_Uprising_(1875%E2%80%9378)" title="Herzegovina Uprising (1875–78)">Herzegovina Uprising (1875–78)</a>, and then led a war against the Ottomans, the <a href="/wiki/Montenegrin%E2%80%93Ottoman_War_(1876%E2%80%9378)" title="Montenegrin–Ottoman War (1876–78)">Montenegrin–Ottoman War (1876–78)</a>. The advancement of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> forces toward Turkey forced Turkey to sign a <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_San_Stefano" title="Treaty of San Stefano">peace treaty</a> on 3 March 1878, recognising the independence of <a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">Montenegro</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, and also increased Montenegro's territory from 4,405 km² to 9,475 km². Montenegro also gained the towns of <a href="/wiki/Nik%C5%A1i%C4%87" title="Nikšić">Nikšić</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kola%C5%A1in" title="Kolašin">Kolašin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spu%C5%BE" title="Spuž">Spuž</a>, <a href="/wiki/Podgorica" title="Podgorica">Podgorica</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C5%BDabljak" title="Žabljak">Žabljak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bar,_Montenegro" title="Bar, Montenegro">Bar</a>, as well as access to the sea. This was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Powers" title="Great Powers">Great Powers</a>' official demarcation between Montenegro and the Ottoman Empire, <i>de facto</i> recognizing Montenegro's independence; Montenegro was recognized by the Ottoman Empire at the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Berlin_(1878)" title="Treaty of Berlin (1878)">Treaty of Berlin</a> (1878). Under the rule of Nikola I, diplomatic relations were established with the Ottoman Empire. Minor border skirmishes excepted, diplomacy ushered in approximately 30 years of peace between the two states until the deposition of <a href="/wiki/Abdul_Hamid_II" title="Abdul Hamid II">Abdul Hamid II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttk.gov.tr_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ttk.gov.tr-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The political skills of Abdul Hamid and Nikola I played a major role on the mutually amicable relations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttk.gov.tr_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ttk.gov.tr-3">[3]</a></sup> Modernization of the state followed, culminating with the draft of a Constitution in 1905. However, political rifts emerged between the parliamentary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Party_(1906)" title="People's Party (1906)">People's Party</a> that supported the process of democratization and union with Serbia and those of the <a href="/wiki/True_People%27s_Party" title="True People's Party">True People's Party</a> who were monarchist.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flags">Flags</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Montenegro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Flags">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The historical war flags were the <i>krstaš-barjak</i>, plain flags with crosses in the centre. The Montenegrin war flag used in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Vu%C4%8Dji_Do" title="Battle of Vučji Do">Battle of Vučji Do</a> (1876) was red with a white <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cross_patty" title="Cross patty">cross patty</a> in the centre and a white border, and this flag was adopted from the Serbian war flag in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kosovo" title="Battle of Kosovo">Battle of Kosovo</a> (1389) which found itself in Montenegro after surviving knights brought it there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The same flag was used in Cetinje in 1878,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> upon recognition of independence by the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_San_Stefano" title="Treaty of San Stefano">at San Stefano</a>. According to the 1905 constitution, the national flag was a <a href="/wiki/Tricolour_(flag)" title="Tricolour (flag)">tricolour</a> of red-blue-white ("црвена, плаветна и бијела"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> which was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbian_tricolour" title="Serbian tricolour">Serbian tricolour</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Constitution_of_1855">Constitution of 1855</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Montenegro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Constitution of 1855">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Danilo I used the Law of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petar_I_Petrovi%C4%87-Njego%C5%A1" title="Petar I Petrović-Njegoš">Petar I Petrović-Njegoš</a>, as an inspiration for his own "General Law of the Land" from 1855, also called "Danilo I's Code" (<i>zakonik Danila prvog</i>). Danilo's Code was based on Montenegrin traditions and customs and it is considered to be the first national constitution in <a href="/wiki/History_of_Montenegro" title="History of Montenegro">Montenegrin history</a>. It also stated rules, protected privacy and banned warring on the Austrian Coast (<a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Kotor" title="Bay of Kotor">Bay of Kotor</a>). It also stated: <i>Although there is no other nationality in this land except Serb nationality and no other religion except Eastern Orthodoxy, each foreigner and each person of different faith can live here and enjoy the same freedom and the same domestic right as Montenegrin or Highlander.</i></p>

<p>At the beginning of the 20th century political differences were starting to culminate. The country was now enlarged territorially and saw almost four decades of peace, very unusual for the country which was in war practically the whole time since it fell to Ottoman hands. The ruler, Prince Nikola I, was the longest reigning of all the Balkan dynasties, and by many perceived as the most experienced diplomat and politician. On the other hand, there was a growing population of dissatisfied young people, educated mainly abroad, who saw his rule as absolutistic and autocratic. Gathered in <a href="/wiki/Belgrade" title="Belgrade">Belgrade</a>, where they had support from certain political parties, they were demanding the reorganisation of government administration, constitutionalisation and the introduction of parliament. The opposition grew as their demands were supported by certain number of old military leaders and various clans' representatives. These primitive forms of nobility were mainly old and conservative, but due to their own personal antagonisms towards the prince or because of their own political ambitions, they sided with the crowd which demanded the modernistaion of the country. For a long time prince and the circle of people around him defended his standpoint with the explanation that the times were not right and that Montenegrin society still hadn't evolved enough to understand the significance of constitutional monarchy. Moreover, they argued that the introduction of parliamentarism and of the political parties would again stir up the old feuds between clans and destabilise hard won unity. Neighboring <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Serbia" title="Kingdom of Serbia">Serbia</a> by this time had already changed five constitutions and saw fifty years of political struggle between various different political parties and factions resulting in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/May_Overthrow" title="May Overthrow">coup and the assassination of the royal couple</a> in 1903. Finally, <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Imperial Russia</a>, the great protector of Montenegrin sovereignty in international politics and a model whose internal organisation was crudely copied by Montenegro, was still without a constitution. However, after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revolution_of_1905" title="Revolution of 1905">revolution of 1905</a> even Russia had to go through certain changes, thus leaving Montenegro to be the only country in Europe without a constitution alongside the Ottoman Empire, whose first constitution was short lived. Finally, after a huge media campaign against him and wide public pressure, both domestic and international, the prince at last decided to step back, so on 31 October 1905 he issued a public proclamation saying that he would grant the constitution. The proclamation also stated that he was granting the constitution of his free will and that the changes it would bring would not be radical at first because he felt that the existing institutions should be preserved from sudden changes. This caused a reply from students in Belgrade in a form of text entitled "The Word of Montenegrin University Youth", in which they criticised his intentions, saying that the new constitution would be merely formal and labeling the prince as the brake which was holding back Montenegro from modernisation and prosperity. Naturally, he did not want to give any power away, or to tie his own hands in any means, so he trusted his friend Stevan Djurčić, a conservative journalist from Serbia, with the task of writing the constitution. The outcome was the constitution that was basically a copy of the Serbian constitution from 1869 (aka The Regency Constitution) with slight changes made by the prince himself. The changes were minor but necessary adaptations for domestic circumstances. The Constitution of the Principality of Montenegro was imposed by Nikola I on 19 December (December 6 on the Julian calendar) 1905 in his speech from the throne in parliament. The parliament did not get to discuss the new constitution, and was dissolved right after it formally accepted it, so despite being brought in by parliament it was de facto imposed. It became remembered as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nikoljdan" title="Nikoljdan">St Nicholas day</a> constitution. It had 15 chapters with 222 articles in total. The 1905 Constitution provided state organization, type of government, the state symbols (partially), competence of the state administration, election of the statesman, the military service, the finances, and human and citizens' rights.</p>
<p>Montenegro is now a constitutional hereditary monarchy. Legislative power is vested in parliament as well as in the prince. The prince is the supreme commander of the armed forces, representing the state in foreign affairs. He declares war and signs the peace and alliances and also informs parliament on the matter; he has the right to appoint government officials; he is the protector of all the recognised religions in the country and he has the right of abolition and amnesty. He calls up meetings of parliament in regular and irregular sessions, opens up and closes the sessions personally, by speech from the throne or by ministry council with his decree. He has the right to dissolve parliament as well as to postpone parliamentary sessions. The dissolution decree must be countersigned by all ministers. Every adult male citizen has the right to be elected as MP, who hasn't been convicted and was not under any form of investigation, no matter the amount of taxes he pays. Active officers, <a href="/wiki/Non-commissioned_officer" title="Non-commissioned officer">NCO's</a> and soldiers in the army did not have the right to vote. Passive suffrage is available to every citizen older than 30, who permanently resides in Montenegro, enjoys full civil rights and pays at least 15 krones of taxes. Administrative officials cannot be elected to parliament. The elections were direct, and despite the fact that the method of voting was not regulated by constitution, it was usually done publicly. The deputies were elected for a four-year term. Aside from the elected MP's, there were 14 parliament seats for the so-called virile deputies (by the position they take in the government or society). These included the Metropolitan of Montenegro, the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Bar" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Bar">Archbishop of Bar and Primate of Serbia</a>, the Montenegrin <a href="/wiki/Mufti" title="Mufti">Mufti</a>, the president and the members of the State Council, the president of the High Court, the president of the Main Control and three brigadiers named by the prince himself.</p>
<p>No law can be passed, repealed, changed or reviewed without the acceptance of parliament. However, the initiative for the law to be passed or for the existing one to be changed can come from government to parliament or vice versa, but formal legislative projects can be made by government only. The role and the position of parliament was quite damaged by this fact. Parliament had the right to pass the budget, but for doing so it could not ask for the conditions that were not related to it. In other words, rejection of the budget cannot be used to dissolve a government, so, if parliament was to repeal the budget, the prince could extend the validness of the last year's budget to the following year. This particular example shows that the constitution did not complete the task of limiting the ruler's power. Finally, no new tax can be imposed without the agreeance of parliament.</p>
<p>The prince is the one who appoints and dismisses the ministers. The Ministry Council stands as the head of country's bureaucracy and is subordinated directly to the prince. For their official actions, the ministers can be held responsible by either parliament or the prince. A minister can be charged for treason, for acting against the constitution, for corruption, for damaging the state out of his own interest and if his actions are against the law in the cases set by the Law of Ministerial Responsibility. A minister can be charged by government, parliament or prince and his statute of limitations is set at five years.</p>
<p>State Council, composed of six members, appointed by the prince, provides the role of supreme administrative court, reviews government's legal initiatives and has jurisdiction over some subjects of financial nature. There are also high courts and municipal courts. The courts are independent in providing justice. The judges cannot be transferred without legal standpoint. The constitution also introduced local self-management through municipal courts, municipal committees and municipal assemblies. It also provided civil rights and freedom, law equality, courts jurisdiction, abolition of death penalty for purely political reasons, excluding the attempt on the life of prince or the members of the royal family.The aforementioned abolition was also not valid in cases where aside from political quilt, some other criminal action was done, as well as in cases punished by death according to military law. Right to personal property, the freedom of press and the right of assembly were also guaranteed. The constitution was followed by Penal Law (1906), Penal Procedure Law, Commerce and Obligations Law and the Lawyer's Management Law (1910).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite all its flaws and restrictions, the Montenegrin Constitution of 1905 was an important introduction of modern liberal tendencies in European societies and of human rights and freedoms in a small patrimonial Balkan country.</p>

By religion, there were 94.38% Orthodox Christians, the rest being mostly Muslims and smaller numbers of Roman Catholics.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Church">Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Montenegro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Metropolitanate_of_Montenegro_and_the_Littoral" title="Metropolitanate of Montenegro and the Littoral">Metropolitanate of Cetinje</a> was divided from the state by Danilo I. It was at the time nominally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbian_Orthodox" title="Serbian Orthodox">Serbian Orthodox</a>, though <i>de jure</i> part of the <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_Patriarchate_of_Constantinople" title="Ecumenical Patriarchate of Constantinople">Ecumenical Patriarchate of Constantinople</a>, it was largely independent. The <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Orthodox Church</a> uncanonically entered the Eparchy of Montenegro in their list of autocephalous churches (Sintagma, V, 1855). In 1908, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eparchy_of_Zahumlje-Ra%C5%A1ka&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eparchy of Zahumlje-Raška (page does not exist)">Eparchy of Zahumlje-Raška</a> was established, existing alongside the Eparchy of Cetinje.</p>
<p>The Metropolitans of Cetinje were: <a href="/wiki/Ilarion_Roganovi%C4%87" title="Ilarion Roganović">Ilarion Roganović</a> (1876-1882), <a href="/wiki/Visarion_Ljubi%C5%A1a" title="Visarion Ljubiša">Visarion Ljubiša</a> (1882-1884) and <a href="/wiki/Mitrofan_Ban" title="Mitrofan Ban">Mitrofan Ban</a> (1884-1918).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Orthodox_Church" title="Serbian Orthodox Church">Serbian Orthodox Church</a> was re-established in 1920, by merging the metropolitanates of Cetinje, Belgrade and Karlovci.</p>
