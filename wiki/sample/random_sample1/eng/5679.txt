<p><b>Montenegrins</b> (<a href="/wiki/Montenegrin_language" title="Montenegrin language">Montenegrin</a>: Црногорци<i>/Crnogorci</i>, <small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Serbo-Croatian" title="Help:IPA for Serbo-Croatian">[tsr̩nǒɡoːrtsi]</a></span> <small>or</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Serbo-Croatian" title="Help:IPA for Serbo-Croatian">[tsr̩noɡǒːrtsi]</a></span>) are a nation and <a href="/wiki/South_Slavs" title="South Slavs">South Slavic</a> people mainly living in the Balkans, primarily inhabiting <a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">Montenegro</a>. Migrant communities exist in <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia and Herzegovina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Macedonia" title="Republic of Macedonia">Republic of Macedonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identity_and_population">Identity and population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montenegrins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Identity and population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Duklja" title="Duklja">Duklja</a>; <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Zeta" title="Principality of Zeta">Principality of Zeta</a>; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tribes_of_Old_Montenegro,_Brda,_Old_Herzegovina_and_Primorje" title="Tribes of Old Montenegro, Brda, Old Herzegovina and Primorje">Tribes of Old Montenegro, Brda, Old Herzegovina and Primorje</a></div>
<p>Slavs have lived in the area of Montenegro since the 6th and 7th centuries in the medieval state of <a href="/wiki/Duklja" title="Duklja">Doclea</a>. <a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">Montenegro</a> (<a href="/wiki/Montenegrin_language" title="Montenegrin language">Montenegrin</a>: <i>Crna Gora</i>) got its present name during the rule of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Crnojevi%C4%87" title="House of Crnojević">Crnojević</a> dynasty. After the <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Uprising" title="Christmas Uprising">Christmas Uprising</a> (1919), which saw fighting between the pro-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petrovic_dynasty" title="Petrovic dynasty">Petrovic</a> guerrillas and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karadjordjevic_dynasty" title="Karadjordjevic dynasty">Karadjordjevic troops</a>, supporters of Montenegrin king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicholas_I_of_Montenegro" title="Nicholas I of Montenegro">Nicholas I</a> expressed opposition to unification with Serbia because it meant total disappearance of Montenegro, their leader Krsto Zrnov Popovic wanted to unify, but under the rule of king Nicholas I. After World War II, many Serbs of Montenegro began to identify themselves as Montenegrins. Following the collapse of Communism in Yugoslavia, however, some Montenegrins began to declare as <a href="/wiki/Serbs" title="Serbs">Serbs</a> again, while the largest proportion of citizens of Montenegro still preserved their Montenegrin self-identification. This has deepened further since the movement for full Montenegrin independence from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Federal Republic of Yugoslavia</a> began to gain ground in 1991, and ultimately narrowly succeeded in the <a href="/wiki/Montenegrin_independence_referendum,_2006" title="Montenegrin independence referendum, 2006">referendum of May 2006</a> (having been <a href="/wiki/Montenegrin_independence_referendum,_1992" title="Montenegrin independence referendum, 1992">rejected in 1992</a>).</p>
<p>In the 2011 census, around 280,000 or <i>44.98%</i> of the population of Montenegro identified themselves as ethnic Montenegrins, while around 180,000 or <i>28.73%</i> identified themselves as Serbs. The number of "Montenegrins", "Serbs" and "Bosniaks" fluctuates wildly from census to census, not due to real changes in the populace, but due to changes in how people experience their identity. According to the <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Serbia" title="Demographics of Serbia">2002 census</a>, there are around 70,000 ethnic Montenegrins in <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, accounting for <i>0.92%</i> of the Republic's population. In addition, a significant number of Serbs in Serbia and <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia-Herzegovina</a> are of Montenegrin ancestry, but exact numbers are difficult to assess – the inhabitants of Montenegro contributed greatly to the repopulation of a depopulated Serbia after two rebellions against the Ottoman Empire in the early 19th century,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> with half of the population of Sumadija and its surroundings being populated by people originally from Montenegro, and with several prominent individuals of the Serbian 18th &amp; early 20th century intelligentsia and entrepreneurs being descendants of people originally from Montenegro.</p>
<p>On 19 October 2007 Montenegro adopted a new Constitution which proclaimed <a href="/wiki/Montenegrin_language" title="Montenegrin language">Montenegrin</a> (a standardized variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian_language" title="Serbo-Croatian language">former Serbo-Croatian</a>) as the official language of Montenegro.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montenegrins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Montenegro" title="History of Montenegro">History of Montenegro</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montenegrins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Slavs settled in the Balkans during the 6th and 7th centuries. According to <i><a href="/wiki/De_Administrando_Imperio" title="De Administrando Imperio">De Administrando Imperio</a></i>, there existed three Slavic polities on the territory of Montenegro: <a href="/wiki/Duklja" title="Duklja">Duklja</a>, roughly corresponding to the southern half; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Travunia" title="Travunia">Travunia</a>, the west; and Serbia (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rascia" title="Rascia">Rascia</a>), the north. Duklja emerged as an independent state during the 11th century, initially held by the <a href="/wiki/Vojislavljevi%C4%87_dynasty" title="Vojislavljević dynasty">Vojislavljević dynasty</a>, later to be incorporated into the state of the <a href="/wiki/Nemanji%C4%87_dynasty" title="Nemanjić dynasty">Nemanjić dynasty</a>.</p>
<p>The region previously known as Duklja later became known as "<a href="/wiki/Zeta_(crown_land)" title="Zeta (crown land)">Zeta</a>". Between 1276 and 1309, Serbian queen <a href="/wiki/Helen_of_Anjou" title="Helen of Anjou">Helen of Anjou</a>, the widow of the Serbian King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uro%C5%A1_I" title="Uroš I">Uroš I</a>, ruled Zeta, where she built and restored several monasteries, most notably the Monastery of Saints Sergius and Bacchus (Srđ and Vakh) on the Bojana river below <a href="/wiki/Shkod%C3%ABr" title="Shkodër">Skadar (Shkodër)</a>. The Venetian name <i>Montenegro</i>, meaning "black mountain" occurred for the first time in the charter of St. Nicholas' monastery in Vranjina, dating to 1296, during Jelena's reign. Under King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stefan_Uro%C5%A1_II_Milutin_of_Serbia" title="Stefan Uroš II Milutin of Serbia">Stefan Milutin</a> (reigned 1282-1321), at the beginning of the 14th century, the Archdiocese in Bar was the biggest feudal domain in Zeta.</p>
<p>In the late 14th century, southern Montenegro (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lordship_of_Zeta" title="Lordship of Zeta">Zeta</a>) came under the rule of the <a href="/wiki/Bal%C5%A1i%C4%87_noble_family" title="Balšić noble family">Balšić noble family</a>, then the <a href="/wiki/Crnojevi%C4%87_noble_family" title="Crnojević noble family">Crnojević noble family</a>, and by the 15th century, Zeta was more often referred to as <i>Crna Gora</i> (<a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">Venetian</a>: <i><span lang="vec" xml:lang="vec">monte negro</span></i>). In 1496, the Ottomans conquered Zeta and subsequently <a href="/wiki/Sanjak_of_Montenegro" title="Sanjak of Montenegro">established a sanjak</a> that was subordinated to the <a href="/wiki/Sanjak_of_Scutari" title="Sanjak of Scutari">Sanjak of Scutari</a>. Ottoman influence remained largely limited to urban areas, while various tribes in the highlands emerged as districts out of reach of the Ottomans. These tribes were at times united against the Ottomans, under the leadership of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metropolitans_of_Montenegro" title="Metropolitans of Montenegro">Metropolitans of Montenegro</a>, the so-called "prince-bishops".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern">Modern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montenegrins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Montenegrins maintained their independence from the Ottoman Empire during the Ottoman's reign over most of the Balkan region (Bosnia, Serbia, Bulgaria, etc.). The Montenegrins were gathered around the Metropolitans of the Cetinje Metropolitanate, which led to further national awakening of the Montenegrins all around. The creation of a theocratic state and its advancement into a secular and independent country was even more evident in the late 15th and early 16th centuries.</p>
<p>The rule of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Petrovi%C4%87" title="House of Petrović">House of Petrović</a> in the 18th and 19th century unified the Montenegrins and established strong ties with Russia and later with Serbia (under Ottoman occupation), with occasional help from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Empire" title="Austro-Hungarian Empire">Austro-Hungarian Empire</a>. That period was marked by numerous battles with Turkish conquerors as well as by a firmer establishment of a self-governed principality.</p>
<p>In 1878, the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Berlin" title="Congress of Berlin">Congress of Berlin</a> recognised Montenegro as the 26th independent state in the world. Montenegro participated in the <a href="/wiki/Balkan_Wars" title="Balkan Wars">Balkan Wars</a> of 1911–1912, as well as in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> on the side of the Allies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yugoslav_era">Yugoslav era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montenegrins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Yugoslav era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Montenegro unconditionally joined Serbia on November 26, 1918 in a controversial decision of the illegal <a href="/wiki/Podgorica_Assembly" title="Podgorica Assembly">Podgorica Assembly</a>, and soon afterwards became a part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Serbs,_Croats_and_Slovenes" title="Kingdom of Serbs, Croats and Slovenes">Kingdom of Serbs, Croats and Slovenes</a>, later renamed as <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>. A number of Montenegrin chieftains, disappointed by the effective disappearance of Montenegro, which they perceived to have resulted from political manipulation, rose up in arms during January 1919 in an uprising known as the <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Uprising" title="Christmas Uprising">Christmas Rebellion</a>, which was crushed in a severe, comprehensive military campaign in 1922–23. Annexation of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Montenegro" title="Kingdom of Montenegro">Kingdom of Montenegro</a> on November 13, 1918 gained international recognition only at the Conference of Ambassadors in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, held on July 13, 1922.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1929 the newly renamed <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Yugoslavia" title="Kingdom of Yugoslavia">Kingdom of Yugoslavia</a> was reorganised into provinces (<i>banovine</i>) one of which, <a href="/wiki/Zeta_Banovina" title="Zeta Banovina">Zeta Banovina</a>, encompassed the old Kingdom of Montenegro and had Cetinje as its administrative centre.</p>
<p>Between the two world wars, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Yugoslavia" title="Communist Party of Yugoslavia">Communist Party of Yugoslavia</a> opposed the Yugoslav monarchy and its unification policy, and supported Montenegrin autonomy, gaining considerable support in Montenegro. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, many Montenegrins joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yugoslav_partisans" title="Yugoslav partisans">Yugoslav partisan</a> forces, although the portion joining the <a href="/wiki/Chetniks" title="Chetniks">chetniks</a> was also significant. One third of all officers in the partisan army were Montenegrins. They also gave a disproportionate number of highest-ranked party officials and generals. During WWII <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> occupied Montenegro (in 1941) and annexed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(1861%E2%80%931946)" title="Kingdom of Italy (1861–1946)">Kingdom of Italy</a> the area of Kotor, where there was a small Roman community (descendants from the populations of the renaissance <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albania_Veneta" title="Albania Veneta">Albania Veneta</a>). There was an attempt to create an independent Montenegro, but it was stillborn and remained a <a href="/wiki/Italian_governorate_of_Montenegro" title="Italian governorate of Montenegro">occupied territory</a>. Montenegro was ravaged by a terrible guerrilla war, mainly after Nazi Germany replaced the defeated Italians in September 1943.</p>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">second Yugoslavia</a> was formed in 1945, the Communists who led the Partisans during the war formed the new régime. They recognised, sanctioned and fostered a national identity of Montenegrins as a people distinct from the Serbs and other South Slavs. The number of people who were registered as Montenegrins in Montenegro was 90% in 1948; it has been dropping since, to 62% in 1991. With the rise of Serbian nationalism in the late 80's the number of citizens who declared themselves Montenegrin dropped sharply from 61.7%, in the 1991 census, to 43.16% in 2003. For a detailed overview of these trends, see the <a href="/wiki/Demographic_history_of_Montenegro" title="Demographic history of Montenegro">Demographic history of Montenegro</a>.</p>
<p>Initially, after the fall of Communism in the early 1990s, the idea of a distinct Montenegrin identity has been taken over by independence-minded Montenegrins. The ruling <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_of_Socialists_of_Montenegro" title="Democratic Party of Socialists of Montenegro">Democratic Party of Socialists</a> (DPS) (reformed communists), led by the prime minister <a href="/wiki/Milo_%C4%90ukanovi%C4%87" title="Milo Đukanović">Milo Đukanović</a> and the president <a href="/wiki/Momir_Bulatovi%C4%87" title="Momir Bulatović">Momir Bulatović</a>, was firmly allied with <a href="/wiki/Slobodan_Milo%C5%A1evi%C4%87" title="Slobodan Milošević">Slobodan Milošević</a> throughout this period and opposed such movements.</p>
<p>During the recent <a href="/wiki/Bosnian_War" title="Bosnian War">Bosnian War</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Croatian_War" title="Croatian War">Croatian War</a> (1991–1995) Montenegro participated with its <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">police</a> and paramilitary forces in the attacks on <a href="/wiki/Dubrovnik" title="Dubrovnik">Dubrovnik</a> and Bosnian towns along with Serbian troops. It conducted persecutions against Bosniak refugees who were arrested by the Montenegrin police and transported to Serb camps in Foča, where they were executed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seeking_Independence">Seeking Independence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montenegrins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Seeking Independence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>However, in 1997 a full-blown rift occurred within DPS, and Đukanović's faction won over Bulatović's, who formed a new <a href="/wiki/Socialist_People%27s_Party_of_Montenegro" title="Socialist People's Party of Montenegro">Socialist People's Party of Montenegro</a> (SNP). The DPS distanced itself from Milošević and gradually took over the independence idea from the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Alliance_of_Montenegro" title="Liberal Alliance of Montenegro">Liberal Alliance of Montenegro</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Montenegro" title="Social Democratic Party of Montenegro">SDP</a>, and has won all elections since.</p>
<p>In the fall of 1999, shortly after the <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> bombing of Yugoslavia, the Đukanović-led Montenegrin leadership came out with a platform for the re-definition of relations within the federation that called for more Montenegrin involvement in the areas of defence and foreign policy, though the platform fell short of pushing for independence. After Milošević's overthrow on October 5, 2000, Đukanović for the first time came out in support of full independence and succeeded in his quest by winning a vote on independence on 21 May 2006.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy_about_Montenegrin_ethnic_identity">Controversy about Montenegrin ethnic identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montenegrins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Controversy about Montenegrin ethnic identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Controversy_over_linguistic_and_ethnic_identity_in_Montenegro" title="Controversy over linguistic and ethnic identity in Montenegro">Controversy over linguistic and ethnic identity in Montenegro</a></div>
<p>Montenegro was part of medieval Serbia during 12th, 13th and first half of the 14th century. Ottoman conquest of the Balkans resulted in separation from Serbia and re-emergence of Zeta. In the 19th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_romanticism" title="National romanticism">national romanticism</a> among the South Slavs fueled the desire for re-unification.</p>
<ul>
<li>During <a href="/wiki/Petar_I_Petrovi%C4%87_Njego%C5%A1" title="Petar I Petrović Njegoš">Petar I Petrović Njegoš</a>'s reign, the basic <a href="/wiki/Textbook" title="Textbook">textbook</a> in state <a href="/wiki/School" title="School">schools</a> was called "The Serb elementary reading book". <p>Regarding the issue of independence of Montenegro, voted on a referendum in 2006, the population was roughly divided between ethnic Montenegrins (Orthodox, Muslim, and Catholic), <a href="/wiki/Bosniaks" title="Bosniaks">Bosniaks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Croats" title="Croats">Croats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Albanians" title="Albanians">Albanians</a> on one side, and ethnic <a href="/wiki/Serbs_of_Montenegro" title="Serbs of Montenegro">Serbs</a> on the other.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Various notable people in Montenegro supported Montenegrin independence and acknowledged the right of citizens in Montenegro to declare themselves as ethnic Montenegrins. Noted supporters of independence include current Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Milo_%C4%90ukanovi%C4%87" title="Milo Đukanović">Milo Đukanović</a> and the Speaker of Montenegro's Parliament <a href="/wiki/Ranko_Krivokapi%C4%87" title="Ranko Krivokapić">Ranko Krivokapić</a>. Of the minorities, these include the historical scientist <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0erbo_Rastoder" title="Šerbo Rastoder">Šerbo Rastoder</a>, a Bosniak from <a href="/wiki/Berane" title="Berane">Berane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Don_(honorific)" title="Don (honorific)">don</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Branko_Sbutega&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Branko Sbutega (page does not exist)">Branko Sbutega</a> (d. 2006), a Croat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> priest from <a href="/wiki/Kotor" title="Kotor">Kotor</a>, and the journalist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Esad_Ko%C4%8Dan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Esad Kočan (page does not exist)">Esad Kočan</a>, of Bosniak descent.</p>

<p>A number of Montenegrins living outside Montenegro, primarily in Serbia, still maintain Montenegrin <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a>, family ties and <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clan</a> affiliation. Some have risen to high <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultural</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">political</a> positions. <a href="/wiki/Slobodan_Milo%C5%A1evi%C4%87" title="Slobodan Milošević">Slobodan Milošević</a>, the Serbian nationalist and President of FR Yugoslavia, was of Montenegrin descent, the first generation of his family to be born in Serbia. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montenegrins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Montenegrin_language" title="Montenegrin language">Montenegrin language</a></div>
<p>Montenegrins speak the Ijekavian variant of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shtokavian_dialect" title="Shtokavian dialect">Shtokavian dialect</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">Serbo-Croatian</a> language. Neo-shtokavian Eastern-Herzegovinian sub-dialect is spoken in the North-West (largest city Niksic), and old shtokavian Zeta subdialect is spoken in the rest of Montenegro, including capitals Podgorica and Cetinje, and eastern <a href="/wiki/Sanjak" title="Sanjak">Sanjak</a>.</p>
<p>The Zeta dialect features additional sounds: a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolo-palatal_fricative" title="Voiceless alveolo-palatal fricative">voiceless alveolo-palatal fricative</a> (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɕ/</span>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolo-palatal_fricative" title="Voiced alveolo-palatal fricative">voiced alveolo-palatal fricative</a> *(<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʑ/</span>, (occurring in other jekavian dialects as well) and a <a href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_affricate" title="Voiced alveolar affricate">voiced alveolar affricate</a> (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">dz</span>, shared with other old-štokavian dialects). Both subdialects are charactericized by highly specific accents (shared with other old-štokavian dialects) and several "hyper-ijekavisms" (i.e. <i>nijesam</i>, where the rest of shtokavian area uses <i>nisam</i>) and "hyper-<a href="/wiki/Iotation" title="Iotation">iotations</a>" (<i>đevojka</i> for <i>djevojka</i>, <i>đeca</i> for <i>djeca</i> etc.) (these features, especially the hyper-iotation, are more prominent in Zeta subdialect), that are common in all Montenegrin vernaculars.</p>
<p>On <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sociolinguistic" title="Sociolinguistic">sociolinguistic</a> level, the language has been classified as a dialect of <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian</a>, being previously a dialect of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian_language" title="Serbo-Croatian language">Serbo-Croatian</a>. The Montenegrin constitution currently defines Montenegrin as the official language. Since the campaign for independence, a movement for recognition of the <a href="/wiki/Montenegrin_language" title="Montenegrin language">Montenegrin language</a> as separate from Serbian has emerged, finding the basis for separate language identity mostly in above-mentioned dialectal specifics. The current pro-independence government did not particularly embrace the movement, but did not oppose it either; trying to overcome the situation, the language school classes were renamed from "Serbian language" to "native language", with fierce opposition from pro-Serbian circles. In the 2011 census, 42.88% of Montenegrin citizens stated that they speak the Serbian language, while 36.97% stated that they speak Montenegrin.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montenegrins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_Montenegro" title="Religion in Montenegro">Religion in Montenegro</a></div>
<p>Most declared Montenegrins are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Christians" title="Orthodox Christians">Orthodox Christians</a>, predominantly belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Orthodox_Church" title="Serbian Orthodox Church">Serbian Orthodox Church</a>, the rest belonging to the uncanonical <a href="/wiki/Montenegrin_Orthodox_Church" title="Montenegrin Orthodox Church">Montenegrin Orthodox Church</a>.</p>
<p>According to the census, people that declared Montenegrin ethnicity declared the following religious identity:</p>
<ul>
<li>Eastern Orthodoxy: 246,733</li>
<li>Sunni Islam: 12,758</li>
<li>Roman Catholicism: 5,667</li>
<li>Atheism: 5,867</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montenegrins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Montenegro" title="Culture of Montenegro">Culture of Montenegro</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Montenegrin_Herzegovinian_costume.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="768" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Montenegrin_Herzegovinian_costume.jpg/220px-Montenegrin_Herzegovinian_costume.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Montenegrin_Herzegovinian_costume.jpg/330px-Montenegrin_Herzegovinian_costume.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Montenegrin_Herzegovinian_costume.jpg/440px-Montenegrin_Herzegovinian_costume.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Montenegrin_Herzegovinian_costume.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Male folk costume from Montenegro</div>

<p>The most important dimension of Montenegrin culture is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethic" title="Ethic">ethic</a> ideal of <i>Čojstvo i Junaštvo</i>, roughly translated as "Humanity and Bravery". Another result of its centuries long <a href="/wiki/Warrior" title="Warrior">warrior</a> history, is the unwritten code of <a href="/wiki/Chivalry" title="Chivalry">Chivalry</a> that <a href="/wiki/Marko_Miljanov" title="Marko Miljanov">Marko Miljanov</a>, one of the most famous warriors in his time, tried to describe in his book <i>Primjeri Čojstva i Junaštva</i> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Examples_of_Humanity_and_Bravery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Examples of Humanity and Bravery (page does not exist)">Examples of Humanity and Bravery</a>) at the end of 19th century. Its main principles stipulate that to deserve a true respect of its people, a warrior has to show virtues of <a href="/wiki/Integrity" title="Integrity">integrity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dignity" title="Dignity">dignity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humility" title="Humility">humility</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-sacrifice" title="Self-sacrifice">self-sacrifice</a> for the just cause if necessary, <a href="/wiki/Respect" title="Respect">respect</a> for others, and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Rectitude" title="wiktionary:Rectitude">Rectitude</a> along with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bravery" title="Bravery">bravery</a>. In the old days of battle, it resulted in Montenegrins fighting to the death, since being captured was considered the greatest <a href="/wiki/Shame" title="Shame">shame</a>.</p>
<p>It is still very much engraved, to a greater or lesser extent, on every Montenegrin's ethical belief system and it is essential in order to truly understand them. Coming from non-warrior backgrounds, most other South-Slavic nations never fully grasped its meaning, resulting in reactions which ranged from totally ignoring it, in the best case, to mocking it and equating it with backwardness.</p>
<p>Most of extraordinary examples of Montenegrin conduct during its long history can be traced to the code. Its importance is also reflected in the generally very low level of religiousness in the Montenegrin population. It is probably fair to say that the ethical beliefs of Montenegrins more closely match those of <a href="/wiki/Stoicism" title="Stoicism">Stoicism</a> than those of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>.</p>
<p>Montenegrins' long-standing history of fighting for independence is invariably linked with strong traditions of folk <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic poetry</a>. A prominent feature of Montenegrin culture is the <a href="/wiki/Gusle" title="Gusle">gusle</a>, a one-stringed instrument played by a story-teller who sings or recites stories of heroes and battles in <a href="/wiki/Decasyllable" title="Decasyllable">decasyllabic verse</a>. These traditions are stronger in the northern parts of the country and are also shared with people in eastern <a href="/wiki/Herzegovina" title="Herzegovina">Herzegovina</a>, western <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a> and central <a href="/wiki/Dalmatia" title="Dalmatia">Dalmatia</a>.</p>
<p>On the substratum of folk epic poetry, poets like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petar_II_Petrovi%C4%87_Njego%C5%A1" title="Petar II Petrović Njegoš">Petar II Petrović Njegoš</a>, the Montenegrin icon, have created their own expression. Njegoš's epic book <i>Gorski Vijenac</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Mountain_Wreath" title="The Mountain Wreath">The Mountain Wreath</a></i>) presents the central point of Montenegrin culture.</p>
<p>On the other hand, Adriatic cities like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herceg-Novi" title="Herceg-Novi">Herceg-Novi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kotor" title="Kotor">Kotor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Budva" title="Budva">Budva</a> had strong trade and maritime tradition, and presented an entry-point for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venetian_Republic" title="Venetian Republic">Venetian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ragusa" title="Republic of Ragusa">Ragusan</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Catholic</a> influences. Possession of those cities often changed, but their population was basically a mixture of people with Orthodox and Catholic religions and traditions. These cities were incorporated into Montenegro only after the fall of Austria-Hungary. In those cities, stronger influences of medieval and renaissance <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painting</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lyric_poetry" title="Lyric poetry">lyric poetry</a> can be found.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropology">Anthropology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montenegrins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Anthropology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vlahović (2008) noted many <a href="/wiki/Biological_anthropology" title="Biological anthropology">anthropological studies</a> which showed that Montenegrin people have strong <a href="/wiki/Dinaric_race" title="Dinaric race">Dinaric</a> type (with seaboard, central, Durmitor, mountain and other subtypes) autochthonous on the <a href="/wiki/Dinaric_Alps" title="Dinaric Alps">Dinaric Alps</a> since the Mesolithic period. Dinaric people, including Montenegrins, are the tallest people in Europe. The type, particularly in Montenegro, is distinguished by brachiochepal shape, broad forehead, wide relief and strong face, wide jaw and noticeably flat notched head, while arms and legs are proportional to the body height. Hair is commonly of black color, with black or blue eyes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VlahovicAnthro_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-VlahovicAnthro-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Bo%C5%BEina_Ivanovi%C4%87" title="Božina Ivanović">Božina Ivanović</a> considered that the development of the Montenegrin Dinaric variety was influenced by gracilization and brachycephalization; they have characteristics which were not found in other Slavic and non-Slavic European populations, nor morphological properties from paleo-anthropological series originating from the Slavic <a href="/wiki/Necropolis" title="Necropolis">necropolis</a> from other South Slavic area. Also, the brachycephalization and width of the face in the last five centuries is growing in Montenegrin, while among other Slavic and European communities decreasing, showing anthropological issues in Montenegro have deeper roots and broader scientific importance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VlahovicAnthro_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-VlahovicAnthro-20">[20]</a></sup> Montenegrin historian Dragoje Živković (1989) noted that modern multidisciplinary research disagrees with older consideration how Sklavinias and Slavic states had ethnical identification, example Serb ethnos, until 12th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.C5.BDivkovi.C4.87198997.2C_103_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.C5.BDivkovi.C4.87198997.2C_103-21">[21]</a></sup> Slavs mixing with native population (in case of Komani culture necropolis in <a href="/wiki/Puk%C3%AB" title="Pukë">Pukë</a>) made a new cultural-historical drift of Albanian-Illyrian and Slavic built upon extinct and present <a href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne_culture" title="La Tène culture">La Tène</a>, Greek-Illyrian, Illyrian-Roman, and Byzantine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.C5.BDivkovi.C4.87198994.E2.80.9395_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.C5.BDivkovi.C4.87198994.E2.80.9395-22">[22]</a></sup> He argued that the Slavs from <a href="/wiki/Duklja" title="Duklja">Duklja</a> promptly blended in social-economical of the natives who historically had more developed and civilized society, as was in their interest to approach the Roman-Illyrian natives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.C5.BDivkovi.C4.87198996.2C_124_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.C5.BDivkovi.C4.87198996.2C_124-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Genetic_genealogy" title="Genetic genealogy">Genetic studies</a> done in 2010 on 404 male individuals from Montenegro confirmed previous anthropological studies; autochthonous haplogroups <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_I-M438" title="Haplogroup I-M438">I2a</a> (<a href="/wiki/Prehistory_of_Southeastern_Europe#Mesolithic" title="Prehistory of Southeastern Europe">Mesolithic</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_E-V68" title="Haplogroup E-V68">E-V13</a> (<a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Europe" title="Neolithic Europe">Neolithic</a>) make 60% (1:1 ratio), <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_J-M172" title="Haplogroup J-M172">J2</a> (Neolithic) 9%, Indo-European <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_R1b" title="Haplogroup R1b">R1b</a> 9.5%, while <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_R1a" title="Haplogroup R1a">R1a</a> which represents the Slavic substrata brought during the Middle Ages only 7.5%, with all others less than 5% each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMirabal2010380.E2.80.93390_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMirabal2010380.E2.80.93390-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
