<p>The <b>Université de Montréal</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> (<b>UdeM</b>; <small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[ynivɛʁsite də mɔ̃ʁeal]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Public_university#Canada" title="Public university">public</a> research university in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, Canada. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francophone" title="Francophone">francophone</a> institution comprises thirteen faculties, more than sixty departments and two<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> affiliated schools: the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Polytechnique_de_Montr%C3%A9al" title="École Polytechnique de Montréal">École Polytechnique</a> (School of Engineering) and <a href="/wiki/HEC_Montr%C3%A9al" title="HEC Montréal">HEC Montréal</a> (School of Business). It offers more than 650 undergraduate programmes and graduate programmes, including 71 doctoral programmes. The Times Higher Education World University Rankings of 2014-2015 ranks the Université de Montréal at 113th place globally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats2_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-stats2-8">[8]</a></sup> The Université de Montréal made it to the 83rd position worldwide according to the Quacquarelli Symonds (QS) World University rankings for 2014-2015 (undergraduate category).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The university has Quebec's largest sponsored research income and the third largest in Canada, allocating close to <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">$</a>524.1 million to research conducted in more than 150 research centres as of 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It is also part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U15_(universities)" title="U15 (universities)">U15</a> universities. More than 55,000 students are enrolled in undergraduate and graduate programs, making it the second-largest university in Canada in terms of student enrolment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-stats-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As an institution, the university was first founded when the <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_Laval" title="Université Laval">Université Laval</a> in <a href="/wiki/Quebec_City" title="Quebec City">Quebec City</a> founded a new branch in Montréal in 1878, which became known as the Université de Laval à Montréal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This initially went against the wishes of Montréal's <a href="/wiki/Prelate" title="Prelate">prelate</a>, who advocated an independent university in his city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Certain parts of the institution's educational facilities, such as those of the <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9minaire_de_Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Séminaire de Québec">Séminaire de Québec</a> and the Faculty of Medicine, founded as the Montreal School of Medicine and Surgery, had already been established in Montréal in 1876 and 1843 respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Vatican</a> granted the university some administrative autonomy in 1889, thus allowing it to choose its own professors and license its own diplomas. However it was not until 8 May 1919 that a papal charter from <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XV" title="Pope Benedict XV">Pope Benedict XV</a> granted full autonomy to the university.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-14">[14]</a></sup> It thus became an independent Catholic university and adopted Université de Montréal as its name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Laval composed by <a href="/wiki/Wilfrid_Beaudry" title="Wilfrid Beaudry">Wilfrid Beaudry</a> was dedicated to the students at Laval University and the Université de Montréal. The music for piano was published in Québec by J. Beaudry, circa 1906.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the time of its creation, less than a hundred students were admitted to the university's three faculties, which at that time were located in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vieux-Montr%C3%A9al" title="Vieux-Montréal">Old Montreal</a>. These were the faculty of theology (located at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_s%C3%A9minaire_de_Montr%C3%A9al" title="Grand séminaire de Montréal">Grand séminaire de Montréal</a>), the faculty of law (hosted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Society_of_Saint-Sulpice" title="Society of Saint-Sulpice">Society of Saint-Sulpice</a>) and the faculty of medicine (at the <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_Ramezay" title="Château Ramezay">Château Ramezay</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Graduate_school" title="Graduate school">Graduate training</a> based on German-inspired American models of specialized course work and completion of a research thesis was introduced and adopted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-13">[13]</a></sup> Most of Québec's <a href="/wiki/Secondary_education" title="Secondary education">secondary education</a> establishments employed classic course methods of varying quality. This forced the university to open a <a href="/wiki/University-preparatory_school" title="University-preparatory school">preparatory school</a> in 1887 to harmonize the education level of its students. Named the "Faculty of Arts", this school would remain in use until 1972 and was the predecessor of Québec's current <a href="/wiki/CEGEP" title="CEGEP">CEGEP</a> system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_by_provincial_charter">Founding by provincial charter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Founding by provincial charter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although a branch of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laval_University" title="Laval University">Laval University</a> was planned as Montreal's first French-language university, it was not until 14 February 1920, that the first provincial charter founding the university was passed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-14">[14]</a></sup> The second provincial charter was passed in 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-14">[14]</a></sup> The policy of university education initiated in the 1960s (following the <a href="/wiki/Quiet_Revolution" title="Quiet Revolution">Quiet Revolution</a>) was a response to popular pressure and the belief that higher education was a key to social justice and economic productivity for individuals as well as society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-13">[13]</a></sup> The third provincial charter, which was passed in 1967, defined the Université de Montréal as a public institution, dedicated to higher learning and research, in the administration of which students and teachers would have the right to participate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus_relocation">Campus relocation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Campus relocation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>From 1876 to 1895, most university classes took place in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_s%C3%A9minaire_de_Montr%C3%A9al" title="Grand séminaire de Montréal">Grand séminaire de Montréal</a>. From 1895 to 1942, it was housed in a building at the intersection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rue_Saint-Denis_(Montreal)" title="Rue Saint-Denis (Montreal)">Saint-Denis</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rue_Sainte-Catherine_(Montreal)" title="Rue Sainte-Catherine (Montreal)">Sainte-Catherine</a> streets in Montreal's eastern downtown <a href="/wiki/Quartier_Latin,_Montreal" title="Quartier Latin, Montreal">Quartier Latin</a>.</p>
<p>Unlike English-language universities in Montréal, such as <a href="/wiki/McGill_University" title="McGill University">McGill University</a>, the university suffered a lack of funding for two major reasons: the relative poverty of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Canadian" title="French Canadian">French Canadian</a> population and the complications ensuing from its being managed remotely, from Quebec City. The downtown campus was hit by three different fires between 1919 and 1921, further complicating the university's already precarious finances and forcing it to spend much of its resources on repairing its own infrastructure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1930, enough funds had been accumulated to start the construction of a new campus on the north west slope of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Royal" title="Mount Royal">Mount Royal</a>, adopting new plans designed by <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Cormier" title="Ernest Cormier">Ernest Cormier</a>. However, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">financial crisis of the 1930s</a> virtually suspended all ongoing construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Many speculated that the university would have to sell off its unfinished building projects in order to ensure its own survival. Not until 1939 did the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Politics_of_Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Politics of Québec">provincial government</a> directly intervene by injecting public funds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Campus construction subsequently resumed and the mountain campus was officially inaugurated on 3 June 1943.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cote-des-Neiges" title="Cote-des-Neiges">Cote-des-Neiges</a> site includes property expropriated from a residential development along Decelles Avenue, known as Northmount Heights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Northmount_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Northmount-24">[24]</a></sup> The university's former downtown facilities would later serve Montreal's second francophone university, the <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_du_Qu%C3%A9bec_%C3%A0_Montr%C3%A9al" title="Université du Québec à Montréal">Université du Québec à Montréal</a> (UQAM).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_research">Nuclear research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nuclear research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montr%C3%A9al_Laboratory" title="Montréal Laboratory">Montréal Laboratory</a></div>
<p>In 1943, the university assisted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Allies" title="Western Allies">Western Allies</a> by providing laboratory accommodations on its campus. Scientists there worked to develop a nuclear reactor, notably by conducting various <a href="/wiki/Heavy_water" title="Heavy water">heavy water</a> experiments. The research was part of the larger <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a>, which aimed to develop the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_bomb" title="Atomic bomb">atomic bomb</a>. Scientists here managed to produce the first <a href="/wiki/Atomic_battery" title="Atomic battery">atomic battery</a> to work outside of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. One of the participating Québécois scientists, Pierre Demers, also discovered a series of radioactive elements issued from <a href="/wiki/Neptunium" title="Neptunium">Neptunium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_and_expansion">Growth and expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Growth and expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Two distinct schools eventually became affiliated to the university. The first was the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Polytechnique_de_Montr%C3%A9al" title="École Polytechnique de Montréal">École Polytechnique</a>, a school of engineering, which was founded in 1873 and became affiliated in 1887. The second was the <a href="/wiki/HEC_Montr%C3%A9al" title="HEC Montréal">École des Hautes Études Commerciales</a>, or HEC, which was founded in 1907 and became part of the university in 1915.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-17">[17]</a></sup> The first francophone school of architecture in Canada opened in 1907 at the École Polytechnique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between 1920 and 1925, seven new faculties were added to the initial three: Philosophy, Literature, Sciences, Veterinary medicine, Dental surgery, Pharmacy and Social sciences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-20">[20]</a></sup> Notably, the Faculty of Social sciences was founded in 1920 by <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Montpetit" title="Édouard Montpetit">Édouard Montpetit</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/La%C3%AFcit%C3%A9" title="Laïcité">laic</a> to lead a faculty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> He thereafter fulfilled the role of secretary-general until 1950.</p>
<p>In 1965, the appointment of the university's first secular rector, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Gaudry" title="Roger Gaudry">Roger Gaudry</a>, paved the way for modernization. The university established the first adult education degree program offered by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Canadian" title="French Canadian">French Canadian</a> university in 1968. That year were inaugurated <i>Lionel-Groulx</i> and <i>3200 Jean-Brillant</i> buildings, the former being named after Quebec nationalist <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Groulx" title="Lionel Groulx">Lionel Groulx</a>. The following year, the Louis Collin parking garage -which won a <a href="/wiki/Governor_General%27s_Awards" title="Governor General's Awards">Governor General's medal</a> in architecture in 1970 - was erected.</p>
<p>An important event that marked the university's history was the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Polytechnique_massacre" title="École Polytechnique massacre">École Polytechnique massacre</a>. On 6 December 1989, a gunman armed with a rifle entered the École Polytechnique building, killing 14 people, all of whom were women, before taking his own life.</p>
<p>Since 2002, the university has embarked on its largest construction projects since the late 1960s, with five new modern buildings planned for advanced research in pharmacology, engineering, aerospace, cancer studies and biotechnology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 4 September 2003 Canada Post issued 'Université de Montréal, 1878-2003' to mark the 125th anniversary of the establishment of Université de Montréal. The stamp was designed by Denis L'Allier, based on a photograph by Guy Lavigueur as part of the Canadian Universities series. The 48¢ stamps are perforated 13.5 and were printed by Canadian Bank Note Company, Limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p>The university's main campus is located on the northern slope of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mont-Royal" title="Mont-Royal">Mont-Royal</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outremont_(borough)" title="Outremont (borough)">Outremont</a> and <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te-des-Neiges" title="Côte-des-Neiges">Côte-des-Neiges</a> boroughs. Its landmark Pavilion Roger-Gaudry - known until 2003 as <i>Pavillon principal</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> - and named for former rector <a href="/wiki/Roger_Gaudry" title="Roger Gaudry">Roger Gaudry</a>, can be seen from around the campus and is known for its imposing tower. It is built mainly in the <a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco">Art Deco</a> style with some elements of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_style_(architecture)" title="International style (architecture)">International style</a> and designed by noted architect <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Cormier" title="Ernest Cormier">Ernest Cormier</a>. On 14 September 1954, a Roll of Honour plaque on the wall at the right of the stairs to the Court of Honour in Roger-Gaudry Pavillon was dedicated alumni of the Université de Montréal who died in armed service during the Second World War. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> On November 1963, a memorial plaque was dedicated to the memory of those members of the Université de Montréal who served in the Armed Forces during the First and Second World Wars and Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The campus is served by the <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te-des-Neiges_(Montreal_Metro)" title="Côte-des-Neiges (Montreal Metro)">Côte-des-Neiges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9-de-Montr%C3%A9al_(Montreal_Metro)" title="Université-de-Montréal (Montreal Metro)">Université-de-Montreal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard-Montpetit_(Montreal_Metro)" title="Édouard-Montpetit (Montreal Metro)">Édouard-Montpetit</a> <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Metro" title="Montreal Metro">metro</a> stations.</p>
<p>Apart from its main Mont-Royal campus, the university also maintains five regional facilities in <a href="/wiki/Terrebonne,_Quebec" title="Terrebonne, Quebec">Terrebonne</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laval,_Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Laval, Québec">Laval</a>, <a href="/wiki/Longueuil" title="Longueuil">Longueuil</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint-Hyacinthe" title="Saint-Hyacinthe">Saint-Hyacinthe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mauricie" title="Mauricie">Mauricie</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The campus in Laval, just north of Montréal, was opened in 2006. It is Laval's first university campus, and is located in the area near the <a href="/wiki/Montmorency_(Montreal_Metro)" title="Montmorency (Montreal Metro)">Montmorency</a> metro station. In October 2009, the university announced an expansion to its Laval satellite campus with the commissioning of its six-storey Cité du Savoir complex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cit.C3.A9_du_Savoir_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cit.C3.A9_du_Savoir-33">[33]</a></sup> In order to solve the problem of lack of space on its main campus, the university is also planning to open a new campus in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outremont_(borough)" title="Outremont (borough)">Outremont</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Centre_hospitalier_de_l%27Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al" title="Centre hospitalier de l'Université de Montréal">Centre hospitalier de l'Université de Montréal</a> (CHUM) and the <a href="/wiki/Centre_hospitalier_universitaire_Sainte-Justine" title="Centre hospitalier universitaire Sainte-Justine">Centre hospitalier universitaire Sainte-Justine</a> are the two teaching hospital networks of the Université de Montréal's Faculty of Medicine, although the latter is also affiliated with other medical institutions such as the Institut universitaire de gériatrie de Montréal, Montreal Heart Institute, Hôpital Sacré-Coeur and Hôpital Maisonneuve-Rosemont. A plaque, which is dedicated to the personnel of the "Hôpital Général Canadien No. 6 (Université Laval de Montréal)" from 1916 to 1920, was donated by Mr. Louis de Gonzague Beaubien in 1939.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The J.-Armand-Bombardier Incubator<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> is part of one the joint building from Université de Montréal and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Polytechnique_de_Montr%C3%A9al" title="École Polytechnique de Montréal">École Polytechnique de Montréal</a>. The incubator is part on the main Campus of Université de Montréal and was built in autumn 2004 with the aim of helping R&amp;D intensive companies to start up by providing complete <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructures</a> at advantageous conditions.</p>
<p>This particular environment helps promote collaboration between industries and academics while gratifying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Québec">Québec</a> <a href="/wiki/Entrepreneurship" title="Entrepreneurship">entrepreneurship</a>. Since its creation the Incubator hosted more than fifteen companies, mainly in the biomedical field (<a class="external text" href="http://cuttlepharma.com/" rel="nofollow">Cuttle Pharmaceuticals</a>, Angiotech, Siegfried, Haemacure) in the field of polymer / surface treatment (<a class="external text" href="http://www.solarischem.com/Home.html" rel="nofollow">Solaris Chem</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://cerestech.ca/" rel="nofollow">Cerestech, Nanomextrix</a>, Novaplasma) in optics / photonics (<a href="/wiki/Photon_etc." title="Photon etc.">Photon etc.</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://www.castoroptics.com/" rel="nofollow">Castor Optics</a>, <a class="external text" href="https://www.thorlabs.com/" rel="nofollow">Thorlabs</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://www.geniaphotonics.com/" rel="nofollow">Genia Photonics</a>) and IT security (<a href="/wiki/ESET" title="ESET">ESET</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://www.urqui.com/" rel="nofollow">Urqui</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academics">Academics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Academics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The University of Montreal is a publicly funded research university, and a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Association_of_Universities_and_Colleges_of_Canada" title="Association of Universities and Colleges of Canada">Association of Universities and Colleges of Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Undergraduate students make the majority of the university community, accounting for 74 percent of the university student body, followed by master students at 19 percent, and doctoral students at 7 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-stats-3">[3]</a></sup> The full-time undergraduate programs comprise the majority of the school's enrolment, made up of 42,684 undergraduate students. From the 1 June 2010 to the 31 May 2011, the university conferred 7,012 bachelor's degrees, 461 doctoral degrees, and 3,893 master's degrees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-stats-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Depending on a student's citizenship, they may be eligible for financial assistance from the Student Financial Assistance program, administered by the provincial <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Education,_Recreation_and_Sports_(Quebec)" title="Ministry of Education, Recreation and Sports (Quebec)">Ministry of Education, Recreation and Sports</a>, and/or the <a href="/wiki/Student_loans_in_Canada" title="Student loans in Canada">Canada Student Loans and Grants</a> through the federal and provincial governments. The university's Office of Financial Aid acts as intermediaries between the students and the Quebec government for all matters relating to financial assistance programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The financial aid provided may come in the form of loans, grants, bursaries, scholarships fellowships and work programs.</p>

<p>The 2013-2014 <a href="/wiki/Times_Higher_Education_World_University_Rankings" title="Times Higher Education World University Rankings">Times Higher Education World University Rankings</a> placed the university 106th in the world, and fifth in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USUnivRankings_THES_W_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-USUnivRankings_THES_W-41">[41]</a></sup> The 2014 <a href="/wiki/QS_World_University_Rankings" title="QS World University Rankings">QS World University Rankings</a> ranked the university 83rd in the world, and fourth in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The 2012 <a href="/wiki/Academic_Ranking_of_World_Universities" title="Academic Ranking of World Universities">Academic Ranking of World Universities</a> (ARWU) rankings, the university ranked 101-150th in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USUnivRankings_ARWU_W_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-USUnivRankings_ARWU_W-39">[39]</a></sup> In terms of national rankings, <i><a href="/wiki/Maclean%27s" title="Maclean's">Maclean's</a></i> ranked the university 12th in their 2011 Medical Doctoral university rankings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macdoc_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Macdoc-44">[44]</a></sup> The university was ranked in spite of having opted out—along with several other universities in Canada—of participating in Maclean's graduate survey since 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>The university was ranked 76-100th in the 2012 ARWU rankings within the field of social sciences, and 5-8th in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USUnivRankings_ARWU_SOC_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-USUnivRankings_ARWU_SOC-48">[48]</a></sup> The 2012-2013 Times Higher Education rankings for clinical, pre-clinical, and health universities, the university's health science programs ranked 47th in the world and fourth in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UnivRankings_THES_HEAL_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-UnivRankings_THES_HEAL-49">[49]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al_Faculty_of_Law" title="Université de Montréal Faculty of Law">Université de Montréal Faculty of Law</a> was ranked second, out of the six <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a> schools in Canada in <i>Maclean's</i> 2011 law school rankings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/HEC_Montr%C3%A9al" title="HEC Montréal">HEC Montréal</a>, an affiliated business school with the university has also received significant recognition. The business school had ranked 12th on the 2011 <a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a> ranking of the best international one-year MBA programs, placing higher than any Canadian business school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The 2011 <a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a> ranking for <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">master's</a> in management programs placed HEC Montréal 39th in the world, and first in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CNN_Expansion" title="CNN Expansion">CNN Expansion's</a> 2011 ranking of the world's best MBA program, HEC Montréal was ranked 62nd in the world, and second nationally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist's</a> 2011 ranking of the best MBA program in North America, HEC Montréal was 56th on the continent, and fifth nationally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The 2010 <a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_Businessweek" title="Bloomberg Businessweek">Bloomberg Businessweek</a> biannual business school rankings had also ranked HEC Montréal as the 15th best business school outside the United States, and the sixth best business school in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The QS ranking of North American MBA programs placed HEC Montréal 30th in North America, and 7th in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> In an employability survey published by the <a class="mw-redirect" <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Research Infosource's 2013 ranking of Canada's 50 top research universities, the university was ranked third, with a sponsored research income of $526,213,000, the third largest in the country. The university has an average of $280,000 per faculty member, making it the fifth most research-intensive full-service university.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> In terms of research performance, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=High_Impact_Universities&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="High Impact Universities (page does not exist)">High Impact Universities</a> 2010 ranked the university 108th out of 500 universities, and sixth in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> In the field of medicine, dentistry, pharmacology, and health sciences, the 2010 High Impact Universities ranking placed Université de Montréal in 68th in the world, and fifth nationally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> In the field of life, agricultural and biological sciences, the 2010 High Impact Universities ranking placed the university 99th in the world, and fourth in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Higher_Education_Evaluation_and_Accreditation_Council_of_Taiwan" title="Higher Education Evaluation and Accreditation Council of Taiwan">Higher Education Evaluation and Accreditation Council of Taiwan</a> (HEEACT), an organization which also evaluates universities based on their scientific paper's performances, ranked the university 101st in the world, and sixth in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> In HEEACT's 2011 rankings which focused on life sciences, the university was ranked 81st in the world, and fourth in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> In HEEACT's rankings focusing on clinical medicines, the university was also ranked 81st in the world, and sixth in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> The HEEACT rankings focusing on social sciences placed the university 83rd in the world and seventh in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Rankings_of_universities_in_Canada#Higher_Education_Strategy_Associates" title="Rankings of universities in Canada">Higher Education Strategy Associates</a> 2012 ranking of Canadian universities based on research strength, the Université de Montréal was placed second nationally in the field of science and engineering and sixth nationally in the field of social sciences and humanities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_life">Student life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Student life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The two main student unions on administrative and policy issues are the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_des_associations_%C3%A9tudiantes_du_campus_de_l%27Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al" title="Fédération des associations étudiantes du campus de l'Université de Montréal">Fédération des associations étudiantes du campus de l'Université de Montréal</a> (FAÉCUM), which represents the interests of all full-time undergraduate and graduate students, and the Association Étudiante de la Maîtrise et du Doctorat de HEC Montréal (AEMD), which represents the interests of those enrolled in HEC Montréal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> FAÉCUM traces its lineage back to 1989, when the Fédération étudiante universitaire du Québec (FEUQ) was founded, and is currently the largest student organization in Quebec.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The organizations and clubs accredited at the university cover a wide range of interests including academics, culture, religion, social issues, and recreation. FAÉCUM is currently associated with 82 student organizations and clubs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Three <a href="/wiki/Fraternities_and_sororities" title="Fraternities and sororities">fraternities and sororities</a> are recognized by the university's student union, <a href="/wiki/Sigma_Th%C3%AAta_Pi" title="Sigma Thêta Pi">Sigma Thêta Pi</a>, Nu Delta Mu and Zeta Lambda Zeta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The university's student population operates a number of media outlets throughout the campus environment. The <i>Quartier Libre</i>, is university's main student newspaper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/CISM-FM" title="CISM-FM">CISM-FM</a> is an independently owned radio station, owned by the students of the Université de Montréal, and operated by the student union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> The radio started in 1970, and received a permit from the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Radio-television_and_Telecommunications_Commission" title="Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission">Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission</a> (CRTC) on 10 July 1990 to transmit on an FM band. On 14 March 1991, CISM's broadcasting antenna was boosted to 10 000 watts. With a broadcasting radius of 70 km, CISM is now the world's largest French-language university radio station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/CFTU-DT" title="CFTU-DT">CFTU-DT</a> television station also receives its technical and administrative support from the student body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>

<p>Sport teams at Université de Montréal are known as the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Carabins" title="Montreal Carabins">Carabins</a>. The Carabins sports teams participate in the <a href="/wiki/U_Sports" title="U Sports">U Sports'</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%C3%A9seau_du_sport_%C3%A9tudiant_du_Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Réseau du sport étudiant du Québec">Réseau du sport étudiant du Québec</a> (RSEQ) conference for most varsity sports. Varsity teams include badminton, Canadian football, cheerleading, golf, hockey, swimming, alpine skiing, soccer, tennis and volleyball.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> The athletics program at the university dates back to 1922.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sporthi_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-sporthi-76">[76]</a></sup> The university's athletic facilities is open to both their varsity teams as well as to their students. The major sports facility at the university is the <a href="/wiki/CEPSUM" title="CEPSUM">Centre d'éducation physique et des sports de l'Université de Montréal</a> (CEPSUM), which is also home to all of the Carabin's varsity teams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carib_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-carib-78">[78]</a></sup> The building itself was built in 1976, in preparation for the <a href="/wiki/1976_Summer_Olympics" title="1976 Summer Olympics">1976 Summer Olympics</a> in Montréal. The outdoor stadium of CEPSUM, which hosts the university's football team, can seat around 5,100 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carib_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-carib-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_alumni_and_faculty">Notable alumni and faculty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Notable alumni and faculty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al_people" title="List of Université de Montréal people">List of Université de Montréal people</a></div>
<p>The university has an extensive alumni network, with more than 300,000 members registered with the university's alumni network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> Throughout the university's history, faculty, alumni, and former students have played prominent roles in a number of fields. Several prominent business leaders have also graduated from the university. Graduates include <a href="/wiki/Philippe_de_Gasp%C3%A9_Beaubien" title="Philippe de Gaspé Beaubien">Philippe de Gaspé Beaubien</a>, founder and CEO of <a href="/wiki/Telemedia" title="Telemedia">Telemedia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Louis_R._Ch%C3%AAnevert" title="Louis R. Chênevert">Louis R. Chênevert</a>, chairman and CEO of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Technologies_Corporation" title="United Technologies Corporation">United Technologies Corporation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Karl_P%C3%A9ladeau" title="Pierre Karl Péladeau">Pierre Karl Péladeau</a>, former president and CEO of <a href="/wiki/Quebecor" title="Quebecor">Quebecor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of students have also gained prominence for their research and work in a number of scientific fields. <a href="/wiki/Roger_Guillemin" title="Roger Guillemin">Roger Guillemin</a>, a graduate of the university, would later be awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> for his work with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurohormones" title="Neurohormones">neurohormones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> Alumnus <a href="/wiki/Ishfaq_Ahmad" title="Ishfaq Ahmad">Ishfaq Ahmad</a>, would also gain prominence for his work with <a href="/wiki/Pakistan_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Pakistan and weapons of mass destruction">Pakistan's nuclear weapon's program</a>, and their <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Pakistan" title="Nuclear power in Pakistan">nuclear power program</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jocelyn_Faubert" title="Jocelyn Faubert">Jocelyn Faubert</a>, known for his work in the fields of visual perception, is currently a faculty member of the university.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gilles_Brassard" title="Gilles Brassard">Gilles Brassard</a>, best known for his fundamental work in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_cryptography" title="Quantum cryptography">quantum cryptography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quantum_teleportation" title="Quantum teleportation">quantum teleportation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quantum_entanglement" title="Quantum entanglement">quantum entanglement</a> distillation, <a href="/wiki/Quantum_pseudo-telepathy" title="Quantum pseudo-telepathy">quantum pseudo-telepathy</a>, and the classical simulation of quantum entanglement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nserc_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-nserc-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many former students have gained local and national prominence for serving in government including Former Supreme Court of Canada Judge and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.N." title="U.N.">U.N.</a> Human Rights Commissioner <a href="/wiki/Louise_Arbour" title="Louise Arbour">Louise Arbour</a>. <a href="/wiki/Micha%C3%ABlle_Jean" title="Michaëlle Jean">Michaëlle Jean</a> served as <a href="/wiki/Governor_General_of_Canada" title="Governor General of Canada">Governor General of Canada</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ahmed_Benbitour" title="Ahmed Benbitour">Ahmed Benbitour</a>, who served as the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Algeria" title="Prime Minister of Algeria">Prime Minister of Algeria</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Trudeau" title="Pierre Trudeau">Pierre Trudeau</a> served as the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Canada" title="Prime Minister of Canada">Prime Minister of Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Ten <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daniel_Johnson,_Jr." title="Daniel Johnson, Jr.">Daniel Johnson, Jr.</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> <a
