<p><b>Maltese</b> (<strong class="selflink">Maltese</strong>: <span lang="mt" xml:lang="mt"><i>Malti</i></span>) is the <a href="/wiki/National_language" title="National language">national language</a> of <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a> and a co-official <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Malta" title="Languages of Malta">language of the country</a> alongside <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> while also serving as an <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_the_European_Union" title="Languages of the European Union">official language of the</a> <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, the only <a href="/wiki/Semitic_languages" title="Semitic languages">Semitic language</a> so distinguished. Maltese is descended from <a href="/wiki/Siculo-Arabic" title="Siculo-Arabic">Siculo-Arabic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Arabic" title="Varieties of Arabic">variety of Arabic</a> that developed in <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> and was later introduced to Malta, between the end of the ninth century and the end of the twelfth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Maltese is a unique branch of Arabic because it has evolved independently of <a href="/wiki/Modern_Standard_Arabic" title="Modern Standard Arabic">Literary Arabic</a> into a standardized language over the past 800 years in a gradual process of Latinisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> About half of the vocabulary is derived from standard <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">Sicilian</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> words make up between 6% and 20% of the Maltese vocabulary, according to different estimates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-8">[8]</a></sup> The original Semitic base (Siculo-Arabic) comprises around one-third of the Maltese vocabulary, and typically includes words that denote basic ideas and the function words.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Maltese has always been written in the Latin script, the earliest surviving example dating from the late Middle Ages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Malta" title="History of Malta">History of Malta</a></div>
<p>The origins of the Maltese language are attributed to the arrival, early in the eleventh century, of settlers from neighbouring Sicily, where Siculo Arabic was spoken, following the <a href="/wiki/Fatimid_Caliphate" title="Fatimid Caliphate">Fatimid Caliphate</a>'s conquest of the island at the end of the ninth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archive.org_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-archive.org-11">[11]</a></sup> This claim has been corroborated by genetic studies, which show that contemporary <a href="/wiki/Maltese_people" title="Maltese people">Maltese people</a> share common ancestry with Sicilians and Calabrians, with little genetic input from North Africa and the Levant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Norman conquest in 1090, followed by the expulsion of the Muslims (complete by 1249) permanently isolated the vernacular from its Arabic source, creating the conditions for its evolution into a distinct language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archive.org_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-archive.org-11">[11]</a></sup> In contrast to Sicily (where Siculo Arabic became extinct, replaced by <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">Sicilian</a>), the vernacular in Malta continued to develop alongside Italian, eventually replacing it as official language in 1934 (alongside English).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archive.org_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-archive.org-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first written reference to the Maltese language is in a will of 1436, where it is called <i>lingua maltensi</i>. The oldest known document in Maltese is "<a href="/wiki/Il_Cantilena" title="Il Cantilena">Il Cantilena</a>" (<i>Xidew il-Qada</i>) by <a href="/wiki/Pietru_Caxaro" title="Pietru Caxaro">Pietru Caxaro</a>. It dates from the 15th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Academy_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Academy-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The earliest known Maltese dictionary was a sixteenth-century manuscript entitled "Maltese-Italiano"; it was included in the <i>Biblioteca Maltese</i> of Mifsud in 1764, but is now lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agius_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-agius-15">[15]</a></sup> A list of Maltese words was included in both the <i>Thesaurus Polyglottus</i> (1603) and <i>Propugnaculum Europae</i> (1606) of <a href="/wiki/Hieronymus_Megiser" title="Hieronymus Megiser">Hieronymus Megiser</a>, who had visited Malta in 1588–89; Domenico Magri gave the etymologies of some Maltese words in his <i>Hierolexicon, sive sacrum dictionarium</i> (1677).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Academy_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Academy-14">[14]</a></sup> An early manuscript dictionary, <i>Dizionario Italiano e Maltese</i>, was discovered in the <a href="/wiki/Biblioteca_Vallicelliana" title="Biblioteca Vallicelliana">Biblioteca Vallicelliana</a> in Rome in the 1980s, together with a grammar, the <i>Regole per la Lingua Maltese</i>, attributed to a French Knight named Thezan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agius_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-agius-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cassola_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-cassola-16">[16]</a></sup> The first systematic lexicon is that of <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Pietro_Francesco_Agius_de_Soldanis" title="Giovanni Pietro Francesco Agius de Soldanis">Giovanni Pietro Francesco Agius de Soldanis</a>, who also wrote the first systematic grammar of the language and proposed a standard <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agius_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-agius-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Maltese became an <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">official language</a> of Malta in 1934, alongside English, when Italian was dropped from official use.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span></h2>
<p>In 1975, there were an estimated 371,000 Maltese speakers, of whom 300,000 resided in Malta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-e18_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-e18-1">[1]</a></sup> Thousands of Maltese emigrants in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> still speak the language,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-e18_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-e18-1">[1]</a></sup> and in 2007 it was reported that Maltese is still spoken by descendants of Maltese immigrants in <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span></h2>
<p>Maltese is descended from Siculo-Arabic, a Semitic language within the <a href="/wiki/Afroasiatic_languages" title="Afroasiatic languages">Afroasiatic family</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> that in the course of its <a href="/wiki/History_of_Malta" title="History of Malta">history</a> has been influenced by Sicilian and Italian, to a lesser extent <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, and more recently English. Today, the core vocabulary (including both the most commonly used vocabulary and <a href="/wiki/Function_word" title="Function word">function words</a>) is Semitic, with large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanwords</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005-7">[7]</a></sup> Because of the Sicilian influence on Siculo Arabic, Maltese has many language contact features and is most commonly described as a language with a large number of loanwords.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBorg1997_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorg1997-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Maltese language has historically been classified in various ways, with some claiming that the ancient <a href="/wiki/Punic_language" title="Punic language">Punic language</a> (another Semitic language) was its origin instead of Siculo Arabic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Academy_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Academy-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVella2004263_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVella2004263-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> while others believed the language to be one of the <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">Berber languages</a> (another family within Afroasiatic),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Academy_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-Academy-14">[14]</a></sup> and under the Fascist <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy" title="Kingdom of Italy">Kingdom of Italy</a>, it was classified as <a href="/wiki/Regional_Italian" title="Regional Italian">regional Italian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>Voiceless stops are only lightly aspirated and voiced stops are fully voiced. Voicing is carried over from the last segment in <a href="/wiki/Obstruent" title="Obstruent">obstruent</a> <a href="/wiki/Consonant_cluster" title="Consonant cluster">clusters</a>; thus, two- and three-obstruent clusters are either voiceless or voiced throughout, e.g. <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/niktbu/</span> is realised <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈniɡdbu]</span> "we write". Maltese has <a href="/wiki/Final-obstruent_devoicing" title="Final-obstruent devoicing">final-obstruent devoicing</a> of voiced obstruents and voiceless stops have <a href="/wiki/No_audible_release" title="No audible release">no audible release</a>, making voiceless–voiced pairs phonetically indistinguishable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBorg1997249.E2.80.93250_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorg1997249.E2.80.93250-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gemination" title="Gemination">Gemination</a> is distinctive word-medially and word-finally in Maltese. The distinction is most rigid <a href="/wiki/Intervocalic_consonant" title="Intervocalic consonant">intervocalically</a> after a stressed vowel. Stressed, word-final <a href="/wiki/Syllable#open_syllable" title="Syllable">closed syllables</a> with short vowels end in a long consonant, and those with a long vowel in a single consonant; the only exception is where historic <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*ʕ</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*ɣ</span> meant the <a href="/wiki/Compensatory_lengthening" title="Compensatory lengthening">compensatory lengthening</a> of the succeeding vowel. Some speakers have lost length distinction in clusters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBorg1997251.E2.80.93252_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorg1997251.E2.80.93252-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The two nasals <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/m/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/n/</span> assimilate for <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">place of articulation</a> in clusters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBorg1997255_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorg1997255-27">[27]</a></sup> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/d/</span> are usually <a href="/wiki/Dental_stop" title="Dental stop">dental</a>, whereas <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t͡s d͡z s z n r l/</span> are all alveolar. <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t͡s d͡z/</span> are found mostly in words of Italian origin, retaining length (if not word-initial).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBorg1997254_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorg1997254-28">[28]</a></sup> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/d͡z/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʒ/</span> are only found in loanwords, e.g. <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɡad͡zd͡zɛtta/</span> "newspaper" and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tɛlɛˈviʒin/</span> "television".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBorg1997247_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorg1997247-29">[29]</a></sup> The pharyngeal fricative <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ħ/</span> is velar (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">x</a>]</span>) or glottal (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">h</a>]</span>) for some speakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBorg1997260_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorg1997260-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">Vowels</span></h3>
<p>Maltese has five short vowels, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɐ ɛ i ɔ ʊ/</span>, written <i>a e i o u</i>; six long vowels, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɐː ɛː iː ɪː ɔː ʊː/</span>, written <i>a e ie i o u</i>; and seven <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">diphthongs</a>: <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɐɪ ɛɪ/</span> both written <i>għi</i>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɐʊ ɔʊ/</span> both written <i>għu</i>, and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɛʊ ɪʊ ɔɪ/</span> written <i>ew iw oj</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBorgAzzopardi-Alexander1997_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorgAzzopardi-Alexander1997-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stress">Stress</span></h3>
<p>Stress is generally on the penultimate syllable, unless some other syllable is heavy (has a long vowel or final consonant), or unless a stress-shifting suffix is added. (Suffixes marking gender, possession, and verbal plurals do not cause the stress to shift.)</p>
<p>When two syllables are equally heavy, the penultimate takes the stress, but otherwise the heavier syllable does, e.g. <i>bajjad</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈbɐj.jɐt]</span> 'he painted' vs <i>bajjad</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[bɐj.ˈjɐːt]</span> 'a painter'.</p>

<p>Final vowels with grave accents (à, è, ì, ò, ù) are also found in some Maltese words of Italian origin, such as <i>libertà</i> ("freedom"), <i>sigurtà</i> (old Italian: <i>sicurtà</i>, "security"), or <i>soċjetà</i> (Italian: <i>società</i>, "society").</p>
<p>The official rules governing the structure of the Maltese language are found in the official guidebook issued by the <i>Akkademja tal-Malti</i>, the Academy of the Maltese language, which is named <i>Tagħrif fuq il-Kitba Maltija</i>, that is, <i>Knowledge on Writing in Maltese</i>. The first edition of this book was printed in 1924 by the Maltese government's printing press. The rules were further expanded in the 1984 book, <i>iż-Żieda mat-Tagħrif</i>, which focused mainly on the increasing influence of Romance and English words. In 1992 the Academy issued the <i>Aġġornament tat-Tagħrif fuq il-Kitba Maltija</i>, which updated the previous works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> All these works were included in a revised and expanded guidebook published in 1996.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Council_for_the_Maltese_Language" title="National Council for the Maltese Language">National Council for the Maltese Language</a> (KNM) is the main regulator of the Maltese language (see Maltese Language Act, below) and not the <i>Akkademja tal-Malti</i>. However, these orthography rules are still valid and official.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Written_Maltese">Written Maltese</span></h3>
<p>Since Maltese evolved after the <a href="/wiki/Italo-Norman" title="Italo-Norman">Italo-Normans</a> ended the Arab rule of the islands, a written form of the language was not developed for a long time after the Arabs' expulsion in the middle of the thirteenth century. Under the rule of the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Hospitaller" title="Knights Hospitaller">Knights Hospitaller</a>, both French and Italian were used for official documents and correspondence. During the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Colony_of_Malta" title="Crown Colony of Malta">British colonial period</a>, the use of English was encouraged through education, with Italian regarded as the next-most important language.</p>
<p>In 1934, Maltese was recognised as an official language. In the late eighteenth century and throughout the nineteenth century, philologists and academics such as <a href="/wiki/Mikiel_Anton_Vassalli" title="Mikiel Anton Vassalli">Mikiel Anton Vassalli</a> made a concerted effort to standardize written Maltese. Many examples of written Maltese exist from before this period, always in the Latin alphabet, <i>Il Cantilena</i> being the earliest example of written Maltese.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">Vocabulary</span></h2>
<p>Although the original vocabulary of the language was <a href="/wiki/Siculo-Arabic" title="Siculo-Arabic">Siculo-Arabic</a>, it has incorporated a large number of borrowings from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">Romance</a> sources of influence (<a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">Sicilian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>), and more recently <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_language" title="Germanic language">Germanic</a> ones (from <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFriggieri199459_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFriggieri199459-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The historical source of modern Maltese vocabulary is 52% Italian/Sicilian, 32% Siculo-Arabic, and 6% English, with some of the remainder being French.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Today, most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Function_words" title="Function words">function words</a> are Semitic. In this way, it is similar to <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, which is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_language" title="Germanic language">Germanic language</a> that had large influence from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_French" title="Norman French">Norman French</a>. As a result of this, Romance language-speakers may easily be able to comprehend more complex ideas expressed in Maltese, such as "Ġeografikament, l-Ewropa hi parti tas-superkontinent ta' l-Ewrasja" (<i>Geographically, Europe is part of the Supercontinent of Eurasia</i>), while not understanding a single word of a simple sentence such as "Ir-raġel qiegħed fid-dar" (<i>The man is in the house</i>), which would be easily understood by any Arabic speaker.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Romance">Romance</span></h3>
<p>An analysis of the etymology of the 41,000 words in Aquilina's <i>Maltese-English Dictionary</i> shows that words of Romance origin make up 52% of the Maltese vocabulary,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005-7">[7]</a></sup> although other sources claim from as low as 40%,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-8">[8]</a></sup> to as high as 55%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> These vocabularies tend to deal with more complicated concepts. They are mostly derived from <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">Sicilian</a> and thus exhibit Sicilian phonetic characteristics, such as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/u/</span> in place of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/o/</span>, and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/</span> in place of <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e/</span> (e.g. <i>tiatru</i> not <i>teatro</i> and <i>fidi</i> not <i>fede</i>). Also, as with Old Sicilian, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃ/</span> (English 'sh') is written 'x' and this produces spellings such as: <i>ambaxxata</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ambaʃːaːta/</span> ('embassy'), <i>xena</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃeːna/</span> ('scene' cf. Italian <i>ambasciata, scena</i>).</p>

<p>A tendency in modern Maltese is to adopt further influences from English and Italian. Complex Latinate English words adopted into Maltese are often given Italianate or Sicilianate forms,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005-7">[7]</a></sup> even if the resulting words do not appear in either of those languages. For instance, the words "<i>evaluation</i>", "<i>industrial action</i>", and "<i>chemical armaments</i>" become "<i>evalwazzjoni</i>", "<i>azzjoni industrjali</i>", and "<i>armamenti kimiċi</i>" in Maltese, while the Italian terms are <i>valutazione</i>, <i>vertenza sindacale</i>, and <i>armi chimiche</i> respectively. English words of Germanic origin are generally preserved relatively unchanged.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Siculo-Arabic">Siculo-Arabic</span></h3>
<p>Siculo-Arabic is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetics_(linguistics)" title="Genetics (linguistics)">ancestor</a> of the Maltese language,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005-7">[7]</a></sup> and supplies between 32%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005-7">[7]</a></sup> and 40%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-8">[8]</a></sup> of the language's vocabulary.</p>

<p><a href="#CITEREF.C5.BBammit2000">Żammit (2000)</a> found that 40% of a sample of 1,820 <a href="/wiki/Classical_Arabic" title="Classical Arabic">Quranic Arabic</a> roots were found in Maltese, a lower percentage than found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moroccan_Arabic" title="Moroccan Arabic">Moroccan</a> (58%) and <a href="/wiki/Lebanese_Arabic" title="Lebanese Arabic">Lebanese Arabic</a> (72%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.C5.BBammit2000241.E2.80.93245_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.C5.BBammit2000241.E2.80.93245-37">[37]</a></sup> An analysis of the etymology of the 41,000 words in Aquilina's <i>Maltese-English Dictionary</i> shows that 32% of the Maltese vocabulary is of Arabic origin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005-7">[7]</a></sup> although another source claims 40%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Usually, words expressing basic concepts and ideas, such as <i>raġel</i> (man), <i>mara</i> (woman), <i>tifel</i> (boy), <i>dar</i> (house), <i>xemx</i> (sun), <i>sajf</i> (summer), are of Arabic origin. Moreover, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belles_lettres" title="Belles lettres">belles lettres</a> in Maltese tend to aim mainly at diction belonging to this group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isser_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-isser-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Maltese language has merged many of the original Arabic consonants, in particular the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emphatic_consonants" title="Emphatic consonants">emphatic consonants</a>, with others that are common in European languages. Thus, original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/d/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ð/</span>, and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/dˤ/</span> all merged into Maltese <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/d/</span>. The vowels, however, separated from the three in Arabic (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a i u/</span>) to five, as is more typical of other European languages (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a ɛ i o u/</span>). Some unstressed short vowels have been elided. The common Arabic greeting <i>as salāmu 'alaykum</i> is cognate with <i>is-sliem għalikom</i> in Maltese (lit. <i>the peace for you</i>, peace be with you), as are similar greetings in other Semitic languages (e.g. <i>shalom ʿalekhem</i> in <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English">English</span></h3>
<p>It is estimated that English loanwords, which are becoming more commonplace, make up 20% of the Maltese vocabulary,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-8">[8]</a></sup> although other sources claim amounts as low as 6%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005-7">[7]</a></sup> This percentage discrepancy is due to the fact that a number of new English loanwords are sometimes not officially considered part of the Maltese vocabulary; hence, they are not included in certain dictionaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005-7">[7]</a></sup> Also, English loanwards of Latinate origin are very often italianised, as discussed above. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span></h2>
<p>Maltese grammar is fundamentally derived from Siculo-Arabic, although <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance</a> and <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> noun pluralization patterns are also used on borrowed words.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adjectives_and_adverbs">Adjectives and adverbs</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">Adjectives</a> follow <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a>. There are no separately formed native <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">adverbs</a>, and word order is fairly flexible. Both nouns and adjectives of <a href="/wiki/Semitic_languages" title="Semitic languages">Semitic</a> origin take the definite article (for example, <i>It-tifel il-kbir</i>, lit. "The boy the elder"="The elder boy"). This rule does not apply to adjectives of Romance origin.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns">Nouns</span></h3>
<p>Nouns are pluralized and also have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dual_grammatical_number" title="Dual grammatical number">dual</a> marker. Semitic plurals are complex; if they are regular, they are marked by <i>-iet</i>/<i>-ijiet</i>, e.g., <i>art</i>, <i>artijiet</i> "lands (territorial possessions or property)" (cf. Arabic <i>-at</i> and Hebrew <i>-ot</i>/<i>-oth</i>) or <i>-in</i> (cf. Arabic <i>-īn</i> and Hebrew <i>-im</i>). If irregular, they fall in the <i><a href="/wiki/Broken_plural" title="Broken plural">pluralis fractus</a></i> category, in which a word is pluralized by internal vowel changes: <i>ktieb</i>, <i>kotba</i> "books", <i>raġel</i>, <i>irġiel</i> "man", "men".</p>
<p>Words of Romance origin are usually pluralized in two manners: addition of <i>-i</i> or <i>-jiet</i>. For example, <i>lingwa</i>, <i>lingwi</i> "languages", from Sicilian <i>lingua</i>, <i>lingui</i>.</p>
<p>Words of English origin are pluralized by adding either an "-s" or "-jiet", for example, <i>friġġ</i>, <i>friġis</i> from the word <i>fridge</i>. Some words can be pluralized with either of the suffixes to denote the plural. A few words borrowed from English can amalgamate both suffixes, like <i>brikksa</i> from the English <i>brick</i>, which can adopt either collective form <i>brikks</i> or the plural form <i>brikksiet</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Article">Article</span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proclitic" title="Proclitic">proclitic</a> <i>il-</i> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">definite article</a>, equivalent to "the" in English and "al-" in Arabic.</p>
<p>The Maltese article becomes <i>l-</i> before or after a vowel.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>l-omm</i> (the mother)</li>
<li><i>rajna l-Papa</i> (we saw the Pope)</li>
<li><i>il-missier</i> (the father)</li>
</ul>
<p>The Maltese article <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assimilation_(linguistics)" title="Assimilation (linguistics)">assimilates</a> to a following <a href="/wiki/Coronal_consonant" title="Coronal consonant">coronal consonant</a> (called <i>konsonanti xemxin</i> "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sun_letter" title="Sun letter">sun consonants</a>"), namely:</p>
<ul>
<li>Ċ <i>iċ-ċikkulata</i> (the chocolate)</li>
<li>D <i>id-dar</i> (the house)</li>
<li>N <i>in-nar</i> (the fire)</li>
<li>R <i>ir-razzett</i> (the farm)</li>
<li>S <i>is-serrieq</i> (the saw)</li>
<li>T <i>it-tifel</i> (the boy)</li>
<li>X <i>ix-xemx</i> (the sun)</li>
<li>Ż <i>iż-żarbun</i> (the shoe)</li>
<li>Z <i>iz-zalzett</i> (the sausage)</li>
</ul>
<p>Maltese <i>il-</i> is coincidentally identical in pronunciation to the one of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_grammar#Articles" title="Italian grammar">Italian masculine articles</a>, <i>il,</i> which is also <i>l’</i> before, but not after, a vowel. Consequently, many nouns borrowed from Standard Italian did not change their original article when used in Maltese. Romance vocabulary taken from <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">Sicilian</a> did change where the Sicilian articles <i>u</i> and <i>a</i>, before a consonant, are used. In spite of its Romance appearance, <i>il-</i> is related to the Arabic article <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-" title="Al-">al-</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Verbs">Verbs</span></h3>
<p>Verbs show a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triliteral" title="Triliteral">triliteral</a> Semitic pattern, in which a verb is <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation" title="Grammatical conjugation">conjugated</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prefix_(linguistics)" title="Prefix (linguistics)">prefixes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffixes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Infix" title="Infix">infixes</a> (for example <i>ktibna</i>, Arabic <i>katabna</i>, Hebrew <i>kathabhnu</i> (Modern Hebrew: katavnu) "we wrote"). There are two <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">tenses</a>: present and perfect. The Maltese verb system incorporates Romance verbs and adds Maltese suffixes and prefixes to them (for example, <i>iddeċidejna</i> "we decided" ← <i>(i)ddeċieda</i> "decide", a Romance verb + <i>-ejna</i>, a Maltese first person plural perfect marker).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">Dialects</span></h2>
<p>Urban varieties of Maltese are closer to Standard Maltese than rural varieties,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isser_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-isser-39">[39]</a></sup> which have some characteristics that distinguish them from Standard Maltese. They tend to show some archaic features<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isser_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-isser-39">[39]</a></sup> such as the realization of <b>kh</b> and <b>gh</b> and the <a href="/wiki/Im%C4%81la" title="Imāla">imāla</a> of Arabic ā into ē (or ī especially in Gozo)-considered archaic because they are reminiscent of 15th century transcriptions of this sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isser_39-3"><a href="#cite_note-isser-39">[39]</a></sup> Another archaic feature is the realization of Standard Maltese ā as ō in rural dialects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isser_39-4"><a href="#cite_note-isser-39">[39]</a></sup> There is also a tendency to diphthongize simple vowels, e.g., ū becomes eo or eu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isser_39-5"><a href="#cite_note-isser-39">[39]</a></sup> Rural dialects also tend to employ more Semitic roots and broken plurals than Standard Maltese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isser_39-6"><a href="#cite_note-isser-39">[39]</a></sup> In general, rural Maltese is less distant from its Siculo-Arabic ancestor than Standard Maltese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isser_39-7"><a href="#cite_note-isser-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Malta#Media" title="Languages of Malta">Language in the Media in Malta</a></div>
<p>With Malta being a multilingual country, the usage of Maltese in the mass media is shared with other European languages, namely <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>. The majority of television stations broadcast from Malta in English or Maltese, although broadcasts from <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> in Italian are also received on the islands. Similarly, there are more Maltese-language radio programs than English ones broadcast from Malta, but again, as with television, Italian broadcasts are also picked up. Maltese generally receives equal usage in newspaper periodicals to English.</p>
<p>The use of the Maltese language on the Internet is not altogether common, and the number of websites written in Maltese are few. In a survey of Maltese cultural websites conducted in 2004 on behalf of the Maltese Government, 12 of 13 were in English only, while the remaining one was multilingual but did not include Maltese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Code-switching">Code-switching</span></h2>
<p>The Maltese population, being fluent in both Maltese and English, displays <a href="/wiki/Code-switching" title="Code-switching">code-switching</a> (referred to as <a href="/wiki/Maltenglish" title="Maltenglish">Maltenglish</a>) in certain localities and between certain social groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005_7-10"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrincat2005-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
