<p>The <b>Megalithic Temples of Malta</b> (<a href="/wiki/Maltese_language" title="Maltese language">Maltese</a>: <span lang="mt" xml:lang="mt"><i>It-Tempji Megalitiċi ta' Malta</i></span>) are several prehistoric temples, some of which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNESCO_World_Heritage_Sites" title="UNESCO World Heritage Sites">UNESCO World Heritage Sites</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> built during three distinct time periods approximately between 3600 BC and 700 BC on the island country of <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> They have been claimed as the oldest free-standing structures on Earth,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> although the largely buried <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6bekli_Tepe" title="Göbekli Tepe">Göbekli Tepe</a> complex is far older.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Archaeologists believe that these megalithic complexes are the result of local innovations in a process of cultural evolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This led to the building of several temples of the Ġgantija phase (3600–3000 BC), culminating in the large <a href="/wiki/Tarxien_Temples" title="Tarxien Temples">Tarxien temple complex</a>, which remained in use until 2500 BC. After this date, the temple building culture disappeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/%C4%A0gantija" title="Ġgantija">Ġgantija</a> temples (two sites) were listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unesco_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Unesco-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1992, the UNESCO Committee further extended the existing listing to include five other megalithic temple sites. These are <a href="/wiki/%C4%A6a%C4%A1ar_Qim" title="Ħaġar Qim">Ħaġar Qim</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Qrendi" title="Qrendi">Qrendi</a>), <a href="/wiki/Mnajdra" title="Mnajdra">Mnajdra</a> (in Qrendi), <a href="/wiki/Ta%27_%C4%A6a%C4%A1rat_Temples" title="Ta' Ħaġrat Temples">Ta' Ħaġrat Temples</a> (in <a href="/wiki/M%C4%A1arr" title="Mġarr">Mġarr</a>), <a href="/wiki/Skorba_Temples" title="Skorba Temples">Skorba Temples</a> (in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%BBebbieg%C4%A7&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Żebbiegħ (page does not exist)">Żebbiegħ</a>) and Tarxien Temples (in <a href="/wiki/Tarxien" title="Tarxien">Tarxien</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unesco_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Unesco-10">[10]</a></sup> Nowadays, the sites are managed by <a href="/wiki/Heritage_Malta" title="Heritage Malta">Heritage Malta</a>, while ownership of the surrounding lands varies from site to site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Apart from these, there are other megalithic temples in Malta which are not included in the UNESCO World Heritage list.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_Temples_of_Malta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many of the names used to refer to the different sites carry a link with the stones used for their building. The <a href="/wiki/Maltese_language" title="Maltese language">Maltese word</a> for boulders, <i>'ħaġar'</i> is common to Ta’ Ħaġrat and <a href="/wiki/%C4%A6a%C4%A1ar_Qim" title="Ħaġar Qim">Ħaġar Qim</a>. While the former uses the word in conjunction with the marker of possession, the latter adds the word <i>'Qim'</i> , which is either a form of the Maltese word for <i>'worship'</i> or an <a href="/wiki/Archaism" title="Archaism">archaic</a> form of the word meaning <i>'standing'.</i></p>
<p>Maltese <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> describes <a href="/wiki/Giant_(mythology)" title="Giant (mythology)">giants</a> as having built the temples, which led to the name Ġgantija, meaning <i>'Giants’ tower'</i> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The Maltese linguist <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Aquilina" title="Joseph Aquilina">Joseph Aquilina</a> believed that Mnajdra (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: منيدرة) was the diminutive of <i>'mandra'</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: مندرة), meaning a plot of ground planted with cultivated trees (the same usage is in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> colloquial today); however he also named the arbitrary derivation from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> root <i>'manzara'</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: منظرة), meaning 'a place with commanding views.'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The Tarxien temples owe their name to the locality where they were found (from <i>Tirix</i>, meaning a large stone), as were the remains excavated at Skorba.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_Temples_of_Malta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Malta" title="History of Malta">History of Malta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Maltese_history" title="Timeline of Maltese history">Timeline of Maltese history</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dates">Dates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_Temples_of_Malta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Skorba1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1106" data-file-width="1687" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Skorba1.jpg/220px-Skorba1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Skorba1.jpg/330px-Skorba1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Skorba1.jpg/440px-Skorba1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>The temples were the result of several phases of construction, from circa 5000 to 2200 BC; there is evidence of human activity in the islands since the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic#Europe" title="Neolithic">Early Neolithic Period</a> (ca. 5000 BC), testified by pottery shards, charred remains of fires and bones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The dating and understanding of the various phases of activity in the temples is not easy. The main problem found is that the sites themselves are evolutionary in nature, in that each successive temple brought with it further refinement to architectural development.</p>
<p>Furthermore, in some cases, later Bronze-age peoples built their own sites over the Neolithic temples, thus adding an element of confusion to early researchers who did not have modern dating technology. <a href="/wiki/Themistocles_Zammit" title="Themistocles Zammit">Sir Temi Żammit</a>, an eminent Maltese archaeologist of the late nineteenth century, had dated the Neolithic temples to 2800 BC and the Tarxien <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> culture to 2000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> These dates were considered "considerably too high" by scholars,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> who proposed a reduction of half a millennium each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> However, radiocarbon testing favoured Żammit’s dating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A theory that the temple art was connected with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aegean_civilization" title="Aegean civilization">Aegean</a>-derived culture collapsed with this proof of the temples' elder origins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>The development of the chronological phases, based on recalibrated <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a>, has split the period up to the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> in Malta into a number of phases. The first evidence of human habitation in the Neolithic occurred in the Għar Dalam phase, in c. 5000 BC. The Temple period, from c. 4100 BC to roughly 2500 BC, produced the most notable monumental remains. This period is split into five phases,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> however the first two of these left mostly pottery shards. The next three phases, starting from the Ġgantija phase, begins in c. 3600 BC, and the last, the Tarxien phase, ends in c. 2500 BC.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id=".C4.A0gantija_phase_.283600.E2.80.933200_BC.29">Ġgantija phase (3600–3200 BC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_Temples_of_Malta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ġgantija phase (3600–3200 BC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%C4%A0gantija_phase&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ġgantija phase (page does not exist)">Ġgantija phase</a> is named after the <a href="/wiki/%C4%A0gantija" title="Ġgantija">Ġgantija</a> site in <a href="/wiki/Gozo" title="Gozo">Gozo</a>. It represents an important development in the cultural evolution of neolithic man on the islands. To this date belong the earliest datable temples and the first two, if not three, of the stages of development in their ground plan: the lobed or kidney-shaped plan found in <a href="/wiki/M%C4%A1arr" title="Mġarr">Mġarr</a> east, the trefoil plan evident in Skorba, Kordin and various minor sites, and the five-apsed plan Ġgantija South, Tarxien East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Saflieni_phase_.283300.E2.80.933000_BC.29">Saflieni phase (3300–3000 BC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_Temples_of_Malta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Saflieni phase (3300–3000 BC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Saflieni_phase" title="Saflieni phase">Saflieni phase</a> constitutes a transitional phase between two major periods of development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Its name derives from the site of the <a href="/wiki/Hypogeum_of_%C4%A6al-Saflieni" title="Hypogeum of Ħal-Saflieni">Hypogeum of Ħal-Saflieni</a>.This period carried forward the same characteristics of the Ġgantija pottery shapes, but it also introduces new biconical bowls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tarxien_phase_.283150.E2.80.932500_BC.29">Tarxien phase (3150–2500 BC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_Temples_of_Malta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tarxien phase (3150–2500 BC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tarxien_phase" title="Tarxien phase">Tarxien phase</a> marks the peak of the temple civilisation. This phase is named after the temple-complex at Tarxien, a couple of kilometres inland from the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Harbour" title="Grand Harbour">Grand Harbour</a>. To it belong the last two stages in the development of the temple plan. The western temple at Ġgantija represents, along with other units in Tarxien, Ħaġar Qim and L-Imnajdra, the penultimate stage in development, that is, the introduction of a shallow niche instead of an apse at the far end of the temple. The final stage is testified in only one temple, the central unit at Tarxien, with its three symmetrical pairs of apses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The Temple culture reached its climax in this period, both in terms of the craftsmanship of pottery, as well as in sculptural decoration, both free-standing and in relief.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spiral reliefs resembling those at Tarxien once adorned the Ġgantija temples, but have faded to a level where they are only clearly recognisable in a series of drawings made by the artist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_de_Brochtorff&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Charles de Brochtorff (page does not exist)">Charles de Brochtorff</a> in 1829, immediately after the temples’ excavation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The Tarxien phase is characterised by a rich variety of <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a> forms and decorative techniques. Most shapes tend to be angular, with almost no handles or lugs. The <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> tends to be well prepared and fired very hard, while the surface of the scratched ware is also highly polished. This scratched decoration remains standard, but it becomes more elaborate and elegant, the most popular motif being a kind of <a href="/wiki/Volute" title="Volute">volute</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture_and_construction">Architecture and construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_Temples_of_Malta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Architecture and construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kordin_II_Temple_Malta.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="545" data-file-width="526" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Kordin_II_Temple_Malta.png/220px-Kordin_II_Temple_Malta.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Kordin_II_Temple_Malta.png/330px-Kordin_II_Temple_Malta.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Kordin_II_Temple_Malta.png/440px-Kordin_II_Temple_Malta.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kordin_II_Temple_Malta.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Part of the Kordin III Temple site, with a two apse design</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Maltese temple complexes were built in different locations, and over a wide span of years; while each individual site has its unique characteristics, they all share a common architecture. The approach to the temples lies on an oval <a href="/wiki/Forecourt" title="Forecourt">forecourt</a>, levelled by terracing if the terrain is sloping. The forecourt is bounded on one side by the temples’ own <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fa%C3%A7ade" title="Façade">façades</a>, which faces south or south-east. The monuments’ façades and internal walls are made up of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthostat" title="Orthostat">orthostats</a>, a row of large stone slabs laid on end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The centre of the façades is usually interrupted by an entrance doorway forming a <i><a href="/wiki/Trilithon" title="Trilithon">trilithon</a></i>, a pair of orthostats surmounted by a massive <a href="/wiki/Lintel" title="Lintel">lintel</a> slab.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated58_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated58-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Further trilithons form a passage, which is always paved in stone. This in turn opens onto an open space, which then gives way to the next element, a pair of D-shaped chambers, usually referred to as ‘<a href="/wiki/Apse" title="Apse">apses</a>’, opening on both sides of the passage. The space between the apses’ walls and the external boundary wall is usually filled with loose stones and earth, sometimes containing cultural debris including <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a> shards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated71_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated71-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main variation in the temples lies in the number of apses found; this may vary to three, four, five or six. If three, they open directly from the central court in a <a href="/wiki/Trefoil" title="Trefoil">trefoil</a> fashion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In cases of more complex temples, a second axial passage is built, using the same trilithon construction, leading from the first set of apses into another later pair, and either a fifth central or a <a href="/wiki/Niche_(architecture)" title="Niche (architecture)">niche</a> giving the four or five apsial form. In one case, at the Tarxien central temple, the fifth apse or niche is replaced by a further passage, leading to a final pair of apses, making six in all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> With the standard temple plan, found in some thirty temples across the islands, there is a certain amount of variation both in the number of apses, and in the overall length – ranging from 6.5m in the Mnajdra east temple to 23m in the six-apsed Tarxien central temple.</p>
<p>The external walls were usually built of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coralline_limestone" title="Coralline limestone">coralline limestone</a>, which is harder than the globigerina limestone used in the internal sections of the temples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated71_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated71-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The softer globigerina was used for decorative elements within the temples, usually carvings. These features are usually sculpted in relief, and they show a variety of designs linked to vegetative or animal <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbolism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> These usually depict running <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Spiral_motif&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Spiral motif (page does not exist)">spiral motifs</a>, trees and plants as well as a selection of animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Although in their present form the temples are unroofed, a series of unproven theories regarding possible ceiling and roof structures have been debated for several years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_UNESCO_Sites">The UNESCO Sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_Temples_of_Malta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The UNESCO Sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".C4.A0gantija">Ġgantija</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_Temples_of_Malta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ġgantija">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/%C4%A0gantija" title="Ġgantija">Ġgantija</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Temple_de_Ggantija.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1106" data-file-width="1687" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Temple_de_Ggantija.jpg/220px-Temple_de_Ggantija.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Temple_de_Ggantija.jpg/330px-Temple_de_Ggantija.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Temple_de_Ggantija.jpg/440px-Temple_de_Ggantija.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Temple_de_Ggantija.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The megalithic remains at Ġgantija</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Ġgantija temples stand at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Xag%C4%A7ra" title="Xagħra">Xagħra</a> <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">plateau</a>, facing towards the south-east. Its presence was known for a very long time, and even before any excavations were carried out a largely correct plan of its layout was drawn by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Hou%C3%ABl" title="Jean-Pierre Houël">Jean-Pierre Houël</a> in the late eighteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In 1827, the site was cleared of debris – the soil and remains being lost without proper examination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zammayr1_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-zammayr1-43">[43]</a></sup> The loss resulting from this clearance was partially compensated by the German artist Brochtorff, who painted the site within a year or two from the removal of the debris. This is the only practical record of the clearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zammayr1_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-zammayr1-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>A boundary wall encloses the two temples. The southerly one is the elder, and is better preserved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The plan of the temple incorporates five large apses, with traces of the plaster that once covered the irregular wall still clinging between the blocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ta.27_.C4.A6a.C4.A1rat">Ta' Ħaġrat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_Temples_of_Malta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ta' Ħaġrat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ta%27_%C4%A6a%C4%A1rat_Temples" title="Ta' Ħaġrat Temples">Ta' Ħaġrat Temples</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ta%27Hagrat2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Ta%27Hagrat2.jpg/220px-Ta%27Hagrat2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Ta%27Hagrat2.jpg/330px-Ta%27Hagrat2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Ta%27Hagrat2.jpg/440px-Ta%27Hagrat2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ta%27Hagrat2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ta' Ħaġrat</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Ta' Ħaġrat temple in <a href="/wiki/M%C4%A1arr" title="Mġarr">Mġarr</a> is on the eastern outskirts of the village, roughly one kilometer from the <a href="/wiki/Skorba_Temples" title="Skorba Temples">Ta' Skorba temples</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The remains consist of a double temple, made up of two adjacent complexes, both in the shape of a trefoil. The two parts are both less regularly planned and smaller in size than many of the other neolithic temples in Malta, and no blocks are decorated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Sir Temi Żammit excavated the site in 1925–27. A village on the site that pre-dates the temples by centuries has provided plentiful examples of what is now known as <i>Mġarr phase</i> pottery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Skorba">Skorba</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_Temples_of_Malta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Skorba">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Skorba_Temples" title="Skorba Temples">Skorba Temples</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sudika_Mgarr_Skorba_temple.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1365" data-file-width="2048" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Sudika_Mgarr_Skorba_temple.jpg/220px-Sudika_Mgarr_Skorba_temple.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Sudika_Mgarr_Skorba_temple.jpg/330px-Sudika_Mgarr_Skorba_temple.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Sudika_Mgarr_Skorba_temple.jpg/440px-Sudika_Mgarr_Skorba_temple.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sudika_Mgarr_Skorba_temple.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Skorba</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The importance of this site lies less in the remains than in the information garnered from their excavations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> This monument has a typical three-apsed shape of the Ġgantija phase, of which the greater part of the first two apses and the whole of the façade have been destroyed to ground level. What remains are the stone paving of the entrance passage, with its perforations, the <i>torba</i> floors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> and a large upright slab of coralline limestone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The north wall is in better shape; originally the entrance opened on a court, but the doorway was later closed off in the Tarxien phase, with altars set in the corners formed by the closure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> East of this temple, a second monument was added in the Tarxien phase, with four apses and a central niche.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Before the temples were built, the area had supported a village over a period of roughly twelve centuries.</p>
<p>The oldest structure is the eleven metre long straight wall to the west of the temples’ first entrance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrumpSkorba_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-TrumpSkorba-54">[54]</a></sup> The deposit against it contained material from the first known human occupation of the island, the Għar Dalam phase. Among the domestic deposits found in this material, which included charcoal and carbonised grain, there were several fragments of daub, accidentally baked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The charcoal fragments were then radiocarbon dated, and their age analysis stood at 4850 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrumpSkorba_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-TrumpSkorba-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".C4.A6a.C4.A1ar_Qim">Ħaġar Qim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_Temples_of_Malta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ħaġar Qim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/%C4%A6a%C4%A1ar_Qim" title="Ħaġar Qim">Ħaġar Qim</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hagar_qim_temples_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="344" data-file-width="524" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Hagar_qim_temples_2.jpg/220px-Hagar_qim_temples_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Hagar_qim_temples_2.jpg/330px-Hagar_qim_temples_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Hagar_qim_temples_2.jpg/440px-Hagar_qim_temples_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hagar_qim_temples_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The forecourt of Ħaġar Qim temple</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/%C4%A6a%C4%A1ar_Qim" title="Ħaġar Qim">Ħaġar Qim</a> stands on a ridge some two kilometers away from the villages of <a href="/wiki/Qrendi" title="Qrendi">Qrendi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Si%C4%A1%C4%A1iewi" title="Siġġiewi">Siġġiewi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Its builders used the soft <a href="/wiki/Globigerina" title="Globigerina">globigerina</a> limestone that caps the ridge to construct the temple.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> One can clearly see the effects of this choice in the outer southern wall, where the great orthostats are exposed to the sea-winds. Here the temple has suffered from severe weathering and surface flaking over the centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>The temple’s façade is typical, with a trilithon entrance, a bench and orthostats. It has a wide forecourt with a retaining wall, through which a passage runs through the middle of the building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> This entrance passage and first court follow the common, though considerably modified, Maltese megalithic design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> A separate entrance gives access to four enclosures, which are independent of each other and replace the north-westerly apse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mnajdra">Mnajdra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_Temples_of_Malta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Mnajdra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mnajdra" title="Mnajdra">Mnajdra</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Malta_16_Mnajdra.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6096" data-file-width="4188" height="247" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Malta_16_Mnajdra.jpg/170px-Malta_16_Mnajdra.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Malta_16_Mnajdra.jpg/255px-Malta_16_Mnajdra.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Malta_16_Mnajdra.jpg/340px-Malta_16_Mnajdra.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Malta_16_Mnajdra.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A trilithon at <a href="/wiki/Mnajdra" title="Mnajdra">Mnajdra</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>L-Imnajdra temples lies in a hollow 500 metres from Ħaġar Qim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> It is another complex site in its own right, and it is centred on a near circular forecourt. Three adjacent temples overlook it from one side, while a terrace from the other separates it from a steep slope that runs down to the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The first buildings on the right are small irregular chambers, similar to the enclosures in Ħaġar Qim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Then there is a small trefoil temple, dating from the Ġgantija phase, with pitted decorations. Its unusual triple entrance was copied on a larger scale in the second temple.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated58_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated58-31">[31]</a></sup> The middle temple was actually the last to be built, inserted between the others in the Tarxien phase, after 3100 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> It has four apses and a niche.</p>
<p>The third temple, built early in the Tarxien phase and so second in date, opens on the court at a lower level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> It has a markedly concave façade, with a bench, orthostats and trilithon entrance. The southern temple is oriented astronomically aligned with the rising sun during <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">solstices</a> and <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinoxes</a>; during the summer solstice the first rays of sunlight light up the edge of a decorated megalith between the first apses, while during the <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">winter solstice</a> the same effect occurs on a megalith in the opposite apse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> During the equinox, the rays of the rising sun pass straight through the principal doorway to reach the innermost central niche.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tarxien">Tarxien</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalithic_Temples_of_Malta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Tarxien">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tarxien_Temples" title="Tarxien Temples">Tarxien Temples</a></div>
<p>The Tarxien temple complex is found some 400 metres to the east of the <a href="/wiki/Hypogeum_of_%C4%A6al-Saflieni" title="Hypogeum of Ħal-Saflieni">Hypogeum of Ħal-Saflieni</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The three temples found here were seriously excavated in the early twentieth century by Temi Żammit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Unlike the other sites, this temple is bounded on all sides by modern urban development; however, this does not detract from its value. One enters into the first great forecourt of the southern temple, marked by its rounded façade and a cistern, which is attributed to the temple.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The earliest temple to the north-east was built between 3600 and 3200 BC; it consisted of two parallel sets of semi-circular apses, with a passage in the middle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Malta_Hal_Tarxien_BW_2011-10-04_12-41-17.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1112" data-file-width="2536" height="96" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Malta_Hal_Tarxien_BW_2011-10-04_12-41-17.JPG/220px-Malta_Hal_Tarxien_BW_2011-10-04_12-41-17.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Malta_Hal_Tarxien_BW_2011-10-04_12-41-17.JPG/330px-Malta_Hal_Tarxien_BW_2011-10-04_12-41-17.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Malta_Hal_Tarxien_BW_2011-10-04_12-41-17.JPG/440px-Malta_Hal_Tarxien_BW_2011-10-04_12-41-17.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Malta_Hal_Tarxien_BW_2011-10-04_12-41-17.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A carved relief at Tarxien temples</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The south and east temples were built in the Tarxien phase, between 3150 and 2500 BC. The second one has three parallel semi-circular apses, connected by a large passage; the third one has two parallel sets of apses with a passage in a direction parallel to that of the first temple. The first temple is solidly built with large stones, of which some are roughly dressed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> The walls are laid with great accuracy, and are very imposing in their simplicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> The second temple is more elaborately constructed, the walls being finished with greater care, some of the standing slabs being decorated with flat raised spirals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> In one of the chambers, two bulls and a sow are cut in low relief across one of the walls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> The third temple has a carelessly-built frame, but most of its standing stones are richly decorated with carved patterns.</p>

<p>Some of these sites were excavated but afterwards reburied, such as the Debdieba site. Others, such as Kordin I and II, were destroyed. The other temples usually consist of only a few scattered megaliths or remains, but some such as Buġibba Temple (which is now in the grounds of a hotel) are in better condition. A few sites, such as Ta' Marżiena, have never been excavated. Tas-Silġ contains few megalithic remains, but many more Bronze Age and later remains since the site was used until at least the ninth century AD.</p>
<p>Apart from these, cart ruts were found at <a href="/wiki/Misra%C4%A7_G%C4%A7ar_il-Kbir" title="Misraħ Għar il-Kbir">Misraħ Għar il-Kbir</a>, but these may or may not date from the temple period. A submerged site known as <a href="/wiki/%C4%A0ebel_%C4%A1ol-Ba%C4%A7ar" title="Ġebel ġol-Baħar">Ġebel ġol-Baħar</a> possibly exists off the coast of Malta, but it is not proven to be a megalithic temple.</p>
