<p><b>Parc Montsouris</b> is a public park in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/XIVe_arrondissement" title="XIVe arrondissement">14th arrondissement</a> of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, at the southern edge of Paris directly south of the center. Opened in 1869, Parc Montsouris is one of the four large urban public parks, along with the <a href="/wiki/Bois_de_Boulogne" title="Bois de Boulogne">Bois de Boulogne</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bois_de_Vincennes" title="Bois de Vincennes">Bois de Vincennes</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Parc_des_Buttes_Chaumont" title="Parc des Buttes Chaumont">Parc des Buttes Chaumont</a>, created by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Napoleon_III" title="Emperor Napoleon III">Emperor Napoleon III</a> and his Prefect of the Seine, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baron_Haussmann" title="Baron Haussmann">Baron Haussmann</a>, at each of the cardinal points of the compass around the city, in order to provide green space and recreation for the rapidly growing population of Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The park is 15.5 <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectares</a> in area, and is designed as an <a href="/wiki/English_landscape_garden" title="English landscape garden">English landscape garden</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paris.fr_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-paris.fr-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Park contains a lake, a cascade, wide sloping lawns, and many notable varieties of trees, shrubs and flowers. It is also home to a meteorology station, a cafe and a guignol theater. The roads of the park are extremely popular with joggers on weekends.</p>
<p>The park is bounded to the south by Boulevard Jourdan and the <a href="/wiki/Cit%C3%A9_Internationale_Universitaire_de_Paris" title="Cité Internationale Universitaire de Paris">Cité Internationale Universitaire de Paris</a> (CIUP); to the north by Avenue Reille; to the east by Rue Gazan and Rue de la Cité Universitaire; and to the west by Rue Nansouty and Rue Émile Deutsch-de-la-Meurthe.</p>
<p>The "<a href="/wiki/Gare_de_Cit%C3%A9_Universitaire_(Paris_RER)" title="Gare de Cité Universitaire (Paris RER)">Cité Universitaire</a>" stop on the line on the <a href="/wiki/RER_B" title="RER B">RER B</a> is located in the center of Parc Montsouris.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_of_.22Montsouris.22">Etymology of "Montsouris"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parc_Montsouris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title='Edit section: Etymology of "Montsouris"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the official website of the Park and other sources, the name of the park came from an old windmill, called the Moulin de Moque-Souris, which in the 18th century stood not far from the park site at the crossroads of rue d'Alesia and rue de la Tomb-Issoire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Moque-Souris ("mocks-the-mice") was a common name for windmills in France at the time; it was a facetious name, suggesting that the miller dared the mice to find any grain inside. The name over time changed from moque-souris to montsouris. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another possible origin of 'Montsouris' is common with the name of a former principal roadway, today's rue de la Tombe Issoire: after leaving the city to the south, it passed through a Roman-era cemetery that had fallen into disuse from the 4th century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and it may have been one these abandoned tombs that an influential 13th-century writer declared to be the burial place of "Ysoré", a defeated giant of popular legend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> No matter the veracity of the story, many of the area's landmarks had taken the 'tombe Issoire' name by the 18th century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and if 'Issoire' emerged from 'Ysoré', 'Montsouris' could be a 'mont Ysoré' that evolved over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parc_Montsouris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The park was built by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Charles_Alphand" title="Jean-Charles Alphand">Jean-Charles Alphand</a>, the engineer who headed the service of promenades and plantations created by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baron_Haussmann" title="Baron Haussmann">Baron Haussmann</a>, with the assistance of city architect <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Davioud" title="Gabriel Davioud">Gabriel Davioud</a> and horticulturist <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Barillet-Deschamps" title="Jean-Pierre Barillet-Deschamps">Jean-Pierre Barillet-Deschamps</a>. This was the team which together made the Bois de Boulogne, the Bois de Vincennes, and the other great landscape parks of the <a href="/wiki/Second_French_Empire" title="Second French Empire">Second Empire</a>. The project was decided in 1865, but construction did not actually begin until 1867, because of the long negotiations needed to buy the parcels of land needed for the park. The purpose of the park, according to Alphand, was "to bring life and movement to the center of a quarter until then left to isolation and abandon. " <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike the Bois de Boulogne and Bois de Vincennes, the site for the future park did not have any trees or other vegetation. It was largely occupied by a large stone quarry, and to make the work more complicated, it was above a network of tunnels of abandoned mines, which were filled with human skeletons. These tunnels were part of the ossuary of Paris, popularly known as the <a href="/wiki/Catacombs_of_Paris" title="Catacombs of Paris">catacombs of Paris</a>, where the remains of some six million Parisians had been moved at the end of the 18th century. Before construction of the park could begin, some eight hundred skeletons were removed from the tunnels. The work was also complicated by the track of the railroad line which circled Paris, which passed directly through the site.</p>
<p>Despite these difficulties, the work went ahead briskly. A one-hectare artificial lake was dug, fed by an artificial stream that passed over an artificial cascade made of rocks and cement. Stairways were constructed up the hills, with rustic-looking railings made of cement formed to resemble logs. Winding roads and paths were built throughout the park. Davioud designed and built picturesque gatehouses, pavilions, a theater, bandstand and a cafe to fit into the landscape. Barillet-Deschamps planted hundreds of trees and bushes, and laid out sloping lawns and flowerbeds. Every feature of the park was designed to create an idealized natural landscape, with space for both relaxation and recreation, which could be enjoyed by all classes of Parisians. The park was officially dedicated in 1869, but work on the park continued until 1878.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to a park legend, on the day of the park official opening someone made a mistake with the plumbing, and the water in the artificial lake drained away in a single day. According to the legend, the park engineer was so distraught that he committed suicide. It is recorded that the lake did in fact drain accidentally in one day in 1878, but there is no record of a suicide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 1871 <a href="/wiki/Paris_Commune" title="Paris Commune">Paris Commune</a>, the park was the site of a military encampment, and witnessed fighting between the army and the Communards.</p>
<p>In October 1897, the park was the setting of secret meetings between some of the figures involved in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dreyfus_Affair" title="Dreyfus Affair">Dreyfus Affair</a>, including Ferdinand Walsin Esterhazy and Max von Schwartzkoppen.</p>
<p>During World War II, a French soldier, Pierre Durand, was killed by a bomb in the park. A small monument near the lake remembers this event. In 1942, during the German occupation, one of the main monuments of the park, an 1893 allegorical statue of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> by sculptor Auguste Paris, was taken away and melted down for its bronze.</p>
<p>For many decades the most famous structure in the park was the <b>Palais du Bardo</b>, a reduced-size replica of the palace of the Bey of <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a>, made of wood and stucco, which was originally made for the Paris Universal Exposition of 1867. It was relocated to the park by park architect Gabriel Davioud, and placed at the highest point in the park (75 meters above sea level) which is also the highest point in the part of Paris on the left bank of the Seine. For many years it was the home of the meteorological station in the park, but the structure, designed to be temporary, began to crumble. The station moved to a new building, and the Palais suffered from decay and vandalism. In 1991 the ruins were destroyed by a fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Meridian_of_Paris">The Meridian of Paris</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parc_Montsouris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Meridian of Paris">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A stone monument in the Park indicates the location of the Meridian of Paris, an imaginary line that passes from north to south through the center of Paris. This line, first defined by French astronomers in 1667, was used as the zero point for <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> on all French maps until 1884, when France agreed, reluctantly, to use longitudes measured from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenwich_Observatory" title="Greenwich Observatory">Greenwich Observatory</a> near London instead of Paris.</p>
<p>The stone was originally located in the garden of the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Observatory" title="Paris Observatory">Paris Observatory</a>, directly north of the park. The inscription shows it was first put in place in 1806 during the time of the Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I" title="Napoleon I">Napoleon I</a>, though his name was scratched off after the Restoration of the French monarchy. Today it is not exactly on the line of the Paris Meridian, but is about seventy meters east.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trees.2C_plants_and_wildlife">Trees, plants and wildlife</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parc_Montsouris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Trees, plants and wildlife">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main In the lower section of the park, an island in the middle of a tiny lake provides sanctuary to forty species of wild ducks, geese, herons, and other migratory birds. Some turtles imported from Florida, regularly sunbathe on the lake's stony shores.</p>
<p>Common trees in the park include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Horse-Chestnut (<a href="/wiki/Aesculus_hippocastanum" title="Aesculus hippocastanum">Aesculus hippocastanum</a>)</li>
<li>Common Yew (<a href="/wiki/Taxus_baccata" title="Taxus baccata">Taxus baccata</a>)</li>
<li>Cedar (<a href="/wiki/Cedrus" title="Cedrus">Cedrus</a>)</li>
<li>Weeping beech (<a href="/wiki/Fagus_sylvatica" title="Fagus sylvatica">Fagus sylvatica</a> tortuosa)</li>
<li>Buttonwood (<a href="/wiki/Platanus" title="Platanus">Platanus</a>)</li>
</ul>
<p>The rarer species include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Ginkgo (<a href="/wiki/Ginkgo_biloba" title="Ginkgo biloba">Ginkgo biloba</a>)</li>
<li>Silk tree (<a href="/wiki/Albizia_julibrissin" title="Albizia julibrissin">Albizia julibrissin</a>)</li>
<li>Honey locust (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gleditsia_triacanthos" title="Gleditsia triacanthos">Gleditsia triacanthos</a>)</li>
<li>Princess tree (<a href="/wiki/Paulownia_tomentosa" title="Paulownia tomentosa">Paulownia tomentosa</a>)</li>
<li>Pride of India(<a href="/wiki/Koelreuteria" title="Koelreuteria">Koelreuteria</a> bipinnata)</li>
</ul>
<p>The most common shrubs are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Spindle (<a href="/wiki/Euonymus" title="Euonymus">Euonymus</a>)</li>
<li>Mahonia (<a href="/wiki/Mahonia" title="Mahonia">Mahonia</a>)</li>
<li>Boxwood (<a href="/wiki/Buxus" title="Buxus">Buxus</a>)</li>
<li>Aucuba (<a href="/wiki/Aucuba" title="Aucuba">Aucuba</a>)</li>
<li>Viburnum rhytidophyllum (<a href="/wiki/Viburnum" title="Viburnum">Viburnum</a>)</li>
<li>Tinus (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laurustinus" title="Laurustinus">Laurustinus</a> Viburnum)</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_and_Architecture_in_the_park">Art and Architecture in the park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parc_Montsouris&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Art and Architecture in the park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sculptures in bronze and marble include:</p>
<ul>
<li>"Column of Armed Peace" by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jules-Felix_Coutan" title="Jules-Felix Coutan">Jules-Felix Coutan</a> (1889). This column was originally located in the Square d'Anvers. It replaced an 1889 allegorical statue of the French Revolution by Auguste Paris, which was removed and melted down for its metal in 1942 during the German occupation of Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Coutan is known in the United States for his frieze on the facade of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Central_Station" title="Grand Central Station">Grand Central Station</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.</li>
<li>"First thrill" by René Baucour (1921)</li>
<li>"Lion's death" by Edmond Desca (1929)</li>
<li>"Women bathers" by Maurice Lipsi (1952)</li>
<li>"Shipwrecked" by <a href="/wiki/Antoine_%C3%89tex" title="Antoine Étex">Antoine Étex</a> (1859)</li>
<li>"Desert drama" by <a href="/wiki/Georges_Gardet" title="Georges Gardet">Georges Gardet</a> (1891)</li>
<li>"Purity" by <a href="/wiki/Costas_Valsamis" title="Costas Valsamis">Costas Valsamis</a> (1955)</li>
<li>"Mine accident" by <a href="/wiki/Henri_Bouchard" title="Henri Bouchard">Henri Bouchard</a> (1900)</li>
<li>"Monument commemorating Colonel Flatters." This monument commemorates a French expedition of ninety-three men, led by the explorer and military officer Paul Flatters, which was massacred in 1881 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuareg" title="Tuareg">Tuareg</a> tribesmen in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a> of <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>.</li>
<li>"Statue of General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_de_San_Martin" title="José de San Martin">José de San Martin</a>," a monument to the leader of the fight for independence of the states of southern South America. This statue, put in place in 1960, is a replica of the original located in <a href="/wiki/Santiago" title="Santiago">Santiago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, made by Louis-Joseph Daumas.</li>
<li>"<a class="external text" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Thomas_Paine_by_Gutzon_Borglum">Thomas Paine</a>" sculpted by Gutzon Borglum in 1936, dedicated in 1948.</li>
</ul>
