<p><i><b>24: Redemption</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Television_film" title="Television film">television film</a> based on the series <i><a href="/wiki/24_(TV_series)" title="24 (TV series)">24</a></i>. It first aired on November 23, 2008, on <a href="/wiki/Fox_Broadcasting_Company" title="Fox Broadcasting Company">Fox</a> in the United States, and was released to <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> on November 25. The film was written by <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer" title="Executive producer">executive producer</a> <a href="/wiki/Howard_Gordon" title="Howard Gordon">Howard Gordon</a> and was directed by <a href="/wiki/Jon_Cassar" title="Jon Cassar">Jon Cassar</a>. <i>24: Redemption</i> takes place almost four years after sixth season and two months before seventh season in <a href="/wiki/Real_time_(media)" title="Real time (media)">real time</a> between 3:00 pm and 5:00 pm (Sangala timezone) on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inauguration_Day" title="Inauguration Day">Inauguration Day</a> in the United States.</p>
<p>The main setting is Sangala, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fictional_African_countries" title="Fictional African countries">fictional African country</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Jack_Bauer" title="Jack Bauer">Jack Bauer</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kiefer_Sutherland" title="Kiefer Sutherland">Kiefer Sutherland</a>) tries to find peace with himself, and works as a <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a> with Carl Benton (<a href="/wiki/Robert_Carlyle" title="Robert Carlyle">Robert Carlyle</a>), who built the Okavango school to aid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_orphan" title="War orphan">war orphans</a>. Bauer is served a <a href="/wiki/Subpoena" title="Subpoena">subpoena</a> to appear before the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> regarding <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> violations, but refuses to go, and a shadow organization among the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">United States government</a> aids General Juma (<a href="/wiki/Tony_Todd" title="Tony Todd">Tony Todd</a>) and his militia in a <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coup d'état</a>.</p>
<p>The working title was <i>24: Exile</i>. The concept of the film started since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%932008_Writers_Guild_of_America_strike" title="2007–2008 Writers Guild of America strike">2007–2008 Writers Guild of America strike</a>, which delayed the seventh season for a year thus leaving a gap in the series during 2008. <i>Redemption</i> was somewhat inspired by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rwandan_Genocide" title="Rwandan Genocide">Rwandan Genocide</a> in 1994. The majority of <i>Redemption</i> was filmed on location outside <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> since it was difficult to mimic an authentic African scenery in America.</p>
<p>Two versions of the film were released to DVD, the original broadcast version and an extended directors cut. The original airing was seen by just over 12 million Americans, and received positive reviews, and was praised for showing a more human side to Bauer. <i>24: Redemption</i> was nominated for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Globe" title="Golden Globe">Golden Globe</a> as well as five <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Awards</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=24:_Redemption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Redemption</i> begins with a brief prologue showing a young boy being kidnapped at night, indoctrinated, and <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">drafted</a> along with other boys into a rebel <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a> so they could take part in a <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coup d'état</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ignfirstlook_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ignfirstlook-1">[1]</a></sup> which is being funded by a shadow organization led by Jonas Hodges (<a href="/wiki/Jon_Voight" title="Jon Voight">Jon Voight</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-redemption_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-redemption-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Bauer performs <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a> work at the Okavango school in Sangala owned by Carl Benton (<a href="/wiki/Robert_Carlyle" title="Robert Carlyle">Robert Carlyle</a>), U.S. embassy official Frank Trammel (<a href="/wiki/Gil_Bellows" title="Gil Bellows">Gil Bellows</a>) serves him a <a href="/wiki/Subpoena" title="Subpoena">subpoena</a> to appear before the Senate regarding <a href="/wiki/Torture" title="Torture">torture</a> charges, but Bauer refuses to go. Upon hearing the embassy will cut funding to Benton's school if it continues to protect him, he decides to leave. Meanwhile, several children playing <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">soccer</a> are ambushed by Juma's rebel soldiers and kidnapped for <a href="/wiki/Military_use_of_children" title="Military use of children">conscription</a>. When two boys run away, the soldiers open fire, killing one. Benton learns that the rebels are planning to attack his school. He calls Bauer, who hides the children in an underground shelter, and kills several rebels before getting captured and tortured. Benton is able to ambush the remaining soldiers, and Bauer kills the leader, Youssou Dubaku (Zolile Nokwe). His brother, Iké (<a href="/wiki/Hakeem_Kae-Kazim" title="Hakeem Kae-Kazim">Hakeem Kae-Kazim</a>) hears of his death and plots revenge, while Bauer and Benton leave with the children to get to the American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embassy" title="Embassy">embassy</a> in the capital before the final helicopter evacuates the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-redemption_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-redemption-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, Chris Whitley (<a href="/wiki/Kris_Lemche" title="Kris Lemche">Kris Lemche</a>) is ordered by the conspirators who fund Juma's militia to erase all information that would incriminate them. Instead, he calls his friend Roger Taylor (<a href="/wiki/Eric_Lively" title="Eric Lively">Eric Lively</a>), the son of <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President Elect</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allison_Taylor_(24_character)" title="Allison Taylor (24 character)">Allison Taylor</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cherry_Jones" title="Cherry Jones">Cherry Jones</a>), for help. After Roger hears of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conspiracy_(political)" title="Conspiracy (political)">conspiracy</a>, Whitley returns to his home to forward the files, only to be stopped by Hodges's men, Halcott and John Quinn (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sebastian_Roche" title="Sebastian Roche">Sebastian Roche</a>) who take the information, kill Whitley and bury the body in concrete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-redemption_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-redemption-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Benton, Bauer and the boys are spotted by Iké's helicopter. While they flee into the forest, Benton steps on a <a href="/wiki/Land_mine" title="Land mine">land mine</a>. With little time to disarm it, Benton urges Bauer to leave, so he can buy time. When he is surrounded by Iké and his men, Benton takes his foot off the trigger and detonates the mine, killing himself and the rebels, though Iké survives. Bauer and the children continue to the capital, where he defeats another rebel ambush. At the gates of the embassy, Trammel denies the children entry and blackmails Bauer into surrendering for the children's safety. He reluctantly accepts, thus sacrificing his freedom. While Taylor is inaugurated President, Bauer and the children evacuate, leaving chaotic Sangala behind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-redemption_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-redemption-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>While the majority of the characters are introduced, there are still a small number of original cast who appeared in the past, namely <a href="/wiki/Kiefer_Sutherland" title="Kiefer Sutherland">Kiefer Sutherland</a>, who plays <a href="/wiki/Jack_Bauer" title="Jack Bauer">Jack Bauer</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Bob_Gunton" title="Bob Gunton">Bob Gunton</a>, who plays <a href="/wiki/Ethan_Kanin" title="Ethan Kanin">Ethan Kanin</a> from the sixth season and appears as the outgoing <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">Secretary of Defense</a> and new <a href="/wiki/White_House_Chief_of_Staff" title="White House Chief of Staff">White House Chief of Staff</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Powers_Boothe" title="Powers Boothe">Powers Boothe</a> continues to play <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noah_Daniels" title="Noah Daniels">Noah Daniels</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outgoing_president" title="Outgoing president">outgoing president</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_MacNicol" title="Peter MacNicol">Peter MacNicol</a> reprises his role as <a href="/wiki/Tom_Lennox" title="Tom Lennox">Tom Lennox</a> from the sixth season also.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-televisionarycomicon08_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-televisionarycomicon08-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Sangala, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Carlyle" title="Robert Carlyle">Robert Carlyle</a> plays Carl Benton, an old friend of Jack Bauer, who runs a school for rescued war orphans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carlylecast_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-carlylecast-7">[7]</a></sup> Carlyle suggested that there is a very close friendship between Benton and Bauer, since he seems to let Benton come closer to him than others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_of_redemption_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-making_of_redemption-8">[8]</a></sup> Towards the end of the film, he sacrifices himself in order to buy time for Bauer and the children to escape. <a href="/wiki/Gil_Bellows" title="Gil Bellows">Gil Bellows</a> plays Frank Tramell, a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">U.S. State Department</a> official who is ordered to subpoena Bauer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prequelcasting_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-prequelcasting-9">[9]</a></sup> Native South African actor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sean_Michael_(South_African_actor)" title="Sean Michael (South African actor)">Sean Michael</a> plays Charles Solenz, a <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">UN</a> aid worker helping at Benton's school, and later abandons Benton and the children, claiming that the United Nations is "neutral" in the Sangala conflict.</p>
<p>Siyabulela Ramba plays Willie, one of the war orphans under the care of Benton, who befriends Bauer. Ramba felt an emotional connection with Sutherland during filming, and believes their friendship shows through onscreen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_of_redemption_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-making_of_redemption-8">[8]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isaach_De_Bankol%C3%A9" title="Isaach De Bankolé">Isaach De Bankolé</a> plays Ule Matobo, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Minister" title="Prime Minister">Prime Minister</a> of the nation, who is forced to evacuate the country during the coup. Among the antagonists in Sangala are General Benjamin Juma (<a href="/wiki/Tony_Todd" title="Tony Todd">Tony Todd</a>), a former dictator and leader of the People's Freedom Army responsible for <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a> in Sangala,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prequelcasting_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-prequelcasting-9">[9]</a></sup> and Iké Dubaku (<a href="/wiki/Hakeem_Kae-Kazim" title="Hakeem Kae-Kazim">Hakeem Kae-Kazim</a>), one of Juma's lieutenants in the coup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times622_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-times622-10">[10]</a></sup> Zolile Nokwe plays Youssou Dubaku, Iké's younger brother.</p>
<p>In Washington, <a href="/wiki/Cherry_Jones" title="Cherry Jones">Cherry Jones</a> plays <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allison_Taylor_(24_character)" title="Allison Taylor (24 character)">Allison Taylor</a>, the first woman to be inaugurated <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Colm_Feore" title="Colm Feore">Colm Feore</a> plays Allison's <a href="/wiki/Husband" title="Husband">husband</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Gentleman" title="First Gentleman">First Gentleman</a> Henry Taylor. <a href="/wiki/Eric_Lively" title="Eric Lively">Eric Lively</a> plays Roger Taylor, the <a href="/wiki/First_Family_of_the_United_States" title="First Family of the United States">First Son</a> and son of Taylor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prequelcasting_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-prequelcasting-9">[9]</a></sup> and appears alongside <a href="/wiki/Carly_Pope" title="Carly Pope">Carly Pope</a> as his <a href="/wiki/Girlfriend" title="Girlfriend">girlfriend</a>, Samantha Roth, and <a href="/wiki/Kris_Lemche" title="Kris Lemche">Kris Lemche</a>, who plays friend Chris Whitley. Among the antagonists in Washington are <a href="/wiki/Jon_Voight" title="Jon Voight">Jon Voight</a>, who plays Jonas Hodges, a "very serious villain" involved in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackwater_Worldwide" title="Blackwater Worldwide">Blackwater</a>-type organization,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eonline_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-eonline-12">[12]</a></sup> as well as <a href="/wiki/Mark_Kiely" title="Mark Kiely">Mark Kiely</a>, who plays <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secret_Service" title="United States Secret Service">Secret Service</a> Agent Edward Vossler.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=24:_Redemption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greater_Cape_Town.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Greater_Cape_Town.jpg/220px-Greater_Cape_Town.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Greater_Cape_Town.jpg/330px-Greater_Cape_Town.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Greater_Cape_Town.jpg/440px-Greater_Cape_Town.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Greater_Cape_Town.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The majority of <i>Redemption</i> was filmed on location around <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>, South Africa.</div>
</div>

<p>The film was conceived as a result of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%932008_Writers_Guild_of_America_strike" title="2007–2008 Writers Guild of America strike">2007–2008 Writers Guild of America strike</a>, which delayed the seventh season for an entire year; the producers were exploring ways to get back on the air earlier. Ideas included airing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Webisodes" title="Webisodes">webisodes</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobisodes" title="Mobisodes">mobisodes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-televisionarycomicon08_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-televisionarycomicon08-6">[6]</a></sup> According to the President of Entertainment at <a href="/wiki/Fox_Broadcasting_Company" title="Fox Broadcasting Company">Fox</a>, "We were going to be off the air for a while and how do you bridge that gap. We thought about doing something online and after talking with [executive producer] Howard Gordon we decided to do it on air."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iFMagazineOnline_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-iFMagazineOnline-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Working_title" title="Working title">working title</a> for the film was <i>24: Exile</i>, until the change to <i>24: Redemption</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an interview, <a href="/wiki/Kiefer_Sutherland" title="Kiefer Sutherland">Kiefer Sutherland</a> revealed that <i>Redemption</i> was inspired by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rwandan_Genocide" title="Rwandan Genocide">Rwandan Genocide</a> and the U.S. Government's response to it. "The truth is, one of the things that was said in the prequel, which I think has been a massive problem with how the Western world has dealt with Africa, no one can justify going there, because they have no viable reason – meaning oil or money. Here [the president's] response is a human one. We can stop a genocide. I think that that's something that <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> apologized for not doing with <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a> and we centered a show around that."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 30, producers began scouting locations in Africa in order to film the feature in the upcoming weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KieferUpfronts_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-KieferUpfronts-17">[17]</a></sup> The original plan was to shoot three days worth of scenes in Africa and then have <a href="/wiki/Simi_Valley,_California" title="Simi Valley, California">Simi Valley, California</a> fill in for the location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-televisionarycomicon08_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-televisionarycomicon08-6">[6]</a></sup> After realizing it would be difficult to fake, it was decided to shoot the majority of the film on location in <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>, South Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buddytvcomicon08_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-buddytvcomicon08-18">[18]</a></sup> Filming took place from June 4 to June 20 with the final scenes being shot in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times622_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-times622-10">[10]</a></sup> By July 13, principal shooting of the film had been completed and <a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">post-production</a> had begun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cassarwebsite_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-cassarwebsite-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=24:_Redemption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_viewership">Release and viewership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=24:_Redemption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Release and viewership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before release of <i>Redemption</i>, an exclusive six-minute clip was shown to fans at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego_Comic-Con_International" title="San Diego Comic-Con International">San Diego Comic-Con, 2008</a>, which showed Bauer escorting a group of African children to the U.S. embassy and becomes involved in a gunfight on a crowded street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-televisionarycomicon08_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-televisionarycomicon08-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buddytvcomicon08_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-buddytvcomicon08-18">[18]</a></sup> On September 21, 2008, another trailer was soon uploaded by Fox, focusing more on the conflict in Africa and how Jonas Hodges is involved in the conflict. It premiered on November 23, 2008 in the U.S. with the DVD released two days later. Fox also released an extended creator's cut of the feature. Other extras include an <a href="/wiki/Audio_commentary" title="Audio commentary">audio commentary</a>, "Making of" and "Children of War" <a href="/wiki/Featurette" title="Featurette">featurettes</a>, a brief summary of the sixth season, an alternate Season 7 trailer featuring scenes from the first thirteen episodes, and the first sixteen minutes of the Season 7 premiere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvdrelease_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-dvdrelease-20">[20]</a></sup> In the United Kingdom, the film premiered on November 24 on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sky1" title="Sky1">Sky1</a>, one day after first airing in America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> It was later released in the UK on December 1, 2008 with both the original and extended versions on DVD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In Australia, <i>Redemption</i> aired on November 24, 2008 on the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Network" title="Seven Network">Seven Network</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The DVD was later released on February 11, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Redemption</i> was placed the second most viewed <a href="/wiki/Fox_Broadcasting_Company" title="Fox Broadcasting Company">Fox</a> release on the week it aired, behind <i><a href="/wiki/House_(TV_series)" title="House (TV series)">House</a></i>; <i>Redemption</i> drew a total of 12.121 million viewers, with a <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_ratings" title="Nielsen ratings">Nielsen rating</a> of 7.0,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> 4.0/9 among adults aged 18–49. <i>Redemption</i> was the highest-rated scripted program of the night, and was 3rd overall for the 8:00 pm to 11:00 pm time slot, behind the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Music_Awards" title="American Music Awards">American Music Awards</a> and <a href="/wiki/NBC_Sunday_Night_Football" title="NBC Sunday Night Football">Sunday Night Football</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> It aired on <a href="/wiki/Sky_1" title="Sky 1">Sky 1</a> in the UK, and received an average of 405,000 viewers, nearly triple the regular amount of viewers for the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gets405000_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-gets405000-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p>The film garnered positive reviews from film critics. It holds a 100% approval rating on <a href="/wiki/Review_aggregator" title="Review aggregator">review aggregator</a> website <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, based on 8 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Alessandra Stanley of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> gave the film a positive review, stating that Redemption is "a more sober, stripped-down version of <i>24</i>, and that’s refreshing," and that it was the first time a terrorist threat did not take place in Southern California, and the depiction of the conflict in Sangala is highly realistic and compelling. Stanley also said that a two-hour version of <i>24</i> proves that downsizing isn't always a bad thing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Mary McNamara of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Los_Angeles_Times" title="The Los Angeles Times">The Los Angeles Times</a></i> praised the film, acknowledging the producers taking on board the criticism and moral issues regarding the use of torture, and stated that a group of innocent young children running for their lives "instantly ratchets up a story's emotional level."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brian Zoromski of <i><a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a></i> rated it "good", 7.5 out of a possible 10. Zoromski stated that the purpose of <i>Redemption</i> was to allow the introduction of new characters for the seventh season. He further praised the action scenes involving Bauer on his own, stating it as a "sort of <a href="/wiki/Die_Hard" title="Die Hard">Die Hard</a> in Africa," and called the change of setting from Los Angeles a "refreshing change of pace". However, Zormski criticized the film for containing cludge dialogue and bland scenes, but suggested that it makes up for it with the action scenes and emotional scenes involving Bauer, allowing Sutherland to show his acting range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Oscar Dah; of <i><a href="/wiki/BuddyTV" title="BuddyTV">BuddyTV</a></i> stated that the film should make up for the critics' poor outing of the sixth season, and has said that the film works wonderfully as the start of the seventh season, and praised Robert Carlyle's performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, some reviews had mixed responses. Simon Brew of <i>Den of Geek</i> rated <i>Redemption</i> 3 out of 5 stars, praising it for showing a more human side of Bauer, the acting of <a href="/wiki/Cherry_Jones" title="Cherry Jones">Cherry Jones</a> as Allison Taylor, and named <i>Redemption</i> as a solid bridge between season six and seven. However, Brew was critical that it plays little to no havoc with the world of <i>24</i>, and that it is lacking at times in urgency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Gerard Gilbert of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i> stated that the introduction of Jonas Hodges was promising, and praised John Voight's acting, as well as stating that the character is the one most to look forward to in the seventh season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Matthew Gilbert of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i> gave the film a negative review, giving it a 2 out of 10, calling it "un-fun", and stating that Bauer's character has not changed at all from past seasons, and asking, "When does a rogue hero [Bauer] become a tired joke? Based on this un-fun movie, I would say yesterday."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_nominations">Awards and nominations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=24:_Redemption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Awards and nominations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In total, the film itself and its lead actor, <a href="/wiki/Kiefer_Sutherland" title="Kiefer Sutherland">Kiefer Sutherland</a>, were nominated for several awards, but none of them resulted in a win. Sutherland was nominated for this performance as "Best Actor in a Miniseries or TV Film" at the <a href="/wiki/66th_Golden_Globe_Awards" title="66th Golden Globe Awards">66th Golden Globe Awards</a>, an award which was won by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Giamatti" title="Paul Giamatti">Paul Giamatti</a> for his performance in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Adams_(TV_miniseries)" title="John Adams (TV miniseries)">John Adams</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The TV film was also nominated with five <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award" title="Primetime Emmy Award">Primetime Emmys</a>, including four <a href="/wiki/Creative_Arts_Emmy_Award" title="Creative Arts Emmy Award">Creative Arts Primetime Emmys</a> in its 61st ceremony. Sutherland was again nominated for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Lead_Actor_in_a_Miniseries_or_a_Movie" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actor in a Miniseries or a Movie">Outstanding Lead Actor in a Miniseries or Movie</a> for his role as Jack Bauer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emmynoms_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-emmynoms-38">[38]</a></sup> but lost to <a href="/wiki/Brendan_Gleeson" title="Brendan Gleeson">Brendan Gleeson</a> for his role as <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Into_the_Storm_(2009_film)" title="Into the Storm (2009 film)">Into the Storm</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sean_Callery" title="Sean Callery">Sean Callery</a> was nominated for "Outstanding Music Composition for a Miniseries, Movie or a Special (Original Dramatic Score)",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emmynoms_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-emmynoms-38">[38]</a></sup> but lost to <i>Into the Storm</i> composer <a href="/wiki/Howard_Goodall" title="Howard Goodall">Howard Goodall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emmywins2_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-emmywins2-40">[40]</a></sup> Scott Powell was nominated for "Outstanding Single-Camera Picture Editing for a Miniseries or a Movie",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emmynoms_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-emmynoms-38">[38]</a></sup> which was won by Lee Percy and Brian A. Kates for <i><a href="/wiki/Taking_Chance" title="Taking Chance">Taking Chance</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emmywins2_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-emmywins2-40">[40]</a></sup> William D. Dotson, Catherine M. Speakman, Jeffrey R. Whitcher, Pembrooke Andrews, Shawn Kennelly, Daryl Fontenault, Melissa Kennelly, Jeffrey Charboneau, Laura Macias and Vincent Nicastro were nominated for "Outstanding Sound Editing for a Miniseries, Movie or a Special",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emmynoms_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-emmynoms-38">[38]</a></sup> but lost to the crew of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generation_Kill_(TV_series)" title="Generation Kill (TV series)">Generation Kill</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emmywins2_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-emmywins2-40">[40]</a></sup> Lastly, William Gocke, Colin McFarlane, Michael Olman and Kenneth Kobett were nominated for "Outstanding Sound Mixing for a Miniseries or a Movie",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emmynoms_38-4"><a href="#cite_note-emmynoms-38">[38]</a></sup> which was won again by the crew of <i>Generation Kill</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emmywins2_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-emmywins2-40">[40]</a></sup> Sutherland was also nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild_Award_for_Outstanding_Performance_by_a_Male_Actor_in_a_Miniseries_or_Television_Movie" title="Screen Actors Guild Award for Outstanding Performance by a Male Actor in a Miniseries or Television Movie">Screen Actors Guild Award for Outstanding Performance by a Male Actor in a Miniseries or Television Movie</a> but once again lost to Paul Giamatti in <i>John Adams</i>. <i>Redemption</i> was also nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Satellite_Award_for_Best_Television_Film" title="Satellite Award for Best Television Film">Satellite Award for Best Television Film</a> but lost to the BBC docudrama <i><a href="/wiki/Filth:_The_Mary_Whitehouse_Story" title="Filth: The Mary Whitehouse Story">Filth: The Mary Whitehouse Story</a></i>.</p>
