<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montauban&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Montauban is the second oldest (after <a href="/wiki/Mont-de-Marsan" title="Mont-de-Marsan">Mont-de-Marsan</a>) of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bastides" title="Bastides">bastides</a></i> of southern France. Its foundation dates from 1144 when Count <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alphonse_I_of_Toulouse" title="Alphonse I of Toulouse">Alphonse Jourdain</a> of <a href="/wiki/County_of_Toulouse" title="County of Toulouse">Toulouse</a>, granted it a liberal charter. The inhabitants were drawn chiefly from Montauriol, a village which had grown up around the neighbouring monastery of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Th%C3%A9odard" title="St Th√©odard">St Th√©odard</a>.</p>
<p>In the 13th century the town suffered much from the ravages of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albigensian_war" title="Albigensian war">Albigensian war</a> and from the <a href="/wiki/Inquisition" title="Inquisition">Inquisition</a>, but by 1317 it had recovered sufficiently to be chosen by <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXII" title="Pope John XXII">John XXII</a> as the head of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Montauban" title="Diocese of Montauban">diocese</a> of which the basilica of St Th√©odard became <a href="/wiki/Montauban_Cathedral" title="Montauban Cathedral">the cathedral</a>.</p>

<p>In 1360, under the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Br%C3%A9tigny" title="Treaty of Br√©tigny">Treaty of Br√©tigny</a>, it was ceded to the English; they were expelled by the inhabitants in 1414. In 1560 the bishops and magistrates embraced Protestantism, expelled the monks, and demolished the cathedral. Ten years later it became one of the four <a href="/wiki/Huguenot" title="Huguenot">Huguenot</a> strongholds under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peace_of_Saint-Germain" title="Peace of Saint-Germain">Peace of Saint-Germain</a>, and formed a small independent republic. It was the headquarters of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huguenot_rebellion" title="Huguenot rebellion">Huguenot rebellion</a> of 1621, and successfully withstood an 86-day siege by <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIII_of_France" title="Louis XIII of France">Louis XIII</a>. It did not submit to royal authority until after the fall of <a href="/wiki/La_Rochelle" title="La Rochelle">La Rochelle</a> in 1629, when its <a href="/wiki/Fortification" title="Fortification">fortifications</a> were destroyed by <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Richelieu" title="Cardinal Richelieu">Cardinal Richelieu</a>. The Protestants again suffered persecution after the revocation of the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Nantes" title="Edict of Nantes">Edict of Nantes</a> in 1685.</p>
<p>In the 17th century, the King of France revoked ‚Äúl‚Äô√âdit de Nantes‚Äù. Montauban was considered as an intellectual city. Because Montauban was a Protestant town, it started to resist and hold its position against the royal power and it refused to give allegiance to the Catholic King. To scare off the King‚Äôs opponents and speed up the end of the siege, 400 cannonballs were fired, but Montauban resisted and the royal army was vanquished. Saint Jacques church is still marked by the cannonballs, and every year in September, the city celebrates ‚Äúles 400 coups‚Äù (the 400 shots), which has become a common phrase in French.</p>
<p>During World War II, Leonardo da Vinci's <a href="/wiki/Mona_Lisa" title="Mona Lisa">Mona Lisa</a> was briefly hidden in a secret vault behind a wine cellar at Montauban.</p>

<p>Montauban‚Äôs climate is temperate and oceanic. Temperatures are rather mild in winter and hot in summer. The town experienced severe droughts in 2003, 2006, 2012 and 2015. On August 31, 2015, the Tarn-et-Garonne area was particularly struck by a wave of violent storms. These storms, accompanied by very important winds, created a tornado, which caused considerable damage in a large part of the department. Montauban was particularly affected, with winds measured between 130 and 150 kilometers per hour (a record) in the city center.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sights">Sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montauban&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Its fortifications have been replaced by boulevards beyond which extend numerous suburbs, while on the left bank of the Tarn is the suburb of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Villebourbon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Villebourbon (page does not exist)">Villebourbon</a>, which is connected to the town by a remarkable bridge of the early 14th century. This bridge is known as <i>Pont Vieux</i> (i.e. "Old Bridge"). King <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_France" title="Philip IV of France">Philip the Fair</a> of France officially launched the building of the bridge in 1303 while on a tour to <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">Toulouse</a>. The project took 30 years to complete, and the bridge was inaugurated in 1335. The main architects were <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%89tienne_de_Ferri%C3%A8res&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="√âtienne de Ferri√®res (page does not exist)">√âtienne de Ferri√®res</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mathieu_de_Verdun&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mathieu de Verdun (page does not exist)">Mathieu de Verdun</a>. It is a pink brick structure over 205 metres (224 yards) in length, but while its fortified towers have disappeared, it is otherwise in a good state of preservation. The bridge was designed to resist the violent floods of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tarn_River" title="Tarn River">Tarn River</a>, and indeed it successfully withstood the two terrible millennial floods of 1441 and 1930. The bridge is a straight level bridge, which is quite unusual for Medieval Europe, where lack of technological skills meant that most bridges were of the humpback type.</p>

<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_Ingres" title="Mus√©e Ingres">Mus√©e Ingres</a></i>, on the site of a castle of the Counts of Toulouse and once the residence of the bishops of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Montauban" title="Bishopric of Montauban">Montauban</a>, stands at the east end of the bridge. It belongs chiefly to the 17th century, but some portions are much older, notably an underground chamber known as the Hall of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Prince" title="Black Prince">Black Prince</a> (<i>Salle du Prince Noir</i>). It comprises most of the work (including his "Jesus among the Teachers of the Law") of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean_Auguste_Dominique_Ingres" title="Jean Auguste Dominique Ingres">Jean Ingres</a>, the celebrated painter, whose birth in Montauban is commemorated by an elaborate monument. It is the largest museum of Ingres paintings in the world. The museum also contains some sculptures by famous sculptor <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Bourdelle" title="Antoine Bourdelle">Antoine Bourdelle</a>, another native of Montauban, as well as collections of antiquities (Greek vases) and 18th and 19th <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">ceramics</a>.</p>
<p>The <i>Place Nationale</i> is a square of the 17th century, entered at each corner by gateways giving access to a large open space surrounded by pink brick houses supported by double rows of arcades.</p>
<p>The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pr%C3%A9fecture" title="Pr√©fecture">pr√©fecture</a></i> is located in the palace built by the <i>intendant</i> of Montauban (the equivalent of a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pr%C3%A9fet" title="Pr√©fet">pr√©fet</a></i> before the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>), and is a large elegant 18th century mansion, built of pink bricks and white stone, with a steep roof of blue gray <a href="/wiki/Slate" title="Slate">slates</a>, in a style combining northern and southern French styles of architecture.</p>
<p>The chief churches of Montauban are <a href="/wiki/Montauban_Cathedral" title="Montauban Cathedral">the cathedral</a>, remarkable only for the possession of the "Vow of Louis XIII", one of the masterpieces of Ingres, and the church of St Jacques (14th and 15th centuries), dedicated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_James_the_Great" title="Saint James the Great">Saint James of Compostela</a>, the fa√ßade of which is surmounted by a handsome octagonal tower, the base of which is in <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a> style, while the upper levels, built later, are in <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> style. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montauban&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The commercial importance of Montauban is due rather to its trade in agricultural produce, horses, game and poultry, than to its industries, which include nursery-gardening, cloth-weaving, cloth-dressing, flour-milling, wood-sawing, and the manufacture of furniture, silk-gauze and straw hats.</p>
<p>However, due to the proximity of Toulouse and the cheaper cost of industrial grounds, more and more mechanical products are being manufactured there.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montauban&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town is a railway junction, and the station <a href="/wiki/Gare_de_Montauban-Ville-Bourbon" title="Gare de Montauban-Ville-Bourbon">Gare de Montauban-Ville-Bourbon</a> offers connections with Toulouse, Bordeaux, Paris, Brive-la-Gaillarde, Marseille and several regional destinations.
