<p><b>Saint Louis-Marie Grignion de Montfort</b> (31 January 1673 – 28 April 1716) was a French Roman Catholic priest and <a href="/wiki/Confessor" title="Confessor">Confessor</a>. He was known in his time as a preacher and was made a missionary apostolic by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_XI" title="Pope Clement XI">Pope Clement XI</a>.</p>
<p>As well as preaching, Montfort found time to write a number of books which went on to become classic Catholic titles and influenced several popes. Montfort is known for his particular devotion to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blessed_Virgin_Mary" title="Blessed Virgin Mary">Blessed Virgin Mary</a> and the practice of praying the <a href="/wiki/Rosary" title="Rosary">Rosary</a>.</p>
<p>Montfort is considered as one of the early writers in the field of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Mariology" title="Roman Catholic Mariology">Mariology</a>. His most notable works regarding <a href="/wiki/Marian_devotions" title="Marian devotions">Marian devotions</a> are contained in <i>The <a href="/wiki/Secret_of_Mary" title="Secret of Mary">Secret of Mary</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/True_Devotion_to_Mary" title="True Devotion to Mary">True Devotion to Mary</a></i>.</p>
<p>The Roman Catholic Church, under the pontificate of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XII" title="Pope Pius XII">Pope Pius XII</a> canonized Montfort on July 20, 1947.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vatican.va_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-vatican.va-1">[1]</a></sup> A "founders statue" created by Giacomo Parisini is located in an upper niche of the south nave of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Peter%27s_Basilica" title="Saint Peter's Basilica">Saint Peter's Basilica</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_de_Montfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Montfort-sur-Meu_(35)_Maison_natale_de_Saint-Louis-Marie-Grignon-de-Montfort.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="596" data-file-width="794" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Montfort-sur-Meu_%2835%29_Maison_natale_de_Saint-Louis-Marie-Grignon-de-Montfort.jpg/220px-Montfort-sur-Meu_%2835%29_Maison_natale_de_Saint-Louis-Marie-Grignon-de-Montfort.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Montfort-sur-Meu_%2835%29_Maison_natale_de_Saint-Louis-Marie-Grignon-de-Montfort.jpg/330px-Montfort-sur-Meu_%2835%29_Maison_natale_de_Saint-Louis-Marie-Grignon-de-Montfort.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Montfort-sur-Meu_%2835%29_Maison_natale_de_Saint-Louis-Marie-Grignon-de-Montfort.jpg/440px-Montfort-sur-Meu_%2835%29_Maison_natale_de_Saint-Louis-Marie-Grignon-de-Montfort.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Montfort-sur-Meu_(35)_Maison_natale_de_Saint-Louis-Marie-Grignon-de-Montfort.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Montfort's birthplace in <i>Montfort-sur-Meu</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>He was born in <a href="/wiki/Montfort-sur-Meu" title="Montfort-sur-Meu">Montfort-sur-Meu</a>, the eldest surviving child of eighteen born to Jean-Baptiste and Jeanne Robert Grignion. His father was a notary. Louis-Marie passed most of his infancy and early childhood in <a href="/wiki/Iffendic" title="Iffendic">Iffendic</a>, a few kilometers from Montfort, where his father had bought a farm. At the age of 12, he entered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit" title="Jesuit">Jesuit</a> College of St Thomas Becket in <a href="/wiki/Rennes" title="Rennes">Rennes</a>, where his uncle was a parish priest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poulain_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-poulain-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the end of his ordinary schooling, he began his studies of philosophy and theology, still at St Thomas in Rennes. Listening to the stories of a local priest, the Abbé Julien Bellier, about his life as an itinerant missionary, he was inspired to preach missions among the very poor. And, under the guidance of some other priests he began to develop his strong devotion to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blessed_Virgin_Mary" title="Blessed Virgin Mary">Blessed Virgin Mary</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Francois-Etienne_Villeret_St_Sulpice_Paris.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="456" data-file-width="646" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Francois-Etienne_Villeret_St_Sulpice_Paris.jpg/220px-Francois-Etienne_Villeret_St_Sulpice_Paris.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Francois-Etienne_Villeret_St_Sulpice_Paris.jpg/330px-Francois-Etienne_Villeret_St_Sulpice_Paris.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Francois-Etienne_Villeret_St_Sulpice_Paris.jpg/440px-Francois-Etienne_Villeret_St_Sulpice_Paris.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Francois-Etienne_Villeret_St_Sulpice_Paris.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
19th century depiction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint-Sulpice,_Paris" title="Saint-Sulpice, Paris">St Sulpice</a> where Montfort had earlier studied for the priesthood</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>He was then given the opportunity, through a benefactor, to go to Paris to study at the renowned Seminary of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint-Sulpice_(Paris)" title="Saint-Sulpice (Paris)">Saint-Sulpice</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foley_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foley-3">[3]</a></sup> towards the end of 1693. When he arrived in Paris, it was to find that his benefactor had not provided enough money for him, so he lodged in a succession of boarding houses, living among the very poor, in the meantime attending the Sorbonne University for lectures in theology. After less than two years, he became very ill and had to be hospitalized, and survived his hospitalization and the blood letting that was part of his treatment at the time.</p>
<p>Upon his release from the hospital, to his surprise he found himself with a place reserved at the Little Saint-Sulpice, which he entered in July 1695. Saint-Sulpice had been founded by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Olier" title="Jean-Jacques Olier">Jean-Jacques Olier</a>, one of the leading exponents of what came to be known as the <a href="/wiki/French_school_of_spirituality" title="French school of spirituality">French school of spirituality</a>. Given that he was appointed the librarian, his time at Saint-Sulpice gave him the opportunity to study most of the available works on spirituality and, in particular, on the Virgin Mary's place in the Christian life. This later led to his focus on the Holy <a href="/wiki/Rosary" title="Rosary">Rosary</a> and his acclaimed book the <a href="/wiki/Secret_of_the_Rosary" title="Secret of the Rosary">Secret of the Rosary</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Devotion_to_the_angels">Devotion to the angels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_de_Montfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Devotion to the angels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Even as a seminarian in Paris, Montfort was known for the veneration he had toward the angels: he "urged his confreres to show marks of respect and tenderness to their guardian angels." He often ended his letters with a salutation to the guardian angel of the person to whom he was writing: "I salute your guardian angel". He also saluted all the angels in the city of Nantes, a custom that, it appears, he repeated when he entered a new village or city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-angels_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-angels-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the reasons why Saint Louis Marie de Montfort had such devotion to the angels is that veneration of the pure spirits was an integral part of his training and also of his culture. His college teachers, the Jesuits, were known for their zeal in propagating devotion to the angels. Montfort's seminary training under the Sulpicians brought him into contact with the thought of <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_B%C3%A9rulle" title="Pierre de Bérulle">Cardinal de Bérulle</a> and Olier, both of whom had deep veneration for the angels. Furthermore, in the course of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, manuals of piety and treatises on the pure spirits were numerous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-angels_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-angels-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="From_priest_to_preacher">From priest to preacher</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_de_Montfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: From priest to preacher">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was ordained a priest in June 1700,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foley_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Foley-3">[3]</a></sup> and assigned to <a href="/wiki/Nantes" title="Nantes">Nantes</a>. His great desire was to go to the foreign missions, preferably to the new French colony of Canada, but his spiritual director advised against it. His letters of this period show that he felt frustrated from the lack of opportunity to preach as he felt he was called to do.</p>
<p>In November 1700 he joined the Third Order of the Dominicans and asked permission not only to preach the rosary, but to also form rosary confraternities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He began to consider the formation of a small company of priests to preach missions and retreats under the standard and protection of the Blessed Virgin. This eventually led to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Company_of_Mary" title="Company of Mary">Company of Mary</a>. At around this time, he first met <a href="/wiki/Beatification" title="Beatification">Blessed</a> <a href="/wiki/Marie_Louise_Trichet" title="Marie Louise Trichet">Marie Louise Trichet</a> when he was appointed the chaplain of the hospital of <a href="/wiki/Poitiers" title="Poitiers">Poitiers</a>. That meeting became the beginning of Blessed Marie Louise's thirty-four years of service to the poor.</p>
<p>He set off to make a pilgrimage to <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, to ask <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_XI" title="Pope Clement XI">Pope Clement XI</a>, what he should do. The Pope recognised his real vocation and, telling him that there was plenty of scope for its exercise in France, sent him back with the title of Apostolic Missionary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heart_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heart-6">[6]</a></sup> On his return from his long pilgrimage to Rome, Montfort made a retreat at Mont Saint Michel "to pray to this archangel to obtain from him the grace to win souls for God, to confirm those already in God's grace, and to fight Satan and sin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-angels_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-angels-4">[4]</a></sup> These occasions gave him time to think, contemplate and write.</p>
<p>For several years he preached in missions from Brittany to Nantes. As his reputation as a missioner grew, he became known as "the good Father from Montfort". At <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pontchateau" title="Pontchateau">Pontchateau</a> he attracted hundreds of people to help him in the construction of a huge <a href="/wiki/Calvary" title="Calvary">Calvary</a>. However, on the very eve of its blessing, the Bishop, having heard that it was to be destroyed on the orders of the King of France under the influence of members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jansenist" title="Jansenist">Jansenist</a> school, forbade its benediction. It is reported that upon receiving this news, he simply said, "Blessed be God."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poulain_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-poulain-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years">Final years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_de_Montfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Final years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>He left Nantes and the next several years were extraordinarily busy for him. He was constantly occupied in preaching missions, always traveling on foot between one and another. Yet he found time also to write - his <i>True Devotion to Mary</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <i>The Secret of Mary</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and the <i>Secret of the Rosary</i>, rules for the <a href="/wiki/Company_of_Mary" title="Company of Mary">Company of Mary</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Daughters_of_Wisdom" title="Daughters of Wisdom">Daughters of Wisdom</a>, and many Hymns. His missions made a great impact, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Vend%C3%A9e" title="Vendée">Vendée</a>.</p>
<p>The heated style of his preaching was regarded by some people as somewhat strange and he was poisoned once.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poulain_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-poulain-2">[2]</a></sup> Although it did not prove fatal, it caused his health to deteriorate. Yet he continued, undeterred. He went on preaching and established free schools for the poor boys and girls.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Daughters_of_Wisdom">Daughters of Wisdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_de_Montfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Daughters of Wisdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bishop of <a href="/wiki/La_Rochelle" title="La Rochelle">La Rochelle</a> had been impressed with Montfort for some time and invited him to open a school there. Montfort enlisted the help of his follower Marie Louise Trichet who was then running the General Hospital in <a href="/wiki/Poitiers" title="Poitiers">Poitiers</a>. In 1715 Marie Louise and Catherine Brunet left Poitiers for La Rochelle to open the school there and in a short time it had 400 students.</p>
<p>On August 22, 1715, Trichet and Brunet, along with Marie Valleau and Marie Régnier from La Rochelle received the approbation of Bishop de Champflour of La Rochelle to perform their religious profession under the direction of Montfort. At the ceremony Montfort told them: "Call yourselves the <a href="/wiki/Daughters_of_Wisdom" title="Daughters of Wisdom">Daughters of Wisdom</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foley_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Foley-3">[3]</a></sup> for the teaching of children and the care of the poor." The Daughters of Wisdom grew into an international organization and the placing of Montfort's <i>founders statue</i> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Peter%27s_Basilica" title="Saint Peter's Basilica">Saint Peter's Basilica</a> was based on that organization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_burial">Death and burial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_de_Montfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death and burial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Montfort's sixteen years of priesthood include many months of solitude, perhaps as many as a total of four years: at the cave of Mervent, amidst the beauty of the forest, at the hermitage of Saint Lazarus near the village of Montfort, and at the hermitage of Saint Eloi in <a href="/wiki/La_Rochelle" title="La Rochelle">La Rochelle</a>.</p>
<p>Worn out by hard work and sickness, he finally came in April 1716 to <a href="/wiki/Saint-Laurent-sur-S%C3%A8vre" title="Saint-Laurent-sur-Sèvre">Saint-Laurent-sur-Sèvre</a> to begin the mission which was to be his last. During it, he fell ill and died on 28 April of that year. He was 43 years old, and had been a priest for only 16 years. His last sermon was on the tenderness of Jesus and the Incarnate Wisdom of the Father. Thousands gathered for his burial in the parish church, and very quickly there were stories of miracles performed at his tomb.</p>
<p>Exactly 43 years later, on April 28, 1759, Marie Louise Trichet also died in Saint-Laurent-sur-Sèvre and was buried next to Montfort. On September 19, 1996, <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> (who beatified Trichet) came to the same site to meditate and pray at their adjacent tombs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spirituality">Spirituality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_de_Montfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Spirituality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Total_Consecration_to_Mary">Total Consecration to Mary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_de_Montfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Total Consecration to Mary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Montfort's approach to Marian consecration, Jesus and Mary are inseparable. He views "consecration to Jesus in Mary" as a special path to being conformed to, united and consecrated to Christ, given that</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>" ...of all creatures the one most conformed to Jesus Christ, it follows that among all devotions that which most consecrates and conforms a soul to our Lord is devotion to Mary, his Holy Mother, and that the more a soul is consecrated to her the more will it be consecrated to Jesus Christ."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rao246_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rao246-11">[11]</a></sup></dd>
<dd><i>"God the Father made an assemblage of all the waters, and He named it the sea (mare). He has made an assemblage of all His graces, and He has called it Mary (Maria)."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Louis de Montfort influenced a number of popes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>In the 19th century, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_IX" title="Pope Pius IX">Pope Pius IX</a> considered it the best and most acceptable form of Marian devotion, while <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_XIII" title="Pope Leo XIII">Pope Leo XIII</a> granted indulgences for practicing Montfort's method of Marian consecration. Leo beatified Montfort in 1888, selecting for Montfort's beatification the day of his own <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Jubilee" title="Golden Jubilee">Golden Jubilee</a> as a priest.</li>
<li>In the 20th century Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pius_X" title="Pius X">Pius X</a> acknowledged the influence of Montfort's writings in the composition of his encyclical <i><a href="/wiki/Ad_diem_illum" title="Ad diem illum">Ad diem illum</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koster_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Koster-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pius_XI" title="Pius XI">Pius XI</a> stated that he had practiced Montfort's devotional methods since his early youth. Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pius_XII" title="Pius XII">Pius XII</a> declared Montfort a saint and stated that Montfort is the guide "who leads you to Mary and from Mary to Jesus."</li>
<li>Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Paul_II" title="John Paul II">John Paul II</a> once recalled how as a young seminarian he "read and reread many times and with great spiritual profit" a work of de Montfort and that: "Then I understood that I could not exclude the Lord's Mother from my life without neglecting the will of God-Trinity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> According to his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostolic_Letter" title="Apostolic Letter">Apostolic Letter</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Rosarium_Virginis_Mariae" title="Rosarium Virginis Mariae">Rosarium Virginis Mariae</a></i>, the pontiff's personal motto was "<a href="/wiki/Totus_Tuus" title="Totus Tuus">Totus Tuus</a>." The thoughts, writings, and example of St. Louis de Montfort were also singled out by Pope John Paul II's encyclical <i><a href="/wiki/Redemptoris_Mater" title="Redemptoris Mater">Redemptoris Mater</a></i> as a distinctive witness of Marian spirituality in the Roman Catholic tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Priest_and_poet">Priest and poet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_de_Montfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Priest and poet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the saint is best known for his spiritual writings, he was also a poet and during his missions managed to compose more than 20,000 verses of hymns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERao.2C_p._9_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERao.2C_p._9-18">[18]</a></sup> Montfort’s hymns and canticles were, for the most part, meant to be sung in village churches and in the homes of the poor. Some authors argue that a reading of Saint Louis’s hymns is essential for an understanding of him as a man and for appreciating his approach to spirituality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Based on the analysis of Bishop Hendrik Frehen of the Company of Mary, Montfortian hymns fall into two major categories: "inspired" and "didactic." The inspired canticles flow spontaneously, on the occasion of a pilgrimage to a Marian shrine, or on the occasion of a joyful celebration. The didactic hymns took more effort and time to compose, and focus on instructional and informative qualities: they teach the audience through the use of a moral and a theme. After Montfort's death, the Company of Mary (which continued his work of preaching parish renewals) made great use of his hymns and used them as instruments of evangelization.</p>
<p>He is also said to have carved at least three statues depicting the Madonna and Child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_de_Montfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Congregations_de_Montfort">Congregations de Montfort</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_de_Montfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Congregations de Montfort">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The congregations Montfort left behind, the <a href="/wiki/Company_of_Mary" title="Company of Mary">Company of Mary</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Daughters_of_Wisdom" title="Daughters of Wisdom">Daughters of Wisdom</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brothers_of_Saint_Gabriel" title="Brothers of Saint Gabriel">Brothers of Saint Gabriel</a> (which congregation developed from the group of lay-brothers gathered round him), grew and spread, first in France, then throughout the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Servants of the Lord and the Virgin of Matará follow the spirituality of Louis de Montfort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-matara_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-matara-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The saint's birthplace and tomb are now sites of "Montfortian pilgrimages" with about 25,000 visitors each year. The house in which he was born is at No 15, Rue de la Saulnerie in <a href="/wiki/Montfort-sur-Meu" title="Montfort-sur-Meu">Montfort-sur-Meu</a>. It is now jointly owned by the three Montfortian congregations he formed. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Saint_Louis_de_Montfort" title="Basilica of Saint Louis de Montfort">Basilica of Saint Louis de Montfort</a> at <a href="/wiki/Saint-Laurent-sur-S%C3%A8vre" title="Saint-Laurent-sur-Sèvre">Saint-Laurent-sur-Sèvre</a> attracts a number of pilgrims each year.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biographies">Biographies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_de_Montfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Biographies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Statue_de_saint_Louis-Marie_Grignion_de_Montfort,_Basilique_Notre_Dame_de_Bonne_Nouvelle,_Rennes,_France.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4862" data-file-width="3241" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Statue_de_saint_Louis-Marie_Grignion_de_Montfort%2C_Basilique_Notre_Dame_de_Bonne_Nouvelle%2C_Rennes%2C_France.jpg/220px-Statue_de_saint_Louis-Marie_Grignion_de_Montfort%2C_Basilique_Notre_Dame_de_Bonne_Nouvelle%2C_Rennes%2C_France.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Statue_de_saint_Louis-Marie_Grignion_de_Montfort%2C_Basilique_Notre_Dame_de_Bonne_Nouvelle%2C_Rennes%2C_France.jpg/330px-Statue_de_saint_Louis-Marie_Grignion_de_Montfort%2C_Basilique_Notre_Dame_de_Bonne_Nouvelle%2C_Rennes%2C_France.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Statue_de_saint_Louis-Marie_Grignion_de_Montfort%2C_Basilique_Notre_Dame_de_Bonne_Nouvelle%2C_Rennes%2C_France.jpg/440px-Statue_de_saint_Louis-Marie_Grignion_de_Montfort%2C_Basilique_Notre_Dame_de_Bonne_Nouvelle%2C_Rennes%2C_France.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Statue_de_saint_Louis-Marie_Grignion_de_Montfort,_Basilique_Notre_Dame_de_Bonne_Nouvelle,_Rennes,_France.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Statue of Montfort at the basilica of Notre-Dame-de-Bonne-Nouvelle, <a href="/wiki/Rennes" title="Rennes">Rennes</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are more than a hundred biographies written of Montfort. They differ in how they reflect the ecclesial and cultural milieu within which each was written.</p>
<p>The first four biographies of Montfort, by Grandet, Blain, Besnard, and Picot de Clorivières, were all written in the eighteenth century. They reflect the hagiographical method current then—the devotional biography. Such an approach reflected little of the critical sensibility that had dominated most of the seventeenth century through the works of the Bollandists, the memorialists of Port-Royal, and Jean de Launoy. They sought to edify, praise, eulogize, and idealize. Such early biographies are filled with anachronism, incoherence, and over-generalization. Despite such limitations, Montfort’s early biographers provide valuable material. They have preserved eyewitness accounts and original documents, and they offer a solid historical foundation for reconstructing many of the truths of Montfort’s life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-defiores_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-defiores-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The nineteenth century’s "romanticized" conception of history influenced hagiography in two main ways. 1) Although a biography should relive the outer events of a saint’s past, it was more important to describe the interior drama of his soul. 2) The nineteenth-century biographies of Montfort reflect this historiographic orientation. Two biographies were prepared for the Montfort’s beatification, one by Fonteneau and the other by Persiani. The latter is almost a translation of the former (1887). They limited themselves to recounting the events that took place and did not go into Montfort’s psychology. On the other hand, Pauvert (1875) published a collection of Montfort’s unpublished letters and established a chronology of the first years of his priesthood. A. Crosnier’s biography (1927) was also influenced by nineteenth-century romanticism. Just before Montfort’s canonization appeared De Luca (1943). The author took into account the literary and spiritual milieu of seventeenth-century France and attempted to introduced his readers to Montfort’s spiritual life, pointing out "phases" in Montfort's growth and development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-defiores_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-defiores-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of the different genres of biography that purport to describe the events of someone’s life, the "realistic" biography is much in favor. Such a method is apparent in the four works dedicated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_Perouas" title="Louis Perouas">Louis Perouas</a> to Grignion de Montfort (1966, 1973, 1989, 1990). The author separated himself from his predecessors by describing Montfort, his life, and his pastoral work using a historical-critical and psycho-sociological approach. Perouas held that the Breton saint’s path was a "tormented journey" because he had difficulty dealing with a strained relationship with his father, who was known for his violent temper. His long and arduous journey toward a balanced life came to a "certain maturation for Father de Montfort when he was in his forties." Generally more acceptable than Perouas’ Freudian psychological interpretation is his understanding of Montfort’s ministry in the context of the sociological and pastoral realities of his times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-defiores_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-defiores-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
