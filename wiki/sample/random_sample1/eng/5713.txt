<p><b>Mödling</b> is the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%B6dling_(district)" title="Mödling (district)">district</a> of the same name located approximately 14 km south of <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>.</p>
<p>Mödling lies in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nieder%C3%B6sterreich" title="Niederösterreich">Niederösterreich</a>'s industrial zone. The Mödlingbach, a brook which rises in the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Woods" title="Vienna Woods">Wienerwald</a>, flows through the town. Near <a href="/wiki/Achau" title="Achau">Achau</a> it joins with the <a href="/wiki/Schwechat" title="Schwechat">Schwechat</a>. Woodland occupies a large part of the municipality, part of the Föhrenberge ('Pine Mountains').</p>
<p>Located immediately south of Vienna, and within the boundaries of the district of Mödling, is one of the largest shopping centres in Europe: <a href="/wiki/Shopping_City_S%C3%BCd" title="Shopping City Süd">Shopping City Süd</a> (SCS).</p>

<p>Grapes are grown on the slopes of the Wienerwald; the area is called <i>Thermenregion</i>, where one can find much <i>Heurigen</i> (the most recent year's wine).</p>
<p>Wiener Neudorf to the east, and Maria Enzersdorf to the north, merge directly into Mödling. South of Mödling is Gumpoldskirchen, separated by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eichkogel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eichkogel (page does not exist)">Eichkogel</a> with its very special flora. In the west a narrow street runs through Vorderbrühl, formally a village in its own rights, and leads to Hinterbrühl. This narrow valley is called Klausen, above it the remains of the Mödling castle, once belonging to the Babenberger, the then ruling family. On the other side of the Klausen is the Kalenderberg, with the castle of Liechtenstein on its far side. The beginning of the Klausen is marked by the large red-brick aqueduct of the <i>Erste Wiener Hochquellenwasserleitung</i>. The steep, rocky valley sides of the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Naturpark_F%C3%B6hrenberge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Naturpark Föhrenberge (page does not exist)">Naturpark Föhrenberge</a></i> grow the typical <i>Wienerwald-Schwarzföhren</i> (the <i>austriaca</i> <a href="/wiki/Form_(botany)" title="Form (botany)">form</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Black_Pine" title="European Black Pine">European Black Pine</a>, <i>Pinus sylvestris</i>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%B6dling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:215px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Richza.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="217" data-file-width="213" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c1/Richza.jpg" width="213"/></a>

<p>The settlement dates back to the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> era. Through the centuries, the name of the town evolved from Medilihha to Medelikch, Medling and, finally, Mödling. These names traces back to old Slavic meaning 'slowly running water'. Today there is a quaint old town with a pedestrian area. The town was the residence of a branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babenberger" title="Babenberger">Babenberger</a> family, as a result of which it received the nickname Babenbergerstadt ('Town of the Babenbergers').</p>
<p>Traces of the first settlements of the <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_culture" title="Hallstatt culture">Hallstatt culture</a> from the Neolithic era were found on the Kalenderberg. Roman coins and a Roman burial site have been found near today's railway station.</p>
<p>After Charlemagne's victory in 803 AD against the <a href="/wiki/Pannonian_Avars" title="Pannonian Avars">Avars</a>, the area around Mödling was settled by settlers from Bavaria. About 500 Avar graves were found in the area of the "Goldenen Stiege" (very close to today's old city).</p>
<p>The first ancient document mentioning "MEDILIHHA ULTRA MONTEM COMMIGENIUM" is dated 8 September 903, when two bishops (of the then Roman Catholic church) exchanged lands. However, in 907 the settlement seems to have been destroyed again. After the battle on the "Lechfeld" settlement in the area of today's Mödling started again.</p>
<p>After this, for some time Mödling housed a relative of the then ruling <a href="/wiki/House_of_Babenberg" title="House of Babenberg">House of Babenberg</a>. Hence their castle's ruin. In 1177, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_the_Elder" title="Henry the Elder">Henry the Elder</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Henry_II,_Duke_of_Austria" title="Henry II, Duke of Austria">Henry II Jasomirgott</a>, became landlord in an area reaching from <a href="/wiki/Liesing" title="Liesing">Liesing</a> to <a href="/wiki/Piesting" title="Piesting">Piesting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bruck_an_der_Leitha" title="Bruck an der Leitha">Bruck an der Leitha</a>. You can read this in old documents kept in the nearby monastery of Heiligenkreuz. In Henry's days arts and culture dominated in the castle of Mödling; the famous minnesinger Walther von der Vogelweide stayed there more than once. The Spitalkirche and today's St. Othmar were built in the 15th century, the Karner (charnel house) in the 12th.</p>
<p>Even in these times, Mödling grew grapes; after Langenlois, it was the largest grape-growing community in Lower Austria.</p>
<p>Duke Albrecht II, in 1343 granted the rights of a "<a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">Market</a>" to Mödling.</p>
<p>In 1529, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottomans" title="Ottomans">Ottomans</a> devastated Mödling for the first time. In 1679, many citizens died of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a>. When the Ottomans came again in 1683, almost all the citizens of Mödling were killed. The second epidemic of the Black Death only brought death to 22 inhabitants, hence the survivors built the monument of the Holy Trinity (Dreifaltigkeits- or Pestsäule, Freiheitsplatz).</p>
<p>In the early 19th century, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Ludwig van Beethoven</a> often visited his favorite pub, the Three Ravens, in Mödling.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arnold_Sch%C3%B6nberg" title="Arnold Schönberg">Arnold Schönberg</a> lived in Mödling between 1918 and 1925, and invented his <a href="/wiki/Twelve-tone_technique" title="Twelve-tone technique">twelve-tone technique</a> of composition there.</p>
<p>On 18 November 1875, Mödling was designated a "City".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>From 1883 to 1932, Mödling was the starting point of the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6dling_and_Hinterbr%C3%BChl_Tram" title="Mödling and Hinterbrühl Tram">Mödling and Hinterbrühl Tram</a>, Austria's first electric railway and world's first long-lasting tram with overhead lines. In 1938, after the <a href="/wiki/Anschluss" title="Anschluss">Anschluss</a> with <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, Mödling was incorporated into the newly formed <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_Vienna" title="Districts of Vienna">24th District of Vienna</a>. In 1954, it became once again a part of <a href="/wiki/Lower_Austria" title="Lower Austria">Lower Austria</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traffic">Traffic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%B6dling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Traffic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mödling functions as a traffic hub for its direct vicinity. Bus lines connect the town and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Railway_(Austria)" title="Southern Railway (Austria)">Südbahn</a> with the surrounding areas. The Südbahn connects Mödling to Vienna, but also to other local centers like <a href="/wiki/Wiener_Neustadt" title="Wiener Neustadt">Wiener Neustadt</a> by commuter trains.</p>
<p>Many bus lines end/start at Mödling's railway station: Most of them lead to Vienna (with different ways) (207, 259, 260, 265, 266, 269, 270...) but also other villages in the district are destinations: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gie%C3%9Fh%C3%BCbel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gießhübel (page does not exist)">Gießhübel</a> (262), <a href="/wiki/Hinterbr%C3%BChl" title="Hinterbrühl">Hinterbrühl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gaaden" title="Gaaden">Gaaden</a>... (364, 365), <a href="/wiki/Guntramsdorf" title="Guntramsdorf">Guntramsdorf</a> (363) and many more.</p>
<p>The closest airport is <a href="/wiki/Vienna_International_Airport" title="Vienna International Airport">Vienna International Airport</a>.</p>
<p>The town lies close to several major motorways (A2, A23, S1).</p>
<p>Until the 1960s, the town was connected to the tram system of Vienna though the line 360 was discontinued after the commuter train system was introduced on the Südbahn. Historically Mödling was the site of the first electrified tram line, which had mainly touristic use.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%B6dling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the old days, because of the rail-connection to the north/Vienna and to the south, several large industries had their plants here. Today most of the firms are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Small_and_medium_enterprise" title="Small and medium enterprise">SMEs</a>. The larger ones have moved to Wiener Neudorf into the 'Industriezentrum Niederösterreich Süd'.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Castles">Castles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%B6dling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Castles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Mödling area in the Vienna Forest contains many old castles (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Burgen</i></span>) and ruins. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castle_Liechtenstein" title="Castle Liechtenstein">Castle Liechtenstein</a> is the most famous and biggest castle. It was owned by the wealthy family who later founded the country of the same name. <a href="/wiki/Burgruine_M%C3%B6dling" title="Burgruine Mödling">Burgruine Mödling</a> also stands nearby and is one of the oldest ruins in the area. It dates back to the 11th century. In addition, Black Tower (Schwarzer Turm) looks out over downtown Mödling from the Calendar mountains. It was built over 200 years ago, but is still privately occupied to this day. Lastly, on the walk between Black Tower and Castle Liechtenstein, one will come across a 200-year-old amphitheatre nearly buried in the woods. It was built in the same time frame as the Black Tower and served as an important cultural centre for the local Mödling population.</p>

<p>The well-preserved and revitalized Old City is protected by the <a href="/wiki/Hague_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_Cultural_Property_in_the_Event_of_Armed_Conflict" title="Hague Convention for the Protection of Cultural Property in the Event of Armed Conflict">Hague Convention</a>. The town hall holds the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Registry_office" title="Registry office">registry office</a>. Because of its lovely environment many couples choose to marry here. Schrannenplatz and Kaiserin-Elisabeth-Strasse were pedestrianised at the very early date of 1976, the first time a 'Bundesstraße' had been declared a pedestrian area.</p>
<p>Nearby is the Eichkogel Nature Reserve, with rare flora, like 'Knollen-Brandkraut' (<i><a href="/wiki/Phlomoides_tuberosa" title="Phlomoides tuberosa">Phlomoides tuberosa</a></i>) and others, on 'Halbtrockenrasen'. Among the rocks in the Klausen rare plants grow, like the 'Mödlinger Federnelke' (<i>Dianthus plumarius</i> subsp. <i>neilreichii</i>), which was discovered as late as the middle of the 19th century by the botanist <a href="/wiki/August_Neilreich" title="August Neilreich">August Neilreich</a>, or the 'Deutsche Alant' (Inula germanica).</p>
