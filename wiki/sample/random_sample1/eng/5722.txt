<p><b>Matzo</b>, <b>matza</b> or <b>matzah</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yiddish_language" title="Yiddish language">Yiddish</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="yi" xml:lang="yi">מצה</span>‎ <i>matsah</i>, <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span class="script-hebrew" dir="rtl" style="font-size: 115%; font-family: Alef, 'SBL BibLit', 'SBL Hebrew', 'David CLM', 'Frenk Ruehl CLM', 'Hadasim CLM', Cardo, Shofar, David, 'Ezra SIL', 'Ezra SIL SR', 'Noto Sans Hebrew', FreeSerif, 'Times New Roman', FreeSans, Arial;">מַצָּה</span>‎ <i>matsa</i>; plural <b>matzot</b>; <b>matzos</b> of <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_Hebrew" title="Ashkenazi Hebrew">Ashkenazi Hebrew</a> dialect) is an <a href="/wiki/Unleavened_bread" title="Unleavened bread">unleavened</a> <a href="/wiki/Flatbread" title="Flatbread">flatbread</a> that is part of <a href="/wiki/Jewish_cuisine" title="Jewish cuisine">Jewish cuisine</a> and forms an integral element of the <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">Passover</a> festival, during which <i><a href="/wiki/Chametz" title="Chametz">chametz</a></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaven" title="Leaven">leaven</a> and five grains that, per Jewish Law, can be leavened) is forbidden.</p>
<p>Matzo that is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosher_for_Passover" title="Kosher for Passover">kosher for Passover</a> is limited in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ashkenazi" title="Ashkenazi">Ashkenazi</a> tradition to plain matzo made from flour and water. The flour may be <a href="/wiki/Whole_grain" title="Whole grain">whole grain</a> or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Processed_grain&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Processed grain (page does not exist)">processed grain</a>, but must be either <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spelt" title="Spelt">spelt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Oat" title="Oat">oat</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sephardic" title="Sephardic">Sephardic</a> tradition also allows <a href="/wiki/Egg" title="Egg">eggs</a> to be used.</p>
<p>Passover and non-Passover matzo may be soft or crisp, but only the crisp "cracker" type is available commercially in most locations. Soft matzo, if it were commercially available, would essentially be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosher" title="Kosher">kosher</a> flour <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tortilla" title="Tortilla">tortilla</a>.</p>
<p>Non-Passover matzo may be made with onion, garlic, poppy seed, etc. It can even be made from <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buckwheat" title="Buckwheat">buckwheat</a> and other non-traditional flours that can never be used for Passover matzo. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gluten-free" title="Gluten-free">Gluten-free</a> matzo-lookalike made from <a href="/wiki/Potato_starch" title="Potato starch">potato starch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tapioca" title="Tapioca">tapioca</a>, and other non-traditional flour is available and may be eaten on Passover, but does not fulfill the commandment of eating matzo, even for people with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celiac_disease" title="Celiac disease">celiac disease</a> who cannot eat Passover matzo, because matzo must be made from one of the 5 grains (wheat, barley, oat, spelt, and rye), all of which contain <a href="/wiki/Gluten" title="Gluten">gluten</a>, except for most (but not all) types of oat matzo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Oat matzo may only be used by those who cannot have any other kind because it's not certain that oat is actually one of the 5 grains (it may be a mistranslation), so those who can have wheat matzo should do so.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biblical_sources">Biblical sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matzo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biblical sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Matzo</i> is mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> several times in relation to <a href="/wiki/The_Exodus" title="The Exodus">The Exodus</a> from <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>That night, they are to eat the meat, roasted in the fire; they are to eat it with <i><b>matzo</b></i> and <i>maror</i>.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Exodus" title="Book of Exodus">Exodus</a> 12:8</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>From the evening of the fourteenth day of the first month until the evening of the twenty-first day, you are to eat <i><b>matzo</b></i>.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Exodus" title="Book of Exodus">Exodus</a> 12:18</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>You are not to eat any <i><a href="/wiki/Chametz" title="Chametz">chametz</a></i> with it; for seven days you are to eat with it <i><b>matzo</b></i>, the bread of affliction; for you came out of the land of Egypt in haste. Thus you will remember the day you left the land of Egypt as long as you live.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deuteronomy" title="Deuteronomy">Deuteronomy</a> 16:3</cite></div>
</blockquote>

<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deuteronomy" title="Deuteronomy">Deuteronomy</a> 16:8</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_significance">Religious significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matzo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Religious significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are numerous explanations behind the symbolism of matzo. One is historical: Passover is a commemoration of the exodus from Egypt. The biblical narrative relates that the <a href="/wiki/Israelites" title="Israelites">Israelites</a> left Egypt in such haste they could not wait for their bread dough to rise; the bread, when baked, was matzo. (<a href="/wiki/Book_of_Exodus" title="Book of Exodus">Exodus</a> 12:39). The other reason for eating matzo is symbolic: On the one hand, matzo symbolizes redemption and freedom, but it is also <i>lechem oni</i>, "poor man's bread". Thus it serves as a reminder to be humble, and to not forget what life was like in servitude. Also, leaven symbolizes corruption and pride as leaven "puffs up". Eating the "bread of affliction" is both a lesson in humility and an act that enhances the appreciation of freedom.</p>
<p>Another explanation is that matzo has been used to replace the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pesach" title="Pesach">pesach</a>, or the traditional Passover offering that was made before the destruction of the Temple. During the Seder the third time the matzo is eaten it is preceded with the <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">Sephardic</a> rite, "zekher l'korban pesach hane'ekhal al hasova". This means "remembrance of the Passover offering, eaten while full". This last piece of the matzo eaten is called <a href="/wiki/Afikoman" title="Afikoman">afikoman</a> and many explain it as a symbol of salvation in the future.</p>
<p>The Passover Seder meal is full of symbols of salvation, including the opening of the door for <a href="/wiki/Elijah" title="Elijah">Elijah</a> and the closing line, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Next_year_in_Jerusalem" title="Next year in Jerusalem">Next year in Jerusalem</a>," but the use of matzo is the oldest symbol of salvation in the Seder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passover_seder" title="Passover seder">Passover seder</a>, simple matzo made of flour and water is mandatory. Sephardic tradition additionally permits the inclusion of eggs in the recipe. The flour must be ground from one of the five grains specified in Jewish law for Passover matzo: wheat, barley, spelt, rye or oat. Matzo made with <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fruit_juice" title="Fruit juice">fruit juice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Onion" title="Onion">onion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">garlic</a>, etc., is not acceptable for use at any time during the Passover festival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matzo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Matzo dough is quickly mixed and rolled out without an <a href="/wiki/Autolysis_(biology)#Use" title="Autolysis (biology)">autolyse</a> step as used for leavened breads. Most forms are pricked with a fork or a similar tool to keep the finished product from puffing up, and the resulting flat piece of dough is cooked at high temperature until it develops dark spots, then set aside to cool and, if sufficiently thin, to harden to crispness. Dough is considered to begin the leavening process 18 minutes from the time it gets wet; sooner if eggs, fruit juice, or milk is added to the dough. The entire process of making matzo takes only a few minutes in efficient modern matzo bakeries.</p>
<p>After baking, matzo may be ground into fine crumbs, known as matzo meal. Matzo meal can be used like flour during the week of Passover when flour can otherwise be used only to make matzo.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matzo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Matzo-forming_machine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1712" data-file-width="2288" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Matzo-forming_machine.jpg/200px-Matzo-forming_machine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Matzo-forming_machine.jpg/300px-Matzo-forming_machine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Matzo-forming_machine.jpg/400px-Matzo-forming_machine.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Matzo-forming_machine.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Matzo-forming machine, ca. early 20th century (the <a href="/wiki/Lviv" title="Lviv">Lviv</a> Museum of the History of Religion)</div>

<p>There are two major forms of matzo. In many western countries the most common form is the hard form of matzo which is cracker-like in appearance and taste and is used in all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ashkenazic" title="Ashkenazic">Ashkenazic</a> and most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sephardic" title="Sephardic">Sephardic</a> communities. <a href="/wiki/Yemenite_Jews" title="Yemenite Jews">Yemenites</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iraqi_Jews" title="Iraqi Jews">Iraqi Jews</a> traditionally made a form of soft matzo which looks like Greek <a href="/wiki/Pita" title="Pita">pita</a> or like a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tortilla" title="Tortilla">tortilla</a>. Soft matzo is made only by hand, and generally with <i>shmurah</i> flour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intime2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-intime2-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flavored varieties of matzo are produced commercially, such as <a href="/wiki/Poppy_seed" title="Poppy seed">poppy seed</a>- or <a href="/wiki/Onion" title="Onion">onion</a>-flavored. Oat and spelt matzo with kosher certification are produced. Oat matzo is generally suitable for those who cannot eat gluten. Whole wheat, bran and organic matzo are also available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">Chocolate</a>-covered matzo is a favorite among children, although some consider it "enriched matzo" and will not eat it during the Passover holiday. A quite different flat confection of chocolate and nuts that resembles matzo is sometimes called "chocolate matzo".</p>
<p>Matzo contains typically 111 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calories" title="Calories">calories</a> per 1-ounce/28g (USDA Nutrient Database), about the same as <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a> crispbread.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shmurah_matzo">Shmurah matzo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matzo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Shmurah matzo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Shĕmura</i> ("guarded") matzo (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span class="script-hebrew" dir="rtl" style="font-size: 115%; font-family: Alef, 'SBL BibLit', 'SBL Hebrew', 'David CLM', 'Frenk Ruehl CLM', 'Hadasim CLM', Cardo, Shofar, David, 'Ezra SIL', 'Ezra SIL SR', 'Noto Sans Hebrew', FreeSerif, 'Times New Roman', FreeSans, Arial;">מַצָּה שְׁמוּרָה</span>‎ <i>matsa shĕmura</i>) is made from grain that has been under special supervision from the time it was harvested to ensure that no <a href="/wiki/Chametz#Fermentation" title="Chametz">fermentation</a> has occurred, and that it is suitable for eating on the first night of Passover. (<i>Shĕmura</i> wheat may be formed into either handmade or machine-made matzo, while non-<i>shĕmura</i> wheat is only used for machine-made matzo. It is possible to hand-bake matzo in <i>shĕmura</i> style from non-shmurah flour—this is a matter of style, it is not actually in any way <i>shĕmura</i>—but such matzo has rarely been produced since the introduction of machine-made matzo.)</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Haredi_Judaism" title="Haredi Judaism">Haredi Judaism</a> is scrupulous about the supervision of matzo and have the custom of baking their own or at least participating in some stage of the baking process. Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Chaim_Halberstam" title="Chaim Halberstam">Haim Halberstam</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sanz" title="Sanz">Sanz</a> ruled that machine-made matzoth were <i>hametz</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> According to that opinion, hand-made non-<i>shmurah</i> matzot may be used on the eighth day of Passover outside of the Holy Land. However the non-Hasidic Haredi community of Jerusalem follows the custom that machine-made matzo may be used, with preference to the use of <i>shĕmurah</i> flour, in accordance with the ruling of Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Yosef_Chaim_Sonnenfeld" title="Yosef Chaim Sonnenfeld">Yosef Haim Sonnenfeld</a>, who ruled that machine-made matzo may be preferable to hand made in some cases. The commentators to the <a href="/wiki/Shulchan_Aruch" title="Shulchan Aruch">Shulhan `Aruch</a> record that it is the custom of some of Diaspora Jewry to be scrupulous in giving Hallah from the dough used for baking "Matzot Mitzvah" (the Shĕmurah Matzo eaten during <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">Passover</a>) to a <a href="/wiki/Kohen" title="Kohen">Kohen</a> child to eat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Egg_matzo">Egg matzo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matzo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Egg matzo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Matza9197.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2983" data-file-width="1887" height="348" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Matza9197.JPG/220px-Matza9197.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Matza9197.JPG/330px-Matza9197.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Matza9197.JPG/440px-Matza9197.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Matza9197.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Children in <a href="/wiki/Ofra" title="Ofra">Ofra</a> preparing matzo</div>

<p>"Egg (sometimes <i>enriched</i>) matzo" are matzot usually made with fruit <a href="/wiki/Juice" title="Juice">juice</a>, often <a href="/wiki/Grape" title="Grape">grape</a> or <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apple</a> juice instead of water, but not necessarily with eggs themselves. There is a custom among some Ashkenazi Jews not to eat them during Passover, except for the elderly, infirm, or children, who cannot digest plain matzo; these matzot are considered to be kosher for Passover if prepared otherwise properly. The issue of whether egg matzo is allowed for Passover comes down to whether there is a difference between the various liquids that can be used. Water facilitates <a href="/wiki/Fermentation_in_food_processing" title="Fermentation in food processing">fermentation</a> of grain flour, but the question is whether fruit juice, eggs, honey, oil or milk are also deemed to do so.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pesachim_(Talmud)" title="Pesachim (Talmud)"><i>Talmud</i>, Pesachim 35a</a>, states that liquid food extracts do not cause flour to leaven the way that water does. According to this view, flour mixed with other liquids would not need to be treated with the same care as flour mixed with water. The <i><a href="/wiki/Tosafot" title="Tosafot">Tosafot</a></i> (commentaries) explain that such liquids only produce a leavening reaction within flour <i>if</i> they themselves have had water added to them and otherwise the dough they produce is completely permissible for consumption during Passover, whether or not made according to the laws applying to matzot.</p>
<p>As a result, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_ben_Ephraim_Karo" title="Joseph ben Ephraim Karo">Joseph ben Ephraim Karo</a>, author of the <i><a href="/wiki/Shulchan_Aruch" title="Shulchan Aruch">Shulchan Aruch</a></i> or "Code of Jewish Law" (<a href="/wiki/Orach_Chayim" title="Orach Chayim">Orach Chayim 462:4</a>) granted blanket permission for the use of any matzo made from non-water-based dough, including egg matzo, on Passover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Many egg matzo boxes no longer include the message, "Ashkenazi custom is that egg matzah is only allowed for children, elderly and the infirm during Passover." Even amongst those who consider that enriched matzot may not be <i>eaten</i> during Passover, it is permissible to <i>retain</i> it in the home.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cooking_with_matzo">Cooking with matzo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matzo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cooking with matzo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Matzah_ball" title="Matzah ball">Matzo balls</a> and matzo <a href="/wiki/Farfel" title="Farfel">farfel</a> are served in soup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheFreeDictionary_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-TheFreeDictionary-9">[9]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matzo_brei" title="Matzo brei">Matzo brei</a> is a dish of Ashkenazi origin made from matzo soaked in water and fried with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" title="Egg (food)">eggs</a>. These items are made from <b>matzo meal</b>, or finely ground matzo sometimes called <b>cake meal</b>, used as a binder in baked goods. Some Ashkenazim do not cook with matzo, believing that mixing it with water may allow leavening;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intime_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-intime-10">[10]</a></sup> the mixture is called "<a href="/wiki/Gebrochts" title="Gebrochts">gebrochts</a>" by Ashkenazi Jews. Kosher for Passover cakes and cookies are made with matzo meal or a finer variety called "cake meal", which gives them a denser texture than ordinary baked foods made with flour.</p>
<p>Sephardim use matzo soaked in water or stock to make pies or <a href="/wiki/Lasagne" title="Lasagne">lasagne</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldstein_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstein-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> known as <i>mina</i>, <i>méguena</i>, <i>mayena</i> or <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><i>scacchi</i></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Romanow_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Romanow-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Matzo meal pancakes are made from a batter of matzo meal, egg, and milk.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Matzah_pizza" title="Matzah pizza">Matzah pizza</a> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Pizza" title="Pizza">pizza</a> made by covering a piece of matzo with sauce and melted cheese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Christianity">In Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matzo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Communion wafers used by the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> as well as in some <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> traditions for the <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Eucharist</a> are flat, unleavened bread. All <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Rite" title="Byzantine Rite">Byzantine Rite</a> churches use leavened bread for the Eucharist as this symbolizes the risen Christ. Some <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic</a> Christians use leavened bread, as in the east there is the tradition that leavened bread was on the table of the Last Supper. In the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Apostolic_Church" title="Armenian Apostolic Church">Armenian Apostolic Church</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Orthodox_Tewahedo_Church" title="Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church">Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eritrean_Orthodox_Tewahedo_Church" title="Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo Church">Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo Church</a>, unleavened bread called <i>qǝddus qurban</i> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ge%27ez" title="Ge'ez">Ge'ez</a>, the liturgical language of the Eritreans and Ethiopians, is used for communion.</p>
<p>Syrian Christians living on the Malabar coast of India (Kerala) have the customary celebration of Pesaha in their homes. On the evening before Good Friday, Pesaha bread is made at home. It is made with unleavened flour and they consume a sweet drink made up of coconut milk and jaggery along with this bread. On the Pesaha night, the bread is baked (steamed) immediately after rice flour is mixed with water and they pierce it many times with handle of the spoon to let out steam so that the bread will not rise (this custom is called "juthante kannu kuthal" in the Malayalam language meaning "piercing the bread according to the custom of Jews"). This bread is cut by the head of the family and shared among the family members. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trivia">Trivia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matzo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Trivia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the end of World War II, the <a href="/wiki/National_Jewish_Welfare_Board" title="National Jewish Welfare Board">National Jewish Welfare Board</a> had a matzo factory (according to the American Jewish Historical Society, it was probably the <a href="/wiki/Manischewitz" title="Manischewitz">Manischewitz</a> matzo factory in <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>) produce matzo in the form of a giant "V" for "Victory", for shipment to military bases overseas and in the U.S., for Passover seders for Jewish military personnel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Passover in 1945 began on 1 April, when the collapse of the Axis in Europe was clearly imminent; Germany <a href="/wiki/German_Instrument_of_Surrender" title="German Instrument of Surrender">surrendered</a> <a href="/wiki/Victory_in_Europe_Day" title="Victory in Europe Day">five weeks later</a>.</p>
