<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Muqaddimah</i></h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Muqaddimah_(disambiguation)" title="Muqaddimah (disambiguation)">muqaddimah (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>The <i><b>Muqaddimah</b></i>, also known as the <i><b>Muqaddimah of Ibn Khaldun</b></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">مقدّمة ابن خلدون</span>‎‎) or <i><b>Ibn Khaldun's <a href="/wiki/Introduction_(writing)" title="Introduction (writing)">Prolegomena</a></b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Προλεγόμενα</span>), is a book written by the <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">Arab</a> historian <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun" title="Ibn Khaldun">Ibn Khaldun</a> in 1377 which records an early view of <a href="/wiki/Universal_history" title="Universal history">universal history</a>. Some modern thinkers view it as the first work dealing with the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_history" title="Philosophy of history">philosophy of history</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAkhtar1997_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAkhtar1997-1">[1]</a></sup> or the <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social sciences</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAkhtar1997_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAkhtar1997-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mowlana_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mowlana-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gates_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gates-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Demography" title="Demography">demography</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mowlana_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mowlana-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">historiography</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gates_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gates-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salahuddin_Ahmed_1999_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salahuddin_Ahmed_1999-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cultural_history" title="Cultural history">cultural history</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mohamad_Abdalla_2007_p._61-70_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mohamad_Abdalla_2007_p._61-70-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> social darwinism,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> ecology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWallace2009303_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWallace2009303-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Darwinism" title="Darwinism">darwinism</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELeuprecht201164_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELeuprecht201164-12">[12]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oweiss_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oweiss-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jean_David_C_1971_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jean_David_C_1971-14">[14]</a></sup> The <i>Muqaddimah</i> also deals with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_theology" title="Islamic theology">Islamic theology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political theory</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">natural sciences</a> of <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>.</p>
<p>Ibn Khaldun wrote the work in 1377 as the introduction chapter and the first book of his planned work of world <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, the <i>Kitābu l-ʻibar</i> ("Book of Lessons"; full title: <i>Kitābu l-ʻibari wa Dīwāni l-Mubtada' wal-Ḥabar fī ayāmi l-ʻarab wal-ʿajam wal-barbar, waman ʻĀsarahum min Dhawī sh-Shalṭāni l-Akbār</i>, i.e.: "Book of Lessons, Record of Beginnings and Events in the history of the Arabs and Foreigners and Berbers and their Powerful Contemporaries"), but already in his lifetime it became regarded as an independent work on its own.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ibn Khaldun starts the <i>Muqaddimah</i> with a thorough criticism of the mistakes regularly committed by his fellow <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historians</a> and the difficulties which await the historian in his work. He notes seven critical issues:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"All records, by their very nature, are liable to error...</p>
<ol>
<li>...Partisanship towards a creed or opinion...</li>
<li>...Over-confidence in one's sources...</li>
<li>...The failure to understand what is intended...</li>
<li>...A mistaken belief in the truth...</li>
<li>...The inability to place an event in its real context</li>
<li>...The common desire to gain favor of those of high ranks, by praising them, by spreading their fame...</li>
<li>...The most important is the ignorance of the laws governing the transformation of human society."</li>
</ol>
</blockquote>
<p>Against the seventh point (the ignorance of social laws) Ibn Khaldun lays out his theory of human <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a> in the <i>Muqaddimah</i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sati%27_al-Husri" title="Sati' al-Husri">Sati' al-Husri</a> suggested that Ibn Khaldun's <i>Muqaddimah</i> is essentially a sociological work, sketching over its six books a general <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>; a <a href="/wiki/Political_sociology" title="Political sociology">sociology of politics</a>; a <a href="/wiki/Urban_sociology" title="Urban sociology">sociology of urban life</a>; a <a href="/wiki/Economic_sociology" title="Economic sociology">sociology of economics</a>; and a <a href="/wiki/Sociology_of_knowledge" title="Sociology of knowledge">sociology of knowledge</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_method">Scientific method</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scientific method">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun" title="Ibn Khaldun">Ibn Khaldun</a> often criticized "idle superstition and uncritical acceptance of historical data." As a result, he introduced the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_sciences" title="Social sciences">social sciences</a>, which was considered something "new to his age", and he often referred to it as his "new science" and developed his own new terminology for it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood1969X_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood1969X-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>His <a href="/wiki/Historical_method" title="Historical method">historical method</a> also laid the groundwork for the observation of the role of <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">state</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systematic_bias" title="Systematic bias">systematic bias</a> in history,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mowlana_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mowlana-3">[3]</a></sup> leading to his development of <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">historiography</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociology">Sociology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sociology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".CA.BFAsabiyyah">ʿAsabiyyah</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: ʿAsabiyyah">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Asabiyyah" title="Asabiyyah">Asabiyyah</a></div>
<p>The concept of "<a href="/wiki/Asabiyyah" title="Asabiyyah">ʿasabiyyah</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a>: "tribalism, clanism, communitarism", or in a modern context, "nationalism") is one of the best known aspects of the <i>Muqaddimah</i>. Ibn Khaldun uses the term ʿasabiyyah to describe the bond of cohesion among humans in a group forming <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a>. The bond, ʿasabiyyah, exists at any level of civilization, from nomadic society to states and empires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tibi_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-tibi-16">[16]</a></sup> ʿAsabiyyah is most strong in the nomadic phase, and decreases as civilization advances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tibi_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-tibi-16">[16]</a></sup> As this ʿasabiyyah declines, another more compelling ʿasabiyyah may take its place; thus, civilizations rise and fall, and history describes these cycles of ʿasabiyyah as they play out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tibi_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-tibi-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ibn Khaldun argues that each dynasty has within itself the seeds of its own downfall. He explains that ruling houses tend to emerge on the peripheries of great empires and use the unity presented by those areas to their advantage in order to bring about a change in leadership. As the new rulers establish themselves at the center of their empire, they become increasingly lax and more concerned with maintaining their lifestyles. Thus, a new dynasty can emerge at the periphery of their control and effect a change in leadership, beginning the cycle anew.</p>
<p>Ibn Khaldun's model is an instinctive one, not requiring a conceptual <a href="/wiki/Social_contract" title="Social contract">social contract</a> present in <a href="/wiki/Classical_republicanism" title="Classical republicanism">classical republicanism</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conflict_theory">Conflict theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conflict theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ibn Khaldun conceived both a central <a href="/wiki/Social_conflict" title="Social conflict">social conflict</a> ("town" versus "desert") as well as a theory (using the concept of a "generation") of the necessary loss of power of city conquerors coming from the desert.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Ibn Khaldun wrote on economic and political theory in the <i>Muqaddimah</i>, relating his thoughts on ʿasabiyyah to the division of labor: the greater the social cohesion, the more complex the division may be, the greater the <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a>.</p>
<p>Ibn Khaldun noted that growth and development positively stimulate both supply and demand, and that the forces of supply and demand are what determine the prices of goods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> He also noted macroeconomic forces of population growth, <a href="/wiki/Human_capital" title="Human capital">human capital</a> development, and technological developments effects on development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Ibn Khaldun held that population growth was a function of wealth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ibn Khaldun understood that money served as a standard of value, a medium of exchange, and a preserver of value, though he did not realize that the value of gold and silver changed based on the forces of supply and demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Ibn Khaldun also introduced the <a href="/wiki/Labor_theory_of_value" title="Labor theory of value">labor theory of value</a>. He described labor as the source of value, necessary for all earnings and capital accumulation, obvious in the case of craft. He argued that even if earning “results from something other than a craft, the value of the resulting profit and acquired (capital) must (also) include the value of the labor by which it was obtained. Without labor, it would not have been acquired.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oweiss_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oweiss-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>His theory of ʿasabiyyah has often been compared to modern <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics" title="Keynesian economics">Keynesian economics</a>, with Ibn Khaldun's theory clearly containing the concept of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spending_multiplier" title="Spending multiplier">multiplier</a>. A crucial difference, however, is that whereas for <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a> it is the <a href="/wiki/Middle_class" title="Middle class">middle class</a>'s greater propensity to save that is to blame for <a href="/wiki/Depression_(economics)" title="Depression (economics)">economic depression</a>, for Ibn Khaldun it is the governmental propensity to save at times when <a href="/wiki/Investment" title="Investment">investment</a> opportunities do not take up the slack which leads to <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_demand" title="Aggregate demand">aggregate demand</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another modern economic theory anticipated by Ibn Khaldun is <a href="/wiki/Supply-side_economics" title="Supply-side economics">supply-side economics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> He "argued that high <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">taxes</a> were often a factor in causing empires to collapse, with the result that lower revenue was collected from high rates." He wrote:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"It should be known that at the beginning of the dynasty, taxation yields a large revenue from small assessments. At the end of the dynasty, taxation yields a small revenue from large assessments."</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laffer_Curve">Laffer Curve</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Laffer Curve">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ibn Khaldun introduced the concept now popularly known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laffer_Curve" title="Laffer Curve">Laffer Curve</a>, that increases in tax rates initially increase tax revenues, but eventually the increases in tax rates cause a decrease in tax revenues. This occurs as too high a tax rate discourages producers in the economy.</p>
<p>Ibn Khaldun used a <a href="/wiki/Dialectic" title="Dialectic">dialectic</a> approach to describe the sociological implications of tax choice (which now forms a part of <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> theory):</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In the early stages of the state, taxes are light in their incidence, but fetch in a large revenue...As time passes and kings succeed each other, they lose their tribal habits in favor of more civilized ones. Their needs and exigencies grow...owing to the luxury in which they have been brought up. Hence they impose fresh taxes on their subjects...and sharply raise the rate of old taxes to increase their yield...But the effects on business of this rise in taxation make themselves felt. For business men are soon discouraged by the comparison of their profits with the burden of their taxes...Consequently production falls off, and with it the yield of taxation.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This analysis is very similar to the modern economic concept known as the Laffer Curve. Laffer does not claim to have invented the concept himself, noting that the idea was present in the work of Ibn Khaldun and, more recently, <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Khaldun-Laffer curve has also been used in Solid State Physics and Chemistry to interpret the dependence of certain macroscopic properties of solids on hydrostatic pressure (e.g. dynamical effective charge, polarizability) see M.Cardona Arxiv:cond-mat/0204606v1, April 29, 2002</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historiography">Historiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Historiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Historiography_of_early_Islam" title="Historiography of early Islam">Historiography of early Islam</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sociology_in_medieval_Islam" title="Sociology in medieval Islam">Sociology in medieval Islam</a></div>
<p>The <i>Muqaddimah</i> is also held to be a foundational work for the schools of <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">historiography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cultural_history" title="Cultural history">cultural history</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_history" title="Philosophy of history">philosophy of history</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mohamad_Abdalla_2007_p._61-70_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mohamad_Abdalla_2007_p._61-70-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The <i>Muqaddimah</i> also laid the groundwork for the observation of the role of <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">state</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systematic_bias" title="Systematic bias">systematic bias</a> in history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mowlana_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Mowlana-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Franz_Rosenthal" title="Franz Rosenthal">Franz Rosenthal</a> wrote in the <i>History of Muslim Historiography</i>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Muslim historiography has at all times been united by the closest ties with the general development of scholarship in Islam, and the position of historical knowledge in MusIim education has exercised a decisive influence upon the intellectual level of historical writing....The Muslims achieved a definite advance beyond previous historical writing in the <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociological</a> understanding of <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a> and the systematisation of <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">historiography</a>. The development of modern historical writing seems to have gained considerably in speed and substance through the utilization of a Muslim Literature which enabled western historians, from the seventeenth century on, to see a large section of the world through foreign eyes. The Muslim historiography helped indirectly and modestly to shape present day historical thinking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_method">Historical method</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Historical method">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <i>Muqaddimah</i>, Ibn Khaldun warned of seven mistakes that he thought that historians regularly committed. In this criticism, he approached the past as strange and in need of interpretation. The originality of Ibn Khaldun was to claim that the cultural difference of another age must govern the evaluation of relevant historical material, to distinguish the principles according to which it might be possible to attempt the evaluation, and lastly, to feel the need for experience, in addition to rational principles, in order to assess a culture of the past. Ibn Khaldun often criticized "idle <a href="/wiki/Superstition" title="Superstition">superstition</a> and uncritical acceptance of historical data." As a result, he introduced a <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a> to the study of history, which was considered something "new to his age", and he often referred to it as his "new science", now associated with <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">historiography</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood1969X_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood1969X-15">[15]</a></sup> His <a href="/wiki/Historical_method" title="Historical method">historical method</a> also laid the groundwork for the observation of the role of <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">state</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systematic_bias" title="Systematic bias">systematic bias</a> in history,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mowlana_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Mowlana-3">[3]</a></sup> and he is thus considered to be the "father of historiography"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salahuddin_Ahmed_1999_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Salahuddin_Ahmed_1999-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enan_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Enan-28">[28]</a></sup> or the "father of the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_history" title="Philosophy of history">philosophy of history</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAkhtar1997_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAkhtar1997-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ibn Khaldun' makes the following comments on his scientific historical method in his <i>Muqaddimah</i>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kujjah_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kujjah-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>"History is a science"</li>
<li>"History has a content and the historian should account for it"</li>
<li>"The historian should account for the elements that gather to make the human history"</li>
<li>"He should also work according to the laws of history"</li>
<li>"History is a philosophical science"</li>
<li>"History is composed of news about the days, states and the previous centuries. It is a theory, an analysis and justification about the creatures and their principles, and a science of how the incidents happen and their reasons"</li>
<li>"Myths have nothing to do with history and should be refuted"</li>
<li>"To build strong historical records, the historian should rely on necessary rules for the truth comparison"</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy_of_history">Philosophy of history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Philosophy of history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ibn Khaldun is considered a pioneer of the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_history" title="Philosophy of history">philosophy of history</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAkhtar1997_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAkhtar1997-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/N._J._Dawood" title="N. J. Dawood">Dawood</a> writes on the <i>Muqaddimah</i>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It can be regarded as the earliest attempt made by any historian to discover a pattern in the changes that occur in man's political and social organization. Rational in its approach, analytical in its method, encyclopaedic in detail, it represents an almost complete departure from traditional historiography, discarding conventional concepts and cliches and seeking, beyond the mere chronicle of events, an explanation—and hence a philosophy of history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood1969IX_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood1969IX-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Systematic_bias">Systematic bias</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Systematic bias">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Muqaddimah</i> emphasized the role of <a href="/wiki/Systemic_bias" title="Systemic bias">systemic bias</a> in affecting the <a href="/wiki/Historical_method" title="Historical method">standard of evidence</a>. Khaldun was quite concerned with the effect of raising the standard of evidence when confronted with uncomfortable claims, and relaxing it when given claims that seemed reasonable or comfortable. He was a jurist, and sometimes participated reluctantly in rulings that he felt were coerced, based on arguments he didn't respect. Besides <a href="/wiki/Al-Maqrizi" title="Al-Maqrizi">al-Maqrizi</a> (1364–1442),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kujjah_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kujjah-29">[29]</a></sup> Ibn Khaldun's focused attempt to systematically study and account biases in the creation of history wouldn't be seen again until <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel" title="Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel">Georg Hegel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">Friedrich Nietzsche</a> in 19th-century Germany, and <a href="/wiki/Arnold_J._Toynbee" title="Arnold J. Toynbee">Arnold J. Toynbee</a>, a 20th-century British historian.</p>
<p>Ibn Khaldun also examines why, throughout history, it has been common for historians to <a href="/wiki/Sensationalism" title="Sensationalism">sensationalize</a> historical events and, in particular, exaggerate numerical figures:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Whenever contemporaries speak about the dynastic armies of their own or recent times, and whenever they engage in discussions about Muslim or Christian soldiers, or when they get to figuring the tax revenues and the money spent by the government, the outlays of extravagant spenders, and the goods that rich and prosperous men have in stock, they are quite generally found to exaggerate, to go beyond the bounds of the ordinary, and to succumb to the temptation of sensationalism. When the officials in charge are questioned about their armies, when the goods and assets of wealthy people are assessed, and when the outlays of extravagant spenders are looked at in ordinary light, the figures will be found to amount to a tenth of what those people have said. The reason is simple. It is the common desire for sensationalism, the ease with which one may just mention a higher figure, and the disregard of reviewers and critics. This leads to failure to exercise self-criticism about one's errors and intentions, to demand from oneself moderation and fairness in reporting, to reapply oneself to study and research. Such historians let themselves go and made a feast of untrue statements. "They procure for themselves entertaining stories in order to lead (others) astray from the path of God."<sup>[<a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a> <a class="external text" href="https://quran.com/31:6" rel="nofollow">31:6</a>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood196913.E2.80.934_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood196913.E2.80.934-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_history">Military history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Military history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Muqaddimah</i> is the earliest known work to critically examine <a href="/wiki/Military_history" title="Military history">military history</a>. It criticizes certain accounts of historical battles that appear to be exaggerated, and takes <a href="/wiki/Military_logistics" title="Military logistics">military logistics</a> into account when questioning the sizes of historical armies reported in earlier sources. In the Introduction to the <i>Muqaddimah</i>, Ibn Khaldun directs this criticism towards to famous historians such as <a href="/wiki/Al-Masudi" title="Al-Masudi">Al-Masudi</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood196911_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood196911-32">[32]</a></sup> who is today regarded as the "<a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> of the Arabs"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETer-Ghevondyan196515_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETer-Ghevondyan196515-33">[33]</a></sup> and who Ibn Khaldun himself regarded as one of the most famous historians up until his time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood19695-6_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood19695-6-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>As an example, Ibn Khaldun notes that Al-Masudi and other historians reported that <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> counted the <a href="/wiki/Israelites" title="Israelites">Israelite</a> army as 600,000 or more soldiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood196911-12_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood196911-12-35">[35]</a></sup> Ibn Khaldun criticizes Al-Masudi for failing to take into account certain <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a>, questioning whether Egypt and Syria could have possibly held such a large number of soldiers, or whether an army of that size would be able to march or fight as a unit. He notes that the whole available territory would have been too small for such a large army, and argues that if "it were in battle formation, it would extend" several times "beyond the <a href="/wiki/Field_of_view" title="Field of view">field of vision</a>." He questions how two such parties could "fight with each other, or one battle formation gain the upper hand when one <a href="/wiki/Flanking_maneuver" title="Flanking maneuver">flank</a> does not know what the other flank is doing,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood196912_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood196912-36">[36]</a></sup> and that a coordinated battle movement in such a large group "would hardly be possible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood196914_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood196914-37">[37]</a></sup> He argues that the "situation in the present day testifies to the correctness of this statement" since the "past resembles the future more than one drop of water another." He then compares it to the Persian <a href="/wiki/Sasanian_Empire" title="Sasanian Empire">Sasanian Empire</a>, noting that it was far more vast than the Israelite Kingdom and yet the size of the <a href="/wiki/Military_of_the_Sasanian_Empire" title="Military of the Sasanian Empire">military of the Sasanian Empire</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_al-Q%C4%81disiyyah" title="Battle of al-Qādisiyyah">Battle of al-Qādisiyyah</a> amounted to 120,000 troops at most (citing the 8th-century historian <a href="/wiki/Sayf_ibn_Umar" title="Sayf ibn Umar">Sayf ibn Umar</a>). The <i>Muqaddimah</i> states that if the Israelites really did have such a large army, the extent of their empire would have been far larger, as "the size of administrative units and provinces under a particular dynasty is in direct proportion to the size of its militia and the groups that support the dynasty."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood196912_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood196912-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Muqaddimah</i> further notes that Moses lived only a few generations after <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">Jacob</a>, the founder of the Israelite tribes, according to the <a href="/wiki/Levite" title="Levite">Levite</a> tribe genealogy, as described by Al-Masudi. Ibn Khaldun argues that it "is improbable that the descendants of one man could branch out into such a number within four generations." The <i>Muqaddimah</i> also states that there was a general assumption that <a href="/wiki/Solomon" title="Solomon">Solomon</a>'s army was similarly large, but Ibn Khaldun refutes this, noting that Solomon came only eleven generations after Jacob, and argues that the "descendants of one man in eleven generations would not branch out into such a number, as has been assumed." He then agrees with another statement from the "Israelite Stories" suggesting that Solomon's army had 12,000 soldiers and 1,400 horses. He notes that this was when the Israelite state was at its strongest, making other claims giving larger numbers for the Israelite army unlikely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood196912-13_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood196912-13-38">[38]</a></sup> Ibn Khaldun notes that <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> have claimed the unrealistically large increase in the Israelite population within several generations was possible because it was a <a href="/wiki/Miracle" title="Miracle">miracle</a> of God, a claim that Ibn Khaldun did not dismiss completely. He considers such a miracle highly unlikely, but appears to be open to the possibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood196914_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood196914-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_theology">Islamic theology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Islamic theology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Muqaddimah</i> contains discussions on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_theology" title="Islamic theology">Islamic theology</a> which show that Ibn Khaldun was a follower of the orthodox <a href="/wiki/Ash%27ari" title="Ash'ari">Ash'ari</a> school of <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunni Islamic</a> thought and a supporter of <a href="/wiki/Al-Ghazali" title="Al-Ghazali">al-Ghazali</a>'s religious views. He was also a critic of <a href="/wiki/Neoplatonism" title="Neoplatonism">Neoplatonism</a>, particularly its notion of a <a href="/wiki/Great_chain_of_being" title="Great chain of being">hierarchy of being</a>. He argued that <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Theosis" title="Theosis">theosis</a><sup class="plainlinks noprint Inline-Template" style="vertical-align:text-top;white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="external text" href="http://dispenser.homenet.org/~dispenser/cgi-bin/dab_solver.py?page=Muqaddimah&amp;editintro=Template:Disambiguation_needed/editintro&amp;client=Template:Dn" rel="nofollow"><span title="Link needs disambiguation. (March 2013)">disambiguation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> requires the participation of <a href="/wiki/Revelation" title="Revelation">revelation</a> and is not possible through <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a> alone. He based his argument on the "irreducibility of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empirical" title="Empirical">empirical</a> nature of our knowledge of facts, which cannot then be converted into abstract and pure concepts at a higher level of human consciousness."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Muqaddimah</i> covers the historical development of <a href="/wiki/Kalam" title="Kalam">kalam</a> and the different schools of Islamic thought, notably the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mu%27tazili" title="Mu'tazili">Mu'tazili</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ash%27ari" title="Ash'ari">Ash'ari</a> schools. Ibn Khaldun, being a follower of the Ash'ari school, criticizes the views of the Mu'tazili school, and bases his criticisms on the views of <a href="/wiki/Abu_al-Hasan_al-Ash%27ari" title="Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari">Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari</a>, whom he describes as "the mediator between different approaches in the <a href="/wiki/Kalam" title="Kalam">kalam</a>." Ibn Khaldun also covers the historical development of <a href="/wiki/Logic_in_Islamic_philosophy" title="Logic in Islamic philosophy">Islamic logic</a> in the context of theology, as he viewed logic as being distinct from <a href="/wiki/Early_Islamic_philosophy" title="Early Islamic philosophy">early Islamic philosophy</a>, and believed that philosophy should remain separate from theology. The book also contains commentaries on verses from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_psychology">Islamic psychology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Islamic psychology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Psychology_in_medieval_Islam" title="Psychology in medieval Islam">Islamic psychology</a>, Ibn Khaldun wrote the following on <a href="/wiki/Dream_interpretation" title="Dream interpretation">dream interpretation</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Often, we may deduce (the existence of) that high spiritual world and the essences it contains, from visions and things we had not been aware of while awake but which we find in our sleep and which are brought to our attention in it and which, if they are true (<a href="/wiki/Dream" title="Dream">dreams</a>), conform with actuality. We thus know that they are true and come from the world of truth. "Confused dreams," on the other hand, are pictures of the imagination that are stored inside by <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perception</a> and to which the ability to think is applied, after (man) has retired from <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">sense</a> perception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood1969338_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood1969338-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_of_hadith">Science of hadith</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Science of hadith">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_of_hadith" title="Science of hadith">Science of hadith</a></div>
<p>Ibn Khaldun discussed the science of hadith. He disagreed with the use of <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a> in the evaluation of a <a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">hadith</a>, arguing that "there is no place for the <a href="/wiki/Intelligence" title="Intelligence">intellect</a> in them, save that the intellect may be used in connection with them to relate problems of detail with basic principles."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the authority of the <i><a href="/wiki/Sahih_al-Bukhari" title="Sahih al-Bukhari">Sahih al-Bukhari</a></i>, the <i>Muqaddimah</i> also argues that, despite the Islamic belief that the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> was altered by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jews</a>, the Muslims should neither believe nor disbelieve historical claims concerning the Torah made by Jews and <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christians</a>, particularly in regards to miraculous events. He states that:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>the statement concerning the alteration (of the Torah by the Jews) is unacceptable to thorough scholars and cannot be understood in its plain meaning, since custom prevents people who have a (revealed) religion from dealing with their divine scriptures in such a manner. This was mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_al-Bukhari" title="Muhammad al-Bukhari">al-Bukhari</a> in the <i>Sahih</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood196914_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood196914-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sharia_law_and_Fiqh_jurisprudence">Sharia law and Fiqh jurisprudence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Sharia law and Fiqh jurisprudence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun" title="Ibn Khaldun">Ibn Khaldun</a> was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulema" title="Ulema">Islamic jurist</a> and discussed the topics of <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">Sharia</a> (Islamic law) and <a href="/wiki/Fiqh" title="Fiqh">Fiqh</a> (Islamic jurisprudence) in his <i>Muqaddimah</i>. Ibn Khaldun wrote that "<a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence" title="Jurisprudence">Jurisprudence</a> is the knowledge of the classification of the laws of <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a>." In regards to jurisprudence, he acknowledged the inevitability of change in all aspects of a <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a>, and wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The conditions, customs and beliefs of peoples and nations do not indefinitely follow the same pattern and adhere to a constant course. There is rather, change with days and epochs, as well as passing from one state to another... such is the law of God that has taken place with regard to His subjects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Ibn Khaldun further described Fiqh jurisprudence as "knowledge of the rules of God which concern the actions of persons who own themselves bound to obey the law respecting what is required (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wajib" title="Wajib">wajib</a></i>), forbidden (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haraam" title="Haraam">haraam</a></i>), recommended (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mustahab" title="Mustahab">mandūb</a></i>), disapproved (<i><a href="/wiki/Makruh" title="Makruh">makruh</a></i>) or merely permitted (<i><a href="/wiki/Mubah" title="Mubah">mubah</a></i>)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_sciences">Natural sciences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Natural sciences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some of Ibn Khaldun's thoughts, according to some commentators, anticipate the <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biological</a> theory of <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kiros_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-kiros-45">[45]</a></sup> Ibn Khaldun asserted that humans developed from "the world of the monkeys", in a process by which "species become more numerous" in Chapter 1 of the <i>Muqaddimah</i>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kiros_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-kiros-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>This world with all the created things in it has a certain order and solid construction. It shows nexuses between causes and things caused, combinations of some parts of creation with others, and transformations of some existent things into others, in a pattern that is both remarkable and endless.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>One should then take a look at the world of creation. It started out from the minerals and progressed, in an ingenious, gradual manner, to plants and animals. The last stage of minerals is connected with the first stage of plants, such as herbs and seedless plants. The last stage of plants, such as palms and vines, is connected with the first stage of animals, such as snails and shellfish which have only the power of touch. The word 'connection' with regard to these created things means that the last stage of each group is fully prepared to become the first stage of the newest group.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The animal world then widens, its species become numerous, and, in a gradual process of creation, it finally leads to man, who is able to think and reflect. The higher stage of man is reached from the world of monkeys, in which both sagacity and perception are found, but which has not reached the stage of actual reflection and thinking. At this point we come to the first stage of man. This is as far as our (physical) observation extends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Ibn Khaldun believed that <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> are the most evolved form of <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, in that they have the ability to reason. The <i>Muqaddimah</i> also states in Chapter 6:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>We explained there that the whole of existence in (all) its simple and composite worlds is arranged in a natural order of ascent and descent, so that everything constitutes an uninterrupted continuum. The essences at the end of each particular stage of the worlds are by nature prepared to be transformed into the essence adjacent to them, either above or below them. This is the case with the simple material elements; it is the case with palms and vines, (which constitute) the last stage of plants, in their relation to snails and shellfish, (which constitute) the (lowest) stage of animals. It is also the case with monkeys, creatures combining in themselves cleverness and perception, in their relation to man, the being who has the ability to think and to reflect. The preparedness (for transformation) that exists on either side, at each stage of the worlds, is meant when (we speak about) their connection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Plants do not have the same fineness and power that animals have. Therefore, the sages rarely turned to them. Animals are the last and final stage of the three permutations. Minerals turn into plants, and plants into animals, but animals cannot turn into anything finer than themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>His evolutionary ideas appear to be similar to those found in the <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclopedia_of_the_Brethren_of_Purity" title="Encyclopedia of the Brethren of Purity">Encyclopedia of the Brethren of Purity</a></i>. Ibn Khaldun was also an adherent of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_determinism" title="Environmental determinism">environmental determinism</a>. He explained that <a href="/wiki/Black_people" title="Black people">black skin</a> was due to the hot climate of <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> and not due to their lineage, a theory that according to Rosenthal may have been influenced by the Greek geographical ideas expounded by Ptolemy's <i><a href="/wiki/Tetrabiblos" title="Tetrabiblos">Tetrabiblos</a></i>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-translator_Franz_Rosenthal_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-translator_Franz_Rosenthal-49">[49]</a></sup> Ibn Khaldun's environmental determinism thus dispelled the <a href="/wiki/Hamitic" title="Hamitic">Hamitic</a> theory, where the sons of <a href="/wiki/Ham_(son_of_Noah)" title="Ham (son of Noah)">Ham</a> were cursed by being black, as a myth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun" title="Ibn Khaldun">Ibn Khaldun</a> was a critic of the practice of <a href="/wiki/Alchemy_and_chemistry_in_medieval_Islam" title="Alchemy and chemistry in medieval Islam">alchemy and chemistry in medieval Islam</a>. In chapter 23 of his work, entitled <i>Fī ʿilm al-kimya</i>, he discussed the history of <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemy</a>, the views of alchemists such as <a href="/wiki/Jabir_ibn_Hayyan" title="Jabir ibn Hayyan">Jabir ibn Hayyan</a> (Geber), and the theories of the <a href="/wiki/Philosopher%27s_stone" title="Philosopher's stone">transmutation of metals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elixir_of_life" title="Elixir of life">elixir of life</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorelonRashed1996853.E2.80.93885_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorelonRashed1996853.E2.80.93885-51">[51]</a></sup> In chapter 26, entitled <i>Fi inkar thamrat al-kimya wa istihalat wujudiha wa ma yansha min al-mafasid</i>, he wrote a systematic refutation of alchemy on social,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorelonRashed1996853.E2.80.93885_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorelonRashed1996853.E2.80.93885-51">[51]</a></sup> scientific, philosophical and religious grounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>He begins his refutation on social grounds, arguing that many alchemists are incapable of earning a living because of the thought of becoming rich through alchemy and end up "losing their credibility because of the futility of their attempts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorelonRashed1996853.E2.80.93885_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorelonRashed1996853.E2.80.93885-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>He also argues that some alchemists resort to <a href="/wiki/Fraud" title="Fraud">fraud</a>, either openly by applying a thin layer of <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>/<a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> on top of silver/<a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> jewelry, or secretly using an artificial procedure of covering whitened <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">sublimated</a> <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, though only skilled <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experimenters</a> can carry out the latter. He admits, however, that most alchemists are honest and carry out their investigations in good faith with the belief that the transmutation of metals is possible, but on the basis that there has never been any successful attempt to date, he argues that transmutation is an implausible theory without any reliable scientific evidence to support it. He reports the earlier opinions of <a href="/wiki/Al-Farabi" title="Al-Farabi">al-Farabi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Al-Tughrai" title="Al-Tughrai">al-Tughrai</a> on alchemy, and then proceeds to advance his own arguments against it. One such argument is that "human <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> is powerless even to attain what is inferior to it" and that alchemy "resembles someone who wants to produce a man, an animal or a plant." Another sociological argument he uses is that, even if transmutation were possible, the disproportionate growth of gold and silver "would make <a href="/wiki/Financial_transaction" title="Financial transaction">transactions</a> useless and would run counter to divine <a href="/wiki/Wisdom" title="Wisdom">wisdom</a>." He ends his arguments with a restatement of his position: "Alchemy can only be achieved through psychic influences (<i>bi-ta'thirat al-nufus</i>). Extraordinary things are either miracles or witchcraft... They are unbounded; nobody can claim to acquire them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorelonRashed1996853.E2.80.93885_51-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorelonRashed1996853.E2.80.93885-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_theories">Other theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Other theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_theory">Climate theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Climate theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Muqaddimah</i> anticipated the <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> climate theory of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_determinism" title="Environmental determinism">environmental determinism</a>, later proposed by <a href="/wiki/Montesquieu" title="Montesquieu">Montesquieu</a> in the 18th century. Like Montesquieu, Ibn Khaldun studied "the physical environment in which man lives in order to understand how it influences him in his non-physical characteristics." He explained the differences between different peoples, whether <a href="/wiki/Nomad" title="Nomad">nomadic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sedentism" title="Sedentism">sedentary</a> peoples, including their customs and institutions, in terms of their "physical environment-habitat, climate, soil, food, and the different ways in which they are forced to satisfy their needs and obtain a living." This was a departure from the climatic theories expressed by authors from <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a> to <a href="/wiki/Jean_Bodin" title="Jean Bodin">Jean Bodin</a>. It has been suggested that Ibn Khaldun may have had an influence upon Montesquieu's theory through the traveller <a href="/wiki/Jean_Chardin" title="Jean Chardin">Jean Chardin</a>, who travelled to Persia and described a theory resembling Ibn Khaldun's climatic theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_theory">Political theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Political theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Asabiyyah" title="Asabiyyah">Asabiyyah</a></div>
<p>The <i>Muqaddimah</i> deals with various questions of <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political theory</a>. In some ways, his political theories show the influence of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, while in other ways they anticipate the works of <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccolò Machiavelli">Niccolò Machiavelli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes" title="Thomas Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</a>.</p>
<p>In the <i>Muqaddimah</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s introductory remarks, Ibn Khaldun agrees with the <a href="/wiki/Classical_republicanism" title="Classical republicanism">classical republicanism</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Aristotelianism" title="Aristotelianism">Aristotelian</a> proposition that man is political by nature, and that man's interdependence creates the need for the political <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a>. Yet Ibn Khaldun argues, like <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes" title="Thomas Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</a> later, that men and tribes need to defend themselves from potential attack by beast or even unjust men, and thus political communities are formed. The glue which holds such tribes together and eventually forms "royal authority" or the state, according to Ibn Khaldun, is ʿasabiyyah. Ibn Khaldun argues that the best type of political community is the Caliphate or the Islamic state, and argues that the neo-Platonist political theories of <a href="/wiki/Al-Farabi" title="Al-Farabi">al-Farabi</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibn_Sina" title="Ibn Sina">Ibn Sina</a> and the "perfect state" (<i>Madinatu l-Faḍīlah</i>) are useless because God's Law, the <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">sharia</a>, has been revealed to take account of public interest and the afterlife. The second most perfect state, Ibn Khaldun argues, is one based on justice and consideration for public welfare in this life, but not based on religious law and so not beneficial to one's afterlife. Ibn Khaldun calls this state blameworthy. Yet the worst type of state, according to Ibn Khaldun, is a tyranny wherein government usurps property rights and rules with injustice against the rights of men.</p>
<p>Ibn Khaldun also anticipates <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccolò Machiavelli">Niccolò Machiavelli</a> by attempting to answer the question of whether it is better for the ruler to be feared or loved. Ibn Khaldun, like Machiavelli, answers that it is best to be both (though in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prince" title="The Prince">The Prince</a></i>, Machiavelli argues it's ultimately more effective for a ruler to retain power through fear). However, unlike Machiavelli, Khaldun believes that if that is not possible then it is better to be loved than feared because fear creates many negative effects in the state's population.</p>
<p>Ibn Khaldun writes that civilizations have lifespans like individuals, and that every state will eventually fall because sedentary luxuries distract them, and eventually government begins to overtax citizens and begin injustice against property rights, and "injustice ruins civilization." Eventually after one dynasty or royal authority falls, it is replaced by another, in a continuous cycle. Machiavelli has a similar notion of sedentary lifestyles, <i>ozio</i>, that corrupts the state, and argues that periodic wars rejuvenate a republic.</p>
<p>The British philosopher-anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Gellner" title="Ernest Gellner">Ernest Gellner</a> considered Ibn Khaldun's definition of <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a>, "an institution which prevents injustice other than such as it commits itself", the best in the history of <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEErnest_Gellner1992239_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEErnest_Gellner1992239-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assessment_of_various_civilizations">Assessment of various civilizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Assessment of various civilizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While discussing his "new science", now associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_sciences" title="Social sciences">social sciences</a>, Ibn Khaldūn states that no other author before him, as far as he was aware, had written about it. However, he was aware that much knowledge of the past had been lost, and thus he was open to the possibility that someone might have anticipated him but that their work had not survived:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Perhaps they have written exhaustively on this topic, and their work did not reach us. There are many sciences. There have been numerous sages among the nations of mankind. The knowledge that has not come down to us is larger than the knowledge that has. Where are the sciences of the <a href="/wiki/Persian_people" title="Persian people">Persians</a> that ‘<a href="/wiki/Umar" title="Umar">Umar</a> ordered to be wiped out at the time of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rashidun_conquest_of_the_Sassanian_Empire" title="Rashidun conquest of the Sassanian Empire">the conquest</a>? Where are the sciences of the <a href="/wiki/Chaldea" title="Chaldea">Chaladaeans</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syrians</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">Babylonians</a>, and the scholarly products and results that were theirs? Where are the sciences of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copt" title="Copt">Copts</a>, their <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">predecessors</a>? The sciences of only one nation, the <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a>, have come down to us, because they were translated through <a href="/wiki/Al-Ma%27mun" title="Al-Ma'mun">Al-Ma'mun</a>'s efforts. He was successful in this direction because he had many translators at his disposal and spent much money in this connection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Ibn Khaldūn characterized <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> as "the First Teacher", for his having "improved the methods of logic and systematized its problems and details."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood196939.2C_383_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood196939.2C_383-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arab_and_Persian_civilizations">Arab and Persian civilizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Arab and Persian civilizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ibn Khaldun makes a clear distinction between two types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab_people" title="Arab people">Arab people</a>; those who are Arab by descent, i.e. of ethnic Arab descent, and those who are Arab by language, i.e. ethnically non-Arab populations who speak <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> as a first language. He never refers to that final group as being Arabs, rather he called them by their ethnicity or places of origin (i.e. 'Persians' or 'the inhabitants of Egypt'):</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In that connection, "non-Arab" meant non-Arab by descent. Such non-Arabs had a long (history of) <a href="/wiki/Sedentism" title="Sedentism">sedentary</a> culture which, as we have established, causes cultivation of the crafts and habits, including the sciences. Being non-Arab in language is something quite different, and this is what is meant here.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood1969433_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood1969433-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>About Arab bedouins, he wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The Arab bedouins dominate only of the plains, because they are, by their savage nature, people of pillage and corruption. They pillage everything that they can take without fighting or taking risks, then flee to their refuge in the wilderness, and do not stand and do battle unless in self-defense. So when they encounter any difficulty or obstacle, they leave it alone and look for easier prey. And tribes well-fortified against them on the slopes of the hills escape their corruption and destruction, because they prefer not to climb hills, nor expend effort, nor take risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood1969_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood1969-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>On the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_conquests" title="Muslim conquests">Arab conquests</a> of the 7th century:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Religious propaganda gives a dynasty at its beginning another power in addition to that of the group feeling it possessed as the result of the number of its supporters... This happened to the <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">Arabs</a> at the beginning of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> during the Muslim conquests. The <a href="/wiki/Rashidun_army" title="Rashidun army">armies of the Muslims</a> at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_al-Q%C4%81disiyyah" title="Battle of al-Qādisiyyah">al-Qadisiyah</a> and at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Yarmouk" title="Battle of Yarmouk">Yarmuk</a> numbered some 30,000 in each case, while the Persian troops at al-Qadisiyah numbered 120,000, and <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_army" title="Byzantine army">the troops</a> of <a href="/wiki/Heraclius" title="Heraclius">Heraclius</a>, according to <a href="/wiki/Al-Waqidi" title="Al-Waqidi">al-Waqidi</a>, 400,000. Neither of the two parties was able to withstand the Arabs, who routed them and seized what they possessed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood1969126_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood1969126-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Some of the content in the book is also related to the "Hadith of Persians and belief":</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Thus the founders of grammar were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sibawaih" title="Sibawaih">Sibawaih</a> and after him, <a href="/wiki/Al-Farisi" title="Al-Farisi">al-Farisi</a> and Az-Zajjaj. All of them were of non-Arab (Persian) descent... They invented rules of <a href="/wiki/Arabic_grammar" title="Arabic grammar">(Arabic) grammar</a>...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood1969429_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood1969429-60">[60]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulema" title="Ulema">great jurists</a> were Persians... only the Persians engaged in the task of preserving knowledge and writing systematic scholarly works. Thus the truth of the statement of <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">the prophet</a> becomes apparent, "If learning were suspended in the highest parts of heaven the Persians would attain it"... The intellectual sciences were also the preserve of the Persians, left alone by the Arabs, who did not cultivate them... as was the case with all crafts... This situation continued in the cities as long as the Persians and Persian countries, Iraq, <a href="/wiki/Greater_Khorasan" title="Greater Khorasan">Khorasan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transoxiana" title="Transoxiana">Transoxiana</a>, retained their sedentary culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Here again he uses the term "Arab" to refer to the ethnic Arabs of the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a> and "<a href="/wiki/Ajam" title="Ajam">Ajam</a>" to refer to non-Arabs in general, though it often it referred more specifically to <a href="/wiki/Iranian_peoples" title="Iranian peoples">Iranian peoples</a> from a sedentary <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Iran" title="Culture of Iran">Persian culture</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iranian_plateau" title="Iranian plateau">Iranian plateau</a>. Ibn Khaldun made a distinction between being linguistically Arabized and being culturally Arabized. Cultural Arabization to him meant adopting a tribal, bedouin and desert livestyle and was opposite to the <a href="/wiki/Sedentism" title="Sedentism">sedentary</a>, urban culture, which was inherently non-Arab. Throughout his work he makes the point that Arabs during the early Muslim expansion, were indeed de-Arabized and to some degree adopted Persian and Greek sedentary culture. Also note that in medieval <a href="/wiki/Islamic_literature" title="Islamic literature">Islamic literature</a>, there were two regions known as Iraq: the <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq-i Arab</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Iraq" title="Persian Iraq">Persian Iraq</a>. The Persian Iraq mentioned by Ibn Khaldun is the historic Iraq-e-Ajam (Persian Iraq) which constitutes the triangle of <a href="/wiki/Isfahan" title="Isfahan">Isfahan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shiraz" title="Shiraz">Shiraz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hamadan" title="Hamadan">Hamadan</a>.</p>
<p>Ibn Khaldun, however, notes that by his time, the study of science in Persian culture had declined and was eventually surpassed by the <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Egypt" title="Culture of Egypt">culture of Egypt</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Mamluk_Sultanate_(Cairo)" title="Mamluk Sultanate (Cairo)">Mamluk Sultanate</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>This situation continued in the cities as long as the Persians and the Persian countries, the 'Iraq, Khurasan, and Transoxania, retained their sedentary culture. But when those cities fell into ruins, sedentary culture, which God has devised for the attainment of sciences and crafts, disappeared from them. Along with it, scholarship altogether disappeared from among the non-Arabs (Persians), who were (now) engulfed by the desert attitude. Scholarship was restricted to cities with an abundant sedentary culture. Today, no (city) has a more abundant sedentary culture than <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a> (Egypt). It is the mother of the world, the great center (<a href="/wiki/Iwan" title="Iwan">Iwan</a>) of Islam, and the mainspring of the sciences and the crafts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood1969430-1_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood1969430-1-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Some sedentary culture has also survived in Transoxania, because the dynasty there provides some sedentary culture. Therefore, they have there a certain number of the sciences and the crafts, which cannot be denied. Our attention was called to this fact by the contents of the writings of a (Transoxanian) scholar, which have reached us in this country. He is <a href="/wiki/Taftazani" title="Taftazani">Sa'd-ad-din at-Taftazani</a>. As far as the other non-Arabs (Persians) are concerned, we have not seen, since the imam <a href="/wiki/Ibn_al-Khatib" title="Ibn al-Khatib">Ibn al-Khatib</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nasir_al-Din_al-Tusi" title="Nasir al-Din al-Tusi">Nasir-ad-din at-Tusi</a>, any discussions that could be referred to as indicating their ultimate excellence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood1969431_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood1969431-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Ibn Khaldun discussed the <a href="/wiki/History_of_science" title="History of science">history of science</a>, and wrote the following on the history of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_in_medieval_Islam" title="Science in medieval Islam">Islamic science</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The Muslims desired to learn the sciences of foreign nations. They made them their own through translations. They pressed them into the mould of their own views. They took them over into their own language from the non-Arab languages and surpassed the achievements of the non-Arabs in them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish_civilization">Jewish civilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Jewish civilization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On Jewish civilization:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The other religious groups did not have a universal mission, and the holy war was not a religious duty to them, save only for purposes of defence...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood1969183_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood1969183-65">[65]</a></sup> They are merely required to establish their religion among their own people. This is why the <a href="/wiki/Israelites" title="Israelites">Israelites</a> after <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joshua" title="Joshua">Joshua</a> remained unconcerned with royal authority for about four hundred years. Their only concern was to establish their religion... The Israelites dispossessed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canaanites" title="Canaanites">Canaanites</a> of the land that God had given them as their heritage in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> and the surrounding region, as it had been explained to them through Moses. The nations of the <a href="/wiki/Philistines" title="Philistines">Philistines</a>, the Canaanites, the <a href="/wiki/Armenians" title="Armenians">Armenians</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edomites" title="Edomites">Edomites</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ammon" title="Ammon">Ammonites</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moabites" title="Moabites">Moabites</a> fought against them. During that time political leadership was entrusted to the elders among them. The Israelites remained in that condition for about four hundred years. They did not have any royal power and were harassed by attacks from foreign nations. Therefore, they asked God through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samuel_(Bible)" title="Samuel (Bible)">Samuel</a>, one of their prophets, that he permit them to make someone king over them. Thus, <a href="/wiki/Saul" title="Saul">Saul</a> became their king. He defeated the foreign nations and killed <a href="/wiki/Goliath" title="Goliath">Goliath</a>, the ruler of Philistines. After Saul, <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">David</a> became king, and then <a href="/wiki/Solomon" title="Solomon">Solomon</a>. His kingdom flourished and extended to the borders of the land of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hijaz" title="Hijaz">Hijaz</a> and further to the borders of <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> and to the borders of the land of the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantines</a>. After Solomon, the tribes split into two dynasties. One of the dynasties was that of the ten tribes in the region of <a href="/wiki/Nablus" title="Nablus">Nablus</a>, the capital of which is <a href="/wiki/Samaria" title="Samaria">Samaria</a> (Sabastiyah), and the other that of the children of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judah_(Bible)" title="Judah (Bible)">Judah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benjamin" title="Benjamin">Benjamin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>. Their royal authority had had an uninterrupted duration of a thousand years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood1969183.E2.80.93184_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood1969183.E2.80.93184-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sub-Saharan_Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Muqaddimah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Sub-Saharan Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ibn Khaldūn's description of the various <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan African</a> states:</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">The Western Sahel</a>:</p>
<p>Many translations of Ibn Khaldun were translated during the colonial era in order to fit the colonial propaganda machine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=6swTAAAAYAAJ&amp;pg=PA61" rel="nofollow"><i>The Negro land of the Arabs Examined and Explained</i></a> was written in 1841 and gives excerpts of older translations that were not part of colonial propaganda:</p>
<dl>
<dd>When the conquest of the West (by the Arabs) was completed, and merchants began to penetrate into the interior, they saw no nation of the Blacks so mighty as Ghanah, the dominions of which extended westward as far as the Ocean. The King's court was kept in the city of Ghanah, which, according to the author of the Book of Roger (El Idrisi), and the author of the Book of Roads and Realms (El Bekri), is divided into two parts, standing on both banks of the Nile, and ranks among the largest and most populous cities of the world. The people of Ghanah had for neighbours, on the east, a nation, which, according to historians, was called Susu; after which came another named Mali; and after that another known by the name of Kaukau; although some people prefer a different orthography, and write this name Kagho. The last-named nation was followed by a people called Tekrur. The people of Ghanah declined in course of time, being overwhelmed or absorbed by the Molaththemun (or muffled people; that is, the Morabites), who, adjoining them on the north towards the Berber country, attacked them, and, taking possession of their territory, compelled them to embrace the Mohammedan religion. The people of Ghanah, being invaded at a later period by the Susu, a nation of Blacks in their neighbourhood, were exterminated, or mixed with other Black nations. [<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>]</dd>
</dl>
<p>Ibn Khaldun suggests a link between the decline of <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a> and rise of the <a href="/wiki/Almoravid_dynasty" title="Almoravid dynasty">Almoravid dynasty</a>. However, there is little evidence of there actually being an Almoravid conquest of Ghana[<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>]<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Of those further south of Ghana he writes (it should be noted that the English translation of the text used the word "<a href="/wiki/Negro" title="Negro">Negro</a>" as a translation for the Arabic world "<a href="/wiki/Zanj" title="Zanj">Zanj</a>"),</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>To the south of this...there is a Negro people called Lamlam. They are unbelievers. They brand themselves on the face and temples. The people of Ghanah and <a href="/wiki/Takrur" title="Takrur">Takrur</a> invade their country, capture them, and sell them to merchants who transport them to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maghrib" title="Maghrib">Maghrib</a>. There, they constitute the ordinary mass of slaves. Beyond them to the south, there is no civilization in the proper sense. There are only humans who are closer to dumb animals than to rational beings. They live in thickets and caves and eat herbs and unprepared grain. They frequently eat each other. They cannot be considered human beings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">Nubia</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In the middle of the first zone along the Nile, lie the countries of the Nubah and the Abyssinians and some of the oases down to Assuan. A settled part of the Nubah country is the city of Dongola, west of the Nile. Beyond it are 'Alwah 83 and Yulaq. Beyond them, a six days' journey north of Yulaq, is the mountain of the cataracts. This is a mountain which rises to a great height on the Egyptian side but is much less elevated on the side of the country of the Nubah, The Nile cuts through it and flows down precipitately in tremendous cascades for a long distance. Boats cannot get through. Cargoes from the Sudanese boats are taken off and carried on pack animals to Assuan at the entrance to Upper Egypt. In the same way, the cargoes of the boats from Upper Egypt are carried over the cataracts. The distance from the cataracts to Assuan is a twelve day's journey. The oases on the west bank of the Nile there are now in ruins. They show traces of ancient settlement.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Abyssinia (<a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>):</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In the middle of the first zone, in its fifth section, is the country of the Abyssinians, through which a river flows, which comes from beyond the equator and 85 flows toward the land of the Nubah, where it flows into the Nile and so on down into Egypt. Many people have held fantastic opinions about it and thought that it was part of the Nile of the Qumr (Mountain of the Moon). Ptolemy mentioned it in the Geography. He mentioned that it did not belong to the Nile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Slavery:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun" title="Ibn Khaldun">Ibn Khaldun</a> wrote that Sub-Saharan Africans in the <a href="/wiki/Arab_slave_trade" title="Arab slave trade">Arab slave trade</a> were submissive to slavery and specifically said "Therefore, the Negro nations are, as a rule, submissive to slavery, because (Negroes) have little that is (essentially) human and possess attributes that are quite similar to those of dumb animals, as we have stated."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawood1969117_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawood1969117-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
