<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Armistice of Mudros</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>The <b>Armistice of Mudros</b> (<a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><i>Mondros Mütarekesi</i></span>), concluded on 30 October 1918, ended the hostilities, at noon the next day, in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Eastern_theatre_of_World_War_I" title="Middle Eastern theatre of World War I">Middle Eastern theatre</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allies_(World_War_I)" title="Allies (World War I)">Allies</a> of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. It was signed by the Ottoman Minister of Marine Affairs <a href="/wiki/Rauf_Orbay" title="Rauf Orbay">Rauf Bey</a> and the British <a href="/wiki/Admiral" title="Admiral">Admiral</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somerset_Arthur_Gough-Calthorpe" title="Somerset Arthur Gough-Calthorpe">Somerset Arthur Gough-Calthorpe</a>, on board <a href="/wiki/HMS_Agamemnon_(1906)" title="HMS Agamemnon (1906)">HMS <i>Agamemnon</i></a> in <a href="/wiki/Moudros" title="Moudros">Moudros</a> harbor on the <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greek</a> island of <a href="/wiki/Lemnos" title="Lemnos">Lemnos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>As part of several conditions to the armistice, the Ottomans surrendered their remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garrisons" title="Garrisons">garrisons</a> outside <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, as well as granted the Allies the right to occupy forts controlling the Straits of the <a href="/wiki/Dardanelles" title="Dardanelles">Dardanelles</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosporus" title="Bosporus">Bosporus</a>; and the right to occupy the same "in case of disorder" any Ottoman territory in the event of a threat to their security. The Ottoman army including the Ottoman air force was demobilized, and all ports, railways, and other strategic points were made available for use by the Allies. In the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>, the Ottomans had to retreat to within the pre-war borders between the Ottoman and the Russian Empires.</p>
<p>The armistice was followed by the <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_Constantinople" title="Occupation of Constantinople">occupation of Constantinople</a> (<a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>) and the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Partitioning_of_the_Ottoman_Empire" title="Partitioning of the Ottoman Empire">partitioning of the Ottoman Empire</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_S%C3%A8vres" title="Treaty of Sèvres">Treaty of Sèvres</a> (10 August 1920) which was signed in the aftermath of World War I was never ratified by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_Parliament" title="Ottoman Parliament">Ottoman Parliament</a> in Istanbul (the Ottoman Parliament was disbanded by the Allies on 11 April 1920 due to the overwhelming opposition of the Turkish MPs to the provisions discussed in Sèvres). It was later superseded by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lausanne" title="Treaty of Lausanne">Treaty of Lausanne</a> (24 July 1923) following the Turkish victory at the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_War_of_Independence" title="Turkish War of Independence">Turkish War of Independence</a> (1919–1922) which was conducted by the <a href="/wiki/Grand_National_Assembly_of_Turkey" title="Grand National Assembly of Turkey">Grand National Assembly of Turkey</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ankara" title="Ankara">Ankara</a> (established on 23 April 1920 by <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Kemal_Atat%C3%BCrk" title="Mustafa Kemal Atatürk">Mustafa Kemal Pasha</a> and his followers, including his colleagues in the disbanded Ottoman military, and numerous former MPs of the closed Ottoman Parliament in Istanbul.)</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armistice_of_Mudros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Middle_Eastern_theatre_of_World_War_I" title="Middle Eastern theatre of World War I">Middle Eastern theatre of World War I</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caucasus_Campaign" title="Caucasus Campaign">Caucasus Campaign</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_Front" title="Macedonian Front">Macedonian Front</a></div>
<p>World War I took a chaotic turn in 1918 for the Ottoman Empire. With <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Yudenich" title="Nikolai Yudenich">Yudenich</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Russian_Caucasus_Army_(World_War_I)" title="Russian Caucasus Army (World War I)">Russian Caucasus Army</a> deserting after the collapse of the Russian Empire, the Ottomans regained ground in <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a> and even pushed into formerly Russian-controlled Caucasus with, at first, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vehip_Pasha" title="Vehip Pasha">Vehip Pasha</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Third_Army_(Ottoman_Empire)" title="Third Army (Ottoman Empire)">Ottoman 3rd Army</a> and, later beginning in June 1918, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuri_Pasha" title="Nuri Pasha">Nuri Pasha</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Army_of_the_Caucasus" title="Islamic Army of the Caucasus">Army of Islam</a> which excluded German officers and men. The <a href="/wiki/Caucasus_Campaign" title="Caucasus Campaign">Caucasus Campaign</a> put the Ottomans at odds with their ally, Germany, which had been hoping to purchase Caucasus oil from the Bolshevik government in Moscow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[a]</a></sup> The Ottomans wanted to establish its eastern borders<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[b]</a></sup> The Ottoman armies advanced far into Caucasus, gathering supporters as far away as <a href="/wiki/Tashkent" title="Tashkent">Tashkent</a>, on the eastern side of the Caspian Sea. Additionally, with the Bolsheviks in power in Moscow, chaos spread in Persia, as the Russo-British favoring government of <a href="/wiki/Ahmad_Shah_Qajar" title="Ahmad Shah Qajar">Ahmad Shah Qajar</a> lost authority outside of the capital. In contrast, in Syria, the Ottomans were steadily pushed back by British forces, culminating in the <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_Damascus_(1918)" title="Capture of Damascus (1918)">fall of Damascus</a> in October 1918. Hopes were initially high for the Ottomans that their losses in Syria might be compensated with successes in the Caucasus. <a href="/wiki/Enver_Pasha" title="Enver Pasha">Enver Pasha</a>, one of the most influential members of the Ottoman government, maintained an optimistic stance, hid information that made the Ottoman position appear weak, and led most of the Ottoman elite to believe that the war was still winnable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fromkin_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-fromkin-4">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Developments in Southeast Europe quashed the Ottoman government's hopes. The <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_Front" title="Macedonian Front">Macedonian Front</a>, also known as the Salonika campaign, had been largely stable since 1916. In September 1918, the <a href="/wiki/Allied_Army_of_the_Orient" title="Allied Army of the Orient">Allied forces</a> (under the command of <a href="/wiki/Louis_Franchet_d%27Esp%C3%A8rey" title="Louis Franchet d'Espèrey">Louis Franchet d'Espèrey</a>) mounted a <a href="/wiki/Vardar_Offensive" title="Vardar Offensive">sudden offensive</a> which proved quite successful. The Bulgarian army was defeated, and Bulgaria was forced to sue for peace in the <a href="/wiki/Armistice_of_Salonica" title="Armistice of Salonica">Armistice of Salonica</a>. That undermined both the German and Ottoman cause simultaneously, as the Germans had no troops to spare to defend Austria-Hungary from the newly formed vulnerability in Southeastern Europe after the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)#1918.E2.80.94Final_offensives" title="Western Front (World War I)">losses it had suffered in France</a>, and the Ottomans suddenly faced having to defend <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> against an overland European siege without help from the Bulgarians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fromkin_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-fromkin-4">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Grand Vizier <a href="/wiki/Talaat_Pasha" title="Talaat Pasha">Talaat Pasha</a> visited <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, Germany, and <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a>, Bulgaria in September 1918. He came away with the understanding that the war was no longer winnable. With Germany likely seeking a separate peace, the Ottomans would be forced to do so as well. Talaat convinced the other members of the ruling party that they must resign, as the Allies would impose far harsher terms if they thought the people who started the war were still in power. He also sought out the United States to see if he could surrender to them and gain the benefits of the <a href="/wiki/Fourteen_Points" title="Fourteen Points">Fourteen Points</a> despite the Ottoman Empire and the United States not being at war; however, the Americans never responded, as they were waiting on British advice as to how to respond that never came. On October 13, Talaat and the rest of his ministry resigned. <a href="/wiki/Ahmed_Izzet_Pasha" title="Ahmed Izzet Pasha">Ahmed Izzet Pasha</a> replaced Talaat as Grand Vizier. Two days after taking office, he sent the captured British General <a href="/wiki/Charles_Vere_Ferrers_Townshend" title="Charles Vere Ferrers Townshend">Charles Vere Ferrers Townshend</a> to the Allies to seek terms on an armistice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fromkin_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-fromkin-4">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Negotiations">Negotiations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armistice_of_Mudros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Negotiations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The British Cabinet received word of the offer and were eager to negotiate a deal. The standing terms of the alliance was that the first member that was approached for an armistice should conduct the negotiations; the British government interpreted that to mean that Britain conduct the negotiations and alone. It is not entirely clear whether it was the sincere British interpretation of the alliance terms, fears that the French would insist on over-harsh demands and foil a treaty or a desire to cut the French out of territorial "spoils" promised to them in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sykes-Picot_agreement" title="Sykes-Picot agreement">Sykes-Picot agreement</a>. Townshend also indicated that the Ottomans preferred to deal with the British; he did not know about the American contact or that Talaat had sent an emissary to the French as well but that emissary had been slower to respond back. The British cabinet empowered <a href="/wiki/Somerset_Gough-Calthorpe" title="Somerset Gough-Calthorpe">Admiral Calthorpe</a> to conduct the negotiations with an explicit exclusion of the French from them. They also suggested an Armistice rather than a full peace treaty, in the belief that a peace treaty would require the approval of all of the Allied nations and so be too slow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fromkin_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-fromkin-4">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The negotiations began on Sunday, October 27 on the <a href="/wiki/HMS_Agamemnon_(1906)" title="HMS Agamemnon (1906)">HMS <i>Agamemnon</i></a>, a British battleship. The British refused to admit French Vice-Admiral <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Fran%C3%A7oise-Charles_Amet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jean-Françoise-Charles Amet (page does not exist)">Jean-Françoise-Charles Amet</a>, the senior French naval officer in the area, despite his desire to join; the Ottoman delegation, headed by Minister of Marine Affairs <a href="/wiki/Rauf_Orbay" title="Rauf Orbay">Rauf Bey</a>, indicated that it was acceptable as they were accredited only to the British, not the French.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fromkin_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-fromkin-4">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both sides did not know that the other was actually quite eager to sign a deal and willing to give up their objectives to do so. The British delegation had been given a list of 24 demands but were told to concede on any of them except allowing the occupation of the forts on the Dardanelles as well as free passage through the <a href="/wiki/Bosphorus" title="Bosphorus">Bosphorus</a>; the British desired access to the Black Sea for the <a href="/wiki/Romania_during_World_War_I" title="Romania during World War I">Rumanian front</a>. Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a> also desired to make a deal quickly before the United States could step in; according to the diary of <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Hankey,_1st_Baron_Hankey" title="Maurice Hankey, 1st Baron Hankey">Maurice Hankey</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>[Lloyd George] was also very contemptuous of President Wilson and anxious to arrange the division of Turkey between France, Italy, and G.B. before speaking to America. He also thought it would attract less attention to our enormous gains during the war if we swallowed our share of Turkey now, and the German colonies later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fromkin_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-fromkin-4">[2]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The Ottoman authorities, for their part, believed the war to be lost and would have accepted almost any demands placed on them. As a result, the initial draft prepared by the British was accepted largely unchanged; the Ottoman side did not know it could have pushed back on most of the clauses, and the British did not know they could have demanded even more. Still, the terms were largely pro-British and close to an outright surrender; the Ottoman Empire ceded the rights to the Allies to occupy "in case of disorder" any Ottoman territory, a vague and broad clause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fromkin_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-fromkin-4">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The French were displeased with the precedent; French Premier <a href="/wiki/Georges_Clemenceau" title="Georges Clemenceau">Georges Clemenceau</a> disliked the British making unilateral decisions in so important a matter. Lloyd George countered that the French had concluded a similar armistice on short notice in the Armistice of Salonica, which had been negotiated by French General d'Esperey and that Great Britain (and Tsarist Russia) had committed the vast majority of troops to the campaign against the Ottoman Empire. The French agreed to accept the matter as closed. The Ottoman educated public, however, was given misleadingly positive impressions of the severity of the terms of the Armistice. It thought its terms were considerably more lenient than they actually were, a source of discontent later that the Allies had betrayed the offered terms during the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_War_of_Independence" title="Turkish War of Independence">Turkish War of Independence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fromkin_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-fromkin-4">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armistice_of_Mudros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Armistice of Mudros officially brought hostilities to an end between the Allies and the Ottoman Empire. However, incursions by the Italians and Greeks into Anatolia in the name of "restoring order" soon came close to an outright partition of the country. The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_S%C3%A8vres" title="Treaty of Sèvres">Treaty of Sèvres</a> in 1920 officially partitioned the Ottoman Empire into zones of influence; however, the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_War_of_Independence" title="Turkish War of Independence">Turkish War of Independence</a> (1919–23) saw the rejection of the treaty by Turkish nationalist forces based in Ankara, who eventually took control of the Anatolian Peninsula. Ottoman territory in Syria, Palestine, and Arabia stayed as distributed by the Treaty of Sèvres while the borders of the modern state of <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> were set by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lausanne" title="Treaty of Lausanne">Treaty of Lausanne</a> in 1923.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armistice_of_Mudros&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: lower-alpha;">
<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-2">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The Bolsheviks had support only in Petrograd and Moscow in 1917 and 1918. After allowing both Trotsky and Lenin to return to Russia by train from Switzerland and lead the October Revolution, Germany considered the Bolshevik government a puppet state under its power. After the signing of the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk, most Russians disliked the terms of the Bolshevik signed treaty and believed that the Bolsheviks were a puppet under German interests, too.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-3"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-3">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Brest-Litovsk" title="Treaty of Brest-Litovsk">Treaty of Brest-Litovsk</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trabzon_peace_conference" title="Trabzon peace conference">Trabzon peace conference</a> convened but failed to define the borders between the Ottoman Empire and the <a href="/wiki/Transcaucasian_Democratic_Federative_Republic" title="Transcaucasian Democratic Federative Republic">Transcaucasian Democratic Federative Republic</a>. This led to the recognition that a state of war exists between Tiflis and Constantinople in April 1918.</span></li>
</ol>
</div>
