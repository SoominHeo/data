<p><b>Murad II</b> (June 1404, <a href="/wiki/Amasya" title="Amasya">Amasya</a> – 3 February 1451, <a href="/wiki/Edirne" title="Edirne">Edirne</a>) (<a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish_language" title="Ottoman Turkish language">Ottoman Turkish</a>: مراد ثانى <i>Murād-ı <u>s</u>ānī</i>, <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>:<i>II. Murat</i>) was the Ottoman <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">Sultan</a> from 1421 to 1444 and 1446 to 1451.</p>
<p>Murad II's reign was marked by the long war he fought against the Christian feudal lords of the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Turkic_peoples" title="Turkic peoples">Turkish</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatolian_Beyliks" title="Anatolian Beyliks">beyliks</a> in <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, a conflict that lasted 25 years. He was brought up in <a href="/wiki/Amasya" title="Amasya">Amasya</a>, and ascended the throne on the death of his father <a href="/wiki/Mehmed_I" title="Mehmed I">Mehmed I</a>. His mother was Valide Sultan <a href="/wiki/Emine_Hatun" title="Emine Hatun">Emine Hatun</a> (daughter of Suleyman Bey, ruler of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dulkadirids" title="Dulkadirids">Dulkadirids</a>), his father's third consort. Their marriage served as an alliance between the Ottomans and this buffer state, and produced a son, <a href="/wiki/Mehmed_the_Conqueror" title="Mehmed the Conqueror">Mehmed II</a>, who would go on to successfully conquer the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>'s capital, <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, in 1453.</p>

<p>Murad was born in June 1404 to Sultan <a href="/wiki/Mehmed_I" title="Mehmed I">Mehmed I</a> and his wife <a href="/wiki/Emine_Hatun" title="Emine Hatun">Emine Hatun</a>, and he spent his early childhood in <a href="/wiki/Amasya" title="Amasya">Amasya</a>. In 1410, Murad came along with his father to the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman capital</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edirne" title="Edirne">Edirne</a>. After his father ascended to the Ottoman throne, he made Murad governor of the Amasya <a href="/wiki/Sanjak" title="Sanjak">Sanjak</a>. Murad remained at Amasya until the death of <a href="/wiki/Mehmed_I" title="Mehmed I">Mehmed I</a> in 1421. He was solemnly recognized as <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">sultan</a> of the Ottoman Sultanate at sixteen years of age, girded with the sabre of Osman at <a href="/wiki/Bursa" title="Bursa">Bursa</a>, and the troops and officers of the state willingly paid homage to him as their sovereign.</p>

<p>Murad's reign was troubled by insurrection early on. The <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> Emperor, <a href="/wiki/Manuel_II_Palaiologos" title="Manuel II Palaiologos">Manuel II</a>, released the 'pretender'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_%C3%87elebi" title="Mustafa Çelebi">Mustafa Çelebi</a> (known as Düzmece Mustafa) from confinement and acknowledged him as the legitimate heir to the throne of <a href="/wiki/Bayezid_I" title="Bayezid I">Bayezid I</a> (1389–1402). The Byzantine Emperor had first secured a stipulation that Mustafa should, if successful, repay him for his liberation by giving up a large number of important cities. The pretender was landed by the Byzantine galleys in the European dominion of the sultan and for a time made rapid progress. Many Turkish soldiers joined him, and he defeated and killed the veteran general <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beyazid_Pasha" title="Beyazid Pasha">Beyazid Pasha</a>, whom Murad had sent to fight him. Mustafa defeated Murad's army and declared himself Sultan of Adrianople (modern <a href="/wiki/Edirne" title="Edirne">Edirne</a>). He then crossed the <a href="/wiki/Dardanelles" title="Dardanelles">Dardanelles</a> to Asia with a large army; but the young Sultan showed in this emergency that he possessed military and political abilities worthy of his best ancestors. Mustafa was out-manoeuvered in the middle of the field, and his troops, whose confidence in his person and cause he had lost by his violence and incapacity, passed over in large numbers to Murad II. Mustafa took refuge in the city of <a href="/wiki/Gallipoli" title="Gallipoli">Gallipoli</a>, but the sultan, who was greatly aided by a <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoese</a> commander named Adorno, besieged him there and stormed the place. Mustafa was taken and put to death by the sultan, who then turned his arms against the Roman emperor and declared his resolution to punish the <a href="/wiki/Palaiologos" title="Palaiologos">Palaiologos</a> for their unprovoked enmity by the capture of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>.</p>

Murad II and John Hunyadi</div>

<p>Murad II then formed a new army called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Azeb&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Azeb (page does not exist)">Azeb</a> in 1421 and marched through the Byzantine Empire and laid siege to Constantinople. While Murad was besieging the city, the Byzantines, in league with some independent <a href="/wiki/Turkic_peoples" title="Turkic peoples">Turkish</a> <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolian</a> states, sent the sultan's younger brother <a href="/wiki/K%C3%BC%C3%A7%C3%BCk_Mustafa" title="Küçük Mustafa">Küçük Mustafa</a> (who was only 13 years old) to rebel against the sultan and besiege Bursa. Murad had to abandon the siege of Constantinople in order to deal with his rebellious brother. He caught Prince Mustafa and executed him. The Anatolian states that had been constantly plotting against him — <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aydinids" title="Aydinids">Aydinids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germiyanids" title="Germiyanids">Germiyanids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Menteshe" title="Menteshe">Menteshe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beylik_of_Teke" title="Beylik of Teke">Teke</a> — were annexed and henceforth became part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_Sultanate" title="Ottoman Sultanate">Ottoman Sultanate</a>.</p>
<p>Murad II then declared war against <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venice</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karamanid" title="Karamanid">Karamanid</a> Emirate, <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>. The Karamanids were defeated in 1428 and <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venice</a> withdrew in 1432 following the defeat at the second <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Thessalonica_(1422%E2%80%931430)" title="Siege of Thessalonica (1422–1430)">Siege of Thessalonica</a> in 1430. In the 1430s Murad captured vast territories in the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> and succeeded in annexing Serbia in 1439. In 1441 the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> joined the <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbian</a>-Hungarian coalition. Murad II won the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Varna" title="Battle of Varna">Battle of Varna</a> in 1444 against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J%C3%A1nos_Hunyadi" title="János Hunyadi">János Hunyadi</a>.</p>
<p>Murad II relinquished his throne<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in 1444 to his son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mehmed_II" title="Mehmed II">Mehmed II</a>, but a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Janissary" title="Janissary">Janissary</a> revolt<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> in the Empire forced him to return.</p>
<p>In 1448 he defeated the <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> coalition at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_Kosovo" title="Second Battle of Kosovo">Second Battle of Kosovo</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kosovo" title="Battle of Kosovo">first one</a> took place in 1389). When the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balkan" title="Balkan">Balkan</a> front was secured, Murad II turned east to defeat <a href="/wiki/Timur" title="Timur">Timur</a>'s son, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shah_Rukh_(Timurid_dynasty)" title="Shah Rukh (Timurid dynasty)">Shah Rokh</a>, and the emirates of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karamanid" title="Karamanid">Karamanid</a> and Çorum-Amasya. In 1450 Murad II led his army into <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a> and unsuccessfully <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Kruj%C3%AB_(1450)" title="Siege of Krujë (1450)">besieged</a> the Castle of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kruje" title="Kruje">Kruje</a> in an effort to defeat the resistance led by <a href="/wiki/Skanderbeg" title="Skanderbeg">Skanderbeg</a>. In the winter of 1450–1451, Murad II fell ill, and died in <a href="/wiki/Edirne" title="Edirne">Edirne</a>. He was succeeded by his son Mehmed II (1451–81).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Murad_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dt>Consorts</dt>
</dl>
<p>Murad II had four known wives:</p>
<ul>
<li>Yeni Hatun, daughter of Şadgeldi Paşazade Mustafa Bey of the <a href="/wiki/Beyliks_of_Canik#Tacettino.C4.9Fullar.C4.B1" title="Beyliks of Canik">Kutluşah</a> of <a href="/wiki/Amasya" title="Amasya">Amasya</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hatice_Halime_Hatun" title="Hatice Halime Hatun">Halime Hatun</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0sfendiyar_Bey" title="İsfendiyar Bey">Izzeddin Isfendiyar Bey</a>, the ruler of <a href="/wiki/Isfendiyarids" title="Isfendiyarids">Isfendiyarids</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/H%C3%BCma_Hatun" title="Hüma Hatun">Hüma Hatun</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mara_Brankovi%C4%87" title="Mara Branković">Despina Hatun</a> (m.1435), the daughter of <a href="/wiki/%C4%90ura%C4%91_Brankovi%C4%87" title="Đurađ Branković">Đurađ Branković</a> of Serbia;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt>Sons</dt>
</dl>
<p>Murad had five sons:</p>
<ul>
<li>Ahmed Çelebi (1419 - 1437, buried in Muradiye Complex, Bursa);</li>
<li>Alaeddin Ali Çelebi (1425 - 1443, buried in Muradiye Complex, Bursa);</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mehmed_the_Conqueror" title="Mehmed the Conqueror">Mehmed the Conqueror</a> (1431 - 3 May 1481, buried in Fatih Mosque, Istanbul) - with Hüma Hatun;</li>
<li>Orhan Çelebi (died 1441, buried in Darülhadis Mausoleum, Edirne);</li>
<li>Hasan Çelebi (1450 - 18 February 1451, buried in Darülhadis Türbesi) - with Halime Hatun;</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt>Daughters</dt>
</dl>
<p>Murad had four daughters:</p>
<ul>
<li>Erhundu Hatun, married to Damat Yakub Bey;</li>
<li>Şehzade Hatun (buried in Muradiye Complex, Bursa), married to Damat Sinan Bey;</li>
<li>Fatma Hatun (buried in Muradiye Complex, Bursa) - with Hüma Hatun, married to Damat Mahmud Çelebi, son of Çandırlı Ibrahim Pasha;</li>
<li>Hatice Hatun (buried in Muradiye Complex, Bursa), married Damat Isa Bey.</li>
</ul>
