<p>The <b>Ashikaga shogunate</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">足利幕府</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Ashikaga bakufu</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, 1336–1573)</span>, also known as the <b>Muromachi shogunate</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">室町幕府</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Muromachi bakufu</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nussbaum669_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-nussbaum669-3">[3]</a></sup> was a dynasty originating from one of the plethora of Japanese <i><a href="/wiki/Daimy%C5%8D" title="Daimyō">daimyōs</a></i> which governed Japan from 1338 to 1573, the year in which <a href="/wiki/Oda_Nobunaga" title="Oda Nobunaga">Oda Nobunaga</a> deposed <a href="/wiki/Ashikaga_Yoshiaki" title="Ashikaga Yoshiaki">Ashikaga Yoshiaki</a>. The heads of government were the <a href="/wiki/Shogun" title="Shogun">shoguns</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoth2002878_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoth2002878-4">[4]</a></sup> Each was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Ashikaga_clan" title="Ashikaga clan">Ashikaga clan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoth200253_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoth200253-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>This period is also known as the <a href="/wiki/Muromachi_period" title="Muromachi period">Muromachi period</a>. It gets its name from the Muromachi district of <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nussbaum669_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-nussbaum669-3">[3]</a></sup> The third shogun, <a href="/wiki/Ashikaga_Yoshimitsu" title="Ashikaga Yoshimitsu">Ashikaga Yoshimitsu</a>, established his residence on <a href="/wiki/Muromachi_Street" title="Muromachi Street">Muromachi Street</a>. This residence, constructed in 1379, is nicknamed "Flower Palace" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">花の御所</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Hana no Gosho</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> because of the abundance of flowers in its landscaping.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginning">Beginning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashikaga_shogunate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Beginning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the preceding <a href="/wiki/Kamakura_period" title="Kamakura period">Kamakura period</a> (1185–1333), the <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dj%C5%8D_clan" title="Hōjō clan">Hōjō clan</a> enjoyed absolute power in the governing of Japan. This monopoly of power, as well as the lack of a reward of lands after the defeat of the <a href="/wiki/Mongol_invasions_of_Japan" title="Mongol invasions of Japan">Mongol invasions</a>, led to simmering resentment among Hōjō vassals. Finally, in 1333, the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Go-Daigo" title="Emperor Go-Daigo">Emperor Go-Daigo</a> ordered local governing vassals to oppose Hōjō rule, in favor of Imperial restoration, in the <a href="/wiki/Kenmu_Restoration" title="Kenmu Restoration">Kenmu Restoration</a>.</p>
<p>To counter this revolt, the Kamakura <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shogunate" title="Shogunate">shogunate</a> ordered <a href="/wiki/Ashikaga_Takauji" title="Ashikaga Takauji">Ashikaga Takauji</a> to quash the uprising. For reasons that are unclear, possibly because Ashikaga was the <a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a> leader of the powerless <a href="/wiki/Minamoto_clan" title="Minamoto clan">Minamoto clan</a>, while the Hōjō clan were from the <a href="/wiki/Taira_clan" title="Taira clan">Taira clan</a> the Minamoto had previously defeated, Ashikaga turned against Kamakura, and fought on behalf of the Imperial court.</p>
<p>After the successful overthrow of the Kamakura regime in 1336, Ashikaga Takauji set up his own military government in Kyoto.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="North_and_South_Court">North and South Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashikaga_shogunate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: North and South Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After Ashikaga Takauji established himself as the shogun, a dispute arose with <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Go-Daigo" title="Emperor Go-Daigo">Emperor Go-Daigo</a> on the subject of how to govern the country. That dispute led Takauji to cause Prince Yutahito, the second son of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Go-Fushimi" title="Emperor Go-Fushimi">Emperor Go-Fushimi</a>, to be installed as <a href="/wiki/Emperor_K%C5%8Dmy%C5%8D" title="Emperor Kōmyō">Emperor Kōmyō</a>. Go-Daigō fled, and Japan was divided between a northern imperial court (in favor of Kōmyō), and a southern imperial court (in favor of Go-Daigō). This <a href="/wiki/Nanboku-ch%C5%8D_period" title="Nanboku-chō period">period of "Northern and Southern Courts"</a> continued for 56 years, until 1392, when the South Court gave up during the reign of Ashikaga Yoshimitsu.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_structure">Government structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashikaga_shogunate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Government structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Instituciones_del_shogunato_Ashikaga_obj_a_trazo.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="700" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Instituciones_del_shogunato_Ashikaga_obj_a_trazo.svg/220px-Instituciones_del_shogunato_Ashikaga_obj_a_trazo.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Instituciones_del_shogunato_Ashikaga_obj_a_trazo.svg/330px-Instituciones_del_shogunato_Ashikaga_obj_a_trazo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Instituciones_del_shogunato_Ashikaga_obj_a_trazo.svg/440px-Instituciones_del_shogunato_Ashikaga_obj_a_trazo.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Instituciones_del_shogunato_Ashikaga_obj_a_trazo.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Structure of the bakufu</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Ashikaga Shogunate is the weakest of the three Japanese military governments. Unlike its predecessor, the Kamakura Shogunate, or its successor, the Tokugawa Shogunate, when Ashikaga Takauji established his government he had little personal territories with which to support his rule. The Ashikaga Shogunate was thus heavily reliant on the prestige and personal authority of its shoguns. The centralized master-vassal system used in the Kamakura system was replaced with the highly de-centralized <i><a href="/wiki/Daimy%C5%8D" title="Daimyō">daimyōs</a></i> (local lord) system, and because of the lack of direct territories, the military power of the shoguns depended heavily on the loyalty of the <i>daimyōs</i>.</p>
<p>On the other hand, the Imperial court was no longer a credible threat to military rule. The failure of the Kenmu Restoration had rendered the court weakened and subservient, a situation the Ashikaga Takauji reinforced by establishing within close proximity of the Emperor at Kyoto. The authority of the local <i>daimyōs</i> greatly expanded from its Kamakura times. In addition to military and policing responsibilities, the shogunate appointed shugos now absorbed the justice, economical and taxation powers of the local Imperial governors, while the government holdings in each province were rapidly absorbed into the personal holdings of the <i>daimyōs</i> or their vassals. The loss of both political clout and economic base deprived the Imperial court of much of its power, which were then assumed by the Ashikaga shoguns. This situation reached its peak under the rule of the third Shogun, Ashikaga Yoshimitsu.</p>
<p>After Yoshimitsu however, the structural weakness of the Ashikaga shogunate were exposed by numerous succession troubles and early deaths. This became dramatically more acute after the Onin War, after which the shogunate itself became reduced to little more than a local political force in Kyoto.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_relations">Foreign relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashikaga_shogunate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Foreign relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Ashikaga shogunate's foreign relations policy choices were played out in evolving contacts with <a href="/wiki/Joseon" title="Joseon">Joseon</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Peninsula" title="Korean Peninsula">Korean Peninsula</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvon_Klaproth1834320_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvon_Klaproth1834320-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKang1997275_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKang1997275-7">[7]</a></sup> and with imperial China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAckroyd1982329_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAckroyd1982329-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvon_Klaproth1834322.E2.80.93324_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvon_Klaproth1834322.E2.80.93324-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fall_of_the_shogunate">Fall of the shogunate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashikaga_shogunate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fall of the shogunate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the <i>daimyōs</i> increasingly feuded among themselves in the pursuit of power in the <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Cnin_War" title="Ōnin War">Ōnin War</a>, that loyalty grew increasingly strained, until it erupted into open warfare in the late Muromachi period, also known as the <a href="/wiki/Sengoku_period" title="Sengoku period">Sengoku period</a>.</p>
<p>When the shogun <a href="/wiki/Ashikaga_Yoshiteru" title="Ashikaga Yoshiteru">Ashikaga Yoshiteru</a> was assassinated in 1565, an ambitious <i>daimyō</i>, <a href="/wiki/Oda_Nobunaga" title="Oda Nobunaga">Oda Nobunaga</a>, seized the opportunity and installed Yoshiteru's brother <a href="/wiki/Ashikaga_Yoshiaki" title="Ashikaga Yoshiaki">Yoshiaki</a> as the 15th Ashikaga shogun. However, Yoshiaki was only a puppet of Nobunaga.</p>
<p>The Ashikaga shogunate was finally destroyed in 1573 when Nobunaga drove Ashikaga Yoshiaki out of Kyoto. Initially, Yoshiaki fled to <a href="/wiki/Shikoku" title="Shikoku">Shikoku</a>. Afterwards, he sought and received protection from the Mōri clan in western Japan. Later, <a href="/wiki/Toyotomi_Hideyoshi" title="Toyotomi Hideyoshi">Toyotomi Hideyoshi</a> requested that Yoshiaki accept him as an adopted son and the 16th Ashikaga shogun, but Yoshiaki refused.</p>
<p>The Ashikaga family survived the 16th century, and a branch of it became the <i>daimyō</i> family of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kitsuregawa_domain&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kitsuregawa domain (page does not exist)">Kitsuregawa domain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

Marker for the site of the Flower Palace, Kyoto</div>

<p>The Shogunal residence, also known as the "Flower Palace", was in Kyoto on the block now bounded by <a href="/wiki/Karasuma_Street" title="Karasuma Street">Karasuma Street</a> (to the east), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Imadegawa_Street&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Imadegawa Street (page does not exist)">Imadegawa Street</a> (to the south), <a href="/wiki/Muromachi_Street" title="Muromachi Street">Muromachi Street</a> (to the west, giving the name), and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kamidachiuri_Street&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kamidachiuri Street (page does not exist)">Kamidachiuri Street</a> (to the north). The location is commemorated by a stone marker at the southwest corner, and the <i>Kanbai-kan</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">寒梅館</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, Winter Plum Hall)</span> of <a href="/wiki/Doshisha_University" title="Doshisha University">Dōshisha University</a> contains relics and excavations of the area.</p>
