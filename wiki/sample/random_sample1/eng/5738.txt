<p>The <b>patella</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>), also known as the <b>kneecap</b> or <b>kneepan</b>, is a thick, circular-triangular <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a> which articulates with the <a href="/wiki/Femur" title="Femur">femur</a> (thigh bone) and covers and protects the anterior articular surface of the <a href="/wiki/Knee" title="Knee">knee</a> <a href="/wiki/Joint" title="Joint">joint</a>. The patella is found in many tetrapods, such as <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, but not in <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a>, most <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> nor <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>.</p>
<p>In humans, the patella is the largest <a href="/wiki/Sesamoid_bone" title="Sesamoid bone">sesamoid bone</a> in the body. Babies are born with a patella of soft cartilage which begins to ossify into bone at about three years of age.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The patella is a <a href="/wiki/Sesamoid_bone" title="Sesamoid bone">sesamoid bone</a> roughly triangular in shape, with the <b>apex of the patella</b> facing downwards.The apex is the most <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Superior_and_inferior" title="Anatomical terms of location">inferior</a> (lowest) part of the patella. It is pointed in shape, and gives attachment to the <a href="/wiki/Patellar_ligament" title="Patellar ligament">patellar ligament</a>.</p>
<p>The front and back surfaces are joined by a thin margin and towards centre by a thicker margin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Platzer-194_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Platzer-194-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Tendon" title="Tendon">tendon</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Quadriceps_femoris_muscle" title="Quadriceps femoris muscle">quadriceps femoris muscle</a> attaches to the base of the patella.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Platzer-194_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Platzer-194-1">[1]</a></sup> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vastus_intermedius" title="Vastus intermedius">vastus intermedius</a> muscle attaching to the base itself, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vastus_lateralis" title="Vastus lateralis">vastus lateralis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vastus_medialis" title="Vastus medialis">vastus medialis</a> are attached to outer lateral and medial borders of patella respectively.</p>
<p>The upper third of the front of the patella is coarse, flattened, and rough, and serves for the attachment of the tendon of the quadriceps and often has <a href="/wiki/Exostosis" title="Exostosis">exostoses</a>. The middle third has numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vascular" title="Vascular">vascular</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canaliculus_(bone)" title="Canaliculus (bone)">canaliculi</a>. The lower third culminates in the apex which serves as the origin of the <a href="/wiki/Patellar_ligament" title="Patellar ligament">patellar ligament</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Platzer-194_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Platzer-194-1">[1]</a></sup> The posterior surface is divided into two parts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Platzer-194_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Platzer-194-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>The upper three-quarters of the patella <a href="/wiki/Joint" title="Joint">articulates</a> with the femur and is subdivided into a medial and a lateral facet by a vertical ledge which varies in shape.</p>
<p>In the adult the articular surface is about 12 cm<sup>2</sup> (1.9 sq in) and covered by <a href="/wiki/Cartilage" title="Cartilage">cartilage</a>, which can reach a maximal thickness of 6 mm (0.24 in) in the centre at about 30 years of age.</p>
<p>The lower part of the posterior surface has vascular canaliculi filled and is filled by fatty tissue, the <a href="/wiki/Infrapatellar_fat_pad" title="Infrapatellar fat pad">infrapatellar fat pad</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Variation">Variation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Patella_bipartita.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1141" data-file-width="1813" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Patella_bipartita.jpg/220px-Patella_bipartita.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Patella_bipartita.jpg/330px-Patella_bipartita.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Patella_bipartita.jpg/440px-Patella_bipartita.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

In this <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Human_body#anatomical_variation" title="Human body">anatomical variation</a> of the patella can be seen – the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patella_bipartita" title="Patella bipartita">bipartate patella</a>, in which the patella is split into two parts.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Emarginations (i.e. <i>patella emarginata</i>, a "missing piece") are common laterally on the proximal edge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Platzer-194_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Platzer-194-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bipartite_patella" title="Bipartite patella">Bipartite patellas</a> are the result of an ossification of a second <a href="/wiki/Cartilage" title="Cartilage">cartilaginous</a> layer at the location of an emargination. Previously, bipartite patellas were explained as the failure of several ossification centres to fuse, but this idea has been rejected.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Partite patellas occur almost exclusively in men. Tripartite and even multipartite patellas occur.</p>
<p>The upper three-quarters of the patella <a href="/wiki/Joint" title="Joint">articulates</a> with the femur and is subdivided into a medial and a lateral facet by a vertical ledge which varies in shape. Four main types of articular surface can be distinguished:</p>
<ol>
<li>Most commonly the medial articular surface is smaller than the lateral.</li>
<li>Sometimes both articular surfaces are virtually equal in size.</li>
<li>Occasionally, the medial surface is <a href="/wiki/Hypoplasia" title="Hypoplasia">hypoplastic</a> or</li>
<li>the central ledge is only indicated.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the patella an <a href="/wiki/Ossification" title="Ossification">ossification</a> centre develops between the ages 3–6 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Platzer-194_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Platzer-194-1">[1]</a></sup> The patella originates from two centres of ossification which unite when fully formed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The primary functional role of the patella is knee extension. The patella increases the <a href="/wiki/Lever" title="Lever">leverage</a> that the <a href="/wiki/Tendon" title="Tendon">tendon</a> can exert on the femur by increasing the angle at which it acts.</p>
<p>It is attached to the <a href="/wiki/Tendon" title="Tendon">tendon</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Quadriceps_femoris_muscle" title="Quadriceps femoris muscle">quadriceps femoris muscle</a>, which contracts to extend/straighten the <a href="/wiki/Knee" title="Knee">knee</a>. The patella is stabilized by the insertion of the horizontal fibres of <a href="/wiki/Vastus_medialis" title="Vastus medialis">vastus medialis</a> and by the prominence of the <a href="/wiki/Lateral_condyle_of_femur" title="Lateral condyle of femur">lateral femoral condyle</a>, which discourages lateral dislocation during flexion. The retinacular fibres of the patella also stabilize it during exercise.</p>
<p>Patella allows uniaxial movements of legs that is back and straightning movements in 180 degree angle</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dislocation">Dislocation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dislocation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Patellar_dislocation" title="Patellar dislocation">Patellar dislocation</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Patellar_dislocation" title="Patellar dislocation">Patellar dislocations</a> occur with significant regularity, particular in young female athletes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palmu2008_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-palmu2008-2">[2]</a></sup> It involves the patella sliding out of its position on the knee, most often laterally, and may be associated with extremely intense pain and swelling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dath2006_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-dath2006-3">[3]</a></sup> The patella can be tracked back into the groove with an extension of the leg, and therefore sometimes returns into the proper position on its own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dath2006_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-dath2006-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fracture">Fracture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fracture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Patella_fracture" title="Patella fracture">Patella fracture</a></div>
<p>The kneecap is prone to injury because of its particularly exposed location, and <a href="/wiki/Patella_fracture" title="Patella fracture">fractures of the patella</a> commonly occur as a consequence of direct trauma onto the knee. These fractures usually cause swelling and pain in the region, bleeding into the joint (<a href="/wiki/Hemarthrosis" title="Hemarthrosis">hemarthrosis</a>), and an inability to extend the knee. Patella fractures are usually treated with surgery, unless the damage is minimum and the extensor mechanism is intact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efort_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-efort-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exostosis">Exostosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Exostosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Exostosis" title="Exostosis">exostosis</a> is the formation of new bone onto a bone, as a result of excess <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> formation. This can be the cause of chronic pain when formed on the patella.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_animals">Other animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The patella has <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergently evolved</a> in <a href="/wiki/Eutheria" title="Eutheria">placental mammals</a> and birds; most marsupials have only rudimentary, non-ossified patellae although a few species possess a bony patella.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A patella is also present in the living monotremes, the platypus and the echidna. In more primitive tetrapods, including living amphibians and most reptiles (except some Lepidosaurs), the muscle tendons from the upper leg are attached directly to the <a href="/wiki/Tibia" title="Tibia">tibia</a>, and a patella is not present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VB_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-VB-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymological_history">Etymological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Etymological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The etymology of <i>patella</i> presents a case of convergent <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a>. In the Western world, 'patella' originated in the late 17th century from the <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">diminutive</a> form of <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">patina</span></i> or <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">patena</span></i> or <i>paten</i>, meaning shallow dish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The use of this term in Eastern medicine, however, predates the Western etymology. As early as the 13th century, traditional <a href="/wiki/Ayurveda" title="Ayurveda">Ayurveda</a> practitioners in the Indian subcontinent referred to the kneecap as <i>paṭēla haḍḍī</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindi_language" title="Hindi language">Hindi</a>: <span lang="hi" xml:lang="hi">पटेल हड्डी</span>), literally 'farmer's bone' because the grueling life of a <a href="/wiki/Patidar" title="Patidar">patidar</a>. The term was subsequently shortened to just 'patela'. During the <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British Raj</a> period, Western medicine was introduced to India, and the two etymologies converged into the modern medical use, with two 'L's.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
