<p><i><b>Scary Movie</b></i> is a 2000 American <a href="/wiki/Horror_film" title="Horror film">horror</a> <a href="/wiki/Comedy_film" title="Comedy film">comedy film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Keenen_Ivory_Wayans" title="Keenen Ivory Wayans">Keenen Ivory Wayans</a>. The film is a <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Horror_film" title="Horror film">horror</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slasher_film" title="Slasher film">slasher</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mystery_film" title="Mystery film">mystery</a> film genres. Several mid- and late-'90s films and TV shows are spoofed, and the script is primarily based on the '90s hit horror films <i><a href="/wiki/Scream_(1996_film)" title="Scream (1996 film)">Scream</a></i> (1996) and <i><a href="/wiki/I_Know_What_You_Did_Last_Summer" title="I Know What You Did Last Summer">I Know What You Did Last Summer</a></i> (1997).</p>
<p>The film was originally titled "<i>Last Summer I Screamed Because Halloween Fell on Friday the 13th</i>", but was changed <i>Scary Movie</i> in homage to the production title of <i>Scream</i>, which was also released through <a href="/wiki/Dimension_Films" title="Dimension Films">Dimension Films</a>. The first in the <a href="/wiki/Scary_Movie_(film_series)" title="Scary Movie (film series)"><i>Scary Movie</i> film series</a>, it was followed by four <a href="/wiki/Sequel" title="Sequel">sequels</a>: <i><a href="/wiki/Scary_Movie_2" title="Scary Movie 2">Scary Movie 2</a></i> (2001), <i><a href="/wiki/Scary_Movie_3" title="Scary Movie 3">Scary Movie 3</a></i> (2003), <i><a href="/wiki/Scary_Movie_4" title="Scary Movie 4">Scary Movie 4</a></i> (2006), and <i><a href="/wiki/Scary_Movie_5" title="Scary Movie 5">Scary Movie 5</a></i> (2013).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite generally mixed reviews from critics, the film was a box office success, grossing $278 million worldwide from a $19 million budget.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scary_Movie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An 18-year-old girl named Drew Decker (<a href="/wiki/Carmen_Electra" title="Carmen Electra">Carmen Electra</a>) receives a threatening phone call while home alone one night. Drew is chased outside by <a href="/wiki/Ghostface_(Scream)" title="Ghostface (Scream)">Ghostface</a>, who stabs her in the breast, removing one of her <a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">silicone</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breast_implants" title="Breast implants">breast implants</a>. She is hit by a vehicle driven by her father, who was distracted by oral sex by his wife, and is then subsequently murdered by Ghostface.</p>
<p>The next morning, Drew's classmate, also an 18-year-old girl, named Cindy Campbell (<a href="/wiki/Anna_Faris" title="Anna Faris">Anna Faris</a>) meets up with her boyfriend Bobby (<a href="/wiki/Jon_Abrahams" title="Jon Abrahams">Jon Abrahams</a>) and her friends, Brenda (<a href="/wiki/Regina_Hall" title="Regina Hall">Regina Hall</a>), Ray (<a href="/wiki/Shawn_Wayans" title="Shawn Wayans">Shawn Wayans</a>), Greg (<a href="/wiki/Lochlyn_Munro" title="Lochlyn Munro">Lochlyn Munro</a>), Buffy (<a href="/wiki/Shannon_Elizabeth" title="Shannon Elizabeth">Shannon Elizabeth</a>), and Brenda's stoner brother Shorty (<a href="/wiki/Marlon_Wayans" title="Marlon Wayans">Marlon Wayans</a>) . Various news teams, including hack reporter Gail Hailstorm (<a href="/wiki/Cheri_Oteri" title="Cheri Oteri">Cheri Oteri</a>), converge on the school in the wake of Drew's murder. Gail hooks up with Buffy's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mentally_disabled" title="Mentally disabled">mentally disabled</a> brother Special Officer Doofy (<a href="/wiki/Dave_Sheridan_(actor)" title="Dave Sheridan (actor)">Dave Sheridan</a>), hoping to milk the facts out of him.</p>
<p>While Cindy is in class, she receives a note reading: "I Know What You Did Last Halloween!". She then realizes that Drew was murdered exactly one year after she and her friends accidentally killed a man during a wild car ride. At a beauty pageant that evening, Greg is killed by Ghostface in plain view, with the audience mistaking Buffy's screams and pleas for help as being part of her act. When Buffy realizes she's won the pageant, she almost immediately forgets about Greg's death and celebrates her victory.</p>
<p>After Cindy goes home alone, she is attacked by Ghostface. Cindy locks herself in her room and contacts the police, and Ghostface disappears. Bobby arrives momentarily after hearing the incident, but a pair of black gloves and a telephone fall out of his pocket, leading Cindy to believe that he was the killer. Bobby is arrested and taken to the police station. Afterwards, Cindy heads to Buffy and Doofy's place, to spend the night with them. When there, she receives a call from Ghostface, mocking her.</p>
<p>The next morning, Bobby is released, being found innocent as he was in the police cell and didn't make any phone calls. Meanwhile, Buffy, high on the success of her victory at the pageant, ignores Cindy's warnings about the killer and is <a href="/wiki/Decapitation" title="Decapitation">beheaded</a> in the girls locker room by Ghostface with a <a href="/wiki/Cleaver" title="Cleaver">cleaver</a>, though her severed head still remains alive. Ghostface, visibly annoyed, dumps Buffy's head into a Lost and Found bin. That night, Ray and Brenda go to a showing of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare_In_Love" title="Shakespeare In Love">Shakespeare In Love</a></i>, where Ray is stabbed in the ear through a bathroom stall. Ghostface then goes after Brenda. Unfortunately, angry movie patrons, fed up with Brenda's rude behavior during the movie, kill her before Ghostface can.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, Cindy throws a house party, hoping for safety in numbers. During the party, Bobby and Cindy go upstairs and have sex, no longer making Cindy a virgin. Suddenly, Ghostface appears and apparently stabs Bobby, before disappearing quickly. Cindy gets a gun from a drawer near the entrance, Bobby follows and she tends to his wounds. Shorty comes up from the basement and informs them that all of the partygoers have fled the house. Bobby takes the gun and shoots Shorty, revealing that his wound was an elaborate ruse. Ray arrives on the scene, alive.</p>
<p>Bobby and Ray confront Cindy in the kitchen and announce their plan that they are going to kill her and her father (<a href="/wiki/Rick_Ducommun" title="Rick Ducommun">Rick Ducommun</a>) despite the fact that they are not actually the killers. They also plan to make themselves look like heroes by giving each other stab wounds to indicate they fought back. However, the plan backfires when Ray stabs Bobby repeatedly, furious because his favorite show, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wayans_Bros." title="The Wayans Bros.">The Wayans Bros.</a></i>, has been cancelled. Ghostface abruptly arrives and stabs Ray. He and Cindy then fight each other, but Cindy successfully subdues him by employing moves copied from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Matrix" title="The Matrix">The Matrix</a></i> and kicks him through a window. However, Ghostface vanishes before the police arrive.</p>
<p>At the police station, Cindy and the sheriff (<a href="/wiki/Kurt_Fuller" title="Kurt Fuller">Kurt Fuller</a>) realize that Doofy, the only person who knew about the car accident, was actually faking his disability and is the true killer. Unfortunately, Doofy has already escaped with Gail Hailstorm. Upon finding his discarded disguise in the street, Cindy begins screaming, but is soon hit by a car.</p>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Post-credits_scene" title="Post-credits scene">post-credits scene</a>, Shorty appears and breaks the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_wall" title="Fourth wall">fourth wall</a> by giving tips on how to survive this sort of "situation". They appear to be on how to survive in a horror movie, but turn out to be tips on how to successfully enact a <a href="/wiki/Robbery" title="Robbery">"snatch 'n' run"</a>. In another post-credits scene, as part of a running gag involving Doofy and his vacuum cleaner, he dons the disguise once more to break up with it. However after a bit of coercion, he has sex with it one last time.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basis_and_humor">Basis and humor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scary_Movie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Basis and humor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Much of the humor of <i>Scary Movie</i> relies upon specific references to other contemporary films. <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> remarked in his review that "to get your money's worth, you need to be familiar with the various teenage horror franchises."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-3">[3]</a></sup> The two films on which the script is most heavily based are <i><a href="/wiki/Scream_(1996_film)" title="Scream (1996 film)">Scream</a></i> (1996) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_Know_What_You_Did_Last_Summer_(film)" title="I Know What You Did Last Summer (film)">I Know What You Did Last Summer</a></i> (1997), utilizing the general narrative arcs of both films, and featuring comedic recreations of key scenes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vulture_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-vulture-4">[4]</a></sup> The backstory in which the teenagers were responsible for killing a man is entirely based on <i>I Know What You Did Last Summer</i> including the teens' accidental murder of an innocent man on a car ride and the murder scene at the beauty pageant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-3">[3]</a></sup> Major references to <i>Scream</i> include the character Ghostface and the murder of Drew Decker in the opening scene (a reference to <a href="/wiki/Drew_Barrymore" title="Drew Barrymore">Drew Barrymore</a>, who played a similar character in <i>Scream</i>).</p>
<p>Additionally, the characters of <i>Scream</i> and <i>I Know What You Did Last Summer</i> are heavily mirrored in the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vulture_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-vulture-4">[4]</a></sup> Cindy Campbell is a play on the character of <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Prescott" title="Sidney Prescott">Sidney Prescott</a> (portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Neve_Campbell" title="Neve Campbell">Neve Campbell</a>) in <i>Scream</i>, as is Gail Hailstorm's character, which resembles that of <a href="/wiki/Gale_Weathers" title="Gale Weathers">Gale Weathers</a>, and Doofy Gilmore, of Deputy Dewey Riley. Buffy Gilmore is a play on the character of Helen Shivers in <i>I Know What You Did Last Summer</i>, portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Michelle_Gellar" title="Sarah Michelle Gellar">Sarah Michelle Gellar</a>, and her name is a reference to Gellar's role as <a href="/wiki/Buffy_Summers" title="Buffy Summers">Buffy Summers</a> in the hit TV series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buffy_the_Vampire_Slayer_(TV_series)" title="Buffy the Vampire Slayer (TV series)">Buffy the Vampire Slayer</a></i>. Greg Phillipe is referential to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ryan_Phillipe" title="Ryan Phillipe">Ryan Phillipe</a>, who plays Gellar's boyfriend in <i>I Know What You Did Last Summer</i>; likewise, Bobby Prinze is in reference to <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Prinze_Jr." title="Freddie Prinze Jr.">Freddie Prinze Jr.</a>, who also starred opposite <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Love_Hewitt" title="Jennifer Love Hewitt">Jennifer Love Hewitt</a> in the film. Cindy makes direct reference to this, saying that "Jennifer Love Huge-tits" would most likely be chosen to play her in a movie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many scenes and jokes parody or reference other films outside the horror film genre. The fight between Cindy and the killer heavily mimics <i><a href="/wiki/The_Matrix" title="The Matrix">The Matrix</a></i>, particularly its use of <a href="/wiki/Bullet_time" title="Bullet time">bullet time</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-3">[3]</a></sup> The final scene, in which Doofy stops feigning his disability and drives away with Gail, is a takeoff of the final scene of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Usual_Suspects" title="The Usual Suspects">The Usual Suspects</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EW_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-EW-6">[6]</a></sup> When asked about her favorite horror movie, Drew answers "<i><a href="/wiki/Kazaam" title="Kazaam">Kazaam</a></i>" due to <a href="/wiki/Shaquille_O%27Neal" title="Shaquille O'Neal">Shaquille O'Neal</a>'s acting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-3">[3]</a></sup> Cindy becomes aggressive and roars "Say my name!" during sex with Bobby, similar to the sex scene between Michelle and Jim in <i><a href="/wiki/American_Pie_(film)" title="American Pie (film)">American Pie</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EW_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-EW-6">[6]</a></sup> A trailer for a fictitious sequel to <i><a href="/wiki/Amistad_(film)" title="Amistad (film)">Amistad</a></i> titled <i>Amistad II</i> with elements of <i><a href="/wiki/Titanic_(1997_film)" title="Titanic (1997 film)">Titanic</a></i> also appears in the movie theater scene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scott-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film also makes other pop culture references beyond the scope of film, including a brief reference to <i><a href="/wiki/Dawson%27s_Creek" title="Dawson's Creek">Dawson's Creek</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Variety_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Variety-8">[8]</a></sup> and a parody of the <i><a href="/wiki/Whassup%3F" title="Whassup?">Whassup?</a></i> ad campaign by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Budweiser_(Anheuser-Busch)" title="Budweiser (Anheuser-Busch)">Budweiser</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Variety_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Variety-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scary_Movie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Scary Movie</i> opened theatrically in the United States on July 7, 2000 on 2,912 screens,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-box_office_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-box_office-9">[9]</a></sup> and debuted at number one at the <a href="/wiki/Box_office" title="Box office">box office</a>, earning $42,346,669 its opening weekend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-box_office_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-box_office-9">[9]</a></sup> The film ultimately grossed $157,019,771 domestically, and earned another $121,000,000 in foreign markets, making it a massive commercial success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-box_office_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-box_office-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception<span id="Critics"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scary_Movie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Scary Movie</i> received mixed reviews from critics. On <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> the film has an approval rating of 53% based on 113 reviews with an average rating of 5.5/10. The site's critical consensus reads, "Critics say <i>Scary Movie</i> overloads on crudity and grossness to get it laughs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, the film has a score of 48 out of 100 based on 32 critics, indicating "mixed or average reviews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Audiences polled by <a href="/wiki/CinemaScore" title="CinemaScore">CinemaScore</a> gave the film an average grade of "B-" on an A+ to F scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CinemaScore_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-CinemaScore-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Joe Leydon of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> gave the film a positive review, remarking that the film was "unbounded by taste, inhibition or political correctness" and that "the outer limits of R-rated respectability are stretched, if not shredded" by the movie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EW_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-EW-6">[6]</a></sup> By contrast, Roger Ebert did not find the film as innovative, saying that the film lacked "the shocking impact of <i><a href="/wiki/Airplane!" title="Airplane!">Airplane!</a></i>, which had the advantage of breaking new ground."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-3">[3]</a></sup> However, Ebert did give the film 3 stars out of 4, saying it "delivers the goods", calling the film a "raucous, satirical attack on slasher movies."</p>
<p>Bob Longino of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Atlanta_Journal-Constitution" title="The Atlanta Journal-Constitution">The Atlanta Journal-Constitution</a></i> felt that the film's crude humor detracted from the film, saying that <i>Scary Movie</i> "dives so deep into tasteless humor that it's a wonder it landed an R rating instead of an NC-17."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longino_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Longino-13">[13]</a></sup> Other reviewers, such as A.O. Scott of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, argued that the jokes were "annoying less for their vulgarity than for their tiredness."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scott-7">[7]</a></sup> Scott remarked in his review, "Couch-bound pot smokers, prison sex, mannish female gym teachers, those <i>Whassssup</i> Budweiser commercials -- hasn't it all been done to death?".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Scott-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
