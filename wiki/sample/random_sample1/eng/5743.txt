<p>The <b>voiceless bilabial stop</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">consonantal</a> sound used in many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speech_communication" title="Speech communication">spoken</a> <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">languages</a>. The symbol in the <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">International Phonetic Alphabet</a> that represents this sound is ⟨<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">p</span>⟩, and the equivalent <a href="/wiki/X-SAMPA" title="X-SAMPA">X-SAMPA</a> symbol is <kbd>p</kbd>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voiceless_bilabial_stop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Features of the voiceless bilabial stop:</p>
<ul>
<li>Its <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">manner of articulation</a> is <a href="/wiki/Occlusive" title="Occlusive">occlusive</a>, which means it is produced by obstructing airflow in the vocal tract. Since the consonant is also oral, with no <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">nasal outlet</a>, the airflow is blocked entirely, and the consonant is a <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">stop</a>.</li>
<li>Its <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">place of articulation</a> is <a href="/wiki/Bilabial_consonant" title="Bilabial consonant">bilabial</a>, which means it is articulated with both <a href="/wiki/Lip" title="Lip">lips</a>.</li>
<li>Its <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">phonation</a> is voiceless, which means it is produced without vibrations of the vocal cords. In some languages the vocal cords are actively separated, so it is always voiceless; in others the cords are lax, so that it may take on the voicing of adjacent sounds.</li>
<li>It is an <a href="/wiki/Oral_consonant" title="Oral consonant">oral consonant</a>, which means air is allowed to escape through the mouth only.</li>
<li>Because the sound is not produced with airflow over the tongue, the <a href="/wiki/Central_consonant" title="Central consonant">central</a>–<a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">lateral</a> dichotomy does not apply.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Airstream_mechanism" title="Airstream mechanism">airstream mechanism</a> is <a href="/wiki/Egressive_sound" title="Egressive sound">pulmonic</a>, which means it is articulated by pushing air solely with the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thoracic_diaphragm" title="Thoracic diaphragm">diaphragm</a>, as in most sounds.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voiceless_bilabial_stop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The stop <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[p]</span> is missing from about 10% of languages that have a <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[b]</span>. (See <a href="/wiki/Voiced_velar_stop" title="Voiced velar stop">voiced velar stop</a> for another such gap.) This is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Areal_feature_(linguistics)" title="Areal feature (linguistics)">areal feature</a> of the "circum-Saharan zone" (Africa north of the equator, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabian_peninsula" title="Arabian peninsula">Arabian peninsula</a>). It is not known how old this areal feature is, and whether it might be a recent phenomenon due to Arabic as a prestige language (Arabic lost its <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/p/</span> in prehistoric times), or whether Arabic was itself affected by a more ancient areal pattern. It is found in other areas as well; for example, in Europe, <a href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic_language" title="Proto-Celtic language">Proto-Celtic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Old Basque</a> are both reconstructed as having <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[b]</span> but no <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[p]</span>.</p>
<p>Nonetheless, the <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[p]</span> sound is very common cross-linguistically. Most languages have at least a plain <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[p]</span>, and some distinguish more than one variety. Many <a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages" title="Indo-Aryan languages">Indo-Aryan languages</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">Hindustani</a>, have a two-way contrast between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspiration_(phonetics)" title="Aspiration (phonetics)">aspirated</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[pʰ]</span> and the plain <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[p]</span> (also transcribed as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[p˭]</span> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extensions_to_the_IPA" title="Extensions to the IPA">extensions to the IPA</a>).</p>
