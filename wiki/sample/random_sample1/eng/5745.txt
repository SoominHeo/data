<p>In <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, <b>voicelessness</b> is the property of sounds being pronounced without the <a href="/wiki/Larynx" title="Larynx">larynx</a> vibrating. Phonologically, it is a type of <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">phonation</a>, which contrasts with other states of the larynx, but some object that the word phonation implies <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">voicing</a> and that voicelessness is the lack of phonation.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">International Phonetic Alphabet</a> has distinct letters for many voiceless and <a href="/wiki/Modal_voice" title="Modal voice">modally voiced</a> pairs of consonants (the <a href="/wiki/Obstruent" title="Obstruent">obstruents</a>), such as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[p b], [t d], [k ɡ], [q ɢ], [f v], and [s z]</span>. Also, there are diacritics for voicelessness, <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;"><a href="/wiki/Phonetic_symbols_in_Unicode" title="Phonetic symbols in Unicode">U+</a>0325</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%"> ̥</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">COMBINING RING BELOW</span> and <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;"><a href="/wiki/Phonetic_symbols_in_Unicode" title="Phonetic symbols in Unicode">U+</a>030A</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%"> ̊</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">COMBINING RING ABOVE</span>, which is used for letters with a <a href="/wiki/Descender" title="Descender">descender</a>. Diacritics are typically used with letters for prototypically voiced sounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">vowels</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonorant_consonant" title="Sonorant consonant">sonorant consonants</a>: <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ḁ], [l̥], [ŋ̊]</span>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voiceless_vowels_and_other_sonorants">Voiceless vowels and other sonorants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voicelessness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Voiceless vowels and other sonorants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sonorant" title="Sonorant">Sonorants</a> are sounds such as vowels and <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">nasals</a> that are voiced in most of the world's languages. However, in some languages sonorants may be voiceless, usually <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">allophonically</a>. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> word <i><a href="/wiki/Sukiyaki" title="Sukiyaki">sukiyaki</a></i> is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[su̥kijaki]</span> and may sound like <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[skijaki]</span> to an English speaker, but the lips can be seen to compress for the <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[u̥]</span>. Something similar happens in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> words like <i>p<b>e</b>culiar</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[pʰə̥ˈkj̊uːliɚ]</span> and <i>p<b>o</b>tato</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[pʰə̥ˈtʰeɪtoʊ]</span>.</p>
<p>Voiceless vowels are also an areal feature in languages of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Southwest" title="American Southwest">American Southwest</a> (like <a href="/wiki/Hopi_language" title="Hopi language">Hopi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Keresan_languages" title="Keresan languages">Keres</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin" title="Great Basin">Great Basin</a> (including all <a href="/wiki/Numic_languages" title="Numic languages">Numic languages</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a>, where they are present in Numic <a href="/wiki/Comanche_language" title="Comanche language">Comanche</a> but also in <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian</a> <a href="/wiki/Cheyenne_language" title="Cheyenne language">Cheyenne</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Caddoan_languages" title="Caddoan languages">Caddoan</a> language <a href="/wiki/Arikara_language" title="Arikara language">Arikara</a>.</p>
<p>Sonorants may also be contrastively, not just environmentally, voiceless. <a href="/wiki/Standard_Tibetan" title="Standard Tibetan">Standard Tibetan</a>, for example, has a voiceless <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/l̥/</span> in <i><a href="/wiki/Lhasa" title="Lhasa">Lhasa</a></i>, which sounds similar to but is less noisy than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_lateral_fricative" title="Voiceless alveolar lateral fricative">voiceless lateral fricative</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɬ/</span> in <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>; it contrasts with a modally voiced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/l/</span>. Welsh contrasts several voiceless sonorants: <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/m, m̥/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/n, n̥/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ŋ, ŋ̊/</span>, and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/r, r̥/</span>, the last represented by "rh".</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Moksha_language" title="Moksha language">Moksha</a>, there is even a voiceless <a href="/wiki/Palatal_approximant" title="Palatal approximant">palatal approximant</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j̊/</span> (written in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyrillic" title="Cyrillic">Cyrillic</a> as &lt;<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%D0%99" title="Й">й</a><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%D0%A5" title="Х">х</a>&gt; <i>jh</i>) along with <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/l̥/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/r̥/</span> (written as ⟨<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%D0%9B" title="Л">л</a>х⟩ <i>lh</i> and ⟨<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%D0%A0" title="Р">р</a>х⟩ <i>rh</i>). The last two have palatalized counterparts <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/l̥ʲ/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/r̥ʲ/</span> (⟨л<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%D0%AC" title="Ь">ь</a>х⟩ and ⟨рьх⟩). <a href="/wiki/Kildin_Sami_language" title="Kildin Sami language">Kildin Sami</a> has also <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j̊/</span> ⟨<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%D2%8A" title="Ҋ">ҋ</a>⟩.</p>
<p>Contrastively voiceless vowels have been reported several times without ever being verified (L&amp;M 1996:315).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_voicing_contrast_in_obstruents">Lack of voicing contrast in obstruents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voicelessness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lack of voicing contrast in obstruents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many languages lack a distinction between voiced and voiceless <a href="/wiki/Obstruent" title="Obstruent">obstruents</a> (stops, affricates, and fricatives). That is true universal in nearly all <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">Dravidian languages</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_languages" title="Australian languages">Australian languages</a>, but it is widely found elsewhere, for example in <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Polynesian_languages" title="Polynesian languages">Polynesian languages</a>. Also, <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_language" title="Hawaiian language">Hawaiian</a> has <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/p/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/k/</span> but no <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/b/</span> or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɡ/</span>.</p>
<p>In many such languages, obstruents are realized as voiced in voiced environments, such as between vowels or between a vowel and a nasal, and voiceless elsewhere, such as at the beginning or end of the word or next to another obstruent. That is the case in Dravidian and Australian languages and in Korean but not in Mandarin or Polynesian. Usually, the variable sounds are transcribed with the voiceless IPA letters, but for Australian languages, the letters for voiced consonants are often used.</p>
<p>It appears that voicelessness is not a single phenomenon in such languages. In some, such as the Polynesian languages, the vocal cords are required to actively open to allow an unimpeded (silent) airstream, which is sometimes called a <i>breathed</i> (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ˈbrɛθt/</span>) phonation (not to be confused with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breathy_voice" title="Breathy voice">breathy voice</a>). In others, such as many Australian languages, voicing ceases during the hold of a stop (few Australian languages have any other kind of obstruent) because airflow is insufficient to sustain it, and if the vocal cords open, that is only from passive relaxation.</p>
<p>Thus, Polynesian stops are reported to be held for longer than Australian stops and are seldom voiced, but Australian stops are prone to having voiced variants (L&amp;M 1996:53), and the languages are often represented as having no phonemically voiceless consonants at all.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, when stops occur at the end of a word they are voiceless because the glottis is closed, not open, so they are said to be unphonated (have no phonation) by some phoneticians, who considered "breathed" voicelessness to be a phonation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
