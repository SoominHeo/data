<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Shutout</h1>

<p>In team sports, a <b>shutout</b> (a <b>clean sheet</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_English" title="UK English">UK English</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a>) is a game in which one team prevents the other from scoring any points. While possible in most major sports, they are highly improbable in some sports, such as basketball.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shutouts are usually seen as a result of effective defensive play even though a weak opposing offense may be as much to blame. Some sports credit individual players, particularly <a href="/wiki/Goalkeeper" title="Goalkeeper">goalkeepers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Starting_pitcher" title="Starting pitcher">starting pitchers</a>, with shutouts and keep track of them as statistics; others do not.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="American_football">American football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shutout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: American football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A shutout in <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> is uncommon but not exceptionally rare. Keeping an opponent scoreless in American football requires a team's defense to be able to consistently shut down both pass and run offenses over the course of a game. The difficulty of completing a shutout is compounded by the many ways a team can score in the game. For example, teams can attempt field goals, which have a high rate of success. The range of NFL caliber kickers makes it possible for a team with a weak offense to get <a href="/wiki/Field_goal_range" title="Field goal range">close enough (within 50 yards) to the goalposts</a> and kick a field goal. In the decade of the 2000s there were 89 shutouts in 2,544 NFL regular-season games, for an average of slightly more than one shutout every two weeks in an NFL season.</p>
<p>There are at least five instances in American football in which a team had been shut out throughout an entire season, and four in which a team has shut out all of their opponents in the season (the longest of these being the ten-game <a href="/wiki/Perfect_season" title="Perfect season">perfect season</a> in which the 1933 <a href="/wiki/Providence_Huskies" title="Providence Huskies">Providence Huskies</a> did not concede a single point).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The achievement of a shutout is much more difficult in <a href="/wiki/Canadian_football" title="Canadian football">Canadian football</a>, where scoring and offensive movement is generally more frequent and a <a href="/wiki/Single_(football)" title="Single (football)">single point</a> can be scored simply by <a href="/wiki/Punt_(gridiron_football)" title="Punt (gridiron football)">punting</a> the ball from any point on the field into the end zone.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Association_football">Association football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shutout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Association football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a> a team, defence or <a href="/wiki/Goalkeeper" title="Goalkeeper">goalkeeper</a> may be said to "keep a clean sheet" if they prevent their opponents from scoring any goals during an entire match. Because association football is a relatively low-scoring game, it is common for one team, or even both teams, to score no goals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wg_cs_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-wg_cs-3">[3]</a></sup> A theory as to the term's origin is that sports reporters used separate pieces of paper to record the different statistical details of a game. If one team did not allow a goal, then that team's "details of goals conceded" page would appear blank, leaving a clean sheet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wg_cs_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-wg_cs-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Baseball">Baseball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shutout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Baseball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Shutouts_in_baseball" title="Shutouts in baseball">Shutouts in baseball</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a>, a <b>shutout</b> (denoted statistically as <b>ShO</b> or <b>SHO</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>) refers to the act by which a single <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitcher</a> pitches a <a href="/wiki/Complete_game" title="Complete game">complete game</a> and does not allow the opposing team to score a <a href="/wiki/Run_(baseball)" title="Run (baseball)">run</a>. If two or more pitchers combine to complete this act, no pitcher will be awarded a shutout, although the team itself can be said to have "shut out" the opposing team. The only exception to this is when a pitcher enters a game before the opposing team scores a run or makes an out and then completes the game without allowing a run to score. That pitcher is then awarded a shutout, although not a complete game.</p>
<p>The all-time career leader in shutouts is <a href="/wiki/Walter_Johnson" title="Walter Johnson">Walter Johnson</a>, who pitched for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Senators_(1901%E2%80%9360)" title="Washington Senators (1901–60)">Washington Senators</a> from 1907–1927. He accumulated 110 shutouts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> which is 20 more than second placed <a href="/wiki/Grover_Cleveland_Alexander" title="Grover Cleveland Alexander">Grover Cleveland Alexander</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The most shutouts recorded in one season was 16, which was a feat accomplished by both Grover Alexander (1916) and <a href="/wiki/George_Bradley" title="George Bradley">George Bradley</a> (1876).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> These records are considered among the most secure records in baseball, as pitchers today rarely earn more than one or two shutouts per season with a heavy emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Pitch_count" title="Pitch count">pitch count</a> and <a href="/wiki/Relief_pitcher" title="Relief pitcher">relief pitching</a>. Complete games themselves have also become rare among <a href="/wiki/Starting_pitcher" title="Starting pitcher">starting pitchers</a>. The current active leader in shutouts is <a href="/wiki/Tim_Hudson" title="Tim Hudson">Clayton Kershaw</a> of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">Los Angeles Dodgers</a>. Entering his ninth season, he has recorded 13 shutouts, which ties him for 463rd all time. Only four pitchers whose entire careers were in the post-1920 <a href="/wiki/Live-ball_era" title="Live-ball era">live-ball era</a> threw as many as 60 career shutouts, with <a href="/wiki/Warren_Spahn" title="Warren Spahn">Warren Spahn</a> leading those pitchers with 63.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BaseballRefSHO_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-BaseballRefSHO-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ice_hockey">Ice hockey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shutout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ice hockey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a>, a <b>shutout</b> (<b>SO</b>) is credited to a <a href="/wiki/Goaltender" title="Goaltender">goaltender</a> who successfully stops the other team from scoring during the entire game. A shutout may be shared between two goaltenders, but will not be listed in either of their individual statistics. The record holder for most regular-season career shutouts in the <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">National Hockey League</a> is <a href="/wiki/Martin_Brodeur" title="Martin Brodeur">Martin Brodeur</a> with 125 (see the <a href="/wiki/List_of_NHL_statistical_leaders#Regular_season_shutouts" title="List of NHL statistical leaders">all-time regular season shutout leaders</a>). The modern-day record for a team being shut out in a season is held by the <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Blue_Jackets" title="Columbus Blue Jackets">Columbus Blue Jackets</a> at 16, during the <a href="/wiki/2006%E2%80%9307_NHL_season" title="2006–07 NHL season">2006–07 season</a>.</p>
<p>In the event a shutout happens while using several goaltenders, the shutout will be credited to the team who shut out the opponent; however, no single goaltender will be awarded the shutout. This has happened several times in NHL history, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>During the <a href="/wiki/1982%E2%80%9383_Washington_Capitals_season" title="1982–83 Washington Capitals season">1982–83 Washington Capitals season</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Capitals" title="Washington Capitals">Washington Capitals</a> and their goalies <a href="/wiki/Al_Jensen" title="Al Jensen">Al Jensen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pat_Riggin" title="Pat Riggin">Pat Riggin</a> shared a shutout.</li>
<li>December 8, 2001: the <a href="/wiki/Anaheim_Ducks" title="Anaheim Ducks">Mighty Ducks of Anaheim</a> won 4–0 over the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Wild" title="Minnesota Wild">Minnesota Wild</a> with <a href="/wiki/Jean-S%C3%A9bastien_Gigu%C3%A8re" title="Jean-Sébastien Giguère">Jean-Sébastien Giguère</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Steve_Shields_(ice_hockey)" title="Steve Shields (ice hockey)">Steve Shields</a> in goal.</li>
<li>November 23, 2006: the <a href="/wiki/Nashville_Predators" title="Nashville Predators">Nashville Predators</a> won 6–0 over the <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Canucks" title="Vancouver Canucks">Vancouver Canucks</a> with <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Vokoun" title="Tomáš Vokoun">Tomáš Vokoun</a>, who left the game injured. He was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Chris_Mason_(ice_hockey)" title="Chris Mason (ice hockey)">Chris Mason</a>, who completed the game.</li>
<li>December 12, 2007: the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Senators" title="Ottawa Senators">Ottawa Senators</a> won 6–0 over the <a href="/wiki/Carolina_Hurricanes" title="Carolina Hurricanes">Carolina Hurricanes</a> with <a href="/wiki/Ray_Emery" title="Ray Emery">Ray Emery</a>, who left the game injured after making one save. He was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Gerber" title="Martin Gerber">Martin Gerber</a>, who made the other 31 saves.</li>
<li>December 1, 2009: the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Maple_Leafs" title="Toronto Maple Leafs">Toronto Maple Leafs</a> won 3–0 over the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Canadiens" title="Montreal Canadiens">Montreal Canadiens</a> with <a href="/wiki/Jonas_Gustavsson" title="Jonas Gustavsson">Jonas Gustavsson</a>, who left the game after the first period because of heart problems. He was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Joey_MacDonald" title="Joey MacDonald">Joey MacDonald</a>, who played the last two periods.</li>
<li>December 6, 2011: <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Luongo" title="Roberto Luongo">Roberto Luongo</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Canucks" title="Vancouver Canucks">Vancouver Canucks</a> stopped all his shots against the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Avalanche" title="Colorado Avalanche">Colorado Avalanche</a> before taking a shot to the neck; his replacement, <a href="/wiki/Cory_Schneider" title="Cory Schneider">Cory Schneider</a>, completed the team shutout.</li>
<li>April 14, 2012: the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Blues" title="St. Louis Blues">St. Louis Blues</a> won 3–0 over the <a href="/wiki/San_Jose_Sharks" title="San Jose Sharks">San Jose Sharks</a> with <a href="/wiki/Jaroslav_Hal%C3%A1k" title="Jaroslav Halák">Jaroslav Halák</a>, who left the game after a collision with teammate <a href="/wiki/Barret_Jackman" title="Barret Jackman">Barret Jackman</a> in the second period. He was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Brian_Elliott" title="Brian Elliott">Brian Elliott</a>, who made 17 saves to conserve the shutout.</li>
<li>February 28, 2013: The <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Blackhawks" title="Chicago Blackhawks">Chicago Blackhawks</a> won 3–0 over the St. Louis Blues. <a href="/wiki/Corey_Crawford" title="Corey Crawford">Corey Crawford</a> left the game after the first period for unspecified reasons, and was replaced by Ray Emery, who completed the second and third periods. This win also extended the Blackhawks' streak of games without a regulation loss to start a season to 20.</li>
<li>March 26, 2013: The <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Penguins" title="Pittsburgh Penguins">Pittsburgh Penguins</a> won 1–0 over the Montreal Canadiens. At the start of the third period, <a href="/wiki/Marc-Andr%C3%A9_Fleury" title="Marc-André Fleury">Marc-André Fleury</a> was replaced by Tomáš Vokoun after sustaining an unspecified injury late in the second period. Fleury stopped all of 25 shots, while Vokoun stopped all of 12.</li>
<li>February 3, 2014: The <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Red_Wings" title="Detroit Red Wings">Detroit Red Wings</a> won 2–0 over the Vancouver Canucks. Jonas Gustavsson stopped all eight shots faced in the first period, but did not return for the second period due to dizziness. <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Howard" title="Jimmy Howard">Jimmy Howard</a> stopped all 16 shots in the remainder of the game.</li>
<li>April 8, 2014: The <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Lightning" title="Tampa Bay Lightning">Tampa Bay Lightning</a> won 3–0 over the Toronto Maple Leafs. <a href="/wiki/Ben_Bishop" title="Ben Bishop">Ben Bishop</a> stopped all three shots faced in the first period, but fell awkwardly making a glove save and left the game. <a href="/wiki/Anders_Lindb%C3%A4ck" title="Anders Lindbäck">Anders Lindbäck</a> stopped all 25 shots in the remainder of the game. Neither goalie was given a shutout, though the team itself received that stat.</li>
<li>February 22, 2015: The Vancouver Canucks won 4–0 over the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Islanders" title="New York Islanders">New York Islanders</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Miller" title="Ryan Miller">Ryan Miller</a> stopped all ten shots faced in the first period. However, early in the second period, he was run into by teammate <a href="/wiki/Jannik_Hansen" title="Jannik Hansen">Jannik Hansen</a>, who crashed into the net and made contact with Miller's right leg. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eddie_Lack" title="Eddie Lack">Eddie Lack</a> then stopped all 27 shots in the remainder of the game. Neither goalie was given a shutout, but Miller was awarded the win because the Canucks led 1-0 at the time of Miller's injury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>December 5, 2015: The <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Wild" title="Minnesota Wild">Minnesota Wild</a> won 3–0 over the Colorado Avalanche. <a href="/wiki/Devan_Dubnyk" title="Devan Dubnyk">Devan Dubnyk</a> stopped all six shots faced in the first period and the first five shots faced in the second period, but he suffered a mild groin strain just after making a save on Avalanche forward <a href="/wiki/Jarome_Iginla" title="Jarome Iginla">Jarome Iginla</a> and eventually left the game midway through the second period. <a href="/wiki/Darcy_Kuemper" title="Darcy Kuemper">Darcy Kuemper</a> then stopped all nine shots in the remainder of the game to conserve the shutout. Neither goaltender was credit with the shutout, but Kuemper was awarded the win because Dubnyk suffered the injury before the first goal of the game was scored.</li>
<li>December 15, 2016: The <a href="/wiki/New_York_Rangers" title="New York Rangers">New York Rangers</a> defeated the <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Stars" title="Dallas Stars">Dallas Stars</a> by a score of 2-0. <a href="/wiki/Henrik_Lundqvist" title="Henrik Lundqvist">Henrik Lundqvist</a> stopped every shot he faced in the first period until <a href="/wiki/Cody_Eakin" title="Cody Eakin">Cody Eakin</a> ran into Lundqvist behind the net. Lundqvist sat out for 5:31 while being checked for injury. In those five and a half minutes, backup goaltender <a href="/wiki/Antti_Raanta" title="Antti Raanta">Antti Raanta</a> faced zero shots on goal. Lundqvist returned roughly five minutes before the end of the first period and finished the game, stopping a total of 27 shots. Neither goaltender was credited with a shutout, but Lundqvist was credited with the win due to Raanta facing zero shots on goal.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rugby">Rugby</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shutout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rugby">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shutouts are not common in either <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rugby_league" title="Rugby league">rugby league</a>. The 2005 Gillette <a href="/wiki/Rugby_League_Tri-Nations" title="Rugby League Tri-Nations">Rugby League Tri-Nations</a> final was the first time that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_national_rugby_league_team" title="Australian national rugby league team">Australia</a> had been 'nilled' since 1981.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The term "shutout" is not in common usage in European sport, and thus is not applied to European rugby, and there is no alternative term for the occurrence of a team achieving a no score, except to say that the team scored "nil". For example, the December 2006 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celtic_League_(rugby_union)" title="Celtic League (rugby union)">Magners League</a> match between <a href="/wiki/Munster_Rugby" title="Munster Rugby">Munster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Connacht_Rugby" title="Connacht Rugby">Connacht</a> ended 13–0 to Munster;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> it was, therefore, said that Munster won "thirteen-nil".</p>
<p>Generally, a defensively well-disciplined team, as well as behaviourally (not giving away penalty kicks), is most likely to not give away scores. This may also occur if there is a significant difference in class between the two teams, for example, when <a href="/wiki/Scotland_national_rugby_union_team" title="Scotland national rugby union team">Scotland</a> beat <a href="/wiki/Spain_national_rugby_union_team" title="Spain national rugby union team">Spain</a> (who were playing in their only Rugby World Cup) 48–0 in the <a href="/wiki/1999_Rugby_World_Cup" title="1999 Rugby World Cup">1999 Rugby World Cup</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> or when Australia beat Namibia 142–0 in the <a href="/wiki/2003_Rugby_World_Cup" title="2003 Rugby World Cup">2003 Rugby World Cup</a>.</p>
