<p><b>Intestacy</b> is the condition of the <a href="/wiki/Estate_(law)" title="Estate (law)">estate</a> of a person who dies without having made a valid <a href="/wiki/Will_and_testament" title="Will and testament">will</a> or other binding declaration. Alternatively this may also apply where a will or declaration has been made, but only applies to part of the estate; the remaining estate forms the "intestate estate".</p>
<p>Intestacy law, also referred to as the <b>law of descent and distribution</b>, refers to the body of law (<a href="/wiki/Statute" title="Statute">statutory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Case_law" title="Case law">case law</a>) that determines who is entitled to the property from the estate under the rules of <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">inheritance</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_the_common_law">History and the common law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intestacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and the common law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Intestacy has a limited application in those jurisdictions that follow <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a> or <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a> because the concept of a will is itself less important; the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Forced_heirship" title="Forced heirship">forced heirship</a> automatically gives a deceased person's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Next-of-kin" title="Next-of-kin">next-of-kin</a> title to a large part (forced estate) of the estate's property <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/By_operation_of_law" title="By operation of law">by operation of law</a>, beyond the power of the deceased person to defeat or exceed by testamentary gift. A forced share (or <a href="/wiki/Legitime" title="Legitime">legitime</a>) can often only be decreased on account of some very specific misconduct by the forced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heir" title="Heir">heir</a>. In matters of cross-border inheritance, the "laws of succession" is the commonplace term covering testate and intestate estates in common law jurisdictions together with forced heirship rules typically applying in civil law and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sharia_law" title="Sharia law">Sharia law</a> jurisdictions. After the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Wills" title="Statute of Wills">Statute of Wills</a> 1540, Englishmen (and unmarried or widowed women) could dispose of their lands and <a href="/wiki/Real_property" title="Real property">real property</a> by a <i>will</i>. Their <a href="/wiki/Personal_property" title="Personal property">personal property</a> could formerly be disposed of by a <i>testament</i>, hence the hallowed legal <a href="/wiki/Merism" title="Merism">merism</a> <i>last will and testament</i>.</p>
<p>Common law sharply distinguished between <a href="/wiki/Real_property" title="Real property">real property</a> and <a href="/wiki/Personal_property" title="Personal property">chattels</a>. Real property for which no disposition had been made by will passed by the law of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinship_and_descent" title="Kinship and descent">kinship and descent</a>; chattel property for which no disposition had been made by testament was <a href="/wiki/Escheat" title="Escheat">escheat</a> to <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">the Crown</a>, or given to the Church for charitable purposes. This law became obsolete as England moved from being a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feudal" title="Feudal">feudal</a> to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercantile" title="Mercantile">mercantile</a> society, and chattels more valuable than land were being accumulated by townspeople.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_law">Current law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intestacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In most contemporary common-law <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdictions</a>, the law of intestacy is patterned after the common law of descent. Property goes first or in major part to a spouse, then to <a href="/wiki/Child" title="Child">children</a> and their descendants; if there are no descendants, the rule sends you back up the family tree to the parents, the siblings, the siblings' descendants, the grandparents, the parents' siblings, and the parents' siblings' descendants, and usually so on further to the more remote degrees of kinship. The operation of these laws varies from one jurisdiction to another.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intestacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="England_and_Wales">England and Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intestacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: England and Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> the Intestacy Rules have been uniform since 1925 and similar rules apply in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> and many <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> countries and <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a>. These rules have been supplemented by the discretionary provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Inheritance_(Provision_for_Family_and_Dependants)_Act_1975" title="Inheritance (Provision for Family and Dependants) Act 1975">Inheritance (Provision for Family and Dependants) Act 1975</a> so that fair provision can be made for a dependent spouse or other relative where the strict divisions set down in the intestacy rules would produce an unfair result, for example by providing additional support for a dependent minor or disabled child vis-a-vis an adult child who has a career and no longer depends on their parent.</p>
<p>If a person dies intestate with no identifiable heirs, the person's estate generally <a href="/wiki/Escheat" title="Escheat">escheats</a> (i.e. is legally assigned) to <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">the Crown</a> (via the <a href="/wiki/Bona_vacantia" title="Bona vacantia">Bona vacantia</a> division of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treasury_Solicitor" title="Treasury Solicitor">Treasury Solicitor</a>) or to the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a> or <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Lancaster" title="Duchy of Lancaster">Duchy of Lancaster</a> when the deceased was a resident of either; in limited cases a discretionary distribution might be made by one of these bodies to persons who would otherwise be without entitlement under strict application of the rules of inheritance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>For deaths after 1 October 2014, the current rules where someone dies leaving a spouse or civil partner are as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>If there are no children, grandchildren, or great-grandchildren then the spouse or civil partner inherits the entire estate.</li>
<li>If there are children, grandchildren, or great-grandchildren, the spouse receives the personal possessions and the first £250,000, then half of everything else passing under the intestacy rules. The other half passes to the children equally at 18, with provision for grandchildren whose parents have died before the intestate deceased.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intestacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The law on intestacy in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> broadly follows that of England and Wales with some variations. A notable difference is that all possible (blood) relatives can qualify for benefit (i.e. they are not limited to grandparents or their descendants). Once a class is 'exhausted', succession continues to the next line of ascendants, followed by siblings, and so on. In a complete absence of relatives of the whole or half-blood, the estate passes to the Crown (as <i><a href="/wiki/Ultimus_haeres" title="Ultimus haeres">ultimus haeres</a></i>). The Crown has a discretion to benefit people unrelated to the intestate, e.g. those with moral claims on the estate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_and_Canada">United States and Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intestacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United States and Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States intestacy laws vary from state to state under the American practice of <a href="/wiki/Federalism" title="Federalism">federalism</a>. Likewise, in Canada the laws vary from province to province. As in England, most jurisdictions apply rules of intestate succession to determine <a href="/wiki/Next_of_kin" title="Next of kin">next of kin</a> who become legal heirs to the estate. Also, as in England, if no identifiable heirs are discovered, the property may <a href="/wiki/Escheat" title="Escheat">escheat</a> to the government.</p>
<p>Attempts in the United States to make the law with respect to intestate succession uniform from state to state have been met with limited success.</p>
<p>The distribution of the property of an intestate decedent is the responsibility of the <i>administrator</i> (or <i>personal representative</i>) of the estate: typically the administrator is chosen by the court having jurisdiction over the decedent's property, and is frequently (but not always) a person nominated by a majority of the decedent's heirs.</p>
<p>Federal law controls intestacy of <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many states have adopted all or part of the <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Probate_Code" title="Uniform Probate Code">Uniform Probate Code</a>, but often with local variations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, the law of intestate succession has been modified significantly from the common law, and has been essentially codified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The state of <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a> also has codified its intestacy law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> has perhaps the most complicated law of descent of distribution,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> having been for many years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Florida's intestacy statute permits the heirs of a deceased spouse of the decedent to inherit, in the event that the decedent has no other heirs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rules">Rules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intestacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Where a person dies without leaving a will, the rules of succession of the person's place of <a href="/wiki/Habitual_residence" title="Habitual residence">habitual residence</a> or of their <a href="/wiki/Domicile_(law)" title="Domicile (law)">domicile</a> often apply, but it is also common for the principality where the property is located to have jurisdiction regardless of the decedent's residence or domicile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In certain jurisdictions such as <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, the US state of <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, and much of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_world" title="Islamic world">Islamic world</a>, entitlements arise whether or not there was a will. These are known as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forced_heir" title="Forced heir">forced heirship</a></i> rights and are not typically found in <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> jurisdictions, where the rules of succession without a will (intestate succession) play a back-up role where an individual has not (or has not fully) exercised his or her right to dispose of property in a will.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>, the rules of succession are the Intestacy Rules set out in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Administration_of_Estates_Act" title="Administration of Estates Act">Administration of Estates Act</a> and associated legislation. The Act sets out the order for distribution of property in the estate of the deceased. For persons with surviving children and a wealth below a certain threshold (£250,000 as from February 2009), the whole of the estate will pass to the deceased's spouse or also, from December 2005, their <a href="/wiki/Civil_partnership_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Civil partnership in the United Kingdom">registered civil partner</a>. For persons with no surviving children but surviving close relatives (such as siblings or parents), the first £450,000 goes to the spouse or civil partner (as from February 2009).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Such transfers below the threshold are exempt from UK <a href="/wiki/Inheritance_tax" title="Inheritance tax">inheritance tax</a>.</p>
<p>In larger estates, the spouse will not receive the entire estate where the deceased left other blood relatives and left no will. They will receive the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>all property passing to them by survivorship (such as the deceased's share in the jointly owned family home);</li>
<li>all property passing to them under the terms of a trust (such as a life insurance policy);</li>
<li>a statutory legacy of a fixed sum (being a larger sum where the deceased left no children); and</li>
<li>a life interest in half of the remaining estate.</li>
</ul>
<p>The children (or more distant relatives if there are no children) of the deceased will be entitled to half of the estate remaining immediately and the remaining half on the death of the surviving spouse. Where no beneficiaries can be traced, see <a href="/wiki/Bona_vacantia" title="Bona vacantia">bona vacantia</a>.</p>
<p>In the United States, each of the separate states uses its own intestacy laws to determine the ownership of residents' intestate property.</p>
