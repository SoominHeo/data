<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Coreless planet</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>A <b>coreless planet</b> is a theoretical type of <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">terrestrial planet</a> that has no metallic core, i.e. the planet is effectively a giant rocky <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>. Despite the name this is not the same as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hollow_earth" title="Hollow earth">hollow earth</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coreless_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to a 2008 paper by <a href="/wiki/Sara_Seager" title="Sara Seager">Sara Seager</a> and Linda Elkins-Tanton,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> there are probably two ways in which a coreless planet may form.</p>
<p>In the first, the planet accretes from <a href="/wiki/Chondrite" title="Chondrite">chondrite</a>-like fully oxidized water-rich material, where all the metallic iron is bound into silicate mineral crystals. Such planets may form in cooler regions farther from the central star.</p>
<p>In the second, the planet accretes from both water-rich and iron metal-rich material. However, the metal iron reacts with water to form iron oxide and release hydrogen before differentiation of a metal core has taken place. Provided the iron droplets are well mixed and small enough (&lt;1 centimeter), the predicted end result is that the iron is oxidized and trapped in the mantle, unable to form a core.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_field">Magnetic field</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coreless_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Magnetic field">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Earth's magnetic field results from its flowing liquid metallic core, according to the <a href="/wiki/Dynamo_theory" title="Dynamo theory">dynamo theory</a>, but in super-Earths the mass can produce high pressures with large viscosities and high melting temperatures which could prevent the interiors from separating into different layers and so result in undifferentiated coreless mantles. Magnesium oxide, which is rocky on Earth, can be a liquid metal at the pressures and temperatures found in super-Earths and could generate a magnetic field in the mantles of super-Earths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coreless_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The predicted sizes of coreless and cored planets are similar within a few percent, which makes it difficult to interpret the interior composition of <a href="/wiki/Exoplanet" title="Exoplanet">exoplanets</a> based on measured planetary masses and radii.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
