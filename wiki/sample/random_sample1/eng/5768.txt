<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Five Civilized Tribes</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>The term "<b>Five Civilized Tribes</b>" derives from the colonial and early federal period. It refers to five <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> nations—the <a href="/wiki/Cherokee" title="Cherokee">Cherokee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chickasaw" title="Chickasaw">Chickasaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Choctaw" title="Choctaw">Choctaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muscogee" title="Muscogee">Creek</a> (Muscogee), and <a href="/wiki/Seminole" title="Seminole">Seminole</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> These are the first five tribes that Anglo-European settlers generally considered to be "civilized" according to <a href="/wiki/Ethnocentrism" title="Ethnocentrism">their own world view</a>, because these five tribes adopted attributes of the colonists' culture,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> for example, Christianity, centralized governments, literacy, market participation, written constitutions, intermarriage with white Americans, and plantation slavery practices. The Five Civilized Tribes tended to maintain stable political relations with the Europeans.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Five_Civilized_Tribes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:297px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chromesun_kincaid_site_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="503" height="235" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Chromesun_kincaid_site_01.jpg/295px-Chromesun_kincaid_site_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Chromesun_kincaid_site_01.jpg/443px-Chromesun_kincaid_site_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2a/Chromesun_kincaid_site_01.jpg 2x" width="295"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chromesun_kincaid_site_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Mississippian_culture" title="Mississippian culture">Mississippian culture</a> was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mound_builders" title="Mound builders">mound building</a> Native American urban culture that flourished in the South and Eastern United States before the arrival of Europeans.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Five "Civilized" Tribes were <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous peoples of the Americas</a> who lived in the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">Southeastern United States</a>. Most were descendants of what is now called the <a href="/wiki/Mississippian_culture" title="Mississippian culture">Mississippian culture</a>, an agrarian culture that grew crops of corn and beans, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hereditary" title="Hereditary">hereditary</a> religious and political elites. The Mississippian Culture flourished in what is now the Midwestern, Eastern, and Southeastern United States from 800 to 1500. Before European contact these tribes were generally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matrilineal" title="Matrilineal">matrilineal</a> societies. Agriculture was the primary economic pursuit. The bulk of the tribes lived in towns (some covering hundreds of acres and populated with thousands of people). These communities regulated their space with planned streets, subdivided into residential and public areas. Their system of government was hereditary. <a href="/wiki/Chiefdom" title="Chiefdom">Chiefdoms</a> were of varying size and complexity, with high levels of military organization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Knox" title="Henry Knox">Henry Knox</a> pursued an agenda of cultural transformation in relation to Native Americans. The Cherokee and Choctaw tended, in turn, to adopt and appropriate certain cultural aspects of the federation of colonies. At the time of the Declaration of Independence, the culture of the United States as a nation was, itself, emergent. Many of the cultural practices appropriated by The Five Tribes were ones that they found useful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perdue_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-perdue-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early part of the 19th Century, the US Government initiated a displacement of the existing societies living east of the Mississippi River, including The Five Tribes, to lands west of the river. This displacement initiative was dubbed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Removal" title="Indian Removal">Indian Removal</a>. The Indian Removal forced a significant number of the Five Tribes to <a href="/wiki/Indian_Territory#Five_Civilized_Tribes" title="Indian Territory">Indian Territory</a> in other parts of the North American continent. A significant number were displaced to the area that would, in future, become the state of <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>. At the time of their removal, the tribes were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suzerain" title="Suzerain">suzerain</a> nations.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trails_of_Tears_en.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1547" data-file-width="2025" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Trails_of_Tears_en.png/350px-Trails_of_Tears_en.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Trails_of_Tears_en.png/525px-Trails_of_Tears_en.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Trails_of_Tears_en.png/700px-Trails_of_Tears_en.png 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trails_of_Tears_en.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Routes of southern removals to the first Indian Territory of the Five Civilized Tribes.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The federally legislated displacement of the tribes from their homes east of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> took place over several decades during the series of removals, which is also known as the <a href="/wiki/Trail_of_Tears" title="Trail of Tears">Trail of Tears</a>. The territory to which they were displaced was, at the time, called <a href="/wiki/Indian_Territory" title="Indian Territory">Indian Territory</a>, currently eastern Oklahoma. The most infamous removal was the <a href="/wiki/Cherokee_removal" title="Cherokee removal">Cherokee Trail of Tears</a> of 1838, when President <a href="/wiki/Martin_Van_Buren" title="Martin Van Buren">Martin Van Buren</a> enforced the contentious <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_New_Echota" title="Treaty of New Echota">Treaty of New Echota</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cherokee_Nation_(19th_century)" title="Cherokee Nation (19th century)">Cherokee Nation</a>. One point of contention regarding the treaty is whether it is an instrument of mass displacement in violation of the human rights on which the new republic had been established, or a legal exchange of territory for land further west.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, the politics of the Five Tribes were divergent. The Choctaw and Chickasaw fought predominantly alongside the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederates</a> while the Creek and Seminole fought alongside the <a href="/wiki/Union_(American_Civil_War)" title="Union (American Civil War)">Union</a>. The Cherokee fought a civil war within their own nation between the majority Confederates and the minority, pro-Union camps. As an element in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reconstruction_Era_of_the_United_States" title="Reconstruction Era of the United States">Reconstruction</a> after the Civil War, new <a href="/wiki/Reconstruction_Treaties" title="Reconstruction Treaties">Reconstruction Treaties</a> were signed with the indigenous nations that had entered into treaties with the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederate States of America</a>. The Civil War was not good to the tribes. The first three battles of the Civil War were fought in Indian territory, with some tribes joining treaties with the Confederates, and others with the Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-muscogeenation-nsn.gov_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-muscogeenation-nsn.gov-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once the tribes had been relocated to <a href="/wiki/Indian_Territory" title="Indian Territory">Indian Territory</a>, the United States government promised that their lands would be free of white settlement. Some settlers violated that with impunity, even before 1893, when the government opened the "<a href="/wiki/Cherokee_Outlet" title="Cherokee Outlet">Cherokee Strip</a>" to outside settlement in the <a href="/wiki/Land_Run_of_1893" title="Land Run of 1893">Oklahoma Land Run</a>. In 1907, the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_Territory" title="Oklahoma Territory">Oklahoma Territory</a> and the Indian Territory were merged to form the state of Oklahoma. Relative to other states, all Five Tribes are represented in significant numbers in the population of Oklahoma today.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Experiment_of_civilizing">Experiment of civilizing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Five_Civilized_Tribes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Experiment of civilizing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Washington promulgated a doctrine that held that American Indians were equals, but that their society was inferior. He formulated and implemented a policy to encourage the "civilising" process, which <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a> continued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-remini_reform_begins_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-remini_reform_begins-6">[6]</a></sup> The noted Andrew Jackson historian Robert Remini wrote "they presumed that once the Indians adopted the practice of <a href="/wiki/Private_property" title="Private property">private property</a>, built homes, farmed, educated their children, and embraced Christianity, these Native Americans would win acceptance from white Americans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-remini_reform_begins_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-remini_reform_begins-6">[6]</a></sup> Washington's six-point plan included impartial justice toward Indians; regulated buying of Indian lands; promotion of commerce; promotion of experiments to <i>civilize</i> or improve Indian society; presidential authority to give <i>presents</i>; and punishing those who violated Indian rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eric_miller_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-eric_miller-7">[7]</a></sup> The government appointed agents, like <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Hawkins" title="Benjamin Hawkins">Benjamin Hawkins</a>, to live among Indians and to encourage them, through example and instruction, to live like whites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perdue_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-perdue-4">[4]</a></sup> The tribes of the southeast adopted Washington's policy as they established schools, took up <a href="/wiki/Yeoman" title="Yeoman">yeoman</a> farming practices, converted to Christianity, and built homes similar to those of their colonial neighbors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eric_miller_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-eric_miller-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cherokee_National_Capitol.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Cherokee_National_Capitol.jpg/220px-Cherokee_National_Capitol.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Cherokee_National_Capitol.jpg/330px-Cherokee_National_Capitol.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Cherokee_National_Capitol.jpg/440px-Cherokee_National_Capitol.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cherokee_National_Capitol.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Cherokee_National_Capitol" title="Cherokee National Capitol">Cherokee Nation Historic Courthouse</a> in Tahlequah, built in 1849, is the oldest public building standing in Oklahoma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>How different would be the sensation of a philosophic mind to reflect that instead of exterminating a part of the human race by our modes of population that we had persevered through all difficulties and at last had imparted our Knowledge of cultivating and the arts, to the Aboriginals of the Country by which the source of future life and happiness had been preserved and extended. But it has been conceived to be impracticable to civilize the Indians of North America – This opinion is probably more convenient than just.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Henry Knox, Notes to George Washington from Henry Knox.</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>It is notable that the legal systems among the five tribes reputedly appropriated <a href="/wiki/Slavery_among_Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Slavery among Native Americans in the United States">slavery</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cherokee">Cherokee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Five_Civilized_Tribes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cherokee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cherokee" title="Cherokee">Cherokee</a></div>
<p>The Cherokee, (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/tʃ/ 'ch' in 'china'">tʃ</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span></span>/</a></span></span>; Cherokee Ani-Yunwiya (ᎠᏂᏴᏫᏯ)) are people of the Southeastern United States, principally upland Georgia, North Carolina and South Carolina. They speak an Iroquoian language. In the 19th century, historians and ethnographers recorded their oral tradition that told of the tribe having migrated south in ancient times from the Great Lakes region, where other Iroquoian-speaking peoples were.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of the three federally recognized Cherokee tribes, the Cherokee Nation and the United Keetoowah Band of Cherokee Indians (UKB) have headquarters in Tahlequah, Oklahoma. The UKB are mostly descendants of "Old Settlers," Cherokee who migrated to Arkansas and Oklahoma about 1817. They are related to the Cherokee who were forcibly relocated there in the 1830s under the Indian Removal Act. The Eastern Band of Cherokee Indians is on the Qualla Boundary in western North Carolina, and are descendants of those who resisted or avoided relocation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In addition, there are numerous Cherokee heritage groups throughout the United States, such as the satellite communities sponsored by the Cherokee Nation. The Cherokee tribe is the second largest tribe in the nation, having more than 300,000 members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chickasaw">Chickasaw</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Five_Civilized_Tribes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chickasaw">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chickasaw" title="Chickasaw">Chickasaw</a></div>
<p>The Chickasaw are Native American people of the United States who originally resided along the <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_River" title="Tennessee River">Tennessee River</a> and other parts of Tennessee, west of present-day <a href="/wiki/Huntsville,_Alabama" title="Huntsville, Alabama">Huntsville, Alabama</a>, parts of <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> and the southwest side of Kentucky. They spoke some French and some English. Some historians credit the Chickasaws' intervention in the French and Indian War on the side of the British as decisive in ensuring that the United States became an English-speaking nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Originating further west, the Chickasaw moved east of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> long before European contact. All historical records indicate the Chickasaw lived in northeastern <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> from the first European contact until they were forced to remove to <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>, where most now live. They are related to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Choctaws" title="Choctaws">Choctaws</a>, who speak a similar language, both forming the Western Group of the <a href="/wiki/Muskogean_languages" title="Muskogean languages">Muskogean languages</a>. "Chickasaw" is the English spelling of <i>Chikasha</i> (<small>IPA: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[tʃikaʃːa]</a></span>), that either means "rebel" or "comes from Chicsa". The Chickasaw are divided in two groups: the "Impsaktea" and the "Intcutwalipa". The Chickasaws were one of the "Five Civilized Tribes" who went to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Territory" title="Indian Territory">Indian Territory</a> during the era of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Removal" title="Indian Removal">Indian Removal</a>. Unlike other tribes, who exchanged land grants, the Chickasaw received financial compensation from the United States for their lands east of the Mississippi River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burt_and_ferguson_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-burt_and_ferguson-13">[13]</a></sup> The Chickasaw Nation is the thirteenth largest federally recognized tribe in the United States. The Chickasaws built some of the first banks, schools, and businesses in Indian territory. They also signed a treaty with the Southern United States during the Civil War and brought troops to fight for the Confederates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Choctaw">Choctaw</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Five_Civilized_Tribes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Choctaw">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Choctaw" title="Choctaw">Choctaw</a></div>
<p>The <b>Choctaw</b> are a Native American people originally from the Southeastern United States (<a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> and, to a lesser extent, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>). There were about 20,000 members of this tribe when they were forced to move to Indian territory. Many of them unfortunately did not survive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> They are of the <a href="/wiki/Muskogean_languages" title="Muskogean languages">Muskogean linguistic</a> group. The word Choctaw (also known as <b>Chahta</b>, <b>Chato</b>, <b>Tchakta</b>, and <b>Chocktaw</b>) is possibly a corruption of the Spanish <i>chato</i>, meaning flattened, in allusion to the tribe's custom of flattening the heads of infants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodge1907_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hodge1907-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cushman1899_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cushman1899-17">[17]</a></sup> Noted anthropologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Swanton" title="John Swanton">John Swanton</a>, however, suggests that the name belonged to a Choctaw leader.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-john_swanton_figure_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-john_swanton_figure-18">[18]</a></sup> They were descended from people of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippian_culture" title="Mississippian culture">Mississippian culture</a> which was located throughout the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> valley. The early <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Spanish explorers</a>, according to the historian <a href="/wiki/Walter_Lee_Williams" title="Walter Lee Williams">Walter Lee Williams</a>, encountered their ancestors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walter_williams_antecedents_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-walter_williams_antecedents-19">[19]</a></sup> Although smaller Choctaw groups are located in the southern region, the <a href="/wiki/Choctaw_Nation_of_Oklahoma" title="Choctaw Nation of Oklahoma">Choctaw Nation of Oklahoma</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Band_of_Choctaw_Indians" title="Mississippi Band of Choctaw Indians">Mississippi Band of Choctaw Indians</a> are the two primary Choctaw associations. This tribe was predominantly farmers (like most Indians were at the time) until they were removed from their land. They have grown substantially since the Trail of Tears and there are currently about 200,000 members, making the Choctaw the third largest Native American population in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Muscogee_Creek">Muscogee Creek</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Five_Civilized_Tribes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Muscogee Creek">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscogee_people" title="Muscogee people">Muscogee people</a></div>
<p>The Muscogee Creek are an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Indians_in_the_United_States" title="American Indians in the United States">American Indian</a> people originally from the southeastern United States, specifically Georgia, Florida, South Carolina, and Alabama.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> They were there from around 1500 AD till they were forced out by the American Government in the early 19th century. Mvskoke is their name in traditional spelling. The Muscogee Creek tribe is not one tribe but a group of several tribes, each of which had their own distinct land. Starting in 1836, the U.S. forced them to move west of the Mississippi along with the other civilized tribes to "Indian territory". About 20,000 Muscogee members were forced to walk the <a href="/wiki/Trail_of_Tears" title="Trail of Tears">Trail of Tears</a>, the same amount as the Choctaws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-muscogeenation-nsn.gov_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-muscogeenation-nsn.gov-5">[5]</a></sup> Modern Muscogee live primarily in <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, and Florida. Their language, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creek_language" title="Creek language">Mvskoke</a></i>, is a member of the Creek branch of the <a href="/wiki/Muskogean_languages" title="Muskogean languages">Muskogean language family</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seminole_(tribe)" title="Seminole (tribe)">Seminole</a> are related to the Muscogee and speak a Creek language as well.</p>
<p>Federally recognized Creek tribes included the <a href="/wiki/Muscogee_(Creek)_Nation" title="Muscogee (Creek) Nation">Muscogee Creek Nation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poarch_Band_of_Creek_Indians" title="Poarch Band of Creek Indians">Poarch Band of Creek Indians</a> in Alabama, <a href="/wiki/Alabama-Quassarte_Tribal_Town" title="Alabama-Quassarte Tribal Town">Alabama-Quassarte Tribal Town</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kialegee_Tribal_Town" title="Kialegee Tribal Town">Kialegee Tribal Town</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thlopthlocco_Tribal_Town" title="Thlopthlocco Tribal Town">Thlopthlocco Tribal Town</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miccosukee_Tribe_of_Indians_of_Florida" title="Miccosukee Tribe of Indians of Florida">Miccosukee Tribe of Indians of Florida</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seminole">Seminole</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Five_Civilized_Tribes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Seminole">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Seminole" title="Seminole">Seminole</a></div>
<p>The Seminole are a Native American people originally of Florida and now residing in Florida and <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>. The Seminole nation came into existence in the 18th century and was composed of renegade and outcast Native Americans from <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, most significantly the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creek_people" title="Creek people">Creek Nation</a>, as well as African Americans who escaped from <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_United_States" title="Slavery in the United States">slavery</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a> and Georgia. While roughly 3,000 Seminoles were forced west of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>, including the Seminole Nation of Oklahoma, who picked up new members along the way, approximately 300 to 500 Seminoles stayed and fought in and around the <a href="/wiki/Everglades" title="Everglades">Everglades</a> of Florida. In a series of United States <a href="/wiki/Seminole_Wars" title="Seminole Wars">wars against the Seminoles</a> in Florida, about 1,500 U.S. soldiers died. The Seminoles never surrendered to the US government, and consequently the Seminole of Florida call themselves the "Unconquered People".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GreenbergProctor1997_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-GreenbergProctor1997-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOS2016_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-DOS2016-24">[24]</a></sup> Federally recognized Seminole tribes today include the <a href="/wiki/Seminole_Nation_of_Oklahoma" title="Seminole Nation of Oklahoma">Seminole Nation of Oklahoma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seminole_Tribe_of_Florida" title="Seminole Tribe of Florida">Seminole Tribe of Florida</a>. For about twenty years after the move to Indian territory (Oklahoma) the Seminoles refused to live with the Muscogee Creek tribe or under their Government until they finally reached an agreement with the Government to sign a treaty and live with them. The Seminoles favored the North during the Civil war and remained loyal to the Union and proceeded to move north into Kansas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology_and_usage">Terminology and usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Five_Civilized_Tribes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Terminology and usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The use of the term "civilized" has historically been employed to differentiate the Five Tribes from other tribes that, by contrast, were referred to during the same period as "wild". Non-indigenous historical texts have used terms like "savages" and "wild" as an identifier of Indian tribes that, after European Contact, maintained their traditional practices, such as hunting for food. Accordingly, the term has virtually fallen out of use in contemporary academic arenas, and is increasingly less favored.</p>
<p>Use of the term "civilized" is of particular significance, however, in reference to the Five Tribes collectively, given that during a particular period of history they had a collective tendency to integrate Anglo-European cultural practices into their own cultures. This common tendency is itself of interest. Sociological, anthropological and interdisciplinary scholars do explore questions of how and why various cultures and sub-cultures adopt, appropriate, and adapt to features of other cultures in their proximity. Suggestions do exist that the concept of "civilization" was itself to a degree internalized among members of the Five Nations, who themselves even used the term. However, much of Native North American history is of the oral tradition, which make this assertion difficult to substantiate. Scholarly research on this particular point is also scarce.</p>
<p>The term "civilized" in present-day discussion of Native American culture, nationhood, or people, is nonetheless contentious and has largely fallen out of common use. At various places and times (for example in the writings of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vine_Deloria,_Jr." title="Vine Deloria, Jr.">Vine Deloria, Jr.</a>) the term has been identified as insulting or derogatory, with its implication that the various nations of people indigenous to the North American continent were objectively "uncivilized" without the influence of Anglo-European ways of life. As such, the term raises questions of what constitutes "civilization" and assumes that qualitative "degrees" of civilization exist between peoples. It is therefore regarded as a judgment-laden term at worst, or highly perspective-dependent at best. Accordingly, its application in contemporary scholarly, journalistic, or political narrative puts those who would use it at risk of criticism for ethnocentricity or bias.</p>
<p>Owing to its historical usage, however, The Five "Civilized" Tribes is a term that can be used to advantage in a piece of writing provided that an explanation addresses the notion of "civilized" at its first mention. "The Five Tribes" functions as a more neutral form of the term thereafter. An alternative term in use is the Five Tribes of the Mississippian Culture, or simply, the Mississippian Culture. Similarly, and depending on the context, terms such as "culturally retentive", "culturally resilient", "culturally resolute" could function as possible variants to replace the highly pejorative adjective "wild" when referring to members of the human race.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
