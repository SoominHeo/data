<p>A <b>document</b> is a written, drawn, presented or recorded representation of thoughts. Originating from the Latin <i>Documentum</i> meaning lesson - the verb doceō means to teach, and is pronounced similarly, in the past it was usually used as a term for a written proof used as evidence. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_age" title="Computer age">computer age</a>, a document is usually used to describe a primarily textual <a href="/wiki/Computer_file" title="Computer file">file</a>, along with its structure and design, such as fonts, colors and additional <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">images</a>.</p>
<p>The modern term 'document' can no longer be defined by it <a href="/wiki/Transmission_medium" title="Transmission medium">transmission medium</a> (such as paper), following the existence of <a href="/wiki/Electronic_document" title="Electronic document">electronic documents</a>. 'Documentation' has more meanings than a written or drawn presentation of thoughts.</p>
<p>The formal term 'document' is defined in <a href="/wiki/Library_and_information_science" title="Library and information science">Library and information science</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Documentation_science" title="Documentation science">documentation science</a>, as a basic theoretical construct. It is everything which may be preserved or represented in order to serve as evidence for some purpose. The classical example provided by <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Briet" title="Suzanne Briet">Suzanne Briet</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Antelope" title="Antelope">antelope</a>: "An antelope running wild on the plains of Africa should not be considered a document, she rules. But if it were to be captured, taken to a zoo and made an object of study, it has been made into a document. It has become physical evidence being used by those who study it. Indeed, scholarly articles written about the antelope are secondary documents, since the antelope itself is the primary document." (Quoted from Buckland, 1998 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>). (This view has been seen as an early expression of what now is known as <a href="/wiki/Actor%E2%80%93network_theory" title="Actor–network theory">actor–network theory</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_document_concept">The document concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Document&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The document concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of "document" has been defined<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (July 2015)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> as <b>“any concrete or symbolic indication, preserved or recorded, for reconstructing or for proving a phenomenon, whether physical or mental"</b> (Briet, 1951, 7; here quoted from Buckland, 1991).</p>
<p>A much-cited article asked "what is a document[?]" and concluded this way: “The evolving notion of ‘‘document’’ among (Jonathan Priest). <a href="/wiki/Paul_Otlet" title="Paul Otlet">Otlet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Briet" title="Suzanne Briet">Briet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Sch%C3%BCrmeyer" title="Walter Schürmeyer">Schürmeyer</a>, and the other <a href="/wiki/Documentalist" title="Documentalist">documentalists</a> increasingly emphasized whatever functioned as a document rather than traditional physical forms of documents. The shift to digital technology would seem to make this distinction even more important. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_M_Levy" title="David M Levy">Levy</a>’s thoughtful analyses have shown that an emphasis on the technology of digital documents has impeded our understanding of digital documents as documents (e.g., Levy, 1994<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>). A conventional document, such as a mail message or a technical report, exists physically in digital technology as a string of bits, as does everything else in a digital environment. As an object of study, it has been made<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (July 2015)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> into a document. It has become physical evidence by those who study it.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_documents">Types of documents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Document&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of documents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Documents are sometimes classified as <a href="/wiki/Classified_information" title="Classified information">secret</a>, <a href="/wiki/Privacy" title="Privacy">private</a> or public. They may also be described as a <a href="/wiki/Draft_document" title="Draft document">draft</a> or <a href="/wiki/Proofreading" title="Proofreading">proof</a>. When a document is <a href="/wiki/Copying" title="Copying">copied</a>, the source is referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Originality" title="Originality">original</a>.</p>
<p>There are accepted standards for specific applications in various fields, such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Academic: <a href="/wiki/Thesis" title="Thesis">thesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academic_publishing" title="Academic publishing">paper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Journal" title="Journal">journal</a>.</li>
<li>Business and accounting: <a href="/wiki/Invoice" title="Invoice">Invoice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Financial_quote" title="Financial quote">quote</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RFP" title="RFP">RFP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proposal_(business)" title="Proposal (business)">Proposal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">Contract</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Packing_slip" title="Packing slip">Packing slip</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ship%27s_manifest" title="Ship's manifest">Manifest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Report" title="Report">Report</a> detailed &amp; summary, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spread_sheet" title="Spread sheet">Spread sheet</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MSDS" title="MSDS">MSDS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waybill" title="Waybill">Waybill</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_of_Lading" title="Bill of Lading">Bill of Lading</a> (BOL), <a href="/wiki/Financial_statement" title="Financial statement">Financial statement</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nondisclosure_agreement" title="Nondisclosure agreement">Nondisclosure agreement</a> (NDA) or sometimes referred to as; Mutual nondisclosure agreement (MNDA).</li>
<li>Law and politics: <a href="/wiki/Summons" title="Summons">summons</a>, <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Certificate_(disambiguation)#Official_document" title="Certificate (disambiguation)">certificate</a>, <a href="/wiki/License" title="License">license</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gazette" title="Gazette">gazette</a>.</li>
<li>Government and industry: <a href="/wiki/White_paper" title="White paper">white paper</a>, application forms, user-guide.</li>
<li>Media and marketing: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=News_in_brief&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="News in brief (page does not exist)">brief</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mock-up" title="Mock-up">mock-up</a>, <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">script</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Such standard documents can be created based on a <a href="/wiki/Template_(word_processing)" title="Template (word processing)">template</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Developing_documents">Developing documents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Document&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Developing documents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Page_layout" title="Page layout">page layout</a> of a document is the manner in which information is graphically arranged in the document space (e.g., on a page). If the appearance of the document is of concern, page layout is generally the responsibility of a <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">graphic designer</a>. <a href="/wiki/Typography" title="Typography">Typography</a> deals with the design of letter and symbol forms, as well as their physical arrangement in the document (see <a href="/wiki/Typesetting" title="Typesetting">typesetting</a>). <a href="/wiki/Information_design" title="Information design">Information design</a> focuses on the effective communication of <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a>, especially in industrial documents and public <a href="/wiki/Sign" title="Sign">signs</a>. Simple text documents may not require a visual design and may be handled by an <a href="/wiki/Author" title="Author">author</a>, <a href="/wiki/Data_entry_clerk" title="Data entry clerk">clerk</a> or <a href="/wiki/Transcriber" title="Transcriber">transcriber</a>. <a href="/wiki/Form_(document)" title="Form (document)">Forms</a> may require a visual design for the initial fields, but not to fill out the forms.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Document&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Traditionally, the medium of a document was <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> and the information was applied to it as <a href="/wiki/Ink" title="Ink">ink</a>, either by hand (to make a hand-written document) or by a mechanical process (such as a <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a> or, more recently, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laser_printer" title="Laser printer">laser printer</a>).</p>
<p>Through time, documents have also been written with ink on <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a> (starting in ancient <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">parchment</a>; scratched as <a href="/wiki/Runes" title="Runes">runes</a> or carved on stone using a sharp apparatus (such as the <a href="/wiki/Tablets_of_Stone" title="Tablets of Stone">Tablets of Stone</a> described in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_bible" title="The bible">the bible</a>); stamped or cut into <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> and then baked to make <a href="/wiki/Clay_tablet" title="Clay tablet">clay tablets</a> (e.g., in the <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerian</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamian</a> civilisations). The paper, papyrus or parchment might be rolled up as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scroll_(parchment)" title="Scroll (parchment)">scroll</a> or cut into sheets and <a href="/wiki/Bookbinding" title="Bookbinding">bound</a> into a <a href="/wiki/Book" title="Book">book</a>. Today short documents might also consist of sheets of paper <a href="/wiki/Staple_(fastener)" title="Staple (fastener)">stapled</a> together.</p>
<p>Modern electronic means of storing and displaying documents include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Desktop_computer" title="Desktop computer">Desktop computer</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_display" title="Computer display">monitor</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Laptop" title="Laptop">laptop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tablet_computer" title="Tablet computer">tablet PC</a>, etc.); optionally with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_printer" title="Computer printer">printer</a> to obtain a <a href="/wiki/Hard_copy" title="Hard copy">hard copy</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Personal_digital_assistant" title="Personal digital assistant">Personal digital assistant</a> (PDA).</li>
<li>Dedicated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-book_device" title="E-book device">e-book device</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Electronic_paper" title="Electronic paper">Electronic paper</a>, typically, using the Portable Document Format <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PDF" title="PDF">PDF</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Information_appliance" title="Information appliance">Information appliances</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_audio_player" title="Digital audio player">Digital audio players</a>.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_law">In law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Document&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Documents in all forms are frequently found to be material <a href="/wiki/Evidence" title="Evidence">evidence</a> in criminal and civil proceedings. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forensic" title="Forensic">forensic</a> analysis of such a document falls under the scope of <a href="/wiki/Questioned_document_examination" title="Questioned document examination">questioned document examination</a>. For the purpose of cataloging and managing the large number of documents that may be produced in the course of a lawsuit, <a href="/wiki/Bates_numbering" title="Bates numbering">Bates numbering</a> is often applied to all documents so that each document has a unique, aribitrary identifying number.</p>
