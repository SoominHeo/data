<p><b>Mañjuśrī</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Bodhisattva" title="Bodhisattva">bodhisattva</a> associated with <a href="/wiki/Praj%C3%B1%C4%81_(Buddhism)" title="Prajñā (Buddhism)">prajñā</a> (insight) in <a href="/wiki/Mahayana" title="Mahayana">Mahayana Buddhism</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism" title="Tibetan Buddhism">Tibetan Buddhism</a>, he is also a <a href="/wiki/Yidam" title="Yidam">yidam</a>. His name means "Gentle Glory" in <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Mañjuśrī is also known by the fuller name of Mañjuśrīkumārabhūta,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keown.2C_Damien_2003_p.172_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keown.2C_Damien_2003_p.172-2">[2]</a></sup> literally "Mañjuśrī, Still a Youth" or, less literally, "Prince Mañjuśrī".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Mah.C4.81y.C4.81na_Buddhism">In Mahāyāna Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manjushri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In Mahāyāna Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Manjushri_statue._Lalung_Gompa,_Spiti.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="768" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Manjushri_statue._Lalung_Gompa%2C_Spiti.jpg/200px-Manjushri_statue._Lalung_Gompa%2C_Spiti.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Manjushri_statue._Lalung_Gompa%2C_Spiti.jpg/300px-Manjushri_statue._Lalung_Gompa%2C_Spiti.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Manjushri_statue._Lalung_Gompa%2C_Spiti.jpg/400px-Manjushri_statue._Lalung_Gompa%2C_Spiti.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

</div>
</div>
<p>Scholars have identified Mañjuśrī as the oldest and most significant bodhisattva in Mahāyāna literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Mañjuśrī is first referred to in early <a href="/wiki/Mahayana_sutras" title="Mahayana sutras">Mahayana sutras</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Prajnaparamita" title="Prajnaparamita">Prajnaparamita</a> sutras and through this association very early in the tradition he came to symbolize the embodiment of <i>prajñā</i> (transcendent wisdom).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keown.2C_Damien_2003_p.172_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Keown.2C_Damien_2003_p.172-2">[2]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Lotus_Sutra" title="Lotus Sutra">Lotus Sutra</a></i> assigns him a <a href="/wiki/Pure_land" title="Pure land">pure land</a> called <i>Vimala</i>, which according to the <i><a href="/wiki/Avatamsaka_Sutra" title="Avatamsaka Sutra">Avatamsaka Sutra</a></i> is located in the East. His pure land is predicted to be one of the two best pure lands in all of existence in all the past, present and future. When he attains <a href="/wiki/Buddhahood" title="Buddhahood">buddhahood</a> his name will be Universal Sight. In the Lotus Sūtra, Mañjuśrī also leads the <a href="/wiki/Nagaraja" title="Nagaraja">Nagaraja</a>'s daughter to enlightenment. He also figures in the <i><a href="/wiki/Vimalakirti_Sutra" title="Vimalakirti Sutra">Vimalakirti Sutra</a></i> in a debate with <a href="/wiki/Vimalakirti" title="Vimalakirti">Vimalakirti</a>.</p>
<p>An example of a wisdom teaching of Mañjuśrī can be found in the <i>Saptaśatikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra</i> (<a href="/wiki/Taish%C5%8D_Tripi%E1%B9%ADaka" title="Taishō Tripiṭaka">Taishō Tripiṭaka</a> 232).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This sūtra contains a dialogue between Mañjuśrī and the Buddha on the One <a href="/wiki/Samadhi" title="Samadhi">Samadhi</a> (Skt. <i>Ekavyūha Samādhi</i>). <a href="/wiki/Sheng-yen" title="Sheng-yen">Sheng-yen</a> renders the following teaching of Mañjuśrī, for entering samādhi naturally through transcendent wisdom:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Contemplate the five <a href="/wiki/Skandha" title="Skandha">skandhas</a> as originally empty and quiescent, non-arising, non-perishing, equal, without differentiation. Constantly thus practicing, day or night, whether sitting, walking, standing or lying down, finally one reaches an inconceivable state without any obstruction or form. This is the Samadhi of One Act (<i>yixing sanmei</i>, 一行三昧).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vajrayana_Buddhism">Vajrayana Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manjushri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vajrayana Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Within <a href="/wiki/Vajrayana" title="Vajrayana">Vajrayana Buddhism</a>, Mañjuśrī is a meditational deity, and considered a fully enlightened Buddha. In <a href="/wiki/Shingon_Buddhism" title="Shingon Buddhism">Shingon Buddhism</a>, he is one of the <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Buddhas" title="Thirteen Buddhas">Thirteen Buddhas</a> to whom disciples devote themselves. He figures extensively in many esoteric texts such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Ma%C3%B1ju%C5%9Br%C4%AB-m%C5%ABla-kalpa" title="Mañjuśrī-mūla-kalpa">Mañjuśrī-mūla-kalpa</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keown.2C_Damien_2003_p.172_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Keown.2C_Damien_2003_p.172-2">[2]</a></sup> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Ma%C3%B1ju%C5%9Br%C4%ABn%C4%81masamg%C4%ABti" title="Mañjuśrīnāmasamgīti">Mañjuśrīnāmasamgīti</a></i>. His consort in some traditions is <a href="/wiki/Saraswati" title="Saraswati">Saraswati</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ma%C3%B1ju%C5%9Br%C4%AB-m%C5%ABla-kalpa" title="Mañjuśrī-mūla-kalpa">Mañjusrimulakalpa</a>, which later came to classified under <a href="/wiki/Outer_Tantras#Kriyatantra" title="Outer Tantras">Kriyatantra</a>, states that mantras taught in the <a href="/wiki/Shaivism" title="Shaivism">Saiva</a>, Garuda and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vaisnava" title="Vaisnava">Vaisnava</a> tantras will be effective if applied by Buddhists since they were all taught originally by Manjushri.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Iconography">Iconography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manjushri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Iconography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mañjuśrī is depicted as a male bodhisattva wielding a flaming sword in his right hand, representing the realization of transcendent wisdom which cuts down ignorance and duality. The scripture supported by the <a href="/wiki/Padma_(attribute)" title="Padma (attribute)">padma</a> (lotus) held in his left hand is a <i>Prajñāpāramitā sūtra</i>, representing his attainment of ultimate realization from the blossoming of wisdom. Mañjuśrī is often depicted as riding on a blue <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lion</a>, or sitting on the skin of a lion. This represents the use of wisdom to tame the mind, which is compared to riding or subduing a ferocious lion.</p>
<p>In Chinese and Japanese Buddhist art, Mañjuśrī's sword is sometimes replaced with a <i><a href="/wiki/Ruyi_(scepter)" title="Ruyi (scepter)">ruyi</a></i> scepter, especially in representations of his <i><a href="/wiki/Vimalakirti_Sutra" title="Vimalakirti Sutra">Vimalakirti Sutra</a></i> discussion with the layman <a href="/wiki/Vimalakirti" title="Vimalakirti">Vimalakirti</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Berthold_Laufer" title="Berthold Laufer">Berthold Laufer</a>, the first Chinese representation of a <i>ruyi</i> was in an 8th-century Mañjuśrī painting by <a href="/wiki/Wu_Daozi" title="Wu Daozi">Wu Daozi</a>, showing it held in his right hand taking the place of the usual sword. In subsequent Chinese and Japanese paintings of Buddhas, a <i>ruyi</i> was occasionally represented as a padma with a long stem curved like a <i>ruyi</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>He is one of the Four Great Bodhisattvas of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Buddhism" title="Chinese Buddhism">Chinese Buddhism</a>, the other three being <a href="/wiki/Kshitigarbha" title="Kshitigarbha">Kṣitigarbha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avalokite%C5%9Bvara" title="Avalokiteśvara">Avalokiteśvara</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Samantabhadra" title="Samantabhadra">Samantabhadra</a>. In China, he is often paired with Samantabhadra.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism" title="Tibetan Buddhism">Tibetan Buddhism</a>, Mañjuśrī is sometimes depicted in a trinity with Avalokiteśvara and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vajrap%C4%81%E1%B9%87i" title="Vajrapāṇi">Vajrapāṇi</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mantras">Mantras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manjushri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mantras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Mantra" title="Mantra">mantra</a> commonly associated with Mañjuśrī is the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd><i>oṃ arapacana dhīḥ</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>The Arapacana is a <a href="/wiki/Syllabary" title="Syllabary">syllabary</a> consisting of forty-two letters, and is named after the first five letters: <i>a, ra, pa, ca, na</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This syllabary was most widely used for the <a href="/wiki/G%C4%81ndh%C4%81r%C4%AB_language" title="Gāndhārī language">Gāndhārī language</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Kharosthi" title="Kharosthi">Kharoṣṭhī script</a>, but also appears in some Sanskrit texts. The syllabary features in Mahāyāna texts such as the longer <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Praj%C3%B1%C4%81p%C4%81ramit%C4%81" title="Prajñāpāramitā">Prajñāpāramitā</a></i> texts, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ga%E1%B9%87%E1%B8%8Davy%C5%ABha_S%C5%ABtra" title="Gaṇḍavyūha Sūtra">Gaṇḍavyūha Sūtra</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Lalitavistara_S%C5%ABtra" title="Lalitavistara Sūtra">Lalitavistara Sūtra</a></i>, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avata%E1%B9%83saka_S%C5%ABtra" title="Avataṃsaka Sūtra">Avataṃsaka Sūtra</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Dharmaguptaka" title="Dharmaguptaka">Dharmaguptaka</a> <a href="/wiki/Vinaya" title="Vinaya">Vinaya</a></i>, and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C5%ABlasarv%C4%81stiv%C4%81da" title="Mūlasarvāstivāda">Mūlasarvāstivāda</a> Vinaya</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In some of these texts, the Arapacana syllabary serves as a <a href="/wiki/Mnemonic" title="Mnemonic">mnemonic</a> for important Mahāyāna concepts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Due to its association with him, <i>Arapacana</i> may even serve as an alternate name for Mañjuśrī.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Sutra on Perfect Wisdom</i> (Conze 1975) defines the significance of each syllable thus:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ol>
<li><i>A</i> is a door to the insight that all dharmas are unproduced from the very beginning (<i>ādya-anutpannatvād</i>).</li>
<li><i>RA</i> is a door to the insight that all dharmas are without dirt (<i>rajas</i>).</li>
<li><i>PA</i> is a door to the insight that all dharmas have been expounded in the ultimate sense (<i>paramārtha</i>).</li>
<li><i>CA</i> is a door to the insight that the decrease (<i>cyavana</i>) or rebirth of any dharma cannot be apprehended, because all dharmas do not decrease, nor are they reborn.</li>
<li><i>NA</i> is a door to the insight that the names (i.e. <i>nāma</i>) of all dharmas have vanished; the essential nature behind names cannot be gained or lost.</li>
</ol>
<p>Tibetan pronunciation is slightly different and so the Tibetan characters read: <i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">oṃ a ra pa tsa na dhīḥ</span></i> (<a href="/wiki/Standard_Tibetan" title="Standard Tibetan">Tibetan</a>: <span lang="bo" style="font-family: 'Qomolangma-Dunhuang', 'Qomolangma-Uchen Sarchen', 'Qomolangma-Uchen Sarchung', 'Qomolangma-Uchen Suring', 'Qomolangma-Uchen Sutung', 'Qomolangma-Title', 'Qomolangma-Subtitle', 'Qomolangma-Woodblock', 'DDC Uchen', Kailash, Jomolhari, 'TCRC Youtso Unicode', 'Tibetan Machine Uni', 'Noto Sans Tibetan', 'Microsoft Himalaya'; font-size:1.25em; vertical-align:top; word-wrap: break-word;" xml:lang="bo">ༀ་ཨ་ར་པ་ཙ་ན་དྷཱི༔</span>, <a href="/wiki/Wylie_transliteration" title="Wylie transliteration">Wylie</a>: <i>om a ra pa tsa na d+hIH</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In Tibetan tradition, this mantra is believed to enhance wisdom and improve one's skills in debating, memory, writing, and other literary abilities. "<span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">Dhīḥ</span>" is the seed syllable of the mantra and is chanted with greater emphasis and also repeated a number of times as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decrescendo" title="Decrescendo">Decrescendo</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_China">In China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manjushri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mañjuśrī is known in China as <i>Wenshu</i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">文殊</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Wénshū</span></i>). <a href="/wiki/Mount_Wutai" title="Mount Wutai">Mount Wutai</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shanxi" title="Shanxi">Shanxi</a>, one of the four <a href="/wiki/Sacred_Mountains_of_China" title="Sacred Mountains of China">Sacred Mountains of China</a>, is considered by Chinese Buddhists to be his <a href="/wiki/Bodhima%E1%B9%87%E1%B8%8Da" title="Bodhimaṇḍa">bodhimaṇḍa</a>. He was said to bestow spectacular visionary experiences to those on selected mountain peaks and caves there. In Wutai Shan's <a href="/wiki/Foguang_Temple" title="Foguang Temple">Foguang Temple</a>, the Manjusri Hall to the right of its main hall was recognized to have been built in 1137 during the <a href="/wiki/Jin_dynasty_(1115%E2%80%931234)" title="Jin dynasty (1115–1234)">Jin dynasty</a>. The hall was thoroughly studied, mapped and first photographed by early twentieth-century Chinese architects <a href="/wiki/Liang_Sicheng" title="Liang Sicheng">Liang Sicheng</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lin_Huiyin" title="Lin Huiyin">Lin Huiyin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> These made it a popular place of pilgrimage, but patriarchs including <a href="/wiki/Linji_Yixuan" title="Linji Yixuan">Linji Yixuan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yunmen_Wenyan" title="Yunmen Wenyan">Yunmen Wenyan</a> declared the mountain off limits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mount Wutai was also associated with the <a href="/wiki/East_Mountain_Teaching" title="East Mountain Teaching">East Mountain Teaching</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Mañjuśrī has been associated with Mount Wutai since ancient times. Paul Williams writes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Apparently the association of Mañjuśrī with Wutai (Wu-t'ai) Shan in north China was known in classical times in India itself, identified by Chinese scholars with the mountain in the 'north-east' (when seen from India or <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>) referred to as the abode of Mañjuśrī in the <i><a href="/wiki/Avatamsaka_Sutra" title="Avatamsaka Sutra">Avataṃsaka Sūtra</a></i>. There are said to have been pilgrimages from India and other Asian countries to Wutai Shan by the seventh century.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>According to official histories from the <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nurhaci" title="Nurhaci">Nurhaci</a>, a military leader of the <a href="/wiki/Jurchen_people" title="Jurchen people">Jurchens</a> of <a href="/wiki/Northeast_China" title="Northeast China">Northeast China</a> and founder of what became the Qing dynasty, named his tribe after Mañjuśrī as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manchus" title="Manchus">Manchus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The true origin of the name Manchu is disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Monk Hanshan (寒山) is widely considered to be a metaphorical manifestation of Mañjuśrī. He is known for co-written the following famous poem about reincarnation with monk Shide:</p>
<p>Drumming your grandpa in the shrine,<br/>
Cooking your aunts in the pot,<br/>
Marrying your grandma in the past,<br/>
Should I laugh or not?<br/></p>
<p>堂上打鼓打公皮，<br/>
鍋內煎煮是姑娘，<br/>
三世祖母娶為婦，<br/>
我今不笑等何時。<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Tibet">In Tibet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manjushri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In Tibet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Tibetan Buddhism, Mañjuśrī manifests in a number of different <a href="/wiki/Tantra" title="Tantra">Tantric</a> forms. <a href="/wiki/Yamantaka" title="Yamantaka">Yamāntaka</a> (meaning 'terminator of <a href="/wiki/Yama" title="Yama">Yama</a> i.e. Death') is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wrathful_deity" title="Wrathful deity">wrathful</a> manifestation of Mañjuśrī, popular within the <a href="/wiki/Gelug" title="Gelug">Gelug</a> school of Tibetan Buddhism. Other variations upon his traditional form as Mañjuśrī include Guhya-Manjusri, Guhya-Manjuvajra, and Manjuswari. The two former appearances are generally accompanied by a <a href="/wiki/Shakti" title="Shakti">shakti</a> deity embracing the main figure, symbolising union of wisdom and bliss-emptiness.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sakya_Pandita" title="Sakya Pandita">Sakya Pandita</a>, <a href="/wiki/Je_Tsongkhapa" title="Je Tsongkhapa">Je Tsongkhapa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jamgon_Ju_Mipham_Gyatso" title="Jamgon Ju Mipham Gyatso">Jamgon Ju Mipham Gyatso</a> are considered to be human manifestations of Manjushri.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Nepal">In Nepal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manjushri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In Nepal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Swayambhu_Purana" title="Swayambhu Purana">Swayambhu Purana</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kathmandu_Valley" title="Kathmandu Valley">Kathmandu Valley</a> was once a lake. It is believed that Mañjuśrī saw a lotus flower in the center of the lake and cut a gorge at Chovar to allow the lake to drain. The place where the lotus flower settled became <a href="/wiki/Swayambhunath" title="Swayambhunath">Swayambhunath</a> <a href="/wiki/Stupa" title="Stupa">Stupa</a> and the valley thus became habitable.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Japan">In Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manjushri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Late apocryphal traditions held that Mañjuśrī (Monju or Monjushiri in Japanese) "invented" <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality_in_Japan#Monastic_same-sex_love" title="Homosexuality in Japan">nanshoku</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Indonesia">In Indonesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manjushri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In Indonesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In eighth century <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Medang_Kingdom" title="Medang Kingdom">Medang Kingdom</a>, Mañjuśrī was a prominent deity revered by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sailendra_dynasty" title="Sailendra dynasty">Sailendra dynasty</a>, patrons of Mahayana Buddhism. The <a href="/wiki/Kelurak_inscription" title="Kelurak inscription">Kelurak inscription</a> (782) and <a href="/wiki/Manjusrigrha_inscription" title="Manjusrigrha inscription">Manjusrigrha inscription</a> (792) mentioned about the construction of a grand prasada named <i>Vajrāsana Mañjuśrīgṛha</i> (Vajra House of Mañjuśrī) identified today as <a href="/wiki/Sewu" title="Sewu">Sewu</a> temple, located just 800 meters north of the <a href="/wiki/Prambanan" title="Prambanan">Prambanan</a>. Sewu is the second largest Buddhist temple in Central Java after <a href="/wiki/Borobudur" title="Borobudur">Borobudur</a>. The depiction of Mañjuśrī in Sailendra art is similar to those of the <a href="/wiki/Pala_Empire" title="Pala Empire">Pala Empire</a> style of <a href="/wiki/Nalanda" title="Nalanda">Nalanda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bihar" title="Bihar">Bihar</a>. Mañjuśrī was portrayed as a youthful handsome man with the palm of his hands tattooed with the image of flower. His right hand is facing down with an open palm while his left hand holds an <i>utpala</i> (blue lotus). He also uses the necklace made of tiger <a href="/wiki/Canine_tooth" title="Canine tooth">canine teeth</a>.</p>
