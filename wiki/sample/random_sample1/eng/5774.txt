<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Character (computing)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a> and machine-based <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunications</a> terminology, a <b>character</b> is a unit of <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a> that roughly corresponds to a <a href="/wiki/Grapheme" title="Grapheme">grapheme</a>, grapheme-like unit, or symbol, such as in an <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabet</a> or <a href="/wiki/Syllabary" title="Syllabary">syllabary</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Written_language" title="Written language">written</a> form of a <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">natural language</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Examples of characters include <a href="/wiki/Letter_(alphabet)" title="Letter (alphabet)">letters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Numerical_digit" title="Numerical digit">numerical digits</a>, common <a href="/wiki/Punctuation" title="Punctuation">punctuation</a> marks (such as "." or "-"), and <a href="/wiki/Whitespace_character" title="Whitespace character">whitespace</a>. The concept also includes <a href="/wiki/Control_character" title="Control character">control characters</a>, which do not correspond to symbols in a particular natural language, but rather to other bits of information used to process text in one or more languages. Examples of control characters include <a href="/wiki/Carriage_return" title="Carriage return">carriage return</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tab_key" title="Tab key">tab</a>, as well as instructions to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_printer" title="Computer printer">printers</a> or other devices that display or otherwise process text.</p>
<p>Characters are typically combined into <a href="/wiki/String_(computer_science)" title="String (computer science)">strings</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Character_encoding">Character encoding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Character_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Character encoding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Character_encoding" title="Character encoding">Character encoding</a></div>
<p>Computers and communication equipment represent characters using a <a href="/wiki/Character_encoding" title="Character encoding">character encoding</a> that assigns each character to something — an <a href="/wiki/Integer" title="Integer">integer</a> quantity represented by a sequence of <a href="/wiki/Numerical_digit" title="Numerical digit">digits</a>, typically — that can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_storage" title="Computer storage">stored</a> or transmitted through a <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">network</a>. Two examples of usual encodings are <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a> and the <a href="/wiki/UTF-8" title="UTF-8">UTF-8</a> encoding for <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a>. While most character encodings map characters to numbers and/or bit sequences, <a href="/wiki/Morse_code" title="Morse code">Morse code</a> instead represents characters using a series of electrical impulses of varying length.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Character_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, the term <i>character</i> has been widely used by industry professionals to refer to an <i>encoded character</i>, often as defined by the programming language or <a href="/wiki/Application_programming_interface" title="Application programming interface">API</a>. Likewise, <i>character set</i> has been widely used to refer to a specific repertoire of characters that have been mapped to specific bit sequences or numerical codes. The term <a href="/wiki/Glyph" title="Glyph">glyph</a> is used to describe a particular visual appearance of a character. Many computer <a href="/wiki/Typeface" title="Typeface">fonts</a> consist of glyphs that are indexed by the numerical code of the corresponding character.</p>
<p>With the advent and widespread acceptance of Unicode<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-movingtounicode_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-movingtounicode-2">[2]</a></sup> and bit-agnostic <i>coded character sets</i>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2009)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> a character is increasingly being seen as a unit of <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">information</a>, independent of any particular visual manifestation. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Character_Set" title="Universal Character Set">ISO/IEC 10646 (Unicode) International Standard</a> defines <i>character</i>, or <i>abstract character</i> as "a member of a set of elements used for the organisation, control, or representation of data". Unicode's definition supplements this with explanatory notes that encourage the reader to differentiate between characters, graphemes, and glyphs, among other things. Such differentiation is an instance of the wider theme of the <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_presentation_and_content" title="Separation of presentation and content">separation of presentation and content</a>.</p>
<p>For example, the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_alphabet" title="Hebrew alphabet">Hebrew letter</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aleph_(letter)" title="Aleph (letter)">aleph</a> ("א") is often used by mathematicians to denote certain kinds of <a href="/wiki/Aleph_number" title="Aleph number">infinity</a>, but it is also used in ordinary Hebrew text. In Unicode, these two uses are considered different characters, and have two different Unicode numerical identifiers ("code points"), though they may be rendered identically. Conversely, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_script" title="Chinese script">Chinese</a> <a href="/wiki/Logogram" title="Logogram">logogram</a> for water ("水") may have a slightly different appearance in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_script" title="Japanese script">Japanese</a> texts than it does in Chinese texts, and local <a href="/wiki/Typeface" title="Typeface">typefaces</a> may reflect this. But nonetheless in Unicode they are considered the same character, and share the same code point.</p>
<p>The Unicode standard also differentiates between these abstract characters and <i>coded characters</i> or <i>encoded characters</i> that have been paired with numeric codes that facilitate their representation in computers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combining_character">Combining character</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Character_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Combining character">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Combining_character" title="Combining character">combining character</a> is also addressed by Unicode. For instance, Unicode allocates a code point to each of i, ["](combining trema) and ï (U+00ef). This makes it possible to code the middle character of the word naïve both as a single code point 'ï' or as a combination of the character i with diacritic (") (U+0069 LATIN SMALL LETTER I + U+0308 COMBINING DIAERESIS).</p>
<p>Both are considered canonically equivalent by the Unicode standard.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="char">char</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Character_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: char">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/C_data_types" title="C data types">C data types</a></div>
<p>A <code>char</code> in the <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C programming language</a> is a data type with the size of exactly one <a href="/wiki/Byte" title="Byte">byte</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> which in turn is defined to be large enough to contain any member of the “basic execution character set” and (in newer C standards) <a href="/wiki/UTF-8" title="UTF-8">UTF-8</a> code units<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which implies a minimum size of 8 bits. The exact number of bits can be checked via <code>CHAR_BIT</code> macro. By far the most common size is 8 bits, and the POSIX standard <i>requires</i> it to be 8 bits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> requires at least 21 bits to store a single code point, it is usually impossible to store one inside a single <code>char</code>; instead a variable-length encoding such as <a href="/wiki/UTF-8" title="UTF-8">UTF-8</a> must be used. Unfortunately, the fact that a character was historically stored in a single byte led to the two terms being used interchangeably in most documentation. This often makes the documentation confusing or misleading when multibyte encodings such as UTF-8 are used, and has led to inefficient and incorrect implementations of string manipulation functions. Modern POSIX documentation attempts to fix this, defining “character” as a sequence of one or more bytes representing a single graphic symbol or control code, and attempts to use “byte” when referring to char data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However it defines <i>Character Array</i> as an array of elements of type char.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unicode can also be stored in strings made up of code units that are larger than <code>char</code>. These are called “<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wide_characters" title="Wide characters">wide characters</a>”. The original C type was called <code><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wchar_t" title="Wchar t">wchar_t</a></code>. Due to some platforms defining <code>wchar_t</code> as 16 bits and others defining it as 32 bits, recent versions have added <code>char16_t</code>, <code>char32_t</code>. Even then the objects being stored might not be characters, for instance the variable-length <a href="/wiki/UTF-16" title="UTF-16">UTF-16</a> is often stored in arrays of <code>char16_t</code>.</p>
<p>Other languages also have a <code>char</code> type. Some such as <a href="/wiki/C%2B%2B" title="C++">C++</a> use 8 bits like C. Others such as <a href="/wiki/Java_(programming_language)" title="Java (programming language)">Java</a> use 16 bits for <code>char</code>, in order to represent UTF-16 values.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Word_character">Word character</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Character_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Word character">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A "word" character has special meaning in some aspects of computing. A "word character" within ASCII typically means a letter of the alphabet A-Z (upper or lower case), the digits 0 to 9, and the underscore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>It might be dependent on localization and encoding in use. If $ or | are not a word character, 'é' (in French) or 'æ' or 'я' (in Russian) or 'ά' (in Greek) are, as used in words such as fédération, Αγορά, or Примечания.</p>
