<p>The <b>history of writing</b> traces the development of expressing language by letters or other marks<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and also the study and description of these developments.</p>
<p>In the history of how <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Writing_systems" title="Writing systems">systems of representation of language through graphic means</a> have evolved in different human civilizations, more complete writing systems were preceded by <i><a href="/wiki/Proto-writing" title="Proto-writing">proto-writing</a></i>, systems of ideographic or early mnemonic symbols. <i>True writing</i>, in which the content of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguistic_utterance" title="Linguistic utterance">linguistic utterance</a> is encoded so that another reader can reconstruct, with a fair degree of accuracy, the exact utterance written down<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[A 1]</a></sup> is a later development. It is distinguished from proto-writing which typically avoids encoding grammatical words and affixes, making it more difficult or impossible to reconstruct the exact meaning intended by the writer unless a great deal of context is already known in advance. One of the earliest forms of written expression is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuneiform" title="Cuneiform">cuneiform</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inventions_of_writing">Inventions of writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Inventions of writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_languages_by_first_written_accounts" title="List of languages by first written accounts">List of languages by first written accounts</a></div>

<p>Writing numbers for the purpose of record keeping began long before the writing of language. See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_writing_ancient_numbers" title="History of writing ancient numbers">History of writing ancient numbers</a> for how the writing of numbers began.</p>
<p>It is generally agreed that true writing of language (not only numbers) was invented independently in at least two places: <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> (specifically, ancient <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumer</a>) around 3200 BC and <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a> around 900 BC. Several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_scripts" title="Mesoamerican scripts">Mesoamerican scripts</a> are known, the oldest being from the <a href="/wiki/Olmec" title="Olmec">Olmec</a> or <a href="/wiki/Zapotec_civilization" title="Zapotec civilization">Zapotec</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.</p>
<p>Historians debate whether writing systems were developed completely independently in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egypt</a> around 3200 BC and in <a href="/wiki/History_of_China" title="History of China">China</a> around 1200 BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> or whether writing appeared in either or both places through a process of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cultural_diffusion" title="Cultural diffusion">cultural diffusion</a>. In other words, was the concept of representing language using writing, though not necessarily the specifics of how such a system worked, passed on by traders or merchants traveling between the two regions?</p>
<p>However, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a> are probably an independent invention, because there is no evidence of contact between China and the literate civilizations of the Near East,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> and because of the distinct differences between the Mesopotamian and Chinese approaches to <a href="/wiki/Logogram" title="Logogram">logography</a> and phonetic representation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">Egyptian script</a> is dissimilar from Mesopotamian cuneiform, but similarities in concepts and in earliest attestation suggest that the idea of writing may have come to Egypt from Mesopotamia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> In 1999, <i><a href="/wiki/Archaeology_(magazine)" title="Archaeology (magazine)">Archaeology Magazine</a></i> reported that the earliest Egyptian glyphs date back to 3400 BC, which "challenge the commonly held belief that early logographs, pictographic symbols representing a specific place, object, or quantity, first evolved into more complex phonetic symbols in Mesopotamia."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell1999_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell1999-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similar debate surrounds the <a href="/wiki/Indus_script" title="Indus script">Indus script</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_civilization" title="Indus Valley civilization">Indus Valley civilization</a> in Ancient India (2600 BC). In addition, the script is still undeciphered and there is debate over whether the script is true writing at all, or instead some kind of proto-writing or non-linguistic sign system.</p>
<p>An additional possibility is the undeciphered <a href="/wiki/Rongorongo" title="Rongorongo">Rongorongo</a> script of <a href="/wiki/Easter_Island" title="Easter Island">Easter Island</a>. It is debated whether this is true writing, and if it is, whether it is another case of cultural diffusion of writing. The oldest example is from 1851, 139 years after their first contact with Europeans. One explanation is that the script was inspired by <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>'s written annexation proclamation in 1770.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Various other known cases of cultural diffusion of writing exist, where the general concept of writing was transmitted from one culture to another but the specifics of the system were independently developed. Recent examples are the <a href="/wiki/Cherokee_syllabary" title="Cherokee syllabary">Cherokee syllabary</a>, invented by <a href="/wiki/Sequoyah" title="Sequoyah">Sequoyah</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Pahawh_Hmong" title="Pahawh Hmong">Pahawh Hmong</a> system for writing the <a href="/wiki/Hmong_language" title="Hmong language">Hmong language</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_systems">Writing systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Writing systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Writing_system" title="Writing system">Writing system</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Symbolic_communication" title="Symbolic communication">Symbolic communication systems</a> are distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Writing_system" title="Writing system">writing systems</a> in that one must usually understand something of the associated spoken language to comprehend the text. In contrast, symbolic systems such as <a href="/wiki/Information_sign" title="Information sign">information signs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">maps</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> often do not require prior knowledge of a spoken language. Every human community possesses language, a feature regarded by many as an innate and defining condition of mankind (see <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_language" title="Origin of language">Origin of language</a>). However the development of writing systems, and their partial supplantation of traditional <a href="/wiki/Orality" title="Orality">oral</a> systems of communication, have been sporadic, uneven and slow. Once established, writing systems on the whole change more slowly than their spoken counterparts, and often preserve features and expressions which are no longer current in the spoken language. The greatest benefit of writing is that it provides the tool by which society can record information consistently and in greater detail, something that could not be achieved as well previously by spoken word. Writing allows societies to transmit information and share knowledge.</p>

<p>Scholars make a reasonable distinction between <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistory</a> and <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a> of early writing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shotwell1922_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shotwell1922-10">[9]</a></sup> but have disagreed concerning when prehistory becomes history and when proto-writing became "true writing". The definition is largely subjective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> Writing, in its most general terms, is a method of recording information and is composed of <a href="/wiki/Grapheme" title="Grapheme">graphemes</a>, which may in turn be composed of <a href="/wiki/Glyph" title="Glyph">glyphs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The emergence of writing in a given area is usually followed by several centuries of fragmentary inscriptions. Historians mark the "historicity" of a culture by the presence of coherent texts in the culture's writing system(s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shotwell1922_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shotwell1922-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The invention of writing was not a one-time event, but a gradual process initiated by the appearance of <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbols</a>, possibly first for cultic purposes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Developmental_stages">Developmental stages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Developmental stages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div>
</td>
<td class="mbox-text"><span class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>. <span class="hide-when-compact">Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>A conventional "proto-writing to true writing" system follows a general series of developmental stages:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Picture writing system</i>: glyphs (simplified pictures) directly represent objects and concepts. In connection with this the following substages may be distinguished:
<ul>
<li>Mnemonic: glyphs primarily a reminder;</li>
<li>Pictographic: glyphs directly represent an object or a concept such as (A) chronological, (B) notices, (C) communications, (D) totems, titles, and names, (E) religious, (F) customs, (G) historical, and (H) biographical;</li>
<li>Ideographic: graphemes are abstract symbols which directly represent an idea or concept.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><i>Transitional system</i>: graphemes refer not only to the object or idea which it represents but to its name as well.</li>
<li><i>Phonetic system</i>: graphemes refer to sounds or spoken symbols, and the form of the grapheme is not related to its meanings. This resolves itself into the following substages:
<ul>
<li>Verbal: grapheme (<a href="/wiki/Logogram" title="Logogram">logogram</a>) represents a whole word;</li>
<li>Syllabic: grapheme represents a syllable;</li>
<li>Alphabetic: grapheme represents an elementary sound.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>The best known picture writing system of <a href="/wiki/Ideogram" title="Ideogram">ideographic</a> or early <a href="/wiki/Mnemonic" title="Mnemonic">mnemonic</a> symbols are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jiahu_symbols" title="Jiahu symbols">Jiahu symbols</a>, carved on <a href="/wiki/Tortoise" title="Tortoise">tortoise</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_shell" title="Animal shell">shells</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jiahu" title="Jiahu">Jiahu</a>, c. 6600 BC</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vin%C4%8Da_signs" title="Vinča signs">Vinča signs</a> (<a href="/wiki/T%C4%83rt%C4%83ria_tablets" title="Tărtăria tablets">Tărtăria tablets</a>), c. 5300 BC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haarmann:_T.C4.83rt.C4.83ria_tablets_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haarmann:_T.C4.83rt.C4.83ria_tablets-13">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>Early <a href="/wiki/Indus_script" title="Indus script">Indus script</a>, c. 3200 BC</li>
</ul>
<p>In the Old World, true writing systems developed from <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">neolithic</a> writing in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Bronze_Age" title="Early Bronze Age">Early Bronze Age</a> (<a href="/wiki/4th_millennium_BC" title="4th millennium BC">4th millennium BC</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_language" title="Sumerian language">Sumerian</a> archaic (pre-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuneiform_(script)" title="Cuneiform (script)">cuneiform</a>) writing and the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">Egyptian hieroglyphs</a> are generally considered the earliest true writing systems, both emerging out of their ancestral proto-literate symbol systems from 3400–3200 BC with earliest coherent texts from about <a href="/wiki/26th_century_BC" title="26th century BC">2600 BC</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature_and_writing">Literature and writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Literature and writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Literature and writing, though obviously connected, are not synonymous. The very first writings from ancient <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumer</a> by any reasonable definition do not constitute literature. The same is true of some of the early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphics" title="Egyptian hieroglyphics">Egyptian hieroglyphics</a> and the thousands of ancient Chinese government records. The <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_literature" title="History of literature">history of literature</a></i> begins with the history of writing. Scholars have disagreed concerning when written record-keeping became more like <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a> than anything else, but "literature" can have several meanings. The term could be applied broadly to mean any symbolic record, from images and sculptures to letters. The oldest surviving literary texts date from a full millennium after the invention of writing, to the late 3rd millennium BC. The earliest literary authors known by name are <a href="/wiki/Ptahhotep" title="Ptahhotep">Ptahhotep</a> (who wrote in <a href="/wiki/Old_Egyptian_language" title="Old Egyptian language">Egyptian</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Enheduanna" title="Enheduanna">Enheduanna</a> (who wrote in <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_language" title="Sumerian language">Sumerian</a>), dating to around the <a href="/wiki/24th_century_BC" title="24th century BC">24th</a> and <a href="/wiki/23rd_century_BC" title="23rd century BC">23rd</a> centuries BC, respectively. In the early literate societies, as much as 600 years passed from the first inscriptions to the first coherent textual sources: i.e. from around 3200 to 2600 BC.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Locations_and_timeframes">Locations and timeframes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Locations and timeframes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proto-writing">Proto-writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Proto-writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Proto-writing" title="Proto-writing">Proto-writing</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_signs_in_China" title="Neolithic signs in China">Neolithic signs in China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_numerals" title="Prehistoric numerals">prehistoric numerals</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_communication" title="History of communication">History of communication</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jiahu_writing.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="326" data-file-width="125" height="574" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Jiahu_writing.svg/220px-Jiahu_writing.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Jiahu_writing.svg/330px-Jiahu_writing.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Jiahu_writing.svg/440px-Jiahu_writing.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jiahu_writing.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Example of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jiahu_symbol" title="Jiahu symbol">Jiahu symbols</a>, writing-like markings found on <a href="/wiki/Tortoise" title="Tortoise">tortoise</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_shell" title="Animal shell">shells</a>, dated around 6000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Writing_systems" title="Writing systems">writing systems</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Bronze_Age" title="Early Bronze Age">Early Bronze Age</a> were not a sudden invention. Rather, they were a development based on earlier traditions of <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbol</a> systems that cannot be classified as proper writing, but have many of the characteristics of writing. These systems may be described as "proto-writing". They used <a href="/wiki/Ideogram" title="Ideogram">ideographic</a> or early <a href="/wiki/Mnemonic" title="Mnemonic">mnemonic</a> symbols to convey information, but probably directly contained no <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">natural language</a>. These systems emerged in the early <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> period, as early as the <a href="/wiki/7th_millennium_BC" title="7th millennium BC">7th millennium BC</a> evidenced by the <a href="/wiki/Jiahu_symbols" title="Jiahu symbols">Jiahu symbols</a> in China.</p>
<p>In 2003, <a href="/wiki/Tortoise" title="Tortoise">tortoise</a> shells were found in 24 Neolithic graves excavated at <a href="/wiki/Jiahu" title="Jiahu">Jiahu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henan" title="Henan">Henan</a> province, northern China, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_date" title="Radiocarbon date">radiocarbon dates</a> from the 7th millennium BC. According to some archaeologists, the <a href="/wiki/Jiahu_symbols" title="Jiahu symbols">symbols</a> carved on the shells had similarities to the late 2nd millennium BCE <a href="/wiki/Oracle_bone_script" title="Oracle bone script">oracle bone script</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-China_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-China-15">[14]</a></sup> Others have dismissed this claim as insufficiently substantiated, claiming that simple geometric designs such as those found on the Jiahu shells cannot be linked to early writing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vin%C4%8Da_signs" title="Vinča signs">Vinča signs</a> show an evolution of simple symbols beginning in the 7th millennium BC, gradually increasing in complexity throughout the 6th millennium and culminating in the <a href="/wiki/T%C4%83rt%C4%83ria_tablets" title="Tărtăria tablets">Tărtăria tablets</a> of c. 5300 BC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haarmann:_T.C4.83rt.C4.83ria_tablets_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haarmann:_T.C4.83rt.C4.83ria_tablets-13">[12]</a></sup> with their rows of symbols carefully aligned, evoking the impression of a text.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Dispilio_Tablet" title="Dispilio Tablet">Dispilio Tablet</a> of the late 6th millennium is similar. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hieroglyphic" title="Hieroglyphic">hieroglyphic</a> scripts of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">Ancient Near East</a> (Egyptian, Sumerian proto-Cuneiform and Cretan) seamlessly emerge from such symbol systems, so that it is difficult to say at what exact time writing developed from proto-writing. Further, very little is known about the symbols' meanings.</p>
<p>Even after the Neolithic, several cultures went through an intermediate stage of proto-writing before they used proper writing. The "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Cyrillic_Slavic_writing" title="Pre-Cyrillic Slavic writing">Slavic runes</a>" from the 7th and 8th centuries AD, mentioned by a few medieval authors, may have been such a system. The <a href="/wiki/Quipu" title="Quipu">quipu</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incas" title="Incas">Incas</a> (15th century AD), sometimes called "talking knots", may have been of a similar nature. Another example is the pictographs invented by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uyaquk" title="Uyaquk">Uyaquk</a> before the development of the <a href="/wiki/Yugtun_script" title="Yugtun script">Yugtun syllabary</a> (c. 1900).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bronze_Age_writing">Bronze Age writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bronze Age writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_alphabet" title="History of the alphabet">History of the alphabet</a></div>
<p>Writing emerged in many different cultures in the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>. Examples are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuneiform" title="Cuneiform">cuneiform</a> writing of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumerians" title="Sumerians">Sumerians</a>, Egyptian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hieroglyphs" title="Hieroglyphs">hieroglyphs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cretan_hieroglyphs" title="Cretan hieroglyphs">Cretan hieroglyphs</a>, Chinese logographs, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olmec_script" title="Olmec script">Olmec script</a> of Mesoamerica. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_script" title="Chinese script">Chinese script</a> likely developed independently of the Middle Eastern scripts, around 1600 BC. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" title="Pre-Columbian">pre-Columbian</a> <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_writing_systems" title="Mesoamerican writing systems">Mesoamerican writing systems</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Olmec" title="Olmec">Olmec</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maya_script" title="Maya script">Maya scripts</a>) are also generally believed to have had independent origins. It is thought that the first true alphabetic writing was developed around 2000 BC for <a href="/wiki/Semitic_people" title="Semitic people">Semitic</a> workers in the Sinai by giving mostly Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Hieratic" title="Hieratic">hieratic</a> glyphs Semitic values (see <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_alphabet" title="History of the alphabet">History of the alphabet</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Sinaitic_alphabet" title="Proto-Sinaitic alphabet">Proto-Sinaitic alphabet</a>). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ge%27ez" title="Ge'ez">Ge'ez</a> writing system of Ethiopia is considered Semitic. It is likely to be of semi-independent origin, having roots in the Meroitic Sudanese ideogram system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> Most other alphabets in the world today either descended from this one innovation, many via the <a href="/wiki/Phoenician_alphabet" title="Phoenician alphabet">Phoenician alphabet</a>, or were directly inspired by its design. In Italy, about 500 years passed from the early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Italic_alphabet" title="Old Italic alphabet">Old Italic alphabet</a> to <a href="/wiki/Plautus" title="Plautus">Plautus</a> (750 to 250 BC), and in the case of the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic peoples</a>, the corresponding time span is again similar, from the first <a href="/wiki/Elder_Futhark" title="Elder Futhark">Elder Futhark</a> inscriptions to early texts like the <i><a href="/wiki/Abrogans" title="Abrogans">Abrogans</a></i> (c. AD 200 to 750).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuneiform_script">Cuneiform script</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cuneiform script">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CunEnv.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="300" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/CunEnv.jpg/220px-CunEnv.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/63/CunEnv.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CunEnv.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Middle Babylonian legal tablet from Alalah in its envelope</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cuneiform_script" title="Cuneiform script">Cuneiform script</a></div>
<p>The original Sumerian writing system derives from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_writing_ancient_numbers#Clay_tokens" title="History of writing ancient numbers">system of clay tokens</a> used to represent commodities. By the end of the <a href="/wiki/4th_millennium_BC" title="4th millennium BC">4th millennium BC</a>, this had evolved into a method of keeping accounts, using a round-shaped stylus impressed into soft clay at different angles for recording numbers. This was gradually augmented with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pictographic" title="Pictographic">pictographic</a> writing using a sharp stylus to indicate what was being counted. Round-stylus and sharp-stylus writing were gradually replaced around 2700-2500 BC by writing using a wedge-shaped stylus (hence the term <a href="/wiki/Cuneiform_script" title="Cuneiform script">cuneiform</a>), at first only for <a href="/wiki/Logogram" title="Logogram">logograms</a>, but developed to include phonetic elements by the 29th century BC. About 2600 BC cuneiform began to represent syllables of the <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_language" title="Sumerian language">Sumerian language</a>. Finally, cuneiform writing became a general purpose writing system for logograms, syllables, and numbers. From the 26th century BC, this script was adapted to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akkadian_language" title="Akkadian language">Akkadian language</a>, and from there to others such as <a href="/wiki/Hurrian_language" title="Hurrian language">Hurrian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hittite_language" title="Hittite language">Hittite</a>. Scripts similar in appearance to this writing system include those for <a href="/wiki/Ugaritic_alphabet" title="Ugaritic alphabet">Ugaritic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Persian_language" title="Old Persian language">Old Persian</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Egyptian_hieroglyphs">Egyptian hieroglyphs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Egyptian hieroglyphs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">Egyptian hieroglyphs</a></div>
<p>Writing was very important in maintaining the Egyptian empire, and literacy was concentrated among an educated elite of scribes. Only people from certain backgrounds were allowed to train as scribes, in the service of temple, royal (pharaonic), and military authorities.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Sampson" title="Geoffrey Sampson">Geoffrey Sampson</a> believes that most scholars hold that Egyptian hieroglyphs "came into existence a little after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumerian_script" title="Sumerian script">Sumerian script</a>, and ... probably [were] invented under the influence of the latter ..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> This view, however, is strongly contested by other scholars. Dreyer's findings at Tomb UJ at <a href="/wiki/Abydos,_Egypt" title="Abydos, Egypt">Abydos</a> in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Egypt" title="Upper Egypt">Upper Egypt</a> clearly show place names written in hieroglyphs (up to four in number) recognizable as signs which persisted and were employed during later periods and which are written and read phonetically. The tomb is dated to c.3250 BC and demonstrates that such writing (on bone and ivory labels) is a more advanced form of writing than was evident in Sumer at that date. It is argued, therefore, that the Egyptian writing system, which is in any case very different from the Mesopotamian, could not have been the result of influence from a less developed system existing at that date in Sumer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Elamite_script">Elamite script</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Elamite script">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Elamite_script" title="Proto-Elamite script">Proto-Elamite script</a></div>
<p>The undeciphered Proto-Elamite script emerges from as early as 3200 BC. It is believed to have evolved into <a href="/wiki/Linear_Elamite" title="Linear Elamite">Linear Elamite</a> by the later 3rd millennium, and then replaced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elamite_Cuneiform" title="Elamite Cuneiform">Elamite Cuneiform</a> adopted from Akkadian.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Indus_script">Indus script</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Indus script">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Indus_script" title="Indus script">Indus script</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Bronze_Age" title="Middle Bronze Age">Middle Bronze Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Indus_script" title="Indus script">Indus script</a> which dates back to the early <a href="/wiki/Harappa" title="Harappa">Harappan</a> phase of around 3000 BC in ancient north western <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and what is now <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, has not yet been deciphered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> It is unclear whether it should be considered an example of proto-writing, or if it is actual writing of the logographic-syllabic type of the other Bronze Age writing systems. <a href="/wiki/Mortimer_Wheeler" title="Mortimer Wheeler">Mortimer Wheeler</a> recognises the style of writing as <a href="/wiki/Boustrophedon" title="Boustrophedon">boustrophedon</a>, where "this stability suggests a precarious maturity".</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Semitic_alphabets">Early Semitic alphabets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Early Semitic alphabets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Bronze_Age_alphabets" title="Middle Bronze Age alphabets">Middle Bronze Age alphabets</a></div>
<p>The first pure <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabets</a> (properly, "<a href="/wiki/Abjad" title="Abjad">abjads</a>", mapping single symbols to single phonemes, but not necessarily each phoneme to a symbol) emerged around 1800 BC in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>, as a representation of language developed by <a href="/wiki/Semitic_people" title="Semitic people">Semitic</a> workers in Egypt, but by then alphabetic principles had a slight possibility of being inculcated into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyph" title="Egyptian hieroglyph">Egyptian hieroglyphs</a> for upwards of a millennium.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> These early abjads remained of marginal importance for several centuries, and it is only towards the end of the Bronze Age that the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Sinaitic_script" title="Proto-Sinaitic script">Proto-Sinaitic script</a> splits into the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Canaanite_alphabet" title="Proto-Canaanite alphabet">Proto-Canaanite alphabet</a> (c. 1400 BC) Byblos syllabary and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Arabian_alphabet" title="South Arabian alphabet">South Arabian alphabet</a> (c. 1200 BC). The Proto-Canaanite was probably somehow influenced by the undeciphered <a href="/wiki/Byblos_syllabary" title="Byblos syllabary">Byblos syllabary</a>, and in turn inspired the <a href="/wiki/Ugaritic_alphabet" title="Ugaritic alphabet">Ugaritic alphabet</a> (c. 1300 BC).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Anatolian_hieroglyphs">Anatolian hieroglyphs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Anatolian hieroglyphs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anatolian_hieroglyphs" title="Anatolian hieroglyphs">Anatolian hieroglyphs</a></div>
<p>Anatolian hieroglyphs are an indigenous hieroglyphic script native to western <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, used to record the <a href="/wiki/Hieroglyphic_Luwian" title="Hieroglyphic Luwian">Hieroglyphic Luwian</a> language. It first appeared on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luwian_hieroglyphs" title="Luwian hieroglyphs">Luwian</a> royal seals from the 14th century BC.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_writing">Chinese writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Chinese writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_writing" title="Chinese writing">Chinese writing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a></div>
<p>The earliest confirmed evidence of the Chinese script yet discovered is the body of inscriptions on oracle bones from the late Shang dynasty (c. 1200–1050 BC). From the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shang_Dynasty" title="Shang Dynasty">Shang Dynasty</a> most of this writing has survived on bones or bronze implements (<i><a href="/wiki/Chinese_bronze_inscriptions" title="Chinese bronze inscriptions">bronze script</a></i>). Markings on turtle shells, or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jiaguwen" title="Jiaguwen">jiaguwen</a></i>, have been carbon-dated to around 1500 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-William_G._Boltz_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-William_G._Boltz-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-William_G._Boltz_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-William_G._Boltz-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-David_N._Keightley_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-David_N._Keightley-22">[21]</a></sup> Historians have found that the type of medium chosen depended on the subject of the writing.</p>
<p>There have recently been discoveries of tortoise-shell carvings dating back to c. 6000 BC, like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jiahu_Script" title="Jiahu Script">Jiahu Script</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banpo_Script" title="Banpo Script">Banpo Script</a>, but whether or not the carvings are complex enough to qualify as writing is under debate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-China_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-China-15">[14]</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Damaidi" title="Damaidi">Damaidi</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ningxia_Hui_Autonomous_Region" title="Ningxia Hui Autonomous Region">Ningxia Hui Autonomous Region</a>, 3,172 cliff carvings dating to 6,000-5,000 BC have been discovered, featuring 8,453 individual characters such as the sun, moon, stars, gods and scenes of hunting or grazing. These pictographs are reputed to be similar to the earliest characters confirmed to be written Chinese. If it is deemed to be a written language, writing in China will predate Mesopotamian cuneiform, long acknowledged as the first appearance of writing, by some 2000 years; however it is more likely that the inscriptions are rather a form of <a href="/wiki/Proto-writing" title="Proto-writing">proto-writing</a>, similar to the contemporary European <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vinca_script" title="Vinca script">Vinca script</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cretan_and_Greek_scripts">Cretan and Greek scripts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cretan and Greek scripts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Cretan_hieroglyphs" title="Cretan hieroglyphs">Cretan hieroglyphs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linear_A" title="Linear A">Linear A</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Linear_B" title="Linear B">Linear B</a></div>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Mesoamerica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_writing_systems" title="Mesoamerican writing systems">Mesoamerican writing systems</a></div>
<p>A stone slab with 3,000-year-old writing, the <a href="/wiki/Cascajal_Block" title="Cascajal Block">Cascajal Block</a>, was discovered in the Mexican state of Veracruz, and is an example of the oldest script in the Western Hemisphere, preceding the oldest <a href="/wiki/Zapotec_civilization" title="Zapotec civilization">Zapotec</a> writing dated to about 500 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" title="Pre-Columbian">pre-Columbian</a> scripts in <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a>, the one that appears to have been best developed, and has been fully deciphered, is the <a href="/wiki/Maya_script" title="Maya script">Maya script</a>. The earliest inscriptions which are identifiably Maya date to the 3rd century BC, and writing was in continuous use until shortly after the arrival of the Spanish conquistadores in the 16th century AD. Maya writing used logograms complemented by a set of syllabic glyphs: a combination somewhat similar to modern Japanese writing.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Phoenician_alphabet" title="Phoenician alphabet">Phoenician alphabet</a> is simply the Proto-Canaanite alphabet as it was continued into the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> (conventionally taken from a cut-off date of 1050 BC). This alphabet gave rise to the <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_alphabet" title="Aramaic alphabet">Aramaic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek</a> alphabets. These in turn led to the writing systems used throughout regions ranging from Western Asia to Africa and Europe. For its part the Greek alphabet introduced for the first time explicit symbols for vowel sounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[26]</a></sup> The Greek and Latin alphabets in the early centuries of the Common Era gave rise to several European scripts such as the <a href="/wiki/Runes" title="Runes">Runes</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_alphabet" title="Gothic alphabet">Gothic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic</a> alphabets while the Aramaic alphabet evolved into the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_alphabet" title="Hebrew alphabet">Hebrew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syriac_alphabet" title="Syriac alphabet">Syriac</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabic_alphabet" title="Arabic alphabet">Arabic</a> abjads and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Arabian_alphabet" title="South Arabian alphabet">South Arabian alphabet</a> gave rise to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ge%27ez_abugida" title="Ge'ez abugida">Ge'ez abugida</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmic_family" title="Brahmic family">Brahmic family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">India</a> is believed by some scholars to have derived from the Aramaic alphabet as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salomon_1996_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salomon_1996-29">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_in_the_Greco-Roman_civilizations">Writing in the Greco-Roman civilizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Writing in the Greco-Roman civilizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NAMA_Alphabet_grec.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1398" data-file-width="1600" height="192" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/NAMA_Alphabet_grec.jpg/220px-NAMA_Alphabet_grec.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/NAMA_Alphabet_grec.jpg/330px-NAMA_Alphabet_grec.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/NAMA_Alphabet_grec.jpg/440px-NAMA_Alphabet_grec.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Greek_alphabet" title="History of the Greek alphabet">history of the Greek alphabet</a> started when the Greeks borrowed the Phoenician alphabet and adapted it to their own language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarter.2C_P._Kyle_1974_page_62_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCarter.2C_P._Kyle_1974_page_62-30">[28]</a></sup> The letters of the Greek alphabet are more or less the same as those of the Phoenician alphabet, and in modern times both alphabets are arranged in the same order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarter.2C_P._Kyle_1974_page_62_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-McCarter.2C_P._Kyle_1974_page_62-30">[28]</a></sup> The adapter(s) of the Phoenician system added three letters to the end of the series, called the "supplementals". Several varieties of the Greek alphabet developed. One, known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumae_alphabet" title="Cumae alphabet">Western Greek or Chalcidian</a>, was used west of <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Italy" title="Southern Italy">southern Italy</a>. The other variation, known as <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Greek_alphabet" title="History of the Greek alphabet">Eastern Greek</a>, was used in present-day <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and by the Athenians, and eventually the rest of the world that spoke Greek adopted this variation. After first writing right to left, like the Phoenicians, the Greeks eventually chose to write from left to right.</p>
<p>Greek is in turn the source for all the modern scripts of Europe. The most widespread descendent of Greek is the <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a>, named for the <a href="/wiki/Latins" title="Latins">Latins</a>, a central Italian people who came to dominate Europe with the rise of Rome. The Romans learned writing in about the 5th century BC from the <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_civilization" title="Etruscan civilization">Etruscan civilization</a>, who used one of a number of Italic scripts derived from the western Greeks. Due to the cultural dominance of the Roman state, the other Italic scripts have not survived in any great quantity, and the Etruscan language is mostly lost.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_during_the_Middle_Ages">Writing during the Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Writing during the Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> authority in Western Europe, the literary development became largely confined to the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Empire" title="Persian Empire">Persian Empire</a>. Latin, never one of the primary literary languages, rapidly declined in importance (except within the Church of Rome). The primary literary languages were <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>, though other languages such as <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coptic_language" title="Coptic language">Coptic</a> were important too.</p>
<p>The rise of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> in the 7th century led to the rapid rise of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> as a major literary language in the region. Arabic and Persian quickly began to overshadow Greek's role as a language of scholarship. <a href="/wiki/Arabic_script" title="Arabic script">Arabic script</a> was adopted as the primary script of the Persian language and the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish language</a>. This script also heavily influenced the development of the <a href="/wiki/Cursive" title="Cursive">cursive</a> scripts of Greek, the <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic languages</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>, and other languages<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="reliable sources for this? (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The Arabic language also served to spread the <a href="/wiki/Hindu%E2%80%93Arabic_numeral_system" title="Hindu–Arabic numeral system">Hindu–Arabic numeral system</a> throughout Europe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> By the beginning of the second millennium the city of <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba,_Andalusia" title="Córdoba, Andalusia">Cordoba</a> in modern Spain, had become one of the foremost intellectual centers of the world and contained the world's largest library at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[29]</a></sup> Its position as a crossroads between the Islamic and Western Christian worlds helped fuel intellectual development and written communication between both cultures.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance_and_the_modern_era">Renaissance and the modern era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Renaissance and the modern era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By the 14th century a rebirth, or <i><a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">renaissance</a></i>, had emerged in Western Europe, leading to a temporary revival of the importance of Greek, and a slow revival of Latin as a significant literary language. A similar though smaller emergence occurred in Eastern Europe, especially in Russia. At the same time Arabic and Persian began a slow decline in importance as the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age" title="Islamic Golden Age">Islamic Golden Age</a> ended. The revival of literary development in Western Europe led to many innovations in the Latin alphabet and the diversification of the alphabet to codify the phonologies of the various languages.</p>
<p>The nature of writing has been constantly evolving, particularly due to the development of new technologies over the centuries. The <a href="/wiki/Pen" title="Pen">pen</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phone</a> are all technological developments which have altered what is written, and the medium through which the written word is produced. Particularly with the advent of digital technologies, namely the computer and the mobile phone, characters can be formed by the press of a button, rather than making a physical motion with the hand.</p>
<p>The nature of the written word has recently evolved to include an informal, colloquial written style, in which an everyday conversation can occur through writing rather than speaking. Written communication can also be delivered with minimal time delay (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-mail" title="E-mail">e-mail</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SMS" title="SMS">SMS</a>), and in some cases, with an imperceptible time delay (<a href="/wiki/Instant_messaging" title="Instant messaging">instant messaging</a>). Writing is a preservable means of communication. Some people regard the growth of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multimedia_literacy" title="Multimedia literacy">multimedia literacy</a> as the first step toward a <a href="/wiki/Postliterate_society" title="Postliterate society">postliterate society</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_materials">Writing materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Writing materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Writing_material" title="Writing material">Writing material</a></div>
<p>There is no very definite statement as to the material which was in most common use for the purposes of writing at the start of the early writing systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McClintock1885_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-McClintock1885-32">[30]</a></sup> In all ages it has been customary to engrave on stone or metal, or other durable material, with the view of securing the permanency of the record; and accordingly, in the very commencement of the national history of Israel, it is read of the two tables of the law written in stone, and of a subsequent writing of the law on stone. In the latter case there is this peculiarity, that plaster (<a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a>, lime or gypsum) was used along with stone, a combination of materials which is illustrated by comparison of the practice of the Egyptian engravers, who, having first carefully smoothed the stone, filled up the faulty places with gypsum or cement, in order to obtain a perfectly uniform surface on which to execute their engravings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McClintock1885_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-McClintock1885-32">[30]</a></sup> Metals, such as stamped <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">coins</a>, are mentioned as a material of writing; they include lead,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup> brass, and gold. To the engraving of gems there is reference also, such as with seals or signets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McClintock1885_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-McClintock1885-32">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The common materials of writing were the tablet and the roll, the former probably having a Chaldean origin, the latter an Egyptian. The tablets of the Chaldeans are among the most remarkable of their remains.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2017)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> There are small pieces of clay, somewhat rudely shaped into a form resembling a pillow, and thickly inscribed with cuneiform characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup> Similar use has been seen in hollow cylinders, or prisms of six or eight sides, formed of fine terra cotta, sometimes glazed, on which the characters were traced with a small stylus, in some specimens so minutely as to be capable of decipherment only with the aid of a magnifying-glass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McClintock1885_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-McClintock1885-32">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Egypt the principal writing material was of quite a different sort. Wooden tablets are found pictured on the monuments; but the material which was in common use, even from very ancient times, was the <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a>. This reed, found chiefly in Lower Egypt, had various economic means for writing, the pith was taken out, and divided by a pointed instrument into the thin pieces of which it is composed; it was then flattened by pressure, and the strips glued together, other strips being placed at right angles to them, so that a roll of any length might be manufactured. Writing seems to have become more widespread with the invention of papyrus in Egypt. That this material was in use in Egypt from a very early period is evidenced by still existing papyrus of the earliest Theban dynasties. As the papyrus, being in great demand, and exported to all parts of the world, became very costly, other materials were often used instead of it, among which is mentioned leather, a few leather mills of an early period having been found in the tombs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McClintock1885_32-4"><a href="#cite_note-McClintock1885-32">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">Parchment</a>, using sheepskins left after the wool was removed for cloth, was sometimes cheaper than papyrus, which had to be imported outside Egypt. With the invention of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood-pulp_paper" title="Wood-pulp paper">wood-pulp paper</a>, the cost of writing material began a steady decline.</p>
