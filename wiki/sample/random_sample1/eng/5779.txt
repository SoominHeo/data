<p><i><b>Moules-frites</b></i> or <i><b>Moules et frites</b></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001162_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001162-1">[1]</a></sup> (<small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[mul.fʁit]</a></span>]; <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <span lang="nl" xml:lang="nl"><i>mosselen-friet</i></span>) is a popular main dish of <a href="/wiki/Mussel" title="Mussel">mussels</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_fries" title="French fries">fries</a> originating in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>. It is also popular in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and in the rest of Northern Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaveur2011_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaveur2011-2">[2]</a></sup> The title of the dish is <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <i>Moules</i> meaning mussels and <i>frites</i> fries, with the Dutch name for the dish meaning the same. It is sometimes considered the <a href="/wiki/National_dish" title="National dish">national dish</a> of Belgium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001169_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001169-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The portion of <i>moules</i> in Belgian restaurants tends to be one and a half kilograms per person,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> which can be prepared in several different ways.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moules-frites&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although <i>moules-frites</i> are popular in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and around the world, it is thought that the dish originated in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ifood1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ifood1-4">[4]</a></sup> It is likely that it was originally created by combining mussels, a popular and cheap foodstuff eaten around the <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flemish</a> coast, and fried potatoes which were commonly eaten around the country in winter when no fish or other food was available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ifood1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ifood1-4">[4]</a></sup> On their own, <a href="/wiki/French_fries" title="French fries">french fries</a> are also closely associated with <a href="/wiki/Belgian_cuisine" title="Belgian cuisine">Belgian cuisine</a> by many Europeans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001166_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001166-5">[5]</a></sup> Although Belgium claims to be the birthplace of french fries, their origin is uncertain and several other countries also claim the title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001166_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001166-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants_and_preparation">Variants and preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moules-frites&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variants and preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moules"><i>Moules</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moules-frites&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Moules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AuPreSale_Moules.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/AuPreSale_Moules.JPG/220px-AuPreSale_Moules.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/AuPreSale_Moules.JPG/330px-AuPreSale_Moules.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/AuPreSale_Moules.JPG/440px-AuPreSale_Moules.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AuPreSale_Moules.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Moules marinière</i> in Belgium</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The ways in which the mussels are cooked in the dish can vary significantly. Some variants commonly seen are:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Moules marinière</i>: Probably the most common and internationally recognisable recipe,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVisit_Belgium_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVisit_Belgium-6">[6]</a></sup> <i>Moules marinière</i> includes <a href="/wiki/White_wine" title="White wine">white wine</a>, shallots, parsley and butter to cook the mussels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBBC_Food_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBBC_Food-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Moules natures</i>: The mussels are steamed with celery, leeks and butter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaveur2011_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaveur2011-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Moules à la crème</i>: Another common recipe, where the white wine stock is thickened with flour and cream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaveur2011_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaveur2011-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Moules parquées</i>: A dish, probably originating in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, of raw mussels on half a shell, served with a lemon-mustard sauce.</li>
<li><i>Moules à la bière</i>: In this recipe, the mussels are cooked in a sauce containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgian_beer" title="Belgian beer">beer</a>, instead of white wine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Moules à l'ail</i>: The mussels are cooked with sliced or minced garlic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaveur2011_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaveur2011-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Less commonly, <a href="/wiki/Fusion_cuisine" title="Fusion cuisine">fusion variants</a> are seen in which the stock may be flavoured with non-local ingredients such as <a href="/wiki/Espelette_pepper" title="Espelette pepper">Espelette pepper</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pernod_Ricard" title="Pernod Ricard">Pernod liqure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaveur2011_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaveur2011-2">[2]</a></sup> They can also be served with "<i>Mosselsaus</i>", a sauce that is made with mayonnaise, mustard and vinegar. In Belgium, the moules used are generally farmed in nearby <a href="/wiki/Zeeland" title="Zeeland">Zeeland</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001168_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001168-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frites"><i>Frites</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moules-frites&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Frites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In various forms, <i>frites</i> or <i>friet</i> play an important role in Belgian culture and cuisine. Within Belgium, <a href="/wiki/Bintje" title="Bintje">bintje</a> potatoes are generally preferred as a basis to make fries because of their high <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001166_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001166-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaveur2011_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaveur2011-2">[2]</a></sup> They are generally double-fried (fried, left to cool and then fried again) in order to make them both moist in the core and crispy on the outside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001166_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001166-5">[5]</a></sup> As a part of this, they are often fried at a particularly high temperature (around 190 °C (374 °F) ).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001167_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001167-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Presentation_and_nutrition">Presentation and nutrition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moules-frites&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Presentation and nutrition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a dish, the <i>moules</i> and the <i>frites</i> are usually served on separate plates or dishes so that the fries do not become moist. Often, the moules are served in the same pan and stock used to cook them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001159_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpoiden2001159-11">[11]</a></sup> A second pan or dish is generally also provided in which the mussels' shells can be left once the mussels themselves have been eaten.</p>
<p>According to a French nutritionist, the optimum quantity of mussels is one litre, considering their nutritional value and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omega_3" title="Omega 3">Omega 3</a> content, but not more than 150 grams (5.3 oz) of fries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELa_Voix_du_Nord2010_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELa_Voix_du_Nord2010-12">[12]</a></sup> This quantity contains between 500-600 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calories" title="Calories">calories</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELa_Voix_du_Nord2010_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELa_Voix_du_Nord2010-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popularity_and_in_popular_culture">Popularity and in popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moules-frites&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Popularity and in popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In both Belgium and France, <i>moules-frites</i> are available in most restaurants. According to a survey conducted by <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Nelson_Sofres" title="Taylor Nelson Sofres">TNS</a>, <i>moules-frites</i> was identified as the second favourite dish in France, receiving a vote of 20 percent, narrowly losing to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duck_(food)" title="Duck (food)">magret de canard</a> which received 21 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoctissimo2011_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoctissimo2011-13">[13]</a></sup> On average, between 25 and 30 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> of moules are consumed each year in Belgium as <i>moules-frites</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERTL2011_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERTL2011-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Belgian musician, <a href="/wiki/Stromae" title="Stromae">Stromae</a>, performed a song entitled "Moules frites" in his 2013 album, <i><a href="/wiki/Racine_Carr%C3%A9e" title="Racine Carrée">Racine Carrée</a></i>.</p>
