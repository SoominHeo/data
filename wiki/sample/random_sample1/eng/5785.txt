<p><b>Mullah</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ʌ/ short 'u' in 'bud'">ʌ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>, <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ʊ/ short 'oo' in 'foot'">ʊ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>, <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uː</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">ملا</span>‎‎, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kurdish_language" title="Kurdish language">Kurdish</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ku" xml:lang="ku">Melle</span>‎, <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">ملا</span>‎‎, <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><i>Molla</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">Albanian</a>: <span lang="sq" xml:lang="sq"><i>Mullah</i></span>) is derived from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quranic" title="Quranic">Quranic</a> term <a href="/wiki/Mawla" title="Mawla">Mawla</a>. However, used ambiguously in the Quran, some publishers have described its usage as a religious title as inappropriate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The term is sometimes applied to a <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> man or woman, educated in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_theology" title="Islamic theology">Islamic theology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">sacred law</a>. The title is derived from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> word <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">مَوْلَى</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (December 2015)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i>mawlā</i>, meaning "vicar," "master" and "guardian." In large parts of the <a href="/wiki/Muslim_world" title="Muslim world">Muslim world</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kurdistan" title="Kurdistan">Kurdistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahrain_(historical_region)" title="Bahrain (historical region)">Eastern Arabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Balkans#Religion" title="Balkans">the Balkans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Horn of Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, it is the name commonly given to local Islamic clerics or <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">mosque</a> leaders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roy_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roy-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The title has also been used in some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sephardic" title="Sephardic">Sephardic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> communities to refer to the community's leadership, especially religious leadership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <i>mullah</i> is primarily understood in the Muslim world as a term of respect for an educated religious man.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taheri_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taheri-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Training_and_duties">Training and duties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mullah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Training and duties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ideally, a trained mullah will have studied Islamic traditions (<a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">hadith</a>), and Islamic law (<a href="/wiki/Fiqh" title="Fiqh">fiqh</a>). Such figures often have memorized the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a>. Uneducated villagers may frequently classify a literate Muslim with a less than complete Islamic training as their "mullah" or religious cleric. Mullahs with varying levels of training lead prayers in mosques, deliver religious sermons, and perform religious ceremonies such as birth rites and funeral services. They also often teach in a type of Islamic school known as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madrasah" title="Madrasah">madrasah</a>. Three kinds of knowledge are applied most frequently in interpreting Islamic texts (i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadiths" title="Hadiths">Hadiths</a>, etc.) for matters of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shariah" title="Shariah">Shariah</a>, i.e., Islamic law.</p>
<p>Mullahs have frequently been involved in politics, but only recently have they served in positions of power, since <a href="/wiki/Islamism" title="Islamism">Islamists</a> <a href="/wiki/Iranian_Revolution" title="Iranian Revolution">seized power in Iran</a> in 1979. In Syria, political militant groups supported by the West have taken root. The <a href="/wiki/Taliban" title="Taliban">Taliban</a> enforced Islamism in Afghanistan.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mullah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term is most often applied to Shi'ite clerics, as <a href="/wiki/Shia_Islam" title="Shia Islam">Shi'a Islam</a> is the predominant tradition in Iran. However, the term is very common in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urdu_language" title="Urdu language">Urdu</a>, spoken throughout <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, and it is used throughout the Indian subcontinent for any Muslim clergy, <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunni</a> or Shi'a. Muslim clergy in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and other former <a href="/wiki/Republics_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Republics of the Soviet Union">Soviet Republics</a> are also referred to as mullahs, regardless of whether they are Sunni or Shi'a.</p>
<p>The term has also been used among <a href="/wiki/Persian_Jews" title="Persian Jews">Persian Jews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bukharan_Jews" title="Bukharan Jews">Bukharan Jews</a>, Afghan Jews, and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Asian" title="Central Asian">Central Asian</a> Jews to refer to the community's religious and/or secular leadership. In <a href="/wiki/Kaifeng" title="Kaifeng">Kaifeng</a>, China, the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_China" title="History of the Jews in China">historic Chinese Jews</a> who managed the synagogue were called "mullahs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Outside of Eastern Arabia, which has a long Shiite tradition and numerous Shiite minorities, the term is seldom used in other Arabic-speaking areas where its nearest equivalent is often <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shaykh" title="Shaykh">shaykh</a></i> (implying formal Islamic training), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imam_(Sunni_Islam)" title="Imam (Sunni Islam)">imam</a></i> (prayer leader; not to be confused with the imams of the Shiite world), or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulema" title="Ulema"><i>ʿālim</i></a> ("scholar", plural <i>ʿulamāʾ</i>). In the Sunni world, the concept of "cleric" is of limited usefulness, as authority in the religious system is relatively decentralized.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The term is frequently used in English, although English-speaking Muslim clergy rarely call themselves mullahs. It was adopted from Urdu by the British rulers of India and subsequently came into more widespread use.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>It is sometimes used in a derogatory and humorous form, to mock <a href="/wiki/Gnosticism" title="Gnosticism">gnostically</a> religious men.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iran">Iran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mullah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Iran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Until the early 20th century, the term <i>mullah</i> was used in Iranian <a href="/wiki/Hawza" title="Hawza">hawzas</a> (seminaries) to refer to low-level clergy who specialized in telling stories of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Day_of_Ashura" title="Day of Ashura">Ashura</a>, rather than teaching or issuing <a href="/wiki/Fatwa" title="Fatwa">fatwas</a>. Today, the term is sometimes used as a derogatory term for any Islamic cleric. In recent years, at least among Shia clerics, the term <i>ruhani</i> (spiritual) has been promoted as an alternative to mullah and <i><a href="/wiki/Akhoond" title="Akhoond">akhoond</a></i>, free of pejorative connotations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
