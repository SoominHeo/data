<p><b>Moulin Rouge</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Histoire_du_Moulin_Rouge_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Histoire_du_Moulin_Rouge-1">[1]</a></sup> (<small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[mu.lɛ̃ ʁuʒ]</a></span>, French for "Red Mill") is a <a href="/wiki/Cabaret" title="Cabaret">cabaret</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris,_France" title="Paris, France">Paris, France</a>.</p>
<p>The original house, which burned down in 1915, was co-founded in 1889 by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Zidler" title="Charles Zidler">Charles Zidler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Oller" title="Joseph Oller">Joseph Oller</a>, who also owned the <a href="/wiki/Olympia_(Paris)" title="Olympia (Paris)">Paris Olympia</a>. Close to <a href="/wiki/Montmartre" title="Montmartre">Montmartre</a> in the Paris district of <a href="/wiki/Quartier_Pigalle" title="Quartier Pigalle">Pigalle</a> on Boulevard de Clichy in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/18%C3%A8me_arrondissement,_Paris" title="18ème arrondissement, Paris">18th <i>arrondissement</i></a>, it is marked by the red <a href="/wiki/Windmill" title="Windmill">windmill</a> on its roof. The closest métro station is <a href="/wiki/Blanche_(Paris_M%C3%A9tro)" title="Blanche (Paris Métro)">Blanche</a>.</p>
<p>Moulin Rouge is best known as the spiritual birthplace of the modern form of the <a href="/wiki/Can-can" title="Can-can">can-can</a> dance. Originally introduced as a seductive dance by the <a href="/wiki/Courtesan" title="Courtesan">courtesans</a> who operated from the site, the can-can dance <a href="/wiki/Revue" title="Revue">revue</a> evolved into a form of entertainment of its own and led to the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Cabaret" title="Cabaret">cabarets</a> across Europe. Today, the Moulin Rouge is a <a href="/wiki/Tourist_attraction" title="Tourist attraction">tourist attraction</a>, offering musical dance entertainment for visitors from around the world. The club's decor still contains much of the romance of <i><a href="/wiki/Fin_de_si%C3%A8cle" title="Fin de siècle">fin de siècle</a></i> France.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moulin_Rouge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth">Birth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moulin_Rouge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Birth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moulin_Rouge_1900.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="314" data-file-width="500" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Moulin_Rouge_1900.jpg/220px-Moulin_Rouge_1900.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Moulin_Rouge_1900.jpg/330px-Moulin_Rouge_1900.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Moulin_Rouge_1900.jpg/440px-Moulin_Rouge_1900.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

The Moulin Rouge in 1900</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Belle_%C3%89poque" title="Belle Époque">Belle Époque</a> was a period of peace and optimism marked by industrial progress, and a particularly rich cultural exuberance was about at the opening of the Moulin Rouge. The Expositions Universelles of <a href="/wiki/Exposition_Universelle_(1889)" title="Exposition Universelle (1889)">1889</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exposition_Universelle_(1900)" title="Exposition Universelle (1900)">1900</a> are symbols of this period. The <a href="/wiki/Eiffel_Tower" title="Eiffel Tower">Eiffel Tower</a> was also constructed in 1889, epitomising the spirit of progress along with the culturally transgressive cabaret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Japonism" title="Japonism">Japonism</a>, an artistic movement inspired by the Orient, with <a href="/wiki/Henri_de_Toulouse-Lautrec" title="Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec">Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec</a> as its most brilliant disciple, was also at its height. <a href="/wiki/Montmartre" title="Montmartre">Montmartre</a>, which, at the heart of an increasingly vast and impersonal Paris, managed to retain a bucolic village atmosphere; festivities and artists mixed, with pleasure and beauty as their values. On 6 October 1889, the Moulin Rouge opened in the Jardin de Paris, at the foot of the Montmartre hill. Its creator <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Oller" title="Joseph Oller">Joseph Oller</a> and his Manager <a href="/wiki/Charles_Zidler" title="Charles Zidler">Charles Zidler</a> were formidable businessmen who understood the public's tastes. The aim was to allow the very rich to come and 'slum it' in a fashionable district, <a href="/wiki/Montmartre" title="Montmartre">Montmartre</a>. The extravagant setting – the garden was adorned with a gigantic elephant – allowed people from all walks of life to mix. Workers, residents of the Place Blanche, artists, the middle classes, businessmen, elegant women and foreigners passing through Paris rubbed shoulders. Nicknamed "The First Palace of Women" by Oller and Zidler, the cabaret quickly became a great success.</p>

<p>The ingredients for its success:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Histoire_du_Moulin_Rouge_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Histoire_du_Moulin_Rouge-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>A revolutionary architecture for the auditorium that allowed rapid changes of décor and where everyone could mix;</li>
<li>Festive champagne evenings where people danced and were entertained thanks to amusing acts that changed regularly, such as the Pétomane;</li>
<li>A new dance inspired by the quadrille which becomes more and more popular: The <a href="/wiki/Can-can" title="Can-can">Can-can</a>, danced to a furious rhythm by dancers in titillating costumes;</li>
<li>Famous dancers whom history still remembers: <a href="/wiki/La_Goulue" title="La Goulue">la Goulue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jane_Avril" title="Jane Avril">Jane Avril</a>, la Môme Fromage, Grille d'Egout, Nini Pattes en l'Air, Yvette Guilbert and the clown <a href="/wiki/Cha-U-Kao" title="Cha-U-Kao">Cha-U-Kao</a>;</li>
<li>A place loved by artists, of whom the most iconic was Toulouse-Lautrec. His posters and paintings secured rapid and international fame for the Moulin Rouge.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greatest_moments">Greatest moments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moulin_Rouge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Greatest moments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-Prose" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="48" data-file-width="48" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div>

<ul>
<li>The early years of the Moulin Rouge are marked by extravagant shows, inspired by the circus, and attractions that are still famous such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P%C3%A9tomane" title="Pétomane">Pétomane</a>. Concert-dances are organised every day at 10pm.</li>
<li>1886–1910: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Footit_and_Chocolat&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Footit and Chocolat (page does not exist)">Footit and Chocolat</a>, a comic act of a white, authoritarian clown and a black, long-suffering <a href="/wiki/Clown#Auguste" title="Clown">Auguste</a>, are very popular and often appear on the Moulin Rouge poster.</li>
<li>19 April 1890: 1st review, "Circassiens et Circassiennes".</li>
<li>26 October 1890: His Royal Highness the Prince of Wales, the future Edward VII, who on a private visit to Paris, booked a table to see this quadrille whose reputation had already crossed the Channel. Recognising him, La Goulue, with her leg in the air and her head in her skirts, spontaneously called out "Hey, Wales, the champagne's on you!".</li>
<li>1891: <i><a href="/wiki/La_Goulue" title="La Goulue">La Goulue</a></i>: Toulouse-Lautrec's first poster for the Moulin Rouge.</li>
<li>1893: The "<a href="/wiki/Bal_des_Quat%27z%27Arts" title="Bal des Quat'z'Arts">Bal des Quat'z'Arts</a>" caused a scandal with its procession of a nude <a href="/wiki/Cleopatra" title="Cleopatra">Cleopatra</a> surrounded by young naked women.<br/>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brooklyn_Museum_-_At_the_Moulin_Rouge_(Au_Moulin_Rouge)_-_Henri_de_Toulouse-Lautrec.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>
</li>
<li>12 November 1897: The Moulin Rouge closed its doors for the first time for the funeral of its manager and cofounder, Charles Zidler. Yvette Guilbert paid him homage saying, "You have the knack of creating popular pleasure, in the finest sense of the word, of entertaining crowds with subtlety, according to the status of those to be entertained".</li>
<li>1900: visitors from around the world, attracted by the Universal Exhibition, flock to the "Moulin Rouch". This gave Paris a reputation as a city of decadent pleasure. In many other countries imitation "Moulin Rouges" and "Montmartres" sprang up.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operetta_and_grand_shows">Operetta and grand shows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moulin_Rouge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Operetta and grand shows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>January 1903: the Moulin Rouge reopened after renovation and improvement work carried out by <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Niermans_(architect)" title="Édouard Niermans (architect)">Édouard Niermans</a>, the most "Parisian" architect of the Belle Époque (amongst other works he designed the brasserie Mollard, the Paris Casino, the <a href="/wiki/Folies_Berg%C3%A8re" title="Folies Bergère">Folies Bergère</a> in Paris, the Palace Hôtel in Ostend in Belgium, the rebuilding of the <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B4tel_du_Palais" title="Hôtel du Palais">Hôtel du Palais</a> in Biarritz, and the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Hotel_Negresco" title="Hotel Negresco">Hotel Negresco</a> on the Promenade des Anglais in Nice). First aperitif concert, where the elite of the fashionable world met for dinner and a show in a setting more beautiful and comfortable than any that existed elsewhere.</li>
<li>Until the First World War, the Moulin Rouge became a real temple of operetta. Further successful shows follow: <i>Voluptata</i>, <i>La Feuille de Vigne</i>, <i>le Rêve d'Egypte</i>, <i>Tais-toi tu m'affoles</i> and many others, each with a more evocative title than the last.</li>
<li>3 January 1907: during the show <i>le Rêve d'Egypte</i>, <a href="/wiki/Colette" title="Colette">Colette</a> exchanged kisses that showed her links with the Duchess of Morny. Deemed to be scandalous, the show was banned.</li>
<li>29 July 1907: first appearance of <a href="/wiki/Mistinguett" title="Mistinguett">Mistinguett</a> on stage at the Moulin Rouge in the <i>Revue de la Femme</i>. Her talent was immediately obvious. The following year she had a huge success with Max Dearly in <i>la Valse chaloupée</i>.</li>
<li>Mistinguett, born in poverty, was not particularly beautiful but had an undeniably quick wit. She wanted to build her own life and said "the poor suburbs, it's not enough just to want to get out. I had a talent: life. All the rest remains to be done, to be thought about. I couldn't allow myself just to be a beautiful animal, I had to think of everything". A peerless businesswoman, she first listened carefully then captivated. She lived wholly for her art, and toured Europe and the United States.</li>
<li>9 April 1910: A former lady-in-waiting to the Empress Eugénie attended a showing of the Revue Amoureuse at the Moulin Rouge. She was so enchanted by the faithful recreation of the ceremony for the return of the troops from Italy that she could not stop herself from calling out "Long Live the Empress!"</li>
<li>27 February 1915: the Moulin Rouge was destroyed by fire.</li>
<li>1921: The rebuilt Moulin Rouge finally reopened.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mistinguett_years">Mistinguett years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moulin_Rouge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mistinguett years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mistinguett_Moulin_Rouge.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1320" data-file-width="1027" height="283" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Mistinguett_Moulin_Rouge.jpg/220px-Mistinguett_Moulin_Rouge.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Mistinguett_Moulin_Rouge.jpg/330px-Mistinguett_Moulin_Rouge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Mistinguett_Moulin_Rouge.jpg/440px-Mistinguett_Moulin_Rouge.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mistinguett_Moulin_Rouge.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Mistinguett" title="Mistinguett">Mistinguett</a> at the Moulin rouge</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>After World War I, <a href="/wiki/Francis_Salabert" title="Francis Salabert">Francis Salabert</a> took charge of the Moulin Rouge. A businessman rather than a showman, he gave Jacques-Charles, the leading impresario of the time, the task of reinvigorating the cabaret. The Moulin Rouge took off again, thanks to stars such as Mistinguett, <a href="/wiki/Jeanne_Aubert" title="Jeanne Aubert">Jeanne Aubert</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Chevalier" title="Maurice Chevalier">Maurice Chevalier</a>, and gave the first showing in Paris of American revues with the Hoffmann Girls.</li>
<li>Gesmar, aged 20, became set designer. His drawings and models will always be associated with the image of the Moulin Rouge.</li>
<li>Jacques-Charles and Mistinguett were the originators of:
<ul>
<li>1925 : <i>la Revue Mistinguett</i></li>
<li>1926 : <i>Ça c'est Paris</i></li>
<li>1928 : <i>Paris qui tourne</i></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>At the Moulin Rouge, Mistinguett created many enduring songs, including "Valencia", "Ça c'est Paris", both by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Padilla_S%C3%A1nchez" title="José Padilla Sánchez">Jose Padilla</a>, "Il m'a vue nue", "On m' suit", "La Java de Doudoune", the latter with <a href="/wiki/Jean_Gabin" title="Jean Gabin">Jean Gabin</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_Mistinguett">After Mistinguett</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moulin_Rouge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: After Mistinguett">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_101I-129-0480-25,_Paris,_deutsche_Soldaten_vor_dem_Moulin_Rouge.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="558" height="315" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Bundesarchiv_Bild_101I-129-0480-25%2C_Paris%2C_deutsche_Soldaten_vor_dem_Moulin_Rouge.jpg/220px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_101I-129-0480-25%2C_Paris%2C_deutsche_Soldaten_vor_dem_Moulin_Rouge.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Bundesarchiv_Bild_101I-129-0480-25%2C_Paris%2C_deutsche_Soldaten_vor_dem_Moulin_Rouge.jpg/330px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_101I-129-0480-25%2C_Paris%2C_deutsche_Soldaten_vor_dem_Moulin_Rouge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Bundesarchiv_Bild_101I-129-0480-25%2C_Paris%2C_deutsche_Soldaten_vor_dem_Moulin_Rouge.jpg/440px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_101I-129-0480-25%2C_Paris%2C_deutsche_Soldaten_vor_dem_Moulin_Rouge.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_101I-129-0480-25,_Paris,_deutsche_Soldaten_vor_dem_Moulin_Rouge.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The shows went on during the German occupation, and <a href="/wiki/Prostitution" title="Prostitution">prostitution</a> was in its heyday</div>
</div>

<ul>
<li>1929: Mistinguett retires from the stage and leaves the Moulin Rouge.</li>
<li>After her departure, the ballroom is transformed into the most ultra-modern Night Club of the time.</li>
<li>June – August 1929: the revue <i>Lew Leslie's Black Birds</i>, starring jazz singer and Broadway star <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_Hall" title="Adelaide Hall">Adelaide Hall</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> with a troop of a hundred black artists accompanied by the Jazz Plantation Orchestra, opens at the Moulin Rouge and becomes the hit of the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Les_secrets_du_Moulin_Rouge_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Les_secrets_du_Moulin_Rouge-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>1937: the Cotton Club, all the rage in New York, is put on at the Moulin Rouge; Ray Ventura and his Collegians also appear.</li>
<li>1939–1945 Second World War. The German Occupation 'Guide aryien' counts the Moulin Rouge among the must visits in Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-we_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-we-5">[5]</a></sup> and its famous stage shows continued for the occupation troops, which are mentioned in various autobiographies of German officers, such as <a href="/wiki/Ernst_J%C3%BCnger" title="Ernst Jünger">Ernst Jünger</a>, Gerhard Heller and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The Germans used the motto "Jeder einmal in Paris" (everyone once in Paris) to provide 'recreational visits' in Paris for its troops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The intensive prostitution during the occupation made way for the Loi de <a href="/wiki/Marthe_Richard" title="Marthe Richard">Marthe Richard</a> (1946), which closed the bordellos and reduced stage shows to dancing events.</li>
<li>1944: a few days after the liberation of Paris, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edith_Piaf" title="Edith Piaf">Edith Piaf</a>, who had been a frequent performer at German Forces social and bordello gatherings during the Second World War, and had been considered a traitor by many, performs again at the Moulin Rouge, with <a href="/wiki/Yves_Montand" title="Yves Montand">Yves Montand</a>, a newcomer chosen to appear with her.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewal">Renewal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moulin_Rouge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Renewal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>22 June 1951: Georges France, called Jo France, founder of the <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balajo" title="fr:Balajo">Balajo</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/rue_de_Lappe" title="fr:rue de Lappe">rue de Lappe</a>, Paris), acquires the Moulin Rouge and starts major renovation work. He gives architects Pierre Devinoy, Bernard de La Tour d’Auvergne and <a href="/wiki/Marion_Tournon-Branly" title="Marion Tournon-Branly">Marion Tournon-Branly</a> the task of improving and fitting out the new auditorium. The décor envisaged by Jo France and largely realized by Henri Mahé, one of the most fashionable designers of the day, has lasted and is still in place.</li>
<li>The evening dances, the acts, and the famous French cancan are back at the Moulin Rouge.</li>
<li>19 May 1953: the 25th "Bal des Petits Lits Blancs", organised by the novelist Guy des Cars, takes place at the Moulin Rouge in the presence of the French President, Vincent Auriol, and it includes, for the first time on a European stage, Bing Crosby. The evening attracts 1,200 artists and stars from around the world, including <a href="/wiki/Josephine_Baker" title="Josephine Baker">Josephine Baker</a> who sings "J'ai deux amours".</li>
<li>Between 1951 and 1960, a succession of famous artists appears: <a href="/wiki/Luis_Mariano" title="Luis Mariano">Luis Mariano</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Tr%C3%A9net" title="Charles Trénet">Charles Trénet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Aznavour" title="Charles Aznavour">Charles Aznavour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Line_Renaud" title="Line Renaud">Line Renaud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bourvil" title="Bourvil">Bourvil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fernand_Raynaud" title="Fernand Raynaud">Fernand Raynaud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lena_Horne" title="Lena Horne">Lena Horne</a>.</li>
<li>1955: Jo France transfers the Moulin Rouge to the brothers Joseph and Louis Clérico who already own the Lido.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-8">[8]</a></sup> Jean Bauchet becomes Manager. The famous French cancan is still performed, soon to be choreographed by Ruggero Angeletti.</li>
<li>1957: Doris Haug creates the "Doriss Girls" troop at the Moulin Rouge. Initially four girls, the troop has eventually grown to sixty.</li>
<li>1959: the Moulin Rouge is transformed with new kitchens.</li>
<li>1960 The <i>Revue Japonaise</i>, entirely composed of Japanese artists, launches the Kabuki in <a href="/wiki/Montmartre" title="Montmartre">Montmartre</a>.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>1962: <a href="/wiki/Jacki_Cl%C3%A9rico" title="Jacki Clérico">Jacki Clérico</a>, son of Joseph Clérico, takes control of the Moulin Rouge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-8">[8]</a></sup> It is the start of a new era: Enlargement of the auditorium, installation of a giant aquarium, and the first aquatic ballet</li>
<li>1962: Revue <i>Cancan</i>, devised by Doris Haug and Ruggero Angeletti.</li>
<li>Since 1963 and the success of the <i>Frou-Frou</i> revue, out of superstition Jacki Clérico chooses only revue titles that start with the letter F. Naturally, the famous French cancan is performed at every revue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Le_Moulin_Rouge_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Le_Moulin_Rouge-9">[9]</a></sup>

<li>1967–1970 : <i>Fascination</i></li>
<li>1970–1973 : <i>Fantastic</i></li>
<li>1973–1976 : <i>Festival</i></li>
<li>1976–1978 : <i>Follement</i></li>

<li>Since 1999: <i><a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9erie_(Moulin_Rouge)" title="Féerie (Moulin Rouge)">Féerie</a></i></li>

</li>
<li>7 September 1979: the Moulin Rouge, again the centre of Paris night life, celebrates its 90th birthday. On stage, for the first time in Paris, <a href="/wiki/Ginger_Rogers" title="Ginger Rogers">Ginger Rogers</a> is surrounded by various stars including <a href="/wiki/Thierry_Le_Luron" title="Thierry Le Luron">Thierry Le Luron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dalida" title="Dalida">Dalida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Aznavour" title="Charles Aznavour">Charles Aznavour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Claude_Brialy" title="Jean-Claude Brialy">Jean-Claude Brialy</a>, Georges Chakiris, the <a href="/wiki/Village_People" title="Village People">Village People</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zizi_Jeanmaire" title="Zizi Jeanmaire">Zizi Jeanmaire</a>.</li>
<li>23 November 1981: the Moulin Rouge closes for one evening to present its show to Her Majesty Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a>.</li>
<li>4 February 1982: one-off show with Liza Minnelli.</li>
<li>3 July 1984: gala with Dean Martin.</li>
<li>25 September 1984: gala with <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sinatra" title="Frank Sinatra">Frank Sinatra</a>.</li>
<li>1 December 1986: the world's most famous classical dancer, <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Baryshnikov" title="Mikhail Baryshnikov">Mikhail Baryshnikov</a>, creates an original ballet by Maurice Béjart at the Moulin Rouge.</li>
<li>20 February 1988: Although the original building had burned down in 1915, the Moulin Rouge turns 100. The premier of the revue <i>Formidable</i> is a "Royal Variety Performance in Paris", a prestigious official event in Britain attended each year in London by a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Family" title="Royal Family">Royal Family</a>. For the second time, the show took place in France, at the Moulin Rouge. Presided over in 1983 by Her Royal Highness Princess Anne, on 20 February 1988 His Royal Highness Prince Edward is the guest of honour.</li>
<li>Spring 1989: one-off performance by the Moulin Rouge in London before their Royal Highnesses the Prince and Princess of Wales.</li>
<li>6 October 1989: Centenary gala with <a href="/wiki/Charles_Aznavour" title="Charles Aznavour">Charles Aznavour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lauren_Bacall" title="Lauren Bacall">Lauren Bacall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ray_Charles" title="Ray Charles">Ray Charles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Curtis" title="Tony Curtis">Tony Curtis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ella_Fitzgerald" title="Ella Fitzgerald">Ella Fitzgerald</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gipsy_Kings" title="Gipsy Kings">Gipsy Kings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Margaux_Hemingway" title="Margaux Hemingway">Margaux Hemingway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Hendricks" title="Barbara Hendricks">Barbara Hendricks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Lamour" title="Dorothy Lamour">Dorothy Lamour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Lewis" title="Jerry Lewis">Jerry Lewis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jane_Russell" title="Jane Russell">Jane Russell</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Tr%C3%A9net" title="Charles Trénet">Charles Trénet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Esther_Williams" title="Esther Williams">Esther Williams</a>.</li>
<li>1994: Cartier gala in aid of the Artists' Foundation against AIDS with a private concert by <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a>.</li>
<li>1995: <a href="/wiki/Lanc%C3%B4me" title="Lancôme">Lancôme</a> gala – launch of the perfume "Poème" with <a href="/wiki/Juliette_Binoche" title="Juliette Binoche">Juliette Binoche</a>. Private concert with <a href="/wiki/Charles_Aznavour" title="Charles Aznavour">Charles Aznavour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jessye_Norman" title="Jessye Norman">Jessye Norman</a>.</li>
<li>14 November 1999: last showing of the Centenary revue <i>Formidable</i>, seen by more than 4.5 million spectators between 1988 and 1999.</li>
<li>23 December 1999: first showing of the new revue <i>Féerie</i>.</li>
<li>24 May 2008: soloist dancer <a href="/wiki/Aleksandar_Josipovi%C4%87" title="Aleksandar Josipović">Aleksandar Josipović</a> was master of ceremonies at the <a href="/wiki/Eurovision_Song_Contest_2008" title="Eurovision Song Contest 2008">53rd Eurovision Song Contest</a></li>
<li>February 2009: for the Year of France in Brazil, and as part of the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Carnival" title="Rio Carnival">Rio Carnival</a>, the Moulin Rouge is produced on the mythical <a href="/wiki/Copacabana,_Rio_de_Janeiro#Copacabana_Beach" title="Copacabana, Rio de Janeiro">Copacabana Beach</a>.</li>
<li>13 January 2013: Moulin Rouge owner <a href="/wiki/Jacki_Cl%C3%A9rico" title="Jacki Clérico">Jacki Clérico</a> dies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>10 August 2014: Mistress du ballet Moulin Rouge since 1957, founder of the Doriss girls (Doriss Haug) dies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>6 October 2014: Moulin Rouge celebrates its 125th anniversary.</li>
</ul>
