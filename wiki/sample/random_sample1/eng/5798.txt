<p>The <b>common kingfisher</b> (<i>Alcedo atthis</i>) also known as the <b>Eurasian kingfisher</b>, and <b>river kingfisher</b>, is a small <a href="/wiki/Kingfisher" title="Kingfisher">kingfisher</a> with seven subspecies recognized within its wide distribution across <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>. It is resident in much of its range, but <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> from areas where rivers freeze in winter.</p>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/House_sparrow" title="House sparrow">sparrow-sized</a> bird has the typical short-tailed, large-headed kingfisher profile; it has blue upperparts, orange underparts and a long bill. It feeds mainly on fish, caught by diving, and has special visual adaptations to enable it to see prey under water. The glossy white eggs are laid in a nest at the end of a burrow in a riverbank.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_kingfisher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> in 1758 as <i>Gracula atthis</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linnaeus_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-linnaeus-2">[2]</a></sup> The modern binomial name derives from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/alcedo" title="wikt:alcedo">alcedo</a></span></i>, "kingfisher" (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <span lang="el" xml:lang="el"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%80%CE%BB%CE%BA%CF%85%CF%8E%CE%BD" title="wikt:ἀλκυών">ἀλκυών</a></span>, <i><span lang="el" xml:lang="el">halcyon</span></i>) and <i>Atthis</i>, a beautiful young woman of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lesbos_Island" title="Lesbos Island">Lesbos</a>, and favourite of <a href="/wiki/Sappho" title="Sappho">Sappho</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BTO_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The genus <i><a href="/wiki/Alcedo" title="Alcedo">Alcedo</a></i> comprises a number of small, exclusively fish-eating kingfishers. The common kingfisher’s closest relatives in the genus are three similar blue-backed, orange-breasted species, the <a href="/wiki/Blue-eared_kingfisher" title="Blue-eared kingfisher">blue-eared</a>, <a href="/wiki/Half-collared_kingfisher" title="Half-collared kingfisher">half-collared</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blyth%27s_kingfisher" title="Blyth's kingfisher">Blyth's kingfishers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fry-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_kingfisher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species has the typical short-tailed, dumpy-bodied large-headed and long-billed kingfisher shape. The adult male of the western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> subspecies, <i>A. a. ispida</i> has green-blue upperparts with pale azure-blue back and rump, a rufous patch by the bill base, and a rufous ear-patch. It has a green-blue neck stripe, white neck blaze and throat, rufous underparts, and a black bill with some red at the base. The legs and feet are bright red.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fry2-5">[5]</a></sup> It is about 16 centimetres (6.3 in) long with a wingspan of 25 cm (9.8 in),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry2_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fry2-5">[5]</a></sup> and weighs 34–46 grams (1.2–1.6 oz).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BWP_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The female is identical in appearance to the male except that her lower <a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">mandible</a> is orange-red with a black tip. The juvenile is similar to the adult, but with duller and greener upperparts and paler underparts. Its bill is black, and the legs are also initially black.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry2_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fry2-5">[5]</a></sup> Feathers are moulted gradually between July and November with the main flight feathers taking 90–100 days to moult and regrow. Some that moult late may suspend their moult during cold winter weather.</p>
<p>The flight of the kingfisher is fast, direct and usually low over water. The short rounded wings whirr rapidly, and a bird flying away shows an electric-blue "flash" down its back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BWP_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In North Africa, Europe and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> north of the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> this is the only small blue kingfisher. In south and southeast Asia it can be confused with six other small blue-and-rufous kingfishers, but the rufous ear patches distinguish it from all but juvenile blue-eared kingfisher; details of the head pattern may be necessary to differentiate the two species where both occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry2_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Fry2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The common kingfisher has no song. The flight call is a short sharp whistle, <i>chee</i>, repeated two or three times. Anxious birds emit a harsh, <i>shrit-it-it</i> and nestlings call for food with a churring noise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry2_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Fry2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>There are seven subspecies differing in the hue of the upperparts and the intensity of the rufous colour of the underparts; size varies across the subspecies by up to 10%. The races resident south of the <a href="/wiki/Wallace_Line" title="Wallace Line">Wallace Line</a> have the bluest upperparts and partly blue ear-patches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry2_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Fry2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>A. a. ispida</i> (Linnaeus, 1758).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref1-7">[7]</a></sup> Breeds from Ireland, Spain and southern Norway to Romania and western Russia and winters south to Iraq and southern Portugal.</li>
<li><i>A. a. atthis</i> (Linnaeus, 1758). Breeds from northwestern Africa and southern Italy east to Afghanistan, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kashmir_region" title="Kashmir region">Kashmir region</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a>, and Siberia; it is a winter visitor south to Israel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> northeastern Sudan, Yemen, Oman and Pakistan. Compared to <i>A. a. ispida</i>, it has a greener crown, paler underparts and is slightly larger.</li>
<li><i>A. a. bengalensis</i> (<a href="/wiki/Samuel_Gottlieb_Gmelin" title="Samuel Gottlieb Gmelin">Gmelin</a>, 1788). Breeds in southern and eastern Asia from India to Indonesia, China, Korea, Japan and eastern Mongolia; winters south to Indonesia and the Philippines. It is smaller and brighter than the European races.</li>
<li><i>A. a. taprobana</i> (<a href="/wiki/Otto_Kleinschmidt" title="Otto Kleinschmidt">Kleinschmidt</a>, 1894). Resident breeder in Sri Lanka and southern India. Its upperparts are bright blue, not green-blue; it is the same size as <i>A. a. bengalensis</i>.</li>
<li><i>A. a. floresiana</i> (<a href="/wiki/Richard_Bowdler_Sharpe" title="Richard Bowdler Sharpe">Sharpe</a>, 1892). Resident breeder from Bali to Timor. Like <i>A. a. taprobana</i>, but the blues are darker and the ear-patch is rufous with a few blue feathers.</li>
<li><i>A. a. hispidoides</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9-Primev%C3%A8re_Lesson" title="René-Primevère Lesson">Lesson</a> 1837). Resident breeder from <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">Sulawesi</a> to New Guinea and the islands of the western Pacific Ocean. Plumage colours are deeper than in <i>A. a. floresiana</i>, the blue on the hind neck and rump is purple-tinged and the ear-patch is blue.</li>
<li><i>A. a. solomonensis</i> (<a href="/wiki/Walter_Rothschild,_2nd_Baron_Rothschild" title="Walter Rothschild, 2nd Baron Rothschild">Rothschild</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a> 1905). Resident breeder in the Solomon Islands east to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Cristobal_(Solomon_Islands)" title="San Cristobal (Solomon Islands)">San Cristobal</a>. The largest southeast Asian subspecies, it has a blue ear-patch and is more purple-tinged than <i>A. a. hispidoides</i>, with which it interbreeds.</li>
</ul>

<p>The common kingfisher is widely distributed over Europe, Asia, and North Africa, mainly south of 60°N. It is a common breeding species over much of its vast Eurasian range, but in North Africa it is mainly a winter visitor, although it is a scarce breeding resident in coastal <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>. In temperate regions, this kingfisher inhabits clear, slow-flowing streams and rivers, and lakes with well-vegetated banks. It frequents scrubs and bushes with overhanging branches close to shallow open water in which it hunts. In winter it is more coastal, often feeding in estuaries or harbours and along rocky seashores. Tropical populations are found by slow-flowing rivers, in <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangrove</a> creeks and in swamps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry2_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-Fry2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Common kingfishers are important members of ecosystems and good indicators of freshwater community health. The highest densities of breeding birds are found in habitats with clear water, which permits optimal prey visibility, and trees or shrubs on the banks. These habitats have also the highest quality of water, so the presence of this bird confirms the standard of the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peris_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peris-9">[9]</a></sup> Measures to improve water flow can disrupt this habitat, and in particular, the replacement of natural banks by artificial confinement greatly reduces the populations of fish, amphibians and aquatic reptiles, and waterside birds are lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-china_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-china-10">[10]</a></sup> It can tolerate a certain degree of urbanisation, provided the water remains clean.</p>
<p>This species is resident in areas where the climate is mild year-round, but must <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> after breeding from regions with prolonged freezing conditions in winter. Most birds winter within the southern parts of the breeding range, but smaller numbers cross the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> into Africa or travel over the mountains of <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> into Southeast Asia. Kingfishers migrate mainly at night, and some Siberian breeders must travel at least 3,000 km (1,900 mi) between the breeding sites and the wintering areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry2_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-Fry2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_kingfisher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Like all kingfishers, the common kingfisher is highly territorial; since it must eat around 60% of its body weight each day, it is essential to have control of a suitable stretch of river. It is solitary for most of the year, roosting alone in heavy cover. If another kingfisher enters its territory, both birds display from perches, and fights may occur, where a bird will grab the other's beak and try to hold it under water. Pairs form in the autumn but each bird retains a separate territory, generally at least 1 km (0.62 mi) long, but up to 3.5 km (2.2 mi) and territories are not merged until the spring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry2_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-Fry2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The courtship is initiated by the male chasing the female while calling continually, and later by ritual feeding, copulation usually following.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BWP_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The nest is in a burrow excavated by both birds of the pair in a low vertical riverbank, or sometimes a quarry or other cutting. The straight, gently inclining burrow is normally 60–90 cm (24–35 in) long and ends in an enlarged chamber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BWP_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-6">[6]</a></sup> The nest cavity is unlined but soon accumulates a litter of fish remains and cast <a href="/wiki/Pellet_(ornithology)" title="Pellet (ornithology)">pellets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coward_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The common kingfisher typically lays two to ten glossy white eggs, which average 1.9 cm (0.75 in) in breadth, 2.2 cm (0.87 in) in length, and weigh about 4.3 g (0.15 oz), of which 5% is shell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BTO_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-3">[3]</a></sup> One or two eggs in most clutches fail to hatch because the parent cannot cover them. Both sexes incubate by day, but only the female at night. An incubating bird sits trance-like, facing the tunnel; it invariably casts a pellet, breaking it up with the bill. The eggs hatch in 19–20 days, and the <a href="/wiki/Altricial" title="Altricial">altricial</a> young are in the nest for a further 24–25 days, often more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fry-4">[4]</a></sup> Once large enough, young birds will come to the burrow entrance to be fed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coward_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-11">[11]</a></sup> Two broods, sometimes three, may be reared in a season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BWP_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Survival">Survival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_kingfisher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Survival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The early days for fledged juveniles are more hazardous; during its first dives into water, about four days after leaving the nest, a fledgling may become waterlogged and drown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry2_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-Fry2-5">[5]</a></sup> Many young will not have learned to fish by the time they are driven out of their parents' territory, and only about half survive more than a week or two. Most kingfishers die of cold or lack of food, and a severe winter can kill a high percentage of the birds. Summer floods can destroy nests or make fishing difficult, resulting in starvation of the brood. Only a quarter of the young survive to breed the following year, but this is enough to maintain the population. Likewise, only a quarter of adult birds survive from one breeding season to the next. Very few birds live longer than one breeding season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rspbst_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-rspbst-12">[12]</a></sup> The oldest bird on record was 21 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-euringt_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-euringt-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other causes of death are cats, rats, collisions with vehicles and windows, and human disturbance of nesting birds, including riverbank works with heavy machinery. Since kingfishers are high up in the food chain, they are vulnerable to build-up of chemicals, and river pollution by industrial and agricultural products excludes the birds from many stretches of otherwise suitable rivers that would be habitats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rspbst_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-rspbst-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>This species was killed in Victorian times for stuffing and display in glass cases and for use in hat making. English naturalist <a href="/wiki/William_Yarrell" title="William Yarrell">William Yarrell</a> also reported the country practice of killing a kingfisher and hanging it from a thread in the belief that it would swing to predict the direction in which the wind would blow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cocker_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-14">[14]</a></sup> Persecution by anglers and to provide feathers for fishing flies were common in earlier decades,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coward_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-11">[11]</a></sup> but are now largely a thing of the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rspbst_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-rspbst-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>The common kingfisher hunts from a perch 1–2 m (3.3–6.6 ft) above the water, on a branch, post or riverbank, bill pointing down as it searches for prey. It bobs its head when food is detected to gauge the distance, and plunges steeply down to seize its prey usually no deeper than 25 cm (9.8 in) below the surface. The wings are opened under water and the open eyes are protected by the transparent <a href="/wiki/Nictitating_membrane" title="Nictitating membrane">third eyelid</a>. The bird rises beak-first from the surface and flies back to its perch. At the perch the fish is adjusted until it is held near its tail and beaten against the perch several times. Once dead, the fish is positioned lengthways and swallowed head-first. A few times each day, a small greyish pellet of fish bones and other indigestible remains is regurgitated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry2_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-Fry2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The food is mainly fish up to 12.5 cm (4.9 in) long, but the average size is 2.3 cm (0.91 in). <a href="/wiki/Common_minnow" title="Common minnow">Minnows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stickleback" title="Stickleback">sticklebacks</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Common_roach" title="Common roach">roach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trout" title="Trout">trout</a> are typical prey. About 60% of food items are fish, but this kingfisher also catches aquatic insects such as <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">dragonfly</a> <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_beetle" title="Water beetle">water beetles</a>, and, in winter, crustaceans including freshwater shrimps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry2_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-Fry2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>A challenge for any diving bird is the change in <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refraction</a> between air and water. The eyes of many birds have two <a href="/wiki/Fovea_centralis" title="Fovea centralis">foveae</a> (the fovea is the area of the <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retina</a> the greatest density of light receptors),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinclair_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair-15">[15]</a></sup> and a kingfisher is able to switch from the main central fovea to the auxiliary fovea when it enters water; a retinal streak of high receptor density which connects the two foveae allows the image to swing temporally as the bird drops onto the prey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwab_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schwab-16">[16]</a></sup> The egg-shaped <a href="/wiki/Lens_(anatomy)" title="Lens (anatomy)">lens</a> of the eye points towards the auxiliary fovea, enabling the bird to maintain <a href="/wiki/Bird_vision" title="Bird vision">visual acuity</a> underwater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinclair_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair-15">[15]</a></sup> Because of the positions of the foveae, the kingfisher has <a href="/wiki/Monocular_vision" title="Monocular vision">monocular vision</a> in air, and <a href="/wiki/Binocular_vision" title="Binocular vision">binocular vision</a> in water. The underwater vision is not as a sharp as in air, but the ability to judge the distance of moving prey is more important than the sharpness of the image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwab_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schwab-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Each <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">cone cell</a> of a bird’s retina contains an oil droplet which may contain <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoid</a> pigments. These droplets enhance colour vision and reduce glare. Aquatic kingfishers have high numbers of red pigments in their oil droplets; the reason red droplets predominate is not understood, but the droplets may help with the glare or the dispersion of light from particulate matter in the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwab_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schwab-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_kingfisher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species has a large range, with an estimated global extent of occurrence of 10 million square kilometres (3.8 million square miles). It has a large population, including an estimated 160,000–320,000 individuals in Europe alone. Global population trends have not been quantified, but populations appear to be stable so the species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the IUCN Red List (i.e., declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations). For these reasons, the species is evaluated as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_concern" title="Least concern">least concern</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BTO_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
