<p>A <b>wet wipe</b>, also known as a <b>wet towel</b>, or a <b>moist towelette</b>, is a small moistened piece of <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cloth" title="Cloth">cloth</a> that often comes folded and individually wrapped for <a href="/wiki/Convenience" title="Convenience">convenience</a>. Wet wipes are used for cleaning purposes, like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_hygiene" title="Personal hygiene">personal hygiene</a> or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Household_cleaning&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Household cleaning (page does not exist)">household cleaning</a>. Because of their failure to break down when flushed, they have been implicated by water companies as contributory to sewer blockages known as <a href="/wiki/Fatberg" title="Fatberg">fatbergs</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Invention">Invention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wet_wipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Invention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>American <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Julius&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Arthur Julius (page does not exist)">Arthur Julius</a> is seen as the inventor of the wet wipes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Julius worked in the <a href="/wiki/Cosmetics" title="Cosmetics">cosmetics</a> industry and adjusted 1957 a soap portionor machine, putting it in a loft in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>. Julius trademarked the name Wet-Nap in 1958, a name for the product that is still being used. After fine tuning his newfangled hand-cleaning aid together with a mechanic, he unveiled his invention at the 1960 National Restaurant Show in Chicago and started selling in 1963 Wet-Nap products to Colonel Sanders for use in his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KFC_restaurant" title="KFC restaurant">KFC restaurant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wet_wipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Air-laid_paper" title="Air-laid paper">Air-laid paper</a></div>
<p>Wet wipes are produced as air-laid paper where the fibres are carried and formed to the structure of paper by air or with nonwoven spun-lace fabric where fibers are intermingled with hydro entanglement through very high pressure of water . They are moistened with water or other liquids like <a href="/wiki/Isopropyl_alcohol" title="Isopropyl alcohol">isopropyl alcohol</a> depending on the applications. The paper might be treated with softeners, lotions or added perfume to adjust the properties or "feeling". Preservatives such as <a href="/wiki/Methylisothiazolinone" title="Methylisothiazolinone">methylisothiazolinone</a> are used to prevent bacterial or fungal growth in the package. The finished wet wipes are folded and put in pocket size package or a box dispenser.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wet_wipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wet wipes can serve a number of personal and household purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Although marketed primarily for wiping infants' bottoms in diaper changing, it is not uncommon for consumers to also use the product to clean floors, toilet seats, and other surfaces around the home. Parents also use wet wipes, or as they are called for baby care, baby wipes, for wiping up baby vomit and to clean babies' hands and faces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Baby_wipes">Baby wipes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wet_wipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Baby wipes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Baby wipes are wet wipes used to cleanse the sensitive skin of infants. These are saturated with solutions anywhere from gentle cleansing ingredients to alcohol based 'cleaners'. Baby wipes are typically different pack counts (ranging up to 80 or more sheets per pack), and come with dispensing mechanisms. The origin of baby wipes most likely came in the mid-1950s as more people were travelling and needed a way to clean up on the go. One of the first companies to produce these was a company called Nice-Pak. They made napkin sized paper cloth saturated with a scented skin cleanser. Rockline Industries of <a href="/wiki/Sheboygan,_Wisconsin" title="Sheboygan, Wisconsin">Sheboygan, Wisconsin</a> (which has a large part of the <a href="/wiki/Private_label" title="Private label">private label</a> wipe market in several segments) went on to be the first to innovate the first baby wipe refill pack and pop-up packs which have become common in the marketplace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first real baby wipe products appeared on the market in 1990<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and were larger companies like <a href="/wiki/Kimberly-Clark" title="Kimberly-Clark">Kimberly-Clark</a> who produced Huggies and <a href="/wiki/Procter_%26_Gamble" title="Procter &amp; Gamble">Procter &amp; Gamble</a>'s Pampers. As the technology to produce wipes matured and became more affordable, smaller brands began to appear.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> By the 1990s, most super stores like Kmart and Wal-Mart had their own private label brand of wipes made by other manufacturers. After this period there was a boom in the industry and many local brands started manufacturing because of low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entry_barrier" title="Entry barrier">entry barriers</a>.</p>
<p>Many green-minded parents, or those looking to save extra money, use washable baby wipes, typically small squares of material (cotton, bamboo or fleece) that can be pre-soaked ready to use, or wet as required. Because they don't contain chemicals or artificial fragrances like most disposable wipes, they are gentle on the skin. They are often reported to be more effective at removing solids from the skin because of their textured nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_hygiene">Personal hygiene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wet_wipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal hygiene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wet wipes began to be marketed as a luxury option to <a href="/wiki/Toilet_paper" title="Toilet paper">toilet paper</a> by 2005 by companies such as <a href="/wiki/Kimberly-Clark" title="Kimberly-Clark">Kimberly-Clark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Procter_%26_Gamble" title="Procter &amp; Gamble">Procter &amp; Gamble</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantic_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic-8">[8]</a></sup> Many adults now use wet wipes in place of toilet paper.</p>
<p>In Southeast Asia, wet wipes are often sold out of refrigerators to give the wipes a refreshingly cool effect. They are often dispensed in restaurants, at service stations, along with <a href="/wiki/Airline_meal" title="Airline meal">airline meals</a>, in doctors' offices, and other similar places. They are often included as part of a standard sealed <a href="/wiki/Cutlery" title="Cutlery">cutlery</a> package.</p>
<p>Wet wipes have also found a use among visitors to outdoor music festivals, particularly those who camp, as an alternative to communal showers. The wet wipes are a preferable option to the communal facilities for which there are long queues.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cleansing_pads">Cleansing pads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wet_wipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cleansing pads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cleansing pads are fiber sponges which have been previously soaked with water, alcohol and other active ingredients for a specific intended use. They are ready to use hygiene products and they are simple and convenient solutions to dispose of dirt or other undesirable elements.</p>
<p>There are different type of cleansing pads offered by the beauty industry: make-up removing pads, anti-spot treatments and anti-acne pads that usually contain <a href="/wiki/Salicylic_acid" title="Salicylic acid">salicylic acid</a>, vitamins, menthol and other treatments).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cleansing pads for preventing infection are usually saturated with alcohol and bundled in sterile package. Hands and instrument may be deinfected with these pads while treating wounds. Disinfecting cleansing pads are often included in first aid kits for this purpose. Since the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/2009_flu_pandemic" title="2009 flu pandemic">H1N1</a> sales of individual impregnated wet wipes and gels in sachets and flowpacks have dramatically increased in the UK following the Government’s advice to keep hands and surfaces clean to prevent the spread of germs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_wipes">Industrial wipes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wet_wipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Industrial wipes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pre-impregnated industrial-strength cleaning wipes with powerful cleaning fluid that cuts through the dirt as the high performance fabric absorbs the residue Industrial wipes has the ability to clean a vast range of though substances from hands, tools and surfaces, including: grime, grease, oil- &amp; water-based paints &amp; coatings, adhesives, silicone &amp; acrylic sealants, poly foam, epoxy, oil, tar and more.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pain_relief">Pain relief</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wet_wipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pain relief">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are pain relief pads sopping with alcohol and <a href="/wiki/Benzocaine" title="Benzocaine">benzocaine</a>. These pads are good for treating minor scrapes, burns, and insect bites. They disinfect the injury and also ease pain and itching.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pet_care">Pet care</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wet_wipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pet care">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Today one can find even wet wipes for pet care, for example eye, ear, or dental cleansing pads (with <a href="/wiki/Boric_acid" title="Boric acid">boric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chloride" title="Potassium chloride">potassium chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc_sulfate" title="Zinc sulfate">zinc sulfate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Borax" title="Borax">sodium borate</a>) for dogs, cats, horses, and birds.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Healthcare">Healthcare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wet_wipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Healthcare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Medical wet wipes are available for various applications. These include alcohol wet wipes, <a href="/wiki/Chlorhexidine" title="Chlorhexidine">chlorhexidine</a> wipes (for disinfection of surfaces and noninvasive medical devices) and sporicidal wipes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Medical wipes can be used to prevent the spread of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathogens" title="Pathogens">pathogens</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Norovirus" title="Norovirus">Norovirus</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clostridium_Difficile" title="Clostridium Difficile">Clostridium Difficile</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_sewage_systems">Effect on sewage systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wet_wipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Effect on sewage systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the mid-2000s, wet wipes such as baby wipes have become more common for use as an alternative to <a href="/wiki/Toilet_paper" title="Toilet paper">toilet paper</a> in affluent countries, including the United States and the United Kingdom. This usage has in some cases been encouraged by manufacturers, who have labelled some wet wipe brands as "flushable". Wet wipes, even "flushable" ones, when flushed into toilets have been known to clog internal plumbing, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Septic_system" title="Septic system">septic systems</a>, and public sewer systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013-one_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-2013-one-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-may2009_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-may2009-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ox2015_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ox2015-14">[14]</a></sup> The tendency for <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a> and wet wipes to cling together encourages the growth of the problematic obstructions in sewers known as '<a href="/wiki/Fatberg" title="Fatberg">fatbergs</a>'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In addition, some brands of wipes contain alcohol, which can kill the bacteria and <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> responsible for breaking down solid waste in septic tanks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sept2013_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-sept2013-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Class_action" title="Class action">class action</a> suit was filed in 2014 in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._District_Court_for_the_Northern_District_of_Ohio" title="U.S. District Court for the Northern District of Ohio">U.S. District Court for the Northern District of Ohio</a> against <a href="/wiki/Target_Corporation" title="Target Corporation">Target Corporation</a>, on behalf of consumers in Ohio that purchased Target-brand flushable wipes. The lawsuit alleged the retailer misled consumers by marking the packaging on its Up &amp; Up® brand wipes as flushable and safe for sewer and septic systems. The lawsuit also alleged that the products were a public health hazard because they clogged pumps at municipal waste-treatment facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, the City of <a href="/wiki/Wyoming,_Minnesota" title="Wyoming, Minnesota">Wyoming, Minnesota</a> launched a class action suit against six companies including Procter &amp; Gamble, Kimberly-Clark and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nice-Pak&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nice-Pak (page does not exist)">Nice-Pak</a>, alleging they were fraudulently promoting their products as 'flushable'. The suit was ongoing in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantic_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2016, several British water companies including <a href="/wiki/Wessex_Water" title="Wessex Water">Wessex Water</a> began a campaign advisiong consumers not to flush wet wipes because of the added maintenance costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
