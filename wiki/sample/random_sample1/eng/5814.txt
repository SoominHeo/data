<p>The <b>National Center for Biotechnology Information</b> (<b><a class="external text" href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov" rel="nofollow">NCBI</a></b>) is part of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Library_of_Medicine" title="United States National Library of Medicine">United States National Library of Medicine</a> (NLM), a branch of the <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a>. The NCBI is located in <a href="/wiki/Bethesda,_Maryland" title="Bethesda, Maryland">Bethesda, Maryland</a> and was founded in 1988 through legislation sponsored by Senator <a href="/wiki/Claude_Pepper" title="Claude Pepper">Claude Pepper</a>.</p>
<p>The NCBI houses a series of databases relevant to <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a> and biomedicine and an important resource for bioinformatics tools and services. Major databases include <a href="/wiki/GenBank" title="GenBank">GenBank</a> for DNA sequences and <a href="/wiki/PubMed" title="PubMed">PubMed</a>, a bibliographic database for the biomedical literature. Other databases include the <a href="/wiki/NCBI_Epigenomics" title="NCBI Epigenomics">NCBI Epigenomics</a> database. All these databases are available online through the <a href="/wiki/Entrez" title="Entrez">Entrez</a> search engine.</p>
<p>NCBI is directed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Lipman" title="David Lipman">David Lipman</a>, one of the original authors of the <a href="/wiki/BLAST" title="BLAST">BLAST</a> sequence alignment program and a widely respected figure in <a href="/wiki/Bioinformatics" title="Bioinformatics">bioinformatics</a>. He also leads an intramural research program, including groups led by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Altschul" title="Stephen Altschul">Stephen Altschul</a> (another <a href="/wiki/BLAST" title="BLAST">BLAST</a> co-author), David Landsman, <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Koonin" title="Eugene Koonin">Eugene Koonin</a> (a prolific author on <a href="/wiki/Comparative_genomics" title="Comparative genomics">comparative genomics</a>), John Wilbur, Teresa Przytycka, and Zhiyong Lu.</p>
<p>NCBI is listed in the <a href="/wiki/Registry_of_Research_Data_Repositories" title="Registry of Research Data Repositories">Registry of Research Data Repositories</a> re3data.org.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="GenBank">GenBank</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Center_for_Biotechnology_Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: GenBank">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/GenBank" title="GenBank">GenBank</a></div>
<p>NCBI has had responsibility for making available the GenBank <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_databases" title="Biological databases">sequence database</a> since 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> GenBank coordinates with individual laboratories and other sequence databases such as those of the <a href="/wiki/European_Molecular_Biology_Laboratory" title="European Molecular Biology Laboratory">European Molecular Biology Laboratory</a> (EMBL) and the <a href="/wiki/DNA_Data_Bank_of_Japan" title="DNA Data Bank of Japan">DNA Data Bank of Japan</a> (DDBJ).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 1992, NCBI has grown to provide other databases in addition to GenBank. NCBI provides <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gene_(database)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gene (database) (page does not exist)">Gene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Online_Mendelian_Inheritance_in_Man" title="Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man">Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man</a>, the Molecular Modeling Database (3D protein structures), <a href="/wiki/DbSNP" title="DbSNP">dbSNP</a> (a database of <a href="/wiki/Single-nucleotide_polymorphism" title="Single-nucleotide polymorphism">single-nucleotide polymorphisms</a>), the Reference Sequence Collection, a map of the <a href="/wiki/Human_genome" title="Human genome">human genome</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomy</a> browser, and coordinates with the National Cancer Institute to provide the Cancer Genome Anatomy Project. The NCBI assigns a unique identifier (taxonomy ID number) to each species of organism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The NCBI has software tools that are available by <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">WWW</a> browsing or by FTP. For example, <a href="/wiki/BLAST" title="BLAST">BLAST</a> is a sequence similarity searching program. BLAST can do sequence comparisons against the GenBank DNA database in less than 15 seconds.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="NCBI_Bookshelf">NCBI Bookshelf</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Center_for_Biotechnology_Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: NCBI Bookshelf">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b><a class="external text" href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books" rel="nofollow">NCBI Bookshelf</a></b> is a collection of freely accessible, downloadable, on-line versions of selected biomedical books. The Bookshelf covers a wide range of topics including <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cell_biology" title="Cell biology">cell biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microbiology" title="Microbiology">microbiology</a>, disease states from a molecular and cellular point of view, research methods, and <a href="/wiki/Virology" title="Virology">virology</a>. Some of the books are online versions of previously published books, while others, such as <a href="/wiki/Coffee_Break_(book)" title="Coffee Break (book)">Coffee Break</a>, are written and edited by NCBI staff. The Bookshelf is a complement to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pubmed" title="Pubmed">Entrez PubMed</a> repository of <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer-reviewed publication</a> abstracts in that Bookshelf contents provide established perspectives on evolving areas of study and a context in which many disparate individual pieces of reported research can be organized.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_Local_Alignment_Search_Tool_.28BLAST.29">Basic Local Alignment Search Tool (BLAST)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Center_for_Biotechnology_Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Basic Local Alignment Search Tool (BLAST)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/BLAST" title="BLAST">BLAST</a> is an algorithm used for calculating sequence similarity between biological sequences such as nucleotide sequences of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> and amino acid sequences of proteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> BLAST is a powerful tool for finding sequences similar to the query sequence within the same organism or in different organisms. It searches the query sequence on NCBI databases and servers and post the results back to the person's browser in chosen format. Input sequences to the BLAST are mostly in FASTA or Genbank format while output could be delivered in variety of formats such as HTML, XML formatting and plain text. HTML is the default output format for NCBI's web-page. Results for NCBI-BLAST are presented in graphical format with all the hits found, a table with sequence identifiers for the hits having scoring related data, along with the alignments for the sequence of interest and the hits received with analogous BLAST scores for these<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Entrez">Entrez</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Center_for_Biotechnology_Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Entrez">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Entrez" title="Entrez">Entrez</a> Global Query Cross-Database Search System is used at NCBI for all the major databases such as Nucleotide and Protein Sequences, Protein Structures, PubMed, Taxonomy, Complete Genomes, OMIM, and several others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Entrez is both indexing and retrieval system having data from various sources for biomedical research. NCBI distributed the first version of Entrez in 1991, composed of nucleotide sequences from <a href="/wiki/Protein_Data_Bank" title="Protein Data Bank">PDB</a> and <a href="/wiki/GenBank" title="GenBank">GenBank</a>, protein sequences from SWISS-PROT, translated GenBank, PIR, PRF and PDB and associated abstracts and citations from PubMed. Entrez is specially designed to integrate the data from several different sources, databases and formats into a uniform information model and retrieval system which can efficiently retrieve that relevant references, sequences and structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gene">Gene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Center_for_Biotechnology_Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gene has been implemented at NCBI to characterize and organize the information about genes. It serves as a major node in the nexus of genomic map, expression, sequence, protein function, structure and homology data. A unique GeneID is assigned to each gene record that can be followed through revision cycles. <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">Gene</a> records for known or predicted genes are established here and are demarcated by map positions or nucleotide sequence. Gene has several advantages over its predecessor, LocusLink, including, better integration with other databases in NCBI, broader taxonomic scope, and enhanced options for query and retrieval provided by Entrez system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protein">Protein</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Center_for_Biotechnology_Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Protein">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Protein database is an important protein resource at NCBI. It maintains the text record for individual protein sequences, derived from many different resources such as NCBI Reference Sequence (RefSeq) project, GenbBank, PDB and UniProtKB/SWISS-Prot. Protein records are present in different formats including FASTA and <a href="/wiki/XML" title="XML">XML</a> and are linked to other NCBI resources. Protein provides the relevant data to the users such as genes, DNA/RNA sequences, biological pathways, expression and variation data and literature. It also provides the pre-determined sets of similar and identical proteins for each sequence as computed by the BLAST. The Structure database of NCBI contains 3D coordinate sets for experimentally-determined structures in PDB that are imported by NCBI. The Conserved Domain database (<a href="/wiki/Conserved_Domain_Database" title="Conserved Domain Database">CDD</a>) of protein contains sequence profiles that characterize highly conserved domains within protein sequences. It also has records from external resources like SMART and <a href="/wiki/Pfam" title="Pfam">Pfam</a>. There is another database in protein known as Protein Clusters database which contains sets of proteins sequences that are clustered according to the maximum alignments between the individual sequences as calculated by BLAST.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pubchem_BioAssay_database">Pubchem BioAssay database</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Center_for_Biotechnology_Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pubchem BioAssay database">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>PubChem BioAssay database of NCBI is a public resource for biological tests of small molecules and siRNA reagents. The major purpose of PubChem repository is to provide easy and free of cost access to all deposited data, and to provide intuitive data analysis tools. It is structured as a set of relational databases organized on Microsoft SQL servers. PubChem’s BioAssay data is searchable and accessible by <a href="/wiki/Entrez" title="Entrez">Entrez</a> information retrieval system. PubChem database provides programmatic and Web-based tools for users to search, review, and download a publications, bioactivity data for a compound, a BioAssay record, a molecular target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
