<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Southern literature</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Southern literature</b> (sometimes called the <b>literature of the American South</b>) is defined as <a href="/wiki/American_literature" title="American literature">American literature</a> about the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">Southern United States</a> or by writers from this region. Characteristics of Southern literature include a focus on a common <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Southern_United_States" title="History of the Southern United States">Southern history</a>, the significance of <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a>, a sense of <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a> and one’s role within it, a sense of justice, the region's dominant <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a> (<a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> — see <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>) and the burdens/rewards religion often brings, issues of <a href="/wiki/Racism_in_the_United_States" title="Racism in the United States">racial tension</a>, land and the promise it brings, a sense of <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">social class</a> and place, and the use of <a href="/wiki/Southern_American_English" title="Southern American English">the Southern dialect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview_of_Southern_literature">Overview of Southern literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview of Southern literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In its simplest form, Southern literature consists of writing about the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_South" title="American South">American South</a>—the South being defined, for historical as well as geographical reasons, as the states of <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Pre-Civil War definitions of the South often included <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a> as well.</p>
<p>In addition to the geographical component of Southern literature, certain themes have appeared because of the similar histories of the Southern states in regard to <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_United_States" title="Slavery in the United States">slavery</a>, the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reconstruction_era_of_the_United_States" title="Reconstruction era of the United States">Reconstruction</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservative" title="Conservative">conservative</a> culture in the South has also produced a strong focus within Southern literature on the significance of family, religion, community in one's personal and social life, the use of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_American_English" title="Southern American English">Southern dialect</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup> and a strong sense of "place."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">The South</a>'s troubled history with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(classification_of_human_beings)" title="Race (classification of human beings)">racial</a> issues also continually appears in its literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite these common themes, there is debate as to what makes writers and their literature "Southern." For example, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">Mark Twain</a>, a Missourian, defined the characteristics that many people associate with Southern writing in his <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novel</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Huckleberry_Finn" title="The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn">The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn</a></i>. In addition, many famous Southern writers headed to the Northern U.S. as soon as they were old enough to make it on their own. So while geography is a factor, the geographical birth of the author is not <i>the</i> defining factor in Southern writing.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_Southern_literature">History of Southern literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of Southern literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_and_antebellum_literature">Early and antebellum literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early and antebellum literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the 17th and 18th centuries, English colonists in the Southern part of the American colonies produced a number of notable works. Two of the most famous were early memoirs of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Smith_of_Jamestown" title="John Smith of Jamestown">Captain John Smith</a>'s account of the founding of Jamestown in the 1610s and 1620s, and <a href="/wiki/William_Byrd_II" title="William Byrd II">William Byrd II</a>'s secret plantation diary, kept in the early 18th century. Both sets of recollections are critical documents in early Southern history.</p>
<p>After American independence, in the early 19th century, the expansion of cotton planting and slavery began to distinguish Southern society and culture more clearly from the rest of the young republic. During this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antebellum_period" title="Antebellum period">antebellum period</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, and particularly the city of Charleston, rivaled and perhaps surpassed Virginia as a literary community. Writing in Charleston, the lawyer and essayist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hugh_Swinton_Legare" title="Hugh Swinton Legare">Hugh Swinton Legare</a>, the poets <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hamilton_Hayne" title="Paul Hamilton Hayne">Paul Hamilton Hayne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Timrod" title="Henry Timrod">Henry Timrod</a>, and the novelist <a href="/wiki/William_Gilmore_Simms" title="William Gilmore Simms">William Gilmore Simms</a> composed some of the most important works in antebellum Southern literature.</p>
<p>Simms was a particularly significant figure, perhaps the most prominent Southern author before the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>. His novels of frontier life and the American revolution celebrated the history of South Carolina. Like <a href="/wiki/James_Fenimore_Cooper" title="James Fenimore Cooper">James Fenimore Cooper</a>, Simms was strongly influenced by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">Walter Scott</a>, and his works bore the imprint of Scott's heroic <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">romanticism</a>. In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Yemassee" title="The Yemassee">The Yemassee</a></i>, <i>The Kinsmen</i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Tom_literature" title="Anti-Tom literature">anti-<i>Uncle Tom's Cabin</i></a> novel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sword_and_the_Distaff" title="The Sword and the Distaff">The Sword and the Distaff</a>, Simms presented idealized portraits of slavery and Southern life. While popular and well regarded in South Carolina—and highly praised by such critics as <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a>—Simms never gained a large national audience.</p>
<p>In Virginia, <a href="/wiki/George_Tucker_(politician)" title="George Tucker (politician)">George Tucker</a> produced in 1824 the first fiction of Virginia colonial life with <i>The Valley of Shenandoah</i>. He followed in 1827 with one of the country's first science fictions, <i>A Voyage to the Moon: With Some Account of the Manners and Customs, Science and Philosophy, of the People of Morosofia, and Other Lunarians</i>. Tucker was the first Professor of Moral Philosophy at the University of Virginia. In 1836 Tucker published the first comprehensive biography of Thomas Jefferson - <i>The Life of Thomas Jefferson, Third President of the United States</i>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Some critics also regard Poe as a Southern author—he was raised in Richmond, attended the University of Virginia, and edited the <i><a href="/wiki/Southern_Literary_Messenger" title="Southern Literary Messenger">Southern Literary Messenger</a></i> from 1835 to 1837. Yet in his poetry and fiction Poe rarely took up distinctively Southern themes or subjects; his status as a "Southern" writer remains ambiguous.</p>
<p>In the Chesapeake region, meanwhile, antebellum authors of enduring interest include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Pendleton_Kennedy" title="John Pendleton Kennedy">John Pendleton Kennedy</a>, whose novel <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Swallow_Barn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Swallow Barn (page does not exist)">Swallow Barn</a></i> offered a colorful sketch of Virginia plantation life; and <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Beverley_Tucker" title="Nathaniel Beverley Tucker">Nathaniel Beverley Tucker</a>, whose 1836 work <i><a href="/wiki/The_Partisan_Leader" title="The Partisan Leader">The Partisan Leader</a></i> foretold the secession of the Southern states, and imagined a guerrilla war in Virginia between federal and secessionist armies.</p>
<p>Not all noteworthy Southern authors during this period were white. <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Douglass" title="Frederick Douglass">Frederick Douglass</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narrative_of_the_Life_of_Frederick_Douglass" title="Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass">Narrative</a></i> is perhaps the most famous first-person account of black slavery in the antebellum South. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harriet_Jacobs" title="Harriet Jacobs">Harriet Jacobs</a>, meanwhile, recounted her experiences in bondage in North Carolina in <a href="/wiki/Incidents_in_the_Life_of_a_Slave_Girl" title="Incidents in the Life of a Slave Girl">Incidents in the Life of a Slave Girl</a>. And another Southern-born ex-slave, <a href="/wiki/William_Wells_Brown" title="William Wells Brown">William Wells Brown</a>, wrote <i>Clotel; or, The President's Daughter</i>—widely believed to be the first novel ever published by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American" title="African-American">African-American</a>. The book depicts the life of its title character, a daughter of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a> and his black mistress, and her struggles under slavery.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_.22Lost_Cause.22_years">The "Lost Cause" years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title='Edit section: The "Lost Cause" years'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the second half of the 19th century, the South lost the Civil War and suffered through what many white southerners considered a harsh occupation (called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reconstruction_era_of_the_United_States" title="Reconstruction era of the United States">Reconstruction</a>). In place of the anti-Tom literature came poetry and novels about the "<a href="/wiki/Lost_Cause_of_the_Confederacy" title="Lost Cause of the Confederacy">Lost Cause of the Confederacy</a>." This nostalgic literature began to appear almost immediately after the war ended; <i><a href="/wiki/The_Conquered_Banner" title="The Conquered Banner">The Conquered Banner</a></i> was published on June 24, 1865. These writers idealized the defeated South and its lost culture. Prominent writers with this point of view included poets <a href="/wiki/Henry_Timrod" title="Henry Timrod">Henry Timrod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_B._Lucas" title="Daniel B. Lucas">Daniel B. Lucas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Abram_Joseph_Ryan" title="Abram Joseph Ryan">Abram Joseph Ryan</a> and fiction writer <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Nelson_Page" title="Thomas Nelson Page">Thomas Nelson Page</a>. Others, like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American" title="African-American">African-American</a> writer <a href="/wiki/Charles_W._Chesnutt" title="Charles W. Chesnutt">Charles W. Chesnutt</a>, dismissed this <a href="/wiki/Nostalgia" title="Nostalgia">nostalgia</a> by pointing out the <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a> and exploitation of blacks that happened during this time period in the South.</p>
<p>in 1856 George Tucker completed his final multivolume work in his <i>History of the United States, From Their Colonization to the End of the 26th Congress, in 1841</i>.</p>
<p>In 1884, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">Mark Twain</a> published what is arguably the most influential southern novel of the 19th century, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Huckleberry_Finn" title="The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn">The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">Ernest Hemingway</a> said of the novel, "All modern American literature comes from one book by Mark Twain called <i>Huckleberry Finn</i>." This statement applies even more to southern literature because of the novel's frank dealings with issues such as race and violence.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kate_Chopin" title="Kate Chopin">Kate Chopin</a> was another central figure in post-Civil War Southern literature. Focusing her writing largely on the Acadian/Cajun communities of Louisiana, Chopin established her literary reputation with the short story collections <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bayou_Folk&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bayou Folk (page does not exist)">Bayou Folk</a></i> (1894) and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A_Night_in_Acadie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A Night in Acadie (page does not exist)">A Night in Acadie</a></i> (1897). These stories offered not only a sociological portrait of a specific Southern culture but also furthered the legacy of the American short story as a uniquely vital and complex narrative genre. But it was with the publication of her second and final novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Awakening_(Chopin_novel)" title="The Awakening (Chopin novel)">The Awakening</a></i> (1899) that she gained notoriety of a different sort. The novel not only shocked audiences with its frank and unsentimental portrayal of female sexuality and psychology. It paved the way for the Southern novel as both a serious genre (based in the realism that had dominated the western novel since Balzac) and one that tackled the complex and untidy emotional lives of its characters. Today she is widely regarded as not only one of the most important female writers in American literature, but one of the most important chroniclers of the post-Civil War South and one of the first writers to treat the female experience with complexity and without condescension.</p>
<p>During the first half of the 20th century, the lawyer, politician, minister, orator, actor, and author <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Dixon,_Jr." title="Thomas Dixon, Jr.">Thomas Dixon, Jr.</a> wrote a number of novels, plays, sermons, and non-fiction pieces which were quite popular with the general public all over the USA. Today Dixon is perhaps best known for writing a trilogy of novels about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reconstruction_era_of_the_United_States" title="Reconstruction era of the United States">Reconstruction</a>, one of which was entitled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Clansman" title="The Clansman">The Clansman</a></i> (1905), a book which would eventually become the inspiration for <a href="/wiki/D._W._Griffith" title="D. W. Griffith">D. W. Griffith</a>'s infamous 1915 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Birth_of_a_Nation" title="The Birth of a Nation">The Birth of a Nation</a></i>. Overall Dixon wrote 22 novels, numerous plays and film scripts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Christian sermons, and some non-fiction works during his lifetime.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Southern_Renaissance">The Southern Renaissance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Southern Renaissance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Southern_Renaissance" title="Southern Renaissance">Southern Renaissance</a></div>
<p>In the 1920s and 1930s, a renaissance in Southern literature began with the appearance of writers such as <a href="/wiki/William_Faulkner" title="William Faulkner">William Faulkner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Katherine_Anne_Porter" title="Katherine Anne Porter">Katherine Anne Porter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Gordon" title="Caroline Gordon">Caroline Gordon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allen_Tate" title="Allen Tate">Allen Tate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wolfe" title="Thomas Wolfe">Thomas Wolfe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Penn_Warren" title="Robert Penn Warren">Robert Penn Warren</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Williams" title="Tennessee Williams">Tennessee Williams</a>, among others. Because of the distance the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Renaissance" title="Southern Renaissance">Southern Renaissance</a> authors had from the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slavery</a>, they were more objective in their writings about the South. During the 1920s, Southern poetry thrived under the <a href="/wiki/Vanderbilt_University" title="Vanderbilt University">Vanderbilt</a> "<a href="/wiki/Fugitives_(poets)" title="Fugitives (poets)">Fugitives</a>". In nonfiction, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H.L._Mencken" title="H.L. Mencken">H.L. Mencken</a>'s popularity increased nationwide as he shocked and astounded readers with his satiric writing highlighting the inability of the South to produce anything of cultural value. In reaction to Mencken's essay, "The Sahara of the Bozart," the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Agrarians" title="Southern Agrarians">Southern Agrarians</a> (also based mostly around Vanderbilt) called for a return to the South's agrarian past and bemoaned the rise of Southern industrialism and urbanization. They noted that creativity and industrialism were not compatible and desired the return to a lifestyle that would afford the Southerner leisure (a quality the Agrarians most felt conducive to creativity). Writers like Faulkner, who won the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize in Literature</a> in 1949, also brought new techniques such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stream_of_consciousness_writing" title="Stream of consciousness writing">stream of consciousness</a> and complex narrative techniques to their writings. For instance, his novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/As_I_Lay_Dying_(novel)" title="As I Lay Dying (novel)">As I Lay Dying</a></i> is told by changing narrators ranging from the deceased Addie to her young son.</p>
<p>The late 1930s also saw the publication of one of the best-known Southern novels, <i><a href="/wiki/Gone_with_the_Wind_(novel)" title="Gone with the Wind (novel)">Gone with the Wind</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Mitchell" title="Margaret Mitchell">Margaret Mitchell</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novel</a>, published in 1936, quickly became a bestseller. It won the 1937 Pulitzer Prize, and in 1939 an equally famous <a href="/wiki/Gone_with_the_Wind_(film)" title="Gone with the Wind (film)">movie</a> of the novel premiered. Southern literature became popular across genres; children's books like <i>Ezekiel</i>, published in 1937 by writer/illustrators like <a href="/wiki/Elvira_Garner" title="Elvira Garner">Elvira Garner</a>, drew audiences outside the South.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_World_War_II_Southern_literature">Post World War II Southern literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Post World War II Southern literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Southern literature following the Second World War grew thematically as it embraced the social and cultural changes in the South resulting from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American_Civil_Rights_Movement_(1955%E2%80%931968)" title="African-American Civil Rights Movement (1955–1968)">American Civil Rights Movement</a>. In addition, more female and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_literature" title="African American literature">African-American writers</a> began to be accepted as part of Southern literature, including African Americans such as <a href="/wiki/Zora_Neale_Hurston" title="Zora Neale Hurston">Zora Neale Hurston</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sterling_Allen_Brown" title="Sterling Allen Brown">Sterling Allen Brown</a>, along with women such as <a href="/wiki/Eudora_Welty" title="Eudora Welty">Eudora Welty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flannery_O%27Connor" title="Flannery O'Connor">Flannery O'Connor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ellen_Glasgow" title="Ellen Glasgow">Ellen Glasgow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carson_McCullers" title="Carson McCullers">Carson McCullers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Katherine_Anne_Porter" title="Katherine Anne Porter">Katherine Anne Porter</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Ann_Grau" title="Shirley Ann Grau">Shirley Ann Grau</a>, among many others. Other well-known Southern writers of this period include <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_Price" title="Reynolds Price">Reynolds Price</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Dickey" title="James Dickey">James Dickey</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Price_Fox" title="William Price Fox">William Price Fox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Davis_Grubb" title="Davis Grubb">Davis Grubb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walker_Percy" title="Walker Percy">Walker Percy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/William_Styron" title="William Styron">William Styron</a>. One of the most highly praised Southern novels of the 20th century, <i><a href="/wiki/To_Kill_a_Mockingbird" title="To Kill a Mockingbird">To Kill a Mockingbird</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Harper_Lee" title="Harper Lee">Harper Lee</a>, won the <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a> when it was published in 1960. New Orleans native and Harper Lee's friend, <a href="/wiki/Truman_Capote" title="Truman Capote">Truman Capote</a> also found great success in the middle 20th century with <i><a href="/wiki/Breakfast_at_Tiffany%27s_(novella)" title="Breakfast at Tiffany's (novella)">Breakfast at Tiffany's</a></i> and later <i><a href="/wiki/In_Cold_Blood" title="In Cold Blood">In Cold Blood</a></i>. Another famous novel of the 1960s is <i><a href="/wiki/A_Confederacy_of_Dunces" title="A Confederacy of Dunces">A Confederacy of Dunces</a></i>, written by <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> native <a href="/wiki/John_Kennedy_Toole" title="John Kennedy Toole">John Kennedy Toole</a> in the 1960s but not published until 1980. It won the <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_Fiction" title="Pulitzer Prize for Fiction">Pulitzer Prize</a> in 1981 and has since become a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cult_classic" title="Cult classic">cult classic</a>.</p>
<p>Southern poetry bloomed in the decades following the Second World War in large part thanks to the writing and efforts of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Penn_Warren" title="Robert Penn Warren">Robert Penn Warren</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Dickey" title="James Dickey">James Dickey</a>. Where earlier work primarily championed a white, agrarian past, the efforts of such poets as Dave Smith, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wright_(poet)" title="Charles Wright (poet)">Charles Wright</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ellen_Bryant_Voigt" title="Ellen Bryant Voigt">Ellen Bryant Voigt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yusef_Komunyakaa" title="Yusef Komunyakaa">Yusef Komunyakaa</a>, Jim Seay, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Stanford" title="Frank Stanford">Frank Stanford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kate_Daniels" title="Kate Daniels">Kate Daniels</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Applewhite" title="James Applewhite">James Applewhite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Betty_Adcock" title="Betty Adcock">Betty Adcock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Jones_(poet)" title="Rodney Jones (poet)">Rodney Jones</a> have opened up the subject matter and form of Southern poetry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_Southern_literature">Contemporary Southern literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Contemporary Southern literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Today, in the early twenty-first century, the American South is undergoing a number of cultural and social changes, including rapid industrialization/<a href="/wiki/Deindustrialization" title="Deindustrialization">deindustrialization</a> and an influx of immigrants. As a result, the exact definition of what constitutes southern literature is changing. Some critics specify that the previous definitions of southern literature still hold, with some of them suggesting, only somewhat in jest, that all southern literature must still contain a dead mule within its pages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Still, the successful crime novels of <a href="/wiki/James_Lee_Burke" title="James Lee Burke">James Lee Burke</a> are not ashamed of making a point of their own southernness; their nationwide popularity has been attributed to their southern appeal<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Others, though, say that the very fabric of the South has changed so much that the old assumptions about southern literature no longer hold. For example, <a href="/wiki/Truman_Capote" title="Truman Capote">Truman Capote</a>, born and raised in the <a href="/wiki/Deep_South" title="Deep South">Deep South</a>, is best known for his novel <i>In Cold Blood</i>, a piece with none of the characteristics associated with "southern writing." Other southern writers, such as popular author <a href="/wiki/John_Grisham" title="John Grisham">John Grisham</a>, rarely write about traditional southern literary issues. <a href="/wiki/John_Berendt" title="John Berendt">John Berendt</a>, who wrote the popular <i><a href="/wiki/Midnight_in_the_Garden_of_Good_and_Evil" title="Midnight in the Garden of Good and Evil">Midnight in the Garden of Good and Evil</a></i>, is not a Southerner.</p>
<p>Among today's prominent southern writers are Kathryn Stockett, <a href="/wiki/Tim_Gautreaux" title="Tim Gautreaux">Tim Gautreaux</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Gay_(author)" title="William Gay (author)">William Gay</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Joy_(author)" title="David Joy (author)">David Joy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_McDowell_(author)" title="Michael McDowell (author)">Michael McDowell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Padgett_Powell" title="Padgett Powell">Padgett Powell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pat_Conroy" title="Pat Conroy">Pat Conroy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melanie_Sumner" title="Melanie Sumner">Melanie Sumner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fannie_Flagg" title="Fannie Flagg">Fannie Flagg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Randall_Kenan" title="Randall Kenan">Randall Kenan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ernest_Gaines" title="Ernest Gaines">Ernest Gaines</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Grisham" title="John Grisham">John Grisham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Hood" title="Mary Hood">Mary Hood</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lee_Smith_(author)" title="Lee Smith (author)">Lee Smith</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morgan_Murphy_(journalist)" title="Morgan Murphy (journalist)">Morgan Murphy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Robbins" title="Tom Robbins">Tom Robbins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wolfe" title="Tom Wolfe">Tom Wolfe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wendell_Berry" title="Wendell Berry">Wendell Berry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cormac_McCarthy" title="Cormac McCarthy">Cormac McCarthy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ron_Rash" title="Ron Rash">Ron Rash</a>, Chris Offutt, <a href="/wiki/Barry_Hannah" title="Barry Hannah">Barry Hannah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anne_Rice" title="Anne Rice">Anne Rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_P._Jones" title="Edward P. Jones">Edward P. Jones</a>, Minrose Gwin, <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Kingsolver" title="Barbara Kingsolver">Barbara Kingsolver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Maron" title="Margaret Maron">Margaret Maron</a>, R.B. Morris, <a href="/wiki/Anne_Tyler" title="Anne Tyler">Anne Tyler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Larry_Brown_(author)" title="Larry Brown (author)">Larry Brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horton_Foote" title="Horton Foote">Horton Foote</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allan_Gurganus" title="Allan Gurganus">Allan Gurganus</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Singleton" title="George Singleton">George Singleton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clyde_Edgerton" title="Clyde Edgerton">Clyde Edgerton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Wallace_(author)" title="Daniel Wallace (author)">Daniel Wallace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kaye_Gibbons" title="Kaye Gibbons">Kaye Gibbons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winston_Groom" title="Winston Groom">Winston Groom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Nordan" title="Lewis Nordan">Lewis Nordan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Ford" title="Richard Ford">Richard Ford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ferrol_Sams" title="Ferrol Sams">Ferrol Sams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natasha_Trethewey" title="Natasha Trethewey">Natasha Trethewey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bobbie_Ann_Mason" title="Bobbie Ann Mason">Bobbie Ann Mason</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claudia_Emerson" title="Claudia Emerson">Claudia Emerson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dave_Smith_(poet)" title="Dave Smith (poet)">Dave Smith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olympia_Vernon" title="Olympia Vernon">Olympia Vernon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jill_McCorkle" title="Jill McCorkle">Jill McCorkle</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mik_Everett&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mik Everett (page does not exist)">Mik Everett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Hudgins" title="Andrew Hudgins">Andrew Hudgins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Manning_(poet)" title="Maurice Manning (poet)">Maurice Manning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sharyn_McCrumb" title="Sharyn McCrumb">Sharyn McCrumb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Ward_Brown" title="Mary Ward Brown">Mary Ward Brown</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jesmyn_Ward" title="Jesmyn Ward">Jesmyn Ward</a>.</p>
