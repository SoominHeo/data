<p>The <b>Culture of the Southern United States</b>, or <b>Southern Culture,</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Subculture" title="Subculture">subculture</a> of the United States. The combination of its unique history and the fact that many Southerners maintain—and even nurture—an identity separate from the rest of the country has led to its being the most studied and written-about region of the U.S.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"More than any other part of America, the South stands apart. Thousands of Northerners and foreigners have migrated to it ... but Southerners they will not become. For this is still a place where you must have either been born or have 'people' there, to feel it is your native ground. "Natives will tell you this. They are proud to be <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">Americans</a>, but they are also proud to be <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginians</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolinians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennesseeans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texans</a>. But they are conscious of another loyalty too, one that transcends the usual ties of national patriotism and state pride. It is a loyalty to a place where habits are strong and memories are long. If those memories could speak, they would tell stories of a region powerfully shaped by its history and determined to pass it on to future generations."</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Tim Jacobson, <i>Heritage of the South</i></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Southern culture has been and remains generally more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socially_conservative" title="Socially conservative">socially conservative</a> than <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_the_United_States" title="Culture of the United States">that of the rest of the country</a>. Because of the central role of agriculture in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antebellum_era" title="Antebellum era">antebellum</a> economy, society remained stratified according to land ownership, and communities often developed strong attachment to their churches as the primary community institution.</p>
<p>From its many cultural influences, the South developed its own unique customs, <a href="/wiki/Southern_literature" title="Southern literature">literature</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_US_cuisine" title="Southern US cuisine">cuisine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">music</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Southern_United_States" title="History of the Southern United States">History of the Southern United States</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_United_States" title="Slavery in the United States">Slavery in the United States</a> had a major role in shaping the South, its agricultural practices, the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Segregation_in_the_United_States" title="Segregation in the United States">segregation in the United States</a>. The presence and practices of Native Americans and the landscape also played a role in Southern culture. The climate is conducive to growing <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, and other crops, and the red clay in many areas was used for the distinctive red brick architecture of many commercial buildings.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="People">People</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: People">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the time of their conception, the predominant cultural influence on the Southern states was that of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="English colonization of the Americas">English colonists</a> who established the original English colonies in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In the 17th century, most were of Southern English origins, mostly from regions such as <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/West_Country" title="West Country">West Country</a> who settled mostly on the coastal regions of the South but pushed as far inland as the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian mountains</a> by the 18th century. In the 18th century, large groups of <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scots lowlanders</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">Northern English</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulster-Scots" title="Ulster-Scots">Ulster-Scots</a> (later called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scotch-Irish_American" title="Scotch-Irish American">Scots-Irish</a>) settled in <a href="/wiki/Appalachia" title="Appalachia">Appalachia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Piedmont_(United_States)" title="Piedmont (United States)">Piedmont</a>. Following them were larger numbers of English indentured servants from across the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Midlands" title="English Midlands">English Midlands</a> and Southern England, they would be the largest group to settle in the Southern Colonies during the colonial period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bethune.2C_Lawrence_E_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bethune.2C_Lawrence_E-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AncestryResponse_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-AncestryResponse-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webcitation.org_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-webcitation.org-8">[8]</a></sup> They were often called "<a href="/wiki/Georgia_cracker" title="Georgia cracker">crackers</a>", a term associated with the cowboys of Georgia and Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cracker_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-cracker-9">[9]</a></sup> Before the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>, the term was applied by the English, as a derogatory epithet for the non-elite settlers of the southern backcountry. This usage can be found in a passage from a letter to the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Dartmouth" title="Earl of Dartmouth">Earl of Dartmouth</a>, "I should explain ... what is meant by <a href="/wiki/Cracker_(pejorative)" title="Cracker (pejorative)">Crackers</a>; a name they have got from being great boasters; they are a lawless set of rascals on the frontiers of Virginia, the Carolinas and Georgia, who often change their places of abode."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cracker_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-cracker-9">[9]</a></sup> Most European Southerners today are of partial or majority English and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scotch-Irish_American" title="Scotch-Irish American">Scots-Irish</a> ancestry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In previous censuses, over a third of Southern responders identified as being of English or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_American" title="English American">partly English</a> ancestry<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AncestryResponse_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-AncestryResponse-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-7">[7]</a></sup> with 19,618,370 self-identifying as "English" on the 1980 census, followed by 12,709,872 identifying as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_American" title="Irish American">Irish</a>, 11,054,127 as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">Afro-American</a>, and 10,742,903 as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_American" title="German American">German</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AncestryResponse_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-AncestryResponse-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AncestryResponse2_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-AncestryResponse2-11">[11]</a></sup> It should also be noted that those who did identify themselves of German ancestry were almost exclusively found in the northern border areas of the region which are adjacent to the American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-West" title="Mid-West">Mid-West</a>. Those from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidewater_(geographic_term)" title="Tidewater (geographic term)">Tidewater</a> area of Virginia and the <a href="/wiki/Tidewater_region_of_North_Carolina" title="Tidewater region of North Carolina">Tidewater region of North Carolina</a> identified themselves almost exclusively as of English origins, while those from the Piedmont areas were a mixture of English, <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Scots_people" title="Ulster Scots people">Scotch-Irish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scottish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a> origins. South Georgia has a large Irish presence, the ancestors of whom were largely at one time <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a>; however, many were converted to various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> sects due to the lack of a missionary presence of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> in the 18th and 19th centuries. The predominance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_surnames" title="Irish surnames">Irish surnames</a> in South Georgia has been noted by American historians for some time.</p>
<p>The other primary population group in the South is made up of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">African American</a> descendants of the slaves brought into the South.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> African Americans comprise the United States' largest racial minority, accounting for 13.6 percent of the total population according to the 2010 census.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Despite <a href="/wiki/Jim_Crow_laws" title="Jim Crow laws">Jim Crow</a> era outflow to the North (see <a href="/wiki/Great_Migration_(African_American)" title="Great Migration (African American)">Great Migration (African American)</a>), the majority of the black population has remained concentrated in the southern states, and blacks have been returning to the South in large numbers since the end of formal segregation (see <a href="/wiki/New_Great_Migration" title="New Great Migration">New Great Migration</a>).</p>
<p>People of many nationalities established communities in the American South. Some examples are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_American" title="German American">German American</a> population of the <a href="/wiki/Edwards_Plateau" title="Edwards Plateau">Edwards Plateau</a> of <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, whose ancestors arrived in the region in the 1840s. German cultural influence continues to be felt in cities like <a href="/wiki/New_Braunfels,_Texas" title="New Braunfels, Texas">New Braunfels, Texas</a> near <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Antonio,_Texas" title="San Antonio, Texas">San Antonio</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Much of the population of East Texas, Louisiana and coastal Mississippi and Alabama traces its primary ancestry to French colonists of the 18th century. Also important is the French community of <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> dating back to the 1880s.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BibleBelt.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="371" data-file-width="572" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/BibleBelt.png/300px-BibleBelt.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/BibleBelt.png/450px-BibleBelt.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c2/BibleBelt.png 2x" width="300"/></a>

<p>Part of the South is known as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bible_Belt" title="Bible Belt">Bible Belt</a>", because of the prevalence there of <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">evangelical</a> Protestantism. South Florida has a large <a href="/wiki/American_Jews" title="American Jews">Jewish</a> element that migrated from New York. Immigrants from Southeast Asia and South Asia have brought Buddhism and Hinduism to the region as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Most Southerners attend church on a regular basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the colonial period and early 19th century the <a href="/wiki/First_Great_Awakening" title="First Great Awakening">First Great Awakening</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Second_Great_Awakening" title="Second Great Awakening">Second Great Awakening</a> transformed Southern religion. The evangelical religion was spread by religious revivals led by local lay Baptist ministers or itinerant Methodist ministers. They fashioned the nation's "Bible Belt."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the Revolution, the Anglican <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> was disestablished (meaning it no longer received local tax money) and was reorganized as the nationalised <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(United_States)" title="Episcopal Church (United States)">Protestant Episcopal Church of the USA</a>. The Revolution turned more people toward <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodist" title="Methodist">Methodist</a> and Baptist preachers in the South. The <a href="/wiki/Cane_Ridge_Revival" title="Cane Ridge Revival">Cane Ridge Revival</a> and subsequent "camp-meetings" on the Kentucky and Tennessee frontiers were the impetus behind the Restoration Movement. Traveling preachers used music and song to convert new members. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shape-note" title="Shape-note">Shape-note</a> singing became a fundamental part of camp meetings in frontier regions. In the early decades of the 18th century, the Baptists in the South reduced their challenge to class and race. Rather than pressing for freedom for slaves, they encouraged planters to improve treatment of them, and ultimately used the Bible to justify slavery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:First_Baptist_2.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1242" data-file-width="1656" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/dc/First_Baptist_2.JPG/220px-First_Baptist_2.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/dc/First_Baptist_2.JPG/330px-First_Baptist_2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/dc/First_Baptist_2.JPG/440px-First_Baptist_2.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<a href="/wiki/First_Baptist_Church_(Charleston,_South_Carolina)" title="First Baptist Church (Charleston, South Carolina)">First Baptist Church</a> in <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</a></div>

<p>In 1845, the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Baptist_Convention" title="Southern Baptist Convention">Southern Baptist Convention</a> separated from other regions. Baptist and Methodist churches proliferated across the Tidewater region, usually attracting common planters, artisans and workers. The wealthiest planters continued to be affiliated with the Episcopal Church.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> By the beginning of the Civil War, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baptist" title="Baptist">Baptist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodist" title="Methodist">Methodist</a> churches had attracted the most members in the South, and their churches were most numerous in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historically <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a> colonists were primarily those from Spain and France who settled in coastal areas of Florida, Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana and Texas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Today,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (March 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> there are significant <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_the_United_States" title="Catholic Church in the United States">Roman Catholic</a> populations along the coast of the Gulf of Mexico (especially the port cities of <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biloxi,_Mississippi" title="Biloxi, Mississippi">Biloxi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pensacola,_Florida" title="Pensacola, Florida">Pensacola</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mobile,_Alabama" title="Mobile, Alabama">Mobile</a>), which preserve the continuing (and broadly popularized<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Puffery" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="Unverified descriptions (March 2016)">peacock term</span></a></i>]</sup>) Catholic traditions of <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">Carnival</a> at the beginning of Lent in <a href="/wiki/Mardi_Gras" title="Mardi Gras">Mardi Gras</a> parades and related customs. Elsewhere in the region, Catholics are typically a minority and of mainly Irish, German and French or modern Hispanic ancestry.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>As of 2013, Catholics comprised 42% of the population in the New Orleans Metropolitan area based on numbers presented by the Roman Catholic Archdiocese of New Orleans.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, in comparison to some other Southern cities had a relatively small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> population prior to the 1990s. Catholics comprised 1.7% of the population in 1960, and 3.1% of the population in 1980. The population has been growing rapidly since then. The number of Catholics grew from 292,300 members in 1998 to 900,000 members in 2010, an increase of 207 percent. The population is expected to top 1 million by 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-catholic_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-catholic-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (March 2016)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> The increase is fueled by Catholics moving to Atlanta from other parts of the U.S. and the world, and from newcomers to the church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-catholic_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-catholic-19">[19]</a></sup> About 16 percent of all metropolitan Atlanta residents are Catholic, comparable to many of <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwestern</a> metropolitan areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Raleigh,_North_Carolina" title="Raleigh, North Carolina">Raleigh, North Carolina</a> also has a rapidly growing number of Catholics, with Catholicism having the largest number of affiliates out of any other religious group (11.3%) and the second largest number in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wake_County" title="Wake County">Wake County</a> (22%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Maryland, which was settled by the British, is historically <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> as well and many historians believe it was named after the <a href="/wiki/Henrietta_Maria_of_France" title="Henrietta Maria of France">Queen Henrietta Maria</a> by <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Calvert,_2nd_Baron_Baltimore" title="Cecil Calvert, 2nd Baron Baltimore">Cecilius Calvert, 2nd Baron of Baltimore</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Maryland was the only Roman Catholic British colony in the Americas, and was considered a refuge for England's Roman Catholic minority which was being persecuted by the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William of Orange</a> rose to power in England, Catholicism was outlawed in Maryland, causing a decrease in the number of practicing Catholics. In the 1840s, the Catholic population rebounded with the mass immigration of Irish due to the Great Potato Famine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Maryland also became home to many <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Polish</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_people" title="Italian people">Italian</a> immigrants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In general, the inland regions of the Deep South and Upper South, such as Arkansas, Tennessee, Mississippi and Alabama were less attractive to immigrants and have stronger concentrations of Baptists, Methodists, <a href="/wiki/Churches_of_Christ" title="Churches of Christ">Churches of Christ</a> and other Protestant or non-Catholic fellowships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Eastern and northern Texas are heavily Protestant, while the southern and western parts of the state are predominantly <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city of <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</a>, has had a significant Jewish population since the colonial period. The first were Sephardic Jews who had been living in London or on the island of Barbados. They were connected to Jewish communities in New England as well. The community figured prominently in the history of <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="History of the Jews in Charleston, South Carolina">South Carolina</a>. <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond</a> also had a Sephardic Jewish community before the Revolution. They built the first synagogue in Virginia about 1791.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> also historically (and in the present day) has a significant Jewish community.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Florida_metropolitan_area" title="South Florida metropolitan area">South Florida</a> area is home to the nation's second largest concentration of Jewish Americans outside New York, most of them early 20th century migrants and descendants from the Northeast. They were descendants of Ashkenazi Jews from Germany, Russia, Poland and other parts of Eastern Europe. Twentieth-century migration and business development have brought significant <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Jewish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Muslim</a> communities to most major business and university cities, such as <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a> and more recently, Charlotte.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Southern_dialect">Southern dialect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Southern dialect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><b>Southern American English</b> is a group of <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialects</a> of the English language spoken throughout the Southern states of the United States, from <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Gulf_Coast" title="U.S. Gulf Coast">Gulf Coast</a>, and from the mid-Atlantic coast to throughout most of <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>.</p>
<p>Southern dialects make up the largest accent group in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Southern American English can be divided into different sub-dialects, with speech differing between regions. <a href="/wiki/African_American_Vernacular_English" title="African American Vernacular English">African American Vernacular English</a> (AAVE) shares similarities with Southern dialect due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">African Americans</a>' strong historical ties to the region.</p>
<p>It has been said that Southerners are most easily distinguished from other Americans by their speech, both in terms of accent and idiom. However, there is no single "Southern Accent." Rather, Southern American English is a collection of <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialects</a> of the English spoken throughout the South. Southern American English can be divided into different sub-dialects, with speech differing between, for example, that of <a href="/wiki/Appalachia" title="Appalachia">Appalachian region</a> and the coastal "low country" around <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</a>. Folklorists in the 1920s and later argued that because of the region's isolation, Appalachian language patterns more closely mirrored <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_English" title="Early Modern English">Elizabethan English</a> than other accents in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The dialect spoken to various degrees by many African Americans, <a href="/wiki/African_American_Vernacular_English" title="African American Vernacular English">African American Vernacular English</a> (AAVE), shares many similarities with Southern dialect, unsurprising given that group's strong historical ties to the South.</p>
<p>While traces of African language remain in AAVE, there are a few distinctively African dialect groups in the South, the <a href="/wiki/Gullah" title="Gullah">Gullah</a> the most famous among them. <a href="/wiki/Gullah_language" title="Gullah language">Gullah</a> is still spoken by some African Americans in the <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina_Lowcountry" title="South Carolina Lowcountry">Low Country</a> of South Carolina, Georgia and Northeast Florida, particularly the older generation. Also called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geechee" title="Geechee">Geechee</a> in Georgia, the language and a strongly African culture developed because of the people's relative isolation in large communities, and continued importation of slaves from the same parts of Africa. As the enslaved people on large plantations were relatively undisturbed by whites, Gullah developed as a creole language, based on African forms. Similarly the people kept many African forms in religious rituals, <a href="/wiki/Foodways" title="Foodways">foodways</a> and similar transportable culture, all influenced by the new environment in the colonies. Other, less known African American dialect groups are the rural blacks of the Mississippi Basin, and Africantown near Mobile, Alabama, where the last known ship to arrive in the Americas with slaves was abandoned in 1860.</p>
<p>There are several other unique linguistic enclaves in the American South. Among them is that of Tangier Island, Virginia, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">Outer Banks</a>, which some scholars claim preserves a unique English dialect from the colonial period. The New Orleans or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yat_(New_Orleans)" title="Yat (New Orleans)">"Yat"</a> dialect is similar to Northeastern port city accents because of an influx of German and Irish immigrants similar to those of the Northeast. Many are familiar with the French-based <a href="/wiki/Cajun_French" title="Cajun French">Cajun French</a> that is spoken in the southern half of <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>.</p>
<p>Other distinct languages include <a href="/wiki/Cajun_French" title="Cajun French">Cajun French</a> (Louisiana), and <a href="/wiki/Isle%C3%B1o" title="Isleño">Isleño</a> Spanish (Louisiana, see also <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">Canarian Spanish</a>).</p>
<p>The US South also contains many indigenous languages from the Native American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muskogean" title="Muskogean">Muskogean</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caddoan" title="Caddoan">Caddoan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siouan%E2%80%93Catawban" title="Siouan–Catawban">Siouan–Catawban</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iroquoian" title="Iroquoian">Iroquoian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yuchi_language" title="Yuchi language">Yuchi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chitimacha_language" title="Chitimacha language">Chitimacha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natchez_language" title="Natchez language">Natchez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunica_language" title="Tunica language">Tunica</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adai_(Native_American_culture)" title="Adai (Native American culture)">Adai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timucua_language" title="Timucua language">Timucua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atakapa_language" title="Atakapa language">Atakapa</a> families. The historical record seems to suggest a picture of great linguistic diversity (similar to California) although most languages mentioned were not documented. Several southeastern languages have become <a href="/wiki/Extinct_language" title="Extinct language">extinct</a> and all are <a href="/wiki/Endangered_language" title="Endangered language">endangered</a>. The influence of native languages has led to distinct Indian varieties of English.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_variations">Regional variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Regional variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There continues to be debate about what constitutes the basic elements of Southern culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> This debate is influenced partly because the South is such a large region. As a result, there are a number of cultural variations among states in the region.</p>
<p>Among the variations found in Southern culture are:</p>
<ul>
<li>The lowland South was first settled by the English in the Chesapeake colonies of Virginia and Maryland, and South Carolina. This was the first area developed as plantations for cash crops of tobacco, rice and <a href="/wiki/Indigo_dye" title="Indigo dye">indigo</a>. Later, cotton, and hemp became important cash crops, as well. Planters imported large numbers of Africans as slave labor. The coastal areas were dominated by wealthy planters, who controlled local government.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Historical, political, and cultural divisions continue to divide the "upcountry" or "hill" culture of the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ozark" title="Ozark">Ozark</a> mountain regions from that of low-lying areas such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidewater_region_of_Virginia" title="Tidewater region of Virginia">Virginia Tidewater</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast" title="Gulf Coast">Gulf Coast</a>, the Low Country of South Carolina and the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Delta" title="Mississippi Delta">Mississippi Delta</a>. By contrast, farmers in the hill country cultivated land for subsistence, and few held slaves. The hill country's population has chiefly <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Scots_people" title="Ulster Scots people">Scots-Irish</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English-American" title="English-American">English</a> ancestry. Because they were chiefly <a href="/wiki/Yeoman" title="Yeoman">yeoman farmers</a>, many upland areas did not support the Confederate cause during the American Civil War (see <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Johnson" title="Andrew Johnson">Andrew Johnson</a>). Those in the hill country continued to support the Republican Party when the remainder of the white South supported Democrats.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Areas having experienced a large influx of newcomers typically have been less likely to hold onto a distinctly Southern identity and cultural influences. For this reason, urban areas during the Civil War were less likely to favor secession than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agricultural" title="Agricultural">agricultural</a> areas. Today, partly because of continuing population migration patterns between urban areas in the North and South, historically "Southern" larger urban areas, such as <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austin" title="Austin">Austin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charlotte" title="Charlotte">Charlotte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Research_Triangle" title="Research Triangle">Raleigh-Durham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Florida" title="Jacksonville, Florida">Jacksonville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>, have assimilated modern metropolitan identities distinct from their historic "Southern" heritage. However, while these metropolitan areas have had their original southern culture somewhat diluted, they nonetheless have largely preserved their distinct "Southern" identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li>Over the past half-century, numerous <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_and_Latino_Americans" title="Hispanic and Latino Americans">Latinos</a> have migrated to the American South from Latin America, most notably in the cases of Texas and Florida. Urban areas such as Atlanta, New Orleans and Nashville have seen a major increase in Latino immigrants over the past ten to fifteen years. Factory and <a href="/wiki/Agribusiness" title="Agribusiness">agribusiness</a> jobs have also attracted Mexican and Latin American workers to more rural regions of the South.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Florida">Florida</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Florida">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Florida" title="Culture of Florida">Culture of Florida</a></div>

<p>Florida is a unique state. There is a popular saying in Florida that "The North is in the South, and the South is in the North" or "The more north you go the more Southern it gets." This refers to the vast cultural difference between peoples of North and South Florida. Despite being the most geographically southeastern state, it has experienced such rapid population growth from regions outside of the traditional South that it is sometimes no longer considered to be "culturally Southern" in some areas (especially true of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Florida_metropolitan_area" title="South Florida metropolitan area">South Florida</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, this depends on the area of the state. For example, while the culturally Southern Florida Panhandle counts for only 7% of the state's population, largely non-Southern South Florida accounts for a full third of the state's population, with <a href="/wiki/Miami-Dade_County,_Florida" title="Miami-Dade County, Florida">Miami-Dade County</a> (where a majority of residents were born outside of the United States) alone having approximately twice as many residents as the entire Panhandle.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>North Floridians commonly are staunch defenders of their southern heritage,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> often noting that they were the third state after South Carolina and Mississippi to secede from the Union prior to the US Civil War and also that <a href="/wiki/Tallahassee,_Florida" title="Tallahassee, Florida">Tallahassee</a> was the only capital east of the Mississippi never captured by Union forces during the war, solidifying their placement within traditional southern culture. Florida and Oklahoma are the only two states in the Census-defined South in which the largest ancestry group is German-American, as opposed to the "American" ancestry that is most common in the South among whites; in this manner, these states more closely resemble states of the Midwest and Mountain West than they do Southern states. While <a href="/wiki/Southern_American_English" title="Southern American English">Southern American English</a> is the dominant dialect across the entire South, this accent is largely absent from South Florida unlike certain areas in the western, northern, and central regions of the state and other rural areas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The religious composition of Florida is also highly atypical for the South; Florida, Louisiana, Maryland and Texas are the only Census-defined Southern states in which Catholics outnumber Southern Baptists. Florida has the largest Jewish population by a significant margin among Census-defined Southern states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The least "Southern" part of the state is South Florida, which has been transformed by the rapid influx of Northern migrants and immigration from (especially) Latin America and the Caribbean, Europe, and Asia. Miami-Dade County is the only county in the entire United States in which a majority (51.4%) of residents were born outside of the country,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Broward_County,_Florida" title="Broward County, Florida">Broward</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palm_Beach_County,_Florida" title="Palm Beach County, Florida">Palm Beach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monroe_County,_Florida" title="Monroe County, Florida">Monroe</a> counties all having far more transplanted residents than native-born Floridians. The northern, central and rural areas are more "southern", with such areas as most of <a href="/wiki/Central_Florida" title="Central Florida">Central Florida</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Heartland" title="Florida Heartland">Florida Heartland</a> demonstrating the typical Southern culture (i.e. the South is in the North).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kentucky">Kentucky</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Kentucky">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With its northern border at the confluence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_South" title="Upper South">Upper South</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> demonstrates multiple cultural influences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> A study in the 1990s revealed that 79% of Kentuckians agreed they were living within the south. The study also showed that 84 percent of Texans, and 82 percent of Virginians believe they live in the south. Between 80–90% of residents in Mississippi, Louisiana, Alabama, Tennessee, Arkansas, Georgia and the Carolinas described themselves as southerners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> This is likely because regional identification often varies dramatically within Kentucky. For example, many consider <a href="/wiki/Northern_Kentucky" title="Northern Kentucky">northern Kentucky</a> to be the most Midwestern region as it shares culture with <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a>. Studies show that a significant minority of people in Northern Kentucky still identify with the South. Conversely, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Ohio" title="Southern Ohio">southern Ohio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Indiana" title="Southern Indiana">southern Indiana</a> are highly Southern in comparison to most of the Midwest, as is the "little Egypt" region of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Illinois" title="Southern Illinois">southern Illinois</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Some sources treat Southern Indiana as essentially the upper tip of Upland South culture while others maintain that Southern culture, while significant, is not dominant in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Louisville is viewed as culturally and economically Midwestern in some analyses, because it rapidly industrialized during the late 19th century (although not to the same extent as most northern cities), as opposed to the slow industrialization that occurred in the South.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Other observers consider Louisville to be southern culturally, due to dialect and various other aspects of culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> It is often described as both "the Gateway to the South" and "the northernmost Southern city and southernmost Northern city." Unlike the remainder of the state, Louisville, Covington, and Newport received large amounts of German immigrants due to manufacturing interests on the Ohio river, thus making the culture there somewhat distinct from the rest of the state. Had Kentucky been a free-state, prior to the Civil War, it would have likely drawn more German immigration, as there was usually a relatively small number of slaves in the areas where Germans did settle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> As of the 1980s the only counties in the United States where over half of the population cited "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_American" title="English American">English</a>" as their only ancestry group were all in the hills of eastern Kentucky (and made up virtually every county in this region).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eugene_James_Turner_1988_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eugene_James_Turner_1988-50">[50]</a></sup> In the 1980 census 1,267,079 Kentuckians out of a total population of 2,554,359 cited that they were of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_American" title="English American">English</a> ancestry making them 49 percent of the state at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>While varying degrees of southern cultural influence can be found in Kentucky inside the Cincinnati area and Louisville, smaller cities such as <a href="/wiki/Owensboro,_Kentucky" title="Owensboro, Kentucky">Owensboro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bowling_Green,_Kentucky" title="Bowling Green, Kentucky">Bowling Green</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hopkinsville,_Kentucky" title="Hopkinsville, Kentucky">Hopkinsville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paducah,_Kentucky" title="Paducah, Kentucky">Paducah</a>, together with most of the state's rural areas, have continued to be more distinctly Southern in character. Outside of those two specific areas, southern culture, dialect, mannerisms, etc. are prominent. Southern cuisine is quite common across the state. Western Kentucky is famous for a regional style of southern barbecue, and other forms of southern food such as catfish, country ham, greens beans etc. can also be found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Today most of the state, outside of Northern Kentucky, shares a cultural identity with Tennessee and the rest of the Upland South in ancestry, dialect, and various other aspects of culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ling.upenn.edu_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-ling.upenn.edu-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>In most contexts, especially culturally, the state is grouped as part of the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acoustics.org_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-acoustics.org-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Louisiana">Louisiana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Louisiana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The state was first colonized by <a href="/wiki/French_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="French colonization of the Americas">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Spain</a> rather than Great Britain, which gave it a different form of law and other distinct cultural traditions. The French, Spanish, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cajun" title="Cajun">Cajun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Creole_people" title="Louisiana Creole people">Creole</a>, African, and Caribbean-influenced culture is especially strong in the southern portion of the state. <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Gulf_Coast" title="Mississippi Gulf Coast">Coastal Mississippi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mobile,_Alabama" title="Mobile, Alabama">Mobile, Alabama</a>, were both part of early French colonial Louisiana; both areas retain strong traces of Louisiana culture which is evident in local cuisine such as gumbo and local celebrations such as <a href="/wiki/Mardi_Gras" title="Mardi Gras">Mardi Gras</a>. <a href="/wiki/Beaumont,_Texas" title="Beaumont, Texas">Beaumont, Texas</a>, was not originally a part of Louisiana but was settled by many Cajuns who relocated there in the early 20th century to take jobs in the oil fields. These areas are all considered as "culturally Louisiana."</p>
<p>In the antebellum years, a significant population of <a href="/wiki/Free_people_of_color" title="Free people of color">free people of color</a> or <a href="/wiki/Creoles_of_color" title="Creoles of color">Creoles of color</a>, as opposed to white Creoles known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Creoles" title="French Creoles">French Creoles</a>, had formed in New Orleans, in part because of the system of <a href="/wiki/Pla%C3%A7age" title="Plaçage">plaçage</a> during the colonial period. Many became educated, had their own businesses, and owned property. They formed a distinct third class between Europeans Americans and enslaved Africans, although their freedoms were reduced after the Louisiana Purchase and imposition of Americans' binary racial views.</p>
<p>Together with the cosmopolitan views of an international seaport, Roman Catholics in metropolitan New Orleans had relatively tolerant attitudes toward <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcohol_consumption" title="Alcohol consumption">alcohol consumption</a>, gambling, and prostitution in contrast to the outwardly conservative, sometimes evangelical, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> beliefs of many in the Deep South.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Carolina">North Carolina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: North Carolina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some say<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> that the most recent shift in Southern cultural influence and demographics has occurred in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>. The state's metropolitan areas, notably that of the <a href="/wiki/Research_Triangle" title="Research Triangle">Research Triangle</a>, have a more liberal tendency, while the rural piedmont, coastal and appalachian regions remain strongly conservative.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Many new residents have come<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> for work from the North and Midwest, especially from the New York City and <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a> metropolitan areas. The <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte</a> and <a href="/wiki/Raleigh,_North_Carolina" title="Raleigh, North Carolina">Raleigh</a>–<a href="/wiki/Durham,_North_Carolina" title="Durham, North Carolina">Durham</a> areas have attracted the most new residents because of economic growth: banking/finance in Charlotte; universities and high-tech in Raleigh-Durham. The <a href="/wiki/Asheville,_North_Carolina" title="Asheville, North Carolina">Asheville</a> area has attracted more retirees.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In addition to Northerners, the job markets in North Carolina's three largest metropolitan regions—Charlotte, Raleigh-Durham and the <a href="/wiki/Greensboro,_North_Carolina" title="Greensboro, North Carolina">Greensboro</a>–<a href="/wiki/Winston-Salem,_North_Carolina" title="Winston-Salem, North Carolina">Winston-Salem</a>–<a href="/wiki/High_Point,_North_Carolina" title="High Point, North Carolina">High Point</a> <a href="/wiki/Piedmont_Triad" title="Piedmont Triad">Piedmont Triad</a>—have also attracted large and growing <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_and_Latino_Americans" title="Hispanic and Latino Americans">Latino</a> and Asian American immigration and migration. A report released by the Brookings Institution in May 2006 entitled <i>Diversity Spreads Out</i>, noted that the Charlotte metro area ranked second nationally with a 49.8% growth rate in its Hispanic population between 2000 and 2004. The Raleigh-Durham metro area followed in third place with a 46.7% rate of growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Oklahoma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> classifies Oklahoma as part of the southern United States. Other sources describe it as a transition zone from the South to the West. Before being admitted as a state in 1907, Oklahoma was known as "Indian Territory." It was the site where the <a href="/wiki/Five_Civilized_Tribes" title="Five Civilized Tribes">Five Civilized Tribes</a> were relocated after having been removed from areas east of the Mississippi River, most of them from the South. The "Five Civilized Tribes" are the Seminoles, Creeks, Chickasaws, Choctaws and Cherokees. The majority of the <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> tribes in Indian Territory sided with the Confederacy during the Civil War, in part because many of them had slaveholding members. Indian Territory maintained a non-voting delegate in the Confederate Congress throughout the Civil War. Southeastern parts of Oklahoma are sometimes referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Little_Dixie_(Oklahoma)" title="Little Dixie (Oklahoma)">Little Dixie</a>" due to the strong Southern cultural ties and characteristics found there.</p>
<p>Oklahoma has the nation's largest Native American population. Its cultural heritage is both southern and western. Oklahoma is home of the <a href="/wiki/Gilcrease_Museum" title="Gilcrease Museum">Gilcrease Museum</a>, which houses the world's largest, most comprehensive collection of art of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_West" title="American West">American West</a>. This includes Native American art and artifacts and historical manuscripts, documents and maps. Oklahoma is sometimes described as being part of the "Great Southwest". Because of its geographic location, eastern Oklahoma is more connected to southern culture than areas in the western half.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Texas">Texas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Texas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In the 1980 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census" title="United States Census">United States Census</a> the largest ancestry group reported in Texas was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_American" title="English American">English</a>, with 3,083,323 Texans who identify as being of English ancestry forming roughly 27% of the population at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Their ancestry primarily goes back to the original thirteen colonies and for this reason many of them today simply claim "American" ancestry. Because of its size and unique history, particularly having once been Mexican territory, and later a nation in its own right (i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Texas" title="Republic of Texas">Republic of Texas</a>), Texas' modern-day relationship to the rest of the South is often a subject of debate and discussion. It has been described as "a Southern state, certainly, yet not completely in or of the South." The size and cultural distinctiveness of Texas prohibit easy categorization of the entire state into any recognized region. Geographic, economic and cultural diversity among regions of the state preclude treating Texas as a region in its own right. Notable extremes range from <a href="/wiki/East_Texas" title="East Texas">East Texas</a>, which is often considered an extension of the <a href="/wiki/Deep_South" title="Deep South">Deep South</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Trans-Pecos" title="Trans-Pecos">Far West Texas</a>, which is generally acknowledged to be part of the <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">interior Southwest</a>.</p>
<p>The upper <a href="/wiki/Texas_Panhandle" title="Texas Panhandle">Texas Panhandle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/South_Plains" title="South Plains">South Plains</a> areas of <a href="/wiki/West_Texas" title="West Texas">West Texas</a> do not easily fit into either category. The former has much in common both culturally and geographically with Midwestern states like Kansas and Nebraska. The South Plains, though originally settled primarily by Anglo Southerners, has become a blend of both Southern and Southwestern culture due to rapidly increasing Hispanic population.</p>
<p>The larger cities of Texas, such as <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>—with their burgeoning knowledge-based economies—have attracted migrants from other regions of the United States, particularly the Midwest and West Coast. Combined with the influence of increasing numbers from Latin America and Asia, the historic "Southern culture" has been transformed.</p>
<p>However—partly due to its membership in the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederacy</a> and history as part of the <a href="/wiki/Solid_South" title="Solid South">Solid South</a>—and the fact much of the state is in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bible_Belt" title="Bible Belt">Bible Belt</a>—it is usually considered more of a Southern than a Western state. Also, linguistic maps of Texas place most of it within the spheres of upper, mid- and Gulf- <a href="/wiki/Southern_American_English" title="Southern American English">Southern dialects</a>, helping to further identify the state as being essentially Southern (use of Southern colloquialisms such as <i>y'all</i> and <i>ain't</i> are still very much widespread in Texas).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Virginia">Virginia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Virginia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Northern_Virginia" title="Northern Virginia">Northern Virginia</a> has attracted many internal migrants coming for job opportunities with the federal government and related businesses during and after World War II, due to the emergence and expansion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northeast_Megalopolis" title="Northeast Megalopolis">Northeast Megalopolis</a>. More expansion resulted from the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a> around the start of the 21st century. Economically linked to Washington, D.C., residents of the northern part of the state tend to consider its culture more Mid-Atlantic than Southern. The rest of the state is considered more southern in culture, with its capital, Richmond, also having served as the capital of the Confederacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Overall, however, based on a study from the late 1990s, 82% of Virginians believe they live in the south and most identify more with the South than with any other region. They still uphold many traditions and beliefs of the south and take pride in their heritage.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="West_Virginia">West Virginia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: West Virginia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>West Virginia was formed during the American Civil War in 1863 from 50 western counties of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> and is currently composed of 55 counties. Many of the counties in the new state had supported Virginia and the Confederacy during the war but were included for territorial reasons, which resulted in a <a href="/wiki/Redeemers" title="Redeemers">"Redeemer"</a> government in 1876.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many legacies of its Virginia heritage remain, such as county and local place names. The state constitution is based on the antebellum constitution of Virginia. As recently as 2007 an 1849 Virginia statute was used in a county prosecution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Historic <a href="/wiki/List_of_plantations_in_West_Virginia" title="List of plantations in West Virginia">plantation houses</a> are found throughout the state, legacies of its antebellum origins. West Virginia was the last slave state admitted to the Union. The state legislature consists of a <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia_Senate" title="West Virginia Senate">senate</a> and a <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia_House_of_Delegates" title="West Virginia House of Delegates">house of delegates</a>. The state government belongs to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Governors_Association" title="Southern Governors Association">Southern Governors Association</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Legislative_Conference&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Southern Legislative Conference (page does not exist)">Southern Legislative Conference</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is the 7th most Protestant state and the 7th most religious state in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Out migration has been a steady phenomenon, beginning after the Civil War when ex-Confederates moved into southern Ohio to escape the political sanctions in their new home state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> In the 20th century out migration increased as West Virginians moved north for jobs in industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>West Virginia has a high rate of family owned farms and the state produces large numbers of poultry, corn, apples and peaches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Tobacco production peaked in 1909 at 14,400,000 pounds, and was the second most valuable crop as recently as 1983 but is no longer a popular commodity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many southern dishes are common in the state; biscuits and sausage gravy, chicken and dumplings, sweet tea, cornbread and beans and condiments such as cole slaw and chow chow accompany barbecued meats. The southern diet has been blamed for health problems such as obesity and diabetes and smoking is among the highest rates in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_English" title="Appalachian English">Southern Appalachian dialect</a> can be heard in much of the state though mostly south of <a href="/wiki/Clarksburg,_West_Virginia" title="Clarksburg, West Virginia">Clarksburg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>Country music is one of the most popular genres in the state, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WWVA_Jamboree" title="WWVA Jamboree">WWVA Jamboree</a> out of Wheeling was the second oldest venue for country music after the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Ole_Opry" title="Grand Ole Opry">Grand Ole Opry</a> in Nashville. <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_West_Virginia" title="Charleston, West Virginia">Charleston</a> is one of the highest per capita markets for country music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Some of West Virginia's notable musicians include <a href="/wiki/Little_Jimmy_Dickens" title="Little Jimmy Dickens">Little Jimmy Dickens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brad_Paisley" title="Brad Paisley">Brad Paisley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hazel_Dickens" title="Hazel Dickens">Hazel Dickens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_Sovine" title="Red Sovine">Red Sovine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawkshaw_Hawkins" title="Hawkshaw Hawkins">Hawkshaw Hawkins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molly_O%27Day" title="Molly O'Day">Molly O'Day</a> and the rockabilly musician <a href="/wiki/Hasil_Adkins" title="Hasil Adkins">Hasil Adkins</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maryland">Maryland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Maryland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Similar to other <a href="/wiki/Border_states_(American_Civil_War)" title="Border states (American Civil War)">Border States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> has regions that are culturally Southern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Prior to the second half of the 20th Century, Maryland was largely Southern. However, after the economic growth and demographic shifts of the 1970s, 1980s and 1990s, a considerable portion of Maryland's Southern culture faded. The growing service economy and ensuing southward migration of <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New Englanders</a> and more solidly <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_states" title="Mid-Atlantic states">Mid-Atlantic</a> workers transformed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I-95" title="I-95">I-95</a> corridor and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore-Washington_Metropolitan_Area" title="Baltimore-Washington Metropolitan Area">Baltimore-Washington Metropolitan Area</a> into robustly Mid-Atlantic areas. After the election of <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> the Washington region has refused to be identified as Southern leading Southern Maryland to consider secession but ultimately siding with the state.</p>
<p>Portions of Maryland, specifically <a href="/wiki/Southern_Maryland" title="Southern Maryland">Southern Maryland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Shore_of_Maryland" title="Eastern Shore of Maryland">Eastern Shore of Maryland</a> remain largely culturally Southern, driven by agriculture and commercial fishing. Most of the land is rural and there are but a few large population centers. Many local restaurants in those two areas still serve <a href="/wiki/Sweet_tea" title="Sweet tea">sweet tea</a> and dishes including or composed entirely of <a href="/wiki/Leaf_vegetable" title="Leaf vegetable">greens</a> in addition to menus heavy with fried food. Many dialectic studies show that <a href="/wiki/St._Mary%27s_County,_Maryland" title="St. Mary's County, Maryland">St. Mary's County</a> in Southern Maryland and <a href="/wiki/Dorchester_County,_Maryland" title="Dorchester County, Maryland">Dorchester County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somerset_County,_Maryland" title="Somerset County, Maryland">Somerset County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wicomico_County,_Maryland" title="Wicomico County, Maryland">Wicomico County</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Worcester_County,_Maryland" title="Worcester County, Maryland">Worcester County</a> in the Eastern Shore have <a href="/wiki/Southern_American_English" title="Southern American English">southern accents</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Western_Maryland" title="Western Maryland">Western Maryland</a> is considered Appalachian, and remains largely rural. That region is very similar to the neighboring West Virginia, Virginia, and <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beyond_the_Census-classified_South">Beyond the Census-classified South</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Beyond the Census-classified South">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Missouri">Missouri</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Missouri">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a> is classified as a Midwestern state by the Census Bureau and many of its residents. St. Louis was known as the "Gateway to the West" when settlement was expanding. The northern edge of the Ozark Plateau was settled chiefly by mid-to-late 19th century German immigrants, however, who founded numerous vineyards and wineries. Due to this, Missouri was the second-largest wine-producing state before Prohibition, which destroyed the industry. Wineries have been rebuilt since the later decades of the 20th century, and Missouri wineries are competing well in national festivals. Part of the Missouri River valley, from beyond St. Louis suburbs in <a href="/wiki/St._Charles_County,_Missouri" title="St. Charles County, Missouri">St. Charles County</a> to east of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jefferson_City" title="Jefferson City">Jefferson City</a>, is known as the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_Rhineland" title="Missouri Rhineland">Missouri Rhineland</a> because of the extensive vineyards and wineries based on German immigrant tradition and descendants.</p>
<p>In the antebellum years, many settlers from Upper South states such as Virginia and Kentucky migrated to the counties of central and western Missouri along the Missouri River, where they could cultivate tobacco and hemp. Because these southerners brought their culture and slaveholding with them, Missouri was admitted to the Union as a slaveholding state. In modern times, this area became known as <a href="/wiki/Little_Dixie_(Missouri)" title="Little Dixie (Missouri)">Little Dixie</a>. Before the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>, six of the counties included in this area had populations in which more than 25% were enslaved African Americans, the highest concentrations in the state outside the cotton plantations in the Mississippi Delta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Antebellum houses typical of the South still stand in some of Little Dixie, although for the most part all of Little Dixie is considered to be Midwestern by modern standards. All the crops grown there today are corn, soybeans and wheat, for which the area was better suited than for Southern crops like cotton, hemp or tobacco. The latter three crops ceased cultivation after the Civil War along with the dominance of Southern culture statewide. However, rural southern Missouri in the Ozark Plateau and the bootheel are still very much culturally southern.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Midwest.2C_Southwest.2C_and_West">Midwest, Southwest, and West</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Midwest, Southwest, and West">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Many areas of <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> and <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> were predominantly settled by European American southerners as they moved west in the 19th and early 20th centuries. For instance, pro-Confederate governments were established in what is now Arizona and New Mexico during the Civil War and, at one point, southern California was on the cusp of breaking away from northern California and joining the Confederacy.</p>
<p>Southerners migrated to industrial cities in the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwest</a> for work before and after World War II. They went to <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression_in_the_United_States" title="Great Depression in the United States">Great Depression</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dust_Bowl" title="Dust Bowl">Dust Bowl</a> crisis, a large influx of migrants from areas such as Oklahoma, Arkansas and the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Panhandle" title="Texas Panhandle">Texas Panhandle</a> settled in California. These "Okie" and "Arkie" migrants and their descendants remain a strong influence on the culture of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Valley_(California)" title="Central Valley (California)">Central Valley</a> of California, especially around the cities of <a href="/wiki/Bakersfield,_California" title="Bakersfield, California">Bakersfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fresno,_California" title="Fresno, California">Fresno</a>.</p>
<p>More than 6.5 million African Americans left the segregated South for the industrial cities of the Midwest and <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">West Coast</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Migration_(African_American)" title="Great Migration (African American)">Great Migration</a>, beginning in World War I and extending to 1970. Many migrants from Arkansas, Louisiana and Texas moved to California during and after World War II because of jobs in the defense industry. As a result, many African Americans as well as European Americans have "Northern" and "Southern" branches of their families. Significant parts of African-American culture, such as music, literary forms and cuisine, have been rooted in the South but have changed with urban northern and western influences, too.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuisine">Cuisine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Cuisine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cuisine_of_the_Southern_United_States" title="Cuisine of the Southern United States">Cuisine of the Southern United States</a></div>

<p>As an important feature of Southern culture, the cuisine of the South is often described as one of its most distinctive traits. Popular sayings include "Food is Love" and "If it ain't fried it ain't cooked". Southern culinary culture has readily adopted Native American influences. <a href="/wiki/Cornmeal" title="Cornmeal">Corn meal</a> cereal known as "<a href="/wiki/Grits" title="Grits">grits</a>", cornfritters, <a href="/wiki/Cornbread" title="Cornbread">cornbread</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brunswick_stew" title="Brunswick stew">brunswick stew</a> are a few of the more common examples of foods adopted directly from southeastern Indians. Nevertheless, a great many regional varieties have also developed. The variety of cuisines range from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tex-Mex_cuisine" title="Tex-Mex cuisine">Tex-Mex cuisine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cajun_cuisine" title="Cajun cuisine">Cajun</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creole_cuisine" title="Creole cuisine">Creole</a>, traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antebellum_era" title="Antebellum era">antebellum</a> fare, all types of seafood, and Texas, Carolina, Virginia (which shares strong similarities with North Carolina) and Memphis styles of <a href="/wiki/Barbecue" title="Barbecue">Barbecue</a>.</p>
<p>Traditional African American Southern food is often called <a href="/wiki/Soul_food" title="Soul food">soul food</a>. While not typically as spicy as cajun food, it incorporates a variety of herbs, flour, and can also be called stick-to-your-ribs food. Of course, most Southern cities and even some smaller towns now offer a wide variety of cuisines of other origins such as <a href="/wiki/Chinese_cuisine" title="Chinese cuisine">Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_cuisine" title="Italian cuisine">Italian</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_cuisine" title="French cuisine">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middle_Eastern_cuisine" title="Middle Eastern cuisine">Middle Eastern</a>, as well as restaurants still serving primarily Southern specialties, so-called "home cooking" establishments. Some notable "home cooking" meals include: <a href="/wiki/Fried_chicken" title="Fried chicken">fried chicken</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corn_on_the_cob" title="Corn on the cob">corn on the cob</a>, greens with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pot_liquor" title="Pot liquor">pot liquor</a>, vegetable stew, chicken and dumplings, and <a href="/wiki/Chicken_fried_steak" title="Chicken fried steak">chicken fried steak</a>.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Iced_tea" title="Iced tea">Iced tea</a> is commonly associated with the South. Specifically, <a href="/wiki/Sweet_tea" title="Sweet tea">sweet tea</a>, or brewed iced tea sweetened with granulated sugar, has traditionally been served in the South. In fact, most southern restaurants serve sweet tea in addition to "unsweet tea", whereas most northern restaurants serve only (unsweetened) iced tea.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>Many of the most popular American <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">soft drinks</a> originated in the South (<a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pepsi-Cola" title="Pepsi-Cola">Pepsi-Cola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain_Dew" title="Mountain Dew">Mountain Dew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Big_Red_(drink)" title="Big Red (drink)">Big Red</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dr_Pepper" title="Dr Pepper">Dr Pepper</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Crown_Cola" title="Royal Crown Cola">RC Cola</a>, and RC Cola's <a href="/wiki/Nehi" title="Nehi">Nehi</a> brand products). In much of Oklahoma, Tennessee, Georgia, Alabama, Texas, and other parts of the South, the term "soft drink" or "soda" is discarded in favor of "Coke" (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genericized_trademark" title="Genericized trademark">Genericized trademark</a>). Some people<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> use the term "co-cola", shortened from Coca-Cola, when ordering a soft drink.</p>
<p>Traditionally, the term "soft drink" was seldom used in the South.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Many older southerners call soft drinks "soda pop" or "soda water." In addition, there are some soft drinks available only in the South, such as <a href="/wiki/Sun_Drop" title="Sun Drop">Sun Drop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheerwine" title="Cheerwine">Cheerwine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ale-8-One" title="Ale-8-One">Ale-8-One</a> in Kentucky. <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Rock" title="Buffalo Rock">Buffalo Rock</a> ginger ale, a strong, dark ginger ale is available and popular in parts of Alabama and Georgia. Blenheim, another southern ginger ale, originated in South Carolina and is only found in the South. <a href="/wiki/Grapico" title="Grapico">Grapico</a> from Alabama is another Southern creation. In parts of Texas and Kentucky, <a href="/wiki/Big_Red_(drink)" title="Big Red (drink)">Big Red</a> is a favorite. <a href="/wiki/Lemonade" title="Lemonade">Lemonade</a> is a popular summer beverage.</p>
<p>Official support for <a href="/wiki/Prohibition" title="Prohibition">Prohibition</a> existed in the Southern states before and after the <a href="/wiki/Eighteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Eighteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">18th Amendment</a> was in force in the USA. Due to widespread restrictions on alcohol production, illegally distilled liquor or <a href="/wiki/Moonshine" title="Moonshine">moonshine</a> has long been associated (often rather stereotypically) with working class and poor people in much of the region, especially in southern <a href="/wiki/Appalachia" title="Appalachia">Appalachia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> Many<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2014)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> southern states are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Control_states" title="Control states">control states</a> that monopolize and highly regulate the distribution and sale of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverages" title="Alcoholic beverages">alcoholic beverages</a>. Many counties in the South, particularly outside of the large metropolitan areas, are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dry_counties" title="Dry counties">dry counties</a> that do not allow for alcohol sales in retail outlets. However, many dry counties still allow for "private clubs" often with low daily fees to serve alcohol on the premises.</p>
<p>There is a saying that "the South votes dry and drinks wet."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Elite" title="Elite">Elite</a> classes imported wine from Europe to enjoy, and drinking was often a major part of local festivals and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Court_days&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Court days (page does not exist)">court days</a>. Today<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup>, Texas is the center of a burgeoning wine boom, due to its climate and well drained limestone-based soils, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Hill_Country" title="Texas Hill Country">Texas Hill Country</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> is known as "the City that Care Forgot", epitomized by the saying <i>laissez les bons temps rouler</i> (let the good times roll). The Crescent City's culture revolves around food, drink, and community celebrations. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_(cocktail)" title="Hurricane (cocktail)">Hurricanes</a> are a famous <a href="/wiki/French_Quarter" title="French Quarter">French Quarter</a> drink, as are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sazerac_(cocktail)" title="Sazerac (cocktail)">sazerac cocktails</a> and <a href="/wiki/Absinthe" title="Absinthe">absinthe</a>.</p>
<p>Beer is widely popular in the South,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> though its consumption is frowned upon in some religious circles. <a href="/wiki/Lager" title="Lager">Lagers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pilsner" title="Pilsner">Pilsners</a> are generally preferred to heavier/darker beers due to the predominantly humid, warm climate. The most popular beers in the south are those produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anheuser_Busch" title="Anheuser Busch">Anheuser Busch</a>, particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Budweiser_(Anheuser-Busch)" title="Budweiser (Anheuser-Busch)">Budweiser</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Busch_Beer" title="Busch Beer">Busch</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Cartersville,_Georgia" title="Cartersville, Georgia">Cartersville, Georgia</a>, north of <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, has a massive production facility for Anheuser Busch. Regional brands such as Dixie and Jax beers of New Orleans or Pearl beer of <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio" title="San Antonio">San Antonio</a>, Texas, had a strong regional following.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The upper South, specifically <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>, is known for its production of <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_whiskey" title="Bourbon whiskey">bourbon whiskey</a>, which is a popular base for cocktails. As of 2005, Kentucky was credited with producing 95% of the world's <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_whiskey" title="Bourbon whiskey">bourbon</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> which has been referred to as America's only native spirit. The <a href="/wiki/Mint_julep" title="Mint julep">mint julep</a> is traditionally depicted as a popular beverage among more affluent Southerners. Many other bourbons are produced in Kentucky including <a href="/wiki/Evan_Williams_(whiskey)" title="Evan Williams (whiskey)">Evan Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wild_Turkey_(bourbon)" title="Wild Turkey (bourbon)">Wild Turkey</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulleit" title="Bulleit">Bulleit</a>.<a href="/wiki/Southern_Comfort" title="Southern Comfort">Southern Comfort</a> is a flavored distilled spirit modeled after bourbon and made in <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>.</p>
<p>Another form of spirit produced in the South is Tennessee Whiskey, with <a href="/wiki/Jack_Daniel%27s" title="Jack Daniel's">Jack Daniel's</a>, made in <a href="/wiki/Lynchburg,_Tennessee" title="Lynchburg, Tennessee">Lynchburg, Tennessee</a> being the number one selling whiskey in the world. <a href="/wiki/George_Dickel" title="George Dickel">George Dickel</a>, is produced in nearby <a href="/wiki/Tullahoma,_Tennessee" title="Tullahoma, Tennessee">Tullahoma, Tennessee</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Southern_literature" title="Southern literature">Southern literature</a></div>
<p>Born in the Little Dixie region of Missouri to parents who had recently emigrated from Tennessee, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">Mark Twain</a> is often placed within the pantheon of great Southern writers. Many of his works demonstrate his extensive knowledge of the Mississippi River and the South; also included in his works as a frequent theme were the injustice of slavery and the culture of Protestant public morality.</p>
<p>One of the best known southern writers of the 20th century is <a href="/wiki/William_Faulkner" title="William Faulkner">William Faulkner</a>, who won the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize in Literature</a> in 1949. Faulkner brought new techniques such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stream_of_consciousness_writing" title="Stream of consciousness writing">stream of consciousness</a> and complex techniques to American writing (such as in his novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/As_I_Lay_Dying_(novel)" title="As I Lay Dying (novel)">As I Lay Dying</a></i>). Faulkner was part of the Southern Renaissance movement.</p>
<p>The Southern Renaissance (also known as Southern Renascence)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> was the reinvigoration of American <a href="/wiki/Southern_literature" title="Southern literature">Southern literature</a> that began in the 1920s and 1930s with the appearance of writers such as Faulkner, <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Gordon" title="Caroline Gordon">Caroline Gordon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Madox_Roberts" title="Elizabeth Madox Roberts">Elizabeth Madox Roberts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Katherine_Anne_Porter" title="Katherine Anne Porter">Katherine Anne Porter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allen_Tate" title="Allen Tate">Allen Tate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Williams" title="Tennessee Williams">Tennessee Williams</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Penn_Warren" title="Robert Penn Warren">Robert Penn Warren</a>, among others.</p>
<p>The Southern Renaissance was the first mainstream movement within Southern literature to address the criticisms of Southern cultural and intellectual life that had emerged both from within the Southern literary tradition and from outsiders, most notably the satirist <a href="/wiki/H._L._Mencken" title="H. L. Mencken">H. L. Mencken</a>. In the 1920s Mencken led the attack on the genteel tradition in American literature, ridiculing the provinicialism of American intellectual life. In his 1920 essay "The Sahara of the Bozart" (a pun on a Southern pronunciation of 'beaux-arts') he singled out the South as the most provincial and intellectually barren region of the US, claiming that since the Civil War, intellectual and cultural life there had gone into terminal decline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> This created a storm of protest from within conservative circles in the South. However, many emerging Southern writers who were already highly critical of contemporary life in the South were emboldened by Mencken's essay. On the other hand, Mencken's subsequent bitter attacks on aspects of Southern culture that they valued amazed and horrified them. In response to the attacks of Mencken and his imitators, Southern writers were provoked to a reassertion of Southern uniqueness and a deeper exploration of the theme of Southern identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other well-known Southern writers include <a href="/wiki/Erskine_Caldwell" title="Erskine Caldwell">Erskine Caldwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joel_Chandler_Harris" title="Joel Chandler Harris">Joel Chandler Harris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Lanier" title="Sidney Lanier">Sidney Lanier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cleanth_Brooks" title="Cleanth Brooks">Cleanth Brooks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pat_Conroy" title="Pat Conroy">Pat Conroy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zora_Neale_Hurston" title="Zora Neale Hurston">Zora Neale Hurston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eudora_Welty" title="Eudora Welty">Eudora Welty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wolfe" title="Thomas Wolfe">Thomas Wolfe</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Styron" title="William Styron">William Styron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flannery_O%27Connor" title="Flannery O'Connor">Flannery O'Connor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carson_McCullers" title="Carson McCullers">Carson McCullers</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Dickey" title="James Dickey">James Dickey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willie_Morris" title="Willie Morris">Willie Morris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Truman_Capote" title="Truman Capote">Truman Capote</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walker_Percy" title="Walker Percy">Walker Percy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Portis" title="Charles Portis">Charles Portis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barry_Hannah" title="Barry Hannah">Barry Hannah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alice_Walker" title="Alice Walker">Alice Walker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cormac_McCarthy" title="Cormac McCarthy">Cormac McCarthy</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Grisham" title="John Grisham">John Grisham</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Agee" title="James Agee">James Agee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hunter_S._Thompson" title="Hunter S. Thompson">Hunter S. Thompson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wendell_Berry" title="Wendell Berry">Wendell Berry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bobbie_Ann_Mason" title="Bobbie Ann Mason">Bobbie Ann Mason</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Crews" title="Harry Crews">Harry Crews</a> and the authors known as the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Agrarians" title="Southern Agrarians">Southern Agrarians</a>.</p>
<p>Possibly the most famous southern novel of the 20th-century is <i><a href="/wiki/Gone_with_the_Wind_(novel)" title="Gone with the Wind (novel)">Gone with the Wind</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Mitchell" title="Margaret Mitchell">Margaret Mitchell</a>, published in 1937. Another famous southern novel, <i><a href="/wiki/To_Kill_a_Mockingbird" title="To Kill a Mockingbird">To Kill a Mockingbird</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Harper_Lee" title="Harper Lee">Harper Lee</a>, won the <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a> after it was published in 1960.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Museum_of_country_music_-_Nashville_(3934616865).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1596" data-file-width="2394" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Museum_of_country_music_-_Nashville_%283934616865%29.jpg/220px-Museum_of_country_music_-_Nashville_%283934616865%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Museum_of_country_music_-_Nashville_%283934616865%29.jpg/330px-Museum_of_country_music_-_Nashville_%283934616865%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Museum_of_country_music_-_Nashville_%283934616865%29.jpg/440px-Museum_of_country_music_-_Nashville_%283934616865%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Museum_of_country_music_-_Nashville_(3934616865).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">Country music</a> originated in the Southern United States, and the country music industry is based in <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville, Tennessee</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The musical heritage of the South was developed by both whites and blacks, both influencing each other directly and indirectly.</p>
<p>The South's musical history actually starts before the Civil War, with the songs of the African slaves and the traditional folk music brought from <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Ireland</a>. <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">Blues</a> was developed in the rural South by African Americans at the beginning of the 20th century. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Old-time_music" title="Old-time music">old-time music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel music</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negro_spirituals" title="Negro spirituals">spirituals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">rhythm and blues</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Funk" title="Funk">funk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beach_music" title="Beach music">beach music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bluegrass_music" title="Bluegrass music">bluegrass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> (including ragtime, popularized by Southerner <a href="/wiki/Scott_Joplin" title="Scott Joplin">Scott Joplin</a>), <a href="/wiki/Zydeco" title="Zydeco">zydeco</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appalachian_folk_music" title="Appalachian folk music">Appalachian folk music</a> were either born in the South or developed in the region.</p>
<p>In general, country music is based on the folk music of white Southerners, and blues and rhythm and blues is based on African American southern forms. However, whites and blacks alike have contributed to each of these genres, and there is a considerable overlap between the traditional music of blacks and whites in the South, particularly in gospel music forms. A stylish variant of country music (predominantly produced in Nashville) has been a consistent, widespread fixture of American pop since the 1950s, while insurgent forms (i.e. bluegrass) have traditionally appealed to more discerning sub-cultural and rural audiences. Blues dominated the African American music charts from the advent of modern recording until the mid-1950s, when it was supplanted by the less guttural and forlorn sounds of rock and R&amp;B. Nevertheless, unadulterated blues (along with early rock and roll) is still the subject of reverential adoration throughout much of Europe and cult popularity in isolated pockets of the United States.</p>
<p>Zydeco, <a href="/wiki/Cajun_music" title="Cajun music">Cajun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swamp_pop" title="Swamp pop">swamp pop</a>, despite having never enjoyed greater regional or mainstream popularity, still thrive throughout <a href="/wiki/French_Louisiana" title="French Louisiana">French Louisiana</a> and its peripheries, such as Southeastern Texas. These unique Louisianan styles of <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a> are celebrated as part of the traditional heritage of the people of Louisiana. Conversely, bluegrass music has acquired a sophisticated cachet and distinct identity from mainstream country music through the fusion recordings of artists like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bela_Fleck" title="Bela Fleck">Bela Fleck</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Grisman" title="David Grisman">David Grisman</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/New_Grass_Revival" title="New Grass Revival">New Grass Revival</a>; traditional bluegrass and Appalachian mountain music experienced a strong resurgence after the release of 2001's <i><a href="/wiki/O_Brother,_Where_Art_Thou%3F" title="O Brother, Where Art Thou?">O Brother, Where Art Thou?</a></i>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Elvis_Presley_promoting_Jailhouse_Rock.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3448" data-file-width="2673" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Elvis_Presley_promoting_Jailhouse_Rock.jpg/220px-Elvis_Presley_promoting_Jailhouse_Rock.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Elvis_Presley_promoting_Jailhouse_Rock.jpg/330px-Elvis_Presley_promoting_Jailhouse_Rock.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Elvis_Presley_promoting_Jailhouse_Rock.jpg/440px-Elvis_Presley_promoting_Jailhouse_Rock.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Elvis_Presley_promoting_Jailhouse_Rock.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a> is an iconic <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a> musician from the South</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Rock n' roll largely began in the South in the late 1940s and early 1950s. Early rock n' roll musicians from the South include <a href="/wiki/Buddy_Holly" title="Buddy Holly">Buddy Holly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Little_Richard" title="Little Richard">Little Richard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fats_Domino" title="Fats Domino">Fats Domino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bo_Diddley" title="Bo Diddley">Bo Diddley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ray_Charles" title="Ray Charles">Ray Charles</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Brown" title="James Brown">James Brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otis_Redding" title="Otis Redding">Otis Redding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Perkins" title="Carl Perkins">Carl Perkins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Lee_Lewis" title="Jerry Lee Lewis">Jerry Lee Lewis</a>, among many others. <a href="/wiki/Hank_Williams" title="Hank Williams">Hank Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willie_Nelson" title="Willie Nelson">Willie Nelson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waylon_Jennings" title="Waylon Jennings">Waylon Jennings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Cash" title="Johnny Cash">Johnny Cash</a>, while generally regarded as "country" singers, also had a significant role in the development of rock music, giving rise to the original "crossover" genre of <a href="/wiki/Rockabilly" title="Rockabilly">rockabilly</a>. In the 1960s, <a href="/wiki/Stax_Records" title="Stax Records">Stax Records</a> emerged as a leading competitor of Motown Records, laying thegroundwork for later stylistic innovations in the process.</p>
<p>The South has continued to produce rock music in later decades. In the 1970s, a wave of Southern rock and <a href="/wiki/Blues_rock" title="Blues rock">blues rock</a> groups, led by <a href="/wiki/The_Allman_Brothers_Band" title="The Allman Brothers Band">The Allman Brothers Band</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lynyrd_Skynyrd" title="Lynyrd Skynyrd">Lynyrd Skynyrd</a>, <a href="/wiki/ZZ_Top" title="ZZ Top">ZZ Top</a>, and <a href="/wiki/38_Special_(band)" title="38 Special (band)">38 Special</a>, became popular. <a href="/wiki/Macon,_Georgia" title="Macon, Georgia">Macon, Georgia</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Capricorn_Records" title="Capricorn Records">Capricorn Records</a> helped to spearhead the Southern rock movement, and was the original home to many of the genre's most famous groups. At the other end of the spectrum, along with the aforementioned Brown and Stax, New Orleans' <a href="/wiki/Allen_Toussaint" title="Allen Toussaint">Allen Toussaint</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Meters" title="The Meters">The Meters</a> helped to define the funk subgenre of rhythm and blues in the 1970s.</p>
<p>Many who got their start in the regional show business in the South eventually banked on mainstream national and international success as well: Elvis Presley and <a href="/wiki/Dolly_Parton" title="Dolly Parton">Dolly Parton</a> are two such examples of artists that have transcended genres.</p>
<p>Many of the roots of <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a> are often considered to come from the South as well, with bands such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R.E.M._(band)" title="R.E.M. (band)">R.E.M.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pylon_(band)" title="Pylon (band)">Pylon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_B-52s" title="The B-52s">the B-52s</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indigo_Girls" title="Indigo Girls">Indigo Girls</a> forever associated with the musically fertile college town of <a href="/wiki/Athens,_Georgia" title="Athens, Georgia">Athens, Georgia</a>. Cities such as <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Knoxville,_Tennessee" title="Knoxville, Tennessee">Knoxville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chapel_Hill,_North_Carolina" title="Chapel Hill, North Carolina">Chapel Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> also have thriving <a href="/wiki/Indie_rock" title="Indie rock">indie rock</a> and live music scenes. Austin is home to the long-running <a href="/wiki/South_by_Southwest" title="South by Southwest">South by Southwest</a> music and arts festival, while several influential independent music labels (Sugar Hill, Merge, Yep Rock and the now-defunct Mammoth Records) were founded in the Chapel Hill area. Several influential <a href="/wiki/Death_metal" title="Death metal">death metal</a> bands have recorded albums at <a href="/wiki/Morrisound_Recording" title="Morrisound Recording">Morrisound Recording</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temple_Terrace" title="Temple Terrace">Temple Terrace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> and the studio is considered an important touchstone in the genre's development.</p>
<p>There is a large underground <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">heavy metal</a> scene in the Southern United States. <a href="/wiki/Death_metal" title="Death metal">Death metal</a> can trace some of its origins to Tampa, Florida. Bands such as <a href="/wiki/Deicide_(band)" title="Deicide (band)">Deicide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morbid_Angel" title="Morbid Angel">Morbid Angel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Six_Feet_Under_(band)" title="Six Feet Under (band)">Six Feet Under</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cannibal_Corpse" title="Cannibal Corpse">Cannibal Corpse</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Volt_negative&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Volt negative (page does not exist)">Volt negative</a> among others, have come out of this scene. The Southern United States are also the place where <a href="/wiki/Sludge_metal" title="Sludge metal">sludge metal</a> was born and it's where its pioneering acts, <a href="/wiki/Eyehategod" title="Eyehategod">Eyehategod</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Eyehategod_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Eyehategod-84">[84]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crowbar_(US_band)" title="Crowbar (US band)">Crowbar</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Crowbar_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Crowbar-85">[85]</a></sup> come from;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_doom_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_doom-86">[86]</a></sup> as well as other notable bands of the style such as <a href="/wiki/Down_(band)" title="Down (band)">Down</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-Down_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-Down-87">[87]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Corrosion_of_Conformity" title="Corrosion of Conformity">Corrosion of Conformity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG-CoC_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-CoC-88">[88]</a></sup> Other well known metal bands from the South include <a href="/wiki/Pantera" title="Pantera">Pantera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hellyeah" title="Hellyeah">Hellyeah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lamb_of_God_(band)" title="Lamb of God (band)">Lamb of God</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mastodon_(band)" title="Mastodon (band)">Mastodon</a>. This has helped coin the term <a href="/wiki/Southern_rock" title="Southern rock">southern metal</a> which is well received by the vast majority in metal circles around the world. Other heavy metal and <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_punk" title="Hardcore punk">hardcore punk</a> subgenres, including <a href="/wiki/Metalcore" title="Metalcore">metalcore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Post-hardcore" title="Post-hardcore">post-hardcore</a>, have also become increasingly popular in this region.</p>
<p>Recently, the spread of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rap_music" title="Rap music">rap music</a> has led to the rise of the musical subgenre of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dirty_South_(music)" title="Dirty South (music)">Dirty South</a>. Atlanta, Houston, Memphis, Miami, and New Orleans have long been major centers of hip-hop culture.</p>

<p>While the South has <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> (NFL) franchises in <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Cowboys" title="Dallas Cowboys">Dallas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Houston_Texans" title="Houston Texans">Houston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miami_Dolphins" title="Miami Dolphins">Miami</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Falcons" title="Atlanta Falcons">Atlanta</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans_Saints" title="New Orleans Saints">New Orleans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Buccaneers" title="Tampa Bay Buccaneers">Tampa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville_Jaguars" title="Jacksonville Jaguars">Jacksonville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carolina_Panthers" title="Carolina Panthers">Charlotte</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Titans" title="Tennessee Titans">Nashville</a>; the region is noted for the intensity with which people follow <a href="/wiki/High_school_football" title="High school football">high school football</a> and <a href="/wiki/College_football" title="College football">college football</a> teams, especially the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_Conference" title="Southeastern Conference">Southeastern Conference</a> (SEC), <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Coast_Conference" title="Atlantic Coast Conference">Atlantic Coast Conference</a> (ACC), and <a href="/wiki/Big_12_Conference" title="Big 12 Conference">Big 12 Conference</a> (Big 12) and in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> where high school football, particularly in smaller communities, is a dominating activity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">Basketball</a> is also popular, particularly <a href="/wiki/College_basketball" title="College basketball">college basketball</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Duke_Blue_Devils_men%27s_basketball" title="Duke Blue Devils men's basketball">Duke Blue Devils</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Tar_Heels_men%27s_basketball" title="North Carolina Tar Heels men's basketball">North Carolina Tar Heels</a> enjoy <a href="/wiki/Carolina%E2%80%93Duke_rivalry" title="Carolina–Duke rivalry">one of the great rivalries</a> in American sports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Taken as a state, <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> has 11 national championships won by two schools, The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Louisville" title="University of Louisville">University of Louisville</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kentucky" title="University of Kentucky">University of Kentucky</a>; <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> also has 11 statewide national championships, coming from the combined victories of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_university" title="Duke university">Duke</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_at_Chapel_Hill" title="University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill">UNC</a>, and <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_State_University" title="North Carolina State University">NC State</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">National Basketball Association</a> (NBA) is well-represented in the South as well, with franchises in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Hawks" title="Atlanta Hawks">Atlanta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Hornets" title="Charlotte Hornets">Charlotte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Magic" title="Orlando Magic">Orlando</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miami_Heat" title="Miami Heat">Miami</a>, <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Grizzlies" title="Memphis Grizzlies">Memphis</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans_Pelicans" title="New Orleans Pelicans">New Orleans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Houston_Rockets" title="Houston Rockets">Houston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Mavericks" title="Dallas Mavericks">Dallas</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio_Spurs" title="San Antonio Spurs">San Antonio</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City_Thunder" title="Oklahoma City Thunder">Oklahoma City</a>.</p>
<p>Taking advantage of warmer late-winter weather, many <a href="/wiki/Professional_baseball" title="Professional baseball">professional baseball</a> teams began training in Florida in the <a href="/wiki/Spring_training" title="Spring training">spring</a>, starting in the 1920s and 15 teams continue to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grapefruit_League" title="Grapefruit League">train there each year</a>. Regular season <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> (MLB) began in 1966, when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Braves_(1953%E2%80%9365)" title="Milwaukee Braves (1953–65)">Milwaukee Braves</a> transferred its franchise to Atlanta. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expansion_teams" title="Expansion teams">Expansion teams</a> were added to Texas with the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a> and <a href="/wiki/Texas_Rangers_(baseball)" title="Texas Rangers (baseball)">Texas Rangers</a> in the 1960s and 70s, while Florida became home to the <a href="/wiki/Miami_Marlins" title="Miami Marlins">Miami Marlins</a> in 1993 and <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Rays" title="Tampa Bay Rays">Tampa Bay Rays</a> in 1998. At one time a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minor_league_baseball" title="Minor league baseball">minor league baseball</a> leagues flourished in the South. The region is still home to more minor league teams than any other region of the United States.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Normally associated with cold climates, five <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">National Hockey League</a> (NHL) franchises are based in the south: the <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Stars" title="Dallas Stars">Dallas Stars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Lightning" title="Tampa Bay Lightning">Tampa Bay Lightning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panthers" title="Florida Panthers">Florida Panthers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nashville_Predators" title="Nashville Predators">Nashville Predators</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carolina_Hurricanes" title="Carolina Hurricanes">Carolina Hurricanes</a> (six if the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Capitals" title="Washington Capitals">Washington Capitals</a> are counted as Southern).</p>

<p>The South is also the birthplace of <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a> auto racing; Shackleford says it flourishes there because "the violence and danger of the sport resonated with growing idealization of the traditional Southern culture."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> Race tracks that host NASCAR sanctioned events are found in several different locations in the South, including <a href="/wiki/Martinsville_Speedway" title="Martinsville Speedway">Martinsville, Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Talladega_Superspeedway" title="Talladega Superspeedway">Talladega, Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Motor_Speedway" title="Bristol Motor Speedway">Bristol, Tennessee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Darlington_Raceway" title="Darlington Raceway">Darlington, South Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dover_International_Speedway" title="Dover International Speedway">Dover, Delaware</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Speedway" title="Kentucky Speedway">Sparta, Kentucky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daytona_International_Speedway" title="Daytona International Speedway">Daytona, Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Motor_Speedway" title="Charlotte Motor Speedway">Charlotte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Motor_Speedway" title="Atlanta Motor Speedway">Atlanta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homestead-Miami_Speedway" title="Homestead-Miami Speedway">Miami</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richmond_International_Raceway" title="Richmond International Raceway">Richmond, Virginia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Texas_Motor_Speedway" title="Texas Motor Speedway">Fort Worth, Texas</a>.</p>
<p>Other popular sports in the South include <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a> (which can be played almost year-round because of the South's mild climate), <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soccer" title="Soccer">soccer</a> (which is the fastest growing sport in the South<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>), and <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wild_game" title="Wild game">wild game</a>. <a href="/wiki/Augusta,_Georgia" title="Augusta, Georgia">Augusta, Georgia</a>, which is the host city of <a href="/wiki/Masters_Tournament" title="Masters Tournament">The Masters</a> (one of golf's <a href="/wiki/Men%27s_major_golf_championships" title="Men's major golf championships">premier tournaments</a> held each spring) and home to 15 golf courses, is considered to be the golf capital of the world.</p>
<p>Atlanta was the host of the <a href="/wiki/1996_Summer_Olympics" title="1996 Summer Olympics">1996 Summer Olympics</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_images_of_Southerners">Popular images of Southerners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_the_Southern_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Popular images of Southerners">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the early 19th century Southerners have been the subject of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereotypes" title="Stereotypes">stereotypes</a>, epithets and ridicule. Traces remain in the media, usually in humorous form, as in the 1960s TV series, "<a href="/wiki/The_Beverly_Hillbillies" title="The Beverly Hillbillies">The Beverly Hillbillies</a>", a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Situation_comedy" title="Situation comedy">situation comedy</a>, which depicts the cultural dissonance of a poor backwoods family that moves to upscale California after striking oil on their land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anthony_A._Harkins_1971.2C_pp_98-126_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anthony_A._Harkins_1971.2C_pp_98-126-97">[97]</a></sup> Many poor Southern whites make fun of the stereotypes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> Images typically depict Southerners as laid-back, hospitable, jolly and carefree—and lazy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> The hostile epithet "<a href="/wiki/White_trash" title="White trash">white trash</a>" originated among house slaves in the 1830s to ridicule whites of low income or low morality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the colonial eras travelers often emphasized the backward, uneducated, uncouth, dirty or unhygienic, impoverished, and violent aspects of Southern life. A favorite theme especially regarding Appalachia and the Ozarks portrayed "hicks" isolated from modern culture as shiftless male hunters, violently feuding clans like the Hatfields and McCoys, degraded women smoking corncob pipes, religious snake handlers, and compulsive banjo players.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup></p>
<p>The national stereotype of the South in 1917 can be glimpsed in a study of tobacco usage in the late 19th century written by a Northern historian who paid close attention to class and gender:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>The chewing of tobacco was well-nigh universal. This habit had been widespread among the agricultural population of America both North and South before the war. Soldiers had found the quid a solace in the field and continued to revolve it in their mouths upon returning to their homes. Out of doors where his life was principally led the chewer spat upon his lands without offense to other men, and his homes and public buildings were supplied with spittoons. Brown and yellow parabolas were projected to right and left toward these receivers, but very often without the careful aim which made for cleanly living. Even the pews of fashionable churches were likely to contain these familiar conveniences. The large numbers of Southern men, and these were of the better class (officers in the Confederate army and planters, worth $20,000 or more, and barred from general amnesty) who presented themselves for the pardon of President Johnson, while they sat awaiting his pleasure in the ante-room at the White House, covered its floor with pools and rivulets of their spittle. An observant traveller in the South in 1865 said that in his belief seven-tenths of all persons above the age of twelve years, both male and female, used tobacco in some form. Women could be seen at the doors of their cabins in their bare feet, in their dirty one-piece cotton garments, their chairs tipped back, smoking pipes made of corn cobs into which were fitted reed stems or goose quills. Boys of eight or nine years of age and half-grown girls smoked. Women and girls "dipped" in their houses, on their porches, in the public parlors of hotels and in the streets.</dd>
</dl>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Era" title="Progressive Era">Progressive Era</a> brought attention to the problems the South faced. An influential scholarly study was Horace Kephart's <i>Our Southern Highlanders</i> (1913), which portrayed an isolated and culturally inert people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> The bleak image inspired northern philanthropy, such as the Rockefeller foundations, to intervene using modern public health techniques and to promote better schooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the 1930s, however, Hollywood has used stereotypes of the South to contrast virtues of simple rural life with the corruption that can be found in the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anthony_A._Harkins_1971.2C_pp_98-126_97-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anthony_A._Harkins_1971.2C_pp_98-126-97">[97]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup></p>
<p>Comic strips dealt with northern urban experiences until 1934, when <a href="/wiki/Al_Capp" title="Al Capp">Al Capp</a> introduced "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%27il_Abner" title="L'il Abner">L'il Abner</a>", the first strip based in the South. Although Capp was from Connecticut, he spent 43 years teaching the world about Dogpatch, reaching 60 million readers in over 900 American newspapers and 100 foreign papers in 28 countries. Inge says Capp, "had a profound influence on the way the world viewed the American South." Other popular strips on Southern life included "Pogo", "Snuffy Smith" and "Kudzu."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> Cultural historian Anthony Harkins argues that Dogpatch's hillbilly setting "remained a central touchstone, serving both as a microcosm and a distorting carnival mirror of broader American society."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup></p>

<p>Collections of Southern art can be found at the <a href="/wiki/Ogden_Museum_of_Southern_Art" title="Ogden Museum of Southern Art">Ogden Museum of Southern Art</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Morris_Museum_of_Art" title="Morris Museum of Art">Morris Museum of Art</a> in <a href="/wiki/Augusta,_Georgia" title="Augusta, Georgia">Augusta</a>. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_expressionism&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Southern expressionism (page does not exist)">Southern expressionism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Folk_art" title="Folk art">folk art</a> are types of art generally considered to be part of Southern art. The <a href="/wiki/Southern_Arts_Federation" title="Southern Arts Federation">Southern Arts Federation</a> maintains a registry of contemporary Southern artists (including visual artists, performing artists, media artists and writers) who have been recognized by their state <a href="/wiki/Arts_council" title="Arts council">arts councils</a> based on the outstanding.</p>
<p>Some famous folk artists from the American South include <a href="/wiki/Clementine_Hunter" title="Clementine Hunter">Clementine Hunter</a> (Natchitoches, Louisiana) and <a href="/wiki/Howard_Finster" title="Howard Finster">Howard Finster</a> (Summerville Georgia) who mixed southern spirituality and traditional religious motifs with surrealism and dream-like post-modernism. Finster's work was featured on album covers by bands such as <a href="/wiki/Talking_Heads" title="Talking Heads">Talking Heads</a> (Little Creatures, 1985) and R.E.M. (Reckoning, 1984). He has a permanent display at the High Museum of Art in Atlanta and his Paradise Gardens is still open to the public almost ten years after his death.</p>
<p>Chris Flesher (Tennessee) has sold folk art as pieces and as concepts all over the world and has a collection at the <a href="/wiki/American_Folk_Art_Museum" title="American Folk Art Museum">American Folk Art Museum</a> in New York City as well as in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carmel-by-the-Sea" title="Carmel-by-the-Sea">Carmel</a>, California. The influence of his art is mainly centered around the enchanting and beautiful landscape of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Smoky_Mountains" title="Great Smoky Mountains">Great Smoky Mountains</a> of Eastern Tennessee and Western North Carolina.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Walter_Inglis_Anderson" title="Walter Inglis Anderson">Walter Inglis Anderson</a> and his art is forever associated with the natural beauty of the Mississippi Gulf Coast and many of his family members continue as artists there still today.</p>
<p>Pop artist <a href="/wiki/Jasper_Johns" title="Jasper Johns">Jasper Johns</a> and African-American modernist <a href="/wiki/Romare_Bearden" title="Romare Bearden">Romare Bearden</a> are two other prominent artists from the South: Johns was a native of <a href="/wiki/Augusta,_Georgia" title="Augusta, Georgia">Augusta, Georgia</a>, while Bearden was born in <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte, North Carolina</a>.</p>
<p>A major center of American modernism was located at the <a href="/wiki/Black_Mountain_College" title="Black Mountain College">Black Mountain College</a> in the town of <a href="/wiki/Black_Mountain,_North_Carolina" title="Black Mountain, North Carolina">Black Mountain, North Carolina</a>. The history of the college – which attracted John Cage, Walter Gropius, Buckminster Fuller, Merce Cunningham, Willem de Kooning and other pioneers of varied mid-20th century arts – has been extensively detailed in several books and studies (notably, Mary Emma Harris' <i>Arts At Black Mountain College</i> and Vincent Katz' <i>Black Mountain College: Experiment In Art</i>). The school, which operated as an interdisciplinary, progressive institution for 23 years, was a key incubator for the American artistic avant-garde of the 1960s and beyond.</p>
