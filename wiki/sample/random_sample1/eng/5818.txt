<p><i><b>United States v. Comstock</b></i>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_Supreme_Court_cases,_volume_560" title="List of United States Supreme Court cases, volume 560">560</a> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Reports" title="United States Reports">U.S.</a> 126 (2010), was a decision by the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court of the United States</a>, which held that the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">federal government</a> has authority under the <a href="/wiki/Necessary_and_Proper_Clause" title="Necessary and Proper Clause">Necessary and Proper Clause</a> to require the <a href="/wiki/Involuntary_commitment" title="Involuntary commitment">civil commitment</a> of individuals already in Federal custody.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The practice, introduced by the <a href="/wiki/Adam_Walsh_Child_Protection_and_Safety_Act" title="Adam Walsh Child Protection and Safety Act">Adam Walsh Child Protection and Safety Act</a>, was upheld against a challenge that it fell outside the <a href="/wiki/Enumerated_powers" title="Enumerated powers">enumerated powers</a> granted to <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">Constitution</a>. The decision did not rule on any other aspect of the law's constitutionality, because only the particular issue of Congressional authority was properly before the Court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scotuspdf_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-scotuspdf-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_v._Comstock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Six days before Graydon Earl Comstock was to have completed a 37-month sentence for receiving <a href="/wiki/Child_pornography" title="Child pornography">child pornography</a>, Attorney General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alberto_R._Gonzales" title="Alberto R. Gonzales">Alberto R. Gonzales</a> certified that Comstock was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexually_dangerous_person" title="Sexually dangerous person">sexually dangerous person</a>. The law that Attorney General Gonzales was applying was ruled unconstitutional by lower courts on the grounds it exceeded Congress’s constitutional authority. Argued in January 2010 by <a href="/wiki/Solicitor_General_of_the_United_States" title="Solicitor General of the United States">Solicitor General</a> <a href="/wiki/Elena_Kagan" title="Elena Kagan">Elena Kagan</a>, the United States' position was that the "necessary and proper" clause gave Congress the power to enact the law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opinion_of_the_Court">Opinion of the Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_v._Comstock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Opinion of the Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stephen_Breyer" title="Stephen Breyer">Stephen Breyer</a> delivered the opinion of the Court, that the "necessary and proper" clause permitted Congress to enact such a provision. John Roberts, and John Paul Stevens, Ruth Bader Ginsburg, and Sonia Sotomayor joined Breyer's opinion. Anthony Kennedy and Samuel Alito filed opinions concurring in the judgment. Clarence Thomas filed a dissenting opinion in which Antonin Scalia joined in all but Part III– A–1–b.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scotuspdf_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-scotuspdf-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Five_considerations">Five considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_v._Comstock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Five considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Court said: "We base this conclusion on five considerations, taken together." The five considerations are as follows.</p>
<ul>
<li>First, the Necessary and Proper Clause grants Congress broad power to enact laws that are "rationally related" and "reasonably adapted" to executing the other enumerated powers.</li>
<li>Second, the statute at issue "constitutes a modest addition" to related statutes that have existed for many decades.</li>
<li>Third, the statute in question reasonably extends longstanding policy.</li>
<li>Fourth, the statute properly accounts for state interests, by ending the federal government's role "with respect to an individual covered by the statute" whenever a state requests.</li>
<li>Fifth, the statute is narrowly tailored to only address the legitimate federal interest.</li>
</ul>
