<p>The <b>United States Department of Justice</b> (<b>DOJ</b>), also known as the <b>Justice Department</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/United_States_federal_executive_departments" title="United States federal executive departments">federal executive department</a> of the U.S. government, responsible for the enforcement of the <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Administration_of_justice" title="Administration of justice">administration of justice</a> in the United States, equivalent to the <a href="/wiki/Justice_ministry" title="Justice ministry">justice</a> or <a href="/wiki/Interior_ministry" title="Interior ministry">interior ministries</a> of other countries.</p>
<p>The Department is headed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Attorney_General" title="United States Attorney General">United States Attorney General</a>, who is nominated by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> and <a href="/wiki/Advice_and_consent" title="Advice and consent">confirmed</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a> and is a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Cabinet" title="United States Cabinet">Cabinet</a>. The current Acting Attorney General is <a href="/wiki/Sally_Yates" title="Sally Yates">Sally Yates</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Justice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The U.S. Attorney General was initially a one-person, part-time job. It was established by the <a href="/wiki/Judiciary_Act_of_1789" title="Judiciary Act of 1789">Judiciary Act of 1789</a>, but this grew with the <a href="/wiki/Bureaucracy" title="Bureaucracy">bureaucracy</a>. At one time the Attorney General gave legal advice to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congress_of_the_United_States" title="Congress of the United States">U.S. Congress</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a>, but this Congressional advice-giving had stopped by 1819 on account of the workload involved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About_USDOJ_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-About_USDOJ-4">[4]</a></sup> To supplement their salaries, which until March 3, 1853, were set by statute at less than the amounts paid to other members of the Cabinet, early Attorneys General, while in office, engaged in extensive private practice of law, often arguing cases before the courts in their private capacities, as attorneys for private (paying) litigants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following unsuccessful efforts (in 1830 and 1846) to put the Attorney General's Office on a full-time footing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> in 1869, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._House_Committee_on_the_Judiciary" title="U.S. House Committee on the Judiciary">U.S. House Committee on the Judiciary</a>, led by Congressman <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Lawrence_(Ohio)" title="William Lawrence (Ohio)">William Lawrence</a>, conducted an inquiry into the creation of a "Law department" headed by the Attorney General and also composed of the various department solicitors and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_attorney" title="United States attorney">United States attorneys</a>. On February 19, 1868, Lawrence introduced a bill in Congress to create the Department of Justice.</p>
<p>President <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_S._Grant" title="Ulysses S. Grant">Ulysses S. Grant</a> then signed the bill into law on June 22, 1870.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The Department of Justice officially began operations on July 1, 1870.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About_USDOJ_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-About_USDOJ-4">[4]</a></sup> Just prior to the Civil War, in February 1861, the Confederate States of America established a Department of Justice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Though "[t]he second American department of justice was brought into being on July 1, 1870, fifty years of amendments were required before it reached a status comparable to that of its Confederate predecessor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The "Act to Establish the Department of Justice" drastically increased the Attorney General's responsibilities to include the supervision of all United States Attorneys, formerly under the Department of the Interior, the prosecution of all federal crimes, and the representation of the United States in all court actions, barring the use of private attorneys by the federal government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The law did create a new office, that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Solicitor_General" title="United States Solicitor General">Solicitor General</a>, to supervise and conduct government litigation in the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court of the United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the passage of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_Commerce_Act" title="Interstate Commerce Act">Interstate Commerce Act</a> in 1887, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">federal government</a> began to take on some law enforcement responsibilities, with the Department of Justice tasked to carry out these duties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-langeluttig-p9_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-langeluttig-p9-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1884, control of federal prisons was transferred to the new department, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Interior" title="United States Department of Interior">Department of Interior</a>. New facilities were built, including the penitentiary at <a href="/wiki/United_States_Penitentiary,_Leavenworth" title="United States Penitentiary, Leavenworth">Leavenworth</a> in 1895, and a facility for women located in <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alderson_Federal_Prison_Camp" title="Alderson Federal Prison Camp">Alderson</a> was established in 1924.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-langeluttig-p14_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-langeluttig-p14-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1933, President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> issued an executive order which conveyed, to the Department of Justice, the responsibility for the "functions of prosecuting in the courts of the United States claims and demands by, and offsenses [<i>sic</i>] against, the Government of the United States, and of defending claims and demands against the Government, and of supervising the work of United States attorneys, marshals, and clerks in connection therewith, now exercised by any agency or officer....".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Headquarters">Headquarters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Justice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Headquarters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy_Department_of_Justice_Building" title="Robert F. Kennedy Department of Justice Building">Robert F. Kennedy Department of Justice Building</a></div>
<p>The U.S. Department of Justice building was completed in 1935 from a design by <a href="/wiki/Milton_Bennett_Medary" title="Milton Bennett Medary">Milton Bennett Medary</a>. Upon Medary's death in 1929, the other partners of his Philadelphia firm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zantzinger,_Borie_and_Medary" title="Zantzinger, Borie and Medary">Zantzinger, Borie and Medary</a> took over the project. On a lot bordered by Constitution and Pennsylvania Avenues and Ninth and Tenth Streets, Northwest, it holds over one million square feet of space. The sculptor <a href="/wiki/C._Paul_Jennewein" title="C. Paul Jennewein">C. Paul Jennewein</a> served as overall design consultant for the entire building, contributing more than 50 separate sculptural elements inside and outside.</p>
<p>Various efforts, none entirely successful, have been made to determine the meaning of the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> motto appearing on the Department of Justice seal, <i>Qui Pro Domina Justitia Sequitur</i>. It is not even known exactly when the original version of the DOJ seal itself was adopted, or when the motto first appeared on the seal. The most authoritative opinion of the DOJ suggests that the motto refers to the Attorney General (and thus, by extension, to the Department of Justice) "who prosecutes on behalf of justice (or the Lady Justice)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The motto's conception of the prosecutor (or government attorney) as being the servant of justice itself finds concrete expression in a similarly-ordered English-language inscription ("THE UNITED STATES WINS ITS POINT WHENEVER JUSTICE IS DONE ITS CITIZENS IN THE COURTS") in the above-door paneling in the ceremonial rotunda anteroom just outside the Attorney General's office in the Department of Justice Main Building in Washington, D.C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The building was renamed in honor of former Attorney General <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a> in 2001. It is sometimes referred to as "Main Justice."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Law_enforcement_agencies">Law enforcement agencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Justice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Law enforcement agencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several <a href="/wiki/Federal_law_enforcement_in_the_United_States" title="Federal law enforcement in the United States">federal law enforcement agencies</a> are administered by the Department of Justice:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/United_States_Marshals_Service" title="United States Marshals Service">United States Marshals Service</a> (USMS) – The office of U.S. Marshal was established by the <a href="/wiki/Judiciary_Act_of_1789" title="Judiciary Act of 1789">Judiciary Act of 1789</a>. The U.S. Marshals Service was established as an agency in 1969, and it was elevated to full bureau status under the Justice Department in 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">Federal Bureau of Investigation</a> (FBI) – On July 26, 1908, a small investigative force was created within the Justice Department under Attorney General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Bonaparte_(Attorney_General)" title="Charles Bonaparte (Attorney General)">Charles Bonaparte</a>. The following year, this force was officially named the Bureau of Investigation by Attorney General <a href="/wiki/George_W._Wickersham" title="George W. Wickersham">George W. Wickersham</a>. In 1935, the bureau adopted its current name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Prisons" title="Federal Bureau of Prisons">Federal Bureau of Prisons</a> (BOP) – the Three Prisons Act of 1891 created the federal prison system. Congress created the Federal Bureau of Prisons in 1930 by Pub. L. No. 71–218, 46 Stat. 325, signed into law by President Hoover on May 14, 1930.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Corrections" title="National Institute of Corrections">National Institute of Corrections</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Alcohol,_Tobacco,_Firearms_and_Explosives" title="Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives">Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives</a> (ATF) – Except for a brief period during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prohibition_era" title="Prohibition era">Prohibition</a>, ATF's predecessor bureaus were part of the Department of the Treasury for more than two hundred years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATFTransfer_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-ATFTransfer-34">[34]</a></sup> ATF was first established by Department of Treasury Order No. 221, effective July 1, 1972; this order "transferred the functions, powers, and duties arising under laws relating to alcohol, tobacco, firearms, and explosives from the Internal Revenue Service to ATF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In 2003, under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Homeland_Security_Act" title="Homeland Security Act">Homeland Security Act</a>, ATF was split into two agencies – the new Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives (ATF) was transferred to the Department of Justice, while the <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_and_Tobacco_Tax_and_Trade_Bureau" title="Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau">Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau</a> (TTB) was retained by the Department of the Treasury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATFTransfer_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-ATFTransfer-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Drug_Enforcement_Administration" title="Drug Enforcement Administration">Drug Enforcement Administration</a> (DEA)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice_Office_of_the_Inspector_General" title="United States Department of Justice Office of the Inspector General">Office of the Inspector General</a> (OIG)</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_offices_and_programs">Other offices and programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Justice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other offices and programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In March 2003, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Immigration_and_Naturalization_Service" title="United States Immigration and Naturalization Service">United States Immigration and Naturalization Service</a> was abolished and its functions transferred to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Homeland_Security" title="United States Department of Homeland Security">United States Department of Homeland Security</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Executive_Office_for_Immigration_Review" title="Executive Office for Immigration Review">Executive Office for Immigration Review</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Immigration_Appeals" title="Board of Immigration Appeals">Board of Immigration Appeals</a>, which review decisions made by government officials under Immigration and Nationality law, remain under jurisdiction of the Department of Justice. Similarly the Office of Domestic Preparedness left the Justice Department for the Department of Homeland Security, but only for executive purposes. The Office of Domestic Preparedness is still centralized within the Department of Justice, since its personnel are still officially employed within the Department of Justice.</p>
<p>In 2003, the Department of Justice created LifeAndLiberty.gov, a website that supported the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USA_PATRIOT_Act" title="USA PATRIOT Act">USA PATRIOT Act</a>. It was criticized by government watchdog groups for its alleged violation of U.S. Code Title 18 Section 1913, which forbids money appropriated by Congress to be used to lobby in favor of any law, actual or proposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dotgovwatch_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-dotgovwatch-37">[37]</a></sup> The website has since been taken offline.</p>
