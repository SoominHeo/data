<p>The <b>United States Department of Veterans Affairs</b> (<b>VA</b> or <b>DVA</b>) is a government-run <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> <a href="/wiki/Veteran" title="Veteran">veteran</a> benefit system with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Cabinet" title="United States Cabinet">Cabinet</a>-level status. While veterans benefits have been provided since the Revolutionary War, the Veterans Administration itself was founded in 1930, and became the cabinet-level Department of Veterans Affairs in 1989.</p>
<p>With a total 2009 budget of about $87.6 billion, VA employs nearly 345,000 people at hundreds of Veterans Affairs medical facilities, clinics, and benefits offices and is responsible for administering programs of veterans’ benefits for veterans, their families, and survivors. In 2012, the proposed budget for VA was $132 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> VA 2014 budget request for 2014 was $152.7 billion. This included $66.5 billion in discretionary resources and $86.1 billion in mandatory funding. The discretionary budget request represented an increase of $2.7 billion, or 4.3 percent, over the 2013 enacted level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is administered by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Veterans_Affairs" title="United States Secretary of Veterans Affairs">United States Secretary of Veterans Affairs</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continental_Congress">Continental Congress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Continental Congress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Continental_Congress" title="Continental Congress">Continental Congress</a> of 1776 encouraged enlistments during the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a> by providing pensions for soldiers who were disabled. Direct medical and hospital care given to veterans in the early days of the U.S. was provided by the individual states and communities. In 1811, the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Naval_Asylum" title="Philadelphia Naval Asylum">first domiciliary and medical facility</a> for veterans was authorized by the federal government, but not opened until 1834. In the 19th century, the nation's veterans assistance program was expanded to include benefits and pensions not only for veterans, but also their widows and dependents.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_American_Civil_War">After the American Civil War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: After the American Civil War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the end of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> in 1865, many state <a href="/wiki/Old_soldiers%27_home" title="Old soldiers' home">veterans' homes</a> were established. Since domiciliary care was available at all state veterans homes, incidental medical and hospital treatment was provided for all injuries and diseases, whether or not of service origin. Indigent and disabled veterans of the Civil War, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Wars" title="Indian Wars">Indian Wars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a>, and Mexican Border period as well as discharged regular members of the Armed Forces were cared for at these homes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_I">World War I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: World War I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Congress established a new system of veterans benefits when the United States entered <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> in 1917. Included were programs for disability compensation, insurance for servicepersons and veterans, and vocational rehabilitation for the disabled. By the 1920s, the various benefits were administered by three different federal agencies: the Veterans Bureau, the Bureau of Pensions of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior" title="United States Department of the Interior">Interior Department</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/National_Home_for_Disabled_Volunteer_Soldiers" title="National Home for Disabled Volunteer Soldiers">National Home for Disabled Volunteer Soldiers</a>.</p>
<p>The establishment of the Veterans Administration came in 1930 when Congress authorized the president to "consolidate and coordinate Government activities affecting war veterans". The three component agencies became bureaus within the Veterans Administration. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brigadier_General_(United_States)" title="Brigadier General (United States)">Brigadier General</a> <a href="/wiki/Frank_T._Hines" title="Frank T. Hines">Frank T. Hines</a>, who directed the Veterans Bureau for seven years, was named as the first <a href="/wiki/Administrator_of_Veterans_Affairs" title="Administrator of Veterans Affairs">Administrator of Veterans Affairs</a>, a job he held until 1945.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The close of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> resulted in not only a vast increase in the veteran population, but also a large number of new benefits enacted by Congress for veterans of the war. In addition, during the late 1940s, the VA had to contend with aging World War I veterans. During that time, "the clientele of the VA increased almost fivefold with an addition of nearly 15,000,000 World War II veterans and approximately 4,000,000 World War I veterans".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Prior to World War II, in response to scandals at the Veterans Bureau, programs that cared for veterans were centralized in Washington, D.C. This centralization caused delays and bottlenecks as the agency tried to serve the World War II veterans. As a result, the VA went through a decentralization process, giving more authority to the field offices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/G.I._Bill" title="G.I. Bill">World War II GI Bill</a> was signed into law on June 22, 1944, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/President_Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="President Franklin D. Roosevelt">President Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>"The United States government began serious consolidated services to veterans in 1930. The GI Bill of Rights, which was passed in 1944, had more effect on the American way of life than any other legislation - with the possible exception of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homestead_Act" title="Homestead Act">Homestead Act</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The VA health care system has grown from 54 hospitals in 1930 to include 153 medical centers; more than 700 outpatient, community, and outreach clinics; 126 nursing home care units; and 35 domiciliaries. VA health care facilities provide a broad spectrum of medical, surgical, and rehabilitative care. The responsibilities and benefits programs of the Veterans Administration grew enormously during the following six decades.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Korean_War_through_2001">Korean War through 2001</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Korean War through 2001">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Further educational assistance acts were passed for the benefit of veterans of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_Era" title="Vietnam Era">Vietnam Era</a>, the introduction of an "all-volunteer force" in the 1970s (following the end of conscription in the United States in 1973), the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Persian Gulf War</a>, and those who served following the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">attacks of September 11, 2001</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Veterans_Affairs_Act" title="Department of Veterans Affairs Act">Department of Veterans Affairs Act</a> of 1988 (<a class="external text" href="http://legislink.org/us/pl-100-527" rel="nofollow">Pub.L. 100–527</a>) changed the former Veterans Administration, an independent government agency established in 1930, primarily to see to the needs of World War I veterans, into a Cabinet-level Department of Veterans Affairs. It was signed into law by President Ronald Reagan on October 25, 1988, but actually came into effect under the term of his successor, George H. W. Bush, on March 15, 1989.</p>
<p>The Department of Veterans Affairs was created due to nearly one third of the population being eligible for veterans benefits. Its proponents argued that due to the large number of Americans affected by the VA, it needed an administrator who had direct access to the president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In their major reform period of 1995–2000, the <a href="/wiki/Veterans_Health_Administration" title="Veterans Health Administration">Veterans Health Administration</a> (VHA) implemented universal primary care, closed 55% of their acute care hospital beds, increased patients treated by 24%, had a 48% increase in ambulatory care visits, and decreased staffing by 12%. By 2000, the VHA had 10,000 fewer employees than in 1995 and a 104% increase in patients treated since 1995, and had managed to maintain the same cost per patient-day, while all other facilities' costs had risen over 30% to 40% during the same period.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Veterans_Health_Administration_scandal_of_2014">Veterans Health Administration scandal of 2014</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Veterans Health Administration scandal of 2014">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Veterans_Health_Administration_scandal_of_2014" title="Veterans Health Administration scandal of 2014">Veterans Health Administration scandal of 2014</a></div>
<p>In May 2014, major problems with scheduling timely access to medical care became public. As of May 2014, at least 40 United States Armed Forces veterans died waiting for care at the Phoenix, Arizona Veterans Health Administration facilities, and an investigation of delays in treatment throughout the Veterans Health Administration system is being conducted by the Veterans Affairs Inspector General.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> On May 30, 2014, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary_of_Veterans_Affairs" title="Secretary of Veterans Affairs">Secretary of Veterans Affairs</a> <a href="/wiki/Eric_Shinseki" title="Eric Shinseki">Eric Shinseki</a> resigned from office due to the fallout from the scandal,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> saying he couldn't explain the lack of integrity among some leaders in VA healthcare facilities. "That breach of integrity is irresponsible, it is indefensible, and unacceptable to me. I said when this situation began weeks to months ago that I thought the problem was limited and isolated because I believed that. I no longer believe it. It is systemic. I was too trusting of some and I accepted as accurate reports that I now know to have been misleading with regard to patient wait times," Shinseki said in a statement. He said he could not defend what happened because it was indefensible, but he could take responsibility for it and he would.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShinsekiStatement_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ShinsekiStatement-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions">Functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The VA's primary function is to support veterans in their time after service by providing benefits and support. A current initiative in the Department is to prevent and end veterans' homelessness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The VA works with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Interagency_Council_on_Homelessness" title="United States Interagency Council on Homelessness">United States Interagency Council on Homelessness</a> to address these issues. The Secretary sits on the Council and is committed to ending veterans' homelessness by 2015 as laid out in <i>Opening Doors: Federal Strategic Plan to Prevent and End Homelessness</i>, released in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Department of Veterans Affairs is headed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary_of_Veterans_Affairs" title="Secretary of Veterans Affairs">Secretary of Veterans Affairs</a>, appointed by the President with the advice and consent of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a>.</p>
<p>The current Secretary of Veterans Affairs is <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._McDonald" title="Robert A. McDonald">Robert A. McDonald</a>. The third listed executive on the VA's official web site is its Chief of Staff (currently <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jose_D._Riojas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jose D. Riojas (page does not exist)">Jose D. Riojas</a>); the Chief of Staff position does not require Senate confirmation. In addition to Secretary and Deputy Secretary, the VA has at least nine more <a href="/wiki/List_of_positions_filled_by_presidential_appointment_with_Senate_confirmation#Committee_on_Veterans.27_Affairs" title="List of positions filled by presidential appointment with Senate confirmation">positions requiring presidential appointment and Senate approval</a>.</p>
<p>The Department has three main subdivisions, known as Administrations, each headed by an Undersecretary:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Veterans_Health_Administration" title="Veterans Health Administration">Veterans Health Administration</a> (VHA): responsible for providing health care in all its forms, as well as for biomedical research (under the <a href="/wiki/Veterans_Health_Administration_Office_of_Research_and_Development" title="Veterans Health Administration Office of Research and Development">Office of Research and Development</a>), Community Based Outpatient Clinics (CBOCs), and Regional Medical Centers</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Veterans_Benefits_Administration" title="Veterans Benefits Administration">Veterans Benefits Administration</a> (VBA): responsible for initial veteran registration, eligibility determination, and five key lines of business (benefits and entitlements): Home Loan Guarantee, Insurance, Vocational Rehabilitation and Employment, Education (GI Bill), and Compensation &amp; Pension</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Cemetery" title="United States National Cemetery">National Cemetery Administration</a>: responsible for providing burial and memorial benefits, as well as for maintenance of VA cemeteries</li>
</ul>
<p>There are Assistant Secretaries of Veteran Affairs for: Congressional and Legislative Affairs; Policy and Planning; Human Resources and Administration; and Operations, Security and Preparedness. Other Senate-approved presidential nominees at the VA include: Chief Financial Officer; Chair, <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Veterans%27_Appeals" title="Board of Veterans' Appeals">Board of Veterans' Appeals</a>; General Counsel; and Inspector General.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For VA benefits for PTSD, see <a href="/wiki/Veterans_benefits_for_post-traumatic_stress_disorder_in_the_United_States" title="Veterans benefits for post-traumatic stress disorder in the United States">Veterans benefits for post-traumatic stress disorder in the United States</a>.</div>
<p>The VA, through its <a href="/wiki/Veterans_Benefits_Administration" title="Veterans Benefits Administration">Veterans Benefits Administration</a> (VBA), provides a variety of services for veterans including disability compensation, pension, education, home loans, life insurance, vocational, rehabilitation, survivors’ benefits, health care, and burial benefits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Department of Labor (DOL) provides job development and job training opportunities for disabled and other veterans through contacts with employers and local agencies such as <a class="external text" href="http://www.newoppinc.org/veterans" rel="nofollow">this one</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Costs_for_care">Costs for care</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Costs for care">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As is common in any time of war, recently there has been an increased demand for nursing home beds, injury rehabilitation, and <a href="/wiki/Mental_health" title="Mental health">mental health</a> care. The VA categorizes veterans into eight priority groups and several additional subgroups, based on factors such as service-connected disabilities, and their income and assets (adjusted to local cost of living).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Veterans with a 50% or higher service-connected disability as determined by a VA regional office “rating board” (e.g., losing a limb in battle, <a href="/wiki/Posttraumatic_stress_disorder" title="Posttraumatic stress disorder">PTSD</a>, etc.) are provided comprehensive care and medication at no charge. Veterans with lesser qualifying factors who exceed a pre-defined income threshold have to make co-payments for care for non-service-connected ailments and pay $9 per 30-day supply for each prescription medication.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (June 2014)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs#Dubious" title="Talk:United States Department of Veterans Affairs">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> VA dental and nursing home care benefits are more restricted.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Reservist#United_States" title="Reservist">Reservists</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_Guard_of_the_United_States" title="National Guard of the United States">National Guard</a> personnel who served stateside in peacetime settings or have no service-related disabilities generally do not qualify for VA health benefits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The VA's budget has been pushed to the limit in recent years by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_on_Terrorism" title="War on Terrorism">War on Terrorism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In December 2004, it was widely reported that VA’s funding crisis had become so severe that it could no longer provide disability ratings to veterans in a timely fashion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> This is a problem because until veterans are fully transitioned from the active-duty <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TRICARE" title="TRICARE">TRICARE</a> healthcare system to VA, they are on their own with regard to many healthcare costs.</p>
<p>The VA's backlog of pending disability claims under review (a process known as "adjudication") peaked at 421,000 in 2001, and bottomed out at 254,000 in 2003, but crept back up to 340,000 in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>No <a href="/wiki/Copayment" title="Copayment">copayment</a> is required for VA services for veterans with military-related medical conditions. VA-recognized service-connected disabilities include problems that started or were aggravated due to military service. Veteran service organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/American_Legion" title="American Legion">American Legion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Veterans_of_Foreign_Wars" title="Veterans of Foreign Wars">Veterans of Foreign Wars</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Disabled_American_Veterans" title="Disabled American Veterans">Disabled American Veterans</a>, as well as state-operated Veterans Affairs offices and County Veteran Service Officers (CVSO), have been known to assist veterans in the process of getting care from the VA.</p>
<p>In his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Federal_Budget" title="United States Federal Budget">budget</a> proposal for <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_year" title="Fiscal year">fiscal year</a> 2009, President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> requested $38.7 billion—or 86.5% of the total Veterans Affairs budget—for veteran medical care alone.</p>
<p>In the 2011 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Costs_of_War&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Costs of War (page does not exist)">Costs of War</a> report from <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">Brown University</a>, researchers projected that the cost of caring for veterans of the <a href="/wiki/War_on_Terror" title="War on Terror">War on Terror</a> would peak 30–40 years after the end of combat operations. They also predicted that medical and disability costs would ultimately total between $600 billion and $1 trillion for the hundreds of thousands treated by the Department of Veterans Affairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_New_GI_Bill">The New GI Bill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The New GI Bill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The new GI Bill authored by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sen._Jim_Webb" title="Sen. Jim Webb">Sen. Jim Webb</a> (D-VA) doubled GI Bill college benefits while providing a 13-week extension to federal unemployment benefits. The new GI Bill doubled the value of the benefit to roughly $90,000 up from $40,000. In-state public universities essentially are covered to provide full scholarships for veterans under the new education package. For those veterans who served at least three years a monthly housing stipend was also added to the law.</p>
<p>Upon passage of the new GI Bill President George W. Bush stated "Our nation has no greater responsibility than to support our men and women in uniform—especially because we're at war... This bill shows the American people that even in an election year, Republicans and Democrats can come together to support our troops and their families", which highlighted that the new GI Bill had been overwhelmingly supported by both parties in the U.S. Congress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Congress and President Barack Obama extended the new GI Bill in August 2009 at the cost of roughly $70 billion over the next decade. Upon passing the GI Bill extension President Obama stated his support of the fighting forces of the United States by saying "Over the last eight years, they have endured tour after tour of duty in dangerous and distant places... They've experienced grueling combat, from the streets of Fallujah to the harsh terrain of Helmand province. They've adapted to complex insurgencies, protected local populations and trained foreign security forces."</p>
<p>The Pentagon worked closely with Congressional lawmakers to ensure military families were protected in the expansion of the law. In doing so, military officials worked non-stop to add a provision to extend the GI Bill to the surviving spouse and children of servicemembers killed while in combat.</p>
<p>This provision was highly favored by the Pentagon which authorized the Department of Defense (DoD) to allow individuals who, on or after August 1, 2009, have served at least 6 years in the Armed Forces and who agree to serve at least another 4 years in the U.S. Armed Forces to transfer unused entitlement to their surviving spouse. Servicemembers reaching 10 year anniversaries could choose to transfer the benefit to any dependent(s) (spouse, children).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1973, the Veterans Administration assumed another major responsibility when the National Cemetery System (NCS) (except for <a href="/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery" title="Arlington National Cemetery">Arlington National Cemetery</a>) was transferred to the Veterans Administration from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Army" title="United States Department of the Army">Department of the Army</a>.</p>
<p>In 1978, Congress established the State Cemetery Grants Program (Public Law 95-476). This act is administered by the National Cemetery Administration to aid States and U.S. territories in the establishment, expansion and improvement of veterans cemeteries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The VA was charged with the operation of the NCS, including the marking of graves of all persons in national and State cemeteries (and the graves of veterans in private cemeteries, upon request) as well and administering the State Cemetery Grants Program. The VA's National Cemetery Administration maintains 131 national cemeteries in 39 states (and Puerto Rico) as well as 33 soldier's lots and monument sites.</p>
<p>The Department of the Army maintains two national cemeteries, the Arlington National Cemetery and the U.S. Soldiers' &amp; Airmen's Home National Cemetery. Many states have established state veterans cemeteries. The American Battle Monuments Commission maintains 25 overseas military cemeteries that serve as resting places for almost 125,000 American war dead; on Tablets of the Missing that memorialize more than 94,000 U.S. servicemen and women; and through 25 memorials, monuments and markers. Fourteen national cemeteries are maintained by the National Park Service.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_reading">Further reading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Further reading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1998, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Institute_of_Medicine" title="Institute of Medicine">Institute of Medicine</a> began a series of studies to respond to requests from the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs and Congress for an examination of the health effects of potentially harmful agents to which Gulf War veterans might have been exposed.</p>
<ul>
<li>Jan. 1, 2000: <a class="external text" href="http://www.iom.edu/CMS/4683/5534.aspx" rel="nofollow">Gulf War and Health: Volume 1. Depleted Uranium, Sarin, Pyridostigmine Bromide, and Vaccines</a></li>
<li>Feb. 18, 2003: <a class="external text" href="http://www.iom.edu/CMS/4683/5407.aspx" rel="nofollow">Gulf War and Health Volume 2: Insecticides and Solvents</a></li>
<li>Aug. 20, 2004: <a class="external text" href="http://www.iom.edu/Reports/2004/Gulf-War-and-Health-Updated-Literature-Review-of-Sarin.aspx" rel="nofollow">Gulf War and Health: Updated Literature Review of Sarin</a></li>
<li>Dec. 20, 2004: <a class="external text" href="http://www.iom.edu/CMS/4683/24236.aspx" rel="nofollow">Gulf War and Health: Volume 3. Fuels, Combustion Products, and Propellants</a></li>
<li>Sep. 12, 2006: <a class="external text" href="http://www.iom.edu/Reports/2006/Gulf-War-and-Health--Volume-4-Health-Effects-of-Serving-in-the-Gulf-War.aspx" rel="nofollow">Gulf War and Health: Volume 4. Health Effects of Serving in the Gulf War</a></li>
<li>Oct. 16, 2006: <a class="external text" href="http://www.iom.edu/Reports/2006/Gulf-War-and-Health-Volume-5-Infectious-Disease.aspx" rel="nofollow">Gulf War and Health: Volume 5. Infectious Disease</a></li>

<li>Jul. 30, 2008: <a class="external text" href="http://www.iom.edu/CMS/4683/56996.aspx" rel="nofollow">Epidemiologic Studies of Veterans Exposed to Depleted Uranium: Feasibility and Design Issues</a></li>
<li>Jul. 30, 2008: <a class="external text" href="http://www.iom.edu/CMS/4683/56994.aspx" rel="nofollow">Gulf War and Health: Updated Literature Review of Depleted Uranium</a></li>
<li>Dec. 4, 2008: <a class="external text" href="http://www.iom.edu/en/Reports/2008/Gulf-War-and-Health-Volume-7-Long-term-Consequences-of-Traumatic-Brain-Injury.aspx" rel="nofollow">Gulf War and Health: Volume 7. Long-term Consequences of Traumatic Brain Injury</a></li>
