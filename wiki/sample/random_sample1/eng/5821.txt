<p>The <b>Evangelical Lutheran Church in America</b> (<b>ELCA</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Mainline_Protestant" title="Mainline Protestant">mainline Protestant</a> <a href="/wiki/Christian_denomination" title="Christian denomination">denomination</a> headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>. The ELCA officially came into existence on January 1, 1988, by the merging of three Lutheran church bodies. As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, it has approximately 3.7 million baptized members in just over 9,300 congregations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elca_stats_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-elca_stats-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2015, Pew Research estimated that 2.1% of the U.S population self-identifies with the ELCA and mainline Lutheranism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Religious_Landscape_Study_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Religious_Landscape_Study-6">[6]</a></sup> It is the seventh-largest religious body<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and the largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a> denomination in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The next two largest Lutheran denominations are the <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Church%E2%80%93Missouri_Synod" title="Lutheran Church–Missouri Synod">Lutheran Church–Missouri Synod</a> (LCMS) (with approximately 2.1 million members<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCMF_Facts_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-LCMF_Facts-9">[9]</a></sup>) and the <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_Evangelical_Lutheran_Synod" title="Wisconsin Evangelical Lutheran Synod">Wisconsin Evangelical Lutheran Synod</a> (WELS) (with approximately 370,000 members).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WELS_ANUAL_REPORT_2015_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-WELS_ANUAL_REPORT_2015-10">[10]</a></sup> There are also many <a href="/wiki/Template:Lutheranism_in_the_United_States" title="Template:Lutheranism in the United States">smaller Lutheran church bodies</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_the_United_States" title="Religion in the United States">United States</a>, some of which came into being composed of dissidents following the major 1988 merger. The ELCA belongs to the <a href="/wiki/World_Council_of_Churches" title="World Council of Churches">World Council of Churches</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_World_Federation" title="Lutheran World Federation">Lutheran World Federation</a>. The ELCA is in full communion with the <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(United_States)" title="Episcopal Church (United States)">Episcopal Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moravian_Church_in_North_America" title="Moravian Church in North America">Moravian Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Presbyterian_Church_(USA)" title="Presbyterian Church (USA)">Presbyterian Church (USA)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reformed_Church_in_America" title="Reformed Church in America">Reformed Church in America</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Church_of_Christ" title="United Church of Christ">United Church of Christ</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Methodist_Church" title="United Methodist Church">United Methodist Church</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1970, a survey by Strommen et al. found that 79% of Lutheran Church in America clergy, 62% of American Lutheran Church clergy, and 58% of Lutheran Church Missouri Synod clergy surveyed agreed that "a merger of all Lutheran groups in the United States into one organization is desirable".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The ELCA formally came into existence on January 1, 1988, creating the largest Lutheran church body in the United States. The Church is a result of a merger among <b>The American Lutheran Church</b> (ALC) with its headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, the <b>Lutheran Church in America</b> (LCA) (centered in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>) and the <b>Association of Evangelical Lutheran Churches</b> (AELC) (which had earlier withdrawn from the <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Church%E2%80%93Missouri_Synod" title="Lutheran Church–Missouri Synod">Lutheran Church–Missouri Synod</a> after 1975), all of which had formally agreed in 1982 to unite after several years of discussions. The ALC and LCA were themselves the product of previous mergers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_American_Lutheran_Church">The American Lutheran Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The American Lutheran Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1960, the <a href="/wiki/American_Lutheran_Church#American_Lutheran_Church_.281930.29" title="American Lutheran Church">American Lutheran Church</a>, formed 1930 (ALC), the <a href="/wiki/United_Evangelical_Lutheran_Church" title="United Evangelical Lutheran Church">United Evangelical Lutheran Church</a> (UELC), and the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_(United_States)" title="Evangelical Lutheran Church (United States)">Evangelical Lutheran Church</a> (ELC) merged to form The <a href="/wiki/American_Lutheran_Church" title="American Lutheran Church">American Lutheran Church</a>, with the <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Free_Church" title="Lutheran Free Church">Lutheran Free Church</a> (LFC) joining in 1963. The ALC brought approximately 2.25 million members into the ELCA. Its immigrant heritage came mostly from <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>. It was the most theologically conservative of the forming bodies, having a heritage of <a href="/wiki/Old_Lutherans" title="Old Lutherans">Old Lutheran</a> theology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Lutherans_in_North_America_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Lutherans_in_North_America-14">[14]</a></sup> It joined in fellowship with the Lutheran Church—Missouri Synod and officially held to biblical <a href="/wiki/Biblical_inerrancy" title="Biblical inerrancy">inerrancy</a> in its constitution, although it seldom enforced it by means of heresy trials and other doctrinal discipline. Its geographic center was in the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Midwest" title="Upper Midwest">Upper Midwest</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>. Some congregations in the ALC opted not to join the merger and instead formed the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_of_Lutheran_Churches" title="American Association of Lutheran Churches">American Association of Lutheran Churches</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Lutheran_Church_in_America">The Lutheran Church in America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Lutheran Church in America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1962 the <a href="/wiki/United_Lutheran_Church_in_America" title="United Lutheran Church in America">United Lutheran Church in America</a> (ULCA), formed 1918, the <a href="/wiki/Augustana_Evangelical_Lutheran_Church" title="Augustana Evangelical Lutheran Church">Augustana Evangelical Lutheran Church</a> (AELC), the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_of_America" title="Finnish Evangelical Lutheran Church of America">Finnish Evangelical Lutheran Church of America</a> (FELCA), and the Danish <a href="/wiki/American_Evangelical_Lutheran_Church" title="American Evangelical Lutheran Church">American Evangelical Lutheran Church</a> (D-AELC) formed the <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Church_in_America" title="Lutheran Church in America">Lutheran Church in America</a>. The LCA brought approximately 2.85 million members into the ELCA. Its immigrant heritage came mostly from Germany, Sweden, <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, Denmark and <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>. Its demographic focus was on the East Coast (centered on <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>), with large numbers in the Midwest and some presence in the <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_States" title="South Atlantic States">Southern Atlantic</a> states. There are notable exceptions, but LCA-background churches tend to be more liturgical than ALC-background churches. Its theological orientation ranged from moderately liberal to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-orthodox" title="Neo-orthodox">neo-orthodox</a>, with tendencies toward conservative <a href="/wiki/Pietism" title="Pietism">Pietism</a> in some rural and small-town congregations. Its theology originated in the <a href="/wiki/Neo-Lutheranism" title="Neo-Lutheranism">Neo-Lutheran</a> movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Lutherans_in_North_America_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Lutherans_in_North_America-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Association_of_Evangelical_Lutheran_Churches">The Association of Evangelical Lutheran Churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Association of Evangelical Lutheran Churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1976 the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Evangelical_Lutheran_Churches" title="Association of Evangelical Lutheran Churches">Association of Evangelical Lutheran Churches</a> (AELC) was formed from congregations that left the Lutheran Church—Missouri Synod (LCMS) in a schism precipitated by progressive-traditionalist disputes over higher criticism, <a href="/wiki/Academic_freedom" title="Academic freedom">academic freedom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecumenism" title="Ecumenism">ecumenism</a>. Its establishment was precipitated by the controversy at the LCMS's <a href="/wiki/Concordia_Seminary" title="Concordia Seminary">Concordia Seminary</a> in <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a> in 1974, which resulted in the formation of a separate institution "Concordia Seminary-in-Exile" known as "<a href="/wiki/Seminex" title="Seminex">Seminex</a>". The AELC brought approximately 100,000 members into the ELCA. Its immigrant heritage came mostly from Germany; the complexion of its theology generally resembled that of the LCA, as the dissenting former "moderate" faction of the LCMS.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The ELCA is headed by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presiding_Bishop" title="Presiding Bishop">Presiding Bishop</a>, who is elected by the Churchwide Assembly for a term of six years (it was a four-year term until 1995). To date, four people have been elected to the position of Presiding Bishop of the ELCA. <a href="/wiki/Herbert_W._Chilstrom" title="Herbert W. Chilstrom">Herbert W. Chilstrom</a> served as the first Presiding Bishop from 1987 to 1995. He was followed by <a href="/wiki/H._George_Anderson" title="H. George Anderson">H. George Anderson</a> (1995–2001), who had previously been the President of <a href="/wiki/Luther_College_(Iowa)" title="Luther College (Iowa)">Luther College</a>. The third Presiding Bishop was <a href="/wiki/Mark_Hanson" title="Mark Hanson">Mark Hanson</a>, who is the past president of the Lutheran World Federation. Hanson began his tenure as Bishop of the Church in 2001 and was re-elected in August 2007 for a second term. <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Eaton" title="Elizabeth Eaton">Elizabeth Eaton</a> was elected Presiding Bishop in August 2013 and took office on November 1, 2013.</p>
<p>In addition, there is the body of the Church Council composed of representatives elected for a stated number of years from the various synods which meet regularly with legislative powers in between sessions of the Churchwide Assemblies.</p>
<p>The Conference of Bishops is formed of the elected synodical bishops from each of the constituent synods and is often consulted by the Presiding Bishop and the Church Council.</p>

<p>The ELCA is divided into <a href="/wiki/List_of_ELCA_synods" title="List of ELCA synods">65 synods</a>, one of which is non-geographical (the <a href="/wiki/Slovak_Zion_Synod" title="Slovak Zion Synod">Slovak Zion Synod</a>) and 64 regional synods in the United States and the Caribbean, each headed by a synodical bishop and council; these synods are in turn grouped into nine regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Within the ELCA the term <i>synod</i> refers to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_judicatory" title="Middle judicatory">middle judicatory</a>, which is referred to in some other denominations as "presbyteries", "districts", "conferences" or "dioceses" (the most ancient and traditional term). In other Christian churches, the term "synod" is used for a meeting or conference of ministers such as priests or bishops of a diocese, province (region) or nation or in some Protestant churches as the term for their annual governing convention. Some Evangelical Lutheran denominations overseas continue to use the ancient church title of "diocese".</p>
<p>Outside of the United States, ELCA also has congregations in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> region (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a> which is combined with Florida in one synod, <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="United States Virgin Islands">U.S. Virgin Islands</a>) and one congregation in the border city of <a href="/wiki/Windsor,_Ontario" title="Windsor, Ontario">Windsor, Ontario</a>, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Slovak_Zion_Synod" title="Slovak Zion Synod">Slovak Zion Synod</a>. Before 1986, some of the congregations that form the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_Canada" title="Evangelical Lutheran Church in Canada">Evangelical Lutheran Church in Canada</a> were part of the ELCA's predecessor churches.</p>
<p>Within the church structure are divisions addressing many programs and ministries. Among these are support for global mission, outdoor ministries, campus ministries, social ministries, and education. They include the <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Peace_Fellowship" title="Lutheran Peace Fellowship">Lutheran Peace Fellowship</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Women%27s_Caucus" title="Lutheran Women's Caucus">Lutheran Women's Caucus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Volunteer_Corps" title="Lutheran Volunteer Corps">Lutheran Volunteer Corps</a>, and the Lutheran Youth Organization <a href="/wiki/ELCA_Youth_Gathering" title="ELCA Youth Gathering">ELCA Youth Gathering</a> (formerly known in predecessor denominations as the <a href="/wiki/Luther_League" title="Luther League">Luther League</a>). They cooperate with an ecumenical inter-Lutheran college/university student organization known as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lutheran_Student_Movement-USA&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lutheran Student Movement-USA (page does not exist)">Lutheran Student Movement-USA</a> founded in 1922 and reorganized in 1969. The denominational publishing house is <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Fortress" title="Augsburg Fortress">Augsburg Fortress</a>, and the official denominational magazine is <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lutheran" title="The Lutheran">The Lutheran</a></i>. ELCA predecessor bodies established twenty-six <a href="/wiki/List_of_ELCA_colleges_and_universities" title="List of ELCA colleges and universities">colleges and universities now affiliated with the ELCA</a> and a large number of associated theological seminaries, some of which are associated with neighboring universities or theological consortia.</p>
<p>Most local congregations are legally independent non-profit corporations within the state where they are located that own their own property. Actual governing practice within the congregation ranges from congregational voters' assemblies or annual and special congregational meetings to elder-and-council-led, to congregations where the senior pastor wields great, if informal, power (more common in larger churches).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Churchwide_Assemblies">Churchwide Assemblies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Churchwide Assemblies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Churchwide Assembly meets biennially in odd-numbered years and consists of elected lay and ordained voting members; between meetings of the Churchwide Assembly, the ELCA Church Council governs the denomination, along with the advisory Conference of Bishops. At the Assembly, elections are held for general officers of the Church such as the Presiding Bishop, Vice President and Secretary, Budgets are adopted, Social Statements examined and approved and various other church business enacted along with reports made and ecumenical visitors acknowledged. A constitutional amendment passed in 2011 that will switch it to a triennially meeting after 2013. In both predecessor churches, the Assembly was known as the "General Convention" in The ALC and the "Biennial Convention" in the LCA.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beliefs">Beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lutheranism">Lutheranism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lutheranism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lutheranism is associated with the German reformer <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a>, with its official confessional writings found in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Concord" title="Book of Concord">Book of Concord</a>. The ELCA accepts the unaltered <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Confession" title="Augsburg Confession">Augsburg Confession</a> (not the <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Confession_Variata" title="Augsburg Confession Variata">variata</a>) as a true witness to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Good_news_(Christianity)" title="Good news (Christianity)">Gospel</a>. The ELCA is less <a href="/wiki/Conservatism" title="Conservatism">conservative</a> than the <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Church%E2%80%93Missouri_Synod" title="Lutheran Church–Missouri Synod">Lutheran Church–Missouri Synod</a> (LCMS) or the even more conservative <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_Evangelical_Lutheran_Synod" title="Wisconsin Evangelical Lutheran Synod">Wisconsin Evangelical Lutheran Synod</a> (WELS), the second and third largest Lutheran bodies in the United States respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Although having a sizable conservative minority, most ELCA Lutherans are theologically moderate-to-liberal. Most other Lutheran bodies in the U.S. hold more strictly to <a href="/wiki/Confessional_Lutheranism" title="Confessional Lutheranism">Confessional Lutheranism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pietism" title="Pietism">Pietism</a>, or a combination of the two, than the ELCA does.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_within_the_ELCA">Differences within the ELCA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Differences within the ELCA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ELCA has many differences of opinion among its constituent congregations, which have caused a number of disputes over social and doctrinal issues. In part, this is due to the fact that it assimilated three different Lutheran church bodies, each with its own factions and divisions, thus inheriting old intra-group conflicts while creating new inter-group ones. Differences on issues usually reflect theological disputes between various parties.</p>
<p>The ELCA is a very broad denomination. It contains groups of socially conservative or liberal factions with emphases on various topics such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liturgical_renewal" title="Liturgical renewal">liturgical renewal</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> confessional Lutheranism, charismatic revivalism, moderate to liberal theology, and liberal activism. The socially liberal segment of the ELCA is represented by independent organizations such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutherans_Concerned/North_America" title="Lutherans Concerned/North America">Lutherans Concerned/North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Extraordinary_Lutheran_Ministries" title="Extraordinary Lutheran Ministries">Extraordinary Lutheran Ministries</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_and_Ecumenical_Women%27s_Caucus" title="Evangelical and Ecumenical Women's Caucus">Evangelical and Ecumenical Women's Caucus</a>. A socially conservative Lutheran organization, once within, and now departed from the ELCA opposed to the denomination's stance on openly-gay clergy is the <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_CORE" title="Lutheran CORE">Lutheran Coalition For Renewal</a> (Lutheran CORE). Adherents of <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Catholic" title="Evangelical Catholic">Evangelical Catholicism</a> practice <a href="/wiki/High_Church_Lutheranism" title="High Church Lutheranism">High Church Lutheranism</a> and include the members of the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_the_Holy_Trinity" title="Society of the Holy Trinity">Society of the Holy Trinity</a>. Those oriented toward Confessional Lutheranism, <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">Evangelicalism</a>, or an admixture of the two include the <a href="/wiki/WordAlone" title="WordAlone">WordAlone</a> network and those involved with <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Congregations_in_Mission_for_Christ" title="Lutheran Congregations in Mission for Christ">Lutheran Congregations in Mission for Christ</a>. Members of the <a href="/wiki/Charismatic_Movement" title="Charismatic Movement">Charismatic Movement</a> include congregations and pastors associated with the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_of_Renewal_Churches" title="Alliance of Renewal Churches">Alliance of Renewal Churches</a>. Additionally, there has been a recent growth in Franciscan spirituality in the ELCA through the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Lutheran_Franciscans" title="Order of Lutheran Franciscans">Order of Lutheran Franciscans</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scripture">Scripture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Scripture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ELCA constitution states: "This church accepts the canonical Scriptures of the Old and New Testaments as the inspired Word of God and the authoritative source and norm of its proclamation, faith, and life."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>ELCA clergy tend not to subscribe to a doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Biblical_inerrancy" title="Biblical inerrancy">Biblical inerrancy</a>, but see validity in various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Higher_criticism" title="Higher criticism">scholarly methods</a> of analysis to help in understanding the Bible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This is in concord with most moderate Protestant bodies and in contrast to the LCMS and WELS, which practice the <a href="/wiki/Historical-grammatical_method" title="Historical-grammatical method">historical-grammatical method</a> of biblical interpretation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sacraments">Sacraments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sacraments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like other Lutheran church bodies, the ELCA confesses at least two <a href="/wiki/Sacrament" title="Sacrament">Sacraments</a>: <a href="/wiki/Eucharistic_theology#Lutherans" title="Eucharistic theology">Communion</a> (or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eucharist_in_the_Lutheran_Church" title="Eucharist in the Lutheran Church">Eucharist</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Baptism#Reformation" title="Baptism">Holy Baptism</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Infant_baptism#Lutherans" title="Infant baptism">infant baptism</a>). Confession and absolution is often included as a Sacrament; however, as it is a return to the forgiveness given in baptism, strictly speaking there are only two sacraments. Guidance on sacramental practices in the ELCA is provided in <i>The Use of the Means of Grace</i>, a statement adopted by the 1997 Churchwide Assembly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the two sacraments, ELCA churches also practice acts that are sacramental in character, or <a href="/wiki/Sacramentals#Protestant_usages" title="Sacramentals">sacramentals</a>. These include <a href="/wiki/Confirmation_(Lutheran_Church)" title="Confirmation (Lutheran Church)">confirmation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holy_orders" title="Holy orders">ordination</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extreme_unction" title="Extreme unction">anointing the sick</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confession_in_the_Lutheran_Church" title="Confession in the Lutheran Church">confession and absolution</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a>. Their practice and their view as "minor sacraments" varies between churches of a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Church_Lutheran" title="High Church Lutheran">high</a>" and "low" church nature.</p>
<p>With respect to the eucharist, the ELCA holds to the Lutheran doctrine of the <a href="/wiki/Sacramental_union" title="Sacramental union">sacramental union</a>, that is, that Christ's body and blood is truly present "in, with and under" the <a href="/wiki/Sacramental_bread" title="Sacramental bread">bread</a> and wine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> All communicants <a href="/wiki/Human_mouth" title="Human mouth">orally</a> receive not only bread and wine, but also the same body and blood of Christ that was given for them on the cross.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Members of other denominations sometimes refer to this as a belief in <a href="/wiki/Consubstantiation" title="Consubstantiation">consubstantiation</a>. Lutherans, however, <a href="/wiki/Sacramental_union#Distinction_from_other_doctrines_of_the_Real_Presence" title="Sacramental union">reject the philosophical explanation of consubstantiation</a>, preferring to see the presence of the Lord's body and blood as mysterious rather than explainable by human philosophy. The Lutheran belief in the mysterious character of the consecrated bread and wine is more similar to Roman Catholic and Eastern Orthodox belief than most Protestants. In contrast, most Protestant church bodies doubt or openly deny that the true body and blood of Christ is eaten in the Lord's Supper.</p>
<p>Unlike certain other American Lutheran church bodies, the ELCA practices <a href="/wiki/Open_communion" title="Open communion">open communion</a>, permitting all persons baptized in the name of the Trinity with water to receive communion. Some congregations also <a href="/wiki/Infant_communion" title="Infant communion">commune baptized infants</a> similarly to Eastern Orthodox practice. The ELCA encourages its churches to practice the Eucharist at all services, although some churches alternate between non-communion services with those containing the Lord's Supper.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_women">Role of women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Role of women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The ELCA <a href="/wiki/Ordination_of_women" title="Ordination of women">ordains women as pastors</a>, a practice that all three of its predecessor churches adopted in the 1970s. Some have become synod bishops. In 2013, Elizabeth Eaton became the first woman to be elected Presiding Bishop of the ELCA. The most recent ELCA hymnal, <i><a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Lutheran_Worship" title="Evangelical Lutheran Worship">Evangelical Lutheran Worship</a></i>, includes alternate gender-neutral invocations and benedictions in all settings. All of the psalms and many of the hymns and parts of the liturgy have been altered to remove masculine pronouns referring to God.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 2000, the Cooperative Clergy Study Project surveyed 681 ELCA pastors and found that 95% of ELCA clergy thought that all clergy positions should be open to women, while 2% disagreed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thearda.com_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-thearda.com-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ordination_of_LGBT_clergy_and_blessings_of_same-sex_marriages">Ordination of LGBT clergy and blessings of same-sex marriages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ordination of LGBT clergy and blessings of same-sex marriages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2009_ELCA_Churchwide_Assembly" title="2009 ELCA Churchwide Assembly">2009 ELCA Churchwide Assembly</a></div>
<p>On August 21, 2009, the ELCA's Churchwide Assembly in Minneapolis voted to allow congregations to call and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordination_of_LGBT_Christian_clergy" title="Ordination of LGBT Christian clergy">ordain gays and lesbians</a> in committed monogamous relationships to serve as clergy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> By a vote of 559 to 451, delegates approved a resolution declaring that the church would find a way for people in "publicly accountable, lifelong, monogamous same-gender relationships" to serve as official ministers. Congregations that do not wish to call these persons to ordained ministry are not required by these policy changes to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In reaction, <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_CORE" title="Lutheran CORE">Lutheran CORE</a>, which opposed the decision, stated that it would, "initiate a process that we hope will lead to a reconfiguration of North American Lutheranism."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In February 2010, <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_CORE" title="Lutheran CORE">Lutheran CORE</a> announced that it would secede from the ELCA and form a new denomination to be named the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Lutheran_Church" title="North American Lutheran Church">North American Lutheran Church</a> (NALC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> As of 2008<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, 37% of ELCA pastors were found to support <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ELCA, in removing sexual orientation as a bar for candidacy in the professional ministry, joined most of its Lutheran sister churches in Europe, including in Germany, Norway, Sweden, Denmark, Iceland, and Austria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The ELCA is also among a growing number of Christian churches in the United States to make this move.</p>
<p>In contrast, the board of one of the ELCA's partner churches, October 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Evangelical_Church_Mekane_Yesus" title="Ethiopian Evangelical Church Mekane Yesus">Evangelical Mekane Yesus Fellowship in North America</a>, voted to declare disunity with the ELCA. A press release stated that the board was no longer "in good conscience" "able to commune and partner with ELCA Church that has willfully disobeyed the word of God and regrettably departed from the clear instructions of the Holy Scriptures" that "marriage is only between a man and a woman."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-christianpost.com_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-christianpost.com-34">[34]</a></sup> This was followed by the general synod of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Evangelical_Church_Mekane_Yesus" title="Ethiopian Evangelical Church Mekane Yesus">Ethiopian Evangelical Church Mekane Yesus</a> also breaking links with the ELCA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-christianpost.com_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-christianpost.com-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2010, The Church Council of the Evangelical Lutheran Church in America adopted revisions to ministry policy documents to bring them in line with the August 2009 vote, as well as adding sections on integrity, substance abuse and addiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-release_april_2010_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-release_april_2010-35">[35]</a></sup> The release noted that the <a class="external text" href="http://www.elca.org/Growing-In-Faith/Vocation/Rostered-Leadership/Ministry-Policies.aspx" rel="nofollow">revised ministry policies</a> would be posted on the church's website by the end of April 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-release_april_2010_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-release_april_2010-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since August 2009, according to the office of the ELCA secretary, over 600 congregations have left the ELCA through January 2011. Income has declined, with a projected income of 48 million in 2011, down from a budgeted 51 million in 2010, and a total budget of 88 million in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 31, 2013, <a href="/wiki/Guy_Erwin" title="Guy Erwin">Guy Erwin</a> became the first openly gay man to be chosen bishop in the ELCA when he was elected to a six-year term as bishop of the Southwest California Synod.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erwin_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Erwin-37">[37]</a></sup> In 2015, the denomination ordained, officially, the first transgender pastor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also in 2015, after the Supreme Court legalized <a href="/wiki/Obergefell_v._Hodges" title="Obergefell v. Hodges">same-sex marriage</a> nationally in the US, the office of the presiding bishop released a letter informing members that each congregation is free to marry same-gender couples or to choose not to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 2009 <a href="/wiki/Blessing_of_same-sex_unions_in_Christian_churches" title="Blessing of same-sex unions in Christian churches">blessings of same-sex marriages</a> are allowed in the Evangelical Lutheran Church in America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation_and_evolution">Creation and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Creation and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The ELCA has not adopted an official position on <a href="/wiki/Creationism" title="Creationism">creation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>, but there is general agreement on interpreting the Bible within its historical contexts and applying critical methods of research. In 2000, the Cooperative Clergy Study Project surveyed 681 ELCA pastors and found that 26% of ELCA clergy thought <a href="/wiki/Creationism#Christianity" title="Creationism">Scientific Creationism</a> should be taught alongside evolution in biology classes, while 57% disagreed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thearda.com_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-thearda.com-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproductive_cloning">Reproductive cloning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Reproductive cloning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The ELCA has not yet taken an official position regarding reproductive cloning. However, Task Force on Genetics of the church's "Church in Society" initiative is studying the theological and ethical issues that the world is likely to face in coming years as a result of Genetic Science. The task force has issued a draft report<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> for comment and discussion. The draft statement covers a wide range of topics, from <a href="/wiki/Genetic_testing" title="Genetic testing">genetic testing</a> to <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organism" title="Genetically modified organism">GMOs</a>. A section in this report which has been described by an independent reviewer as "a remarkably nuanced analysis and statement regarding a very complex scientific, social, and religious issue."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eigen_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eigen-42">[42]</a></sup> The task force recommends opposition to reproductive cloning, as almost all religious groups currently do. However, the main theological reasoning is unique. Lewis D. Eigen explains:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eigen_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eigen-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>The argument they articulate is not the common but weak argument that it would be "offensive to God," "against the will of God" or "man encroaching into God's domain", but they observe that the clone would be denied the dignity of possessing a unique human genotype. This is an extremely interesting argument—that each and every human being has the right to his or her own uniqueness—particularly a unique genotype.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The draft statement further asserts that any clones that might be created should have full acceptance as human beings and access to rites of the church.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Abortion">Abortion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Abortion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The issue of <a href="/wiki/Abortion_debate" title="Abortion debate">abortion</a> is a matter of contention within the ELCA. In a Social Statement adopted in 1991,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> the church set out its position on the matter as follows. The ELCA describes itself as "a community supportive of life," and encourages women to <a href="/wiki/Crisis_pregnancy_center" title="Crisis pregnancy center">explore alternatives</a> to abortion such as <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">adoption</a>. However, the Social Statement asserts that there are certain circumstances under which a decision to end a <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a> can be "morally responsible." These include cases where the pregnancy "presents a clear threat to the physical life of the woman," situations where "the pregnancy occurs when both parties do not participate willingly in sexual intercourse," and "circumstances of extreme fetal <a href="/wiki/Disability" title="Disability">abnormality</a>, which will result in severe suffering and very early death of an infant." Regardless of the reason, the ELCA opposes abortion when "a fetus is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viability_(fetal)" title="Viability (fetal)">developed enough to live outside a uterus</a> with the aid of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neonatal_intensive-care_unit" title="Neonatal intensive-care unit">reasonable and necessary technology</a>." The ELCA opposes "laws that deny access to safe and affordable services for morally justifiable abortions," and "laws that are primarily intended to harass those contemplating or deciding for an abortion." The statement emphasizes the prevention of circumstances leading to abortion, specifically encouraging "appropriate forms of <a href="/wiki/Sex_education#United_States" title="Sex education">sex education</a> in schools, community <a href="/wiki/Christian_views_on_contraception#Lutheranism" title="Christian views on contraception">pregnancy prevention</a> programs, and <a href="/wiki/Parenting" title="Parenting">parenting</a> preparation classes." In 2000, the Cooperative Clergy Study Project found that one fifth of ELCA clergy favored banning abortion with a constitutional amendment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Euthanasia">Euthanasia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Euthanasia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The ELCA official statement on "End of Life Decisions", adopted on November 9, 1992, disapproves euthanasia: "We oppose the legalization of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physician_assisted_suicide" title="Physician assisted suicide">physician-assisted death</a>, which would allow the private killing of one person by another. Public control and regulation of such actions would be extremely difficult, if not impossible. The potential for abuse, especially of people who are most vulnerable, would be substantially increased."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practices">Practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Practices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ministry">Ministry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Ministry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Richard_and_bonnie_jensen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1852" data-file-width="1852" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f0/Richard_and_bonnie_jensen.jpg/200px-Richard_and_bonnie_jensen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f0/Richard_and_bonnie_jensen.jpg/300px-Richard_and_bonnie_jensen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f0/Richard_and_bonnie_jensen.jpg/400px-Richard_and_bonnie_jensen.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>As a Lutheran church body, the ELCA professes belief in the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Priesthood_of_all_believers" title="Priesthood of all believers">priesthood of all believers</a>" as reflected in Martin Luther's <a href="/wiki/To_the_Christian_Nobility_of_the_German_Nation" title="To the Christian Nobility of the German Nation">To the Christian Nobility of the German Nation</a>, that all baptized persons have equal access to God and are all called to use their gifts to serve the body of Christ. Some people are called to "rostered ministry", or vocations of church leadership and service. After formation, theological training, and approval by local synods these people are "set aside, but not above" through ordination or commissioning/consecration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> More and more ELCA congregations are employing specialized and even general ministers outside of this national oversight. An extensive "Study of Ministry" was embarked upon immediately after the 1988 merger as it became apparent that there were still discordant viewpoints and influences, especially by the pressure for a renewal of the office of deacon and its different manifestations to a more ancient and traditional view predating the Reformation into the earliest days of Christianity. The ELCA currently has four types of rostered ministers:</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pastor_.28clergy.29">Pastor (clergy)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Pastor (clergy)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>An ordained minister is called to the "office of public ministry" of "Word and sacrament" and considered a "steward of the mysteries" of the Church (i.e., the <a href="/wiki/Means_of_grace" title="Means of grace">means of grace</a>). Pastors primarily serve congregations, but this role has been expanded to include other forms of ministry as well (e.g., hospital and military chaplains). Pastors are ordinarily trained at one of <a href="/wiki/List_of_ELCA_seminaries" title="List of ELCA seminaries">eight ELCA seminaries</a> located throughout the United States, although there are alternative paths for ordination to serve particular communities in which it is difficult to provide trained leaders or to allow rostering of clergy transferred from other denominations. Pastors generally hold a Bachelor of Arts degree or its equivalent, as well as a four-year <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Master_of_divinity" title="Master of divinity">master of divinity</a> degree, are required to learn biblical <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>, spent at least a summer doing clinical pastoral education—an intensive program that gives them time to reflect on their pastoral craft, usually in a hospital setting, and are required to complete a one-year internship of full-time service in pastoral ministry. A <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a> is a pastor called to serve either a <a href="/wiki/Synod" title="Synod">synod</a> or as <a href="/wiki/Presiding_bishop" title="Presiding bishop">presiding bishop</a> of the ELCA. A bishop is only a bishop as long as he or she serves in that office and returns to being known simply as a pastor when service as a bishop ends.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Diaconal_minister">Diaconal minister</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Diaconal minister">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Deacon#Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America" title="Deacon">Diaconal ministers</a> are "ministers of Word and Service" who may serve as <a href="/wiki/Chaplain" title="Chaplain">chaplains</a>, youth ministers, or in some aspect of social justice or advocacy work, along with an assisting role in the various church liturgies and services. This is the newest category established by the ELCA. A Diaconal minister is similar to the role performed by permanent <a href="/wiki/Deacon#Roman_Catholicism.2C_Eastern_Orthodoxy.2C_.26_Anglicanism" title="Deacon">deacons</a> in the Roman Catholic Church, Eastern Orthodoxy, the Episcopal Church (and Anglican Communion) and the United Methodist Church, of which the restoration and revisions of the concepts of the office resulted from reforms initiated in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council</a> of 1962–65. A variety of formation models with education and training were adopted by various programs, synods, diaconate orders and seminaries.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Deaconess">Deaconess</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Deaconess">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Deaconess" title="Deaconess">deaconess</a> is a lay woman, married or single, who serves the church in a variety of ways. Traditionally, deaconesses served in the caring professions as nurses, social workers, or teachers. The office of deaconess, or the female diaconate, was established in several predecessor ELCA bodies beginning in the 1840s under influence from Germany and <a href="/wiki/William_Passavant" title="William Passavant">William Passavant</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Associate_in_ministry">Associate in ministry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Associate in ministry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Serves local congregations, synods or other ministries in a variety of roles as parish administrators, <a href="/wiki/Church_music" title="Church music">parish musicians</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Youth_ministry_(Evangelical)" title="Youth ministry (Evangelical)">youth ministry</a> leaders, or other positions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Worship">Worship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Worship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Published in 2006, <i><a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Lutheran_Worship" title="Evangelical Lutheran Worship">Evangelical Lutheran Worship</a></i> is the main <a href="/wiki/Hymnal" title="Hymnal">hymnal</a> used in congregations. Some congregations, however, continue to use the older <i><a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Book_of_Worship" title="Lutheran Book of Worship">Lutheran Book of Worship</a></i> published by the Inter-Lutheran Commission on Worship in 1978, and some even continue to use the older <i><a href="/wiki/Service_Book_and_Hymnal" title="Service Book and Hymnal">Service Book and Hymnal</a></i> (SBH) of 1958 or its antecedent precedent-setting <i><a href="/wiki/Divine_Service_(Lutheran)" title="Divine Service (Lutheran)">Common Service</a></i> of 1888 which laid out a traditional American Lutheran liturgy and later was included in subsequent worship books and hymnals of various churches especially <i><a href="/wiki/Divine_Service_(Lutheran)" title="Divine Service (Lutheran)">The Common Service Book</a></i> of 1917, adopted by the old <a href="/wiki/United_Lutheran_Church_in_America" title="United Lutheran Church in America">United Lutheran Church in America</a>, a predecessor of the LCA to 1962, and <i>The Lutheran Hymnal</i> (TLH-1941) of the LCMS. Many congregations also make use of supplementary resources recently published as well besides those authorized for the <i>LBW</i> by Augsburg-Fortress, Publishers. Many ELCA congregations are classically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liturgical" title="Liturgical">liturgical</a> churches. Their liturgy is rooted in the Western liturgical tradition, though recent international Lutheran-Orthodox dialog sessions have had some minimal influence on Lutheran liturgy. Because of its use of the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Concord" title="Book of Concord">Book of Concord</a> of 1580, with the Confessions, documents and beliefs of the Reformers, including the <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Confession" title="Augsburg Confession">Augsburg Confession</a> of 1530, <a href="/wiki/Luther%27s_Small_Catechism" title="Luther's Small Catechism">Luther's Small Catechism</a> of 1529 and the <a href="/wiki/Luther%27s_Large_Catechism" title="Luther's Large Catechism">Large Catechism</a> and its retention of many pre-Reformation traditions, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vestments" title="Vestments">vestments</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feast_days" title="Feast days">feast days</a> and the celebration of the <a href="/wiki/Liturgical_year" title="Liturgical year">Church Year</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sign_of_the_Cross#Lutheranism" title="Sign of the Cross">sign of the cross</a>, and the usage of a church-wide <a href="/wiki/Agenda_(liturgy)" title="Agenda (liturgy)">liturgy</a>, there are many aspects of the typical ELCA church that are very catholic and traditional in nature. Many Evangelical Lutheran churches use traditional vestments (<a href="/wiki/Cassock" title="Cassock">cassock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surplice" title="Surplice">surplice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stole_(vestment)" title="Stole (vestment)">stole</a> for services of the Word or non-Eucharistic liturgies or <a href="/wiki/Alb" title="Alb">alb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cincture" title="Cincture">cincture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stole_(vestment)" title="Stole (vestment)">stole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chasuble" title="Chasuble">chasuble</a> (pastor) or <a href="/wiki/Dalmatic" title="Dalmatic">dalmatic</a> (deacon), <a href="/wiki/Cope" title="Cope">cope</a> (processions) for Eucharists (Mass, Holy Communion), etc.). On special rare occasions even a bishop's cross/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crozier" title="Crozier">crozier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitre" title="Mitre">mitre</a> (bishop's headpiece) have been used to designate the ancient robes and traditions of the Church originating in <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Roman</a> times of which <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Luther</a> and his fellow Reformers like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phillip_Melancthon" title="Phillip Melancthon">Phillip Melancthon</a> considered as "adiaphora" or of permissive use. Since the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council</a> of the 1960s, most major parts of the ELCA's popular liturgies are worded exactly like the English language Mass of 1970 of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> Church. Many ELCA congregations use informal styles of worship or a blend of traditional and contemporary liturgical forms.</p>
<p>Springing from its revered heritage in the Lutheran <a href="/wiki/Chorale" title="Chorale">Chorale</a>, the musical life of ELCA congregations is just as diverse as its worship. <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Johann Sebastian Bach</a>, the most famous Lutheran composer and African songs are part of the heritage and breadth of Evangelical Lutheran church music. The musical portion of the Lutheran liturgy includes <a href="/wiki/Metrical_psalter" title="Metrical psalter">metrical psalter</a>, metrical responses and hymns. <i>Evangelical Lutheran Worship</i> has ten settings of Holy Communion, for example. They range from plainsong chant, to Gospel, to Latin-style music. Congregations worship in many languages, many of which are represented in <i>Evangelical Lutheran Worship</i>. Other books used in ELCA churches include the <i><a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Book_of_Worship" title="Lutheran Book of Worship">Lutheran Book of Worship</a></i> (1978), and its supplements: <i>With One Voice</i>, <i>This Far by Faith</i>, and for Latino/Hispanic congregations: <i>Libro de Liturgia y Cántico</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecumenical_relations">Ecumenical relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Ecumenical relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ELCA is a member of the <a href="/wiki/National_Council_of_Churches" title="National Council of Churches">National Council of Churches</a>, the <a href="/wiki/World_Council_of_Churches" title="World Council of Churches">World Council of Churches</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Churches_Together" title="Christian Churches Together">Christian Churches Together</a> and is a "partner in mission and dialog" with the <a href="/wiki/Churches_Uniting_in_Christ" title="Churches Uniting in Christ">Churches Uniting in Christ</a> (formerly the Consultation on Church Union) formed in 1960.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Full_communion">Full communion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Full communion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Church maintains <a href="/wiki/Full_communion" title="Full communion">full communion</a> relationships with other member churches of the <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_World_Federation" title="Lutheran World Federation">Lutheran World Federation</a> (which is a communion of 140 autonomous national/regional Lutheran church bodies in 78 countries around the world, representing nearly 66 million Christians) which was reorganized in 1948 from the earlier <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lutheran_World_Convention&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lutheran World Convention (page does not exist)">Lutheran World Convention</a> of 1923. Also the ELCA has established official relationships with other Christian denominations such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moravian_Church_in_America" title="Moravian Church in America">Moravian Church in America</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterian_Church_USA" title="Presbyterian Church USA">Presbyterian Church (USA)</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Reformed_Church_in_America" title="Reformed Church in America">Reformed Church in America</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Church_of_Christ" title="United Church of Christ">United Church of Christ</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Episcopal_Church" title="The Episcopal Church">The Episcopal Church</a>, and The <a href="/wiki/United_Methodist_Church" title="United Methodist Church">United Methodist Church</a>.</p>
<p>In 2013, the <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Evangelical_Church_Mekane_Yesus" title="Ethiopian Evangelical Church Mekane Yesus">Ethiopian Evangelical Church Mekane Yesus</a> severed ties, including communion and pulpit fellowship, with the ELCA over the issue of homosexuality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Catholic_Church">Roman Catholic Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Roman Catholic Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On October 31, 1999, in <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a>, Germany, the Lutheran World Federation – of which the ELCA is a member – signed the <i><a href="/wiki/Joint_Declaration_on_the_Doctrine_of_Justification" title="Joint Declaration on the Doctrine of Justification">Joint Declaration on the Doctrine of Justification</a></i> with the Roman Catholic Church. The statement is an attempt to reconcile a historical theological divide between the two faiths. The <i>Declaration</i> also states that the mutual condemnations between 16th century Lutherans and the Roman Catholic Church no longer apply to those that have signed onto the document. This was part of a series of "Lutheran-Roman Catholic Dialogues" have been taking place on an official basis every few years with statements and booklets on various theological topics published since 1966.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lutheran_Church.E2.80.93Missouri_Synod">Lutheran Church–Missouri Synod</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Lutheran Church–Missouri Synod">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The differences between the Evangelical Lutheran Church in America (ELCA) and the <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Church%E2%80%93Missouri_Synod" title="Lutheran Church–Missouri Synod">Lutheran Church–Missouri Synod</a> (LCMS) arise from theological, historical, and cultural factors. The LCMS was briefly in fellowship with the former The American Lutheran Church, one of the ELCA predecessor bodies from 1969 to the early 1980s. Although the denominations cooperate through Lutheran World Relief and some university/college student ministries and military chaplaincy, they are not officially in communion with each other.</p>
<p>When the first Lutheran immigrants came to North America, they started church bodies that reflected, to some degree, the churches they left behind in Europe. Many maintained until the early 20th century their immigrant languages. They sought pastors from the "old country" until patterns for the education of clergy could be developed here. Eventually, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seminaries" title="Seminaries">seminaries</a> and church colleges were established in many places to prepare pastors to serve congregations.</p>
<p>The earliest predecessor synod of the Evangelical Lutheran Church in America was constituted on August 25, 1748, in Philadelphia under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Muhlenberg" title="Henry Muhlenberg">Henry Muhlenberg</a>, known as the "Patriarch of American Lutheranism". It was known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministerium_of_Pennsylvania" title="Ministerium of Pennsylvania">Ministerium of Pennsylvania and Adjacent States</a>. The earliest nationwide "synod" or "union of synods" was established in 1820 as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Synod_(Lutheran)" title="General Synod (Lutheran)">General Synod</a>, followed later by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Council_(Lutheran)" title="General Council (Lutheran)">General Council</a>. The ELCA was created in 1988 by the merging of the 2.85-million-member Lutheran Church in America (1962), 2.25-million-member The American Lutheran Church (1960), and the 100,000-member Association of Evangelical Lutheran Churches (1978). Previously, the ALC and LCA in the early 1960s came into being as a result of two mergers of eight smaller ethnically-based Lutheran bodies composed of German, Norwegian, Danish, Finnish, Swedish, Slovak, Dutch, and others organized over 150 years. Some of these smaller ethnically based bodies previously had ecumenical arrangements involving the Missouri Synod.</p>
<p>The LCMS was established in 1847 by German immigrants fleeing the forced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prussian_Union_(Evangelical_Christian_Church)" title="Prussian Union (Evangelical Christian Church)">Prussian Union</a> between Lutherans and Reformed church members in European Germany, who later settled in <a href="/wiki/Perry_County,_Missouri" title="Perry County, Missouri">Perry County, Missouri</a>. It grew through immigration, offspring, and church mergers while participating in some, but not all of the dialogues, controversies, and compromises which affected the various predecessors of the ELCA during the 19th and 20th Centuries. In the mid-1970s the <a href="/wiki/Seminex" title="Seminex">Seminex</a> controversy at their Concordia Seminary in St. Louis over use of historical-critical biblical study led to the formation of the Association of Evangelical Lutheran Churches, later one of the predecessor bodies of the ELCA. The LCMS is the second largest Lutheran church body in North America with 2.1 million adherents.</p>
<p>The ELCA tends to be more involved in ecumenical endeavors than the LCMS, which prohibits its clergy from worshiping in ecumenical gatherings. The ELCA is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_World_Federation" title="Lutheran World Federation">Lutheran World Federation</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Council_of_Churches" title="World Council of Churches">World Council of Churches</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Council_of_Churches" title="National Council of Churches">National Council of Churches of Christ, USA</a>. The LCMS rejects these as being <a href="/wiki/Indifferentism" title="Indifferentism">unionist</a>.</p>
<p>Both the LCMS and the ELCA have policies relating to clergy sexual misconduct. Perry C. Francis, a former ELCA pastor turned professor, along with psychology professor Tracy D. Baldo, published the results of their 1994 study of clergy sexual misconduct in the journal <i>Pastoral Psychology</i>. Out of 270 ELCA and 117 LCMS clergy surveyed, 13.7% of ELCA clergy and 4.3% of LCMS clergy admitted to sexual misconduct with another person since they began their ministry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistics">Statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Over 500,000 people have left the ELCA since the church's human sexuality decision in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stats_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stats-52">[52]</a></sup> Approximately 700 congregations have voted to withdraw from the ELCA, representing about 7% of its 2009 congregational membership. The ELCA currently has a baptized membership of 3,668,034 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elca.org_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-elca.org-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2015, Pew Research estimated that 1.4% of the U.S population, or roughly 4.4 million adherents, identify with the ELCA, and another .7%, or 2.2 million adherents, identify with mainline Lutheranism in general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Religious_Landscape_Study_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Religious_Landscape_Study-6">[6]</a></sup> Today, the ELCA has approximately 9,320 congregations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elca.org_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-elca.org-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
