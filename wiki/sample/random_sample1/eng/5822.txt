<p>The <b>United States Army North</b> is a formation of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> Service Component Command of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Northern_Command" title="United States Northern Command">United States Northern Command</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-organization_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-organization-2">[2]</a></sup> As the joint force land component command, it is responsible for homeland defense and defense support of civil authorities. It is garrisoned at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Sam_Houston" title="Fort Sam Houston">Fort Sam Houston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. From early January 1943, when it was first created during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, under the command of <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_general_(United_States)" title="Lieutenant general (United States)">Lieutenant General</a> <a href="/wiki/Mark_W._Clark" title="Mark W. Clark">Mark Wayne Clark</a>, until 2004, it was previously designated as the <b>United States Fifth Army</b>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Army_North&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Army_North&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The United States Fifth Army was one of the principal formations of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">U.S. Army</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_and_Middle_East_theatre_of_World_War_II" title="Mediterranean and Middle East theatre of World War II">Mediterranean during World War II</a>, and was the first American <a href="/wiki/Field_army" title="Field army">field army</a> ever to be activated outside of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was officially activated on 5 January 1943 at <a href="/wiki/Oujda" title="Oujda">Oujda</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Morocco" title="French Morocco">French Morocco</a> and made responsible for the defence of <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>. It was also given the responsibility for planning the American part of the <a href="/wiki/Allied_invasion_of_Italy" title="Allied invasion of Italy">invasion of mainland Italy</a>, and therefore was not involved in the <a href="/wiki/Allied_invasion_of_Sicily" title="Allied invasion of Sicily">Allied invasion of Sicily</a> (codenamed <a href="/wiki/Allied_invasion_of_Sicily" title="Allied invasion of Sicily">Operation Husky</a>), where it was instead assigned the role of training combat troops destined for Sicily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The United States Fifth Army was initially commanded by <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_general_(United_States)" title="Lieutenant general (United States)">Lieutenant General</a> <a href="/wiki/Mark_W._Clark" title="Mark W. Clark">Mark Wayne Clark</a>, who would lead the Fifth Army for nearly two years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and was to experience some of the toughest fighting of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, where it was engaged on the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Campaign_(World_War_II)" title="Italian Campaign (World War II)">Italian Front</a>, which was, in many ways, often more reminiscent of the <a href="/wiki/Trench_warfare" title="Trench warfare">trench warfare</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. Writing to Lieutenant General <a href="/wiki/Jacob_L._Devers" title="Jacob L. Devers">Jacob L. Devers</a> (American deputy to <a href="/wiki/Field_marshal_(United_Kingdom)" title="Field marshal (United Kingdom)">Field Marshal</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Maitland_Wilson" title="Henry Maitland Wilson">Sir Henry Maitland Wilson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Theater_of_Operations" title="Mediterranean Theater of Operations">Mediterranean Theater</a> commander)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> in late March 1944, Clark explained the difficulties of the fighting in Italy so far, which could be said of the whole campaign. They were, he claimed, "<i>Terrain, weather, carefully prepared defensive positions in the mountains, determined and well-trained enemy troops, grossly inadequate means at our disposal while on the offensive, with approximately equal forces to the defender.</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Fifth Army first saw action during the <a href="/wiki/Allied_invasion_of_Italy#Salerno_landings" title="Allied invasion of Italy">Salerno landings</a> (Operation Avalanche), the assault landings at <a href="/wiki/Salerno" title="Salerno">Salerno</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Allied_invasion_of_Italy" title="Allied invasion of Italy">Allied invasion of Italy</a>, in September 1943. Due to the comparatively low numbers of American troops available in the Mediterranean Theater it was made up of one American and one British corps. They were the <a href="/wiki/VI_Corps_(United_States)" title="VI Corps (United States)">U.S. VI Corps</a>, under <a href="/wiki/Major_general_(United_States)" title="Major general (United States)">Major General</a> <a href="/wiki/Ernest_J._Dawley" title="Ernest J. Dawley">Ernest J. Dawley</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/X_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="X Corps (United Kingdom)">British X Corps</a>, under <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant-general_(United_Kingdom)" title="Lieutenant-general (United Kingdom)">Lieutenant-General</a> <a href="/wiki/Richard_McCreery" title="Richard McCreery">Richard L. McCreery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> At Salerno, VI Corps landed on the right flank, and X Corps on the left flank. Progress was initially slow, due in part to a lack of initiative by Dawley, the VI Corps commander, and due also to heavier than expected German resistance. However, heavy naval and air bombardment, along with a parachute drop by elements of the <a href="/wiki/82nd_Airborne_Division" title="82nd Airborne Division">U.S. 82nd Airborne Division</a>, had saved the forces from any danger of being driven back into the sea, combined with the approach of the <a href="/wiki/Eighth_Army_(United_Kingdom)" title="Eighth Army (United Kingdom)">British Eighth Army</a>, under <a href="/wiki/General_(United_Kingdom)" title="General (United Kingdom)">General</a> <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Montgomery" title="Bernard Montgomery">Bernard Montgomery</a> (the Eighth Army had landed further south in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Baytown" title="Operation Baytown">Operation Baytown</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> six days before Avalanche), the <a href="/wiki/10th_Army_(Wehrmacht)" title="10th Army (Wehrmacht)">German 10th Army</a> began to retreat. On 20 September, by which time the Fifth and Eighth Armies had linked up,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Major General Dawley, VI Corps commander, was relieved of his command by Clark. Dawley was temporarily made deputy army commander, and was soon replaced in command of VI Corps by Major General <a href="/wiki/John_P._Lucas" title="John P. Lucas">John P. Lucas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>Progress was then good for a couple of weeks and the Fifth Army crossed the <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Line" title="Barbara Line">Barbara Line</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Volturno_Line" title="Volturno Line">Volturno Line</a> until the Germans turned, stood and fought. They had established a position on the <a href="/wiki/Winter_Line" title="Winter Line">Winter Line</a> (also known as the Gustav Line), which included the formidable defensive positions at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_San_Pietro_Infine" title="Battle of San Pietro Infine">San Pietro Infine</a> in the Liri Valley and at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Monte_Cassino" title="Battle of Monte Cassino">Monte Cassino</a>. By this point, Fifth Army had been reinforced by a second American corps, <a href="/wiki/II_Corps_(United_States)" title="II Corps (United States)">II Corps</a>, commanded by Major General <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Keyes" title="Geoffrey Keyes">Geoffrey Keyes</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> By the end of November Clark's Fifth Army had almost doubled in size, with the addition of French General <a href="/wiki/Alphonse_Juin" title="Alphonse Juin">Alphonse Juin</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Expeditionary_Corps_(1943-1944)" title="French Expeditionary Corps (1943-1944)">French Expeditionary Corps</a>, from 130,247 men to 243,827.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> With the failure of the first operations to capture Monte Cassino, an attempt was made to exploit the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> preponderance in seapower before the coming <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Normandy" title="Invasion of Normandy">invasion of Normandy</a> robbed the Mediterranean of the naval forces necessary for an <a href="/wiki/Amphibious_warfare" title="Amphibious warfare">amphibious assault</a> to seize <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>VI Corps, with its experience of amphibious landings at Salerno, was chosen for the assault and withdrawn from the line, replaced by the French Expeditionary Corps. They made a second attempt to capture Monte Cassino in conjunction with the amphibious assault by VI Corps, which again failed. VI Corps <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Anzio" title="Battle of Anzio">landed at Anzio</a>, unopposed, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> on 22 January 1944 in <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Anzio" title="Battle of Anzio">Operation Shingle</a>, and suffered many of the same problems as had been seen at Salerno. A perceived lack of initiative on the part of the commander, Major General Lucas, combined with worries about the Germans catching VI Corps off balance if it advanced too far inland resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Beachhead" title="Beachhead">beachhead</a> being bottled up. The Germans launched a series of attacks and counterattacks, with both sides sustaining heavy losses, and nearly breached the last beachhead defences before again being driven off by heavy naval and air support. The fault, however, <i>"was not due to Lucas's incompetence; it was due instead to wishful thinking, faulty operational planning, and the <a href="/wiki/German_Army_(Wehrmacht)" title="German Army (Wehrmacht)">German army</a>'s ability to respond forcefully and aggressively.</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the failure of Shingle, a large reorganisation took place. Previously the <a href="/wiki/Apennine_Mountains" title="Apennine Mountains">Apennines</a> had been the rough dividing line between Fifth and Eighth Armies. However, the dividing line was shifted westwards, to allow the concentration of both armies on the western side of Italy for maximum firepower to break through to Rome. <a href="/wiki/V_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="V Corps (United Kingdom)">British V Corps</a> was left on the Adriatic coast to pin down any German units there. Fifth Army was relieved of responsibility for Cassino<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and the final phases of that battle saw <a href="/wiki/Indian_Army_during_World_War_II" title="Indian Army during World War II">Indian</a>, <a href="/wiki/2nd_New_Zealand_Division" title="2nd New Zealand Division">New Zealand</a> and finally <a href="/wiki/II_Corps_(Poland)" title="II Corps (Poland)">Polish troops</a> thrown against the fortress. Fifth Army also lost McCreery's British X Corps at this time, since it was felt that having exclusively American-organised units under Fifth Army and British-organised units under Eighth Army would ease logistics.</p>
<p>The breakthrough was achieved during the spring of 1944. Coordinated assaults by all the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied forces</a>, except V Corps, which was confined to a holding action, broke through. II Corps attacked along the coast, the French Expeditionary Corps, in a classic demonstration of <a href="/wiki/Mountain_warfare" title="Mountain warfare">mountain warfare</a>, broke through on the right flank of Fifth Army, and VI Corps, now commanded by Major General <a href="/wiki/Lucian_Truscott" title="Lucian Truscott">Lucian K. Truscott</a>, broke out of the Anzio beachhead. By early summer, Allied forces were well on their way to capturing Rome.</p>
<p>At this point, one of the more controversial incidents in the history of Fifth Army occurred. The strategic conception of <a href="/wiki/General_(United_Kingdom)" title="General (United Kingdom)">General</a> <a href="/wiki/Harold_Alexander,_1st_Earl_Alexander_of_Tunis" title="Harold Alexander, 1st Earl Alexander of Tunis">Sir Harold Alexander</a>, commanding the <a href="/wiki/Allied_Armies_in_Italy" title="Allied Armies in Italy">Allied Armies in Italy</a> (later redesigned <a href="/wiki/15th_Army_Group" title="15th Army Group">15th Army Group</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> was that the forces of VI Corps, coming out of Anzio, would trap the retreating German forces, and leave them to be annihilated by the advancing Fifth and Eighth Armies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> However, in contravention of orders, Clark diverted units of VI Corps towards Rome, leaving a small blocking force to attempt to stop the Germans. It failed to do so, and the German forces were able to escape and reestablish a coherent line to the north of Rome. Clark claimed that there were significant German threats which necessitated the diversion, but many believe that he was primarily glory-seeking by being the first to liberate Rome.</p>
<p>Two days after Rome fell, on 4 June 1944, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Overlord" title="Operation Overlord">Operation Overlord</a> was launched.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The strategic conception of Overlord called for a supporting operation to be mounted by invading southern France. In order to do so, forces would have to be withdrawn from the Allied Armies in Italy. In the end, VI Corps was withdrawn, forming the nucleus of the field forces of the <a href="/wiki/Seventh_United_States_Army" title="Seventh United States Army">U.S. Seventh Army</a> for the invasion of the <a href="/wiki/French_Riviera" title="French Riviera">French Riviera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Dragoon" title="Operation Dragoon">Operation Dragoon</a>. The French Expeditionary Corps was also withdrawn, to allow its men to be used to for the <a href="/wiki/First_Army_(France)" title="First Army (France)">French First Army</a>, a follow-up formation for Dragoon. In slightly less than two months, the strength of the Fifth Army dropped from 248,989 down to 153,323.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> However, the 25,000-strong <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Expeditionary_Force" title="Brazilian Expeditionary Force">Brazilian Expeditionary Force</a>, under <a href="/wiki/Marshal_(Brazil)" title="Marshal (Brazil)">Marshal</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joao_Baptista_Mascarenhas_de_Morais" title="Joao Baptista Mascarenhas de Morais">J.B. Mascarenhas de Morais</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> as well as other divisions had arrived to align with <a href="/wiki/IV_Corps_(United_States)" title="IV Corps (United States)">U.S. IV Corps</a> (which had arrived in June) under Major General <a href="/wiki/Willis_D._Crittenberger" title="Willis D. Crittenberger">Willis D. Crittenberger</a>, so two corps were maintained within Fifth Army.</p>
<p>In the second half of 1944, the Allied forces fighting on the Italian Front within the U.S. Fifth Army and British Eighth Army resembled more a multi-national force being constituted by: Americans (including segregated African/and/Japanese-Americans), British, French, members of French and British colonies (New Zealanders, Canadians, Indians, Gurkhas, Black Africans, Moroccans, Algerians, Jews and Arabs from the <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_Palestine" title="Mandatory Palestine">British Mandate in Palestine</a>, South Africans, <a href="/wiki/Rhodesia" title="Rhodesia">Rhodesians</a>), as well as Brazilians and exiled forces from Poland, Greece, former Czechoslovakia and anti-fascist Italians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Germans reestablished their line across Italy at the level of <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rimini" title="Rimini">Rimini</a>. The Allied forces spent another winter, after fierce fighting in the summer and autumn in front of the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Line" title="Gothic Line">Gothic Line</a>, frustrated at their lack of ability to break through. This time Fifth Army, with <a href="/wiki/XIII_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="XIII Corps (United Kingdom)">British XIII Corps</a> under command, led by Lieutenant-General <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Kirkman" title="Sidney Kirkman">Sidney Kirkman</a> (whose relationship with Clark was apparently very stormy) was straddling the Apennines, with many of its units occupying high, exposed positions which were miserable to garrison. That winter also saw a significant change of command. Lieutenant General Clark moved to command 15th Army Group (previously styled the Allied Armies in Italy), and Lieutenant General Lucian K. Truscott was appointed to command Fifth Army in his place. Truscott would command the Army from 16 December 1944 until the war's end. Another change came in January 1945 when XIII Corps reverted to control of British Eighth Army, which had also seen many changes in composition and command, and was now commanded by Lieutenant-General Richard L. McCreery.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Spring_1945_offensive_in_Italy" title="Spring 1945 offensive in Italy">final offensive</a> of the Italian campaign, launched in April 1945, against the German <a href="/wiki/Army_Group_C" title="Army Group C">Army Group C</a>, the Eighth Army initiated the main offensive on the Adriatic coast, and then the Fifth Army also broke through the German defenses around Bologna. The German units, in the main, were pinned against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Po_River" title="Po River">Po River</a> and destroyed, or at the very least deprived of their transport and heavy weapons, which effectively made many of them useless. II Corps units raced through Milan towards the French frontier and the great port of <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a>. IV Corps pushed due north through Verona, Vicenza and as far as Bolzano and to the Brenner Pass, where they linked up with elements of the U.S. Seventh Army, under Lieutenant General <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Patch" title="Alexander Patch">Alexander Patch</a>.</p>
<p>Its role in Italy cost Fifth Army dearly. It suffered 109,642 casualties in 602 days of combat, of which 19,475 were killed in action. The Fifth Army headquarters returned to the United States in September 1945. Fifth Army was deactivated on 2 October 1945 at <a href="/wiki/Camp_Myles_Standish" title="Camp Myles Standish">Camp Myles Standish</a>, Massachusetts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the informal athletic competitions held between units of the European and North African theaters, the Fifth Army was among the most successful, winning titles in baseball, boxing, swimming and football during the 1944 season. The football championship was gained after a victory over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/12th_Air_Force" title="12th Air Force">12th Air Force</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Spaghetti_Bowl_(American_football)" title="Spaghetti Bowl (American football)">Spaghetti Bowl</a> on January 1, 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_war">Post war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Army_North&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Army's next role was considerably less violent, and it was reactivated on 11 June 1946 at <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> under the command of <a href="/wiki/Major_general_(United_States)" title="Major general (United States)">Major General</a> <a href="/wiki/John_P._Lucas" title="John P. Lucas">John P. Lucas</a>, who had commanded <a href="/wiki/VI_Corps_(United_States)" title="VI Corps (United States)">U.S. VI Corps</a> in the early stages of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Anzio" title="Battle of Anzio">Battle of Anzio</a> (<a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Anzio" title="Battle of Anzio">Operation Shingle</a>) during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> before being relieved. It was redesignated as the <b>Fifth United States Army</b> on 1 January 1957. Its postwar role was as a command and control headquarters for <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Reserve" title="United States Army Reserve">U.S. Army Reserve</a> units, formally responsible for the training of many Army troops and also the ground defense of part of the continental United States. In June 1971, the Fifth Army moved to its current base at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Sam_Houston" title="Fort Sam Houston">Fort Sam Houston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Redesignation_in_2004">Redesignation in 2004</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Army_North&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Redesignation in 2004">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2004, Fifth Army transferred its Reserve preparation obligations to <a href="/wiki/First_United_States_Army" title="First United States Army">First Army</a>, and became responsible for homeland defense and Defense Support of Civil Authorities (DSCA) as United States Army North, the Army Service Component Command of United States Northern Command. <a href="/wiki/Joint_Task_Force-Civil_Support" title="Joint Task Force-Civil Support">Joint Task Force-Civil Support</a>, a subordinate command, is designated as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> (DoD) command element for Department of Defense assistance to the overall federal response to a state governments request for assistance in the event of a catastrophic chemical, biological, nuclear or high yield explosive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CBRNE" title="CBRNE">CBRNE</a> emergency. The command also has a subordinate Contingency Command Post (CCP), known as Task Force-51, which is responsible for responding to all hazards incidents that require DOD assistance. TF-51 can be employed as an all-hazards task force or a Joint Task Force (JTF) with joint augmentation.</p>
