<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">United States federal executive departments</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The <b>United States federal executive departments</b> are among the oldest primary units of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executive_branch" title="Executive branch">executive branch</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">federal government of the United States</a>—the Departments of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">State</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_War" title="United States Department of War">War</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Department of the Treasury">Treasury</a> all having been established within a few weeks of each other in 1789.</p>
<p>Federal executive departments are analogous to <a href="/wiki/Ministry_(government_department)" title="Ministry (government department)">ministries</a> common in <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_system" title="Parliamentary system">parliamentary</a> or <a href="/wiki/Semi-presidential_system" title="Semi-presidential system">semi-presidential</a> systems but, with the United States being a <a href="/wiki/Presidential_system" title="Presidential system">presidential system</a>, their heads otherwise equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Minister_(government)" title="Minister (government)">ministers</a>, do not form a <a href="/wiki/Ministry_(collective_executive)" title="Ministry (collective executive)">government</a> (in a parliamentary sense) nor are they led by a <a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">head of government</a> separate from the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a>. The heads of the federal executive departments, known as secretaries of their respective department, form the traditional <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_United_States" title="Cabinet of the United States">Cabinet of the United States</a>, an executive organ that serves at the disposal of the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">president</a> and normally acts as an advisory body to the presidency.</p>
<p>Since 1792, by statutory specification, the cabinet constituted a <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_line_of_succession" title="United States presidential line of succession">line of succession</a> to the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">presidency</a>, after the <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="Speaker of the United States House of Representatives">Speaker of the House</a> and the <a href="/wiki/President_pro_tempore_of_the_United_States_Senate" title="President pro tempore of the United States Senate">president pro tempore of the Senate</a>, in the event of a vacancy in both the presidency and the <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">vice presidency</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">Constitution</a> refers to these officials when it authorizes the President, in Article II, section 2, to "require the Opinion, in writing, of the principal Officer in each of the executive Departments, upon any Subject relating to the Duties of their respective Offices." In brief, they and their organizations are the administrative arms of the President.</p>

<ol>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> as the first in line;</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="Speaker of the United States House of Representatives">Speaker of the House</a>; and</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/President_pro_tempore_of_the_United_States_Senate" title="President pro tempore of the United States Senate">President pro tempore of the Senate</a>.</li>
</ol>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Executive_Departments_of_the_past">Executive Departments of the past</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_federal_executive_departments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Executive Departments of the past">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="wikitable">
<tr>
<th>Department</th>
<th>Dates of Operation</th>
<th>Notes</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<td nowrap="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_War" title="United States Department of War">Department of War</a></td>
<td nowrap="nowrap">1789–1947</td>
<td>Split into <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Army" title="United States Department of the Army">Department of the Army</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Air_Force" title="United States Department of the Air Force">Department of the Air Force</a> in the National Security Act of 1947</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td nowrap="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/United_States_Post_Office_Department" title="United States Post Office Department">Post Office Department</a></td>
<td nowrap="nowrap">1792–1971</td>
<td>Reorganized as quasi-independent agency, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td nowrap="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Commerce_and_Labor" title="United States Department of Commerce and Labor">Department of Commerce and Labor</a></td>
<td nowrap="nowrap">1903–1913</td>
<td>Divided between <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Commerce" title="United States Department of Commerce">Department of Commerce</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Labor" title="United States Department of Labor">Department of Labor</a></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td nowrap="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Army" title="United States Department of the Army">Department of the Army</a></td>
<td nowrap="nowrap">1947–1949</td>
<td rowspan="3">From 1947-1949, these departments were executive departments with non-cabinet level secretaries who reported to the civilian Secretary of Defense with cabinet rank but no department. From 1949 on, they were <i>Military Departments</i> within the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td nowrap="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Navy" title="United States Department of the Navy">Department of the Navy</a></td>
<td nowrap="nowrap">1798–1949</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td nowrap="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Air_Force" title="United States Department of the Air Force">Department of the Air Force</a></td>
<td nowrap="nowrap">1947–1949</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td nowrap="nowrap">Department of Health, Education, and Welfare</td>
<td nowrap="nowrap">1953–1979</td>
<td>Divided between <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" title="United States Department of Health and Human Services">Department of Health and Human Services</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Education" title="United States Department of Education">Department of Education</a></td>
</tr>
