<p>The <b>United States Army Intelligence and Security Command</b> (<b>INSCOM</b>) is a direct reporting unit that conducts <a href="/wiki/Military_intelligence" title="Military intelligence">intelligence</a>, security, and information operations for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a> commanders and national decision makers. INSCOM is headquartered at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Belvoir" title="Fort Belvoir">Fort Belvoir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>.</p>
<p>INSCOM is an organization within both the United States Army and the <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Agency" title="National Security Agency">National Security Agency</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>'s unified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SIGINT" title="SIGINT">signals intelligence</a> organization. Within the NSA, INSCOM and its counterparts in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> , <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marine_Corps_Intelligence" title="Marine Corps Intelligence">Marine Corps Intelligence</a>, are known as <a href="/wiki/Central_Security_Service" title="Central Security Service">Central Security Service</a>. INSCOM's budget has been estimated to be $6 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spies4hire_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-spies4hire-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission">Mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Army_Intelligence_and_Security_Command&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>INSCOM collects intelligence information in all intelligence disciplines to provide unit commanders intelligence for the battlefield and the focus of combat power. The organization also conducts intelligence production activities, ranging from intelligence preparation of the battlefield to situation development, SIGINT analysis, imagery exploitation, and science and technology intelligence production. INSCOM also has significant responsibilities in <a href="/wiki/Counterintelligence" title="Counterintelligence">counterintelligence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Force_protection" title="Force protection">force protection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_warfare" title="Electronic warfare">electronic warfare</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Information_warfare" title="Information warfare">information warfare</a>. Additionally, INSCOM supports force modernization and training.</p>
<p>INSCOM's stated vision for operations includes: (1) conducting and supporting relevant intelligence, security and information operations for U.S. Army, joint and combined forces; (2) optimizing national/theater/tactical partnerships; (3) exploiting leading edge technology; and (4) meeting the challenge of today, tomorrow and the 21st Century.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Army_Intelligence_and_Security_Command&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Merger_and_creation_of_INSCOM">Merger and creation of INSCOM</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Army_Intelligence_and_Security_Command&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Merger and creation of INSCOM">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On January 1, 1977, the United States Army Intelligence and Security Command (INSCOM) was organized at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arlington_Hall_Station" title="Arlington Hall Station">Arlington Hall Station</a>, Virginia, to provide the U.S. Army with a single organization for conducting multi-discipline intelligence, security operations, and electronic warfare at the level above corps. The new organization merged the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Security_Agency" title="U.S. Army Security Agency">U.S. Army Security Agency</a>, the signal intelligence and signal security organizations previously located at Arlington Hall, Virginia, the U.S. Army Intelligence Agency, a counterintelligence and human intelligence agency based at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Meade,_Maryland" title="Fort Meade, Maryland">Fort Meade, Maryland</a>, and several intelligence production units formerly controlled by the Assistant Chief of Staff for Intelligence and U.S. Army Forces Command. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brigadier_General" title="Brigadier General">Brigadier General</a> (later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_General" title="Major General">Major General</a>) William I. Rolya, former commanding general of the Army Security Agency, became INSCOM’s first commanding general.</p>
<p>On October 1, 1977, the former U.S. Army Intelligence Agency headquarters was integrated into INSCOM, and the command established a unified intelligence production element, the Intelligence and Threat Analysis Center, on 1 January 1978. Additionally, INSCOM assumed command of three military intelligence groups located overseas: the 66th Military Intelligence Group in Germany, the 470th Military Intelligence Group in Panama, and the 500th Military Intelligence Group in Japan. These groups were transformed into multidisciplinary units by incorporating former Army Security Agency assets into the previously existing elements. A fourth such group, the 501st Military Intelligence Brigade,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inscom.army.mil_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-inscom.army.mil-4">[4]</a></sup> was soon organized in <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>. All of these groups were eventually reorganized and redesignated as brigades.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parapsychologic_research">Parapsychologic research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Army_Intelligence_and_Security_Command&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Parapsychologic research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In association with the <a href="/wiki/Defense_Intelligence_Agency" title="Defense Intelligence Agency">Defense Intelligence Agency</a>, and under the leadership of commanding general <a href="/wiki/Albert_Stubblebine" title="Albert Stubblebine">Albert Stubblebine</a>, INSCOM attempted to use parapsychologic methods such as <a href="/wiki/Remote_viewing" title="Remote viewing">remote viewing</a> in operation <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Center_Lane" title="Center Lane">Center Lane</a></i>. This was done as late as 1981. Other U.S. intelligence services attempted similar projects during the same period, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Stargate_Project" title="Stargate Project">Stargate Project</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">Central Intelligence Agency</a>.</p>
