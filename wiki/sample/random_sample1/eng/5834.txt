<p>The <b>United States Department of Housing and Urban Development</b> (<b>HUD</b>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Cabinet" title="United States Cabinet">Cabinet</a> department in the Executive branch of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">federal government</a>. Although its beginnings were in the House and Home Financing Agency, it was founded as a Cabinet department in 1965, as part of the "<a href="/wiki/Great_Society" title="Great Society">Great Society</a>" program of President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyndon_Johnson" title="Lyndon Johnson">Lyndon Johnson</a>, to develop and execute policies on housing and metropolises.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Housing_and_Urban_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The department was established on September 9, 1965, when <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> signed the Department of Housing and Urban Development Act<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> into law. It stipulated that the department was to be created no later than November 8, sixty days following the date of enactment. The actual implementation was postponed until January 13, 1966, following the completion of a special study group report on the federal role in solving urban problems.</p>
<p>HUD is administered by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Housing_and_Urban_Development" title="United States Secretary of Housing and Urban Development">United States Secretary of Housing and Urban Development</a>. <a href="/wiki/Julian_Castro" title="Julian Castro">Julian Castro</a>, a former Mayor of San Antonio, Texas, is the current and 16th United States Secretary of Housing and Urban Development since July 28, 2014. Dr. <a href="/wiki/Ben_Carson" title="Ben Carson">Ben Carson</a> has been nominated as his successor in the incoming <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Trump</a> Administration. Its headquarters is located in the <a href="/wiki/Robert_C._Weaver_Federal_Building" title="Robert C. Weaver Federal Building">Robert C. Weaver Federal Building</a>. Some important milestones for HUD's development include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>June 27, 1934 – The <a href="/wiki/National_Housing_Act_of_1934" title="National Housing Act of 1934">National Housing Act</a> creates the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Housing_Administration" title="Federal Housing Administration">Federal Housing Administration</a>, which helps provide mortgage insurance on loans made by FHA-approved lenders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>September 1, 1937 – <a href="/wiki/Housing_Act_of_1937" title="Housing Act of 1937">Housing Act of 1937</a> creates the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Housing_Authority" title="United States Housing Authority">United States Housing Authority</a>, which helps enact slum-clearance projects and construction of low-rent housing</li>
<li>February 3, 1938 – The National Housing Act Amendments of 1938 is signed into law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The law creates the <a href="/wiki/Fannie_Mae" title="Fannie Mae">Federal National Mortgage Association</a> (FNMA), which provides a secondary market to the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Housing_Administration" title="Federal Housing Administration">Federal Housing Administration</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>July 27, 1947 – The <a href="/wiki/Housing_and_Home_Finance_Agency" title="Housing and Home Finance Agency">Housing and Home Finance Agency</a> is established through <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Reorganization_Plan_Number_3&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Reorganization Plan Number 3 (page does not exist)">Reorganization Plan Number 3</a></li>
<li>July 15, 1949 – The <a href="/wiki/Housing_Act_of_1949" title="Housing Act of 1949">Housing Act of 1949</a> is enacted to help eradicate slums and promote community development and redevelopment programs</li>
<li>August 2, 1954 – The <a href="/wiki/Housing_Act_of_1954" title="Housing Act of 1954">Housing Act of 1954</a> establishes comprehensive planning assistance</li>
<li>September 23, 1959 – The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Housing_Act_of_1959&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Housing Act of 1959 (page does not exist)">Housing Act of 1959</a> allows funds for elderly housing</li>
<li>September 2, 1964 – The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Housing_Act_of_1964&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Housing Act of 1964 (page does not exist)">Housing Act of 1964</a> allows rehabilitation loans for homeowners</li>
<li>August 10, 1965 – The <a href="/wiki/Housing_and_Urban_Development_Act_of_1965" title="Housing and Urban Development Act of 1965">Housing and Urban Development Act of 1965</a> instituted several major expansions in federal housing programs</li>
<li>September 1965 – HUD is created as a cabinet-level agency by the Department of Housing and Urban Development Act</li>
<li>April 1968 – The <a href="/wiki/Fair_Housing_Act" title="Fair Housing Act">Fair Housing Act</a> is passed to ban discrimination in housing</li>
<li>During 1968 – The <a href="/wiki/Housing_and_Urban_Development_Act_of_1968" title="Housing and Urban Development Act of 1968">Housing and Urban Development Act of 1968</a> establishes the <a href="/wiki/Government_National_Mortgage_Association" title="Government National Mortgage Association">Government National Mortgage Association</a> (Ginnie Mae)</li>
<li>August 1969 – The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Brooke_Amendment&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Brooke Amendment (page does not exist)">Brooke Amendment</a> establishes that low income families only pay no more than 25 percent of their income for rent</li>
<li>August 1974 – <a href="/wiki/Housing_and_Community_Development_Act_of_1974" title="Housing and Community Development Act of 1974">Housing and Community Development Act of 1974</a> allows community development <a href="/wiki/Block_grant" title="Block grant">block grants</a> and help for urban homesteading</li>
<li>October 1977 – The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Housing_and_Community_Act_of_1977&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Housing and Community Act of 1977 (page does not exist)">Housing and Community Act of 1977</a> sets up Urban Development Grants and continues elderly and handicapped assistance</li>
<li>July 1987 – The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McKinney-Vento_Homeless_Assistance_Act" title="McKinney-Vento Homeless Assistance Act">Stewart B. McKinney Homeless Assistance Act</a> gives help to communities to deal with homelessness. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission">Mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Housing_and_Urban_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>HUD’s mission is to create strong, sustainable, inclusive communities and quality affordable homes for all. HUD is working to strengthen the housing market to bolster the economy and protect consumers; meet the need for quality affordable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rental_home" title="Rental home">rental homes</a>; utilize housing as a platform for improving quality of life; build inclusive and sustainable communities free from discrimination; and transform the way HUD does business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_Programs">Major Programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Housing_and_Urban_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Major Programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The major program offices are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Community Planning and Development: Many major affordable housing and homelessness programs are administered under Community Planning and Development. These include the <a href="/wiki/Community_Development_Block_Grant" title="Community Development Block Grant">Community Development Block Grants</a> (CDBG), the HOME program, Shelter Plus Care, Emergency Shelter Grants (ESG), <a href="/wiki/Section_8_(housing)" title="Section 8 (housing)">Section 8</a> Moderate Rehabilitation Single Room Occupancy program (Mod Rehab SRO), and Housing Opportunities for Persons with AIDS (HOPWA).</li>
<li>Housing: This office is responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Housing_Administration" title="Federal Housing Administration">Federal Housing Administration</a>; mission regulation of <a href="/wiki/Fannie_Mae" title="Fannie Mae">Fannie Mae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Mac" title="Freddie Mac">Freddie Mac</a>; regulation of <a href="/wiki/Manufactured_housing" title="Manufactured housing">manufactured housing</a>; administration of <a href="/wiki/Public_housing_in_the_United_States" title="Public housing in the United States">multifamily housing programs</a>, including Supportive Housing for the Elderly (Section 202) and Supportive Housing for Persons with Disabilities (Section 811); and Healthcare facility loan insurance.</li>
<li>Public and Indian Housing: This office administers the public housing program <a href="/wiki/HOPE_VI" title="HOPE VI">HOPE VI</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Section_8_(housing)" title="Section 8 (housing)">Housing Choice Voucher Program</a> (formerly –  yet more popularly –  known as <a href="/wiki/Section_8_(housing)" title="Section 8 (housing)">Section 8</a>), Project-Based Vouchers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and individual loan programs housing block grants<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> for Indian tribes, Native Hawaiians and Alaskans.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Office_of_Fair_Housing_and_Equal_Opportunity" title="Office of Fair Housing and Equal Opportunity">Office of Fair Housing and Equal Opportunity</a>: This office enforces Federal laws against discrimination based on race, color, religion, national origin, sex, disability and familial status.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/HUD_USER" title="HUD USER">Policy Development and Research</a> (PD&amp;R): This office is responsible for maintaining current information on housing needs, market conditions, and existing programs, as well as conducting research on priority housing and community development issues through the <a href="/wiki/HUD_USER" title="HUD USER">HUD USER</a> Clearinghouse.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Government_National_Mortgage_Association" title="Government National Mortgage Association">Government National Mortgage Association</a> (Ginnie Mae)</li>
<li>Healthy Homes and Lead Hazard Control.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Partnership_for_Advancing_Technology_in_Housing" title="Partnership for Advancing Technology in Housing">Partnership for Advancing Technology in Housing</a> (developed in 1998)</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Office_of_Inspector_General">Office of Inspector General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Housing_and_Urban_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Office of Inspector General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> enacted the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Inspector_General_Act_of_1978&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Inspector General Act of 1978 (page does not exist)">Inspector General Act of 1978</a> to ensure integrity and efficiency in government. The Inspector General is appointed by the President and subject to Senate confirmation. The Inspector General is responsible for conducting and supervising audits, investigations, and inspections relating to the programs and operations of HUD. The OIG is to examine, evaluate and, where necessary, critique these operations and activities, recommending ways for the Department to carry out its responsibilities in the most effective, efficient, and economical manner possible.</p>
<p>The mission of the Office of Inspector General (OIG) is to:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Promote the integrity, efficiency and effectiveness of HUD programs and operations to assist the Department in meeting its mission</li>
<li>Detect and prevent waste, fraud, and abuse</li>
<li>Seek administrative sanctions, civil recoveries and/ or criminal prosecution of those responsible for waste, fraud and abuse in HUD programs and operations</li>
</ul>
<p>The OIG accomplishes its mission by conducting investigations pertinent to its activities; by keeping Congress, the Secretary, and the public fully informed of its activities, and by working with staff (in this case of HUD) in achieving success of its objectives and goals. David A. Montoya, who was sworn in on December 1, 2011, is the current Inspector General.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>The 203(k) program offers low down payment loans to primary resident owner occupants or nonprofit groups to buy and renovate a house.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>One of the most successful HUD programs over the years has been the Multifamily Housing Service Coordinator Program. Each year since 1992, HUD has included in its Notice of Fund Availability (NOFA), a specific allocation of dollars to allow sponsors and owners of HUD multifamily housing for the elderly the opportunity to hire a Service Coordinator. The Service Coordinator provides case management and coordinative services to elderly residents, particularly to those who are "frail" and "at-risk" allowing them to remain in their current residence. As a result, thousands of senior citizens throughout the United States have been given the opportunity to continue to live independently instead of in an institutional facility such as a nursing home. Professional organizations such as the American Association of Service Coordinators provide support to HUD Service Coordinator through education, training, networking and advocacy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>HUD has experimented with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enterprise_Zone" title="Enterprise Zone">Enterprise Zones</a> granting economic incentives to economically depressed urban areas, but this function has largely been taken over by states.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Due to HUD's lending practices, it occasionally takes possession of a home when a lender it insures forecloses. Such properties are then generally sold off to the highest bidder through the <a href="/wiki/HUD_auction" title="HUD auction">HUD auction</a> process. Buyers of HUD homes as their primary residences who make a full-price offer to HUD using <a href="/wiki/Federal_Housing_Administration" title="Federal Housing Administration">FHA</a>-insured mortgage financing receive seller concessions from HUD enabling them to use only a $100 down payment.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Housing_and_Urban_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A scandal arose in the 1990s when at least 700 houses were sold for profit by real estate speculators taking the loans; at least 19 were arrested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimesharlem_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimesharlem-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (October 2014)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> The scandal devastated the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harlem" title="Harlem">Harlem</a> housing market and with $70 million in HUD loans going into <a href="/wiki/Default_(finance)" title="Default (finance)">default</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-villagevoicehud_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-villagevoicehud-15">[15]</a></sup> Critics said that HUD's lax oversight of their program allowed the fraud to occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimescuomo_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimescuomo-16">[16]</a></sup> and in 1997, the HUD Inspector General issued a report saying: "The program design encourages risky property deals, land sale and refinance schemes, overstated property appraisals, and phony or excessive fees."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-villagevoice_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-villagevoice-17">[17]</a></sup> In June 1993, HUD Secretary <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cisneros" title="Henry Cisneros">Henry Cisneros</a> admitted that "HUD has in many cases exacerbated the declining quality of life in America."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimesleaderofhud_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimesleaderofhud-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1996, Vice President <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a>, referring to public housing projects, declared that, "These crime-infested monuments to a failed policy are killing the neighborhoods around them."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>HUD Assistant Secretary for Fair Housing <a href="/wiki/Roberta_Achtenberg" title="Roberta Achtenberg">Roberta Achtenberg</a> has been quoted as saying "...HUD walks a tightrope between free speech and fair housing. We are ever mindful of the need to maintain the proper balance between these rights." Libertarian critic <a href="/wiki/James_Bovard" title="James Bovard">James Bovard</a> commented that, "The more aggressive HUD becomes, the fewer free speech rights Americans have. Many words and phrases are now effectively forbidden in real estate ads. ... Apparently, there are two separate versions of the Bill of Rights -- one for private citizens and the other for federal bureaucrats and politicians"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i> called HUD "New York City's worst landlord" and "the #1 worst in the United States" based upon decrepit conditions of buildings and questionable eviction practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-villagevoice_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-villagevoice-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2010, HUD started auctioning off delinquent home mortgage loans, defined as at least 90 days past due, to the highest bidder. It sold 2,000 loans in six national auctions. In 2012, this sale was massively increased under a "Distressed Asset Stabilization Program" (DASP), and the 100,000 loans sold as of 2014 have netted 8.8 billion for the FHA, rebuilding cash reserves that had been depleted by loan defaults. The second stated and eponymous objective is to stabilize communities, by requiring purchasers to service the loans in a manner that stabilizes the surrounding communities by getting the loans to re-perform, renting the home to the borrower, gifting the property to a land bank or paying off the loans in full.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aljaz_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-aljaz-20">[20]</a></sup> An audit published August 2014 found "only about 11 percent of the loans sold through DASP [were] considered 're-performing'".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aljaz_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-aljaz-20">[20]</a></sup> "Rather than defaulting— [FHA] keeps many of the properties they’re tied to from going through the typical foreclosure process. As a result, the FHA might actually be diverting housing stock from first-time homebuyers, the very group it was formed to serve..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aljaz_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-aljaz-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
