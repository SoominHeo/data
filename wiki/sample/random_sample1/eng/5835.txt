<p>The <b>United States Soccer Federation</b> (<b>USSF</b>), commonly referred to as <b>U.S. Soccer</b>, is the official <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sport_governing_body" title="Sport governing body">governing body</a> of the sport of <a href="/wiki/Soccer_in_the_United_States" title="Soccer in the United States">soccer in the United States</a>. With headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, the <a href="/wiki/FIFA" title="FIFA">FIFA</a> member governs U.S. <a href="/wiki/Amateur" title="Amateur">amateur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Professional" title="Professional">professional</a> soccer, including the <a href="/wiki/United_States_men%27s_national_soccer_team" title="United States men's national soccer team">men's</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_women%27s_national_soccer_team" title="United States women's national soccer team">women's</a>, youth, <a href="/wiki/United_States_national_beach_soccer_team" title="United States national beach soccer team">beach soccer</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_national_futsal_team" title="United States national futsal team">futsal</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Men%27s_Paralympic_Soccer_Team" title="United States Men's Paralympic Soccer Team">Paralympic</a> national teams. U.S. Soccer sanctions <a href="/wiki/Referee_(association_football)" title="Referee (association football)">referees</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soccer_tournament" title="Soccer tournament">soccer tournaments</a> for most soccer leagues in the United States. The U.S. Soccer Federation also administers and operates the <a href="/wiki/Lamar_Hunt_U.S._Open_Cup" title="Lamar Hunt U.S. Open Cup">Lamar Hunt U.S. Open Cup</a>, which was first held in 1914.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization_and_governance">Organization and governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Soccer_Federation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Organization and governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>U.S. Soccer is governed by a Board of Directors that administers the affairs of U.S. Soccer. People in key leadership positions include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sunil_Gulati" title="Sunil Gulati">Sunil Gulati</a> — President</li>
<li>Carlos Cordeiro — Executive Vice President<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Dan Flynn — CEO and Secretary General</li>
</ul>
<p>U.S. Soccer is a member of the worldwide soccer body <a href="/wiki/FIFA" title="FIFA">FIFA</a> and the North American soccer body <a href="/wiki/CONCACAF" title="CONCACAF">CONCACAF</a>, and also has a relationship with the U.S. Olympic Committee and the International Olympic Committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Soccer_Federation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>U.S. Soccer was originally known as the United States Football Association. It formed on April 5, 1913<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and on August 15 of that year was accepted as one of the earliest member organizations of FIFA and the first from North and Central America. The affiliation was temporary and at the following year's FIFA Congress in 1914, the USFA, as it was abbreviated at the time, was accepted as a full FIFA member.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The governing body of the sport in the United States added the word "soccer" to its name in 1945, when it became the United States Soccer Football Association. It dropped the word <i>football</i> from its name in 1974 to become known as the United States Soccer Federation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>U.S. Soccer has hosted several global soccer tournaments, including the <a href="/wiki/1994_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1994 FIFA World Cup">1994 FIFA World Cup</a>, the <a href="/wiki/FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup" title="FIFA Women's World Cup">FIFA Women's World Cup</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup_1999" title="FIFA Women's World Cup 1999">1999</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup_2003" title="FIFA Women's World Cup 2003">2003</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Football_at_the_Summer_Olympics" title="Football at the Summer Olympics">Summer Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/1984_Summer_Olympics" title="1984 Summer Olympics">1984</a> and <a href="/wiki/1996_Summer_Olympics" title="1996 Summer Olympics">1996</a>.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_men%27s_national_soccer_team" title="United States men's national soccer team">United States men's national soccer team</a></div>
<p>The United States national team was first assembled in 1885 to play <a href="/wiki/Canada_men%27s_national_soccer_team" title="Canada men's national soccer team">Canada</a> in the first international match held outside the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The men's national team was invited to the inaugural World Cup in <a href="/wiki/1930_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1930 FIFA World Cup">1930</a> and qualified for the World Cup in <a href="/wiki/1934_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1934 FIFA World Cup">1934</a>, finishing third place in 1930 out of 13 teams participating. In <a href="/wiki/1950_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1950 FIFA World Cup">1950</a> the United States scored one of its most surprising victories with a <a href="/wiki/United_States_v_England_(1950_FIFA_World_Cup)" title="United States v England (1950 FIFA World Cup)">1–0 win</a> over heavily favored <a href="/wiki/England_national_football_team" title="England national football team">England</a>, who were amongst the world's best sides at the time.</p>
<p>The United States failed to reach another World Cup until an upstart team qualified for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1990_World_Cup" title="1990 World Cup">1990 World Cup</a> with the "goal heard around the world" scored by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Caligiuri" title="Paul Caligiuri">Paul Caligiuri</a> against <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago_national_football_team" title="Trinidad and Tobago national football team">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, which started the modern era of soccer in the United States. The 1990 men's national team was quickly disposed of at the World Cup, but nonetheless had qualified for its first World Cup in 40 years.</p>
<p>The United States hosted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1994_World_Cup" title="1994 World Cup">1994 World Cup</a>, setting total and average attendance records that still stand, including drawing 94,194 fans to the <a href="/wiki/1994_FIFA_World_Cup_Final" title="1994 FIFA World Cup Final">final</a>. The United States made a surprising run to the second round with a shocking victory over <a href="/wiki/Colombia_national_football_team" title="Colombia national football team">Colombia</a> which saw <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Escobar" title="Andrés Escobar">Andrés Escobar</a>, the player responsible for the United States' first goal (an own goal), later shot to death in his homeland.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/1998_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1998 FIFA World Cup">1998</a> saw another disappointing addition to the history of the men's national team as it finished last out of the 32 teams that qualified for the World Cup. This embarrassment, which included a total collapse of team chemistry and leadership, led to the firing of manager <a href="/wiki/Steve_Sampson" title="Steve Sampson">Steve Sampson</a>.</p>
<p>The U.S. team hired <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Arena" title="Bruce Arena">Bruce Arena</a>, who had won the first two <a href="/wiki/MLS_Cup" title="MLS Cup">MLS Cups</a> in <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Soccer" title="Major League Soccer">Major League Soccer</a> history, and who went on to become the most successful United States men's national team manager in history. In <a href="/wiki/2002_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2002 FIFA World Cup">2002</a> Bruce Arena led a mix of veterans and MLS-seasoned youth to a quarterfinal appearance, dispatching contenders <a href="/wiki/Portugal_national_football_team" title="Portugal national football team">Portugal</a> in group play and archrivals <a href="/wiki/Mexico_national_football_team" title="Mexico national football team">Mexico</a> in the Round of 16, before losing a closely fought game with eventual runners-up <a href="/wiki/Germany_national_football_team" title="Germany national football team">Germany</a> in the quarterfinal.</p>
<p>The team looked to match or surpass that feat in <a href="/wiki/2006_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2006 FIFA World Cup">2006</a>; the U.S. was drawn into a group with <a href="/wiki/Italy_national_football_team" title="Italy national football team">Italy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic_national_football_team" title="Czech Republic national football team">Czech Republic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ghana_national_football_team" title="Ghana national football team">Ghana</a>. The United States lost to the Czech Republic 3–0 in their opening game, drew Italy, 1–1, in their second game (a match that saw two U.S. players and an Italian player <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_card_(soccer)" title="Red card (soccer)">red carded</a>), and lost to Ghana, 2–1. The United States did not advance out of the group, but were the only team to face eventual winner Italy without losing. In the wake of the team's disappointing performance, Arena's contract was not renewed.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bob_Bradley" title="Bob Bradley">Bob Bradley</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C.D._Chivas_USA" title="C.D. Chivas USA">Chivas USA</a> manager and Arena's assistant manager with the men's national team, eventually succeeded Arena in 2007. The U.S. qualified for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2010_World_Cup" title="2010 World Cup">2010 World Cup</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> winning the <a href="/wiki/2010_FIFA_World_Cup_qualification_%E2%80%93_CONCACAF_Fourth_Round" title="2010 FIFA World Cup qualification – CONCACAF Fourth Round">CONCACAF qualifying tournament</a>. At the World Cup, the Americans tied England 1–1, tied <a href="/wiki/Slovenia_national_football_team" title="Slovenia national football team">Slovenia</a>, 2–2. and then won their group by defeating <a href="/wiki/Algeria_national_football_team" title="Algeria national football team">Algeria</a> 1–0 on a stoppage time goal by Landon Donovan. In the Round of 16, the United States played <a href="/wiki/Ghana_national_football_team" title="Ghana national football team">Ghana</a>, and fell 2–1 in extra time.</p>
<p>Entering the <a href="/wiki/2014_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2014 FIFA World Cup">2014 World Cup</a> in Brazil, the U.S team won all three friendly "send-off" matches leading up to the competition: 2–0, over <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan_national_football_team" title="Azerbaijan national football team">Azerbaijan</a>, 2–1, over <a href="/wiki/Turkey_national_football_team" title="Turkey national football team">Turkey</a>, and 2–1 over fellow World Cup participant and defending <a href="/wiki/Africa_Cup_of_Nations" title="Africa Cup of Nations">African</a> champions <a href="/wiki/Nigeria_national_football_team" title="Nigeria national football team">Nigeria</a>. They were led at the time by <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Klinsmann" title="Jürgen Klinsmann">Jürgen Klinsmann</a>, who helped lead West Germany to victory in the <a href="/wiki/1990_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1990 FIFA World Cup">1990 World Cup</a> and was the first player to score at least three goals in three consecutive World Cups.</p>
<p>During the 2014 World Cup, the U.S. won their first match against Ghana, 2–1. <a href="/wiki/Clint_Dempsey" title="Clint Dempsey">Clint Dempsey</a> scored in the first minute of the match giving the U.S. the early lead. Ghana did not respond until the 82nd minute scoring the equalizer goal. The U.S. then reclaimed the lead, thanks to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Anthony_Brooks" title="John Anthony Brooks">John Brooks</a> scoring the game-winning goal off his head just four minutes later in the 86th minute to regain the lead and take the match. The U.S. gained three points for their win and was off to a great start in the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_of_Death" title="Group of Death">Group of Death</a>" claimed by critics for the teams the U.S. would have to go through (<a href="/wiki/Germany_national_football_team" title="Germany national football team">Germany</a>, Ghana, and <a href="/wiki/Portugal_national_football_team" title="Portugal national football team">Portugal</a>).</p>
<p>The second match of the World Cup for the U.S. was a different story. Portugal claimed the early lead, with <a href="/wiki/Nani" title="Nani">Nani</a> scoring in the 5th minute to take the early 1–0 lead. It wasn't till the 64th minute till the U.S. scored the equalizing goal, thanks to <a href="/wiki/Jermaine_Jones" title="Jermaine Jones">Jermaine Jones</a>, tying the match at 1 apiece. The U.S. then claimed the lead on a goal by Clint Dempsey again, scoring in the 81st minute to take a 2–1 lead. However, in the final minute of extra time, the world player of the year, <a href="/wiki/Cristiano_Ronaldo" title="Cristiano Ronaldo">Cristiano Ronaldo</a> drilled a perfect cross to teammate <a href="/wiki/Silvestre_Varela" title="Silvestre Varela">Silvestre Varela</a> who headed in the tying goal, making the final score 2–2. The tie gave each team a point in the overall standings, bringing the U.S. to 4 points total, and gave Portugal their first point of the World Cup having lost their opening match to Germany, 4–0. The U.S. claimed a spot in the knockout round in spite of a 1–0 loss to eventual champion Germany in their final group game due to them winning the tiebreaker with Portugal. However, they bowed out the tournament in the round of 16 in a 2–1 loss to <a href="/wiki/Belgium_national_football_team" title="Belgium national football team">Belgium</a>. Goalkeeper <a href="/wiki/Tim_Howard" title="Tim Howard">Tim Howard</a> helped the U.S. keep a 0–0 tie at full time. In extra time, there were two Belgian goals. The U.S. struck back with a goal by 19-year-old phenom <a href="/wiki/Julian_Green" title="Julian Green">Julian Green</a> but could not manage another goal. Klinsman was let go as US MNT Director of Coaching and has been replaced by Bruce Arena in November 2016.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._women.27s_national_team">U.S. women's national team</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Soccer_Federation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: U.S. women's national team">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_women%27s_national_soccer_team" title="United States women's national soccer team">United States women's national soccer team</a></div>
<p>The women's national team has won three Women's World Cups in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup_1991" title="FIFA Women's World Cup 1991">1991</a>, 1999 and 2015 (placing second in 2011 and third in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup_1995" title="FIFA Women's World Cup 1995">1995</a>, 2003, and 2007); the Olympic Gold Medal in 1996, 2004, 2008, and 2012; and seven <a href="/wiki/Algarve_Cup" title="Algarve Cup">Algarve Cups</a> and six <a href="/wiki/CONCACAF_Women%27s_Gold_Cup" title="CONCACAF Women's Gold Cup">CONCACAF Women's Gold Cups</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup" title="FIFA Women's World Cup">FIFA Women's World Cup</a> was inaugurated in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup_1991" title="FIFA Women's World Cup 1991">1991</a>, and the women's national team became the first team to win the prize after beating <a href="/wiki/Norway_women%27s_national_football_team" title="Norway women's national football team">Norway</a> in the final. That tournament helped demonstrate the high caliber of play in women's soccer. In 1999, the United States hosted the <a href="/wiki/FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup" title="FIFA Women's World Cup">FIFA Women's World Cup</a> for the first time. During their tournament run, the women's national team established a new level of popularity for the women's game, culminating in a final against <a href="/wiki/China_women%27s_national_football_team" title="China women's national football team">China</a> that drew 90,185 fans, an all-time attendance record for a women's sports event, to a sold-out <a href="/wiki/Rose_Bowl_(stadium)" title="Rose Bowl (stadium)">Rose Bowl</a>. After neither team scored in regulation or extra time, the final went to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penalty_shootout_(football)" title="Penalty shootout (football)">penalty shootout</a>, which the United States won 5–4. The <a href="/wiki/Goal_celebration" title="Goal celebration">celebration</a> by <a href="/wiki/Brandi_Chastain" title="Brandi Chastain">Brandi Chastain</a> after she converted the winning penalty, in which she took off her shirt, revealing her sports bra in the process, is one of the more famous images in U.S. women's sports.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Paralympic_National_Team">U.S. Paralympic National Team</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Soccer_Federation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: U.S. Paralympic National Team">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_men%27s_Paralympic_soccer_team" title="United States men's Paralympic soccer team">United States men's Paralympic soccer team</a></div>
<p>The U.S. Paralympic Soccer Team is an elite level program for men that selects players from across the United States in preparation for International standard competition. The team competes in 7-a-side football. The squad is composed of athletes who have cerebral palsy or have suffered a stroke or traumatic brain injury. The program is Coached by Stuart Sharp under the oversight of the U.S. Soccer Federation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Headquarters_and_national_training_center">Headquarters and national training center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Soccer_Federation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Headquarters and national training center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>U.S. Soccer House is located in two refurbished mansions at 1801 South Prairie Avenue in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> and serves as the headquarters for the U.S. Soccer Federation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003, U.S. Soccer opened their National Training Center at <a href="/wiki/StubHub_Center" title="StubHub Center">StubHub Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Carson,_California" title="Carson, California">Carson</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. The $130 million facility includes a <a href="/wiki/Soccer-specific_stadium" title="Soccer-specific stadium">soccer-specific stadium</a>, home to the MLS team <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Galaxy" title="Los Angeles Galaxy">Los Angeles Galaxy</a>. Additionally, four grass soccer fields, a FieldTurf soccer field and a general training area are specifically dedicated to U.S. Soccer. Both the senior and youth men's and women's US national teams hold regular camps at StubHub Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>U.S. Soccer was also exploring a possibility of building the National Training and Coaching Development Center in <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Kansas" title="Kansas City, Kansas">Kansas City, Kansas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> On April 9, 2015, the Training Center received final approval from the local governments. U.S. Soccer agreed to a 20-year lease, with the project set to break ground in 2016 and finishing some time in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_leagues">Professional leagues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Soccer_Federation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Professional leagues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite the growth of men's and women's professional soccer in the United States in the last few decades, by far the largest category of soccer in the United States, at least in terms of participation, is boys and girls youth soccer. Though organized locally by organizations all over the United States, there are two main youth soccer organizations working nationwide through affiliated local associations. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Youth_Soccer_Association" title="United States Youth Soccer Association">United States Youth Soccer Association</a> boasts over three million players between the ages of five and 19, while <a href="/wiki/American_Youth_Soccer_Organization" title="American Youth Soccer Organization">American Youth Soccer Organization</a> has more than 300,000 players between the ages of four and 19. This makes soccer one of the most played sports by children in the United States.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Men">Men</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Soccer_Federation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Men">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Soccer" title="Major League Soccer">Major League Soccer</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_American_Soccer_League" title="North American Soccer League">North American Soccer League</a>, and <a href="/wiki/United_Soccer_League" title="United Soccer League">United Soccer League</a></div>
<p>The professional first-division league in North America is <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Soccer" title="Major League Soccer">Major League Soccer</a> which, as of the current 2016 season, has 17 teams in the U.S. and 3 in Canada. The league expanded to 20 teams for the 2015 season with the addition of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_FC" title="New York City FC">New York City FC</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orlando_City_Soccer_Club_(2015)" title="Orlando City Soccer Club (2015)">Orlando City SC</a>, and the exit of Chivas USA (A rebranded and new ownership group bought the rights to the franchise that will begin play in 2018). The league operates as a single-entity league which means MLS, and not the individual teams, holds the contracts on players. Two more franchises apart from the reorganized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Football_Club" title="Los Angeles Football Club">new LA based team</a> (formerly Chivas USA) are expected to begin play before 2018 (<a href="/wiki/Atlanta_United_FC" title="Atlanta United FC">Atlanta United FC</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minnesota_United_FC_(MLS)" title="Minnesota United FC (MLS)">Minnesota United FC</a> (moving from NASL)).</p>
<p>The professional second-division league in North America is the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Soccer_League" title="North American Soccer League">North American Soccer League</a> (NASL). The new NASL has no official tie to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_Soccer_League_(1968%E2%80%931984)" title="North American Soccer League (1968–1984)">former NASL</a> that operated from 1968 to 1984; though, some of the teams share names with their historic counterparts. Unlike MLS who is a single-entity operation, the new NASL, like the old NASL, has no salary cap and players are contracted by the individual teams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The current 2015 season is a split format (similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Apertura_and_Clausura" title="Apertura and Clausura">many leagues in Latin America</a>) that features nine U.S. and two Canadian teams. An additional team from <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Islanders" title="Puerto Rico Islanders">Puerto Rico</a> played in the league's 2011 and 2012 seasons, but has suspended operations. Previous to the reorganization of the NASL in 2009, the <a href="/wiki/USL_First_Division" title="USL First Division">USL First Division</a> operated as the professional second-division league in the United States. However, a dispute among its teams and ownership led to the creation of the NASL which applied for and was awarded by USSF second division status. The 2010 season was played as a combined USL/NASL league format before NASL officially separated in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> A U.S. based team, Miami FC, and a Puerto Rican team (owned by <a href="/wiki/Carmelo_Anthony" title="Carmelo Anthony">Carmelo Anthony</a>), PRFC, have been announced and are expected to begin play in 2016.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Soccer_Leagues" title="United Soccer Leagues">United Soccer Leagues</a> (USL) were a collection of five leagues spanning the lower divisions of men's professional soccer, as well as women's soccer and youth soccer. After the 2010 season, the USL folded its former First and <a href="/wiki/USL_Second_Division" title="USL Second Division">Second Divisions</a> into a new professional third-division league, <a href="/wiki/United_Soccer_League" title="United Soccer League">USL Pro</a>, that launched in 2011. At launch, it had 15 teams in all—11 on the U.S. mainland, three in Puerto Rico, and one in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antigua_Barracuda_FC" title="Antigua Barracuda FC">Antigua and Barbuda</a>—but the Puerto Rican teams, plagued by ownership and economic issues, were dropped from the league after 2011, and the Antigua team discontinued operations due to inability to win after 2013. Additionally in 2013 after only one year of operation, a U.S. based team, <a href="/wiki/VSI_Tampa_Bay_FC" title="VSI Tampa Bay FC">VSI Tampa Bay FC</a>, folded. However, in January 2013, USL and MLS reached an agreement to integrate USL Pro league competition with the MLS Reserve League spawning the creation of secondary teams directly affiliated with MLS franchises. This was done primarily to improve player development in North America, strengthen league competition and build ties between divisions in the American soccer pyramid. This multi-year deal encourages MLS and USL Pro team affiliations and player loans, and it will lead to more games for teams and to the development of American players. The deal has proven to be a boon for USL Pro, and in 2015, after a rebrand to <a href="/wiki/United_Soccer_League" title="United Soccer League">USL</a>, 24 teams are now participating in a healthy and stable 3rd division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MLS_USL_Deal_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-MLS_USL_Deal-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MLS_Partnership_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-MLS_Partnership-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>A fourth-division league in the United States is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USL_Premier_Development_League" title="USL Premier Development League">USL Premier Development League</a>, which as of 2015 is expected to have 58 U.S. teams, and six Canadian teams. Though the PDL does have some paid players, it also has many teams that are made up entirely or almost entirely of <a href="/wiki/College_soccer" title="College soccer">college soccer</a> players who use the league as an opportunity to play competitive soccer in front of professional scouts during the summer, while retaining amateur status and NCAA eligibility. Another fourth-division league in the United States is the <a href="/wiki/National_Premier_Soccer_League" title="National Premier Soccer League">National Premier Soccer League</a>.</p>
<p>In addition to MLS and the USL, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Adult_Soccer_Association" title="United States Adult Soccer Association">United States Adult Soccer Association</a> governs amateur soccer competition for adults throughout the United States, which is effectively the amateur fifth-division of soccer in the United States. The USASA sanctions regional tournaments that allow entry into the <a href="/wiki/Lamar_Hunt_U.S._Open_Cup" title="Lamar Hunt U.S. Open Cup">Lamar Hunt U.S. Open Cup</a>, the oldest continuous national soccer competition in the United States. Since 1914, the competition has been open to all U.S. Soccer affiliated clubs, and currently pits teams from all five levels of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_soccer_pyramid" title="American soccer pyramid">American soccer pyramid</a> against each other each year, similarly to England's <a href="/wiki/FA_Cup" title="FA Cup">FA Cup</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Women">Women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Soccer_Federation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Women's soccer in the United States has also been played at the professional level, but has not seen sustained success. The first two attempts at professional leagues lasted only three seasons each. The <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_United_Soccer_Association" title="Women's United Soccer Association">Women's United Soccer Association</a> (WUSA) was founded in 2001, but folded after its 2003 season. The second attempt, <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Professional_Soccer" title="Women's Professional Soccer">Women's Professional Soccer</a>, was founded in 2009, with involvement from many former WUSA figures. WPS folded in May 2012 after having suspended its planned 2012 season. The champion of WPS' first season in 2009 was <a href="/wiki/Sky_Blue_FC" title="Sky Blue FC">Sky Blue FC</a>, out of the New York–New Jersey area. They defeated the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Sol" title="Los Angeles Sol">Los Angeles Sol</a> 1–0 at The Home Depot Center in Carson, California. WPS launched with seven teams, all based in the United States. The Sol folded after the league's inaugural season, and two new teams joined for 2010, bringing WPS to eight teams. However, the 2010 season saw considerable instability, with another charter team, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Louis_Athletica" title="Saint Louis Athletica">Saint Louis Athletica</a>, folding during the season, champions <a href="/wiki/FC_Gold_Pride" title="FC Gold Pride">FC Gold Pride</a> folding after the season, and the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Red_Stars" title="Chicago Red Stars">Chicago Red Stars</a> deciding to regroup in the second-tier <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Premier_Soccer_League" title="Women's Premier Soccer League">Women's Premier Soccer League</a> (WPSL). The 2011 season, in which six teams based along the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a> played, was marked by low attendance for most of the season and conflict with <a href="/wiki/Dan_Borislow" title="Dan Borislow">Dan Borislow</a>, who had purchased the former <a href="/wiki/Washington_Freedom" title="Washington Freedom">Washington Freedom</a>, moved the team to <a href="/wiki/Miami_metropolitan_area" title="Miami metropolitan area">South Florida</a>, and renamed it <a href="/wiki/MagicJack_(WPS)" title="MagicJack (WPS)">magicJack</a>. The dispute between WPS and Borislow led the league to suspend the magicJack franchise, with Borislow responding by suing. The legal battle led WPS to suspend its 2012 season, with hopes of returning in 2013, but WPS soon decided to fold completely.</p>
<p>On November 21, 2012, U.S. Soccer, in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Soccer_Association" title="Canadian Soccer Association">Canadian Soccer Association</a> (CSA) and <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Football_Federation" title="Mexican Football Federation">Mexican Football Federation</a> (FMF), announced the formation of a new professional league for the 2013 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013_women.27s_league_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-2013_women.27s_league-22">[22]</a></sup> The league, unnamed at the time of the initial announcement but later unveiled as the <a href="/wiki/National_Women%27s_Soccer_League" title="National Women's Soccer League">National Women's Soccer League</a> (NWSL), launched in April 2013 with eight teams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013_women.27s_league_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-2013_women.27s_league-22">[22]</a></sup> Like WUSA and WPS, NWSL teams are privately owned, but in a departure from past league models, national federations are heavily involved in league financing and operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlisle_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carlisle-23">[23]</a></sup> All three federations are paying salaries for many of their respective national team members. U.S. Soccer committed to funding up to 24 national team members, with the CSA committing to paying 16 players and FMF pledging support for at least 12 and possibly as many as 16.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlisle_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carlisle-23">[23]</a></sup> This meant that each charter team was freed from having to pay salaries for up to seven players.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_women.27s_league_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP_women.27s_league-24">[24]</a></sup> In addition, U.S. Soccer houses the new league's front office, and schedules matches to avoid any possible conflict with international tournaments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlisle_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Carlisle-23">[23]</a></sup> NWSL teams are also generally playing in smaller stadiums and have fewer staffers than those in previous leagues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlisle_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-Carlisle-23">[23]</a></sup> U.S. Soccer president Sunil Gulati indicated that at the lower end of the salary scale, players would essentially be semi-professional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_women.27s_league_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-AP_women.27s_league-24">[24]</a></sup> Four of the league's charter teams have WPS ties—the Boston Breakers, Chicago Red Stars, a revived Sky Blue FC, and the Western New York Flash. The other four are in <a href="/wiki/FC_Kansas_City" title="FC Kansas City">Kansas City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portland_Thorns_FC" title="Portland Thorns FC">Portland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Reign_FC" title="Seattle Reign FC">Seattle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Washington_Spirit" title="Washington Spirit">Washington, D.C.</a>, with the Portland team run by the <a href="/wiki/Portland_Timbers" title="Portland Timbers">Portland Timbers</a> of MLS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carlisle_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-Carlisle-23">[23]</a></sup> The NWSL expanded to nine teams for 2014 by adding the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Dash" title="Houston Dash">Houston Dash</a>, run by the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Dynamo" title="Houston Dynamo">Houston Dynamo</a> of MLS.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Soccer_Federation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concussions">Concussions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Soccer_Federation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Concussions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2014 parents and former players filed a Class Action Lawsuit against the United States Soccer Federation, <a href="/wiki/FIFA" title="FIFA">FIFA</a>, and other Soccer Organizations for failure to create policies that would prevent, evaluate and manage <a href="/wiki/Concussion" title="Concussion">concussion</a> injuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Soccer is second only to American football in the number of concussion injuries per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MLS_relationship">MLS relationship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Soccer_Federation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: MLS relationship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The USSF has been accused by representatives of the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Soccer_League" title="North American Soccer League">North American Soccer League</a>, among others, of unfairly protecting MLS's leading role in American professional soccer. Among their concerns is that the USSF benefits from financial dealings with MLS that it does not have with other leagues, giving it an apparent incentive to protect MLS from competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> This includes the contract that the USSF has with MLS's <a href="/wiki/Soccer_United_Marketing" title="Soccer United Marketing">Soccer United Marketing</a>(SUM) subsidiary in which most USSF sponsorship, television licensing and royalty revenues (outside of its apparel deal with <a href="/wiki/Nike,_Inc." title="Nike, Inc.">Nike, Inc.</a>) are paid through SUM. The USSF reported $15,433,754 in revenues through the SUM relationship in its 2014 audited financial report.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, the NASL took issue with proposed USSF rule changes reportedly making it harder to gain co-equal "Division 1" status with MLS that would increase the NASL's influence within the USSF as well as presumably allow more access to international competition and larger media and sponsorship contracts, calling the draft proposal "...an anti-competitive bait and switch, with the purpose of entrenching MLS’s monopoly position at the very time when the NASL is threatening to become a significant competitor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Seats on the USSF's Professional Council governing committee are also based proportionally on pyramid level, giving MLS more votes when choosing the two professional league representatives on the USSF's Board of Directors. In 2015, those representatives are MLS Commissioner Don Garber and Alec Papadakis, CEO of the <a href="/wiki/United_Soccer_League" title="United Soccer League">United Soccer League</a> that announced an affiliation with MLS in 2015.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_competitiveness">International competitiveness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Soccer_Federation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: International competitiveness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>High-profile international soccer figures including former USMNT Head Coach <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Klinsmann" title="Jürgen Klinsmann">Jürgen Klinsmann</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> former <a href="/wiki/LA_Galaxy" title="LA Galaxy">LA Galaxy</a> head coach and USMNT Head Coach <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Arena" title="Bruce Arena">Bruce Arena</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manchester_City" title="Manchester City">Manchester City</a> manager and former <a href="/wiki/2011_FIFA_Ballon_d%27Or" title="2011 FIFA Ballon d'Or">FIFA World Coach of the Year</a> <a href="/wiki/Pep_Guardiola" title="Pep Guardiola">Pep Guardiola</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> have expressed beliefs that the top-down structure of soccer developed and managed by the USSF in the United States, including pressure to have the best American players in MLS rather than higher-quality leagues in other countries, is retarding the nation's competitiveness in international soccer.</p>
<p>Conversely, Klinsmann has been criticized in turn by MLS representatives for recommending that American players leave MLS development systems to pursue professional careers in Europe in order to test themselves against higher levels of players in preparation for international competition. In 2015, MLS Commissioner Don Garber said, "I do believe our national team coach has a short-term objective. That's what he's hired to do. That doesn't mean next week, but it's to win the Gold Cup, it's to have the best possible team in 2018. And our goals and objectives are broader than that, and that's why we agree on some things but don't agree on others."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January of 2017 <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Arena" title="Bruce Arena">Bruce Arena</a> invited only MLS players to train with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USMNT" title="USMNT">USMNT</a> during the annual January training camp.</p>
