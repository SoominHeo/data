<p><b>Hedylidae</b>, the "American moth-butterflies", is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">family</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insects" title="Insects">insects</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">lepidopteran</a> order, representing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">superfamily</a> <b>Hedyloidea</b>. They have traditionally been viewed as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extant_taxon" title="Extant taxon">extant</a> <a href="/wiki/Sister_group" title="Sister group">sister group</a> of the butterfly superfamilies <a href="/wiki/Papilionoidea" title="Papilionoidea">Papilionoidea</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hesperioidea" title="Hesperioidea">Hesperioidea</a>. In 1986, Scoble combined all species into a single genus <i><b>Macrosoma</b></i>, comprising 35 currently recognized and entirely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neotropical" title="Neotropical">Neotropical</a> species, as a novel concept of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butterflies" title="Butterflies">butterflies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scoble86_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scoble86-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hedylidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Hedylidae</b> were previously treated as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">tribe</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geometridae" title="Geometridae">Geometridae</a>: Oenochrominae, the "Hedylicae"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scoble86_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scoble86-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Prout<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> considered they might even merit treatment as their own family. Scoble first considered them to be a hitherto unrecognised group of butterflies and also suggested Hedylidae might possibly constitute the <a href="/wiki/Sister_group" title="Sister group">sister group</a> of the "true" butterflies (<a href="/wiki/Papilionoidea" title="Papilionoidea">Papilionoidea</a>), rather than of (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hesperioidea" title="Hesperioidea">Hesperioidea</a> + Papilionoidea). Weintraub and Miller<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> argued against this placement (but see<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>). In 1995, Weller and Pashley<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> found that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular" title="Molecular">molecular</a> data did indeed place Hedylidae with the butterflies and a more comprehensive study in 2005<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> based on 57 exemplar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxa" title="Taxa">taxa</a>, three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genes" title="Genes">genes</a> and 99 <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphological</a> characters, recovered the genus <i>Macrosoma</i> as sister to the ("<a href="/wiki/Papilionoidea" title="Papilionoidea">true butterflies</a>" + <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hesperiidae" title="Hesperiidae">"skippers"</a>). However, the most recent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogeny" title="Phylogeny">phylogenetic</a> analyses suggest that both skippers and hedylids are true butterflies belonging within the clade Papilionoidea, as without these two groups the traditionally circumscribed Papilionoidea is <a href="/wiki/Paraphyly" title="Paraphyly">paraphyletic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since there are no obvious gaps between supposed species groups, according to basic morphological structure, Scoble (1986) synonymised the five pre-existing genera of Hedylidae (33 of which had been <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">described</a> in <i>Phellinodes</i>) into just one genus. However, a phylogenetic analysis of all <i>Macrosoma</i> species is still needed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclatural_notes">Nomenclatural notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hedylidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nomenclatural notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoological_nomenclature" title="Zoological nomenclature">zoological nomenclature</a>, numerous junior <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homonym_(zoology)" title="Homonym (zoology)">homonyms</a> of <i>Macrosoma</i> (Hübner, 1818) exist,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> (<i>Macrosoma</i> Leach 1819 (a <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptile</a>), <i>Macrosoma</i> de Haan 1826 (<a href="/wiki/Odonata" title="Odonata">Odonata</a>), <i>Macrosoma</i> Robineau-Desvoidy 1830 (<i>Macrosoma multisulcata</i> Berlese 1913 and <i>M. floralis</i>, Diptera: <a href="/wiki/Muscidae" title="Muscidae">Muscidae</a>), <i>Macrosoma</i> Brandt 1835 (<a href="/wiki/Coelenterata" title="Coelenterata">Coelenterata</a>), <i>Macrosoma</i> Hope 1837 (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coleoptera" title="Coleoptera">Coleoptera</a>), <i>Macrosoma</i> Lioy 1864 or 1865 (Diptera: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarcophagidae" title="Sarcophagidae">Sarcophagidae</a>), Macrosoma Hammer 1979<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> (<i>M.rugosa</i>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acarina" title="Acarina">Acarina</a>: Oribatidae). To add to this potential confusion in lists of names, there exist two junior homonyms of <i>Hedyle</i> Guenée, 1857: <i>Hedyle</i> Bergh, 1895 (<a href="/wiki/Sea_slug" title="Sea slug">sea slugs</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">order</a> Opisthobranchia: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">superfamily</a> "Acochlidioidea", <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">family</a> Hedylopsidae Odhner, 1952<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> that are currently placed in the genus <i>Hedylopsis</i> Thiele, 1931),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and <i>Hedyle</i> Malmgren 1865 (a <a href="/wiki/Polychaete" title="Polychaete">polychaete</a> worm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The sea slug family name "Hedylidae Bergh, 1895" (type species <i>Hedyle weberi</i> Bergh, 1895) is thus also invalid.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology_and_identification">Morphology and identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hedylidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Morphology and identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The eggs of hedylid moths have an upright configuration and are variable in shape: in <i>Macrosoma inermis</i> they are particularly narrow and spindle-shaped<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scoble90a_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scoble90a-15">[15]</a></sup> resembling those of some <a href="/wiki/Pieridae" title="Pieridae">Pieridae</a> and in the case of <i>M. tipulata</i> they are more barrel-shaped,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lourido07_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lourido07-16">[16]</a></sup> like certain <a href="/wiki/Nymphalidae" title="Nymphalidae">Nymphalidae</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> resemble probably <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergently</a> those of <a href="/wiki/Apaturinae" title="Apaturinae">Apaturinae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scoble90a_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scoble90a-15">[15]</a></sup> Adult hedylids resemble <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geometridae" title="Geometridae">geometer</a> moths. They share many morphological and <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> characteristics both with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">superfamilies</a> <a href="/wiki/Papilionoidea" title="Papilionoidea">Papilionoidea</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hesperioidea" title="Hesperioidea">Hesperioidea</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Insect#Morphology" title="Insect">abdomen</a> is very long and slim, like many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neotropical" title="Neotropical">Neotropical</a> butterflies of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">subfamilies</a> <a href="/wiki/Nymphalidae" title="Nymphalidae">Ithomiinae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heliconiinae" title="Heliconiinae">Heliconiinae</a>, hence the name of one <i>Macrosoma</i> species "<i>heliconiaria</i>". Unlike other butterflies however, the <a href="/wiki/Insect#Morphology" title="Insect">antennae</a> are un-<a href="/wiki/Antenna_(biology)#Insects" title="Antenna (biology)">clubbed</a>, but rather <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(biology)#Insects" title="Antenna (biology)">filiform</a> or <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(biology)#Insects" title="Antenna (biology)">bipectinate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scoble95_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scoble95-17">[17]</a></sup> Unlike the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">family</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geometridae" title="Geometridae">Geometridae</a>, in which they had been placed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geometridae" title="Geometridae">geometer</a> expert L.B.Prout, hedylids lack <a href="/wiki/Tympanal_organ" title="Tympanal organ">tympanic organs</a> at the base of the abdomen, but have them on the wings (see under <a href="/wiki/Hedylidae#Behaviour" title="Hedylidae">Behaviour</a>). Unlike other butterflies, however, (except in the unique case of the remarkable Australian skipper butterfly <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euschemon_rafflesia" title="Euschemon rafflesia">Euschemon rafflesia</a></i> whose males possess these structures), the single-spined <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wing_coupling_(Lepidoptera_anatomy)#frenulum" title="Wing coupling (Lepidoptera anatomy)">frenulum</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wing_coupling_(Lepidoptera_anatomy)#retinaculum" title="Wing coupling (Lepidoptera anatomy)">retinaculum</a> is not lost or reduced in males, except in three <i>Macrosoma</i> species where there is no functional wing coupling system; the retinaculum is always lost in females and the frenulum may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vestigial" title="Vestigial">vestigial</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scoble86_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Scoble86-1">[1]</a></sup> The family have been fully catalogued<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scoble90a_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Scoble90a-15">[15]</a></sup> and illustrated in an identification guide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scoble90b_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scoble90b-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hedylidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hedylidae range in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> south from central <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_basin" title="Amazon basin">Amazon</a> from southern <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> (where there are a full 26 species,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> up to 12 at a single site:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grados98_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grados98-28">[28]</a></sup> to central <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a> and southwestern <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scoble90b_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scoble90b-18">[18]</a></sup>). In the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, they also occur in <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scoble90b_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Scoble90b-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grados98_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grados98-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hedylidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hedylids are <a href="/wiki/Moth#Attraction_to_light" title="Moth">attracted to articifial lights</a>, but occasionally some species can be found flying by day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kendall76_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kendall76-29">[29]</a></sup> Thus, they may be involved in some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mimic#Types_of_mimicry" title="Mimic">mimicry</a> complexes with <a href="/wiki/Nymphalidae" title="Nymphalidae">Ithomiinae</a> (e.g. the female only of <i>Macrosoma lucivittata</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> A few species are white<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and resemble pierid butterflies (e.g. <i>Macrosoma napiaria</i>). Based on a study of <i>Macrosoma heliconiaria</i>, it has been found that hedylids have <a href="/wiki/Tympanal_organ" title="Tympanal organ">tympanic organs</a> on their <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_entomology_terms" title="Glossary of entomology terms">forewings</a> for hearing<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> apparently <a href="/wiki/Homology_(biology)" title="Homology (biology)">homologous</a> to the "Vogel's organ" in some <a href="/wiki/Papilionoidea" title="Papilionoidea">Papilionoidea</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> that would help them evade bats at night. They have been shown to exhibit typical moth evasive behaviour towards bats such as erratic spiralling movements and dives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The resting posture is often at a curious angle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> with the thorax tilted and the posterior edge of the hindwings nearly touching the substrate (Scoble, 1986). The larvae which lack the prominent horns in the first <a href="/wiki/Instar" title="Instar">instar</a> tend to rest on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midrib" title="Midrib">midrib</a> of the leaf and often skeletonise leaves or at either side produce an untidy patchwork of holes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The elegant pupa is attached by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chrysalis#Chrysalis" title="Chrysalis">cremaster</a> and silken girdle<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and sometimes resembles a bird dropping.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology_and_host_plants">Biology and host plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hedylidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Biology and host plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The life history of <i>Macrosoma heliconiaria</i> was originally described from plants of <i>Byttneria aculeata</i> in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kendall76_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kendall76-29">[29]</a></sup> This was a historical breakthrough into the biology of hedylids. In this study, Kendall commented notably "I thought the larvae might represent a satyr species, but when the first larva pupated I was sure it was a pierid. The first adult emerged as a complete surprise. The pupa...is secured by girdle and cremaster, not unlike a pierid". <i>Macrosoma cascaria</i> was later also reared on this plant in <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScobleAiello90_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-ScobleAiello90-22">[22]</a></sup> More life histories are now known. From these data, known host plants span a wide range of (according to the <a href="/wiki/APG_II_system" title="APG II system">APG II system</a>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosid" title="Rosid">rosid</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dicotyledonous" title="Dicotyledonous">dicotyledonous</a> plants, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosid" title="Rosid">rosid</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">order</a> <a href="/wiki/Myrtales" title="Myrtales">Myrtales</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Melastomataceae" title="Melastomataceae">Melastomataceae</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">genera</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Miconia" title="Miconia">Miconia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Melastomataceae" title="Melastomataceae">Conostegia</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Melastomataceae" title="Melastomataceae">Ossaea</a></i>), the <a href="/wiki/Rosids" title="Rosids">eurosid I</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">order</a> <a href="/wiki/Malpighiales" title="Malpighiales">Malpighiales</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">families</a> <a href="/wiki/Euphorbiaceae" title="Euphorbiaceae">Euphorbiaceae</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Croton_(plant)" title="Croton (plant)">Croton</a></i>), and <a href="/wiki/Malpighiaceae" title="Malpighiaceae">Malpighiaceae</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Byrsonima" title="Byrsonima">Byrsonima</a></i>), the <a href="/wiki/Rosids" title="Rosids">eurosid II</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">orders</a> <a href="/wiki/Sapindales" title="Sapindales">Sapindales</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Rutaceae" title="Rutaceae">Rutaceae</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Zanthoxylum" title="Zanthoxylum">Zanthoxylum</a></i>) and more commonly<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Malvales" title="Malvales">Malvales</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Malvaceae" title="Malvaceae">Malvaceae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" title="Scientific classification">tribes</a>: <a href="/wiki/Malvaceae" title="Malvaceae">Bombacoideae</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Ochroma" title="Ochroma">Ochroma</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Malvaceae" title="Malvaceae">Malvoideae</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Malvaceae" title="Malvaceae">Hampea</a></i> and also <i><a href="/wiki/Hibiscus" title="Hibiscus">Hibiscus</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scoble90b_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-Scoble90b-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Malvaceae" title="Malvaceae">Byttnerioideae</a> (<i>Byttneria aculeata</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Theobroma" title="Theobroma">Theobroma</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Grewioideae" title="Grewioideae">Grewioideae</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Grewioideae" title="Grewioideae">Luehea</a></i>). The "green lizard caterpillar" <i>Macrosoma tipulata</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> attacks an economically important local fruit tree "Cupuaçu" (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theobroma_grandiflorum" title="Theobroma grandiflorum">Theobroma grandiflorum</a></i>) in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and can defoliate saplings; the biology of this species has been studied and illustrated in some detail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lourido07_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lourido07-16">[16]</a></sup> The larva of this species lives about 15 days in 5 instars, the pupal stage lasts about 7 days and the adult lives about 10 days. <i>M. tipulata</i> and many other species can be found as adults through most of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scoble90b_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-Scoble90b-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="DNA_sequences">DNA sequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hedylidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: DNA sequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A few species have been <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequencing" title="DNA sequencing">sequenced</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondrial" title="Mitochondrial">mitochondrial</a> genes "cytochrome oxidase I", and "ND1" and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_gene" title="Nuclear gene">nuclear genes</a> "Wingless" and "Ef-1?",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> including <i>Macrosoma semiermis</i>. Some species are currently being <a href="/wiki/DNA_barcoding" title="DNA barcoding">barcoded</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
