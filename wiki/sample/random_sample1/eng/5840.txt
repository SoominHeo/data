<p><b>Transportation in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a></b> is facilitated by road, air, rail, and waterways (via boats). The vast majority of passenger travel occurs by automobile for shorter distances, and airplane or railroad for some people, for longer distances. In descending order, most cargoes travel by railroad, truck, pipeline, or boat; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_shipping" title="Air shipping">air shipping</a> is typically used only for perishables and premium express shipments.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ownership_and_jurisdiction">Ownership and jurisdiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ownership and jurisdiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The overwhelming majority of roads in the United States are owned and maintained by state and local governments. <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_federally_maintained_roads" title="List of United States federally maintained roads">Federally maintained roads</a> are generally found only on federal lands (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_National_Parks_of_the_United_States" title="List of National Parks of the United States">national parks</a>) and at federal facilities (like military bases). The <a href="/wiki/Interstate_Highway_System" title="Interstate Highway System">Interstate Highway System</a> is partly funded by the federal government but owned and maintained by individual state governments. There are a few <a href="/wiki/Private_highways_in_the_United_States" title="Private highways in the United States">private highways in the United States</a>, which use tolls to pay for construction and maintenance. There are many local <a href="/wiki/Private_road" title="Private road">private roads</a>, generally serving remote or insular residences.</p>
<p>Passenger and freight rail systems, bus systems, water ferries, and dams may be under either public or private ownership and operation. Civilian airlines are all privately owned and financed. Most airports are owned and operated by local government authorities, but there are also some private airports. The <a href="/wiki/Transportation_Security_Administration" title="Transportation Security Administration">Transportation Security Administration</a> has provided security at most major airports since 2001.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Transportation" title="United States Department of Transportation">United States Department of Transportation</a> and its divisions provide regulation, supervision, and funding for all aspects of transportation, except for customs, immigration, and security, which are the responsibility of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Homeland_Security" title="United States Department of Homeland Security">United States Department of Homeland Security</a>. Each state has its own <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Transportation" title="Department of Transportation">Department of Transportation</a>, which builds and maintains state highways, and depending upon the state, may either directly operate or supervise other modes of transportation.</p>
<p>Aviation law is almost entirely a federal matter, while automobile traffic laws are enacted and enforced by state and local authorities. Economic jurisdiction over <a href="/wiki/Tidelands" title="Tidelands">tidelands</a> is shared between the state and federal governments, while the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">United States Coast Guard</a> is the primary enforcer of law and security on U.S. waterways.</p>

<p>Passenger <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transportation</a> is dominated by a network of over 3.9 million miles of highways<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> which is pervasive and highly developed by global standards. Passenger transportation is dominated by passenger vehicles (including cars, trucks, vans, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorcycles" title="Motorcycles">motorcycles</a>), which account for 86% of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passenger-mile" title="Passenger-mile">passenger-miles</a> traveled. The remaining 14% was handled by planes, trains, and buses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-btsPM_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-btsPM-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The world's second largest automobile market,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> the United States has the highest rate of per-capita vehicle ownership in the world, with 865 vehicles per 1,000 Americans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bicycle usage is minimal with the <a href="/wiki/American_Community_Survey" title="American Community Survey">American Community Survey</a> reporting that <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_commuting" title="Bicycle commuting">bicycle commuting</a> had a 0.61% mode share in 2012 (representing 856,000 American workers nationwide).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Freight transportation is carried by a variety of networks. The largest percentage of US freight is carried by trucks (60%), followed by pipelines (18%), rail (10%), ship (8%), and air (0.01%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Other modes of transportation, such as <a href="/wiki/Package_delivery" title="Package delivery">parcels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intermodal_freight_transport" title="Intermodal freight transport">intermodal</a> freight accounted for about 3% of the remainder. Air freight is commonly used only for perishables and premium express shipments. The difference in percentage of rail's share by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ton-mile" title="Ton-mile">ton-miles</a> and by weight (10% vs 38%) is accounted for by the extreme efficiency of trains. A single railroad locomotive may pull fifty boxcars full of freight while a truck only pulls one. Trucks surpass trains in the weight category due their greater numbers, while trains surpass trucks in the ton-miles category due to the vast distances they travel carrying large amounts of freight.</p>
<p>Usually cargo, apart from petroleum and other bulk commodities, is imported in <a href="/wiki/Containerization" title="Containerization">containers</a> through seaports, then distributed by road and rail. The quasi-governmental <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a> has a monopoly on letter delivery (except for express services) but several large private companies such as <a href="/wiki/FedEx" title="FedEx">FedEx</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Parcel_Service" title="United Parcel Service">UPS</a> compete in the package and cargo delivery market.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Speed to destination is an important factor in choosing a mode of transportation. In the late 18th century overland transportation was by horse, while water and river transportation was primarily by sailing vessel. The United States population was centered on its Atlantic coast, with all major population centers located on a natural harbor or navigable waterway. Low population density between these centers resulted in a heavy reliance on coastwise and <a href="/wiki/Riverboat" title="Riverboat">riverboat</a> shipping. The first government expenditures on highway transportation were funded to speed the delivery of overland mail, such as the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Post_Road" title="Boston Post Road">Boston Post Road</a> between New York City and Boston. Due to the distances between these population centers and the cost to maintain the roads, many highways in the late 18th century and early 19th century were private <a href="/wiki/Toll_road" title="Toll road">turnpikes</a>. Other highways were mainly unimproved and impassable by wagon at least some of the year. Economic expansion in the late 18th century to early 19th century spurred the building of canals to speed goods to market, of which the most prominently successful example was the <a href="/wiki/Erie_Canal" title="Erie Canal">Erie Canal</a>.</p>
<p>Access to water transportation shaped the geography of early settlements and boundaries. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Erie_Canal" title="Erie Canal">Erie Canal</a> escalated the <a href="/wiki/Toledo_War" title="Toledo War">Toledo War</a> between Ohio and Michigan in the 1830s. The disputed <a href="/wiki/Erie_Triangle" title="Erie Triangle">Erie Triangle</a> was awarded to <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> to give it access to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Erie" title="Lake Erie">Lake Erie</a>. Most of <a href="/wiki/West_Florida" title="West Florida">West Florida</a> was given to <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> to guarantee their access to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>.</p>
<p>Development of the mid-western and southern states drained by the Mississippi River system (<a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Missouri_River" title="Missouri River">Missouri Rivers</a>) was accelerated by the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Steamboats_of_the_Mississippi" title="Steamboats of the Mississippi">steamboats</a> on these rivers in the early 19th Century. These three rivers (<a href="/wiki/List_of_river_borders_of_U.S._states" title="List of river borders of U.S. states">among others</a>) also form the borders of several states. Prior to the introduction of steamboats, transit upstream was impractical because of strong currents on parts of these waterways. Steamboats provided both passenger and freight transportation until the development of railroads later in the 19th Century gradually reduced their presence.</p>
<p>The rapid expansion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rail_transport_in_the_United_States#History" title="Rail transport in the United States">Railroads</a> brought the canal boom to a sudden end, providing a quick, scheduled and year round mode of transportation that quickly spread to interconnect the states by the mid-19th century. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialization" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a> of the United States after the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>, railroads, led by the <a href="/wiki/First_Transcontinental_Railroad" title="First Transcontinental Railroad">transcontinental rail system</a> in the 1860s, expanded quickly across the United States to serve industries and the growing cities. During the late 19th century, railroads often had built redundant routes to a competitor's road or built through sparsely populated regions that generated little traffic. These marginal rail routes survived the pricing pressures of competition, or the lack of revenue generated by low traffic, as long as railroads provided the only efficient economical way to move goods and people across the United States. In addition to the intercity passenger network running on Class I and II railroads, a large network of <a href="/wiki/Interurban" title="Interurban">interurban</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tram" title="Tram">trolley</a> or "street running") rail lines extended out from the cities and interchanged passenger and freight traffic with the railroads and also provided competition.</p>
<p>The advent of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobile</a> signaled the end of railroads as the predominant transportation for people and began an era of mobility in the United States that added greatly to its economic output. The early 20th century <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Highway" title="Lincoln Highway">Lincoln Highway</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Auto_trail" title="Auto trail">auto trails</a> gave way in the 1920s to an early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Numbered_Highways" title="United States Numbered Highways">national highway</a> system making the automobile the preferred mode of travel for most Americans. <a href="/wiki/Interurban" title="Interurban">Interurban</a> rail service declined, followed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trolley_car" title="Trolley car">trolley cars</a> due in part to the advent of motorized buses and the lack of dedicated rights-of-way but also by deliberate efforts to dismantle urban rail infrastructure (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_American_streetcar_scandal" title="Great American streetcar scandal">Great American streetcar scandal</a>). The scarcity of industrial materials during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> slowed the growth of the automobile, briefly reemphasizing much of the nation's declining rail network. In the 1950s, the United States renewed building a network of high-capacity, high-speed <a href="/wiki/Highway" title="Highway">highways</a> to link its vast territory. The most important element is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_Highway" title="Interstate Highway">Interstate Highway</a> system, first commissioned in the 1950s by President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> and modeled after the Italian <a href="/wiki/Autostrade_of_Italy" title="Autostrade of Italy">autostrada</a> and the German <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">Autobahn</a> system.</p>
<p>By 1945, nearly every city in America had at least one electric tram company providing intra-city transportation. There were an estimated 36,377 light rail vehicles in operation. Improved automobile ownership cut this number by 1/3 by 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The airline industry began to successfully compete with intercity rail, which suffered a loss of ridership. As the civil air transportation network of <a href="/wiki/Airport" title="Airport">airports</a> and other infrastructure expanded, air travel became more accessible to the general population. Technological advances ushered in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jet_age" title="Jet age">jet age</a>, which increased airline capacity, while decreasing travel times and the cost of flights. The costs of flying rapidly decreased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intercity_rail" title="Intercity rail">intercity rail</a> ridership by the late 1960s to a point where railroads could no longer profitably operate networks of passenger trains.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> By the early 1970s almost all passenger rail operation and ownership had been transferred to various federal, municipal and state agencies. Passenger rail came to be heavily subsidized, as it is today.</p>
<p>Freight railroads continued to decline as motor freight captured a significant portion of the less-than-carload business. This loss of business, when combined the highly regulated operating environment and constrained <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pricing_power" title="Pricing power">pricing power</a>, forced many railroads into receivership and the nationalization of several critical eastern carriers into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consolidated_Rail_Corporation" title="Consolidated Rail Corporation">Consolidated Rail Corporation</a> (Conrail). Deregulation of the railroads by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Staggers_Act" title="Staggers Act">Staggers Act</a> in 1980 created a regulatory environment more favorable to the economics of the railroad industry. In the 1990s, the increase in foreign trade and intermodal container shipping led to a revival of the freight railroads, which have effectively consolidated into two eastern and two western private transportation networks: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Pacific" title="Union Pacific">Union Pacific</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BNSF" title="BNSF">BNSF</a> in the west, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CSX" title="CSX">CSX</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norfolk_Southern" title="Norfolk Southern">Norfolk Southern</a> in the east. <a href="/wiki/Canadian_National_Railway" title="Canadian National Railway">Canadian National Railway</a> took over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illinois_Central" title="Illinois Central">Illinois Central</a> route down the Mississippi River valley.</p>
<p>Wartime expediency encouraged long distance <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipeline transport</a> of petroleum and natural gas, which was greatly expanded in the middle 20th century to take over most of the domestic long-haul market.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Road_transportation">Road transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Road transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In comparison to some parts of the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a>, both the United States and Canada rely more heavily on its roads both for commercial and personal transit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Car ownership is nearly universal except in the largest cities where extensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_transit" title="Mass transit">mass transit</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railroad" title="Railroad">railroad</a> systems have been built.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>With the development of the extensive <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Eisenhower</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Interstate_Highway_System" title="Interstate Highway System">Interstate Highway System</a></i> in the 1950s, both long-distance trips and daily the commute were mostly by private <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobile</a>. This network was designed to exacting federal standards in order to receive federal funding. The system, as of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, has a total length of 47,182 miles (75,932 km),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> making it the world's second longest after <a href="/wiki/Expressways_of_China" title="Expressways of China">China</a>'s, and the largest <a href="/wiki/Public_works" title="Public works">public works</a> project in US history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Interstate system joined an existing <a href="/wiki/National_Highway_System_(United_States)" title="National Highway System (United States)">National Highway System</a> (a designation created for the legacy highway network in 1995), comprising 160,000 miles (256,000 kilometers) of roadway, a fraction of the total mileage of roads. The Interstate system serves nearly all major U.S. cities, often through the <a href="/wiki/Downtown" title="Downtown">downtown</a> areas (a point which triggered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freeway_and_expressway_revolts" title="Freeway and expressway revolts">freeway and expressway revolts</a> in the 1960s and 1970s). The distribution of virtually all goods and services involves Interstate highways at some point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caltrans_50_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-caltrans_50-15">[15]</a></sup> Residents of American cities commonly use urban Interstates to travel to their places of work. The vast majority of long-distance travel, whether for vacation or business, is by the national road network;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> of these trips, about one-third (by the total number of miles driven in the country in 2003) utilize the Interstate system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the routes of the Interstate system, there are those of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Numbered_Highways" title="United States Numbered Highways">U.S. highway system</a>, not to be confused with the above-mentioned National Highway System. These networks are further supplemented by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Highway" title="State Highway">State Highways</a>, and the local roads of <a href="/wiki/County_highway" title="County highway">counties</a>, municipal <a href="/wiki/Street" title="Street">streets</a>, and federal agencies, such as the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Indian_Affairs" title="Bureau of Indian Affairs">Bureau of Indian Affairs</a>. There are approximately 4,071,000 miles (6,552,000 km) of roads in the United States, 2,678,000 miles (4,310,000 km) paved and 1,394,000 miles (2,243,000 km) unpaved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> State highways are constructed by each state, but frequently maintained by county governments aided by funding from the state, where such counties exist as governing entities (mostly every state except the Northeastern). Counties construct and maintain all remaining roads outside cities, except in private communities. Local, unnumbered roads are often constructed by private contractors to local standards, then maintenance is assumed by the local government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>All federal <a href="/wiki/Highway" title="Highway">highways</a> are maintained by state governments, although they receive federal aid to build and maintain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freeway" title="Freeway">freeways</a> signed as part of the 46,000 mile (75,000 km) nationwide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_highway" title="Interstate highway">Interstate highway</a> network. Changes by state initiative may be made with federal approval. A large number of expressways are actually government or privately operated <a href="/wiki/Toll_road" title="Toll road">toll roads</a> in many East Coast and Midwestern states. West Coast freeways are generally free to users ("freeways", no toll charged per use), although since the 1990s there have been some small experiments with toll roads operated by private companies.</p>
<p>After the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/I-35W_Mississippi_River_bridge" title="I-35W Mississippi River bridge">I-35W Mississippi River bridge</a> in Minnesota in August 2007, the backlog of road and bridge maintenance across the country became an issue in transportation funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mpr_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-mpr-20">[20]</a></sup> The collapse prompted a tax increase in Minnesota to speed up bridge repairs, and action in other states, such as the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Department_of_Transportation#Accelerated_Bridge_Program" title="Massachusetts Department of Transportation">Accelerated Bridge Program</a> in Massachusetts, but after some debate no increase in federal funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mpr_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-mpr-20">[20]</a></sup> The 2013 <a href="/wiki/I-5_Skagit_River_Bridge_collapse" title="I-5 Skagit River Bridge collapse">I-5 Skagit River Bridge collapse</a>, caused by a collision with an <a href="/wiki/Oversize_load" title="Oversize load">over-height truck</a>, highlighted "fracture critical" bridges in which the failure of only one structural member will lead to complete collapse. According to the <a href="/wiki/National_Bridge_Inventory" title="National Bridge Inventory">National Bridge Inventory</a>, there are at least 600,000 bridges of 20 feet or more in length in the United States, all subject to deterioration in the absence of preventative maintenance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rdmbridges_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-rdmbridges-21">[21]</a></sup> In December 2008, 72,868 bridges in the United States (12.1%) were categorized as "structurally deficient", representing an estimated $48 billion in repairs. President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> proposed $50 billion of spending on road and bridge repair, plus a national infrastructure bank, but as of 2013 Congress has not acted on these proposals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2010, <a href="/wiki/Seat_belt_legislation_in_the_United_States" title="Seat belt legislation in the United States">seat belt use is mandatory</a> in all states except New Hampshire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intercity_bus">Intercity bus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Intercity bus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Greyhound_Lines" title="Greyhound Lines">Greyhound Lines</a> is the largest intercity <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">bus</a> company<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> in the United States, with routes in all parts of the <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">contiguous U.S.</a> There are also many smaller regional bus companies, many of which use the terminal and booking facilities provided by Greyhound. Intercity bus is, in most cases, the least expensive<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> way to travel long distances in the United States.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Congestion">Congestion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Congestion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Traffic congestion, especially at rush hour, is a problem in many of the country's larger cities. A 2009 study found that traffic congestion costs the United States almost $87.2 billion. The economic costs of traffic congestion have increased 63% over the past decade, and despite the declining traffic volumes caused by the economic downturn, Americans still waste more than 2.8 billion US gallons (11,000,000 m<sup>3</sup>) of fuel each year as a result of traffic congestion. Motorists also waste 4.2 billion hours annually, or one full workweek per traveler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cargo_2">Cargo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cargo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Trucking_industry_in_the_United_States" title="Trucking industry in the United States">Trucking industry in the United States</a></div>
<p>The trucking industry (also referred to as the transportation or logistics industry) involves the transport and distribution of commercial and industrial goods using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_motor_vehicles" title="Commercial motor vehicles">commercial motor vehicles</a> (CMV). In this case, CMVs are most often <a href="/wiki/Truck" title="Truck">trucks</a>; usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi_truck" title="Semi truck">semi trucks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Box_truck" title="Box truck">box trucks</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Dump_truck" title="Dump truck">dump trucks</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Truck_driver" title="Truck driver">truck driver</a> (commonly referred to as a "trucker") is a person who earns a living as the driver of a CMV.</p>
<p>The trucking industry provides an essential service to the <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_the_United_States" title="Economy of the United States">American economy</a> by transporting large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">raw materials</a>, <a href="/wiki/Work_in_process" title="Work in process">works in process</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Finished_good" title="Finished good">finished goods</a> over land—typically from manufacturing plants to retail distribution centers. Trucks are also important to the construction industry, as dump trucks and portable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concrete_mixers" title="Concrete mixers">concrete mixers</a> are necessary to move the large amounts of rocks, dirt, concrete, and other construction material. Trucks in America are responsible for the majority of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freight" title="Freight">freight</a> movement over land, and are vital tools in the manufacturing, transportation, and warehousing industries.</p>
<p>Large trucks and buses require a <a href="/wiki/Commercial_driver%27s_license" title="Commercial driver's license">commercial driver's license</a> (CDL) to operate. Obtaining a CDL requires extra education and training dealing with the special knowledge requirements and handling characteristics of such a large vehicle. Drivers of CMVs must adhere to the <a href="/wiki/Hours_of_service" title="Hours of service">hours of service</a>, which are regulations governing the driving hours of commercial drivers. These, and all other rules regarding the safety of interstate commercial driving, are issued by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Motor_Carrier_Safety_Administration" title="Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration">Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration</a> (FMCSA). The FMCSA is also a division of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Transportation" title="United States Department of Transportation">United States Department of Transportation</a> (USDOT), which governs all transportation-related industries such as trucking, <a href="/wiki/Ship_transport" title="Ship transport">shipping</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rail_transport_in_the_United_States" title="Rail transport in the United States">railroads</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_airlines#North_America" title="List of airlines">airlines</a>. Some other issues are handled by another branch of the USDOT, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Highway_Administration" title="Federal Highway Administration">Federal Highway Administration</a> (FHWA).</p>
<p>Developments in technology, such as computers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_communication" title="Satellite communication">satellite communication</a>, and the internet, have contributed to many improvements within the industry. These developments have increased the <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">productivity</a> of company operations, saved the time and effort of drivers, and provided new, more accessible forms of entertainment to men and women who often spend long periods of time away from home. In 2006, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="U.S. Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> implemented revised <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_standards" title="Emission standards">emission standards</a> for diesel trucks (reducing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airborne_pollutants" title="Airborne pollutants">airborne pollutants</a> emitted by diesel engines) which promises to improve air quality and public health.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_transportation">Air transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Air transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_transport_in_the_United_States" title="Air transport in the United States">Air transport in the United States</a></div>

<p>The United States has advanced air transportation infrastructure which utilizes approximately 5,000 paved runways. In terms of passengers, seventeen of the world's thirty busiest airports in 2004 were in the United States, including the world's busiest, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hartsfield-Jackson_Atlanta_International_Airport" title="Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport">Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport</a>. In terms of cargo, in the same year, twelve of the world's thirty busiest airports were in the United States, including the world's busiest, <a href="/wiki/Memphis_International_Airport" title="Memphis International Airport">Memphis International Airport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Private aircraft are also used for medical emergencies, government agencies, large businesses, and individuals, see <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a>.</p>
<p>There is no single national <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flag_airline" title="Flag airline">flag airline</a>; <a href="/wiki/Passenger_airline" title="Passenger airline">passenger airlines</a> in the United States have always been privately owned. There are over 200 domestic passenger and cargo <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">airlines</a> and a number of international carriers. The major international carriers of the United States are <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines" title="United Airlines">United Airlines</a>. <a href="/wiki/Low-cost_carrier" title="Low-cost carrier">Low-cost carrier</a> <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a> operates few international routes, but has grown its domestic operations to a size comparable to the major international carriers. There is currently no government regulation of ticket pricing, although the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">federal government</a> retains jurisdiction over aircraft safety, pilot training, and accident investigations (through the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Transportation_Safety_Board" title="National Transportation Safety Board">National Transportation Safety Board</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Transportation_Security_Administration" title="Transportation Security Administration">Transportation Security Administration</a> provides security at airports.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Passenger_2">Passenger</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Passenger">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Passenger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trains" title="Trains">trains</a> were the dominant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mode_of_transportation" title="Mode of transportation">mode of transportation</a> until the mid-twentieth century. The introduction of jet airplanes on major U.S. routes and the completion of the Interstate Highway system accelerated a decline in intercity rail passenger demand during the 1960s, resulting in the sharp curtailment of passenger service by private railroads. This led to the creation of National Railroad Passenger Corporation (branded as <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a>) by the federal government in 1971 to maintain limited intercity rail passenger service in most parts of the country. Amtrak serves most major cities but, outside of the Northeast, California, and Illinois, often by only few trains per day. Amtrak does not serve several major destinations, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Las_Vegas,_Nevada" title="Las Vegas, Nevada">Las Vegas, Nevada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix, Arizona</a>. Frequent service is available in regional corridors between certain major cities, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Corridor" title="Northeast Corridor">Northeast Corridor</a> between <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, between <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albany,_New_York" title="Albany, New York">Albany</a>, around <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, and in parts of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Railroad" title="Alaska Railroad">Alaska Railroad</a> is the only other intercity passenger railroad still operating, and it has no connections with Amtrak.</p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/High-speed_rail_in_the_United_States" title="High-speed rail in the United States">High-speed rail in the United States</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cargo_3">Cargo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cargo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The United States makes extensive use of its rail system for freight. According to the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_American_Railroads" title="Association of American Railroads">Association of American Railroads</a>: "U.S. freight railroads are the world's busiest, moving more freight than any rail system in any other country. In fact, U.S. railroads move more than four times as much freight as do all of Western Europe's freight railroads combined."</p>
<p>Nearly all railroad corridors (not including local transit rail systems) are owned by private companies that provide freight service. Amtrak pays these companies for the right to use the tracks for passenger service. There are approximately 150,000 mi (240,000 km) of mainline track in the United States—the world's longest national railroad network. <i>See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_railroads" title="List of United States railroads">List of United States railroads</a></i></p>
<p>Rail freight has a major national bottleneck in Chicago and the Midwest (about one-third of the nation's freight trains pass through the region),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heartland_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-heartland-28">[28]</a></sup> which is the subject of a $1.5 billion infrastructure improvement project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-create_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-create-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railway_links_with_adjacent_countries">Railway links with adjacent countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Railway links with adjacent countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Transportation_in_Canada" title="Transportation in Canada">Canada</a> - Yes. <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a> connections run daily between <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>. <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> is currently rail-accessible by <a href="/wiki/Train_ferry" title="Train ferry">train ferry</a> from Bellingham, Washington and narrow gauge railroad from Whitehorse, Yukon Territory to Skagway. A proposed link to the <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">contiguous United States</a> would link Alaska and Canada via the <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">Yukon</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Canadian_National_Railway" title="Canadian National Railway">Canadian National Railway</a> system includes the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illinois_Central" title="Illinois Central">Illinois Central</a> route from <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> via <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis</a> to <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Transportation_in_Mexico" title="Transportation in Mexico">Mexico</a> - Yes. Several private firms run touristic trains from near <a href="/wiki/El_Paso,_Texas" title="El Paso, Texas">El Paso, Texas</a>, through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chihuahua,_Chihuahua" title="Chihuahua, Chihuahua">Chihuahua, Chihuahua</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Copper_Canyon" title="Copper Canyon">Copper Canyon</a> to <a href="/wiki/El_Fuerte,_Sinaloa" title="El Fuerte, Sinaloa">El Fuerte, Sinaloa</a>. Such trains also run from <a href="/wiki/Nogales,_Sonora" title="Nogales, Sonora">Nogales, Sonora</a>, but no U.S. passenger trains run near <a href="/wiki/Nogales,_Arizona" title="Nogales, Arizona">Nogales, Arizona</a>, on the other side of the border. Another touristic train runs occasional trains between <a href="/wiki/Campo,_California" title="Campo, California">Campo, California</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tecate,_Baja_California" title="Tecate, Baja California">Tecate, Baja California</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transportation_in_Cuba" title="Transportation in Cuba">Cuba</a> - No. Since the <a href="/wiki/United_States_embargo_against_Cuba" title="United States embargo against Cuba">United States embargo against Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Train_ferry" title="Train ferry">train ferry</a> service from <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> to <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">Havana</a> ceased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CubaFerry_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-CubaFerry-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transportation_in_Russia" title="Transportation in Russia">Russia</a> - No. But <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bering_Strait_Bridge" title="Bering Strait Bridge">proposed tunnel or bridge</a> under or over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bering_Straits" title="Bering Straits">Bering Straits</a> to connect with <a href="/wiki/Trans-Siberian_Railway" title="Trans-Siberian Railway">Trans-Siberian Railway</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>With few exceptions, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rail_gauge" title="Rail gauge">rail gauge</a> is <a href="/wiki/Standard_gauge" title="Standard gauge">standard gauge</a> <span class="nowrap">4 ft <span class="frac">8<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> in</span> (<span class="nowrap">1,435 mm</span>). The <a href="/wiki/White_Pass_and_Yukon_Route" title="White Pass and Yukon Route">White Pass and Yukon Route</a> from <a href="/wiki/Skagway,_Alaska" title="Skagway, Alaska">Skagway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> to <a href="/wiki/Whitehorse,_Yukon" title="Whitehorse, Yukon">Whitehorse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">Yukon</a> by way of <a href="/wiki/Bennett,_British_Columbia" title="Bennett, British Columbia">Bennett</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> is <span class="nowrap">3 ft</span> (<span class="nowrap">914 mm</span>) gauge.</p>

</div>
<p>The miles traveled by passenger vehicles in the United States fell by 3.6% in 2008, while the number of trips taken on mass transit increased by 4.0%. At least part of the drop in urban driving can be explained by the 4% increase in the use of public transportation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most medium-sized cities have some sort of local public transportation, usually a network of fixed bus routes. Among larger cities many of the older cities also have <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit" title="Rapid transit">metro</a> rail systems (also known as <a href="/wiki/Passenger_rail_terminology#Heavy_rail" title="Passenger rail terminology">heavy rail</a> in the United States) and/or extensive light rail systems, while the newer cities found in the <a href="/wiki/Sun_Belt" title="Sun Belt">Sun Belt</a> either have modest <a href="/wiki/Light_rail" title="Light rail">light rail</a> systems or have no intracity rail at all.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation">Legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On June 26, 2008, the House passed the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saving_Energy_Through_Public_Transportation_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saving Energy Through Public Transportation Act (page does not exist)">Saving Energy Through Public Transportation Act</a> (H.R. 6052),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-issuesnpspeaker_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-issuesnpspeaker-32">[32]</a></sup> which gives grants to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_transit_authority&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mass transit authority (page does not exist)">mass transit authorities</a> to lower <a href="/wiki/Fare" title="Fare">fares</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commuter" title="Commuter">commuters</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pinched" title="wikt:pinched">pinched</a> at the pump and expand transit services. The bill also:</p>
<ul>
<li>Requires that all Federal agencies offer their employees transit pass transportation fringe benefits. Federal agencies within the National Capital Region have successful transit pass benefits programs.</li>
<li>Increases the federal cost-share of grants for construction of additional parking facilities at the end of subway lines from 80 to 100% to cover an increase in the number of people taking mass transit.</li>
<li>Creates a pilot program for <a href="/wiki/Vanpool" title="Vanpool">vanpool</a> demonstration projects in urban and rural areas.</li>
<li>Increases federal help for local governments to purchase alternative fuel buses, locomotives, and ferries from 90 to 100%.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_transportation">Water transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Water transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_transport" title="Water transport">Water transport</a> is largely used for freight. Fishing and pleasure boats are numerous, and passenger service connects many of the nation's islands and remote coastal areas, crosses lakes, rivers, and harbors, and provides alternative access to <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> which bypasses <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Several major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seaport" title="Seaport">seaports</a> in the United States include <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> on the east coast, <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a> on the gulf coast, and <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> on the west coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The interior of the U.S. also has major <a href="/wiki/Channel_(geography)" title="Channel (geography)">shipping channels</a>, via the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes_Waterway" title="Great Lakes Waterway">Great Lakes Waterway</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Lawrence_Seaway" title="St. Lawrence Seaway">St. Lawrence Seaway</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River_System" title="Mississippi River System">Mississippi River System</a>. Freight on the Mississippi River system is carried on <a href="/wiki/Barge" title="Barge">barges</a> pushed by approximately 8000 "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Towboat" title="Towboat">towboats</a>" and largely consists of bulk goods, such as petrochemicals, grain and cement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MissR_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-MissR-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many U.S. ports are served by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cruise_ships" title="Cruise ships">cruise ships</a>. Popular destinations include the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Riviera" title="Mexican Riviera">Mexican Riviera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Inside_Passage" title="Inside Passage">Inside Passage</a> to Alaska.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">Automobile ferries</a> operate in many locations where bridges are impractical and in congested metropolitan areas, including <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay" title="San Francisco Bay">San Francisco Bay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waterways">Waterways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Waterways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Inland_waterways_of_the_United_States" title="Inland waterways of the United States">Inland waterways of the United States</a></div>
<p>The United States has 25,482 miles (41,009 km) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navigable" title="Navigable">navigable</a> inland channels (<a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canals</a>), exclusive of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>. Out of this 12,006 miles (19,322 km) is used in commerce. About 15,000 miles (24,000 km) of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River_System" title="Mississippi River System">Mississippi River System</a> are presently navigable, although not all is used for commerce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MissR_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-MissR-34">[34]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_Seaway" title="Saint Lawrence Seaway">Saint Lawrence Seaway</a> of 2,342 miles (3,769 km), including the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_River" title="Saint Lawrence River">Saint Lawrence River</a> of 1,900 miles (3,100 km), is shared with Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ciafactbook_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-ciafactbook-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Merchant_marine">Merchant marine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Merchant marine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/United_States_Merchant_Marine" title="United States Merchant Marine">United States Merchant Marine</a>.</div>
<p>Most U.S. exports and imports are on foreign ships. The 1920 <a href="/wiki/Merchant_Marine_Act_of_1920" title="Merchant Marine Act of 1920">Jones Act</a> bars foreign ships from trade within the United States, thus creating a domestic "Jones Act fleet". Deck officers and ship's engineers of U.S.-flagged ships are usually trained at one of the established maritime academies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding">Funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Update" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ambox_current_red.svg"><img alt="Ambox current red.svg" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="360" height="34" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/42px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/63px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/84px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 2x" width="42"/></a></div>
</td>
<td class="mbox-text"><span class="mbox-text-span">Parts of this article (those related to reauthorization) need to be <b>updated</b>. <span class="hide-when-compact">Please update this article to reflect recent events or newly available information.</span> <small><i>(May 2010)</i></small></span></td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Federal, state, and local tax revenues support upkeep of most roads, which are generally free to drivers. There are also some <a href="/wiki/Toll_road" title="Toll road">toll roads</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toll_bridge" title="Toll bridge">toll bridges</a>. Most other forms of transportation charge a fee for use as they are not given much, if any, tax support by Congress.</p>
<p>Government funding of transportation exists at many levels. Federal funding for highway, rail, bus, water, air, and other forms of transportation is allocated by Congress for several years at a time. The current authorization bill is the <a href="/wiki/Safe,_Accountable,_Flexible,_Efficient_Transportation_Equity_Act:_A_Legacy_for_Users" title="Safe, Accountable, Flexible, Efficient Transportation Equity Act: A Legacy for Users">Safe, Accountable, Flexible, Efficient Transportation Equity Act: A Legacy for Users</a> (SAFETEA-LU), which runs from 2005 to 2009. A Congressionally chartered committee is considering future funding issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nstifc_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-nstifc-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earmark_(finance)" title="Earmark (finance)">earmarks</a> are often made for specific projects, the allocation of most federal dollars is controlled by <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_planning_organization" title="Metropolitan planning organization">metropolitan planning organizations</a> (MPOs) and state governments. Usually "matching" funds are required from local sources. All projects have a sponsoring agency that will receive the funding from the various federal and local sources, and be responsible for implementing the project directly or through contracts. Large projects require a <b>Major Investment Study</b> and both a Draft and a Final <b><a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_assessment" title="Environmental impact assessment">Environmental Impact Review</a></b>. A patchwork of federal laws and accounts govern the allocation of federal transportation dollars, most of which is reserved for capital projects, not operating expenses. Some roads are federally designated as part of the <a href="/wiki/National_Highway_System_(United_States)" title="National Highway System (United States)">National Highway System</a> and get preferential funding as a result, but there are few <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_federally_maintained_roads" title="List of United States federally maintained roads">federally maintained roads</a> outside of Washington, D.C., and national parks.</p>
<p>State governments are sovereign entities which use their powers of taxation both to match federal grants, and provide for local transportation needs. Different states have different systems for dividing responsibility for funding and maintaining road and transit networks between the state department of transportation, counties, municipalities, and other entities. Typically cities or counties are responsible for local roads, financed with block grants and local property taxes, and the state is responsible for major roads that receive state and federal designations. Many mass transit agencies are quasi-independent and subsidized branches of a state, county, or city government.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_impact">Economic impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Economic impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Transportation" title="U.S. Department of Transportation">U.S. Department of Transportation</a> (DOT): "Transportation's vital importance to the U.S. economy is underscored by the fact that more than $1 out of every $10 produced in the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> is related to transportation activity. This includes all aspects of transportation, including the movement of goods and the purchase of all transportation-related products and services as well as the movement of people".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Employment in the transportation and material moving industry accounted for 7.4% of all employment, and was the 5th largest employment group in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>The United States invests 0.6% of its GDP on transportation annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impacts">Environmental impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two-thirds of U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_consumption" title="Oil consumption">oil consumption</a> is due to the transportation sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-btstable4-3_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-btstable4-3-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The "<i><a href="/wiki/Energy_Independence_and_Security_Act_of_2007" title="Energy Independence and Security Act of 2007">Energy Independence and Security Act of 2007</a></i>" has a significant impact on <a href="/wiki/Energy_policy_of_the_United_States" title="Energy policy of the United States">U.S. Energy Policy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EISA_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-EISA-45">[45]</a></sup> The United States—an important export country for food stocks—will convert 18% of its grain output to <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel_in_the_United_States" title="Ethanol fuel in the United States">ethanol</a> in 2008. Across the United States, 25% of the whole corn crop went to ethanol in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25perc_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-25perc-46">[46]</a></sup> The percentage of corn going to biofuel is expected to go up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> In 2006, U.S. Senators introduced the <a href="/wiki/BioFuels_Security_Act" title="BioFuels Security Act">BioFuels Security Act</a>, which would mandate the production of dual-fuel vehicles and the sale of <a href="/wiki/E85" title="E85">E85</a> ethanol fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
