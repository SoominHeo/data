<p>A <b>legal remedy</b>, also <b>judicial relief</b> or a <b>judicial remedy</b>, is the means with which a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Court_of_law" title="Court of law">court of law</a>, usually in the exercise of <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(common_law)" title="Civil law (common law)">civil law</a> jurisdiction, enforces a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right" title="Right">right</a>, imposes a <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(law)" title="Sentence (law)">penalty</a>, or makes another <a href="/wiki/Court_order" title="Court order">court order</a> to impose its will.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> jurisdictions and mixed civil-common law jurisdictions, the law of remedies distinguishes between a legal remedy (e.g. a specific amount of monetary <a href="/wiki/Damages" title="Damages">damages</a>) and an <a href="/wiki/Equitable_remedy" title="Equitable remedy">equitable remedy</a> (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Injunctive_relief" title="Injunctive relief">injunctive relief</a> or <a href="/wiki/Specific_performance" title="Specific performance">specific performance</a>). Another type of remedy available in these systems is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Declaratory_relief" title="Declaratory relief">declaratory relief</a>, where a court determines the rights of the parties to an <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">action</a> without awarding damages or ordering equitable relief.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> and American <a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence" title="Jurisprudence">jurisprudence</a>, there is a <a href="/wiki/Legal_maxim" title="Legal maxim">legal maxim</a> (albeit one sometimes honored in the breach) that <i>for every right, there is a remedy; where there is no remedy, there is no right</i>. That is, lawmakers claim to provide appropriate remedies to protect rights. This legal maxim was first enunciated by <a href="/wiki/William_Blackstone" title="William Blackstone">William Blackstone</a>: "It is a settled and invariable principle in the laws of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, that every right when with-held must have a remedy, and every injury its proper redress." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_Remedies_in_Common_Law_Systems">Types of Remedies in Common Law Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Legal_remedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of Remedies in Common Law Systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three crucial categories of remedies in common law system. One is from the law courts of England, and is seen in the form of a payment of money to the victim. This payment is commonly referred to as damages. Compensatory damages compensate an injured victim or plaintiff, and punitive damages punish someone who because of fraud or intentional conduct, is deemed to deserve punishment. Punitive damages serve the function in civil law that fines do in criminal law.</p>
<p>The second category of remedy comes from the <a href="/wiki/Equity_(law)" title="Equity (law)">equitable</a> jurisdiction developed in the English <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Chancery" title="Court of Chancery">Court of Chancery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exchequer_of_Pleas" title="Exchequer of Pleas">Court of Exchequer</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Injunction" title="Injunction">injunction</a> is a type of equitable remedy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> as is specific performance, in which someone who enters into a contract is forced to perform whatever promise has been reneged upon. Two additional equitable remedies are the equitable lien and the constructive trust.</p>
<p>The third broad group is declaratory remedies. Common examples are the <a href="/wiki/Declaratory_judgment" title="Declaratory judgment">declaratory judgment</a> and the action to quiet title, and these remedies usually involve a court's determination of how the law applies to particular facts without any command to the parties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Courts give declaratory remedies about many different kinds of questions, including whether a person has a legal status, who the owner of a property is, whether a statute has a particular meaning, or what the rights are under a contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>While those are the three basic categories of remedies in common law, there are also a handful of others (such as reformation and rescission, both dealing with contracts whose terms need to be rewritten or undone).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Case-by-case_remedies_versus_announced_remedies">Case-by-case remedies versus announced remedies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Legal_remedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Case-by-case remedies versus announced remedies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Remedies can be, and in American law usually are, determined case by case, and take into account many different facts including the amount of harm caused to the victim. Remedies can also be determined in advance for an entire class of cases. For example, there can be a fixed fine for all violations of a legal rule, regardless of how much harm was caused in the particular case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
