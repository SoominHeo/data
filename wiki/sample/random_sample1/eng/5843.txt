<p>The <b>Individuals with Disabilities Education Act</b> (<b>IDEA</b>) is a four-part (A-D) piece of American legislation that ensures students with a disability are provided with Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) that is tailored to their individual needs. IDEA was previously known as the Education for All Handicapped Children Act (EHA) from 1975 to 1990. In 1990, the United States Congress reauthorized EHA and changed the title to IDEA (Public Law No. 94-142). Overall, the goal of IDEA is to provide children with disabilities the same opportunity for education as those students who do not have a disability.</p>
<p>IDEA is composed of four parts, the main two being part A and part B.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Part A covers the general provisions of the law, Part B covers assistance for education of all children with disabilities, Part C covers infants and toddlers with disabilities which includes children from birth to age three, and Part D is the national support programs administered at the federal level. Each part of the law has remained largely the same since the original enactment in 1975.</p>
<p>In practice, IDEA is composed of six main elements that illuminate its main points. These six elements are: Individualized Education Program (IEP), Free and Appropriate Public Education (FAPE), Least Restrictive Environment (LRE), Appropriate Evaluation, Parent and Teacher Participation, and Procedural Safeguards. To go along with those six main elements there are also a few other important components that tie into IDEA: Confidentiality of Information, Transition Services, and Discipline. Throughout the years of IDEA being reauthorized these components have become key concepts when learning about IDEA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-See_note_1_above_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-See_note_1_above-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before the Education for All Handicapped Children Act was enacted in 1975, U.S. public schools accommodated only 1 out of 5 children with disabilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ed.gov_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ed.gov-3">[3]</a></sup> Until that time, many states had laws that explicitly excluded children with certain types of disabilities from attending public school, including children who were blind, deaf, and children labeled "emotionally disturbed" or "mentally retarded."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Civil_Rights_2000_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Civil_Rights_2000-4">[4]</a></sup> At the time the EHA was enacted, more than 1 million children in the U.S. had no access to the public school system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Civil_Rights_2000_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Civil_Rights_2000-4">[4]</a></sup> Many of these children lived at state institutions where they received limited or no educational or rehabilitation services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Another 3.5 million children attended school but were âwarehousedâ in segregated facilities and received little or no effective instruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Civil_Rights_2000_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Civil_Rights_2000-4">[4]</a></sup> As of 2006, more than 6 million children in the U.S. receive special education services through IDEA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_Context">Historical Context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical Context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1954, the established educational format in the United States of segregating black and white students into separate schools was declared unconstitutional by the United States Supreme Court during the ruling of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brown_v._Board_of_Education_of_Topeka" title="Brown v. Board of Education of Topeka">Brown v. Board of Education of Topeka</a>, 347 U.S. 483 (1954).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This caused a great deal of unrest in the political sphere and marks a gateway moment in the Civil Rights Movement. Education was an important aspect of the Civil Rights Movement. The years that led up to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Education_for_All_Handicapped_Children_Act" title="Education for All Handicapped Children Act">Education for All Handicapped Children Act</a> of 1975 were marked by strife in the United States, from the assassination of John F. Kennedy in 1963 to the Vietnam war ongoing from 1955 until 1975. On top of those events, the Civil Rights Movement was in full force in the United States. From schools being integrated to the Montgomery Bus Boycott, from Greensboro sit-ins to marches on Washington, equal rights for all was a prevalent ideal. President John F. Kennedy showed interest in mental retardation studies and President Lyndon Johnson used Federal funds to increase research on âat-riskâ youth. Early intervention programs for children living in low socioeconomic situations, such as the Head Start Program, began showing up around the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Education was soon at the forefront of many political agendas.</p>
<p>The first legislation which provided relief was the <a href="/wiki/Rehabilitation_Act_of_1973" title="Rehabilitation Act of 1973">Rehabilitation Act of 1973</a>. Congress then enacted the Education for All Handicapped Children Act to alleviate the financial burden created by litigation pursuant to the Rehabilitation Act. Public schools were required to evaluate handicapped children and create an educational plan with parent input that would emulate as closely as possible the educational experience of non-disabled students.Students should be placed in the least restrictive environment-one that allows the maximum possible opportunity to interact with non-impaired students. Separate schooling may only occur when the nature or severity of the disability is such that instructional goals cannot be achieved in the regular classroom. Finally, the law contains a due process clause that guarantees an impartial hearing to resolve conflicts between the parents of disabled children to the school system.</p>
<p>The act also required that school districts provide administrative procedures so that parents of disabled children could dispute decisions made about their childrenâs education. Once the administrative efforts were exhausted, parents were then authorized to seek judicial review of the administrationâs decision</p>
<p>17 years later, in 1990, the EHA was replaced by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act in order to place more focus on the individual, as opposed to a condition that individual may have.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The IDEA also had many improvements on the EHA, such as promoting research and technology development, details on transition programs for students post-high school and programs that educate children in their neighborhood schools, as opposed to separate schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Six_Pillars_of_IDEA">Six Pillars of IDEA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Six Pillars of IDEA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Individualized_Education_Program_.28IEP.29">Individualized Education Program (IEP)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Individualized Education Program (IEP)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Individualized_Education_Program" title="Individualized Education Program">Individualized Education Program</a>.</div>
<p>"The basis for the handicapped child's entitlement to an individualized and appropriate education is the individualized educational program ("IEP"), that a school system must design to meet the unique needs of each child with a disability." <i>Phillip C. v. Jefferson County Bd. of Educ</i>., 701 F. 3d 691, 694 (11th Cir. 2012), citing <i>Doe v. Ala. State Dep't of Educ</i>., 915 F.2d 651, 654 (11th Cir. 1990) and <i>Winkelman v. Parma City Sch. Dist</i>., 550 U.S. 516, 524 (2007) (internal quotation marks omitted). See also <i>Honig v. Doe,</i> 484 U.S. 305, 311 (1988) (underscoring the importance of the IEP by describing it as "the centerpiece of the statute's education delivery system for disabled children"). The act requires that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_school_(government_funded)" title="Public school (government funded)">public schools</a> create an <a href="/wiki/Individualized_Education_Program" title="Individualized Education Program">Individualized Education Program</a> (IEP) for each student who is found to be eligible under both the federal and state eligibility/disability standards. The IEP is the cornerstone of a student's educational program. It specifies the services to be provided and how often, describes the student's present levels of performance and how the student's disabilities affect academic performance, and specifies accommodations and modifications to be provided for the student.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>An IEP must be designed to meet the unique educational needs of that one child in the Least Restrictive Environment appropriate to the needs of that child. When a child qualifies for services, an IEP team is convened to design an education plan. In addition to the childâs parents, the IEP team must include at least one of the childâs regular education teachers (if applicable), a special education teacher, someone who can interpret the educational implications of the childâs evaluation, such as a school psychologist, any related service personnel deemed appropriate or necessary, and an administrator or CSE (Committee of Special Education) representative who has adequate knowledge of the availability of services in the district and the authority to commit those services on behalf of the child. Parents are considered to be equal members of the IEP team along with the school staff. Based on the full educational evaluation results, this team collaborates to write an IEP for the individual child, one that will provide a free, appropriate public education. The required content of an IEP is further described in the <a href="/wiki/Individualized_Education_Program" title="Individualized Education Program">Individualized Education Program</a> article.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_Appropriate_Public_Education">Free Appropriate Public Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Free Appropriate Public Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_Appropriate_Public_Education_(FAPE)" title="Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE)">Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE)</a>.</div>
<p>Guaranteed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), FAPE is defined as "special education and related services that A) are provided at the publicâs expense, under public supervision and direction, and without charge, B) meet the standards of the State educational agency, C) include an appropriate preschool, elementary, or secondary school education in the State involved; and D) are provided in conformity with the individualized education program under section 614(d). (Pub. L. No. 94-142, Â§ 602(9))â<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-See_note_1_above_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-See_note_1_above-2">[2]</a></sup> To provide FAPE, schools must provide students with an ââ¦ education that emphasizes special education and related services designed to meet their unique needs and prepare them for further education, employment, and independent living.â<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of the criteria specified in various sections of the IDEA statute includes requirements that schools provide each disabled student an education that:</p>
<ul>
<li>Is designed to meet the unique needs of that one student</li>
<li>Provides â â¦access to the general curriculum to meet the challenging expectations established for all childrenâ (that is, it meets the approximate grade-level standards of the state educational agency.)</li>
<li>Is provided in accordance with the Individualized Education Plan (IEP) as defined in 1414(d)(3).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20_U.S.C._.C2.A714019_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-20_U.S.C._.C2.A714019-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>Results in educational benefit to the child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20_U.S.C._.C2.A714019_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-20_U.S.C._.C2.A714019-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Least_Restrictive_Environment_.28LRE.29">Least Restrictive Environment (LRE)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Least Restrictive Environment (LRE)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_Restrictive_Environment" title="Least Restrictive Environment">Least Restrictive Environment</a>.</div>
<p>The U.S. Dept. Education, 2005a regulations implementing IDEA states: "...to the maximum extent appropriate, children with disabilities including children in public or private institutions or care facilities, are educated with children who are <i>non</i>disabled; and special classes, separate schooling or other removal of children with disabilities from regular educational environment occurs only if the nature or severity of the disability is such that education in regular classes with the use of supplementary aids and services cannot be achieved satisfactorily."</p>
<p>Simply put, the LRE is the environment most like that of typical children in which the child with a disability can succeed academically (as measured by the specific goals in the student's IEP). This refers to the two questions decided upon in <i>Daniel R. R. v. State Board of Education</i>, 874 F.2D 1036 (5th Cir. 1989).</p>
<p>This court, relying on Roncker, also developed a two-part test for determining if the LRE requirement is met. The test poses two questions:</p>
<ol>
<li>Can an appropriate education in the general education classroom with the use of supplementary aids and services be achieved satisfactorily?</li>
<li>If a student is placed in a more restrictive setting, is the student "integrated" to the "maximum extent appropriate"? (Standard in AL, DE, GA, FL, LA, MS, NJ, PA, TX).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Appropriate_Evaluation">Appropriate Evaluation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Appropriate Evaluation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Children are placed in special education services through an evaluation process. If the evaluation is not appropriately conducted, or does not monitor the information that is needed to determine placement it is not appropriate. The goal of IDEAâs regulations for evaluation is to help minimize the number of misidentifications, to provide a variety of assessment tools and strategies, to prohibit the use of any single evaluation as the sole criterion of which a student is placed in special education services, and to provide protections against evaluation measures that are racially or culturally discriminatory. Overall, the goal of appropriate evaluation is to get students who need help, extra help that is appropriate for the student and helps that specific student to reach his or her goals set by the IEP team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-See_note_1_above_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-See_note_1_above-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parent_and_Teacher_Participation">Parent and Teacher Participation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Parent and Teacher Participation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A good family-professional partnership is key for a student to receive the education necessary for success. Parents and teachers need to be willing to work together and communicate to determine the best ways of working with and providing information for a student. Both the family and the teacher work together on the IEP team to determine goals, the LRE, and to discuss other important considerations for each individual student. Throughout the whole IEP and special education process parents and families should be updated and kept informed of any decisions made about their specific student. Parents should also be able to provide valuable input about their student to determine placement and other educational goals. Parents as well as teachers are able to challenge any decisions that they feel are inappropriate for the student.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-See_note_1_above_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-See_note_1_above-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Procedural_Safeguards">Procedural Safeguards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Procedural Safeguards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>IDEA includes a set of procedural safeguards designed to protect the rights of children with disabilities and their families, and to ensure that children with disabilities receive a FAPE. The procedural safeguards include the opportunity for parents to review their child's full educational records; full parent participation in identification and IEP team meetings; parent involvement in placement decisions; Prior Written Notice; the right of parents to request independent educational evaluations at public expense; Notice of Procedural Safeguards; Resolution Process; and objective mediation funded by the state education agency and impartial Due Process Hearings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> IDEA guarantees the following rights to parents:</p>
<ul>
<li>Access to educational records</li>
<li>Parent Participation (In any and all meetings regarding placement and educational decisions)</li>
<li>Prior Written Notice (Any time anything will be changed in a studentâs IEP their parents must first be notified)</li>
<li>Procedural Safeguards Notice (A written copy should be provided to parents under federal and state law)</li>
<li>Understandable language (Translators must be provided when needed)</li>
<li>Informed Consent (Before any evaluations or services are provided the studentâs parents must be informed and agree in writing before the school can move forward)</li>
<li>âStay Putâ Rights (If parents disagree with the schoolâs decision the student can stay put while the parents and school go through dispute resolution)</li>
<li>Due Process (If a parent has a dispute with the school about their studentâs special education placement or teaching a process called due process is used to resolve issues; both parties are then able to tell their sides of the story in a court like setting)</li>
<li>Civil Action (If due process results are not to the liking of the parent or the school a civil lawsuit can be filed)</li>
<li>Mediation (An alternative to due process hearings) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Important_Relevant_Issues">Important Relevant Issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Important Relevant Issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Confidentiality_of_Information">Confidentiality of Information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Confidentiality of Information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Throughout the entire IEP process the school has to protect the confidentiality of the student. Some schools may think that providing a teacher with the IEP is a violation of the studentâs confidentiality, but the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act States that "if the disclosure is to other school officials, including teachers, within the educational institution or local education agency who have been determined by the agency or institution to have legitimate educational interests" the school does not need written consent from a parent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> For more information about confidentiality see the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act of 1974 (FERPA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_Services">Transition Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Transition Services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the age of 16 students are required to attend IEP meetings to discuss transition services with the IEP team. Transition services can be started earlier if the IEP teams deems it necessary, but the student must be at the meeting or appropriate measures must be taken to account for student preference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Transition services coordinate the transition between school and post school activities, such as secondary education, vocational training, employment, independent living, etc. These transitional decisions should be based on the students strengths/weaknesses, preferences, and the skills possessed by the individual. Once a decision has been made on the transition service a plan should be formed to allow the student to be able to fully reach this goal. In order for this to happen objectives, instruction needed, and other skills should be assessed and taken into account to prepare the individual for this transition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-See_note_1_above_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-See_note_1_above-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discipline_of_a_Child_with_a_Disability">Discipline of a Child with a Disability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Discipline of a Child with a Disability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pursuant to IDEA, when disciplining a child with a disability, one must take that disability into consideration to determine the appropriateness of the disciplinary actions. For example, if a child with Autism is sensitive to loud noises, and she runs out of a room filled with loud noises due to sensory overload, appropriate disciplinary measure for that behavior (running out of the room) must take into account the child's disability; such as avoiding punishments that involve loud noises. Moreover, an assessment should be made as to whether appropriate accommodations were in place to meet the needs of the child. According to the United States Department of Education, in cases of children with disabilities who have been suspended for 10 or more days for each school year (including partial days), the local education agency (LEA) must hold a manifestation determination hearing within 10 school days of any decision to change the placement of a child resulting from a violation of code of student conduct. The Stay Put law states that a child shall not be moved from his or her current placement or interim services into an alternative placement if the infraction was deemed to cause danger to other students. The LEA, the parent, and relevant members of the individualized education program (IEP) team (as determined by the parent and LEA) shall review all relevant information in the student's file, including the child's IEP, any teacher observations, and any relevant information provided by the parents to determine if the conduct in question was:</p>
<ul>
<li>Caused by, or had a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability; or</li>
<li>The direct result of the LEA's failure to implement the IEP.</li>
</ul>
<p>If the LEA, the parent, and relevant members of the IEP team make the determination that the conduct was a manifestation of the childâs disability, the IEP team shall:</p>
<ul>
<li>Conduct a functional behavioral assessment and implement a behavioral intervention plan for such child, provided that the LEA had not conducted such assessment prior to such determination before the behavior that resulted in a change in placement described in Section 615(k)(1)(C) or (G);</li>
<li>In the situation where a behavioral intervention plan has been developed, review the behavioral intervention plan if the child already has such a behavioral intervention plan, and modify it, as necessary, to address the behavior; and</li>
<li>Except as provided in Section 615(k)(1)(G), return the child to the placement from which the child was removed, unless the parent and the LEA agree to a change of placement as part of the modification of the behavior intervention plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>If it is determined that a student's behavior is a manifestation of his or her disability, then he or she may not be suspended or expelled. However, under IDEA 2004,if a student "brings a weapon to school or a school function; or knowingly possess, uses, or sells illegal drugs or controlled substances at school or a school function"; or causes "serious bodily injury upon another person," he or she may be placed in an interim alternate educational setting (IAES) for up to 45 school days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This allows the student to continue receiving educational services while the IEP team has time to determine the appropriate placement and the appropriate course of action including reviewing the FBA and the BIP.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prohibition_on_Mandatory_Medication">Prohibition on Mandatory Medication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Prohibition on Mandatory Medication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to allegations that school officials coerced parents into administering medication such as <a href="/wiki/Methylphenidate" title="Methylphenidate">Ritalin</a> to their child, an amendment to the IDEA was added called prohibition on mandatory medication. Schools may not require parents to obtain a controlled substance as a condition of:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ed.gov_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ed.gov-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>attending school</li>
<li>receiving an evaluation or reevaluation</li>
<li>receiving special education services</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alignment_with_No_Child_Left_Behind">Alignment with No Child Left Behind</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Alignment with No Child Left Behind">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The reauthorization of IDEA in 2004 revised the statute to align with the requirements of the No Child Left Behind Act (NCLB). NCLB allows financial incentives to states who improve their special education services and services for all students. States who do not improve must refund these incentives to the federal government, allow parents choice of schools for their children, and abide by other provisions. Some states are still reluctant to educate special education students and seek remedies through the courts. However, IDEA and NCLB are still the laws of the land to date.</p>
<p>In looking to align NCLB and the 2004 reauthorization of IDEA there are a few key areas of alignment: requirement of highly qualified teachers, establishment of goals for students with special needs and assessment levels for these students<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The alignment of NCLB and IDEA requires that all special education teachers be highly qualified. While the standards for being highly qualified may differ between state or school district the minimum requirements are that a teacher holds a bachelor's degree from a four-year college, is certified and licensed to teach by the state and has taken the necessary tests to indicate competency in one's subject area,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> although special education teachers are often exempt from such testing. These requirements for highly qualified teachers do not always exist for private schools, elementary or secondary. Next, goals and assessments must be provided that align with students' educational needs. A state is allowed to develop alternate or modified assessments for students in special education programs but benchmarks and progress must still be met on these tests that indicate adequate yearly progress (AYP). In addition, these goals and assessments must be aligned similarly to students enrolled in general education. Finally, in order to make AYP schools may additionally require that schools meet state standards of student retention, in terms of dropout rates and graduate rates for their special education students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_intervention">Early intervention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Early intervention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Education for All Handicapped Children Act of 1975 started the course of action for early intervention programs. In this act, public schools that received federal funding were required to provide equal access to education for children with disabilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Services for infants and toddlers were not included in the Act until the reauthorization in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 6, 2011, the US Department of Education updated the IDEA to include specific interventions for children of ages 2 and under that have disabilities. This section of the IDEA is entitled Part C and serves children with developmental delays or children that have conditions that may lead to developmental delays in the future. Part C is a $436 million initiative that will be administered at the state level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 28, 2011, the Department of Education published an article in the Federal Register detailing the updates that have been made to Part C of the IDEA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The regulations are effective on October 28, 2011. Major changes in the regulations are detailed below:</p>
<ul>
<li>The definition of multidisciplinary has been revised to respect aspects of an updated individualized family service plan (IFSP) team.</li>
<li>Native language is the language normally used by the parents of the child for any child that is deemed limited English proficient</li>
<li>Stateâs applications to must include how the State plans to follow the payor of last resort requirements in Section 303.511</li>
<li>Distinguishes between pre-referral, referral, and post-referral IFSP activities such as screening, evaluations, assessments, IFSP development, etc.</li>
<li>Specifies that early identification information is provided in the native languages of various population groups in the State</li>
<li>State must report to the public the performance of each Early Intervention System program in relation to the Stateâs Annual Performance Report</li>
</ul>
<p>More specific details on Early Intervention requirements are found below.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Part_C_of_IDEA">Part C of IDEA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Part C of IDEA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Individualized_Family_Service_Plan_.28IFSP.29">Individualized Family Service Plan (IFSP)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Individualized Family Service Plan (IFSP)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An Individualized Family Service Plan (IFSP) is a strengths-based plan of care for the infant/toddler with a developmental delay or disability. The plan is based on a child and family assessment of strengths and needs as well as the results of multidisciplinary evaluations administered by qualified professionals meeting their state's certification guidelines. The IFSP is similar to an IEP in that it addresses specific services, who will provide them and when/where, how often, etc. and the plan is monitored and updated frequently. Unlike an IEP, however, the IFSP addresses not only the needs of the child but also the needs of the family to meet their family goals and specified outcomes as related to assisting in their child's development. All infants and toddlers receiving early intervention services under Part C of IDEA are required to have an IFSP in order to receive services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ldanatl.org_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ldanatl.org-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Part C of IDEA is the program that awards grants to every state in the United States to provide early intervention services to children from birth to age 3 who have disabilities and to their families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ldanatl.org_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-ldanatl.org-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Part C of IDEA also allows states to define "developmental delay" (either as a standard deviation or a percent delay in chronological months) for eligibility. States provide early intervention services to the children who have medically diagnosed disabilities as well as children who exhibit developmental delays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ideapractices.org_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-ideapractices.org-33">[33]</a></sup> In order to receive funding, participating states must provide early intervention to every eligible child and the respective family, regardless of pay source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ideapractices.org_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-ideapractices.org-33">[33]</a></sup> Lastly, services from Part C are not necessarily free â early intervention programs, as the payor of last resort, make use of public and private insurance, community resources, and some states implement a "sliding scale" of fees for services not covered by public or private insurance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Goals_for_an_IFSP">Goals for an IFSP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Goals for an IFSP">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The goal of an IFSP is to assist the family in meeting their child's developmental needs in order to the infant or toddler (birth to age three) to increase functional abilities, gain independence and mobility, and be an active participant in his/her family and community. Another goal of early intervention in general is to improve a child's functional abilities, particularly in the domains of communication, cognitive ability, and social/emotional well-being in preparation for preschool and later kindergarten so that extensive special education services will not be necessary for the child's academic success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Once an infant/toddler is determined eligible (each state sets its own eligibility requirements), the family identifies whom they would like to participate as part of the IFSP team. The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) Part C requires that the IFSP team consist of the family and at least two early intervention professionals from different disciplines (one being the service coordinator) â consistent with CFR Â§303.343(a)(1)(iv). However, the family may choose to include other members on the team such as the child's pediatrician, an early intervention service provider who may be working with the child, a parent advocate or trusted friend/family member.</p>
<p>The IFSP team works with the family to create a "service plan" to address the deficits of the infant or toddler and to assist the family in meeting their goals for their child's (and family's) development. The team uses information that the family provides as well as the results of at least two evaluations, all available medical records, and the informed clinical opinion of the professionals serving on the IFSP team. An initial IFSP is then created with the family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-34">[34]</a></sup> An IFSP will outline the following:</p>
<ol>
<li>The child's current levels of physical, cognitive, communication, social or emotional, and adaptive development.</li>
<li>The family's resources, priorities, and concerns to help in their child's development.</li>
<li>The desired end result for the child and for the family (goals/outcomes), as well as the steps needed to achieve said end result (objectives). The plan will be monitored and evaluated quarterly to gauge progress. If the family chooses to revise the goals or the plan, they include updates as revised additions to the plan.</li>
<li>The early intervention services for the child and the family. This includes how often and the method of how the child and the family will receive the services.</li>
<li>The different environments that the services will be provided in and justification for services not provided in the "natural environment" as defined by IDEA (the location that a child without a disability would spend most of his/her time). For example, the family might have requested to receive services for the child at a day care center or in their home.</li>
<li>The date the services will begin and their anticipated duration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Individual_Family_Service_Plan_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Individual_Family_Service_Plan-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li>The identification of the service coordinator, from the profession most immediately relevant to the infant's or toddler's family's needs, who will be responsible for the coordination and implementation of the plan with the other agencies and persons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Individual_Family_Service_Plan_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Individual_Family_Service_Plan-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li>For toddlers approaching the third birthday, the IFSP will include a transition plan outlining the steps, activities, and services needed to support the transition of the toddler with a disability to preschool or other appropriate services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Individual_Family_Service_Plan_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-The_Individual_Family_Service_Plan-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>In summary, a key to an effective IFSP is to include outcomes that âaddress the entire familyâs well-being and not only outcomes designed to benefit the childâs development.â<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-34">[34]</a></sup> For this reason, the IFSP will inherently have goals that are designed for the family as well as for the child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The service coordinator helps the early intervention team of service providers write objectives that meet the familyâs priorities and concerns.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_between_IFSP_and_IEP">Differences between IFSP and IEP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Differences between IFSP and IEP">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When writing the IFSP for a child, the IFSP can (but not always) outline services that are not one of the seventeen mandated early intervention services under Part C of the IDEA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-search.proquest.com_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-search.proquest.com-38">[38]</a></sup> For example, a parent may need counseling services to overcome debilitating depression in order to better care for the infant or toddler, and these services will be written into the family's plan. The IEP (Individualized Education Plan) cannot include services to meet "family goals" but must focus solely on what the child needs to achieve academic success in an educational setting (whether the class or activity is academic or extra-curricular in nature).</p>
<p>The Individualized Family Service Plan is different compared to an Individual Education Plan in other key ways:</p>
<ol>
<li>Eligibility for early intervention (birth to three) under Part C of IDEA is set by each state individually and is often different from eligibility for special education (3â21) under Part B of IDEA.</li>
<li>The IFSP will have goals and outcomes for the family and for the infant's/toddler's development.</li>
<li>Goals on the IFSP may be in non-academic areas of development such as mobility, self-care, and social/emotional well-being.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-search.proquest.com_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-search.proquest.com-38">[38]</a></sup> The IEP has goals and outcomes for the child only and related entirely to his/her ability to adapt to and progress in an educational setting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li>The IFSP includes services to help a family in natural environment settings (not just in daycare/preschool) but at home, in the community, etc. Services and activities on the IFSP could be tailored to "nap time," "infant swimming lessons at the YMCA," "church outings," etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-search.proquest.com_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-search.proquest.com-38">[38]</a></sup> The IEP provides services solely on what happens in a pre-school or K-5 school environment or school-sponsored field trip/activity<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li>The IFSP team involves a service coordinator who assists the family in developing and implementing the IFSP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-search.proquest.com_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-search.proquest.com-38">[38]</a></sup> The IEP team also involves the family, but the school district generally does not provide a professional who represents them and provides case-management/service coordination. The family will have to communicate with the special education department's designee.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Child_Abuse_Prevention_and_Treatment_Act">Child Abuse Prevention and Treatment Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Child Abuse Prevention and Treatment Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Keeping Children and Families Safe Act of 2003 (P.L. 108-36) amended CAPTA by requiring that cases of abused and neglected children, or those pre- or post-natally exposed to illegal substances, are referred to early intervention services using IDEA Part C funds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-40">[40]</a></sup> This provision is also reflected in the 2004 revision of IDEA. Specifically, states can apply for grant money from IDEA for specific identification and referral programs.</p>
<p>Abused and neglected children are included under IDEA part C due to the growing body of evidence showing increased risk of developmental delay among children in the child welfare system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-40">[40]</a></sup> In 2013, there were an estimated 679,000 victims of child abuse and neglect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-41">[41]</a></sup> Nearly half (47%) were five years or younger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-41">[41]</a></sup> The results of the 2008 National Survey of Child and Adolescent Well-Being (NSCAW) reported that children in Child Welfare had below average cognitive, behavioral, daily living, language, social-emotional and social skills than their peers. Slightly less than half of children five and under showed developmental delay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-42">[42]</a></sup> A social and emotional assessment given to caretakers of these children showed 34.1% had a possible problem, and 27.0% had a possible social/emotional deficit or delay compared to 25% and 15%, respectively, in a standardized population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-42">[42]</a></sup> Neurodevelopmentally, children in the child welfare system have similar risks to premature and low birth weight infants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-42">[42]</a></sup> Children in this population scored nearly one standard deviation below the mean of the early cognitive development tool used for assessment. Language skills fell almost one standard deviation below the norm as well. Overall, 42.6% of children aged one to five years showed a need for developmental support, making them potentially eligible for early intervention services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_42-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In order to track the adherence to the law, the Child Abuse Prevention and Treatment Act Reauthorization Act of 2010 (P.L. 111-320) required that eligible children, and those actually referred to EI be reported by each state beginning in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-40">[40]</a></sup> A 2008 survey of 30 participating states by the IDEA Infant and Toddler Coordinators Association showed that 65% of children under three that are abused or neglected are being routinely screened for developmental delays. Fifty percent of respondents did not know whether their referrals for Part C had increased or decreased in the prior year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> As noted by many respondents to this survey, the referral system needs more funding and better communication among child welfare personnel.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_relationship_between_IDEA_and_Section_504">The relationship between IDEA and Section 504</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: The relationship between IDEA and Section 504">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Section_504_of_the_Rehabilitation_Act" title="Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act">Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973</a> is another law which assures certain protections to certain students with disabilities. Â§504 states that:</p>
<p>"No otherwise qualified individual with a disability in the United States . . . shall, solely by reason of her or his disability, be excluded from the participation in, be denied the benefits of, or be subjected to discrimination under any program or activity receiving Federal financial assistance . . . .". 29 U.S.C. 794(a).</p>
<p>Recipients of this Federal financial assistance include public school districts, institutions of higher education, and other state and local education agencies. The regulations implementing Section 504 in the context of educational institutions appear at 34 C.F.R. Part 104 D.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Â§504 applies to all programs or activities, including schools, that receive federal financial assistance. See 29 U.S.C. 794(b)(2)(B) (defining "program or activity" to include the operations of "local educational agenc[ies]").</p>
<p>Eligibility under Â§504 is different than under IDEA. While IDEA recognizes thirteen categories of disability, Â§504 defines individuals with disabilities to include any individual with a physical or mental condition which substantially limits at least one major life activity. 29 U.S.C. 705(20). It also includes persons with a history of such a disability and those who are perceived to have a disability. Most, if not all, children eligible under IDEA are also entitled to Â§504's protections. Regulations promulgated by the Department of Education offer additional guidance regarding the statute's prohibitions in the context of this case. See 34 C.F.R. 104 <i>et seq</i>.</p>
<p>Like IDEA, Â§504's regulations include âchild findâ provisions. Thus, public school districts have an affirmative duty to identify and evaluate every qualified handicapped child residing in the recipient's jurisdiction who is not receiving a public education and take appropriate steps to notify handicapped persons and their parents or guardians of the recipient's duties under Â§504. 34 C.F.R. 104.32.</p>
<p>The Section 504 regulations require a school district to provide a "free appropriate public education" (FAPE) to each qualified student with a disability who is in the school district's jurisdiction, regardless of the nature or severity of the disability. Under Section 504, FAPE consists of the provision of regular or special education and related aids and services designed to meet the student's individual educational needs as adequately as the needs of nondisabled students are met.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislative_history">Legislative history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Legislative history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>1975</b> â The Education for All Handicapped Children Act (EAHCA) became law. It was renamed the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) in 1990.</p>
<p><b>1990</b>â IDEA first came into being on October 30, 1990 when the "Education of All Handicapped Children Act" (itself having been introduced in 1975) was renamed "Individuals with Disabilities Education Act." (Pub. L. No. 101-476, 104 Stat. 1142). IDEA received minor amendments in October 1991 (Pub. L. No. 102-119, 105 Stat. 587).</p>
<p><b>1997</b>â IDEA received significant amendments. The definition of disabled children expanded to include developmentally delayed children between three and nine years of age. It also required parents to attempt to resolve disputes with schools and Local Educational Agencies (LEAs) through <a href="/wiki/Mediation" title="Mediation">mediation</a>, and provided a process for doing so. The amendments authorized additional grants for technology, disabled infants and toddlers, parent training, and professional development. (Pub. L. No. 105-17, 111 Stat. 37).</p>
<p><b>2004</b>â On December 3, 2004, IDEA was amended by the Individuals With Disabilities Education Improvement Act of 2004, now known as IDEIA. Several provisions aligned IDEA with the <a href="/wiki/No_Child_Left_Behind_Act" title="No Child Left Behind Act">No Child Left Behind Act</a> of 2001, signed by President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>. It authorized fifteen states to implement 3-year IEPs on a trial basis when parents continually agree. Drawing on the report of the President's Commission on Excellence in Special Education,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> the law revised the requirements for evaluating children with learning disabilities. More concrete provisions relating to discipline of special education students was also added. (Pub. L. No. 108-446, 118 Stat. 2647).</p>
<p><b>2009</b>â Following a campaign promise for "funding the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> signed the <a href="/wiki/American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act_of_2009" title="American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009">American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009</a> (ARRA) on February 17, 2009, including $12.2 billion in additional funds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>2009</b>â Americans with Disabilities Amendments Act was signed into law in September 2008 and became effective on January 1, 2009</p>

<p>The case of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_Grove_School_District_v._T.A." title="Forest Grove School District v. T.A.">Forest Grove School District v. T.A.</a></i>, 129 S.Ct. 2484, argued before the Supreme Court on April 28, 2009, addressed the issue of whether the parents of a student who has never received <a href="/wiki/Special_education" title="Special education">special education</a> services from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_school_(government_funded)" title="Public school (government funded)">public school</a> district are potentially eligible for reimbursement of private school <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuition" title="Tuition">tuition</a> for that student under the IDEA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> On June 22, 2009 the Supreme Court held that parents of disabled children can seek reimbursement for private education expenses regardless whether their child had previously received special-education services from a public school. <p>In <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Rapids_Community_School_Dist._v._Garret_F." title="Cedar Rapids Community School Dist. v. Garret F.">Cedar Rapids Community School Dist. v. Garret F.</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_Supreme_Court_cases,_volume_526" title="List of United States Supreme Court cases, volume 526">526</a> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Reports" title="United States Reports">U.S.</a> 66 (1999), was a Supreme Court case in which the Court, relying heavily on Irving Independent School Dist. v. Tatro, 468 U. S. 883 (1984), ruled that the related IDEA services provision required public school districts to fund "continuous, one-on-one nursing care for disabled children" such as the ventilator-dependent child in this case, despite arguments from the school district concerning the costs of the services."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Javier_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Javier-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:6</sup> There is no undue burden exemption. Under the Court's reading of the IDEA's relevant provisions, medical treatments such as suctioning, ventilator checks, catheterization, and others which can be administered by non-physician personnel come within the parameters of the special education law's related services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrightslaw_1999_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-wrightslaw_1999-51">[51]</a></sup> Disability advocates considered the Court decision to be a "substantial victory for families of children with disabilities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Javier_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-Javier-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:6</sup> Amendments were made in the Education Flexibility Partnership Act of 1999 to increase IDEA funding as a result of the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Javier_50-2"><a href="#cite_note-Javier-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:6</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ongoing_Supreme_Court_groundbreaking_case">Ongoing Supreme Court groundbreaking case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Ongoing Supreme Court groundbreaking case">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Endrew_F._v._Douglas_County_School_District">Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individuals_with_Disabilities_Education_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District is a Supreme Court case about "the level of educational benefit school districts must provide students with disabilities as defined by IDEA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-senate_118__amicus_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-senate_118_amicus-52">[52]</a></sup> On January 10, 2017, Supreme Court Judges began hearing the case which is described by advocates as "the most significant special-education issue to reach the high court in three decades."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washingtonpost_2017_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-washingtonpost_2017-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2016, the U.S. Supreme Court announced that it would hear the "potentially groundbreaking case" brought by a "Douglas County couple who claim that their autistic son was not provided an adequate education in the public school system as required by federal law."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SC_groundbreaking_denverpost_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-SC_groundbreaking_denverpost-54">[54]</a></sup> Access to public education through IDEA was affirmed in 1982 in Board of Education v. Rowley, but the quality of guaranteed education for students with disabilities under IDEA had not been addressed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SC_groundbreaking_denverpost_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-SC_groundbreaking_denverpost-54">[54]</a></sup> This Supreme Court case has the potential to "affect the education of 6.7 million children with disabilities" as the Court "struggles "to decide whether it should require public schools to do more under a federal law that calls for them to provide a free education that addresses the childrenâs needs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_SC_2017_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_SC_2017-55">[55]</a></sup> In the Solicitor General's Amicus brief the Supreme Court was advised that, "Resolving the conflict among the circuits will ensure that millions of children with disabilities receive a consistent level of education, while providing parents and educators much-needed guidance regarding their rights and obligations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrightslaw_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-wrightslaw-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, Endrew, who was in Grade 5 at the time at Summit View Elementary school in <a href="/wiki/Douglas_County_School_District_RE-1" title="Douglas County School District RE-1">Douglas County School District RE-1</a>, began to exhibit "severe behavioral issues." The parents removed their child from Summit View and enrolled him in a specialized school for children with <a href="/wiki/Autism" title="Autism">autism</a>, Firefly Autism in Denver, with an annual tuition of $70,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Endrew_v_Douglas_County_2016_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Endrew_v_Douglas_County_2016-57">[57]</a></sup> The family requested reimbursement for the Firefly tuition claiming the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_County_School_District_RE-1" title="Douglas County School District RE-1">Douglas County School District</a> had not fulfilled the requirements of IDEA. On May 15, 2016, they lost their case before the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_District_of_Colorado" title="United States District Court for the District of Colorado">United States District Court for the District of Colorado</a> with circuit judges <a href="/wiki/Harris_Hartz" title="Harris Hartz">Harris Hartz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Tymkovich" title="Timothy Tymkovich">Timothy Tymkovich</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gregory_A._Phillips" title="Gregory A. Phillips">Gregory A. Phillips</a> presiding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Endrew_v_Douglas_County_2016_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-Endrew_v_Douglas_County_2016-57">[57]</a></sup> Their argument was that "the federal statute only requires that schools provide students with "some educational benefit.""<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SC_groundbreaking_denverpost_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-SC_groundbreaking_denverpost-54">[54]</a></sup> In an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amicus_brief" title="Amicus brief">amicus brief</a> submitted by the Office of the <a href="/wiki/Solicitor_General_of_the_United_States" title="Solicitor General of the United States">Solicitor General</a>, the Supreme Court was urged to take the case stating that the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Tenth_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit">10th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals</a> " had set the bar â a standard of âmerely â¦ more than de minimisâ educational benefit â too low."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SC_groundbreaking_denverpost_54-3"><a href="#cite_note-SC_groundbreaking_denverpost-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"Thus, for over 30 years, this Court has held that if a State provides a program 'reasonably calculated to enable the child to receive educational benefits,' then it 'has complied with the obligations imposed by Congress and the courts can require no more...No parent or educator in America would say that a child has received an 'appropriate' or a 'specially suitable' or 'properâ education 'in the circumstances' when all the child has received are benefits that are barely more than trivial."</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>ââ<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amicus_brief" title="Amicus brief">Amicus brief</a> Office of the <a href="/wiki/Solicitor_General_of_the_United_States" title="Solicitor General of the United States">Solicitor General</a> August 18, 2016</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Supreme Court Justices <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stephen_G._Breyer" title="Stephen G. Breyer">Stephen G. Breyer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samuel_A._Alito_Jr." title="Samuel A. Alito Jr.">Samuel A. Alito Jr.</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthony_M._Kennedy" title="Anthony M. Kennedy">Anthony M. Kennedy</a> expressed concerns about the implications of implementing IDEA with changes in quality of education standards. Breyer cautioned about potential rising costs of litigation, for example, extraneous lawsuits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_SC_2017_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_SC_2017-55">[55]</a></sup> Kennedy questioned the financial cost to districts with severely disabled students; Alito considered the burden on poorer school districts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_SC_2017_55-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_SC_2017-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p><br/>
Not all of the eleven circuit courts have considered the issue of standards and in those that have, only two set "meaningful educational benefit" standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laschoolreport_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-laschoolreport-58">[58]</a></sup> The Supreme Court will decide whether a uniform standard should apply nationally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washingtonpost_2017_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-washingtonpost_2017-53">[53]</a></sup> Alito expressed frustration with the "blizzard of words" produced in the last thirty years of appeals courts hearings which offered different views on standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_SC_2017_55-3"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_SC_2017-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Justice <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Bader_Ginsburg" title="Ruth Bader Ginsburg">Ruth Bader Ginsburg</a> cited the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Education_of_the_Hendrick_Hudson_Central_School_District_v._Rowley" title="Board of Education of the Hendrick Hudson Central School District v. Rowley">Board of Education v. Rowley (1982)</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_Supreme_Court_cases,_volume_458" title="List of United States Supreme Court cases, volume 458">458</a> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Reports" title="United States Reports">U.S.</a> 176 (1982) in which the Court held that public schools were "not required by law to provide sign language interpreters to deaf students who are otherwise receiving an equal and adequate education."</p>
<p>The lawyer for the parents, <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_L._Fisher" title="Jeffrey L. Fisher">Jeffrey L. Fisher</a>, claimed that schools should provide "substantially equal educational opportunities" and in most cases, the costs involve "things like providing Braille textbooks, providing an iPad, providing some specialized instruction by a staff member whoâs already on staff...[T]here are going to be some extreme cases....[IDEA] does not permit cost to trump what the act otherwise requires. Schools should provide "a level of educational services designed to allow the child to progress from grade to grade in the general curriculum."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_SC_2017_55-4"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_SC_2017-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>On November 21, 2016, 118 lawmakers filed a bicameral amicus brief supporting the rights of students with disabilities to receive a "meaningful" public education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-senate_118__amicus_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-senate_118_amicus-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118_members_Congress_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-118_members_Congress-59">[59]</a></sup> Since August 2016, <a href="/wiki/National_Education_Association" title="National Education Association">National Education Association</a> (NEA), the United States' largest <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade union</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> former officials of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Education" title="United States Department of Education">U.S. Department of Education</a>, National Disability Rights Network, the <a href="/wiki/National_Education_Association" title="National Education Association">National Education Association</a>, Advocates for Children of New York, the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Parent_Attorneys_and_Advocates" title="Council of Parent Attorneys and Advocates">Council of Parent Attorneys and Advocates</a>, the Coalition of Texans with Disabilities, and the National Center for Special Education in Charter Schools" and others have "submitted amicus briefs in support of the child."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrightslaw_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-wrightslaw-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
