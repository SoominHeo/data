<p>The term "<a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>", when used in the geographical sense, is the <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">contiguous United States</a>, the state of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, the island state of <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, the five insular <a href="/wiki/United_States_territory" title="United States territory">territories</a> of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Mariana_Islands" title="Northern Mariana Islands">Northern Mariana Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="United States Virgin Islands">U.S. Virgin Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a>, and <a href="/wiki/American_Samoa" title="American Samoa">American Samoa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Insular_area" title="Insular area">minor outlying possessions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The United States shares land borders with <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and maritime borders with <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">the Bahamas</a> in addition to Canada and Mexico.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Area">Area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From 1989 through 1996, the total area of the US was listed as 9,372,610 km<sup>2</sup> (3,618,780 sq mi) (land + inland water only). The listed total area changed to 9,629,091 km<sup>2</sup> (3,717,813 sq mi) in 1997 (Great Lakes area and coastal waters added), to 9,631,418 km<sup>2</sup> (3,718,711 sq mi) in 2004, to 9,631,420 km<sup>2</sup> (3,718,710 sq mi) in 2006, and to 9,826,630 km<sup>2</sup> (3,794,080 sq mi) in 2007 (territorial waters added). Currently, the CIA <i>World Factbook</i> gives 9,826,675 km<sup>2</sup> (3,794,100 sq mi),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WF_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-WF-2">[2]</a></sup> the United Nations Statistics Division gives 9,629,091 km<sup>2</sup> (3,717,813 sq mi),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and the <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i> gives 9,522,055 km<sup>2</sup> (3,676,486 sq mi)(Great Lakes area included but not coastal waters).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> These sources consider only the 50 states and the Federal District, and exclude overseas territories.</p>
<p>By <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_countries_and_outlying_territories_by_total_area" title="List of countries and outlying territories by total area">total area</a> (<a href="/wiki/Territorial_waters" title="Territorial waters">water</a> as well as land), the United States is either slightly larger or smaller than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>, making it the world's third or fourth largest country. China and the United States are smaller than <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> in total area, but are larger than <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. By land area only (exclusive of waters), the United States is the world's third largest country, after Russia and China, with Canada in fourth. Whether the US or China is the third largest country by total area depends on two factors: (1) The validity of China's claim on <a href="/wiki/Aksai_Chin" title="Aksai Chin">Aksai Chin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trans-Karakoram_Tract" title="Trans-Karakoram Tract">Trans-Karakoram Tract</a>. Both these territories are also claimed by India, so are not counted; and (2) How US calculates its own surface area. Since the initial publishing of the World Factbook, the CIA has updated the total area of United States a number of times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_characteristics">General characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: General characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USA-satellite.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1050" data-file-width="1680" height="281" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/USA-satellite.jpg/450px-USA-satellite.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/USA-satellite.jpg/675px-USA-satellite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/USA-satellite.jpg/900px-USA-satellite.jpg 2x" width="450"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USA-satellite.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A satellite composite image of the <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">contiguous United States</a>. <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">Deciduous</a> vegetation and <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a> prevail in the east, transitioning to <a href="/wiki/Prairie" title="Prairie">prairies</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boreal_forest" title="Boreal forest">boreal forests</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rockies</a> in the west, and <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">deserts</a> in the southwest. In the northeast, the coasts of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_seaboard" title="Atlantic seaboard">Atlantic seaboard</a> host much of the country's population.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The United States shares land borders with Canada (to the north) and Mexico (to the south), and a <a href="/wiki/Territorial_waters" title="Territorial waters">territorial water</a> border with Russia in the northwest, and two territorial water borders in the southeast between Florida and Cuba, and Florida and the Bahamas. The contiguous forty-eight states are otherwise bounded by the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> on the west, the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> on the east, and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> to the southeast. Alaska borders the Pacific Ocean to the south and southwest, the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">Bering Strait</a> to the west, and the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> to the north, while Hawaii lies far to the southwest of the mainland in the Pacific Ocean.</p>
<p>Forty-eight of the states are in the single region between Canada and Mexico; this group is referred to, with varying precision and formality, as the <i>continental</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">contiguous United States</a></i>, and as the <i>Lower 48</i>. Alaska, which is not included in the term <i>contiguous United States</i>, is at the northwestern end of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, separated from the Lower 48 by Canada.</p>
<p>The capital city, <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, District of Columbia</a>, is a federal district located on land donated by the state of <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>. (<a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> had also donated land, but it was returned in 1849.) The United States also has <a href="/wiki/Insular_area" title="Insular area">overseas territories</a> with varying levels of independence and organization: in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean</a> the territories of Puerto Rico and the U.S. Virgin Islands, and in the Pacific the inhabited territories of Guam, American Samoa, and the Northern Mariana Islands, along with a number of uninhabited island territories.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiographic_divisions">Physiographic divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physiographic divisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_physiographic_region" title="United States physiographic region">United States physiographic region</a></div>

<p>The eastern United States has a varied topography. A broad, flat coastal plain lines the Atlantic and Gulf shores from the Texas-Mexico border to New York City, and includes the Florida peninsula. Areas further inland feature rolling hills and temperate forests. The <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian Mountains</a> form a line of low mountains separating the eastern seaboard from the Great Lakes and the Mississippi Basin.</p>
<p>The five <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> are located in the north-central portion of the country, four of them forming part of the border with <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, only <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a> situated entirely within United States. The southeast United States contain subtropical forests and <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangrove</a> wetlands in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. West of the Appalachians lies the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> basin and two large eastern tributaries, the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_River" title="Tennessee River">Tennessee River</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ohio_Valley" title="Ohio Valley">Ohio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Valley" title="Tennessee Valley">Tennessee</a> Valleys and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midwestern_U.S." title="Midwestern U.S.">Midwest</a> consist largely of rolling hills and productive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farmland" title="Farmland">farmland</a>, stretching south to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Gulf_Coast" title="U.S. Gulf Coast">Gulf Coast</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> lie west of the Mississippi River and east of the Rocky Mountains. A large portion of the country's agricultural products are grown in the Great Plains. Before their general conversion to farmland, the Great Plains were noted for their extensive grasslands, from <a href="/wiki/Tallgrass_prairie" title="Tallgrass prairie">tallgrass prairie</a> in the eastern plains to <a href="/wiki/Shortgrass_prairie" title="Shortgrass prairie">shortgrass steppe</a> in the western <a href="/wiki/High_Plains_(United_States)" title="High Plains (United States)">High Plains</a>. Elevation rises gradually from less than a few hundred feet near the Mississippi River to more than a mile high in the High Plains. The generally low relief of the plains is broken in several places, most notably in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Ozarks" title="The Ozarks">Ozark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ouachita_Mountains" title="Ouachita Mountains">Ouachita Mountains</a>, which form the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Interior_Highlands" title="U.S. Interior Highlands">U.S. Interior Highlands</a>, the only major mountainous region between the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian Mountains</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Great Plains come to an abrupt end at the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a>. The Rocky Mountains form a large portion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_U.S." title="Western U.S.">Western U.S.</a>, entering from <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and stretching nearly to <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. The Rocky Mountain region is the highest region of the United States by average elevation. The Rocky Mountains generally contain fairly mild slopes and wider peaks compared to some of the other great mountain ranges, with a few exceptions (such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teton_Mountains" title="Teton Mountains">Teton Mountains</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sawatch_Range" title="Sawatch Range">Sawatch Range</a> in <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>). The highest peaks of the Rockies are found in Colorado, the tallest peak being <a href="/wiki/Mount_Elbert" title="Mount Elbert">Mount Elbert</a> at 14,440 ft (4,400 m). The Rocky Mountains contain some of the most spectacular, and well known scenery in the world. In addition, instead of being one generally continuous and solid mountain range, it is broken up into a number of smaller, intermittent mountain ranges, forming a large series of basins and valleys.</p>
<p>West of the Rocky Mountains lies the <a href="/wiki/Intermontane_Plateaus" title="Intermontane Plateaus">Intermontane Plateaus</a> (also known as the <a href="/wiki/Intermountain_West" title="Intermountain West">Intermountain West</a>), a large, arid desert lying between the Rockies and the <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Range" title="Cascade Range">Cascades</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> ranges. The large southern portion, known as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin" title="Great Basin">Great Basin</a>, consists of salt flats, drainage basins, and many small north-south mountain ranges. The <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">Southwest</a> is predominantly a low-lying desert region. A portion known as the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Plateau" title="Colorado Plateau">Colorado Plateau</a>, centered around the <a href="/wiki/Four_Corners" title="Four Corners">Four Corners</a> region, is considered to have some of the most spectacular scenery in the world. It is accentuated in such national parks as <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon_National_Park" title="Grand Canyon National Park">Grand Canyon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arches_National_Park" title="Arches National Park">Arches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesa_Verde_National_Park" title="Mesa Verde National Park">Mesa Verde National Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bryce_Canyon_National_Park" title="Bryce Canyon National Park">Bryce Canyon</a>, among others. Other smaller Intermontane areas include the Columbia Plateau covering eastern Washington, western Idaho and northeast Oregon and the Snake River Plain in Southern Idaho.</p>

<p>The Intermontane Plateaus come to an end at the <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Range" title="Cascade Range">Cascade Range</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a>. The Cascades consist of largely intermittent, volcanic mountains, many rising prominently from the surrounding landscape. The Sierra Nevada, further south, is a high, rugged, and dense mountain range. It contains the highest point in the contiguous 48 states, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Whitney" title="Mount Whitney">Mount Whitney</a> (14,505 ft or 4,421 m)[1] It is located at the boundary between California's Inyo and Tulare counties, just 84.6 mi or 136.2 km west-northwest of the lowest point in North America at the <a href="/wiki/Badwater_Basin" title="Badwater Basin">Badwater Basin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Death_Valley_National_Park" title="Death Valley National Park">Death Valley National Park</a> at 279 ft or 85 m below sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NED_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NED-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>These areas contain some spectacular scenery as well, as evidenced by such national parks as <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_National_Park" title="Yosemite National Park">Yosemite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Rainier_National_Park" title="Mount Rainier National Park">Mount Rainier</a>. West of the Cascades and Sierra Nevada is a series of valleys, such as the <a href="/wiki/Central_Valley_(California)" title="Central Valley (California)">Central Valley</a> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Willamette_Valley" title="Willamette Valley">Willamette Valley</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>. Along the coast is a series of low mountain ranges known as the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_Ranges" title="Pacific Coast Ranges">Pacific Coast Ranges</a>. Much of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> coast is inhabited by some of the densest vegetation outside of the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">Tropics</a>, and also the tallest trees in the world (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coast_Redwood" title="Coast Redwood">Redwoods</a>).</p>
<p>Alaska contains some of the most dramatic and untapped scenery in the country. Tall, prominent mountain ranges rise up sharply from broad, flat tundra plains. On the islands off the south and southwest coast are many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanoes" title="Volcanoes">volcanoes</a>. Hawaii, far to the south of Alaska in the Pacific Ocean, is a chain of tropical, volcanic islands, popular as a tourist destination for many from <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> and the mainland United States.</p>
<p>The territories of Puerto Rico and the U.S. Virgin Islands encompass a number of tropical isles in the northeastern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>. In the Pacific Ocean the territories of Guam and the Northern Mariana Islands occupy the limestone and volcanic isles of the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">Mariana archipelago</a>, and American Samoa (the only populated US territory in the southern hemisphere) encompasses volcanic peaks and coral atolls in the eastern part of the <a href="/wiki/Samoan_Islands" title="Samoan Islands">Samoan Islands</a> chain.</p>

<p>The geography of the United States varies across their immense area. Within the continental U.S., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physiographic_Regions_of_the_United_States" title="Physiographic Regions of the United States">eight distinct physiographic divisions</a> exist, though each is composed of several smaller physiographic subdivisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> These major divisions are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Laurentian_Upland" title="Laurentian Upland">Laurentian Upland</a> - part of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Shield" title="Canadian Shield">Canadian Shield</a> that extends into the northern United States <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Lakes_(North_America)" title="Great Lakes (North America)">Great Lakes</a> area.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Plain" title="Atlantic Plain">Atlantic Plain</a> - the coastal regions of the eastern and southern parts includes the continental shelf, the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">Atlantic Coast</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Gulf_Coast" title="U.S. Gulf Coast">Gulf Coast</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appalachian_Highlands" title="Appalachian Highlands">Appalachian Highlands</a> - lying on the eastern side of the United States, it includes the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian Mountains</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Watchung_Mountains" title="Watchung Mountains">Watchung Mountains</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adirondacks" title="Adirondacks">Adirondacks</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> province originally containing the Great Eastern Forest.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Interior_Plains" title="Interior Plains">Interior Plains</a> - part of the interior contentintal United States, it includes much of what is called the Great Plains.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interior_Highlands" title="Interior Highlands">Interior Highlands</a> - also part of the interior contentintal United States, this division includes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ozark" title="Ozark">Ozark</a> Plateau.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountain System</a> - one branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_cordillera" title="American cordillera">Cordilleran</a> system lying far inland in the western states.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geography_of_the_United_States_Intermontane_Plateaus" title="Geography of the United States Intermontane Plateaus">Intermontane Plateaus</a> - also divided into the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Plateau" title="Columbia Plateau">Columbia Plateau</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Plateau" title="Colorado Plateau">Colorado Plateau</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Basin_and_Range_Province" title="Basin and Range Province">Basin and Range Province</a>, it is a system of plateaus, basins, ranges and gorges between the Rocky and Pacific Mountain Systems. It is the setting for the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon" title="Grand Canyon">Grand Canyon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin" title="Great Basin">Great Basin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Death_Valley" title="Death Valley">Death Valley</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_Ranges" title="Pacific Coast Ranges">Pacific Mountain System</a> - the coastal mountain ranges and features in the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">west coast of the United States</a>.</li>
</ul>

<p>The Atlantic coast of the United States is low, with minor exceptions. The Appalachian Highland owes its oblique northeast-southwest trend to crustal deformations which in very early geological time gave a beginning to what later came to be the Appalachian mountain system. This system had its climax of deformation so long ago (probably in <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> time) that it has since then been very generally reduced to moderate or low relief. It owes its present-day altitude either to renewed elevations along the earlier lines or to the survival of the most resistant rocks as residual mountains. The oblique trend of this coast would be even more pronounced but for a comparatively modern crustal movement, causing a depression in the northeast resulting in an encroachment of the sea upon the land. Additionally, the southeastern section has undergone an elevation resulting in the advance of the land upon the sea.</p>
<p>While the Atlantic coast is relatively low, the Pacific coast is, with few exceptions, hilly or mountainous. This coast has been defined chiefly by geologically recent crustal deformations, and hence still preserves a greater relief than that of the Atlantic. The low Atlantic coast and the hilly or mountainous Pacific coast foreshadow the leading features in the distribution of mountains within the United States.</p>
<p>The east coast Appalachian system, originally forest covered, is relatively low and narrow and is bordered on the southeast and south by an important coastal plain. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_cordillera" title="American cordillera">Cordilleran system</a> on the western side of the continent is lofty, broad and complicated having two branches, the Rocky Mountain System and the Pacific Mountain System. In between these mountain systems lie the Intermontaine Plateaus. Both the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_River" title="Columbia River">Columbia River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Colorado_River" title="Colorado River">Colorado River</a> rise far inland near the easternmost members of the Cordilleran system, and flow through plateaus and intermontaine basins to the ocean. Heavy forests cover the northwest coast, but elsewhere trees are found only on the higher ranges below the Alpine region. The <a href="/wiki/Intermontane" title="Intermontane">intermontane</a> valleys, plateaus and basins range from treeless to desert with the most arid region being in the southwest.</p>
<p>The Laurentian Highlands, the Interior Plains and the Interior Highlands lie between the two coasts, stretching from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> northward, far beyond the national boundary, to the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a>. The central plains are divided by a hardly perceptible height of land into a <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> and a United States portion. It is from the United States side, that the great <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi</a> system discharges southward to the Gulf of Mexico. The upper Mississippi and some of the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio</a> basin is the semi-arid prairie region, with trees originally only along the watercourses. The uplands towards the Appalachians were included in the great eastern forested area, while the western part of the plains has so dry a climate that its native plant life is scanty, and in the south it is practically barren.</p>

<li>Highest point: <a href="/wiki/Denali" title="Denali">Denali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denali_Borough,_Alaska" title="Denali Borough, Alaska">Denali Borough, Alaska</a> 20,310 ft (6,190.5 m)</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Due to its large size and wide range of geographic features, the United States contains examples of nearly every global climate. The climate is temperate in most areas, subtropical in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">Southern United States</a>, tropical in Hawaii and southern <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, polar in Alaska, semiarid in the Great Plains west of the <a href="/wiki/100th_meridian_west" title="100th meridian west">100th meridian</a>, Mediterranean in coastal <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> and arid in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin" title="Great Basin">Great Basin</a>. Its comparatively favorable agricultural climate contributed (in part) to the country's rise as a world power, with infrequent severe drought in the major agricultural regions, a general lack of widespread flooding, and a mainly temperate climate that receives adequate precipitation.</p>
<p>The main influence on U.S. weather is the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">polar jet stream</a> which migrates northward into Canada in the summer months, and then southward into the USA in the winter months. The jet stream brings in large low pressure systems from the northern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> that enter the US mainland over the Pacific Northwest. The <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Range" title="Cascade Range">Cascade Range</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a> pick up most of the moisture from these systems as they move eastward. Greatly diminished by the time they reach the High Plains, much of the moisture has been sapped by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)#Orographic_precipitation" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">orographic effect</a> as it is forced over several mountain ranges.</p>
<p>Once it moves over the Great Plains, uninterrupted flat land allows it to reorganize and can lead to major clashes of air masses. In addition, moisture from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> is often drawn northward. When combined with a powerful jet stream, this can lead to violent thunderstorms, especially during spring and summer. Sometimes during winter these storms can combine with another low pressure system as they move up the East Coast and into the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, where they intensify rapidly. These storms are known as <a href="/wiki/Nor%27easter" title="Nor'easter">Nor'easters</a> and often bring widespread, heavy snowfall to <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>. The uninterrupted flat grasslands of the Great Plains also lead to some of the most extreme climate swings in the world. Temperatures can rise or drop rapidly and winds can be extreme, and the flow of heat waves or Arctic air masses often advance uninterrupted through the plains.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin" title="Great Basin">Great Basin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Plateau" title="Columbia Plateau">Columbia Plateau</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Intermontane_Plateaus" title="Intermontane Plateaus">Intermontane Plateaus</a>) are arid or semiarid regions that lie in the rain shadow of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cascade_Mountains" title="Cascade Mountains">Cascades</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a>. Precipitation averages less than 15 inches (38 cm). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southwestern_U.S." title="Southwestern U.S.">Southwest</a> is a hot desert, with temperatures exceeding 100 °F (37.8 °C) for several weeks at a time in summer. The Southwest and the Great Basin are also affected by the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_California" title="Gulf of California">Gulf of California</a> from July to September, which brings localized but often severe thunderstorms to the region.</p>
<p>Much of California consists of a <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">Mediterranean climate</a>, with sometimes excessive rainfall from October–April and nearly no rain the rest of the year. In the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> rain falls year-round, but is much heavier during winter and spring. The mountains of the west receive abundant precipitation and very heavy snowfall. The Cascades are one of the snowiest places in the world, with some places averaging over 600 inches (1,524 cm) of snow annually, but the lower elevations closer to the coast receive very little snow.</p>
<p>Florida has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical_climate" title="Subtropical climate">subtropical climate</a> in the northern and central part of the state and a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate" title="Tropical climate">tropical climate</a> in the far southern part of the state. Summers and wet and winters are dry in Florida. Annually much of central and southern Florida are frost-free. The mild winters of Florida allow a massive citrus industry to thrive in the central part of the state, and Florida is second to only Brazil in <a href="/wiki/Citrus_production" title="Citrus production">citrus production</a> in the world.</p>
<p>Another significant (but localized) weather effect is <a href="/wiki/Lake-effect_snow" title="Lake-effect snow">lake-effect snow</a> that falls south and east of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>, especially in the hilly portions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_Peninsula" title="Upper Peninsula">Upper Peninsula</a> of <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> and on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tug_Hill_Plateau" title="Tug Hill Plateau">Tug Hill Plateau</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>. The lake effect dumped well over 5 feet (1.52 m) of snow in the area of Buffalo, New York throughout the 2006-2007 winter. The <a href="/wiki/Wasatch_Front" title="Wasatch Front">Wasatch Front</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wasatch_Range" title="Wasatch Range">Wasatch Range</a> in <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a> can also receive significant lake effect accumulations from the <a href="/wiki/Great_Salt_Lake" title="Great Salt Lake">Great Salt Lake</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extremes">Extremes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Extremes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/U.S._state_temperature_extremes" title="U.S. state temperature extremes">U.S. state temperature extremes</a></div>
<p>In northern Alaska, <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">arctic</a> conditions predominate, and the temperature has fallen as low as −80 °F (−62.2 °C).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> On the other end of the spectrum, <a href="/wiki/Death_Valley_National_Park" title="Death Valley National Park">Death Valley, California</a> once reached 134 °F (56.7 °C), the highest temperature ever recorded on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Death_Valley_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Death_Valley-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On average, the mountains of the western states receive the highest levels of snowfall on Earth. The greatest annual snowfall level is at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Rainier" title="Mount Rainier">Mount Rainier</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, at 692 inches (1,758 cm); the record there was 1,122 inches (2,850 cm) in the winter of 1971–72. This record was broken by the Mt. Baker Ski Area in northwestern Washington which reported 1,140 inches (2,896 cm) of snowfall for the 1998-99 snowfall season. Other places with significant snowfall outside the Cascade Range are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wasatch_Mountains" title="Wasatch Mountains">Wasatch Mountains</a>, near the <a href="/wiki/Great_Salt_Lake" title="Great Salt Lake">Great Salt Lake</a>, the <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Mountains" title="San Juan Mountains">San Juan Mountains</a> in <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tahoe" title="Lake Tahoe">Lake Tahoe</a>.</p>
<p>In the east, while snowfall does not approach western levels, the region near the Great Lakes and the mountains of the Northeast receive the most. Along the northwestern Pacific coast, rainfall is greater than anywhere else in the continental U.S., with <a href="/wiki/Quinault_Rainforest" title="Quinault Rainforest">Quinault Rainforest</a> in Washington having an average of 137 inches (348 cm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Hawaii receives even more, with 460 inches (1,168 cm) measured annually on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Waialeale" title="Mount Waialeale">Mount Waialeale</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Kauai" title="Kauai">Kauai</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Mojave_Desert" title="Mojave Desert">Mojave Desert</a>, in the southwest, is home to the driest locale in the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Yuma,_Arizona" title="Yuma, Arizona">Yuma, Arizona</a>, has an average of 2.63 inches (6.7 cm) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In central portions of the U.S., <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> are more common than anywhere else on Earth<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and touch down most commonly in the spring and summer. Deadly and destructive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricanes</a> occur almost every year along the Atlantic seaboard and the Gulf of Mexico. The Appalachian region and the Midwest experience the worst floods, though virtually no area in the U.S. is immune to flooding. The Southwest has the worst droughts; one is thought to have lasted over 500 years and to have hurt <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancestral_Pueblo_peoples" title="Ancestral Pueblo peoples">Ancestral Pueblo peoples</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The West is affected by large wildfires each year.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_disasters">Natural disasters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Natural disasters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The United States is affected by a variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_disasters" title="Natural disasters">natural disasters</a> yearly. Although drought is rare, it has occasionally caused major disruption, such as during the <a href="/wiki/Dust_Bowl" title="Dust Bowl">Dust Bowl</a> (1931–1942). Farmland failed throughout the Plains, entire regions were virtually depopulated, and dust storms ravaged the land.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tornadoes_and_hurricanes">Tornadoes and hurricanes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tornadoes and hurricanes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Great Plains and Midwest, due to the contrasting air masses, sees frequent severe thunderstorms and tornado outbreaks during spring and summer with around 1,000 tornadoes occurring each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The strip of land from north <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> north to <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">Nebraska</a> and east into <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> is known as <a href="/wiki/Tornado_Alley" title="Tornado Alley">Tornado Alley</a>, where many houses have tornado shelters and many towns have tornado sirens.</p>
<p>Hurricanes are another natural disaster found in the US, which can hit anywhere along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Gulf_Coast" title="U.S. Gulf Coast">Gulf Coast</a> or the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">Atlantic Coast</a> as well as Hawaii in the Pacific Ocean. Particularly at risk are the central and southern <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> coasts, the area from southeastern <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> east to the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a>, peninsular <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">Outer Banks</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, although any portion of the coast could be struck.</p>
<p>Hurricane season runs from June 1 to November 30, with a peak from mid-August through early October. Some of the more devastating hurricanes have included the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galveston_Hurricane_of_1900" title="Galveston Hurricane of 1900">Galveston Hurricane of 1900</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">Hurricane Andrew</a> in 1992, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> in 2005. The remnants of tropical cyclones from the Eastern Pacific also occasionally impact the western United States, bringing moderate to heavy rainfall.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flooding">Flooding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Flooding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Occasional severe flooding is experienced. There was the <a href="/wiki/Great_Mississippi_Flood_of_1927" title="Great Mississippi Flood of 1927">Great Mississippi Flood of 1927</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Flood_of_1993" title="Great Flood of 1993">Great Flood of 1993</a>, and widespread flooding and mudslides caused by the 1982-1983 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ENSO" title="ENSO">El Niño</a> event in the western United States. Localized flooding can, however, occur anywhere, and mudslides from heavy rain can cause problems in any mountainous area, particularly the Southwest. Large stretches of desert shrub in the west can fuel the spread of <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfires</a>. The narrow canyons of many mountain areas in the west and severe thunderstorm activity during the summer lead to sometimes devastating <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash floods</a> as well, while Nor'Easter snowstorms can bring activity to a halt throughout the Northeast (although heavy snowstorms can occur almost anywhere).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geologic">Geologic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Geologic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The West Coast of the continental United States and areas of Alaska (including the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian Islands</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alaskan_Peninsula" title="Alaskan Peninsula">Alaskan Peninsula</a> and southern Alaskan coast) make up part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Ring_of_Fire" title="Pacific Ring of Fire">Pacific Ring of Fire</a>, an area of heavy tectonic and volcanic activity that is the source of 90% of the world's earthquakes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="90% needs footnote (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The American Northwest sees the highest concentration of active volcanoes in the United States, in <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_California" title="Northern California">northern California</a> along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cascade_Mountains" title="Cascade Mountains">Cascade Mountains</a>. There are several active volcanoes located in the islands of Hawaii, including Kilauea in ongoing eruption since 1983, but they do not typically adversely affect the inhabitants of the islands. There has not been a major life-threatening eruption on the Hawaiian islands since the 17th century. Volcanic eruptions can occasionally be devastating, such as in the <a href="/wiki/1980_eruption_of_Mount_St._Helens" title="1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens">1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens</a> in Washington.</p>
<p>The Ring of Fire makes <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> particularly vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a>. Earthquakes can cause extensive damage, such as the <a href="/wiki/1906_San_Francisco_earthquake" title="1906 San Francisco earthquake">1906 San Francisco earthquake</a> or the 1964 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Good_Friday_earthquake" title="Good Friday earthquake">Good Friday earthquake</a> near <a href="/wiki/Anchorage,_Alaska" title="Anchorage, Alaska">Anchorage, Alaska</a>. California is well known for seismic activity, and requires large structures to be <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_resistant_structures" title="Earthquake resistant structures">earthquake resistant</a> to minimize loss of life and property.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Outside of devastating earthquakes, California experiences minor earthquakes on a regular basis.</p>
<p>There have been about 100 significant earthquakes annually from 2010 to 2012. Past averages were 21 a year. This is believed to be due to the deep disposal of wastewater from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fracking" title="Fracking">fracking</a>. None has exceeded a magnitude of 5.6, and no one has been killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_natural_disasters">Other natural disasters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other natural disasters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Other natural disasters include: <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunamis</a> around Pacific Basin, mud slides in California, and forest fires in the western half of the contiguous U.S. Although drought is relatively rare, it has occasionally caused major economic and social disruption, such as during the <a href="/wiki/Dust_Bowl" title="Dust Bowl">Dust Bowl</a> (1931–1942), which resulted in widespread crop failures and duststorms, beginning in the southern Great Plains and reaching to the Atlantic Ocean.</p>
