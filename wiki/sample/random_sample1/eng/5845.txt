<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Political divisions of the United States</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Political divisions of the United States</b> or <b>administrative divisions of the United States</b> are the various governing entities that together form the United States. The first subdivision of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, meaning the union of states, is the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">state</a>. The admission of sovereign states into the United States, i.e., the union of states, beyond the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Original_thirteen_states" title="Original thirteen states">original thirteen states</a>, is authorized under <a href="/wiki/Article_Four_of_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Article Four of the United States Constitution">Article IV, Section 3, of the Constitution for the United States of America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-ART4SEC3_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-USC-ART4SEC3-1">[1]</a></sup> Most of the states admitted to the union after the original 13 have been formed within <a href="/wiki/Organized_incorporated_territories_of_the_United_States" title="Organized incorporated territories of the United States">territories of the United States</a> (that is, land under the sovereignty of the federal government but not part of any state) that were organized by an act or resolution of the United States Congress, subject to the Congress' plenary powers under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Territorial_clause" title="Territorial clause">territorial clause</a> of Article IV, sec. 3, of the Constitution for the United States of America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-ART4SEC3_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-USC-ART4SEC3-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>State governments within the United States may enact their own laws and prosecute crimes pursuant thereto. Member states of the United States are then typically subdivided into <a href="/wiki/County_(United_States)" title="County (United States)">counties</a>. <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> uses the term <a href="/wiki/List_of_parishes_in_Louisiana" title="List of parishes in Louisiana">parish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> uses the term <a href="/wiki/List_of_boroughs_and_census_areas_in_Alaska" title="List of boroughs and census areas in Alaska">borough</a> for what the Census terms <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/County-equivalent" title="County-equivalent">county-equivalents</a> in those states.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Territories_of_the_United_States" title="Territories of the United States">Territories</a>, except the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Minor_Outlying_Islands" title="United States Minor Outlying Islands">Minor Outlying Islands</a>, are subdivided into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipalities" title="Municipalities">municipalities</a>. <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a> uses the term <a href="/wiki/List_of_populated_places_in_Guam#List_of_villages" title="List of populated places in Guam">Village</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="United States Virgin Islands">U.S. Virgin Islands</a> uses the term <a href="/wiki/Districts_and_sub-districts_of_the_United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="Districts and sub-districts of the United States Virgin Islands">Districts</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Samoa" title="American Samoa">American Samoa</a> uses the terms <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_American_Samoa" title="Administrative divisions of American Samoa">district</a> and Unorganized atolls.</p>
<p>While not explicitly part of the U.S.'s political divisional makeup, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Associated_State" title="Associated State">Associated States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Federated_States_of_Micronesia" title="Federated States of Micronesia">Federated States of Micronesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a> are divided into states (<a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_the_Federated_States_of_Micronesia" title="Administrative divisions of the Federated States of Micronesia">States of Micronesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/States_of_Palau" title="States of Palau">States of Palau</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">Marshall Islands</a> is divided into <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_of_the_Marshall_Islands" title="List of islands of the Marshall Islands">atolls and islands</a>. While Micronesia is a <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">federation</a>, Palau and the Marshall Islands are both <a href="/wiki/Unitary_state" title="Unitary state">unitary states</a>.</p>
<p>Each <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Reservation" title="Indian Reservation">Indian Reservation</a> is subdivided in various ways. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Navajo_Nation" title="Navajo Nation">Navajo Nation</a> is subdivided into agencies and <a href="/wiki/Chapter_house_(Navajo_Nation)" title="Chapter house (Navajo Nation)">Chapter houses</a>. While the <a href="/wiki/Blackfeet_Indian_Reservation" title="Blackfeet Indian Reservation">Blackfeet Nation</a> is subdivided into <a href="/wiki/Blackfeet_Indian_Reservation" title="Blackfeet Indian Reservation">Communities</a>.</p>
<p>Counties and county equivalents may be further subdivided into <a href="/wiki/Township_(United_States)" title="Township (United States)">townships</a>. <a href="/wiki/New_England_town" title="New England town">Towns</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> are treated as equivalents to townships by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>. Towns or townships are used as subdivisions of a county in 20 states, mostly in the Northeast and Midwest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-census_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-census-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Population centers may be organized into <a href="/wiki/Municipal_corporation" title="Municipal corporation">incorporated</a> <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">cities</a>, <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">towns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Village_(United_States)" title="Village (United States)">villages</a>, and other types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipalities" title="Municipalities">municipalities</a>. Municipalities are typically subordinate to a county government, with some exceptions. Certain cities, for example, have consolidated with their county government as <a href="/wiki/Consolidated_city-county" title="Consolidated city-county">consolidated city-counties</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, cities are completely <a href="/wiki/Independent_city_(United_States)" title="Independent city (United States)">independent</a> from the county in which they would otherwise be a part. In some states, particularly in <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_England_town" title="New England town">towns</a> form the primary unit of local government below the state level, in some cases eliminating the need for county government entirely.</p>
<p>Other divisions include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_D.C." title="Washington D.C.">federal district</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insular_area" title="Insular area">insular areas</a> administered by the Federal government, and <a href="/wiki/Indian_reservation" title="Indian reservation">American Indian reservations</a>. The Federal government also maintains exclusive jurisdiction over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_bases" title="Military bases">military installations</a> and American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embassy" title="Embassy">embassies</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consulate" title="Consulate">consulates</a> located in foreign countries. Other special purpose divisions exist separately from those for general governance, examples of which include <a href="/wiki/Conservation_district" title="Conservation district">conservation districts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Congressional_district" title="Congressional district">Congressional districts</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="States_and_their_subdivisions">States and their subdivisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_divisions_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: States and their subdivisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="States">States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_divisions_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_U.S._states" title="List of U.S. states">List of U.S. states</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_United_States" title="Local government in the United States">Local government in the United States</a></div>
<p>The primary political unit of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> is the state. According to numerous decisions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a>, the 50 individual states and the United States as a whole are each sovereign jurisdictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The 13 original states <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">Thirteen Colonies</a> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">declared independence</a> from the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> in 1776. In 1777, they established a collective government under the <a href="/wiki/Articles_of_Confederation" title="Articles of Confederation">Articles of Confederation</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitution_for_the_United_States_of_America" title="Constitution for the United States of America">Constitution for the United States of America</a> superseded the Articles in 1789 in the sense that it established and superimposed a consolidated political government over the confederated union of geographical states. The Constitution for the United States of America establishes the political government for the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">Federal government of the United States</a>, which includes, among other things, judicial review,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-ART3_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-USC-ART3-4">[4]</a></sup> power to regulate citizenship,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-ART1SEC8_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-USC-ART1SEC8-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-AMD14_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-USC-AMD14-6">[6]</a></sup> power to coin money,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-ART1SEC8_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-USC-ART1SEC8-5">[5]</a></sup> power to regulate commerce with foreign nations, and among the several states,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-ART1SEC8_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-USC-ART1SEC8-5">[5]</a></sup> conduct <a href="/wiki/Foreign_policy" title="Foreign policy">foreign policy</a>, and to make all laws which shall be necessary and proper for carrying into execution the foregoing powers, and all other powers vested by this Constitution in the government of the United States, or in any department or officer thereof.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-ART1SEC8_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-USC-ART1SEC8-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The idea of "dual sovereignty" or "separate sovereigns" is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Tenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Tenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Tenth Amendment</a> to the Constitution, which states that "the powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution, nor prohibited by it to the States, are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-AMD10_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-USC-AMD10-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Texas_v._White" title="Texas v. White">Texas v. White</a></i> held that political states do not have the right to secede, though it did allow some possibility of the divisibility "through revolution, or through consent of the States."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Four states (<a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>) call themselves "<a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_(U.S._state)" title="Commonwealth (U.S. state)">commonwealths</a>." The word <i>commonwealth</i> in this context refers to the common "wealth", or welfare, of the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The term has no legal impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>While state governments within the United States may enact their own laws and prosecute crimes pursuant thereto,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-AMD10_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-USC-AMD10-7">[7]</a></sup> they are not sovereign in the Westphalian sense in <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a> which says that each State has sovereignty over its territory and domestic affairs, to the exclusion of all external powers, on the principle of non-interference in another State's domestic affairs, and that each State (no matter how large or small) is equal in international law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krasner_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Krasner-12">[12]</a></sup> Additionally, the member states of the United States do not possess international legal sovereignty, meaning that they are not recognized by other sovereign States such as, for example, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> or the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krasner_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Krasner-12">[12]</a></sup> nor do they possess full interdependence sovereignty, meaning that they cannot control movement of persons across state borders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krasner_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Krasner-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>The 50 states can be <a href="/wiki/List_of_regions_of_the_United_States" title="List of regions of the United States">divided into regions in many different ways</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_United_States" title="Continental United States">continental United States</a> typically refers to the main block of 48 states and their off-shore islands with the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> to the east, <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> to the west, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> to the north, and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> to the south. Since this grouping contains 48 states, it is also commonly referred to as "the Lower 48". The designation "coterminous" or "contiguous" United States is also used for this grouping of 48 states, arguably more accurately, since <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> is also located on the <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> continent, and therefore "continental United States" should really refer to the Lower 48, plus Alaska.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclave" title="Exclave">exclave</a> of the United States, physically connected only to <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> is located on an <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> in the central <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Counties">Counties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_divisions_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Counties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/County_(United_States)" title="County (United States)">County (United States)</a></div>
<p>The states are divided into smaller administrative regions, called <a href="/wiki/County_(United_States)" title="County (United States)">counties</a> in all but two states. The exceptions are Alaska where main subdivisions is the <a href="/wiki/Borough_(United_States)" title="Borough (United States)">boroughs</a> (parts of the state are not included in any borough, called the <a href="/wiki/Unorganized_Borough,_Alaska" title="Unorganized Borough, Alaska">Unorganized Borough</a>, is divided into "census areas"), and Louisiana (which is divided into county-equivalents that are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parish_(subnational_entity)" title="Parish (subnational entity)">parishes</a>). Counties have varying degrees of political and legal significance. In some states, mainly in <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, they are primarily used as judicial districts. In other states, counties have broad powers in housing, education, transportation and recreation.</p>
<p>Counties may contain a number of <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">cities</a>, towns, villages, or <a href="/wiki/Hamlet_(place)" title="Hamlet (place)">hamlets</a>, or sometimes just a part of a city. Some cities are consolidated with, and coterminous with, their counties, including <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">Honolulu</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nashville" title="Nashville">Nashville</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">Denver</a>—that is to say, these counties consist in their entirety of a single municipality the government of which also operates as the county government. Some counties, such as <a href="/wiki/Arlington_County,_Virginia" title="Arlington County, Virginia">Arlington County, Virginia</a>, do not have any additional subdivisions. Some states contain <a href="/wiki/Independent_city_(United_States)" title="Independent city (United States)">independent cities</a> that are not part of any county. <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> is coterminous with five counties.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Towns_and_townships">Towns and townships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_divisions_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Towns and townships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Township_(United_States)" title="Township (United States)">Township (United States)</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Incorporated_town#United_States" title="Incorporated town">Incorporated town § United States</a></div>
<p>Towns and townships are subdivisions of counties in many states. States not having such subdivisions may divide counties into other <a href="/wiki/Minor_civil_division" title="Minor civil division">minor civil divisions</a>. The terms "township" and "town" are closely related (in many historical documents the terms are used interchangeably). However, the powers granted to towns or townships vary considerably from state to state. In <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, towns are a principal form of local government, providing many of the functions of counties in other states. In <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, by contrast, the pertinent statutes of the Government Code clarify that "town" is simply another word for "city", especially a general law city as distinct from a <a href="/wiki/Charter_city" title="Charter city">charter city</a>. Also, in some states, large areas have no general-purpose local government below the county level.</p>
<p>Some townships or other minor civil divisions have governments and political power; others are simply geographic designations. Townships in many states are generally the product of the <a href="/wiki/Public_Land_Survey_System" title="Public Land Survey System">Public Land Survey System</a>. For more information, see <a href="/wiki/Survey_township" title="Survey township">survey township</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civil_township" title="Civil township">civil township</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Municipalities">Municipalities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_divisions_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Municipalities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/City#United_States" title="City">City § United States</a></div>
<p>There are approximately <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_the_United_States" title="List of cities in the United States">30,000 incorporated cities</a> in the United States, with varying degrees of self-rule.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_subdivisions">Other subdivisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_divisions_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other subdivisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>, the state universities are constitutionally autonomous jurisdictions, possessed of a special status somewhat equivalent to that of metropolitan municipality. That is, as bodies corporate, they operate as though they were municipalities, but they have autonomy from legislative and executive control. Each university has a board which is the sole legislative body for the campuses they control. These campuses are independent of all state laws, and under the sole control of the boards. The boards are responsible for all public services, e.g. policing, and fire protection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> They often contract with the city they are located in for these services, but all have <a href="/wiki/List_of_law_enforcement_agencies_in_Michigan#College_and_university_agencies" title="List of law enforcement agencies in Michigan">their own police</a> departments.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Federal_oversight_of_United_States_territory">Federal oversight of United States territory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_divisions_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Federal oversight of United States territory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Congress_of_the_United_States">Congress of the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_divisions_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Congress of the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article IV, Section 3 of the Constitution for the United States of America defines the extent of the authority that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congress_of_the_United_States" title="Congress of the United States">U.S. Congress</a> exercises over the territory of the United States:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>New States may be admitted by the Congress into this Union; but no new State shall be formed or erected within the Jurisdiction of any other State; nor any State be formed by the Junction of two or more States, or Parts of States, without the Consent of the Legislatures of the States concerned as well as of the Congress.</i></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd><i>The Congress shall have Power to dispose of and make all needful Rules and Regulations respecting the Territory or other Property belonging to the United States; and nothing in this Constitution shall be so construed as to Prejudice any Claims of the United States, or of any particular State.</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>The power of Congress over territorial divisions that are not part of one of the states is exclusive and universal. Once a <a href="/wiki/Territories_of_the_United_States" title="Territories of the United States">territory</a> becomes a state of the Union, the state must consent to any changes pertaining to the jurisdiction of that state. The only potential violation of this occurred when the legislature of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> declared the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">secession of Virginia</a> from the United States at the start of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> and a newly formed alternative Virginia legislature, recognized by the federal government, consented to have <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a> secede from Virginia.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_Department_of_the_Interior">United States Department of the Interior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_divisions_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: United States Department of the Interior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On March 3, 1849, the last day of the 30th Congress, a bill was passed to create the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_the_Interior" title="U.S. Department of the Interior">U.S. Department of the Interior</a> to take charge of the internal affairs of <i>United States territory</i>. The Interior Department has a wide range of responsibilities (which include the regulation of territorial governments, the basic responsibilities for public lands, and other various duties).</p>
<p>In contrast to similarly named Departments in other countries, the United States Department of the Interior is not responsible for local government or for civil administration except in the cases of Indian reservations, through the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Indian_Affairs" title="Bureau of Indian Affairs">Bureau of Indian Affairs</a> (BIA), and island dependencies, through the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Insular_Affairs" title="Office of Insular Affairs">Office of Insular Affairs</a> (OIA).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_federal_oversight">Other federal oversight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_divisions_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other federal oversight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The federal government also exercises exclusive jurisdiction over overseas <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_bases" title="Military bases">military installations</a> and American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embassy" title="Embassy">embassies</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consulate" title="Consulate">consulates</a> located in foreign countries. It exercises <a href="/wiki/Concurrent_jurisdiction" title="Concurrent jurisdiction">concurrent jurisdiction</a> to varying degrees with the states in many domestic <a href="/wiki/Federal_enclave" title="Federal enclave">federal enclaves</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jurisdictions_not_administered_by_the_states">Jurisdictions not administered by the states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_divisions_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Jurisdictions not administered by the states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="District_of_Columbia">District of Columbia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_divisions_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: District of Columbia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" title="District of Columbia">District of Columbia</a></div>
<p>A separate federal district, the District of Columbia (DC), which is under the direct authority of Congress, was formed from land ceded to the Federal Government by the states of <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>; however, the territory ceded by Virginia was returned to that state in 1846. The District does not form part of any state and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> exercises "exclusive jurisdiction in all cases whatsoever", over the city; however, the <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia_Home_Rule_Act" title="District of Columbia Home Rule Act">District of Columbia Home Rule Act</a> provides for limited <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia_home_rule" title="District of Columbia home rule">home rule</a>, including an elected mayor and city council. Residents of the District can vote in presidential elections, as the <a href="/wiki/Twenty-third_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Twenty-third Amendment to the United States Constitution">Twenty-third Amendment to the United States Constitution</a> grants the District three electors in the <a href="/wiki/Electoral_College_(United_States)" title="Electoral College (United States)">Electoral College</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Insular_areas">Insular areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_divisions_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Insular areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insular_Area" title="Insular Area">Insular Area</a> and <a href="/wiki/Territories_of_the_United_States" title="Territories of the United States">Territories of the United States</a></div>
<p>The insular areas of the United States are those jurisdictions that are neither a part of one of the 50 states nor the federal district.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doi_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-doi-14">[14]</a></sup> Unlike within the States, sovereignty over insular areas rests not with the local people, but in Congress. In most cases, however, Congress has granted considerable self-rule through an Organic Act, which functions as a local constitution. Insular areas are administered by the Federal Government through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_the_Interior" title="Department of the Interior">Department of the Interior's</a> <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Insular_Affairs" title="Office of Insular Affairs">Office of Insular Affairs</a>.</p>
<p>The insular areas include a number of territories under the sovereignty of the United States and three sovereign nations in free association with the United States. Territories incorporated within the provisions of the Constitution for the United States of America are designated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incorporated_territories" title="Incorporated territories">incorporated territories</a>. Territories not so incorporated are designated "unincorporated". Territories may also be <i>organized</i>, if granted by an Organic Act of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Congress" title="U.S. Congress">Congress</a> or <i>unorganized</i> (without direct authorization of self-government by such an act).</p>
<p>The Northwest Ordinance grants territories the right to send a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delegate_(United_States_Congress)" title="Delegate (United States Congress)">non-voting delegate</a> to the U.S. Congress. Since the organization of the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territory" title="Northwest Territory">Northwest Territory</a> in 1789, all areas not admitted to the United States as States were under the direct control of Congress as <a href="/wiki/Organized_incorporated_territories_of_the_United_States" title="Organized incorporated territories of the United States">organized incorporated territories</a>, with some political <a href="/wiki/Autonomy" title="Autonomy">autonomy</a> at the local level. These organized incorporated territories subsequently became states. Thirty-one of the current 50 states were <a href="/wiki/Organized_incorporated_territories_of_the_United_States" title="Organized incorporated territories of the United States">organized incorporated territories</a> before their admission to the Union. Since the admission of <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> to the Union in 1959, there has been a single incorporated territory, the uninhabited <a href="/wiki/Palmyra_Atoll" title="Palmyra Atoll">Palmyra Atoll</a> (formerly part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hawaii_Territory" title="Hawaii Territory">Hawaii Territory</a>, but excluded from the act of admission).</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Philippines" title="The Philippines">the Philippines</a> are two additional former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unincorporated_territories" title="Unincorporated territories">unincorporated territories</a> that are now independent countries. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> has held the <a href="/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_Naval_Base" title="Guantanamo Bay Naval Base">Guantanamo Bay Naval Base</a> in a portion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guant%C3%A1namo_Bay,_Cuba" title="Guantánamo Bay, Cuba">Guantánamo Bay, Cuba</a>, since 1898. The United States government possesses a lease to this land, which only mutual agreement or United States abandonment of the area can terminate. The present <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Cuba" title="Politics of Cuba">Cuban government</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ra%C3%BAl_Castro" title="Raúl Castro">Raúl Castro</a> disputes this arrangement, claiming Cuba was not truly <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereign</a> at the time of the signing. The United States argues this point is irrelevant because Cuba apparently ratified the lease post-revolution, and with full sovereignty, when it cashed one rent check in accordance with the disputed treaty.</p>
<p>The United States government is part of several international disputes over the disposition of certain maritime and insular sovereignties, some of which would be considered territories. <a href="/wiki/Bajo_Nuevo_Bank" title="Bajo Nuevo Bank">Bajo Nuevo Bank</a> and <a href="/wiki/Serranilla_Bank" title="Serranilla Bank">Serranilla Bank</a> are two such disputed claims. See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_territorial_disputes_of_the_United_States" title="International territorial disputes of the United States">International territorial disputes of the United States</a> for more information.</p>
<p>The United States also holds several other territories, districts, and possessions, notably the <a href="/wiki/Federal_district" title="Federal district">federal district</a> of the District of Columbia, and several overseas <a href="/wiki/Insular_area" title="Insular area">insular areas</a>, the most significant of which are <a href="/wiki/American_Samoa" title="American Samoa">American Samoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Mariana_Islands" title="Northern Mariana Islands">Northern Mariana Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="United States Virgin Islands">United States Virgin Islands</a>. Islands gained by the United States in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">war against Spain</a> at the turn of the 20th century under the control of the federal government is considered part of the "United States" for purposes of law;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> on the other hand, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a> declared in a series of opinions known as the <a href="/wiki/Insular_Cases" title="Insular Cases">Insular Cases</a> that the Constitution extended <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ex_proprio_vigore&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ex proprio vigore (page does not exist)">ex proprio vigore</a></i> to the territories. However, the Court in these cases also established the doctrine of territorial incorporation. Under the same, the Constitution only applied fully in incorporated territories such as <a href="/wiki/Territory_of_Alaska" title="Territory of Alaska">Alaska</a> and <a href="/wiki/Territory_of_Hawaii" title="Territory of Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, whereas it only applied partially in the new unincorporated territories of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-puertoricoadvancement.org_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-puertoricoadvancement.org-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Marianas_Islands" title="Northern Marianas Islands">Northern Marianas Islands</a> is a territory which are <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_(U.S._insular_area)" title="Commonwealth (U.S. insular area)">commonwealths</a> associated with the United States. They might some day advance to statehood, or they might become independent—as did the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_the_Philippines" title="Commonwealth of the Philippines">Philippines</a> in 1946, after it was a commonwealth of the United States for many years. A territory—whether "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organized_territory" title="Organized territory">organized</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/Unorganized_territory" title="Unorganized territory">unorganized</a>"—has significantly fewer rights in the grand scheme of things than a commonwealth (let alone a state), but it ranks at least a notch above "<a href="/wiki/Territories_of_the_United_States" title="Territories of the United States">possessions</a>" such as <a href="/wiki/Wake_Island" title="Wake Island">Wake Island</a>, which has no permanent population and thus does not require even a simple territorial government.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Indian_reservations">American Indian reservations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_divisions_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: American Indian reservations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Indian_reservation" title="Indian reservation">Indian reservation</a></div>
<p>American <a href="/wiki/Indian_reservation" title="Indian reservation">Indian reservations</a> are areas of <a href="/wiki/Land_(economics)" title="Land (economics)">land</a> managed by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_Americans_of_the_United_States" title="Native Americans of the United States">American Indian</a> <a href="/wiki/Tribe" title="Tribe">tribe</a> under the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior" title="United States Department of the Interior">United States Department of the Interior's</a> <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Indian_Affairs" title="Bureau of Indian Affairs">Bureau of Indian Affairs</a>. There are about 310 <a href="/wiki/List_of_Indian_reservations_in_the_United_States" title="List of Indian reservations in the United States">Indian reservations in the United States</a>. Tribes possess limited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tribal_sovereignty" title="Tribal sovereignty">tribal sovereignty</a> over the land in their reservation. As a result, laws on tribal lands may vary from the surrounding area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The tribal council, not the county or state government, generally has jurisdiction over reservations. Different reservations have different systems of government, which may or may not replicate the forms of government found outside the reservation. Most Indian reservations were established by the federal government; a limited number, mainly in the East, owe their origin to state recognition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Residents of a reservation may vote as residents of a state and are required to pay federal taxes. The special status of reservations has created both opportunities (such as gambling in states that normally disallow it) and challenges (such as the unwillingness of some companies to do business in an area where they are not certain what laws will apply to them).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_defined_areas">Other defined areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_divisions_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Other defined areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to general-purpose government entities legislating at the state, county, and city level, special-purpose areas may exist as well. <a href="/wiki/Conservation_district" title="Conservation district">Conservation districts</a> are one such type of special purpose area, created for the purpose of conserving land, natural scenery, flora, and fauna.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Congressional_district" title="Congressional district">Congressional districts</a> are another example, formed for the purpose of electing members to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a>.</p>
<p>Additionally, U.S. courts have ruled that there are smaller areas which are to be considered as fulfilling government functions, and should therefore be bound by the same restrictions placed on "traditional" (US-aligned) government bodies. These include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeowners_association" title="Homeowners association">homeowners associations</a> (determined in <i><a href="/wiki/Shelley_v._Kraemer" title="Shelley v. Kraemer">Shelley v. Kraemer</a></i>, <i>Loren v. Sasser</i>, <i>Committee for a Better Twin Rivers v. Twin Rivers Homeowners' Association</i>), and company-owned towns (both for employees and for consumers, decided in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">USSC</a> case <i><a href="/wiki/Marsh_v._Alabama" title="Marsh v. Alabama">Marsh v. Alabama</a></i> in 1946). Many homeowners' and neighborhood associations are considered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-profit_organization" title="Non-profit organization">non-profit organizations</a>, but have the ability to raise <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxes" title="Taxes">taxes</a> or fees, fine members for infractions against association-rules, and initiate lawsuits. The question of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_rights" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a> in such communities has not yet been conclusively determined, and varies from state to state.</p>
