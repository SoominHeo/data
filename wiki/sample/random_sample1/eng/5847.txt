<p>The <b>National Cattlemen's Beef Association</b> (NCBA) is a <a href="/wiki/Trade_association" title="Trade association">trade association</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lobbying" title="Lobbying">lobbying group</a> for beef producers in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The NCBA operates the Cattle Industry Annual Convention &amp; Trade Show and Cattle Industry Summer Conference.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advertising_campaign">Advertising campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Cattlemen%27s_Beef_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Advertising campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The NCBA is the group responsible for the ad campaign run in the U.S. using the slogan <b>"<a href="/wiki/Beef._It%27s_What%27s_For_Dinner" title="Beef. It's What's For Dinner">Beef. It's What's For Dinner</a>"</b> Music from the ballet <i><a href="/wiki/Rodeo_(ballet)" title="Rodeo (ballet)">Rodeo</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Copland" title="Aaron Copland">Aaron Copland</a> is used in the radio and television commercials. On January 21, 2008, <a href="/wiki/Matthew_McConaughey" title="Matthew McConaughey">Matthew McConaughey</a> became the current spokesman of the organization, having taken over from <a href="/wiki/Sam_Elliott" title="Sam Elliott">Sam Elliott</a> and the late <a href="/wiki/Jim_Davis_(actor)" title="Jim Davis (actor)">Jim Davis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Mitchum" title="Robert Mitchum">Robert Mitchum</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation">Legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Cattlemen%27s_Beef_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The NCBA supported the <a href="/wiki/Farmers_Undertake_Environmental_Land_Stewardship_Act" title="Farmers Undertake Environmental Land Stewardship Act">Farmers Undertake Environmental Land Stewardship Act</a>, a bill that would require the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) to modify the Spill Prevention, Control, and Countermeasure (SPCC) rule, which regulates oil discharges into navigable waters and adjoining shorelines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbo311_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbo311-2">[2]</a></sup> The rule requires certain farmers to develop an <a href="/wiki/Oil_spill" title="Oil spill">oil spill</a> prevention plan that is certified by a professional <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a> and may require them to make <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a> changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbo311_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-cbo311-2">[2]</a></sup> According to supporters, this bill would "ease the burden placed on farmers and ranchers" by making it easier for smaller farms to self-certify and raising the level of storage capacity under which farms are exempted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HighPlainsJ1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-HighPlainsJ1-3">[3]</a></sup> The NCBA's president said that they were "pleased" that the "bill will keep many of our producers from having to undertake excess costs as a result of the EPA's overregulation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HighPlainsJ1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-HighPlainsJ1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The NCBA supported the <a href="/wiki/Water_Rights_Protection_Act" title="Water Rights Protection Act">Water Rights Protection Act</a>, a bill that would prevent federal agencies from requiring certain entities to relinquish their water rights to the United States in order to use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_lands" title="Public lands">public lands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3189cbo_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-3189cbo-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCBAsupport_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCBAsupport-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_background">Financial background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Cattlemen%27s_Beef_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Financial background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The National Cattlemen's Beef Association is funded partially by membership dues and partially through the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Beef_Checkoff&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Beef Checkoff (page does not exist)">Beef Checkoff</a> which imposes a mandatory assessment each time a head of cattle is sold<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This tax, which was authorized by Congress in the <a href="/wiki/Food_Security_Act_of_1985" title="Food Security Act of 1985">Beef Promotion and Research Act of 1985</a>, brings in around $85 million a year, of which the NCBA receives roughly $55 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The constitutionality of the mandatory assessment was called into question with the hearing of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Supreme_Court" title="U.S. Supreme Court">Supreme Court of the United States</a> case <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Johanns_v._Livestock_Marketing_Assn.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Johanns v. Livestock Marketing Assn. (page does not exist)">Johanns v. Livestock Marketing Assn.</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> in which the justices ruled in favor of the tax in a 6–3 decision.</p>
