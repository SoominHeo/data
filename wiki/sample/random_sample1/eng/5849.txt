<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, the <b>labyrinth</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a>: λαβύρινθος <i>labyrinthos</i>) was an elaborate structure designed and built by the legendary artificer <a href="/wiki/Daedalus" title="Daedalus">Daedalus</a> for King <a href="/wiki/Minos" title="Minos">Minos</a> of <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> at <a href="/wiki/Knossos" title="Knossos">Knossos</a>. Its function was to hold the <a href="/wiki/Minotaur" title="Minotaur">Minotaur</a> eventually killed by the <a href="/wiki/Hero" title="Hero">hero</a> <a href="/wiki/Theseus" title="Theseus">Theseus</a>. Daedalus had so cunningly made the Labyrinth that he could barely escape it after he built it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although early Cretan coins occasionally exhibit branching (multicursal) patterns,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the single-path (unicursal) seven-course "Classical" design without branching or dead ends became associated with the Labyrinth on coins as early as 430 BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and similar non-branching patterns became widely used as visual representations of the Labyrinth – even though both logic and literary descriptions make it clear that the Minotaur was trapped in a complex branching maze.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Even as the designs became more elaborate, visual depictions of the mythological Labyrinth from Roman times until the Renaissance are almost invariably unicursal. Branching mazes were reintroduced only when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garden_maze" title="Garden maze">garden mazes</a> became popular during the Renaissance.</p>
<p>In English, the term <i>labyrinth</i> is generally synonymous with <i><a href="/wiki/Maze" title="Maze">maze</a></i>. As a result of the long history of unicursal representation of the mythological Labyrinth, however, many contemporary scholars and enthusiasts observe a distinction between the two. In this specialized usage <i>maze</i> refers to a complex branching multicursal puzzle with choices of path and direction, while a unicursal <i>labyrinth</i> has only a single path to the center. A labyrinth in this sense has an unambiguous route to the center and back and is not difficult to navigate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unicursal labyrinths appeared as designs on <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basketry" title="Basketry">basketry</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Body_art" title="Body art">body art</a>, and in etchings on walls of caves or churches. The Romans created many primarily decorative unicursal designs on walls and floors in tile or mosaic. Many labyrinths set in floors or on the ground are large enough that the path can be walked. Unicursal patterns have been used historically both in group ritual and for private <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">meditation</a>, and are increasingly found for therapeutic use in hospitals and hospices.</p>

<p><i>Labyrinth</i> is a word of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Greek" title="Pre-Greek">Pre-Greek</a> (<a href="/wiki/Minoan_civilization" title="Minoan civilization">Minoan</a>) origin, which the Greeks associated with the palace of <a href="/wiki/Knossos" title="Knossos">Knossos</a> in <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>. It is also widely associated with the <a href="/wiki/Lydian_language" title="Lydian language">Lydian</a> word <i><a href="/wiki/Labrys" title="Labrys">labrys</a></i> ("double-edged axe"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F.Schachermeyer_pp._161.2C_237_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-F.Schachermeyer_pp._161.2C_237-10">[10]</a></sup> and since the double axe motif appears in the ruins at Knossos, it has been suggested that the original labyrinth was the royal Minoan palace in Crete. This designation may not have been limited to Knossos, because the same symbols were discovered in other palaces in <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Labrys</i> was a cult-word that was probably introduced from <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, where such symbols have been found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%87atal_H%C3%B6y%C3%BCk" title="Çatal Höyük">Çatal Höyük</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Labraunda" title="Labraunda">Labraunda</a> of <a href="/wiki/Caria" title="Caria">Caria</a> the double-axe accompanies the storm-god <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zeus_Labraundos" title="Zeus Labraundos">Zeus Labraundos</a> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ζεὺς Λαβρανδεύς</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It also accompanies the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurrian" title="Hurrian">Hurrian</a> god of sky and storm <a href="/wiki/Teshub" title="Teshub">Teshub</a> (his <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luwian_language" title="Luwian language">Luwian</a> name was <i>Tarhun</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Labrys</i>, however, comes from Lydian, not Minoan, and the association of <i>labyrinth</i> with <i>labrys</i> remains speculative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Linear_B" title="Linear B">Linear B</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mycenaean_language" title="Mycenaean language">Mycenaean</a>) inscription <span lang="gmy" xml:lang="gmy">𐀅𐁆𐀪𐀵𐀍𐄀𐀡𐀴𐀛𐀊</span> on tablet <a href="/wiki/Knossos" title="Knossos">ΚΝ</a> Gg 702 is interpreted as <i>da-pu<sub>2</sub>-ri-to-jo, po-ti-ni-ja</i> (<i>labyrinthoio <a href="/wiki/Potnia" title="Potnia">potnia</a></i>, "Mistress of the labyrinth).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F.Schachermeyer_pp._161.2C_237_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-F.Schachermeyer_pp._161.2C_237-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The word <i>daburinthos</i> (<i>labyrinthos</i>) may show the same equivocation between initial <i>d-</i> and <i>l-</i> as is found in the variation of the early Hittite royal name <a href="/wiki/Labarna_I" title="Labarna I"><i>Tabarna</i> / <i>Labarna</i></a> (where written <i>t-</i> may represent phonetic <i>d-</i>). The original Minoan word, which is attested in <a href="/wiki/Linear_A" title="Linear A">Linear A</a> tablets, appears to refer to labyrinthine underground grottoes, such as seen at <a href="/wiki/Gortyn" title="Gortyn">Gortyn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarullo_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sarullo-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>'s four examples of labyrinths are all complex underground structures,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kern_III_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kern_III-19">[19]</a></sup> and this appears to have been the standard Classical understanding of the word.</p>
<p>By the 4th century BC, the Greeks also associated the labyrinth with the familiar "Greek key" patterns of endlessly running <a href="/wiki/Meander_(art)" title="Meander (art)">meanders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kern_II_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kern_II-20">[20]</a></sup> Coins from Knossos were struck with the labyrinth symbol in the 5th through 3rd centuries BC. The predominant labyrinth form during this period is the simple seven-circuit style known as the <i>classical</i> labyrinth, and over time the term <i>labyrinth</i> came to be applied to any unicursal maze, whether of a particular circular shape (illustration) or rendered as square.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cretan_labyrinth">Cretan labyrinth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labyrinth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cretan labyrinth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> site at <a href="/wiki/Knossos" title="Knossos">Knossos</a> was excavated by explorer <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Evans" title="Arthur Evans">Arthur Evans</a>, the complexity of the architecture prompted him to suggest that the palace had been the original of the Minotaur Labyrinth. Evans found various bull motifs, including an image of <a href="/wiki/Bull-leaping" title="Bull-leaping">a man leaping over the horns of a bull</a>, as well as depictions of a <a href="/wiki/Labrys" title="Labrys">labrys</a> carved into the walls. On the strength of a passage in the <i>Iliad</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dancingground_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-dancingground-21">[21]</a></sup> it has been suggested that the palace was the site of a dancing-ground made for <a href="/wiki/Ariadne" title="Ariadne">Ariadne</a> by the craftsman <a href="/wiki/Daedalus" title="Daedalus">Daedalus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> where young men and women, of the age of those sent to Crete as prey for the Minotaur, would dance together. By extension, in popular legend the palace is associated with the myth of the Minotaur.</p>
<p>In the 2000s, archaeologists explored other potential sites of the labyrinth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skotinogortyn_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-skotinogortyn-24">[24]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_University" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a> geographer Nicholas Howarth believes that 'Evans's hypothesis that the palace of Knossos is also the Labyrinth must be treated sceptically.'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skotinogortyn_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-skotinogortyn-24">[24]</a></sup> Howarth and his team conducted a search of an underground complex known as the <a href="/wiki/Skotino_cave" title="Skotino cave">Skotino cave</a> but concluded that it was formed naturally. Another contender is a series of underground tunnels at <a href="/wiki/Gortyn" title="Gortyn">Gortyn</a>, accessed by a narrow crack but expanding into interlinking caverns. Unlike the Skotino cave, these caverns have smooth walls and columns, and appear to have been at least partially man-made. This site corresponds to an unusual labyrinth symbol on a 16th-century map of Crete contained in a book of maps in the library of <a href="/wiki/Christ_Church,_Oxford" title="Christ Church, Oxford">Christ Church, Oxford</a>. A map of the caves themselves was produced by the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> in 1821. The site was also used by German soldiers to store ammunition during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>. Howarth's investigation was shown on a documentary<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-natgeodocumentary_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-natgeodocumentary-25">[25]</a></sup> produced for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Channel" title="National Geographic Channel">National Geographic Channel</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Herodotus.27_Egyptian_labyrinth">Herodotus' Egyptian labyrinth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labyrinth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Herodotus' Egyptian labyrinth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Even more generally, <i>labyrinth</i> might be applied to any extremely complicated maze-like structure. <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>, in Book II of his <i><a href="/wiki/Histories_(Herodotus)#Book_II_.28Euterpe.29" title="Histories (Herodotus)">Histories</a></i>, describes as a "labyrinth" a building complex in Egypt, "near the place called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crocodilopolis" title="Crocodilopolis">City of Crocodiles</a>", that he considered to surpass the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_pyramids" title="Egyptian pyramids">pyramids</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It has twelve covered courts — six in a row facing north, six south — the gates of the one range exactly fronting the gates of the other. Inside, the building is of two storeys and contains three thousand rooms, of which half are underground, and the other half directly above them. I was taken through the rooms in the upper storey, so what I shall say of them is from my own observation, but the underground ones I can speak of only from report, because the Egyptians in charge refused to let me see them, as they contain the tombs of the kings who built the labyrinth, and also the tombs of the sacred crocodiles. The upper rooms, on the contrary, I did actually see, and it is hard to believe that they are the work of men; the baffling and intricate passages from room to room and from court to court were an endless wonder to me, as we passed from a courtyard into rooms, from rooms into galleries, from galleries into more rooms and thence into yet more courtyards. The roof of every chamber, courtyard, and gallery is, like the walls, of stone. The walls are covered with carved figures, and each court is exquisitely built of white marble and surrounded by a colonnade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>During the 19th century, the remains of the Labyrinth were discovered "11½ miles from the pyramid of <a href="/wiki/Hawara" title="Hawara">Hawara</a>, in the province of <a href="/wiki/Faiyum_Oasis" title="Faiyum Oasis">Faioum</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-W.S._27-0"><a href="#cite_note-W.S.-27">[27]</a></sup> The Labyrinth was likely modified and added upon "at various times. The names of more than one king have been found there, the oldest name being that of <a href="/wiki/Amenemhat_III" title="Amenemhat III">Amenemhat III</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-W.S._27-1"><a href="#cite_note-W.S.-27">[27]</a></sup> "It is unnecessary to imagine more than that it was monumental, and a monument of more than one king of Egypt."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-W.S._27-2"><a href="#cite_note-W.S.-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1898, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harpers_Dictionary_of_Classical_Antiquities" title="Harpers Dictionary of Classical Antiquities">Harpers Dictionary of Classical Antiquities</a></i> described the structure as "the largest of all the temples of Egypt, the so-called Labyrinth, of which, however, only the foundation stones have been preserved."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Herodotus' description of the Egyptian Labyrinth inspired some central scenes in <a href="/wiki/Boles%C5%82aw_Prus" title="Bolesław Prus">Bolesław Prus</a>' 1895 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_novel" title="Historical novel">historical novel</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pharaoh_(novel)#Inspirations" title="Pharaoh (novel)">Pharaoh</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pliny.27s_Lemnian_labyrinth">Pliny's Lemnian labyrinth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labyrinth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pliny's Lemnian labyrinth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>'s <i>Natural History</i> (36.90) lists the legendary <a href="/wiki/Smilis" title="Smilis">Smilis</a>, reputed to be a contemporary of Daedalus, together with the historical mid-sixth-century BC architects and sculptors Rhoikos and Theodoros as two of the makers of the Lemnian labyrinth, which Andrew Stewart<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> regards as "evidently a misunderstanding of the Samian temple's location <i>en limnais</i> ['in the marsh']."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pliny.27s_Italian_labyrinth">Pliny's Italian labyrinth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labyrinth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pliny's Italian labyrinth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to Pliny, the tomb of the great Etruscan general <a href="/wiki/Lars_Porsena" title="Lars Porsena">Lars Porsena</a> contained an underground maze. Pliny's description of the exposed portion of the tomb is intractable; Pliny, it seems clear, had not observed this structure himself, but is quoting the historian and Roman antiquarian <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Terentius_Varro" title="Marcus Terentius Varro">Varro</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_labyrinths_outside_Europe">Ancient labyrinths outside Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labyrinth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ancient labyrinths outside Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>A design essentially identical to the 7-course "classical" pattern appeared in <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> culture, the <a href="/wiki/Tohono_O%27odham_people" title="Tohono O'odham people">Tohono O'odham people</a> labyrinth which features <a href="/wiki/I%27itoi" title="I'itoi">I'itoi</a>, the "Man in the Maze". The Tonoho O'odham pattern has two distinct differences from the classical: it is radial in design, and the entrance is at the top, where traditional labyrinths have the entrance at the bottom (see below). The earliest appearances cannot be dated securely; the oldest is commonly dated to the 17th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>A prehistoric petroglyph on a riverbank in <a href="/wiki/Goa" title="Goa">Goa</a> shows the same pattern and has been dated to circa 2500 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Other examples have been found among cave art in northern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and on a dolmen shrine in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nilgiri_Mountains" title="Nilgiri Mountains">Nilgiri Mountains</a>, but are difficult to date accurately. Early labyrinths in India all follow the Classical pattern; some have been described as plans of forts or cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="Not a reliable source, and provides no references (January 2017)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Labyrinths appear in Indian manuscripts and <a href="/wiki/Vajrayana" title="Vajrayana">Tantric</a> texts from the 17th century onward. They are often called "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chakravyuha" title="Chakravyuha">Chakravyuha</a>" in reference to an impregnable battle formation described in the ancient <a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a> epic. Lanka, the capital city of mythic Rāvana, is described as a labyrinth in the 1910 translation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_Rayhan_Biruni" title="Abu Rayhan Biruni">Al-Beruni</a>'s <i>India</i> (c. 1030 AD) p. 306 (with a diagram on the following page).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the <a href="/wiki/White_Sea" title="White Sea">White Sea</a>, notably on the <a href="/wiki/Solovetsky_Islands" title="Solovetsky Islands">Solovetsky Islands</a>, there have been preserved more than 30 stone labyrinths. The most remarkable monument is the <a href="/wiki/Stone_labyrinths_of_Bolshoi_Zayatsky_Island" title="Stone labyrinths of Bolshoi Zayatsky Island">Stone labyrinths of Bolshoi Zayatsky Island</a> - a group of 13–14 stone labyrinths on 0.4 km<sup>2</sup> area of one small island. These labyrinths are thought to be 2,000–3,000 years old.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Labyrinth_as_pattern">Labyrinth as pattern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labyrinth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Labyrinth as pattern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In antiquity, the less complicated labyrinth pattern familiar from medieval examples was already developed. In Roman <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floor_mosaic" title="Floor mosaic">floor mosaics</a>, the simple classical labyrinth is framed in the meander border pattern, squared off as the medium requires, but still recognisable. Often an image of the <a href="/wiki/Minotaur" title="Minotaur">Minotaur</a> appears in the center of these mosaic labyrinths. Roman meander patterns gradually developed in complexity towards the fourfold shape that is now familiarly known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> form. The labyrinth retains its connection with death and a triumphant return: at <a href="/wiki/Hadrumetum" title="Hadrumetum">Hadrumentum</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Sousse" title="Sousse">Sousse</a>), a Roman family tomb has a fourfold labyrinth mosaic floor with a dying minotaur in the center and a mosaic inscription: <small>HICINCLUSUS.VITAMPERDIT</small> "Enclosed here, he loses life" (Kern 169; Kerényi fig.31).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_labyrinths_and_turf_mazes">Medieval labyrinths and turf mazes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labyrinth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Medieval labyrinths and turf mazes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Inneres_der_Kathedrale.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2574" data-file-width="1669" height="339" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Inneres_der_Kathedrale.jpg/220px-Inneres_der_Kathedrale.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Inneres_der_Kathedrale.jpg/330px-Inneres_der_Kathedrale.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Inneres_der_Kathedrale.jpg/440px-Inneres_der_Kathedrale.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Inneres_der_Kathedrale.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Chartres_Cathedral" title="Chartres Cathedral">Chartres Cathedral</a>, about 1750, Jean Baptiste Rigaud</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When the early humanist <a href="/wiki/Benzo_d%27Alessandria" title="Benzo d'Alessandria">Benzo d'Alessandria</a> visited <a href="/wiki/Verona" title="Verona">Verona</a> before 1310, he noted the "<i>Laberinthum</i> which is now called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arena_of_Verona" title="Arena of Verona">Arena</a>";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> perhaps he was seeing the <i>cubiculi</i> beneath the arena's missing floor. The full flowering of the medieval labyrinth came about from the twelfth through fourteenth centuries with the grand pavement labyrinths of the gothic <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedrals</a>, notably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Chartres" title="Cathedral of Chartres">Chartres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reims_Cathedral" title="Reims Cathedral">Reims</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amiens_Cathedral" title="Amiens Cathedral">Amiens</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. These labyrinths may have originated as symbolic allusion to the <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Holy City</a>; and some modern thinkers have theorized that prayers and devotions may have accompanied the perambulation of their intricate paths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Although some books (in particular guidebooks) suggest that the mazes on cathedral floors served as substitutes for pilgrimage paths, the earliest attested use of the phrase "chemin de Jerusalem" (path to Jerusalem) dates to the late 18th century when it was used to describe mazes at <a href="/wiki/Reims" title="Reims">Reims</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint-Omer" title="Saint-Omer">Saint-Omer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wright_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-36">[36]</a></sup> The accompanying ritual, supposedly involving pilgrims following the maze on their knees while praying, may have been practiced at Chartres during the 17th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wright_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-36">[36]</a></sup> However, no contemporary evidence supports the idea that labyrinths had such a purpose for early Christians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-russell_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-russell-37">[37]</a></sup> The cathedral labyrinths are thought to be the inspiration for the many <a href="/wiki/Turf_maze" title="Turf maze">turf mazes</a> in the UK, such as survive at <a href="/wiki/Wing,_Rutland" title="Wing, Rutland">Wing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hilton,_Cambridgeshire" title="Hilton, Cambridgeshire">Hilton</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkborough_Turf_Maze" title="Alkborough Turf Maze">Alkborough</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saffron_Walden" title="Saffron Walden">Saffron Walden</a>.</p>
<p>Over the same general period, some 500 or more non-ecclesiastical labyrinths were constructed in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>. These labyrinths, generally in coastal areas, are marked out with stones, most often in the simple 7- or 11-course classical forms. They often have names which translate as "<a href="/wiki/Troy_Town" title="Troy Town">Troy Town</a>". They are thought to have been constructed by fishing communities: trapping malevolent <a href="/wiki/Troll" title="Troll">trolls</a> or winds in the labyrinth's coils might ensure a safe fishing expedition. There are also stone labyrinths on the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>, although none is known to date from before the nineteenth century.</p>
<p>There are examples of labyrinths in many disparate cultures. The symbol has appeared in various forms and media (<a href="/wiki/Petroglyph" title="Petroglyph">petroglyphs</a>, classic-form, medieval-form, pavement, turf, and basketry) at some time throughout most parts of the world, from Native <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> to <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Java_(island)" title="Java (island)">Java</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_labyrinths">Modern labyrinths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labyrinth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Modern labyrinths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In recent years, there has been a resurgence of interest in labyrinths and a revival in labyrinth building, of both unicursal and multicursal patterns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In modern imagery, the labyrinth of Daedalus is often represented by a multicursal maze, in which one may become lost.</p>
<p>The Argentine writer <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Luis_Borges" title="Jorge Luis Borges">Jorge Luis Borges</a> was entranced with the idea of the labyrinth, and used it extensively in his short stories (such as "The House of Asterion" in <i>The Aleph</i>). His use of it has inspired other authors (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Umberto_Eco" title="Umberto Eco">Umberto Eco</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Name_of_the_Rose" title="The Name of the Rose">The Name of the Rose</a></i>, Mark Z. Danielewski's <i><a href="/wiki/House_of_Leaves" title="House of Leaves">House of Leaves</a></i>). Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Zelazny" title="Roger Zelazny">Roger Zelazny</a>'s fantasy series, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronicles_of_Amber" title="The Chronicles of Amber">The Chronicles of Amber</a></i>, features a labyrinth, called "the Pattern", which grants those who walk it the power to move between parallel worlds. The avant-garde multi-screen film, <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Labyrinth_(film)" title="In the Labyrinth (film)">In the Labyrinth</a></i>, presents a search for meaning in a symbolic modern labyrinth. In <a href="/wiki/Rick_Riordan" title="Rick Riordan">Rick Riordan</a>'s series <a href="/wiki/Percy_Jackson_%26_the_Olympians" title="Percy Jackson &amp; the Olympians">Percy Jackson &amp; the Olympians</a>, the events of the fourth novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Battle_of_the_Labyrinth" title="The Battle of the Labyrinth">The Battle of the Labyrinth</a></i> predominantly take place within the labyrinth of Daedalus, which has followed <a href="/wiki/History_of_Western_civilization" title="History of Western civilization">the heart of the West</a> to settle beneath the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Australian author <a href="/wiki/Sara_Douglass" title="Sara Douglass">Sara Douglass</a> incorporated some labyrinthine ideas in her series <a href="/wiki/The_Troy_Game" title="The Troy Game">The Troy Game</a>, in which the Labyrinth on Crete is one of several in the ancient world, created with the cities as a source of magical power. <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Durrell" title="Lawrence Durrell">Lawrence Durrell</a>'s <i>The Dark Labyrinth</i> depicts travelers trapped underground in Crete. A magical labyrinth, based on the original myth, appears in the third episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Librarians_(2014_TV_series)" title="The Librarians (2014 TV series)">The Librarians</a></i> ("And The Horns of a Dilemma").</p>
<p>The labyrinth is also treated in contemporary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine_arts" title="Fine arts">fine arts</a>. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Piet_Mondrian" title="Piet Mondrian">Piet Mondrian</a>'s <i>Dam and Ocean</i> (1915), <a href="/wiki/Joan_Mir%C3%B3" title="Joan Miró">Joan Miró</a>'s <i>Labyrinth</i> (1923), <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a>'s <i>Minotauromachia</i> (1935), <a href="/wiki/M._C._Escher" title="M. C. Escher">M. C. Escher</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Relativity_(M._C._Escher)" title="Relativity (M. C. Escher)">Relativity</a></i> (1953), <a href="/wiki/Friedensreich_Hundertwasser" title="Friedensreich Hundertwasser">Friedensreich Hundertwasser</a>'s <i>Labyrinth</i> (1957), <a href="/wiki/Jean_Dubuffet" title="Jean Dubuffet">Jean Dubuffet</a>'s <i>Logological Cabinet</i> (1970), <a href="/wiki/Richard_Long_(artist)" title="Richard Long (artist)">Richard Long</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Connemara" title="Connemara">Connemara</a> sculpture</i> (1971), <a href="/wiki/Joe_Tilson" title="Joe Tilson">Joe Tilson</a>'s <i>Earth Maze</i> (1975), <a href="/wiki/Richard_Fleischner" title="Richard Fleischner">Richard Fleischner</a>'s <i>Chain Link Maze</i> (1978), <a href="/wiki/Istv%C3%A1n_Orosz" title="István Orosz">István Orosz</a>'s <i>Atlantis Anamorphosis</i> (2000), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dmitry_Rakov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dmitry Rakov (page does not exist)">Dmitry Rakov</a>'s <i>Labyrinth</i> (2003), and drawings by contemporary American artist Mo Morales employing what the artist calls "Labyrinthine projection". The Italian painter Davide Tonato has dedicated many of his artistic works to the labyrinth theme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mark_Wallinger" title="Mark Wallinger">Mark Wallinger</a> has created a set of 270 <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamel</a> plaques of unicursal labyrinth designs, one for every tube station in the <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a>, to mark the 150th anniversary of the Underground. The plaques were installed over a 16-month period in 2013 and 2014, and each is numbered according to its position in the route taken by the contestants in the 2009 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Record" title="Guinness World Record">Guinness World Record</a> <a href="/wiki/Tube_Challenge" title="Tube Challenge">Tube Challenge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Labyrinths and mazes have been embraced by the <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> industry, and countless video games include such a feature.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_meanings">Cultural meanings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labyrinth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cultural meanings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prehistoric" title="Prehistoric">Prehistoric</a> labyrinths are believed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (September 2013)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup>to have served as traps for malevolent spirits or as defined paths for ritual dances. However, one function of the labyrinth in Christian places of worship may been apotropaic, since complex designs were thought to frighten and "prevent demons access", according to the author Ruth Mellinkoff [see:'Averting Demons', page 164, vol 2, 2004]. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> times, the labyrinth symbolized a hard path to God with a clearly defined center (God) and one entrance (birth). In their cross-cultural study of signs and symbols, <i>Patterns that Connect</i>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Schuster" title="Carl Schuster">Carl Schuster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Snow_Carpenter" title="Edmund Snow Carpenter">Edmund Carpenter</a> present various forms of the labyrinth and suggest various possible meanings, including not only a sacred path to the home of a sacred ancestor, but also, perhaps, a representation of the ancestor him/herself: "...many [New World] Indians who make the labyrinth regard it as a sacred symbol, a beneficial ancestor, a deity. In this they may be preserving its original meaning: the ultimate ancestor, here evoked by two continuous lines joining its twelve primary joints."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuster_1996_307_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schuster_1996_307-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>One can think of labyrinths as symbolic of <a href="/wiki/Pilgrimage" title="Pilgrimage">pilgrimage</a>; people can walk the path, ascending toward salvation or enlightenment. Many people could not afford to <a href="/wiki/Travel" title="Travel">travel</a> to holy sites and lands, so labyrinths and prayer substituted for such travel. Later, the religious significance of labyrinths faded, and they served primarily for entertainment, though recently their spiritual aspect has seen a resurgence.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Author <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ben_Radford" title="Ben Radford">Ben Radford</a> conducted an investigation into some of the claims of spiritual and healing effects of labyrinths, reporting on his findings in his book <a href="/wiki/Mysterious_New_Mexico" title="Mysterious New Mexico">Mysterious New Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many newly made labyrinths exist today, in <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">churches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Park" title="Park">parks</a>. Modern <a href="/wiki/Mysticism" title="Mysticism">mystics</a> use labyrinths to help them achieve a contemplative state.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Walking among the turnings, one loses track of direction and of the outside world, and thus quiets the mind. The Labyrinth Society<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> provides a locator for modern labyrinths all over the world.</p>
<p>In addition, the labyrinth can serve as a metaphor for situations that are difficult to be extricated from, as an image that suggests getting lost in a subterranean dungeon-like world. <a href="/wiki/Octavio_Paz" title="Octavio Paz">Octavio Paz</a> titled his book on <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexican</a> <a href="/wiki/National_identity" title="National identity">identity</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Labyrinth_of_Solitude" title="The Labyrinth of Solitude">The Labyrinth of Solitude</a></i>, describing the Mexican condition as orphaned and lost.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_use">Christian use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labyrinth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Christian use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Labyrinth_at_Chartres_Cathedral.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Labyrinth_at_Chartres_Cathedral.JPG/300px-Labyrinth_at_Chartres_Cathedral.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Labyrinth_at_Chartres_Cathedral.JPG/450px-Labyrinth_at_Chartres_Cathedral.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Labyrinth_at_Chartres_Cathedral.JPG/600px-Labyrinth_at_Chartres_Cathedral.JPG 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Labyrinth_at_Chartres_Cathedral.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Walking the labyrinth at Chartres Cathedral</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Labyrinths have on various occasions been used in Christian tradition as a part of worship. The earliest known example is from a fourth-century pavement at the Basilica of St Reparatus, at Orleansville, Algeria, with the words "Sancta Eclesia"  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] at the center, though it is unclear how it might have been used in worship.</p>
<p>In medieval times, labyrinths began to appear on church walls and floors around 1000 C.E.. The most famous medieval labyrinth, with great influence on later practice, was created in <a href="/wiki/Chartres_Cathedral" title="Chartres Cathedral">Chartres Cathedral</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kern_church_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-kern_church-45">[45]</a></sup> The purpose of the labyrinths is not clear, though there are surviving descriptions of French clerics performing a ritual Easter dance along the path on Easter Sunday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kern_church_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-kern_church-45">[45]</a></sup> Some books (guidebooks in particular) suggest that mazes on cathedral floors originated in the medieval period as alternatives to pilgrimage to the Holy Land, but the earliest attested use of the phrase "chemin de Jerusalem" (path to Jerusalem) dates to the late 18th century when it was used to describe mazes at <a href="/wiki/Reims" title="Reims">Reims</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint-Omer" title="Saint-Omer">Saint-Omer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wright_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-36">[36]</a></sup> The accompanying ritual, depicted in Romantic illustrations as involving pilgrims following the maze on their knees while praying, may have been practiced at Chartres during the 17th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wright_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The use of labyrinths has recently been revived in some contexts of Christian worship. Many churches in Europe and North America have constructed permanent, typically unicursal, labyrinths, or employ temporary ones (e.g., painted on canvas or outlined with candles). For example, a labyrinth was set up on the floor of <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_Cathedral" title="St Paul's Cathedral">St Paul's Cathedral</a> for a week in March 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Some conservative Christians disapprove of labyrinths, considering them pagan practices or "new age" fads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
