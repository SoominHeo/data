<p><b>Minami</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">南区</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Minami-ku</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is one of the eleven <a href="/wiki/Wards_of_Kyoto" title="Wards of Kyoto">wards</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Japan" title="Municipalities of Japan">city</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyoto,_Kyoto" title="Kyoto, Kyoto">Kyoto</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Prefecture" title="Kyoto Prefecture">Kyoto Prefecture</a>, Japan. Its name means "South Ward." It was established in 1955 when it was separated from Shimogyo-ku. As of April, 2016 the ward has an estimated population of 100,781 people. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kamo_River_(Kyoto)" title="Kamo River (Kyoto)">Kamo River</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Katsura_River" title="Katsura River">Katsura River</a> flow through the district. It is home to several historical places and temples.</p>
<p>Minami-ku is roughly bound on the east by the <a href="/wiki/Kamo_River" title="Kamo River">Kamo River</a>, on the west by the <a href="/wiki/Katsura_River" title="Katsura River">Katsura River</a> (although it extends over the Katsura River in some places), on the north by Hachijō Street and the JR Kyoto line (leading into Kyoto station), and on the south by the <a href="/wiki/Meishin_Expressway" title="Meishin Expressway">Meishin Expressway</a> (although it extends over the expressway in some places).</p>
<p>The southern portion of Kyoto city (including Minami-ku) is sometimes referred to as Rakunan. This area is the only side of Kyoto not lined with mountains, a feature that historically made Rakunan important as a gateway into Kyoto.</p>
<p>Minami-ku is more working-class than some other Kyoto districts, and includes a large population of Korean nationals<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Community.2C_Democracy.2C_and_Performance:_The_Urban_Practice_of_Kyoto.27s_Higashi-Kujo_Madang_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Community.2C_Democracy.2C_and_Performance:_The_Urban_Practice_of_Kyoto.27s_Higashi-Kujo_Madang-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zainichi_Koreans" title="Zainichi Koreans">Zainichi Koreans</a>. Instead of restaurants and clubs, portions of the land near the Kamo River are lined with factories and industrial buildings. Residential areas include both standard houses and apartments, and occasional government-subsidized housing projects.</p>
<p>The area around Kujo Street from around Toji station to the Kamo River is known as Higashi (east) Kujo. This area is particularly working-class, and home to a relatively large number of Korean nationals and other working class members of Japanese society. As such, it has been called "Kyoto's only international town".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Community.2C_Democracy.2C_and_Performance:_The_Urban_Practice_of_Kyoto.27s_Higashi-Kujo_Madang_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Community.2C_Democracy.2C_and_Performance:_The_Urban_Practice_of_Kyoto.27s_Higashi-Kujo_Madang-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Minami-ku has few notable areas of tourist interest compared to other parts of Kyoto. A notable exception is the To-ji Temple.</p>
<p>The Minami-ku ward office is located on Route 1, near its intersection with Kujō Street. The Kyoto City Disaster Prevention Center is also in Minami-ku, near the intersection of Route 1 and Jujō Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>To the east of Minami-ku is <a href="/wiki/Higashiyama-ku,_Kyoto" title="Higashiyama-ku, Kyoto">Higashiyama-ku</a>, and to the southeast is <a href="/wiki/Fushimi-ku,_Kyoto" title="Fushimi-ku, Kyoto">Fushimi-ku</a>.</p>
