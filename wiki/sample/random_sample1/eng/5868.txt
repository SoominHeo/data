<p><i><b>Mini Metro</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Puzzle_video_game" title="Puzzle video game">puzzle</a> <a href="/wiki/Strategy_game" title="Strategy game">strategy</a> video game developed by <a href="/wiki/Independent_video_game_development" title="Independent video game development">indie development</a> team Dinosaur Polo Club. Players are tasked with constructing an efficient <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">rail</a> <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit" title="Rapid transit">transit</a> network for a rapidly growing city. Stations are represented different shaped nodes and players can build tracks to connect them by drawing lines. Levels are based on real cities and the appearance of stations and passengers is <a href="/wiki/Procedural_generation" title="Procedural generation">procedurally generated</a>. The game's visual style makes use of bold colours and simple geometry to replicate the appearance of modern <a href="/wiki/Transit_map" title="Transit map">transit maps</a>. The game uses a procedural audio system to generate sounds based on the player actions and transit network. The rhythms and sounds created took inspiration from works of <a href="/wiki/Minimal_music" title="Minimal music">minimal music</a>.</p>
<p><i>Mini Metro</i> was conceived in April 2013 for a <a href="/wiki/Game_jam" title="Game jam">video game development competition</a>, with a prototype version being released freely for <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">web browsers</a>. During the game's conception phase, the developers imposed a set of constraints to limit its scope and ensure the project could reach a finished state. Production of the game continued following the event and an in-development version was released commercially on <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OS_X" title="OS X">OS X</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windows" title="Windows">Windows</a> in August 2014. <i>Mini Metro</i> received its full release on <a href="/wiki/PC_game" title="PC game">PC</a> platforms in November 2015, and on <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> and <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> platforms in October 2016. The game received a positive reception from critics, with praise directed towards its intuitive interface, simple gameplay, and <a href="/wiki/Minimalism" title="Minimalism">minimalist</a> approach.</p>

<p><i>Mini Metro</i> is <a href="/wiki/Puzzle_video_game" title="Puzzle video game">puzzle</a> <a href="/wiki/Strategy_game" title="Strategy game">strategy</a> video game, in which players are tasked with building an efficient <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">rail</a> <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit" title="Rapid transit">transit</a> network for a rapidly growing city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamestm_review_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamestm_review-1">[1]</a></sup> Each level is a flat depiction of a real city and begins with three train stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiegames_nov_9_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-indiegames_nov_9-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-killscreen_review_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-killscreen_review-3">[3]</a></sup> Train stations are represented by different shaped nodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiegames_nov_9_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-indiegames_nov_9-2">[2]</a></sup> Players can construct routes to link stations by drawing lines between them; each route has a different colour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiegames_nov_9_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-indiegames_nov_9-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rps_aug_2014_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-rps_aug_2014-4">[4]</a></sup> Passengers appear at stations, each denoted by a shape that identifies which station they want to travel to.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiegames_nov_9_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-indiegames_nov_9-2">[2]</a></sup> Trains travel along routes taking passengers to their desired stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rps_aug_2014_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-rps_aug_2014-4">[4]</a></sup> Rivers, intersecting train lines and the appearance of more stations and passengers over time causes the transit network to become more complex and difficult to manage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiegames_nov_9_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-indiegames_nov_9-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-killscreen_aug_2014_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-killscreen_aug_2014-5">[5]</a></sup> Common-shaped stations can also change their node shape into rarer ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiegames_nov_9_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-indiegames_nov_9-2">[2]</a></sup> The game's visual style is minimalist, employing straight lines and bold colours, similar to modern <a href="/wiki/Transit_map" title="Transit map">transit maps</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-killscreen_nov_2015_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-killscreen_nov_2015-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the "Normal" game mode, each station can accommodate a limited capacity of waiting passengers; if the stations become too congested and the threshold is reached, the level ends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiegames_nov_9_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-indiegames_nov_9-2">[2]</a></sup> Upon failing, players can continue to build their network in the "Endless" game mode where stations do not have a limited capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiegames_nov_9_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-indiegames_nov_9-2">[2]</a></sup> To alleviate the network's growth, players earn upgrades each week of in-game time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamestm_review_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamestm_review-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiegames_nov_9_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-indiegames_nov_9-2">[2]</a></sup> At each upgrade, the player receives a new train car and a choice among two other random options such as an extra rail line, an extra train carriage, or increased station capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiegames_nov_9_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-indiegames_nov_9-2">[2]</a></sup> In addition, the transit simulation can be paused at any point to allow for reconstruction and adjustments to train routes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rps_aug_2014_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-rps_aug_2014-4">[4]</a></sup> In the "Extreme" and "Daily Challenge" modes, lines between stations cannot be adjusted once their position is set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiegames_nov_9_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-indiegames_nov_9-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamezebo_review_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamezebo_review-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_and_release">Development and release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mini_Metro_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development and release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Mini Metro</i> was developed by Dinosaur Polo Club, a two-man <a href="/wiki/Independent_video_game_development" title="Independent video game development">independent video game development</a> studio based in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dinopoloclub_press_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-dinopoloclub_press-8">[8]</a></sup> It is the first game from the studio, which was founded in 2013 by brothers Peter and Robert Curry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dinopoloclub_press_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-dinopoloclub_press-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_road_to_igf_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_road_to_igf-9">[9]</a></sup> They had previously worked at video game developer <a href="/wiki/Sidhe_(game_developer)" title="Sidhe (game developer)">Sidhe</a>, but left in 2006 to pursue a career in developing <a href="/wiki/Indie_game" title="Indie game">indie games</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_road_to_igf_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_road_to_igf-9">[9]</a></sup> Following a series of abandoned projects, Peter Curry recognised that he would have to restrict the scope of his projects in order to complete them,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> and with hopes of releasing a finished product, the brothers set a number of constraints while proposing ideas for a prototype game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_road_to_igf_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_road_to_igf-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> Minimising the amount of production art was a necessary restriction, as they had little experience in creating art assets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_road_to_igf_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_road_to_igf-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> Likewise, they did not want the game's concept to rely on audio content, due to lack of skills in producing music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> They also did not want to hand-build each level in the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> Imposing these constraints meant that they could discard most of their potential game ideas immediately, and led to them concentrating on concepts that involved <a href="/wiki/Procedural_generation" title="Procedural generation">procedurally generated</a> levels and abstract visual styles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Development of <i>Mini Metro</i> began in April 2013, under the title <i>Mind the Gap</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_road_to_igf_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_road_to_igf-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> Robert Curry suggested building a subway navigation game after visiting <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and using its <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">underground transit system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_road_to_igf_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_road_to_igf-9">[9]</a></sup> The concept was to let the player build a transit system that navigates <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_agent" title="Intelligent agent">intelligent agents</a> around, with nodes and lines representing <a href="/wiki/Train_station" title="Train station">train stations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Track_(rail_transport)" title="Track (rail transport)">tracks</a> respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_road_to_igf_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_road_to_igf-9">[9]</a></sup> It was prototyped over a weekend, and entered in the <a href="/wiki/Ludum_Dare" title="Ludum Dare">Ludum Dare</a> 26 <a href="/wiki/Game_jam" title="Game jam">video game development competition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> They chose to develop the game using the <a href="/wiki/Unity_(game_engine)" title="Unity (game engine)">Unity</a> <a href="/wiki/Game_engine" title="Game engine">game engine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-8"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> The Unity Web Player allowed them to release a freely playable <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">web browser</a> version of the prototype during the competition, so more people would have access to play it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-9"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> <i>Mind the Gap</i> was ranked number 1 in the "Innovation" category and number 7 in the "Overall" category during the competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dinopoloclub_press_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-dinopoloclub_press-8">[8]</a></sup> Part-time development on the game continued after the event concluded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_road_to_igf_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_road_to_igf-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>In September 2013, the first playable alpha version of <i>Mini Metro</i> was released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-10"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> They decided to keep the web version of the game freely available, at least until the release of the final game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-11"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> They also submitted <i>Mini Metro</i> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_Greenlight" title="Steam Greenlight">Steam Greenlight</a>, a community voting system that allows games to be released on digital distribution service <a href="/wiki/Steam_(software)" title="Steam (software)">Steam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-12"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> Initially, they intended on releasing the final version of the game by the end of 2013, however despite the game's limited scope, development took far longer than they had expected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-13"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> A graphical overhaul, issues within the game's <a href="/wiki/Balance_(game_design)" title="Balance (game design)">balance</a>, and scheduling someone to work on the game's audio contributed to its delay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-14"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> In August 2014, they decided to release an in-development version of the game commercially through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_Early_Access" title="Steam Early Access">Steam Early Access</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-15"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rps_aug_2014_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-rps_aug_2014-4">[4]</a></sup> They felt that the <a href="/wiki/Early_access" title="Early access">early access</a> model was suitable for <i>Mini Metro</i> because the game was designed to have <a href="/wiki/Replay_value" title="Replay value">replay value</a> and there was no fixed narrative to be spoiled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-16"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> They continually released new builds of the game based on the community's feedback.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-17"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> The early access release provided them with funds to work full-time on the game. Peter Curry had been working on <i>Mini Metro</i> full-time since March 2014, and his brother Robert started working full-time in November 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_road_to_igf_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_road_to_igf-9">[9]</a></sup> Originally <i>Mini Metro</i> was being built for <a href="/wiki/Mobile_game" title="Mobile game">mobile platforms</a>, however they shifted their focus to <a href="/wiki/PC_game" title="PC game">desktop PCs</a> because of the ability to release their game in the early access state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-18"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The brothers hired external help to address two of their initial issues: art and audio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-19"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> Jamie Churchman, a former colleague at Sidhe, oversaw the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visual_design" title="Visual design">visual design</a> for <i>Mini Metro</i> and also contributed to the game design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_road_to_igf_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_road_to_igf-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-20"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> They approached American composer <a href="/wiki/Disasterpeace" title="Disasterpeace">Disasterpeace</a> to work on the game's audio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-21"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup> He developed a procedural audio system that would generate sounds based on events in the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_postmortem_10-22"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_postmortem-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamestm_disasterpeace_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamestm_disasterpeace-11">[11]</a></sup> Each level in the game has a corresponding set of rhythms and sounds; the harmonic structure of these elements changes based on the size and shape of the player's subways system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-designingsound_interview_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-designingsound_interview-12">[12]</a></sup> The audio was inspired by <a href="/wiki/Minimal_music" title="Minimal music">minimalism</a> and the works of <a href="/wiki/Philip_Glass" title="Philip Glass">Philip Glass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Reich" title="Steve Reich">Steve Reich</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-designingsound_interview_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-designingsound_interview-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 6 November 2015, <i>Mini Metro</i> was released out of early access for <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OS_X" title="OS X">OS X</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windows" title="Windows">Windows</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rps_oct_2015_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-rps_oct_2015-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rps_nov_2015_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-rps_nov_2015-14">[14]</a></sup> Dinosaur Polo Club partnered with <a href="/wiki/Playism" title="Playism">Playism</a> and Plug In Digital to release the game on <a href="/wiki/Digital_distribution" title="Digital distribution">digital storefronts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Koch_Media" title="Koch Media">Koch Media</a> to distribute the game in retail stores in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-devblog_may_2016_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-devblog_may_2016-15">[15]</a></sup> A version for <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> and <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> was released on 18 October 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pocket_gamer_mobile_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-pocket_gamer_mobile-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>Mini Metro</i> received "generally favourable" reception from critics according to aggregate review website <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metacritic_pc_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-metacritic_pc-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metacritic_ios_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-metacritic_ios-18">[18]</a></sup> <i>Technology Tell</i> writer, Jenni Lada described <i>Mini Metro</i> as "aesthetically pleasing" and found playing the game an oddly relaxing experience, despite the game's attempt to put the player under pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-techtell_review_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-techtell_review-20">[20]</a></sup> Lena LeRay, of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IndieGames.com" title="IndieGames.com">IndieGames.com</a>, also felt that <i>Mini Metro</i> had a relaxing tone and complimented its intuitive interface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiegames_nov_9_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-indiegames_nov_9-2">[2]</a></sup> LeRay appreciated changes that were made during the early access phase, noting that audio and the Daily Challenge mode were welcome additions, and the interface improvements had diminished her initial complaints about the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indiegames_nov_9_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-indiegames_nov_9-2">[2]</a></sup> Reviewing the PC version, <i><a href="/wiki/GamesTM" title="GamesTM">GamesTM</a></i> thought that the depth of <i>Mini Metro</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s gameplay along with the difficulty scaling kept the game interesting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamestm_review_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-gamestm_review-1">[1]</a></sup> However, they did question why the game hadn't been released for <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphones</a> yet, pointing out that such devices would be suited for the game's simple interface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamestm_review_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-gamestm_review-1">[1]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Kill_Screen" title="Kill Screen">Kill Screen</a></i> editor, Ethan Gach commended the game for its simplicity and elegance, having combined interactive aesthetics with a robust simulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-killscreen_review_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-killscreen_review-3">[3]</a></sup> Alec Meer of <i><a href="/wiki/Rock,_Paper,_Shotgun" title="Rock, Paper, Shotgun">Rock, Paper, Shotgun</a></i> praised the game for being a "mesmerising challenge of logic and aesthetics".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rps_aug_2014_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-rps_aug_2014-4">[4]</a></sup> He particularly enjoyed how beautifully the gameplay descended into chaos as the difficulty gradually increased, calling it "elegant even in disaster". He also thought that <i>Mini Metro</i> was a good example of how to release game in early access.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rps_aug_2014_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-rps_aug_2014-4">[4]</a></sup> Reviewing the mobile version, <i><a href="/wiki/Gamezebo" title="Gamezebo">Gamezebo</a></i> writer Rob Rich commended the visual design and intuitive touch controls. He thought that the use of geometric shapes to represent commuters and stations helped simplify the complicated tasks, resulting in an accessible game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamezebo_review_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-gamezebo_review-7">[7]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Pocket_Gamer" title="Pocket Gamer">Pocket Gamer</a></i> reviewer Christian Valentin remarked that the game felt confusing early on but became "surprisingly engaging".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pocket_gamer_review_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-pocket_gamer_review-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the 2016 <a href="/wiki/Independent_Games_Festival" title="Independent Games Festival">Independent Games Festival</a>, <i>Mini Metro</i> won an award for "Excellence in Audio",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gameindustry_igf_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-gameindustry_igf-21">[21]</a></sup> and was nominated for awards in three other categories: "Excellence in Visual Art", "Excellence in Design", and the "<a href="/wiki/Seumas_McNally_Grand_Prize" title="Seumas McNally Grand Prize">Seumas McNally Grand Prize</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_igf_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_igf-22">[22]</a></sup> <i>Mini Metro</i> also received a nominated for "Debut Game" at the <a href="/wiki/12th_British_Academy_Games_Awards" title="12th British Academy Games Awards">12th British Academy Games Awards</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamasutra_bafta_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_bafta-23">[23]</a></sup> and received an honourable mention for "Best Debut" at the 16th Annual <a href="/wiki/Game_Developers_Choice_Awards" title="Game Developers Choice Awards">Game Developers Choice Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gdc_awards_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-gdc_awards-24">[24]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/GameSpot" title="GameSpot">GameSpot</a></i> listed <i>Mini Metro</i> in their best five mobile games of 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespot_best_mobile_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamespot_best_mobile-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
