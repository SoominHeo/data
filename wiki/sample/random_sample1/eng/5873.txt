<p><b>Midwest Airlines</b> (formerly <b>Midwest Express</b>) was a <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a>-based airline and, for a short time, an operating brand of <a href="/wiki/Republic_Airways_Holdings" title="Republic Airways Holdings">Republic Airways Holdings</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Oak_Creek,_Wisconsin" title="Oak Creek, Wisconsin">Oak Creek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> operating from Milwaukee's <a href="/wiki/General_Mitchell_International_Airport" title="General Mitchell International Airport">General Mitchell International Airport</a>. On April 13, 2010, parent company Republic announced that Midwest Airlines and <a href="/wiki/Frontier_Airlines" title="Frontier Airlines">Frontier Airlines</a> would merge, with the Midwest brand disappearing in late 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newname_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-newname-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Midwest Airlines' final flight operated with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_717-200" title="Boeing 717-200">Boeing 717-200</a> and staffed with Midwest Airlines flight crews landed in Milwaukee on November 2, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Effective November 3, 2009, Midwest Airlines ceased to exist as an actual operating airline (allowing its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Department_of_Transportation" title="US Department of Transportation">DOT</a> air carrier operator certificate to lapse).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="K-C_Aviation">K-C Aviation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: K-C Aviation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Midwest Airlines began its existence in 1948, when <a href="/wiki/Kimberly-Clark" title="Kimberly-Clark">Kimberly-Clark</a> began providing air transportation for company executives and engineers between the company's <a href="/wiki/Neenah,_Wisconsin" title="Neenah, Wisconsin">Neenah, Wisconsin</a> headquarters and its mills. Operating out of the nearby <a href="/wiki/Appleton_International_Airport" title="Appleton International Airport">Appleton airport</a>, early employee shuttle destinations included Chicago O'Hare, Memphis, and Atlanta's Fulton County Airport.</p>
<p>In 1969, K-C Aviation was born from this, and was dedicated to the maintenance of corporate aircraft. Kimberly-Clark moved its corporate headquarters in 1985 to <a href="/wiki/Irving,_Texas" title="Irving, Texas">Irving, Texas</a>, a suburb of <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>. K-C Aviation was sold in 1998 to <a href="/wiki/Gulfstream_Aerospace" title="Gulfstream Aerospace">Gulfstream Aerospace</a> for $250 million; included were its operations in Appleton, Dallas, and <a href="/wiki/Westfield,_Massachusetts" title="Westfield, Massachusetts">Westfield, Massachusetts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kcavrnt_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-kcavrnt-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Midwest_Express">Midwest Express</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Midwest Express">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Airline_Deregulation_Act" title="Airline Deregulation Act">Airline Deregulation Act</a> of 1978, Kimberly-Clark and K-C Aviation decided to form a regular scheduled passenger airline, and out of that initiative, Midwest Express began operations on June 11, 1984. At the time the airline had two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-9-10" title="Douglas DC-9-10">Douglas DC-9-10s</a> and 83 employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Early plans for the airline called for it to serve Appleton, Chicago, and Atlanta. Kimberly-Clark opted against this plan after local resistance over the carrier's desire to serve Atlanta's <a href="/wiki/Fulton_County_Airport_(Georgia)" title="Fulton County Airport (Georgia)">Fulton County Airport</a>, which is a general aviation airport on the city's west side. From 1983 to 1985, the airline also operated a single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convair_580" title="Convair 580">Convair 580</a> twin turboprop aircraft from Kimberly-Clark's corporate aviation department.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The airline slowly grew by adding additional DC-9 aircraft to its fleet, including larger <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_DC-9#Series_30" title="McDonnell Douglas DC-9">DC-9-30</a> jets, with a total of 24 by the end of 1996. Eventually, Midwest Express served most major Midwestern and East Coast destinations. Its longtime slogan, "The Best Care in the Air", represented its inflight product. For many years, all flights featured 2-by-2 leather seating (in aircraft usually fitted with 3-2 seating), ample legroom, complimentary gourmet meals, and warm chocolate chip cookies. This made the airline popular with business travelers. In addition, Midwest Express operated a sizable executive charter operation with a specially configured DC-9.</p>

<p>In 1989, Midwest Express added two <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_MD-80" title="McDonnell Douglas MD-80">McDonnell Douglas MD-80</a> aircraft to its fleet, eventually acquiring eleven additional aircraft between 1998 and 2001. These enabled the airline to expand services to the West Coast and Florida. The airline experienced steady growth and continued profitability, opening an additional hub in <a href="/wiki/Eppley_Airfield" title="Eppley Airfield">Omaha, Nebraska</a> in early 1995. Midwest Express also started its own regional subsidiary, <a href="/wiki/Skyway_Airlines" title="Skyway Airlines">Skyway Airlines</a>, The Midwest Express Connection, to provide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commuter_airline" title="Commuter airline">commuter airline</a> service to small communities in Wisconsin and the surrounding region. Kimberly-Clark relinquished its ownership in two initial public offerings on September 22, 1995 and May 8, 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fi_20050412_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-fi_20050412-7">[7]</a></sup> The airline's new parent company, <a href="/wiki/Midwest_Air_Group" title="Midwest Air Group">Midwest Air Group</a>, traded on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Stock_Exchange" title="American Stock Exchange">American Stock Exchange</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Ticker_symbol" title="Ticker symbol">ticker symbol</a> "MEH."</p>
<p>Midwest Express also added Midwest Vacations in the 1990s, naming GOGO Worldwide Vacations as the original partner to provide hotel service<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and later partnering with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Travel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mark Travel (page does not exist)">Mark Travel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Midwest Airlines Vacations continues to operate as a vacation provider.</p>
<p>In 1997, according to the Midwest Express timetable, the airline was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Code_sharing" title="Code sharing">code sharing</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virgin_Atlantic_Airways" title="Virgin Atlantic Airways">Virgin Atlantic Airways</a> for flights between London <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">Heathrow Airport</a> and Milwaukee and Kansas City with passengers connecting between the two air carriers in Boston.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>After fourteen years of profit-making, Midwest Express was affected with serious financial problems after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11_terrorist_attacks" title="September 11 terrorist attacks">September 11 terrorist attacks</a>. To return to profitability, the airline made major changes. The Omaha hub was reduced to a focus city in early 2002, with hub status transferred to <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_International_Airport" title="Kansas City International Airport">Kansas City</a>. Some MD-80 series aircraft were reconfigured into a new "Saver Service", featuring cloth coach seats in a 2-by-3 arrangement. Saver Service, while decreasing the width of the seats, continued to feature ample legroom. This service was initially offered from the Milwaukee and Kansas City hubs to leisure destinations such as Florida, Los Angeles, Las Vegas, and Phoenix on <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_MD-80" title="McDonnell Douglas MD-80">McDonnell Douglas MD-80</a> aircraft. The airline's Signature Service was also affected by the financial difficulties. The signature gourmet meal services, which had been served on china after being cooked on board, were discontinued in 2002 and replaced with a buy-on-board product.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Midwest_Airlines">Midwest Airlines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Midwest Airlines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 2002, the airline made another major change, shortening its name from Midwest Express to simply Midwest. A major reason for the change was the modern association of "express" with a <a href="/wiki/Regional_airline" title="Regional airline">regional airline</a>, which Midwest was not. At the same time, Midwest's commuter airline subsidiary changed its name from Skyway Airlines, the Midwest Express Connection, to <a href="/wiki/Midwest_Connect" title="Midwest Connect">Midwest Connect</a>. In a move to save money on jet fuel, the airline accelerated the replacement of DC-9 aircraft with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_717-200" title="Boeing 717-200">Boeing 717-200</a>. On May 23, 2006, Midwest Airlines accepted one of the last two Boeing 717s delivered in a ceremony with <a href="/wiki/AirTran_Airways" title="AirTran Airways">AirTran Airways</a>, who accepted the other 717. Midwest also announced that select MD-80 aircraft would leave the fleet.</p>
<p>In May 2005, Midwest announced a new buy-on-board meal service for customers. The new program was a change from the previous 'In-flight Cafe' and featured chefs and inspiration from the renowned Mader's restaurant. Chocolate chip cookies continued to be baked on the plane and served warm.</p>
<p>Midwest became the largest longstanding operation at Mitchell Airport and served 21 cities non-stop (serving San Antonio only through Kansas City), while its regional partner <a href="/wiki/Skyway_Airlines" title="Skyway Airlines">Skyway Airlines</a>, operating as <a href="/wiki/Midwest_Connect" title="Midwest Connect">Midwest Connect</a>, served nearly 30 destinations throughout the Central United States.</p>
<p>On May 17, 2007, Midwest Airlines signed a Memorandum of Understanding with <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Airlines" title="Northwest Airlines">Northwest Airlines</a> to form a code share agreement with them. The code share agreement added 250 city pairs and 1,000 new flight options for Midwest Airlines customers. Northwest routes that included the Midwest Airlines YX code were destinations beyond Northwest's hubs at <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minneapolis/St._Paul" title="Minneapolis/St. Paul">Minneapolis/St. Paul</a> and <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis</a> throughout the United States and Canada. Midwest placed its code on Northwest flights from <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis" title="Indianapolis">Indianapolis</a>, then a Northwest focus city, as well as a number of Northwest-operated flights to <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. Routes operated by Midwest Airlines that carried the NW <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Airlines" title="Northwest Airlines">Northwest</a> code were flights that connected at Midwest's <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Missouri" title="Kansas City, Missouri">Kansas City</a> hubs, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omaha" title="Omaha">Omaha</a>, a Midwest focus city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Airlines" title="Northwest Airlines">Northwest</a> also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Code_share" title="Code share">code shared</a> on Midwest Airlines-operated flights between <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Missouri" title="Kansas City, Missouri">Kansas City</a> to <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hartford" title="Hartford">Hartford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> that connected to the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Airlines" title="Northwest Airlines">Northwest</a>/<a href="/wiki/KLM" title="KLM">KLM</a> trans-Atlantic network and trans-Pacific network.</p>
<p>Midwest won more awards for exceptional service in <i><a href="/wiki/Cond%C3%A9_Nast_Traveler" title="Condé Nast Traveler">Condé Nast Traveler</a></i> magazine than any other U.S. airline, although it won no further awards from the magazine after ceasing to be an independent company.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Signature_and_Saver_service_added">Signature and Saver service added</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Signature and Saver service added">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On May 29, 2007, Midwest announced the next phase of the company's strategic plan, which offered customers the choice of Signature and Saver seating on all flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The dual-seating option, replacing the original 88-seat configuration, was available on the airline's Boeing 717s until their retirement. The same amenities were provided to all passengers in either cabin, including leather seats.</p>
<p>The new configuration consisted of 40 Signature leather seats, arranged in 10 rows of two-by-two, offering a 36" pitch and providing 2-3 more inches of legroom than the previous Signature seating,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and 59 Saver seats arranged two-by-three which the company claimed were among the roomiest coach seats in the industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company projected that the implementation of seating choices would generate $30–35 million in annualized revenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The addition of 11 seats on each of the airline's 9 Boeing 717 aircraft would have reduced the airline's unit costs by increasing capacity 12.5%. Had the new seating configuration on the Boeing 717s been in effect in 2006, Midwest Airlines' cost per available seat mile excluding fuel of 7.22 cents would have been 6.77 cents, a 6.3% improvement.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_by_TPG">Acquisition by TPG</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Acquisition by TPG">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 2006, <a href="/wiki/AirTran_Holdings" title="AirTran Holdings">AirTran Holdings Inc.</a>—owner of <a href="/wiki/AirTran_Airways" title="AirTran Airways">AirTran Airways</a>—made public that in December 2005 it had approached the Board of Directors of Midwest Air Group—owner of Midwest Airlines and Midwest Connect—and had asked the board to negotiate a sale of the company. That AirTran offer in 2005 was rebuffed by Midwest's board, which also rebuffed a second offer in late 2006. In December 2006, AirTran disclosed the rejection of both offers in hopes of bringing shareholder pressure on Midwest's board to reconsider, which the board recommended that shareholders reject.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 12, 2007, it was announced that AirTran had lost the bid for Midwest. A private equity group, headed by <a href="/wiki/TPG_Capital" title="TPG Capital">TPG Capital</a> and including <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Airlines" title="Northwest Airlines">Northwest Airlines</a>, purchased Midwest and turned the airline into a privately funded company. The inclusion of Northwest in the investing partners required <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-trust" title="Anti-trust">anti-trust</a> review from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">United States Department of Justice</a>, which reviews all airline mergers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 14, 2007, AirTran increased its offer to the equivalent of $16.25 a share, slightly more than the $16 a share from TPG Capital investors group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> However, Midwest announced TPG would increase its offer to $17 per share and a definitive agreement had been reached late on August 16, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 17, 2007 TPG and Northwest Airlines finalized their bid for Midwest with the final offer of $17 per share and a total deal of $450 million.</p>
<p>On February 1, 2008, Midwest Air Group announced that the US Department of Justice had cleared the acquisition of Midwest by TPG Capital and Northwest. This finalized the acquisition; trading of Midwest Air Group on the American Stock Exchange ceased at the end of the trading day on January 31, 2008, and stockholders in Midwest received the agreed-upon $17 per share. This ended the independent existence of Midwest Airlines.</p>
<p>In accordance with the rest of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airline_industry" title="Airline industry">airline industry</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_price_increases_since_2003" title="Oil price increases since 2003">oil price increases since 2003</a>, Midwest Airlines was forced to cut back services. To do this, Midwest Airlines announced its intent to retire the 12 remaining <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_MD-80" title="McDonnell Douglas MD-80">McDonnell Douglas MD-80</a> jets in its fleet. According to the company, the MD-80 was "a very fuel inefficient airplane and at the cost of fuel today it's just become economically infeasible to operate these planes." The MD-80s, and the crews that operated them, left Midwest in late 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/TPG_Capital" title="TPG Capital">TPG Capitals</a> <a href="/wiki/Midwest_Air_Group" title="Midwest Air Group">Midwest Air Group</a> failed to pay a $3.3 million <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> receivable due the outsourced <a href="/wiki/Regional_airline" title="Regional airline">regional airline</a> replacement for <a href="/wiki/Skyway_Airlines" title="Skyway Airlines">Skyway Airlines</a> which previously <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D/b/a" title="D/b/a">d/b/a</a> as <a href="/wiki/Midwest_Connect" title="Midwest Connect">Midwest Connect</a>. SkyWest was due the amount by June 30, 2008 forcing <a href="/wiki/SkyWest_Airlines" title="SkyWest Airlines">SkyWest Airlines</a> to record a full reserve and corresponding reduction in revenue during the second quarter of that year.</p>
<p>Additional changes were announced on September 3, 2008, when the airline announced that it had raised $60 million from TPG, Northwest Airlines, and <a href="/wiki/Republic_Airways_Holdings" title="Republic Airways Holdings">Republic Airways Holdings</a>. As part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outsource" title="Outsource">outsourcing</a> deal, <a href="/wiki/Republic_Airlines" title="Republic Airlines">Republic Airlines</a> operated 12 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embraer_170" title="Embraer 170">Embraer 170</a> aircraft under the Midwest Airlines brand, though Midwest had the option to convert the aircraft into a long-term lease and operate them directly. The airline also reached an agreement with <a href="/wiki/Boeing_Capital" title="Boeing Capital">Boeing Capital</a> to return 16 Boeing 717s, leaving it at the time with a fleet of only 9 aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_by_Republic_Airways_Holdings">Acquisition by Republic Airways Holdings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Acquisition by Republic Airways Holdings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On June 23, 2009, Republic Airways Holdings, Inc announced they would acquire Midwest Airlines for $31 million. The deal closed on July 30.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Midwest became a wholly owned subsidiary of Republic Airways and continued to operate under current branding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midwestairlines.com_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-midwestairlines.com-24">[24]</a></sup> The Midwest Airlines FAA operating certificate expired on November 3, 2009. The acquisition by Republic was 22 months after TPG and Northwest Airlines paid $450 million for Midwest. The total loss of investment by TPG and Northwest was 93% or $419 million.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_outsourcing">Flight outsourcing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Flight outsourcing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On September 3, 2008, Midwest Airlines announced its plan to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outsource" title="Outsource">outsource</a> all of its flight operations to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_Airways" title="Republic Airways">Republic Airways</a>. Republic began operating 12 76-seat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embraer_ERJ-170" title="Embraer ERJ-170">Embraer ERJ-170</a> jets under the Midwest Connect name while Midwest returned its 25 <a href="/wiki/Boeing_717" title="Boeing 717">Boeing 717</a> planes to Boeing under a lease renegotiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MidwestRepublicPR_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-MidwestRepublicPR-25">[25]</a></sup> While this change caused the additional layoffs bringing the total of pink-slipped Midwest pilots to nearly 300<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ALPARepublicPR_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-ALPARepublicPR-26">[26]</a></sup> and total employee cuts for the year to 1,850,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Midwest indicated that it hoped to begin operating these new planes itself with Midwest crews in 8–12 months from that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MidwestRepublicPR_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-MidwestRepublicPR-25">[25]</a></sup> Additional aircraft from Republic were added to the operation in the form of larger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embraer_ERJ-190" title="Embraer ERJ-190">Embraer ERJ-190s</a> in August 2009.</p>
<p>The Midwest branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Line_Pilots_Association" title="Air Line Pilots Association">Air Line Pilots Association</a> launched a campaign protesting the Midwest outsourcing plan shortly after it was announced. They argued that the pilots had already made significant concessions to help Midwest Airlines survive and that the company's new contract offers represented draconian demands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dontoutsourcemidwest.com_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-dontoutsourcemidwest.com-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Merge_into_Frontier">Merge into Frontier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Merge into Frontier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In early 2010, parent company <a href="/wiki/Republic_Airways_Holdings" title="Republic Airways Holdings">Republic Airways Holdings</a> announced that its Frontier and Midwest Airlines brands would merge by October 2011. On April 13, Republic announced that the Frontier Airlines name would be kept.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newname_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-newname-2">[2]</a></sup> Parts of the Midwest brand were incorporated into the Frontier brand as part of the merger, namely the iconic Midwest cookie and the slogan of Midwest Airlines, "The Best Care In The Air."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newname_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-newname-2">[2]</a></sup> Additionally, several Embraer aircraft retained the Midwest livery into late 2013 until their retirement or reassignment by Republic.</p>
<p>On October 1, 2010, midwestairlines.com was shut down for future reservations (besides frequent flyer tickets), and shut down completely on October 28. Users were then redirected to Frontier Airlines' website.</p>
<p>Midwest's YX code was retired in early November 2010 and adopted by Republic Airlines.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Midwest_Miles">Midwest Miles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Midwest Miles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Midwest Airlines' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequent_flyer_program" title="Frequent flyer program">frequent flyer program</a> was called Midwest Miles. It maintained one <a href="/wiki/Airport_lounge" title="Airport lounge">airport lounge</a>, the <b>Best Care Club</b> at its Milwaukee hub in the D Concourse, until its closure on May 31, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Midwest was not a member of any <a href="/wiki/Airline_alliance" title="Airline alliance">airline alliance</a>, Midwest Miles were redeemable in the <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a> <a href="/wiki/SkyMiles" title="SkyMiles">SkyMiles</a> program, and vice versa. As of 2006<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Northwest route maps showed Midwest as a partner airline and Northwest (now Delta) pulled its non-hub flights out of Milwaukee.</p>
<p>Midwest Miles was unusual in that it had links to the Amtrak program. Midwest Miles members could transfer blocks of 5,000 miles (8,000 km), up to a maximum of 25,000 miles per year to Amtrak's program.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Amtrak points could be used for travel on Amtrak and United Airlines (ex Continental Airlines).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cookies">Cookies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cookies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Save-cookie.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="165" data-file-width="300" height="121" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5e/Save-cookie.jpg/220px-Save-cookie.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/5e/Save-cookie.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>

<p>A defining feature of the airline was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chocolate_chip_cookies" title="Chocolate chip cookies">chocolate chip cookies</a> baked on the aircraft and served during flights. The airline began serving the cookies after an employee experimented with different snacks on an empty leg of a charter flight.</p>
<p>The cookie was featured in Midwest advertisements, such as the "Save the Cookie" campaign in opposition to the proposed and failed <a href="/wiki/AirTran_Airways" title="AirTran Airways">AirTran</a> takeover. The cookie was served during professional baseball games at <a href="/wiki/Kauffman_Stadium" title="Kauffman Stadium">Kauffman Stadium</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Bucks" title="Milwaukee Bucks">Bucks</a> basketball and <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Admirals" title="Milwaukee Admirals">Admirals</a> hockey games at Milwaukee's <a href="/wiki/Bradley_Center" title="Bradley Center">Bradley Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cookie outlived the brand as it was briefly served on Frontier Airlines flights;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> the airline's branded cookie dough sold at Milwaukee-area grocery stores was subsequently re-labeled as a Frontier product until its discontinuation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Frontier announced in 2012 that the airline would discontinue its cookie service as a cost-per-value cut, and instead added Denver-based for-purchase snacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fleet">Fleet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Fleet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fleet_at_time_of_merger_into_Frontier">Fleet at time of merger into Frontier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Fleet at time of merger into Frontier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of November 2009<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, <a href="/wiki/Frontier_Airlines" title="Frontier Airlines">Frontier Airlines</a> pilots and crew operated five Airbus <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A319-100" title="A319-100">A319-100</a> aircraft for Midwest Airlines. All former Midwest Airlines <a href="/wiki/Boeing_717" title="Boeing 717">Boeing 717s</a> were retired on November 3, 2009. The majority of Midwest Airlines routes were operated by Midwest Connect through <a href="/wiki/Outsourcing" title="Outsourcing">outsourcing</a>, allowing Midwest to advertise and maintain a <a href="/wiki/Airway_(aviation)" title="Airway (aviation)">route</a> system similar to what existed prior to <a href="/wiki/Midwest_Air_Group" title="Midwest Air Group">Midwest Air Group</a>'s takeover by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Texas_Pacific_Group" title="Texas Pacific Group">Texas Pacific Group</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The Boeing 717 aircraft were replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A319" title="Airbus A319">Airbus A319</a> flown by Frontier Airlines and 15 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embraer_190" title="Embraer 190">Embraer 190</a> aircraft flown by <a href="/wiki/Republic_Airlines" title="Republic Airlines">Republic Airlines</a>. Ten of these aircraft were purchased from <a href="/wiki/US_Airways" title="US Airways">US Airways</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-euroinvestor.co.uk_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-euroinvestor.co.uk-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the merger with Frontier Airlines, it was announced that the Midwest Airlines fleet of Embraers would not be merged into Frontier Airlines <a href="/wiki/Air_operator%27s_certificate" title="Air operator's certificate">operating certificate</a>; the two types of aircraft would continue to be crewed and operated separately by the two airlines.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Onboard_services">Onboard services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Onboard services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Until 2002, Midwest Express served gourmet meals on china along with complimentary alcoholic beverages. With the cost-cutting programs instituted following the post-September 11 industry downturn, this was changed to a <a href="/wiki/Buy_on_board" title="Buy on board">buy on board</a> food program, "Best Care Cuisine," with breakfast, lunch, and dinner meals available for purchase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Throughout the airline's existence, warm chocolate chip cookies were served on most flights.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidents_and_accidents">Incidents and accidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Midwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Incidents and accidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<ul>
<li>On September 6, 1985, <a href="/wiki/Midwest_Express_Airlines_Flight_105" title="Midwest Express Airlines Flight 105">Midwest Express Flight 105</a> crashed shortly after takeoff from Milwaukee, bound for Atlanta. According to the <a href="/wiki/National_Transportation_Safety_Board" title="National Transportation Safety Board">NTSB</a> investigation, the crash was caused by improper control inputs by the pilots when the plane's right engine failed due to stress corrosion cracking. The improper reaction caused the aircraft to enter an uncommanded roll and accelerated stall. All 31 people on board died in the airline's sole fatal accident.</li>
<li>On September 11, 2001, Midwest Airlines Flight 7 narrowly missed a collision with <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_hijacking" title="Aircraft hijacking">hijacked</a> <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines_Flight_175" title="United Airlines Flight 175">United Airlines Flight 175</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a> at approximately 9:01 a.m. local time. The pilot of Midwest 7 was forced to maneuver the aircraft, under the direction of an <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_controller" title="Air traffic controller">air traffic controller</a>, out of the flight path of Flight 175. One minute later, Flight 175 hit the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_Attacks" title="September 11, 2001 Attacks">South Tower of the World Trade Center</a> killing all 65 passengers and crew on board and resulting in the collapse of the South Tower.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>On November 13, 2007, a Midwest Connect flight from <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a> bound for <a href="/wiki/Dayton,_Ohio" title="Dayton, Ohio">Dayton</a> was in a near-collision with a <a href="/wiki/United_Express" title="United Express">United Express</a> jet heading to <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greensboro" title="Greensboro">Greensboro</a> while flying over northern Indiana. <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_controller" title="Air traffic controller">Air traffic controllers</a> with Chicago Center directed the Midwest flight to begin its descent while traveling head-on towards the United Express CRJ a few thousand feet below. The planes came as close as 1.3 miles (2.1 km) apart horizontally and 600 feet (180 m) vertically. The Midwest Connect Dornier 328JET was just above the United Express aircraft and descending while they were closing in on each other.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://ap.google.com/article/ALeqM5idPXez08XA1XpXhNpMKPXjIyxyJQD8SV4Q6O0" rel="nofollow">[3]</a>. An audible TCAS alarm in the Midwest cockpit alerted the pilots of the proximity, allowing them to pull up in time to avert disaster.</li>
<li>On July 8, 2008, a Midwest charter MD-81 carrying Presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> made an emergency landing at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lambert_Field" title="Lambert Field">Lambert Field</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a>, after an <a href="/wiki/Evacuation_slide" title="Evacuation slide">evacuation slide</a> inside the plane underneath the tail in the <a href="/wiki/Airstair" title="Airstair">airstair</a> passageway deployed, interfering with the plane's control cables, which affected the control of the pitch of the plane. The pilot radioed the control tower "at this time we would like to declare an emergency and also have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aircraft_Rescue_and_Firefighting" title="Aircraft Rescue and Firefighting">CFR</a> [crash equipment] standing by in St. Louis." A preliminary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NTSB" title="NTSB">NTSB</a> report said it detected "marks consistent with rubbing of elevator control cables" and a broken railing that "impinged upon elevator control cables."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> No one was injured. At the time, Obama's main campaign plane a <a href="/wiki/North_American_Airlines" title="North American Airlines">North American Airlines</a> <a href="/wiki/Boeing_757" title="Boeing 757">Boeing 757</a> on lease by the dual Obama and McCain campaign contributing <a href="/wiki/MatlinPatterson" title="MatlinPatterson">MatlinPatterson</a> <a href="/wiki/Hedge_fund" title="Hedge fund">hedge fund</a> firm and owner directors of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Aero_Logistics" title="Global Aero Logistics">Global Aero Logistics</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D/b/a" title="D/b/a">d/b/a</a> <a href="/wiki/North_American_Airlines" title="North American Airlines">North American Airlines</a>, was being overhauled. It went back into campaign service on July 20.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p>Midwest Express' original livery consisted of dark blue on the upper half, and white on the lower half. The two were separated by blue and red <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cheatline" title="Cheatline">cheatlines</a> with white in between, which ran up the <a href="/wiki/Trailing_edge" title="Trailing edge">trailing edge</a> of the tail. The engines were white, and on the tail was a bold 'M' and a script 'E', representing the airline name, and the way the titles were printed on the fuselage. This scheme could still be found on one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beech_1900D" title="Beech 1900D">Beech 1900D</a> of <a href="/wiki/Midwest_Connect" title="Midwest Connect">Midwest Connect</a>, without the 'M E' on the tail, as late as 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1989, the airline started to add the <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_MD-80" title="McDonnell Douglas MD-80">McDonnell Douglas MD-80</a> to the fleet, initially in the same livery. In the mid-1990s, the livery was substantially revised. The top half of the aircraft remained blue (albeit slightly brighter), but the bottom half was repainted light grey, along with the engines, and they were now separated by a gold, white, and red cheatline. The tail logo also underwent minor changes, adding a circle around the lettering and the same cheatline from the fuselage connecting the circle to the leading edge of the tail. Despite the unchanged lettering on the tail, the titles on the fuselage were changed to all bold letters, rather than the script "Express" titles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003, Midwest Express Airlines began to create a new identity, as the first Boeing 717's were being delivered, and the DC-9 aircraft were being retired. The company started with removing the "Express" from its name (and thus, off the fuselage), and it designed a new logo that would help point it out as a representative of <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a>. The result was a logo that looked much like a wing, with a small 'M' inside of it. However, if the logo is turned on its side, it bears a resemblance to the <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Art_Museum" title="Milwaukee Art Museum">Milwaukee Art Museum</a>, designed by <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Calatrava" title="Santiago Calatrava">Santiago Calatrava</a>, which was being constructed at the same time. The Art Museum has risen as an icon of Milwaukee, and so the new Midwest Airlines felt this would be a good representation. Along with this new identity came a new livery. The bottom of the aircraft remained grey, while the top was repainted in a deeper blue, with essentially the same gold, white, and red cheatlines separating the two. On the lower half, there was also a blue swoop, starting at the front (looking much like another stripe), getting larger as it went back, until it covered the entire rear fuselage. The engines on these planes were painted in the same blue color, with four gold rings on the port engine, and three on the starboard. This was meant to represent the ranking of Captain and First Officer, along with where they sit. Only three MD-80 aircraft, registered N813ME, N822ME, and N823ME ever wore the full new livery; all other MD-80s wore a hybrid livery until retirement, combining the second Midwest Express livery with the current logo and titling of Midwest Airlines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In the transitional period, some MD-80s also received the new titles before the new tail logo, resulting in another variation on the livery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
