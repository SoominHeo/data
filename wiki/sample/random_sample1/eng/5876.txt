<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Media franchise</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>A <b>media franchise</b> is a set of related media in which several derivative works have been produced from an original creative work (usually that of <a href="/wiki/Fiction" title="Fiction">fiction</a>), such as a <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">film</a>, a work of <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">television program</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a>. A media franchise is characterized by its components generally sharing a common <a href="/wiki/Fictional_universe" title="Fictional universe">fictional universe</a>.</p>
<p>The intellectual property from the original work can be licensed to other parties or partners for further derivative works and commercial exploitation across a range of mediums and by a variety of industries for <a href="/wiki/Merchandising" title="Merchandising">merchandising</a> purposes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Multimedia_franchise"><span id="metaseries"></span> Multimedia franchise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Media_franchise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Multimedia franchise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <b>multimedia franchise</b> or <b>transmedia franchise</b> is a type of media franchise that typically consists of <a href="/wiki/Marketing_co-operation" title="Marketing co-operation">cross-marketing</a> across more than one medium. For the owners, the goal of increasing profit through diversity can extend the commercial profitability of the franchise and can create strong feelings of identity and ownership in its consumers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Aarseth describes the financial logic of cost-recovery for expensive productions by identifying that a single medium launch is a lost opportunity, the timeliness of the production and release is more important than its integrity, the releases should raise brand awareness and the cross-ability of the work is critical for its success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/American_Idol" title="American Idol">American Idol</a> was a transmedia franchise from its beginnings, with the first season winner <a href="/wiki/Kelly_Clarkson" title="Kelly Clarkson">Kelly Clarkson</a> signing with <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">RCA Records</a> and having the release of <i><a href="/wiki/A_Moment_Like_This" title="A Moment Like This">A Moment Like This</a></i> becoming a #1 hit on Billboard Hot 100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ai_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ai-3">[3]</a></sup> The success resulted in a nationwide concert tour, an <i>American Idol</i> book that made the bestseller list and the film <i><a href="/wiki/From_Justin_to_Kelly" title="From Justin to Kelly">From Justin to Kelly</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ai_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ai-3">[3]</a></sup> A transmedia franchise however is often referred to by the simpler term "media franchise." The term media franchise is often used to describe the popular adaptation of a work into films, like the popular <a href="/wiki/Twilight_(novel_series)" title="Twilight (novel series)"><i>Twilight</i> book series</a> that was adapted into the five films of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Twilight_Saga_(film_series)" title="The Twilight Saga (film series)">The Twilight Saga</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Other neologisms exist to describe various franchise types including "metaseries," which can be used to describe works such as <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Foundation_series" title="Foundation series"><i>Foundation</i> series</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Multimedia franchises usually develop through a character or fictional world becoming popular in one medium, and then expanding to others through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Licensing_agreement" title="Licensing agreement">licensing agreements</a>, with respect to <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property" title="Intellectual property">intellectual property</a> in the franchise's characters and settings. As one author explains, "[f]or the studios, a home-run is a film from which a multimedia 'franchise' can be generated; the colossally expensive creation of cross-media conglomerates predicated on synergistic rewards provides an obvious imperative to develop such products."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The trend later developed wherein franchises would be launched in multiple forms of media simultaneously:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In one of the most celebrated ventures in media convergence, Larry and Andy Wachowski, creators of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Matrix_trilogy" title="The Matrix trilogy">The Matrix trilogy</a></i>, produced the game <i><a href="/wiki/Enter_the_Matrix" title="Enter the Matrix">Enter the Matrix</a></i> (2003) simultaneously with the last two films of the trilogy, shooting scenes for the game on the movie's sets with the movie's actors, and releasing the game on the same day as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Matrix:_Reloaded" title="The Matrix: Reloaded">The Matrix: Reloaded</a></i>. Likewise, on September 21, 2004, <a href="/wiki/Lucasfilm" title="Lucasfilm">Lucasfilm</a> jointly released a new DVD box set of the original <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_Wars_trilogy" title="Star Wars trilogy">Star Wars trilogy</a></i> with <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_Battlefront" title="Star Wars: Battlefront">Star Wars: Battlefront</a></i>, a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Combat_game&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Combat game (page does not exist)">combat game</a> in which players can reenact battles from all six <i>Star Wars</i> films. In 2005, Peter Jackson likewise produced his blockbuster film <i><a href="/wiki/King_Kong_(2005_film)" title="King Kong (2005 film)">King Kong</a></i> (2005) in tandem with a successful <i>King Kong</i> game designed by Michael Ancel and published by Ubisoft. In the last several years, numerous licensed videogame adaptations of major summer and holiday blockbusters were released a few days before or a few days after their respective films, including: all three <i>Star Wars films</i> (1999–2005); all five <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_films" title="Harry Potter films">Harry Potter films</a></i> (2001–2008); all three <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spider-Man_films" title="Spider-Man films">Spider-Man films</a></i> (2002–2007); <i><a href="/wiki/Hulk_(film)" title="Hulk (film)">Hulk</a></i> (2003); <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Two_Towers" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Two Towers">The Lord of the Rings: The Two Towers</a></i> (2002); <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Return_of_the_King" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Return of the King">The Lord of the Rings: The Return of the King</a></i> (2003); <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Chronicles_of_Narnia:_The_Lion,_the_Witch,_and_the_Wardrobe" title="The Chronicles of Narnia: The Lion, the Witch, and the Wardrobe">The Chronicles of Narnia: The Lion, the Witch, and the Wardrobe</a></i> (2005); <i><a href="/wiki/Pirates_of_the_Caribbean:_Dead_Man%27s_Chest" title="Pirates of the Caribbean: Dead Man's Chest">Pirates of the Caribbean: Dead Man's Chest</a></i> (2006); <i><a href="/wiki/Pirates_of_the_Caribbean:_At_World%27s_End" title="Pirates of the Caribbean: At World's End">Pirates of the Caribbean: At World's End</a></i> (2007); and <i><a href="/wiki/Transformers" title="Transformers">Transformers</a></i> (2007). These multimedia franchises have made it more difficult to distinguish the production of films and videogames as separate enterprises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_to_other_forms">Development to other forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Media_franchise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development to other forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiction">Fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Media_franchise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Long-running film franchises were common in the early studio era, when Hollywood studios had actors and directors under long-term contract. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Andy_Hardy" title="Andy Hardy">Andy Hardy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ma_and_Pa_Kettle" title="Ma and Pa Kettle">Ma and Pa Kettle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulldog_Drummond" title="Bulldog Drummond">Bulldog Drummond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Superman" title="Superman">Superman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tarzan" title="Tarzan">Tarzan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Batman" title="Batman">Batman</a>. The longest-running modern film franchises include <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">James Bond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Godzilla" title="Godzilla">Godzilla</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Friday_the_13th_(franchise)" title="Friday the 13th (franchise)">Friday the 13th</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Nightmare_on_Elm_Street_(franchise)" title="A Nightmare on Elm Street (franchise)">A Nightmare on Elm Street</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek" title="Star Trek">Star Trek</a></i>. In such cases, even lead actors are often replaced as they age, lose interest, or their characters are killed.</p>
<p>Media franchises tend to cross over from their original media to other forms. Literary franchises are often transported to film, such as <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Drew" title="Nancy Drew">Nancy Drew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miss_Marple" title="Miss Marple">Miss Marple</a>, and other popular <a href="/wiki/Detective" title="Detective">detectives</a>, as well as popular <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">comic book</a> <a href="/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superheroes</a>. Television and film franchises are often expanded upon in <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novels</a>, particularly those in the <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> genres, such as <a href="/wiki/The_Twilight_Zone" title="The Twilight Zone">The Twilight Zone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Star_Trek" title="Star Trek">Star Trek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Who" title="Doctor Who">Doctor Who</a> and <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a>. Similarly, fantasy, science fiction films and television shows are frequently adapted into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animated_television_series" title="Animated television series">animated television series</a>, video games, or both.</p>
<p>A media franchise does not have to include the same characters or theme, as the brand identity can be the franchise, like <a href="/wiki/Square_Enix" title="Square Enix">Square Enix</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Final_Fantasy" title="Final Fantasy">Final Fantasy</a> or the <a href="/wiki/National_Lampoon,_Incorporated" title="National Lampoon, Incorporated"><i>National Lampoon</i> series</a>, and can suffer from critical failures even if the media fictional material is unrelated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-fiction">Non-fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Media_franchise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Non-fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Non-fiction" title="Non-fiction">Non-fiction</a> literary franchises include the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/...For_Dummies" title="...For Dummies">...For Dummies</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Complete_Idiot%27s_Guide_to..." title="The Complete Idiot's Guide to...">The Complete Idiot's Guide to...</a></i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reference_book" title="Reference book">reference books</a>. An enduring and extensive example of a media franchise is <a href="/wiki/Playboy_Enterprises" title="Playboy Enterprises">Playboy Enterprises</a>, which began expanding well beyond its successful magazine, <i><a href="/wiki/Playboy" title="Playboy">Playboy</a></i>, within a few years after its first publication, into such enterprises as a modeling agency, several television shows (<i>Playboy's Penthouse</i>, in 1959), and even its own <a href="/wiki/Playboy_TV" title="Playboy TV">television channel</a>. Twenty-five years later, Playboy released private clubs and restaurants, movie theaters, <a href="/wiki/Playboy_Radio" title="Playboy Radio">a radio show</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_to_video" title="Direct to video">direct to video</a> films, music and book publishing (including original works in addition to its anthologies of cartoons, photographs, recipes, advice, articles or fiction that had originally appeared in the magazine), footwear, clothing of every kind, jewelry, housewares (lamps, clocks, bedding, glassware), guitars and gambling, playing cards, pinball machines and pet accessories, billiard balls, bedroom appurtenances, enhancements, plus countless other items of merchandise.</p>
