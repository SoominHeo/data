<p><b>Taste buds</b> contain the taste receptor cells, which are also known as gustatory cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Taste_receptor" title="Taste receptor">taste receptors</a> are located around the small structures known as <a href="/wiki/Lingual_papillae" title="Lingual papillae">papillae</a> found on the upper surface of the <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">tongue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soft_palate" title="Soft palate">soft palate</a>, upper <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cheek" title="Cheek">cheek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Epiglottis" title="Epiglottis">epiglottis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> These structures are involved in detecting the five elements of <a href="/wiki/Taste" title="Taste">taste perception</a>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saltiness" title="Saltiness">salty</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sour" title="Sour">sour</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitterness_(taste)" title="Bitterness (taste)">bitter</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweet" title="Sweet">sweet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Umami" title="Umami">umami</a>; through the combination of these elements we detect "flavors." A popular myth assigns these different tastes to <a href="/wiki/Tongue_map" title="Tongue map">different regions of the tongue</a>; in reality these tastes can be detected by any area of the tongue. Via small openings in the tongue epithelium, called taste pores, parts of the food dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">saliva</a> come into contact with the taste receptors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> These are located on top of the taste receptor cells that constitute the taste buds. The taste receptor cells send information detected by clusters of various receptors and ion channels to the <a href="/wiki/Gustatory_cortex" title="Gustatory cortex">gustatory areas</a> of the brain via the seventh, ninth and tenth cranial nerves.</p>
<p>On average, the human tongue has 2,000–8,000 taste buds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_papillae">Types of papillae</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Taste_bud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of papillae">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The taste buds on the tongue sit on raised protrusions of the tongue surface called papillae. There are three types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lingual_papilla" title="Lingual papilla">lingual papillae</a> that contain taste buds present on the human tongue:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungiform_papillae" title="Fungiform papillae">Fungiform papillae</a> - as the name suggests, these are slightly <a href="/wiki/Mushroom" title="Mushroom">mushroom</a>-shaped if looked at in longitudinal section. These are present mostly at the dorsal surface of the tongue, as well as at the sides. Innervated by facial nerve.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foliate_papillae" title="Foliate papillae">Foliate papillae</a> - these are ridges and grooves towards the posterior part of the tongue found at the lateral borders. Innervated by <a href="/wiki/Facial_nerve" title="Facial nerve">facial nerve</a> (anterior papillae) and <a href="/wiki/Glossopharyngeal_nerve" title="Glossopharyngeal nerve">glossopharyngeal nerve</a> (posterior papillae).</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circumvallate_papillae" title="Circumvallate papillae">Circumvallate papillae</a> - there are only about 10 to 14 of these papillae on most people, and they are present at the back of the oral part of the tongue. They are arranged in a circular-shaped row just in front of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terminal_sulcus_(tongue)" title="Terminal sulcus (tongue)">sulcus terminalis of the tongue</a>. They are associated with ducts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Von_Ebner%27s_glands" title="Von Ebner's glands">Von Ebner's glands</a>, and are innervated by the glossopharyngeal nerve.</li>
</ul>
<p>The fourth type of papillae the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filiform_papillae" title="Filiform papillae">filiform papillae</a> are the most numerous but do not contain taste buds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> They are characterized by increased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keratinisation" title="Keratinisation">keratinisation</a> and are involved in the mechanical aspect of providing abrasion.</p>
<p>Salt, sweet, sour and umami tastes causes depolarization of the taste cells, although different mechanisms are applied.</p>
<p>Bitter causes an internal release of Ca<sup>2+</sup>, no external Ca<sup>2+</sup> is required.</p>
<p>The bud is formed by two kinds of cells: supporting cells and gustatory cells.</p>
<p>The <i>supporting (<a href="/wiki/Sustentacular_cell" title="Sustentacular cell">sustentacular cells</a>)</i> are mostly arranged like the staves of a cask, and form an outer envelope for the bud. Some, however, are found in the interior of the bud between the gustatory cells.</p>
<p>The <i>gustatory (taste) cells</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Chemoreceptor" title="Chemoreceptor">chemoreceptor</a>, occupy the central portion of the bud; they are spindle-shaped, and each possesses a large spherical nucleus near the middle of the cell.</p>
<p>The peripheral end of the cell terminates at the gustatory pore in a fine hair filament, the gustatory hair.</p>
<p>The central process passes toward the deep extremity of the bud, and there ends in single or <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bifurcation" title="wikt:bifurcation">bifurcated</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Varicosities" title="Varicosities">varicosities</a>.</p>
<p>The nerve fibrils after losing their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medullary_sheath" title="Medullary sheath">medullary sheaths</a> enter the taste bud, and end in fine extremities between the gustatory cells; other nerve fibrils ramify between the supporting cells and terminate in fine extremities; these, however, are believed to be nerves of ordinary sensation and not gustatory.</p>
<p>The average life of a taste bud is 10 days.</p>
