<p><b>Mir Akbar Khyber</b> (sometimes spelled <b>Khaibar</b>) (1925 – April 17, 1978) was an <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghan</a> intellectual and a leader of the <a href="/wiki/Parcham" title="Parcham">Parcham</a> faction of <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Democratic_Party_of_Afghanistan" title="People's Democratic Party of Afghanistan">People's Democratic Party of Afghanistan</a> (PDPA). His <a href="/wiki/Assassination" title="Assassination">assassination</a> led to the overthrow of <a href="/wiki/Mohammed_Daoud_Khan" title="Mohammed Daoud Khan">Mohammed Daoud Khan</a>'s republic, and to the advent of a socialist regime in Afghanistan, the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Afghanistan" title="Democratic Republic of Afghanistan">Democratic Republic of Afghanistan</a>.</p>
<p>Born in 1925, Khyber graduated from Harbi Pohantoon Military University in 1947. In 1950, he was imprisoned for his revolutionary activities. Later he was employed by the Ministry of education, until he was expelled from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paktia" title="Paktia">Paktia</a> for taking part in a riot in 1965. After returning to <a href="/wiki/Kabul" title="Kabul">Kabul</a>, he became editor of the Parcham newspaper, <i>Parcham</i>, and oversaw the Parchams recruitment program in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_of_Afghanistan" title="Military of Afghanistan">Afghan army</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-book-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was assassinated outside his home on 17 April 1978. The Daoud regime attempted to put the blame for Kyber's death on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hekmatyar" title="Hekmatyar">Hekmatyar</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Hezbi_Islami" title="Hezbi Islami">Hezbi Islami</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SH_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SH-2">[2]</a></sup> but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nur_Mohammad_Taraki" title="Nur Mohammad Taraki">Nur Mohammad Taraki</a> of the PDPA charged that the government itself was responsible, a belief that was shared by much of the Kabul intelligentsia. <a href="/wiki/Louis_Dupree_(professor)" title="Louis Dupree (professor)">Louis Dupree</a>, an American historian and specialist of Afghanistan, concluded that interior minister Nuristani, a virulent anti-communist, had ordered the killing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SH_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-SH-2">[2]</a></sup> However, several sources, including fellow parchamis <a href="/wiki/Babrak_Karmal" title="Babrak Karmal">Babrak Karmal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anahita_Ratebzad" title="Anahita Ratebzad">Anahita Ratebzad</a>, claimed that <a href="/wiki/Hafizullah_Amin" title="Hafizullah Amin">Hafizullah Amin</a>, a leader of the rival <a href="/wiki/Khalq" title="Khalq">Khalq</a> faction, was the instigator of the assassination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-book-1">[1]</a></sup> Daoud's confidant, Abdul Samad Ghaus, suggested that a strong rivalry existed between Amin and Khyber as they both attempted to infiltrate the military for their respective factions. Also, Khyber's attempts to reunite Khalq and Parcham cells within the military would have undermined Amin's power, according to communist sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SH_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-SH-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>At Khyber's funeral on April 19, some 15,000 PDPA sympathizers gathered in Kabul, and paraded through the streets chanting slogans against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIA" title="CIA">CIA</a> and the <a href="/wiki/SAVAK" title="SAVAK">SAVAK</a>, the Shah of Iran's secret police.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Alarmed by this demonstration of communist strength, Daoud ordered a crackdown on the PDPA leadership, which in turn prompted the PDPA to launch a military coup that became known as the <a href="/wiki/Saur_Revolution" title="Saur Revolution">Saur Revolution</a>, during which Daoud was killed, and the PDPA took power.</p>
