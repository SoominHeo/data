<p><b>Mirna</b> (<small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Slovene" title="Help:IPA for Slovene">[ˈmiːɾna]</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de"><i>Neydeck</i></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vista_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vista-3">[3]</a></sup> or <span lang="de"><i>Neudegg</i></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Topole1998-139_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Topole1998-139-4">[4]</a></sup>) is a nucleated village<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O_Mirni_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-O_Mirni-5">[5]</a></sup> and a minor economic centre<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Till2005_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Till2005-6">[6]</a></sup> in central <a href="/wiki/Lower_Carniola" title="Lower Carniola">Lower Carniola</a>, Slovenia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Topole1998-160_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Topole1998-160-7">[7]</a></sup> It is the largest settlement of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mirna_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mirna Valley (page does not exist)">Mirna Valley</a> and the centre of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mirna_(municipality)" title="Mirna (municipality)">Municipality of Mirna</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O_Mirni_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-O_Mirni-5">[5]</a></sup> It is situated at the crossing of regional roads and a confluence of several creeks with the <a href="/wiki/Mirna_(Sava)" title="Mirna (Sava)">Mirna River</a>, along the <a href="/wiki/Sevnica%E2%80%93Trebnje_Railway" title="Sevnica–Trebnje Railway">railway line linking Sevnica and Trebnje</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O_Mirni_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-O_Mirni-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>Mirna was first mentioned in 1180.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O_Mirni_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-O_Mirni-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mirna_(settlement)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The village lies at the westernmost part of the Mirna–Mokronog basin in the Mirna Valley, mainly at raised terraces safe from flooding. To the southwest from Mirna stretches the Vejar Basin, connected with the Mirna–Mokronog basin by a narrow glen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Topole1998-33_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Topole1998-33-8">[8]</a></sup> It is included into the statistical region of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southeastern_Slovenia_Statistical_Region" title="Southeastern Slovenia Statistical Region">Southeastern Slovenia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nose_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nose-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZUODNO-G_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZUODNO-G-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The crossroad of the regional roads R1-215, connecting Trebnje and <a href="/wiki/Mokronog" title="Mokronog">Mokronog</a>, and R2-417, connecting Mirna, <a href="/wiki/Tihaboj" title="Tihaboj">Tihaboj</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morav%C4%8De" title="Moravče">Moravče</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a> of the Lipoglavščica, the Zabrščica, and the Vejar Creeks with the Mirna are located in the settlement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O_Mirni_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-O_Mirni-5">[5]</a></sup> The part of the R1-215 road traversing Mirna was until July 1996 named <i>3rd Battalion of the Army of the National Security Street</i> (<span lang="sl"><i>Cesta III. bataljona VDV</i></span>) and is now named <i>Main Street</i> (<span lang="sl"><i>Glavna cesta</i></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The larger part of the older part of Mirna is situated on the left bank of the river, and a smaller part on the right bank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nose_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nose-9">[9]</a></sup> Both parts are connected with a stone bridge dating to the 18th century and a wooden footbridge next to it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nose_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nose-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smuk2010-05-21_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smuk2010-05-21-13">[13]</a></sup> The oldest part of the town with St. John's the Baptist church lies at the foot and the southwestern slope of the Trbinc Hill, whereas the younger part has grown at the ridge on the other side of the Mirna river, which turns 90° here.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Topole1998-150_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Topole1998-150-14">[14]</a></sup> The lowest parts along the Mirna banks have been settled by industry, which employs people from the whole basin as well as the nearby hilly areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Topole1998-139_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Topole1998-139-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">

Mirna Pond</div>

<p>Due to its basin location and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperature_inversion" title="Temperature inversion">temperature inversion</a>, Mirna has a bit lower average temperatures (around 9 °C or 48 °F) and more hot (above 25 °C or 77 °F) and cold (below 0 °C or 32 °F) days than the villages on hills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Topole1998-66_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Topole1998-66-15">[15]</a></sup> There is also less wind, the predominant being the north and the southwestern wind. There is more fog, particularly from August to January.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Topole1998-72_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Topole1998-72-16">[16]</a></sup> The average annual precipitation is 1,165 mm (45.9 in), classifying the climate of the area as moist. It occurs all over the year, least in winter (January and February) and more in summer (June, July, and August) as well as in autumn (November).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Topole1998-74_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Topole1998-74-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Outside the village, near the western border of the settlement,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Geopedia-V1-Mirna_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Geopedia-V1-Mirna-1">[1]</a></sup> stands <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mirna_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mirna Castle (page does not exist)">Mirna Castle</a> (<span lang="sl"><i>Grad Mirna</i></span>). It is situated on a relief pier, at the extreme edge of the Upper Hill (<span lang="sl"><i>Gorenjska Gora</i></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Mirna Pond (<span lang="sl"><i>ribnik Mirna</i></span>), used as a swimming pool in the past and now primarily by fishermen,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> is situated under the castle. It was created at the end of the 1960s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VK2007-06-14_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-VK2007-06-14-20">[20]</a></sup> by damming of the Vejar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nose_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nose-9">[9]</a></sup> A jogging path leads past it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VK2007-06-14_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-VK2007-06-14-20">[20]</a></sup> There are also the confluence of the Mirna and the Vejar<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SitulaGrad_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-SitulaGrad-21">[21]</a></sup> and a bridge across the Mirna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smuk2010-05-21_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smuk2010-05-21-13">[13]</a></sup> A plain with <a href="/wiki/Flood-meadow" title="Flood-meadow">flood-meadows</a> stretches from the castle to the village.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mirna Cave (<span lang="sl"><i>Jama pri Mirni</i></span>), also known as Roje Cave (<span lang="sl"><i>Jama na Rojah</i></span>), (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Mirna_%28settlement%29&amp;params=45.95596_N_15.05959_E_scale:500"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">45°57′21″N</span> <span class="longitude">15°03′35″E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">45.95596°N 15.05959°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">45.95596; 15.05959</span></span></span></a></span>), is a fossil (i.e. not forming anymore) and isolated <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">karst</a> spring cave<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> situated in the northern part of the settlement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Geopedia-jama_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Geopedia-jama-25">[25]</a></sup> It is 115 metres (377 ft) long and 7 m (23 ft) deep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Geopedia-jama_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Geopedia-jama-25">[25]</a></sup> It was discovered on 8 September 1939 by the Underground Cave Exploration Society (<span lang="sl"><i>Društvo za raziskavanje podzemskih jam</i></span>) from Ljubljana.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Geopedia-jama_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Geopedia-jama-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> It serves as an illegal waste dump.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy_and_demographics">Economy and demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mirna_(settlement)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economy and demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Until the middle of the 19th century, the inhabitants made their living by farming. Then, a spinning mill and a tannery appeared in the settlement. Since 1908, a railway has connected Mirna with the towns of <a href="/wiki/Trebnje" title="Trebnje">Trebnje</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sevnica" title="Sevnica">Sevnica</a>, with a station in the southern part of the settlement. The settlement was electrified after 1920.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O_Mirni_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-O_Mirni-5">[5]</a></sup> Due to the development of infrastructure, the existence of trades, the start of industry, and a vision, Mirna surpassed nearby <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0entrupert" title="Šentrupert">Šentrupert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mokronog" title="Mokronog">Mokronog</a> as the centre of the Mirna Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O_Mirni_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-O_Mirni-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nose_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nose-9">[9]</a></sup> Currently, Mirna is industrially the most developed settlement in the valley with the largest number of workplaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O_Mirni_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-O_Mirni-5">[5]</a></sup> Major companies in Mirna include <a href="/wiki/Dana_(Mirna)" title="Dana (Mirna)">Dana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Droga_Kolinska" title="Droga Kolinska">Droga Kolinska</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tomplast&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tomplast (page does not exist)">Tomplast</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=JGZ_Pohorje&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="JGZ Pohorje (page does not exist)">JGZ Pohorje</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nose_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Nose-9">[9]</a></sup> Tourism has been gaining in importance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O_Mirni_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-O_Mirni-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 1961, the number of inhabitants in Mirna has tripled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nose_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-Nose-9">[9]</a></sup> It was 292 in 1869, 274 in 1900, 417 in 1931, 550 in 1961, 882 in 1971, 1183 in 1981, 1500 in 1991, 1465 in 2002, and 1374 in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O_Mirni_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-O_Mirni-5">[5]</a></sup> The local dialect is the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Lower_Carniolan_subdialect" title="Eastern Lower Carniolan subdialect">Eastern Lower Carniolan subdialect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p>The main sports practiced in Mirna are <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a>, <a href="/wiki/Badminton" title="Badminton">badminton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ski_jumping" title="Ski jumping">ski jumping</a>. The Mirna Football Club was established in 1967 and as of June 2012<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mirna_(settlement)&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> has about 120 registered players, up to 14 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DL2012-06-17_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-DL2012-06-17-29">[29]</a></sup> The Mirna Badminton Club was established in 1991 and reaches the highest places among Slovenian clubs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DL2012-06-17_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-DL2012-06-17-29">[29]</a></sup> The most known ski jumper from Mirna is <a href="/wiki/Maja_Vti%C4%8D" title="Maja Vtič">Maja Vtič</a>, who placed fifth in the <a href="/wiki/FIS_Nordic_World_Ski_Championships_2011" title="FIS Nordic World Ski Championships 2011">2011 World Championship</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Ski_jumping_hill" title="Ski jumping hill">ski jumping hill</a> stands behind the building of the Mirna Post Office in the western part of the settlement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DL2012-06-17_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-DL2012-06-17-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> It is eight meters (26 ft) tall and was completed in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Municipal_centre">Municipal centre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mirna_(settlement)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Municipal centre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mirna was a municipal centre before World War II. After the war, it was a municipal centre from the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Slovenia" title="Municipalities of Slovenia">municipalities in Slovenia</a> in 1952 until 1959, when the Municipality of Mirna merged with the <a href="/wiki/Municipality_of_Trebnje" title="Municipality of Trebnje">Municipality of Trebnje</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nose_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-Nose-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2009, residents of the <a href="/wiki/Parish_(administrative_division)" title="Parish (administrative division)">parish</a> of Mirna voted in a <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">referendum</a> in support of secession from the Municipality of Trebnje and the establishment of an independent municipality,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> but the act enacting this was rejected in April 2010 by the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_(Slovenia)" title="National Assembly (Slovenia)">National Assembly</a>, mainly because it also included the establishment of the Municipality of <a href="/wiki/Ankaran" title="Ankaran">Ankaran</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 1 February 2011, the National Assembly passed another act on the establishment of the Municipality of Mirna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> That happened after the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Court_of_Slovenia" title="Constitutional Court of Slovenia">Constitutional Court of Slovenia</a> had ordered in December 2010 the National Assembly to establish the Municipality of Mirna within two months and to call the election to its municipal council within 20 days after the establishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Mirna regained its position as a municipal centre on 26 February 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nose_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-Nose-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZUODNO-G_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ZUODNO-G-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>The main sights in Mirna are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mirna_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mirna Castle (page does not exist)">Mirna Castle</a> (<a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">Slovene</a>: <span lang="sl"><i>Grad Mirna</i></span>), a 12th-century castle, destroyed by the <a href="/wiki/Slovene_Partisans" title="Slovene Partisans">Partisans</a> in December 1942 and restored since 1962 by <a href="/wiki/Marko_Marin_(professor)" title="Marko Marin (professor)">Marko Marin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SitulaGrad_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-SitulaGrad-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dedi.C5.A1.C4.8Dina_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dedi.C5.A1.C4.8Dina-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li>The central bridge over the Mirna River, part of the main road from Trebnje to Mokronog. It was built in the 18th century as a wooden bridge on the order of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maria_Theresa_of_Austria" title="Maria Theresa of Austria">Maria Theresa of Austria</a>, and rebuilt in stone in the second half of the 19th century due to heavy traffic and the danger of flooding. There is a chapel on its west pier dedicated to the <a href="/wiki/Christian_cross" title="Christian cross">Holy Cross</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nose_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-Nose-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SitulaMost_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-SitulaMost-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li>The core of the village, along the Mirna River and the bridge below the parish church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SitulaJedro_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-SitulaJedro-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a> dedicated to <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">Saint John the Baptist</a> containing 15th-century and 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">frescos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dedi.C5.A1.C4.8Dina_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dedi.C5.A1.C4.8Dina-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SitulaKrst_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-SitulaKrst-41">[41]</a></sup> It is on a raised terrace at the western border of the core of the village, at the foot of Trbinc Hill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dedi.C5.A1.C4.8Dina_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dedi.C5.A1.C4.8Dina-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SitulaKrst_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-SitulaKrst-41">[41]</a></sup> The parish was established in 1863.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zgodovina_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zgodovina-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Ocvirk <a href="/wiki/Hayrack" title="Hayrack">hayrack</a> (Slovene: <span lang="sl"><i>Ocvirkov kozolec</i></span>), at the southern entrance to Mirna to the left from the main road from Trebnje. It is a single stretched hayrack with ten frames. Between the third and the fifth pillar, it has a projecting roof. The pillars are made from oak, and the laths and the roofing from spruce and fir. It has simple roofing, covered with a double-pitched roof. Originally, it was thatched; however, since 1985, it has been covered with corrugated <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a>-cement roof panels. The hayrack was erected in the second half of the 19th century, on the right side of the road, but was moved to the left side in 1906 after the construction of the railroad started in 1908. A <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">dendrochronological</a> investigation, published in 2006, has shown that it was significantly renovated after 1923. It is part of the Path to Laze 1 homestead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SitulaKozolec_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-SitulaKozolec-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=National_Liberation_Struggle_(Slovenia)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="National Liberation Struggle (Slovenia) (page does not exist)">National Liberation Struggle</a> (NOB) monument on the prominence of Roje in the northeastern part of the settlement, above the road from Mirna to Mokronog. It is an <a href="/wiki/Obelisk" title="Obelisk">obelisk</a> of three concrete pillars, joined together in the middle by a metal sphere. 106 combatants are buried in the vault. The monument was erected in 1965 based on plans by the architect <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Janez_Lenassi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Janez Lenassi (page does not exist)">Janez Lenassi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li>A church dedicated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helena_(Empress)" title="Helena (Empress)">Saint Helena</a>, built in the second half of the 18th century. It is in the extreme eastern part of the settlement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SitulaHelena_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-SitulaHelena-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Zapuže Manor (Slovene: <span lang="sl"><i>Graščina Zapuže</i></span> or <span lang="sl"><i>Grad Zapuže</i></span>, German: <span lang="de"><i>Schneckenbüchel</i></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vista_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vista-3">[3]</a></sup>), in the southeastern part of Mirna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SitulaZapu.C5.BEe_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-SitulaZapu.C5.BEe-47">[47]</a></sup> It was built by Erhard von Pelzhofer in 1578 and is said to be named after a large snail found when the foundations were laid (but this is not certain).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In the 17th century, it was a refuge of <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutherans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zgodovina_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zgodovina-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li>The house at Main Street no. 10 (<a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">Slovene</a>: <span lang="sl"><i>Glavna cesta 10</i></span>), in the southwestern part of the village, along the road to Trebnje. This is a two-story stone house from the first half of the 19th century with a five-axis facade and a wooden balcony on the courtyard side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SitulaGlavna10_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-SitulaGlavna10-49">[49]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Kramar farm (Slovene: <span lang="sl"><i>Kramarjeva domačija</i></span>), built in the first quarter of the 20th century. It is in the northeastern part of the settlement's nucleus, along the road to Trebnje.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SitulaKramar_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-SitulaKramar-50">[50]</a></sup></li>
<li>The two-story building of the Francka Inn (Slovene: <span lang="sl"><i>Gostilna Pri Francki</i></span>), in the southwestern part of the settlement, along the main road. It was erected in 1899, and has a five-axis street facade and a stone portal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>The two-story building of the Kolar Inn (Slovene: <span lang="sl"><i>Gostilna Kolar</i></span>), in the southwestern part of the settlement, along the road to Trebnje. It was erected in the second half of the 19th century, and has a five-axis street <a href="/wiki/Historicism_(art)" title="Historicism (art)">historicistic</a> facade and a stone portal, closed with a segment arch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>The house on Laze Road (Slovene: <span lang="sl"><i>Pot na Laze</i></span>) no. 1, near the Church of St. John the Baptist, in the southwestern part of the settlement. This is a single-story house, built from stone and into a slope in 1862.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Zaplatar farm (Slovene: <span lang="sl"><i>Zaplatarjeva domačija</i></span>), built in the 19th century, in the extreme eastern part of the settlement, near the Church of Saint Helena. It consists of a two-story stone-built house from 1838, a single-story outbuilding, a workshop, a smaller workshop with a shed, a <a href="/wiki/Hayrack" title="Hayrack">toplar</a> (double-rack hayrack with five pairs of frames, built in 1855, and a well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Böhm Mansion (Slovene: <span lang="sl"><i>Böhmova vila</i></span>), in the western part of the settlement, opposite the elementary school, at Fužine Street (Slovene: <span lang="sl"><i>Cesta na Fužine</i></span>) no. 2. It is a two-story building with a cellar, built in 1908 based on the plans of an unknown German architect, and has well-preserved furniture. It was built by L. Böhm, a professor at the Faculty of Maritime Studies in <a href="/wiki/Rijeka" title="Rijeka">Rijeka</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></li>
<li>The mansion at Fužine Street no. 8, in the western part of the settlement. It was built in 1930 in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neoromantic" title="Neoromantic">neoromantic</a> style based on plans by the factory owner A. Kolenc and plans by the engineer Šmidt. It has a corner tower, a <a href="/wiki/Veranda" title="Veranda">veranda</a> with arcades, and a front staircase. A garage and a double hayrack with sides of two different heights (Slovene: <span lang="sl">kozolec na psa</span>) stand next to the house.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Škarjat farm (Slovene: <span lang="sl"><i>Škarjatova domačija</i></span>), at Main Street no. 21, in the core of the old part of the settlement next to the main road. It consists of a single-story stone house from 1844, an outbuilding with a barn, a pigsty with a workshop, and a double hayrack (<i>toplar</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
<li>The single-story stone building of the Kolenc Inn (Slovene: <span lang="sl"><i>Gostilna Kolenc</i></span>), in the village core, next to the crossing of the main road and the road to Fužine. It has a <a href="/wiki/Dormer" title="Dormer">dormer</a> and a five-axis street facade. There is a stone portal in its central axis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_people">Notable people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mirna_(settlement)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Notable people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following prominent <a href="/wiki/Slovenes" title="Slovenes">Slovenes</a> have been associated with Mirna:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lojze_Grozde" title="Lojze Grozde">Lojze Grozde</a> (1923–1943), a Roman Catholic martyr, killed in Mirna during World War II by the Partisans;</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Marjan_Jenko&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Marjan Jenko (page does not exist)">Marjan Jenko</a> (1913–2006), a lawyer, mayor of Ljubljana from 1960 to 1961;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rado_Len%C4%8Dek" title="Rado Lenček">Rado Lenček</a> (1921–2005), a linguist, a cultural historian, and an ethnologist;</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sandi_Leskovec&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sandi Leskovec (page does not exist)">Sandi Leskovec</a> (1936–1997), a naïve sculptor;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marko_Marin_(professor)" title="Marko Marin (professor)">Marko Marin</a> (born 1930), a theatre director, an art historian, a professor and a restaurator;</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Franjo_Neubauer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Franjo Neubauer (page does not exist)">Franjo Neubauer</a> (1872–1945), a poet and a priest;</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ivan_Rostan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ivan Rostan (page does not exist)">Ivan Rostan</a> (1872–1957), an economist;</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ivan_%C5%A0karja&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ivan Škarja (page does not exist)">Ivan Škarja</a> (1879–1941), a lawyer;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maja_Vti%C4%8D" title="Maja Vtič">Maja Vtič</a> (born 1988), a ski jumper, the national champion in 2011.</li>
</ul>
