<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mirepoix (cuisine)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>mirepoix</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɪər/ 'ear' in 'near'">ɪər</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i>meer-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">PWAH</span></span></b></i></a></span>; <small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[miʁˈpwa]</a></span>) is a roughly chopped vegetable cut, usually a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Onion" title="Onion">onions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carrot" title="Carrot">carrots</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Celery" title="Celery">celery</a> (either common pascal celery or <a href="/wiki/Celeriac" title="Celeriac">celeriac</a>); the traditional ratio is two parts onions, one part carrots, and one part celery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-CIA-1">[1]</a></sup> Mirepoix, raw, roasted, or <a href="/wiki/Saut%C3%A9ing" title="Sautéing">sautéed</a> with butter or olive oil, is the flavor base for a wide variety of dishes, such as <a href="/wiki/Stock_(food)" title="Stock (food)">stocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soup" title="Soup">soups</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stew" title="Stew">stews</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sauce" title="Sauce">sauces</a>.</p>
<p>Similar flavor bases include the Italian <i>soffritto</i>, the Spanish <i><a href="/wiki/Sofrito" title="Sofrito">sofrito</a></i>, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lusosphere" title="Lusosphere">Portuguese-speaking</a> nations <i>refogado</i> (braised onions, garlic, and tomato), the German <i>Suppengrün</i> (leeks, carrots, and celeriac), the Polish <i>włoszczyzna</i> (leeks, carrots, celery root, and parsley root), the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Cajun_cuisine" title="Cajun cuisine">Cajun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Creole_cuisine" title="Louisiana Creole cuisine">Creole</a> <a href="/wiki/Holy_trinity_(cuisine)" title="Holy trinity (cuisine)">holy trinity</a> (onions, celery, and bell peppers), and the <a href="/wiki/French_cuisine" title="French cuisine">French</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Duxelles" title="Duxelles">duxelles</a></i> (onions, shallots, and mushrooms, sauteed in butter).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mirepoix_(cuisine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though the cooking technique is probably older, the term "mirepoix" dates from the 18th century and derives, as do many other appellations in French cuisine,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> from the aristocratic employer of the cook credited with establishing and stabilizing it: in this case,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles-Pierre-Gaston_Fran%C3%A7ois_de_L%C3%A9vis,_duc_de_L%C3%A9vis-Mirepoix" title="Charles-Pierre-Gaston François de Lévis, duc de Lévis-Mirepoix">Charles-Pierre-Gaston François de Lévis, duc de Lévis-Mirepoix</a> (1699–1757), French field marshal and ambassador and a member of the noble family of Lévis, lords of <a href="/wiki/Mirepoix,_Ari%C3%A8ge" title="Mirepoix, Ariège">Mirepoix</a> in <a href="/wiki/Languedoc" title="Languedoc">Languedoc</a> since the 11th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Larousse" title="Pierre Larousse">Pierre Larousse</a> (quoted in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_Companion_to_Food" title="Oxford Companion to Food">Oxford Companion to Food</a></i>), the unfortunate Duke of Mirepoix was "an incompetent and mediocre individual...who owed his vast fortune to the affection <a href="/wiki/Louis_XV_of_France" title="Louis XV of France">Louis XV</a> felt toward his wife and who had but one claim to fame: he gave his name to a sauce made of all kinds of meat and a variety of seasonings":</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The term is not encountered regularly in French culinary texts until the 19th century, so it is difficult to know what a dish <i>à la mirepoix</i> was like in 18th-century France. <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Beauvilliers" title="Antoine Beauvilliers">Beauvilliers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> for instance, in 1814, gives a short recipe for a Sauce à la Mirepoix which is a buttery, wine-laced stock garnished with an aromatic mixture of carrots, onions, and a <i><a href="/wiki/Bouquet_garni" title="Bouquet garni">bouquet garni</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Marie-Antoine_Car%C3%AAme" title="Marie-Antoine Carême">Carême</a>, in the 1830s, gives a similar recipe, calling it simply Mire-poix; and, by the mid-19th century, <a href="/wiki/Jules_Gouff%C3%A9" title="Jules Gouffé">Gouffé</a> refers to a mirepoix as "a term in use for such a long time that I do not hesitate to use it here". His mirepoix is listed among essences and, indeed, is a meaty concoction (laced with two bottles of <a href="/wiki/Madeira_wine" title="Madeira wine">Madeira</a>!), which, like all other essences, was used to enrich many a classic sauce. By the end of the 19th century, the mirepoix had taken on its modern meaning and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Favre" title="Joseph Favre">Joseph Favre</a> in his <i>Dictionnaire universel de cuisine</i> (<i>circa</i> 1895, reprinted 1978) uses the term to describe a mixture of ham, carrots, onions, and herbs used as an aromatic condiment when making sauces or braising meat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alan_Davidson_1999_p._509_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alan_Davidson_1999_p._509-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Matignon_(cuisine)" title="Matignon (cuisine)"><i>matignon</i></a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alan_Davidson_1999_p._509_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Alan_Davidson_1999_p._509-6">[6]</a></sup> is very similar to the mirepoix, except that the <i>matignon</i> is designed to be brought to the table and eaten with the dish or alone as a side dish.</p>
<p>According to the 1938 <i><a href="/wiki/Larousse_Gastronomique" title="Larousse Gastronomique">Larousse Gastronomique</a></i>, a mirepoix may be prepared "<i>au gras</i>" (with meat) or "<i>au maigre</i>" ("lean").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <i>Mirepoix au maigre</i> is sometimes called a <i><a href="/wiki/Brunoise" title="Brunoise">brunoise</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> (though strictly speaking this term more accurately merely designates the technique of cutting into <a href="/wiki/Dicing" title="Dicing">small dices</a> with a knife). A <i>mirepoix au gras</i> contains diced ham or pork belly as an additional ingredient. Similar combinations, both in and out of the <a href="/wiki/French_cuisine" title="French cuisine">French culinary</a> repertoire, may include leeks, parsnips, garlic, tomatoes, shallots, mushrooms, bell peppers, chilies, and ginger, according to the requirements of the regional cuisine or the instructions of the particular chef or recipe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The analogous <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soffritto" title="Soffritto">soffritto</a></i> (frequently containing parsley) is the basis for many traditional dishes in classic Italian cuisine, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Sofrito" title="Sofrito">sofrito</a></i> serves a similar purpose in Spanish cuisines. In Cajun and Creole cuisine, a mirepoix or (jocularly so-called) <a href="/wiki/Holy_trinity_(cuisine)" title="Holy trinity (cuisine)">"holy trinity"</a> is a combination of onions, celery, and bell peppers.</p>
<p>Traditionally, the weight ratio for mirepoix is 2:1:1 of onions, celery, and carrots;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-CIA-1">[1]</a></sup> the ratio for bones to mirepoix for stock is 10:1.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> When making a white stock, or <i>fond blanc</i>, <a href="/wiki/Parsnip" title="Parsnip">parsnips</a> are used instead of carrots to maintain the pale color.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_versions">International versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mirepoix_(cuisine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: International versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="German_Suppengr.C3.BCn">German <i>Suppengrün</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mirepoix_(cuisine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: German Suppengrün">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Schnittlauch_und_Zwiebeln_(23199864535).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Schnittlauch_und_Zwiebeln_%2823199864535%29.jpg/220px-Schnittlauch_und_Zwiebeln_%2823199864535%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Schnittlauch_und_Zwiebeln_%2823199864535%29.jpg/330px-Schnittlauch_und_Zwiebeln_%2823199864535%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Schnittlauch_und_Zwiebeln_%2823199864535%29.jpg/440px-Schnittlauch_und_Zwiebeln_%2823199864535%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Schnittlauch_und_Zwiebeln_(23199864535).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Chopped chives and onions</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Suppengrün</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ˈzʊpm̩ˌɡʁyːn]</a></span> means soup greens in German, and the Dutch equivalent is <i>soepgroente</i>. Soup greens usually come in a bundle and consists of a leek, a carrot, and a piece of celeriac. It may also contain parsley, thyme, celery leaves, <a href="/wiki/Rutabaga" title="Rutabaga">rutabaga</a> (swede), parsley root, and onions. The mix depends on regional traditions, as well as individual recipes. The vegetables used are cold-climate roots and bulbs with long shelf lives. <i>Suppengrün</i> act as herbs and impart hearty, strong flavors to the soup or sauce, providing a foil for other strong tasting ingredients such as dried peas and beans or pot roast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Large chunks of vegetables are slow cooked to make flavorful soups and stocks, and are discarded when the vegetables have given up most of their flavor. Finely chopped <i>Suppengrün</i> are browned in fat and used as a basis for a finished sauce. The vegetables may also be cooked long enough until they fall apart, and may become part of the sauce or pureed to form the sauce.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Italian_soffritto">Italian <i>soffritto</i><span id="Italian_Soffritto"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mirepoix_(cuisine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Italian soffritto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Italian_cuisine" title="Italian cuisine">Italian</a> version of mirepoix is called <i>soffritto</i> (not to be confused with the Spanish <i>sofrito</i>), a base of finely chopped parsley and onion sauted in lard, but most modern cooks substitute olive oil or butter. Garlic, celery, or carrot may also be included.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hazan168_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hazan168-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the Italian restaurateur Benedetta Vitali, <i>soffritto</i> means "underfried" and describes it as "a preparation of lightly browned minced vegetables, not a dish by itself." At one time it was called "false <a href="/wiki/Ragout" title="Ragout">ragout</a>", because <i>soffritto</i> was thought to vaguely recall the flavor of meat sauce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

A typical set of soup greens, known as <i><span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl">włoszczyzna</span></i>, used in <a href="/wiki/Polish_cuisine" title="Polish cuisine">Polish cuisine</a>: carrots, parsley root and leaves, leek, and celeriac. Bay leaves and allspice grains are also shown.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i><span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl">Włoszczyzna</span></i> <span class="IPA" style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Polish" title="Help:IPA for Polish">[vwɔʂˈt͡ʂɨzna]</a></span> is the Polish word for soup vegetables or greens. The word literally means "Italian stuff" because Queen <a href="/wiki/Bona_Sforza" title="Bona Sforza">Bona Sforza</a>, who was Italian and married Polish King <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_I_the_Old" title="Sigismund I the Old">Sigismund I the Old</a> in 1518, introduced this concept to Poland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> A <i>włoszczyzna</i> may consist of carrots, parsnips or parsley root, celery root or celeriac, leeks, and savoy or white cabbage leaves, and sometimes celery leaves and flat-leaf parsley. The most typical, packaged combination is celery root, parsley root, carrots, and leeks. <i>Włoszczyzna</i> is usually tied up and boiled to form a flavour base for soups and stews.</p>
