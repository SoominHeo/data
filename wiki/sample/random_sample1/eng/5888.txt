<p><b>Miriam Margolyes</b>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Officer_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Officer of the Order of the British Empire">OBE</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɑːr/ 'ar' in 'far'">ɑːr</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/əl/ 'le' in 'bottle'">əl</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>; born 18 May 1941)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailymail1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-dailymail1-1">[1]</a></sup> is an English <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Character_actress" title="Character actress">character actress</a> and voice artist. Her earliest roles were in theatre and after several supporting roles in film and television she won a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award" title="BAFTA Award">BAFTA Award</a> for her role in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Age_of_Innocence_(1993_film)" title="The Age of Innocence (1993 film)">The Age of Innocence</a></i> (1993) and went on to take the role of <a href="/wiki/Hogwarts_staff" title="Hogwarts staff">Professor Sprout</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_(film_series)" title="Harry Potter (film series)"><i>Harry Potter</i> film series</a>.</p>
<p>For many years she has divided her time between England and Australia, and she has starred in television shows in both countries, including the Australian premiere of the 2013 play <i><a href="/wiki/I%27ll_Eat_You_Last:_A_Chat_with_Sue_Mengers" title="I'll Eat You Last: A Chat with Sue Mengers">I'll Eat You Last</a></i>. In 2013 she achieved Dual-citizenship by becoming an Australian citizen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miriam_Margolyes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Margolyes was born in <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>, England, the only child of Ruth (née Walters; 1905–1974), a property investor and developer, and Joseph Margolyes (1899–1996), a physician from <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailymail1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-dailymail1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> She grew up in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> family;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> her ancestors migrated to the UK from Poland and <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>. Her great-grandfather, Symeon Sandmann, was born in the town of <a href="/wiki/Margonin" title="Margonin">Margonin</a> in central-western Poland, which Margolyes visited in 2013.</p>
<p>She attended <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_High_School_(Oxford)" title="Oxford High School (Oxford)">Oxford High School</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailymail1_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-dailymail1-1">[1]</a></sup> from 1955 until 1959, and later <a href="/wiki/Newnham_College,_Cambridge" title="Newnham College, Cambridge">Newnham College, Cambridge</a>, where she read English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> There, in her twenties, she began acting and appeared in productions by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_Footlights" title="Cambridge Footlights">Cambridge Footlights</a> comedy troupe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acting_career">Acting career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miriam_Margolyes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Acting career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>With her distinctive voice, Margolyes first gained recognition for her work as a voice artist. She recorded a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft-porn" title="Soft-porn">soft-porn</a> audio called <i>Sexy Sonia: Leaves from my Schoolgirl Notebook</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> She performed most of the supporting female characters in the dubbed Japanese action TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Monkey_(TV_series)" title="Monkey (TV series)">Monkey</a></i>. She also worked with the theatre company Gay Sweatshop and provided voiceovers in the Japanese TV series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Water_Margin_(1973_TV_series)" title="The Water Margin (1973 TV series)">The Water Margin</a></i> (credited as Mirium Margolyes)<i>.</i></p>
<p>She plays recurring character Prudence Stanley in the Australian-based TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Miss_Fisher%27s_Murder_Mysteries" title="Miss Fisher's Murder Mysteries">Miss Fisher's Murder Mysteries</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1974, she appeared with <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Williams" title="Kenneth Williams">Kenneth Williams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ted_Ray_(comedian)" title="Ted Ray (comedian)">Ted Ray</a> in the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2" title="BBC Radio 2">BBC Radio 2</a> comedy series <i>The Betty Witherspoon Show</i>.</p>
<p>Margolyes' first major role in a film was as Elephant Ethel in <i><a href="/wiki/Stand_Up,_Virgin_Soldiers" title="Stand Up, Virgin Soldiers">Stand Up, Virgin Soldiers</a></i> (1977). In the 1980s, she made appearances in <i><a href="/wiki/Blackadder" title="Blackadder">Blackadder</a></i> opposite <a href="/wiki/Rowan_Atkinson" title="Rowan Atkinson">Rowan Atkinson</a>: these roles include the Spanish <a href="/wiki/Infante" title="Infante">Infanta</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Black_Adder" title="The Black Adder">The Black Adder</a></i>, Lady Whiteadder in <i><a href="/wiki/Blackadder_II" title="Blackadder II">Blackadder II</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Blackadder%27s_Christmas_Carol" title="Blackadder's Christmas Carol">Blackadder's Christmas Carol</a></i>. In 1986 she played a major supporting role in the BBC drama <i><a href="/wiki/The_Life_and_Loves_of_a_She-Devil" title="The Life and Loves of a She-Devil">The Life and Loves of a She-Devil</a></i>. She won the 1989 LA Critics Circle Award for Best Supporting Actress for her portrayal of Flora Finching in the 1988 film <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Dorrit_(film)" title="Little Dorrit (film)">Little Dorrit</a></i>. On American television, she headlined the short-lived 1992 <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Frannie%27s_Turn" title="Frannie's Turn">Frannie's Turn</a></i>. In 1994 she won the <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_of_Film_and_Television_Arts" title="British Academy of Film and Television Arts">BAFTA</a> Award for Best Supporting Actress for her role as Mrs Mingott in <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">Martin Scorsese</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Age_of_Innocence_(1993_film)" title="The Age of Innocence (1993 film)">The Age of Innocence</a></i> (1993).</p>
<p>Margolyes came to the notice of younger audiences when she starred as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aunt_Sponge" title="Aunt Sponge">Aunt Sponge</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/James_and_the_Giant_Peach_(film)" title="James and the Giant Peach (film)">James and the Giant Peach</a></i> (1996); she also provided the voice of the Glowworm in the same film. During the same time she played the Nurse in <a href="/wiki/Baz_Luhrmann" title="Baz Luhrmann">Baz Luhrmann</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Romeo_%2B_Juliet" title="Romeo + Juliet">Romeo + Juliet</a></i> (1996). Around this time, she voiced the rabbit character in the animated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_commercial" title="Television commercial">commercials</a> for <a href="/wiki/Cadbury" title="Cadbury">Cadbury</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Cadbury_Dairy_Milk_Caramel" title="Cadbury Dairy Milk Caramel">Caramel</a> bars<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and provided the voice of Fly the dog in the Australian-American family film <i><a href="/wiki/Babe_(film)" title="Babe (film)">Babe</a></i> (1995).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-southernhighlandnews1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-southernhighlandnews1-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>She played <a href="/wiki/Hogwarts_staff" title="Hogwarts staff">Professor Sprout</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Chamber_of_Secrets_(film)" title="Harry Potter and the Chamber of Secrets (film)">Harry Potter and the Chamber of Secrets</a></i> in 2002.</p>
<p>In 2004, Margolyes played the role of Peg Sellers, the mother of Peter Sellers, in the Golden Globe winning film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Life_and_Death_of_Peter_Sellers" title="The Life and Death of Peter Sellers">The Life and Death of Peter Sellers</a></i>.</p>
<p>She was one of the original cast of the London production of the musical <i><a href="/wiki/Wicked_(musical)" title="Wicked (musical)">Wicked</a></i> in 2006, playing Madame Morrible opposite <a href="/wiki/Idina_Menzel" title="Idina Menzel">Idina Menzel</a>, a role she also played on Broadway in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009, she appeared in a new production of <i><a href="/wiki/Endgame_(play)" title="Endgame (play)">Endgame</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Beckett" title="Samuel Beckett">Samuel Beckett</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Duchess_Theatre" title="Duchess Theatre">Duchess Theatre</a> in London's West End.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Margolyes voiced the role of Mrs. Plithiver, a blind snake in 3D-animated-epic film <i><a href="/wiki/Legend_of_the_Guardians:_The_Owls_of_Ga%27Hoole" title="Legend of the Guardians: The Owls of Ga'Hoole">Legend of the Guardians: The Owls of Ga'Hoole</a></i> in 2010. Margolyes reprised her role as Professor Sprout in <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Deathly_Hallows_%E2%80%93_Part_2" title="Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows – Part 2">Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows – Part 2</a></i> in 2011.</p>
<p>In 2014 she voiced Nana in the <a href="/wiki/Disney_Junior" title="Disney Junior">Disney Junior</a> animated series for preschoolers <i><a href="/wiki/Nina_Needs_to_Go!" title="Nina Needs to Go!">Nina Needs to Go!</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2016 she appears in <i>The Real Marigold Hotel</i>, a travel documentary series which "takes an all-star cast on the journey of a lifetime."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The series was reprised for two Christmas Specials <i>The Real Marigold On Tour</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>She narrated the 2016 ITV documentary about <a href="/wiki/Lady_Colin_Campbell" title="Lady Colin Campbell">Lady Colin Campbell</a> entitled <i>Lady C and the Castle</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_work">Other work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miriam_Margolyes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Margolyes is a supporter of Sense (the National Deafblind and Rubella Association) and was the host at the first Sense Creative Writing Awards, held at the <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens_Museum" title="Charles Dickens Museum">Charles Dickens Museum</a> in London in December 2006, where she read a number of works written by talented deafblind people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailymail1_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-dailymail1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sense_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-sense-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>She has also been the Patron of Trinity Community Arts Centre in Bristol since 2014 where she recently held 'A night with Miriam' comedy event to raise funds for its Youth Activities programme.</p>
<p>In 2011, Margolyes recorded a narrative for the album <i>The Devil's Brides</i> by <a href="/wiki/Klezmer" title="Klezmer">klezmer</a> musician-ethnographer <a href="/wiki/Yale_Strom" title="Yale Strom">Yale Strom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was announced in January 2014 that Margolyes was to record the narration for "Magic in the Skies" – the summer season of firework displays held at <a href="/wiki/Land%27s_End" title="Land's End">Land's End</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miriam_Margolyes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Margolyes is a <a href="/wiki/Lesbian" title="Lesbian">lesbian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Since 1967 her partner has been Heather Sutherland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-southernhighlandnews1_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-southernhighlandnews1-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> a retired Australian Professor of Indonesian Studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Formerly based in Amsterdam,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailymail1_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-dailymail1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-divamag1_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-divamag1-25">[25]</a></sup> Margolyes divides her time between homes in London, <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robertson,_New_South_Wales" title="Robertson, New South Wales">Robertson, New South Wales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Margolyes appeared on the British television quiz <i><a href="/wiki/University_Challenge" title="University Challenge">University Challenge</a></i> in 1963, whilst at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" title="Cambridge University">Cambridge University</a>. As part of a <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> documentary, <i>University Challenge: The Story so Far</i>, she claimed that during her appearance she swore after getting a question wrong, although the actual word was <a href="/wiki/Bleep_censor" title="Bleep censor">bleeped out</a> of the recording.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Margolyes is also a humanitarian advocate. She is a <a href="/wiki/State_of_Palestine" title="State of Palestine">Palestinian</a> human rights activist, having been a member of the British-based <i>ENOUGH!</i> coalition that seeks to end the "Israeli occupation of the Gaza Strip and West Bank."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> She is also a signatory of <a href="/wiki/Jews_for_Justice_for_Palestinians" title="Jews for Justice for Palestinians">Jews for Justice for Palestinians</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Margolyes is a campaigner for a <a href="/wiki/Respite_care" title="Respite care">respite care</a> charity, <a href="/wiki/Crossroads_Care" title="Crossroads Care">Crossroads</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latenightlive_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-latenightlive-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Margolyes is a lifelong admirer of the works of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a> and has performed all over the world a one-woman show, <i><a href="/wiki/Dickens%27_Women" title="Dickens' Women">Dickens' Women</a></i>, in which she plays 23 characters from Dickens' novels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Author and comedian <a href="/wiki/David_Walliams" title="David Walliams">David Walliams</a> says he used Margolyes as a model for the title character in his children's book <i><a href="/wiki/Awful_Auntie" title="Awful Auntie">Awful Auntie</a></i> after a rude exchange with the actress during a stage production. He stresses however that he has nothing against Margolyes and is a fan of her work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Margolyes_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Margolyes-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>On becoming an <a href="/wiki/Australian_nationality_law" title="Australian nationality law">Australian citizen</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latenightlive_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-latenightlive-31">[31]</a></sup> on <a href="/wiki/Australia_Day" title="Australia Day">Australia Day</a> 2013, Margolyes referred to herself as a "<a href="/wiki/Dyke_(slang)" title="Dyke (slang)">dyke</a>" live on national television and in front of the then <a href="/wiki/Prime_minister" title="Prime minister">prime minister</a>, <a href="/wiki/Julia_Gillard" title="Julia Gillard">Julia Gillard</a>.</p>
<p>Margolyes caused controversy in 2016 at <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Waverley_railway_station" title="Edinburgh Waverley railway station">Edinburgh Waverley railway station</a> when she asked a man to give up a seat only for him to refuse, pointing out there was another one nearby. By her own admission, she then swore at the man and poured a bottle of water over him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
