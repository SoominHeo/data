<p>The <b>Battle of Myriokephalon</b>, also known as the <b>Battle of <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">Myriocephalum</span></b>, or <i><span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr">Miryokefalon Savaşı</span></i> in <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>, was a battle between the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seljuk_Sultanate_of_R%C3%BBm" title="Seljuk Sultanate of Rûm">Seljuk Turks</a> in <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygia</a> on September 17, 1176. The battle was a strategic reverse for the Byzantine forces, who were ambushed when moving through a mountain pass. It was to be the final, unsuccessful effort by the Byzantines to recover the interior of <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> from the Seljuk Turks.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Myriokephalon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Between 1158 and 1161 a series of Byzantine campaigns against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seljuk_Turks" title="Seljuk Turks">Seljuk Turks</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seljuk_Sultanate_of_R%C3%BBm" title="Seljuk Sultanate of Rûm">Sultanate of Rûm</a> resulted in a treaty favourable to the Empire, with the sultan recognising a form of subordination to the Byzantine emperor. Immediately after peace was negotiated the Seljuk sultan <a href="/wiki/Kilij_Arslan_II" title="Kilij Arslan II">Kilij Arslan II</a> visited <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> where he was treated by Emperor <a href="/wiki/Manuel_I_Komnenos" title="Manuel I Komnenos">Manuel I Komnenos</a> as both an honoured guest and an imperial vassal. Following this event there was no overt hostility between the two powers for many years. It was a fragile peace, however, as the Seljuks wanted to push from the arid central plateau of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a> into the more fertile coastal lands, while the Byzantines wanted to recover the Anatolian territory they had lost since the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Manzikert" title="Battle of Manzikert">Battle of Manzikert</a> one hundred years earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the long peace with the Seljuks Manuel was able to concentrate his military power in other theatres. In the west he defeated Hungary and imposed Byzantine control over all the Balkans. In the east he recovered <a href="/wiki/Cilicia" title="Cilicia">Cilicia</a> from local Armenian dynasts and managed to reduce the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusader</a> <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Antioch" title="Principality of Antioch">Principality of Antioch</a> to vassal status. However, the peace with Byzantium also allowed Killij Arslan to eliminate internal rivals and strengthen his military resources. When the strongest Muslim ruler in Syria <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nur_ad-Din_Zangi" title="Nur ad-Din Zangi">Nur ad-Din Zangi</a> died in 1174, his successor <a href="/wiki/Saladin" title="Saladin">Saladin</a> was more concerned with <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Jerusalem" title="Kingdom of Jerusalem">Palestine</a> than the territory bordering the Empire. This shift in power gave Kilij Arslan the freedom to destroy the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danishmend" title="Danishmend">Danishmend</a> emirates of eastern Anatolia and also eject his brother Shahinshah from his lands near Ankara. Shahinshah, who was Manuel's vassal, and the Danishmend emirs fled to the protection of Byzantium. In 1175 the peace between Byzantium and the Sultanate of Rûm fell apart when Kilij Arslan refused to hand over to the Byzantines, as he was obliged to do by treaty, a considerable proportion of the territory he had recently conquered from the Danishmends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="March">March</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Myriokephalon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: March">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The army gathered at <a href="/wiki/Lopadion" title="Lopadion">Lopadion</a> by Manuel was supposedly so large that it spread across ten miles, and marched towards the border with the Seljuks via <a href="/wiki/Laodicea_on_the_Lycus" title="Laodicea on the Lycus">Laodicea</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chonae" title="Chonae">Chonae</a>, Lampe, <a href="/wiki/Celaenae" title="Celaenae">Celaenae</a>, Choma and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antioch,_Pisidia" title="Antioch, Pisidia">Antioch</a>. Arslan tried to negotiate but Manuel was convinced of his superiority and rejected a new peace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He sent part of the army under <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Andronikos_Vatatzes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Andronikos Vatatzes (page does not exist)">Andronikos Vatatzes</a> towards <a href="/wiki/Amasya" title="Amasya">Amasia</a> while his larger force marched towards the Seljuk capital at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iconium" title="Iconium">Iconium</a> (<a href="/wiki/Konya" title="Konya">Konya</a>). Both routes were through heavily wooded regions, where the Turks could easily hide and set up ambushes; the army moving towards Amasia was destroyed in one such ambush. The Turks later displayed Andronikos's head, impaled on a lance, during the fighting at Myriokephalon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Turks also destroyed crops and poisoned water supplies to make Manuel's march more difficult. Arslan harassed the Byzantine army in order to force it into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maeander_River" title="Maeander River">Meander valley</a>, and specifically the mountain pass of Tzivritze near the fortress of Myriokephalon. Once at the pass Manuel decided to attack, despite the danger from further ambushes, and also despite the fact that he could have attempted to bring the Turks out of their positions to fight them on the nearby plain of Philomelion, the site of an earlier <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Philomelion" title="Battle of Philomelion">victory</a> won by his grandfather <a href="/wiki/Alexios_I_Komnenos" title="Alexios I Komnenos">Alexios</a>. The lack of forage, and water for his troops, and the fact that dysentery had broken out in his army may have induced Manuel to decide to force the pass regardless of the danger of ambush.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle">Battle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Myriokephalon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Battle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>All sources agree that the Byzantine force was of exceptional size. The historian John Haldon estimates the army at 25,000 men, while John Birkenmeier puts it at 35,000 men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haldon198_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haldon198-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The latter number is derived from the fact that sources indicated a supply train of 3,000 wagons accompanied the army, which was enough to support 30–40,000 men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Birkenmeier.2C_p._132_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Birkenmeier.2C_p._132-4">[4]</a></sup> Birkenmeier believes that the army contained 25,000 Byzantine troops with the remainder composed of an allied contingent of Hungarians sent by Manuel's kinsman <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_III_of_Hungary" title="Béla III of Hungary">Béla III of Hungary</a> and tributary forces supplied by the Principality of Antioch and Serbia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Byzantine army was divided into a number of divisions, which entered the pass in the following order: a vanguard, largely of <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">infantry</a> (the other divisions being composed of a mix of infantry and <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalry</a>); the main division (of eastern and western <i><a href="/wiki/Tagma_(military)" title="Tagma (military)">Tagmata</a></i>); then the right wing (largely composed of Antiochenes and other Westerners), led by <a href="/wiki/Baldwin_of_Antioch" title="Baldwin of Antioch">Baldwin of Antioch</a> (Manuel's brother-in-law); the baggage and siege trains; the Byzantine left wing, led by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Theodore_Mavrozomes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Theodore Mavrozomes (page does not exist)">Theodore Mavrozomes</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Kantakouzenos_(sebastos)" title="John Kantakouzenos (sebastos)">John Kantakouzenos</a>; the emperor and his picked troops; and finally the rear division under the experienced general <a href="/wiki/Andronikos_Kontostephanos" title="Andronikos Kontostephanos">Andronikos Kontostephanos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Birkenmeier.2C_p._132_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Birkenmeier.2C_p._132-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> No estimate of Seljuk numbers has been possible. However, primary sources have provided figures for other Seljuk campaigns. In 1160, John Kontostephanos defeated a force of 22,000 Seljuk Turks and about 20–24,000 Turks invaded the Maeander river valley in 1177.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Birkenmeier.2C_p._132_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Birkenmeier.2C_p._132-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> These numbers offer a reasonable range for the Seljuk Sultanate of Rum's military strength.</p>
<p>The Byzantine vanguard was the first to encounter Arslan's troops, and went through the pass with few casualties, as did the main division. Possibly the Turks had not yet fully deployed in their positions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> These divisions sent their infantry up onto the slopes to dislodge the Seljuk soldiers, who were forced to withdraw to higher ground. The following divisions did not take this precaution, also they were negligent in not maintaining a defensive formation of closed ranks and they did not deploy their archers effectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> By the time the first two Byzantine divisions exited the far end of the pass, the rear was just about to enter; this allowed the Turks to close their trap on those divisions still within the pass. The Turkish attack, descending from the heights, fell especially heavily on the Byzantine right wing. This division seems to have quickly lost cohesion and been broken, soldiers fleeing one ambush often running into another. Heavy casualties were sustained by the right wing and its commander, Baldwin, was killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The Turks then concentrated their attacks on the baggage and siege trains, shooting down the draught animals and choking the roadway. The left wing division also suffered significant casualties and one of its leaders, John Kantakouzenos, was slain when fighting alone against a band of Seljuk soldiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The remaining Byzantine troops were panicked by the carnage in front of them and the realisation that the Turks had also begun to attack their rear. The sudden descent of a blinding dust-storm did nothing to improve the morale or organisation of the Byzantine forces, though it must have confused the Seljuk troops also. At this point, Manuel seems to have suffered a crisis of confidence and reputedly sat down, passively awaiting his fate and that of his army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haldon.2C_p._143_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haldon.2C_p._143-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The emperor was eventually roused by his officers, re-established discipline and organised his forces into a defensive formation; when formed up, they pushed their way past the wreck of the baggage and out of the pass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haldon.2C_p._143_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haldon.2C_p._143-23">[23]</a></sup> Debouching from the pass they rejoined the unscathed van and main divisions, commanded by John and Andronikos Angelos, <a href="/wiki/Constantine_Makrodoukas" title="Constantine Makrodoukas">Constantine Makrodoukas</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andronikos_Lampardas" title="Andronikos Lampardas">Andronikos Lampardas</a>. Whilst the rest of the army had been under attack in the pass the troops of the van and main divisions had constructed a fortified encampment. The rear division, under Andronikos Kontostephanos, arrived at the camp somewhat later than the emperor, having suffered few casualties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The night was spent in successfully repulsing further attacks by Seljuk mounted archers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haldon.2C_p._143_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Haldon.2C_p._143-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Niketas_Choniates" title="Niketas Choniates">Niketas Choniates</a> states that Manuel considered abandoning his troops but was shamed into staying by the scathing words of an anonymous soldier and the disapproval of a shocked Kontostephanos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> However, this would appear to be hyperbole on the historian's part as Manuel would have placed himself in much greater danger by flight than if he remained in the midst of his army. The following day, the Turks circled the camp firing arrows; Manuel ordered two counterattacks, led by John Angelos and Constantine Makrodoukas respectively, but there was no renewal of a general action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outcome">Outcome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Myriokephalon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Outcome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Both sides, it appears, had suffered casualties, though their extent is difficult to quantify. As the Byzantine army moved back through the pass after the battle it was seen that the dead had been scalped and their genitals mutilated, "It was said that the Turks took these measures so that the circumcised could not be distinguished from the uncircumcised and the victory therefore disputed and contested since many had fallen on both sides."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Most importantly Manuel's siege equipment had been captured and destroyed. The Byzantines, without any means of attacking Iconium, were now no longer in a position to continue the campaign. Also the Seljuk Sultan was keen for peace to be restored as soon as possible; he sent an envoy named Gabras, together with gifts of a Nisaean warhorse and a sword, to Manuel in order to negotiate a truce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> As a result of these negotiations, the Byzantine army was to be allowed to retreat unmolested on condition that Manuel destroy his forts and evacuate the garrisons at <a href="/wiki/Dorylaeum" title="Dorylaeum">Dorylaeum</a> and Sublaeum in the Byzantine-Seljuk border lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> However, despite Kilij Arslan's protestations of good faith, the retreat of the Byzantine army was harassed by the attacks of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkmen_people" title="Turkmen people">Turcoman</a> tribesmen (over whom Kilij Arslan probably had very little control). This, taken with an earlier failure by the sultan to keep his side of a treaty signed in 1162, gave Manuel an excuse to avoid observing the terms of this new arrangement in their entirety. He therefore demolished the fortifications of the less important fortress of Sublaeum but left Dorylaeum intact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Manuel himself compared his defeat to that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manzikert" title="Manzikert">Manzikert</a>, sending a message to Constantinople ahead of his army likening his fate to that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanos_Diogenes" title="Romanos Diogenes">Romanos Diogenes</a>. However, in the same message he: "Then extolled the treaties made with the sultan, boasting that these had been concluded beneath his own banner which had waved in the wind in view of the enemy's front line so that trembling and fear fell upon them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> It is notable that it was the sultan who initiated peace proposals by sending an envoy to Manuel and not the reverse. The conclusion that Kilij Arslan, though negotiating from a position of strength, did not consider that his forces were capable of destroying the Byzantine army is inescapable. A possible reason for Kilij Arslan's reluctance to renew the battle is that a large proportion of his irregular troops may have been far more interested in securing the plunder they had taken than in continuing the fight, thus leaving his army seriously weakened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Myriokephalon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Myriokephalon, although a significant defeat for the Byzantines, did not materially affect the capabilities of the Byzantine army. This is underlined by the notable victory the Byzantines won over the Seljuks at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hyelion_and_Leimocheir" title="Battle of Hyelion and Leimocheir">Hyelion and Leimocheir</a> on the Meander River the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Manuel continued to meet the Seljuks in smaller battles with some success, and concluded a probably advantageous peace with Kilij Arslan in 1179.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> However, like Manzikert, Myriokephalon was a pivotal event and following it the balance between the two powers in Anatolia gradually began to shift, for Manuel never again launched a strategically offensive campaign against the Turks and remained on the defensive.</p>
<p>Myriokephalon had more of a psychological impact than a military impact, as it proved that the Empire could not destroy Seljuk power in central Anatolia, despite the advances made during Manuel's reign. Essentially, the problem was that Manuel had allowed himself to be distracted by a series of military adventures in Italy and Egypt, instead of dealing with the more pressing issue of the Turks. This had given the Sultan many years in which to eliminate his rivals, enabling him to build up a force capable of facing the Byzantine army in the field. Without the years required to build up Seljuk military power, the battle could not have taken place. Furthermore, during the campaign, Manuel made several serious tactical errors, such as failing to scout out the route ahead effectively and ignoring the advice of his senior officers. These failings caused him to lead his forces straight into a classic ambush. However, in defence of Manuel's generalship it is clear that he organised his army in a very effective manner. The army was composed of a number of 'divisions,' each of which was self-reliant and could act as a small independent army; it has been argued that it was this organisation which allowed the greater part of his army to survive the ambush inflicted on it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>An important facet of Manuel's dispositions was that the vanguard was composed of infantry. Infantry are far better troops than cavalry when operating in mountainous terrain and it appears that the infantry van was meant to dislodge any Seljuk soldiery from the high ground dominating the pass. They signally failed to sweep the Seljuks from the pass and this failure was a major cause of the Byzantine defeat. Added to this there seems to have been a failure in generalship by the commanders of the right and left wings, who did not deploy their troops as effectively as had the commanders of the two leading divisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Manuel's death, the empire drifted into anarchy, and it was never again in a position to mount a major offensive in the east. The defeat of Myriokephalon marked the end of Byzantine attempts to recover the Anatolian plateau, which was now lost to the empire forever.</p>

<li id="cite_note-24"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-24">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="#CITEREFChoniates1984">Choniates 1984</a>, p. 105.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-25"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-25">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="#CITEREFChoniates1984">Choniates 1984</a>, pp. 105–106. Manuel may have had the fate of Romanos Diogenes in mind and have had some apprehension of being captured. His situation was, however, very different to that of Diogenes. Unlike the case of the earlier emperor, Manuel's troops had not dispersed from the battlefield but had drawn together following their defeat and were still capable of defending themselves.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-26"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-26">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="#CITEREFChoniates1984">Choniates 1984</a>, p. 106. It is notable that the two generals leading the counterattacks commanded units which had suffered negligible losses the previous day. It is probable that the Byzantine counter-attacks achieved little because, once in open country, the Seljuks were reluctant to come to close combat with the more heavily armoured Byzantine cavalry, and the Byzantines were unwilling to pursue too far for fear of further ambushes.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-27"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-27">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="#CITEREFChoniates1984">Choniates 1984</a>, p. 107. Presumably the scalping took place because the Turks wore their hair in a distinctive style.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-28"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-28">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="#CITEREFChoniates1984">Choniates 1984</a>, p. 107. The "Gabras" who acted as emissary was possibly Iktiyar ad-Din Hasan ibn Gabras, who was Kilij Arslan's vizier and was of Greek origins. He was a member of the Gabras family that had ruled <a href="/wiki/Trabzon" title="Trabzon">Trebizond</a> earlier in the 12th century. There were a number of prominent Greek aristocrats in Seljuk employ, including Manuel's first cousin <a href="/wiki/John_Tzelepes_Komnenos" title="John Tzelepes Komnenos">John Tzelepes Komnenos</a>.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-29"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-29">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="#CITEREFAngold1984">Angold 1984</a>, pp. 192–193.</span></li>

<li id="cite_note-32"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-32">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="#CITEREFFinlay1877">Finlay 1877</a>, p. 195.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-33"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-33">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Ironically, this battle was a reverse of Myriokephalon with a Seljuk army blundering into a classic ambush laid by the Byzantine general <a href="/wiki/John_Komnenos_Vatatzes" title="John Komnenos Vatatzes">John Komnenos Vatatzes</a>.</span></li>
