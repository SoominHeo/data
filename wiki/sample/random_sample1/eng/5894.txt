<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <b>differential topology</b> is the field dealing with <a href="/wiki/Differentiable_function" title="Differentiable function">differentiable functions</a> on <a href="/wiki/Differentiable_manifold" title="Differentiable manifold">differentiable manifolds</a>. It is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Differential_geometry" title="Differential geometry">differential geometry</a> and together they make up the geometric theory of differentiable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manifolds" title="Manifolds">manifolds</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Differential_topology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Differential topology considers the properties and structures that require only a <a href="/wiki/Smooth_structure" title="Smooth structure">smooth structure</a> on a manifold to be defined. Smooth manifolds are 'softer' than manifolds with extra geometric structures, which can act as obstructions to certain types of equivalences and <a href="/wiki/Deformation_theory" title="Deformation theory">deformations</a> that exist in differential topology. For instance, volume and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riemannian_curvature" title="Riemannian curvature">Riemannian curvature</a> are <a href="/wiki/Invariant_(mathematics)" title="Invariant (mathematics)">invariants</a> that can distinguish different geometric structures on the same smooth manifold—that is, one can smoothly "flatten out" certain manifolds, but it might require distorting the space and affecting the curvature or volume.</p>
<p>On the other hand, smooth manifolds are more rigid than the <a href="/wiki/Topological_manifold" title="Topological manifold">topological manifolds</a>. <a href="/wiki/John_Milnor" title="John Milnor">John Milnor</a> discovered that some spheres have more than one smooth structure—see <a href="/wiki/Exotic_sphere" title="Exotic sphere">exotic sphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Donaldson%27s_theorem" title="Donaldson's theorem">Donaldson's theorem</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kervaire" title="Kervaire">Kervaire</a> exhibited topological manifolds with no smooth structure at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kervaire_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kervaire-1">[1]</a></sup> Some constructions of smooth manifold theory, such as the existence of tangent bundles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lashof_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-lashof-2">[2]</a></sup> can be done in the topological setting with much more work, and others cannot.</p>
<p>One of the main topics in differential topology is the study of special kinds of smooth mappings between manifolds, namely <a href="/wiki/Immersion_(mathematics)" title="Immersion (mathematics)">immersions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Submersion_(mathematics)" title="Submersion (mathematics)">submersions</a>, and the intersections of submanifolds via <a href="/wiki/Transversality_(mathematics)" title="Transversality (mathematics)">transversality</a>. More generally one is interested in properties and invariants of smooth manifolds which are carried over by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffeomorphisms" title="Diffeomorphisms">diffeomorphisms</a>, another special kind of smooth mapping. <a href="/wiki/Morse_theory" title="Morse theory">Morse theory</a> is another branch of differential topology, in which topological information about a manifold is deduced from changes in the <a href="/wiki/Rank_(differential_topology)" title="Rank (differential topology)">rank</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Jacobian_matrix_and_determinant" title="Jacobian matrix and determinant">Jacobian</a> of a function.</p>
<p>For a list of differential topology topics, see the following reference: <a href="/wiki/List_of_differential_geometry_topics" title="List of differential geometry topics">List of differential geometry topics</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Differential_topology_versus_differential_geometry">Differential topology versus differential geometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Differential_topology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Differential topology versus differential geometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Geometry_and_topology" title="Geometry and topology">geometry and topology</a>.</div>
<p>Differential topology and differential geometry are first characterized by their <i>similarity</i>. They both study primarily the properties of differentiable manifolds, sometimes with a variety of structures imposed on them.</p>
<p>One major difference lies in the nature of the problems that each subject tries to address. In one view,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> differential topology distinguishes itself from differential geometry by studying primarily those problems which are <i>inherently global</i>. Consider the example of a coffee cup and a donut (see <span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/26/Mug_and_Torus_morph.gif">this example</a></span>). From the point of view of differential topology, the donut and the coffee cup are <i>the same</i> (in a sense). This is an inherently global view, though, because there is no way for the differential topologist to tell whether the two objects are the same (in this sense) by looking at just a tiny (<i>local</i>) piece of either of them. He or she must have access to each entire (<i>global</i>) object.</p>
<p>From the point of view of differential geometry, the coffee cup and the donut are <i>different</i> because it is impossible to rotate the coffee cup in such a way that its configuration matches that of the donut. This is also a global way of thinking about the problem. But an important distinction is that the geometer does not need the entire object to decide this. By looking, for instance, at just a tiny piece of the handle, he can decide that the coffee cup is different from the donut because the handle is thinner (or more curved) than any piece of the donut.</p>
<p>To put it succinctly, differential topology studies structures on manifolds which, in a sense, have no interesting local structure. Differential geometry studies structures on manifolds which do have an interesting local (or sometimes even infinitesimal) structure.</p>
<p>More mathematically, for example, the problem of constructing a <a href="/wiki/Diffeomorphism" title="Diffeomorphism">diffeomorphism</a> between two manifolds of the same dimension is inherently global since <i>locally</i> two such manifolds are always diffeomorphic. Likewise, the problem of computing a quantity on a manifold which is invariant under differentiable mappings is inherently global, since any local invariant will be <i>trivial</i> in the sense that it is already exhibited in the topology of <b>R</b><sup>n</sup>. Moreover, differential topology does not restrict itself necessarily to the study of diffeomorphism. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symplectic_topology" title="Symplectic topology">symplectic topology</a>—a subbranch of differential topology—studies global properties of <a href="/wiki/Symplectic_manifold" title="Symplectic manifold">symplectic manifolds</a>. Differential geometry concerns itself with problems—which may be local <i>or</i> global—that always have some non-trivial local properties. Thus differential geometry may study differentiable manifolds equipped with a <i><a href="/wiki/Connection_(mathematics)" title="Connection (mathematics)">connection</a></i>, a <i><a href="/wiki/Metric_(mathematics)" title="Metric (mathematics)">metric</a></i> (which may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riemannian_metric" title="Riemannian metric">Riemannian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_metric" title="Pseudo-Riemannian metric">pseudo-Riemannian</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finsler_metric" title="Finsler metric">Finsler</a>), a special sort of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frobenius_integration_theorem" title="Frobenius integration theorem">distribution</a></i> (such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CR_structure" title="CR structure">CR structure</a>), and so on.</p>
<p>This distinction between differential geometry and differential topology is blurred, however, in questions specifically pertaining to local diffeomorphism invariants such as the <a href="/wiki/Tangent_space" title="Tangent space">tangent space</a> at a point. Differential topology also deals with questions like these, which specifically pertain to the properties of differentiable mappings on <b>R</b><sup>n</sup> (for example the <a href="/wiki/Tangent_bundle" title="Tangent bundle">tangent bundle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jet_(mathematics)" title="Jet (mathematics)">jet bundles</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Whitney_extension_theorem" title="Whitney extension theorem">Whitney extension theorem</a>, and so forth).</p>
<p>The distinction is concise in abstract terms. Differential topology is the study of the (infinitesimal, local, and global) properties of structures on manifolds that have only trivial <a href="/wiki/Moduli_space" title="Moduli space">local moduli</a>; differential geometry, at least one non-trivial local moduli.</p>
