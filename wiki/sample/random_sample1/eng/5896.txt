<p>A <b>missile launch facility</b>, also known as an <b>underground missile silo</b>, <b>launch facility—LF</b> or <b>nuclear silo</b>, is a vertical cylindrical structure constructed underground, for the storage and launching of <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">intercontinental ballistic missiles</a> (ICBMs).</p>
<p>The structures typically have the <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missile</a> some distance below ground, protected by a large "<a href="/wiki/Blast_shelter" title="Blast shelter">blast door</a>" on top. They are usually connected, physically and/or electronically, to a <a href="/wiki/Missile_launch_control_center" title="Missile launch control center">missile launch control center</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missile_launch_facility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Until the 1960s ICBMs had been launched from surface bases. The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> used completely above-ground launchers similar to those found at a <a href="/wiki/Spaceport" title="Spaceport">spaceport</a>. In many cases they were the same ones used for civilian launches, such as <a href="/wiki/Gagarin%27s_Start" title="Gagarin's Start">Site 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome_Site_31" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome Site 31">Site 31</a>. They were vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Bomber" title="Bomber">bomber</a> attacks by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="La_Coupole">La Coupole</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missile_launch_facility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: La Coupole">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/La_Coupole" title="La Coupole">La Coupole</a> facility is the earliest known precursor to modern underground missile silos still in existence. It was built by the forces of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> in northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occupied_France" title="Occupied France">Occupied France</a>, between 1943 and 1944, to serve as a launch base for <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V-2 rockets</a>. The facility was designed with an immense concrete dome to store a large stockpile of V-2s, warheads and fuel, and was intended to launch V-2s on an industrial scale. Dozens of missiles a day were to be fuelled, prepared and rolled just outdoors of the facility's concrete casing, launched from either of two outdoor launch pads in rapid sequence against London and southern England. A similar-purpose but less-developed facility, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blockhaus_d%27Eperlecques" title="Blockhaus d'Eperlecques">Blockhaus d'Eperlecques</a> had also been built, some 14.4 kilometers (8.9 miles) north-northwest of La Coupole, and closer to both facilities' intended targets for V-2 rocket bombardment meant for launch from both of them, towards southeastern England.</p>
<p>Following repeated heavy bombing by Allied forces during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Crossbow" title="Operation Crossbow">Operation Crossbow</a>, the Germans were unable to complete construction of the works and the complex never entered service. The United Kingdom conducted <a href="/wiki/La_Coupole#Post_war_investigations" title="La Coupole">Post war investigations</a>, determining that it was "an assembly site for long projectiles most conveniently handled and prepared in a vertical position".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missile_launch_facility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first underground missile silo was built in the 1950s by the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, to house their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_Streak_missile" title="Blue Streak missile">Blue Streak missiles</a>. Only one test underground missile silo was built in the UK, at <a href="/wiki/RAF_Spadeadam" title="RAF Spadeadam">RAF Spadeadam</a>. The UK cancelled the Blue Streak silo project, since the Soviets had developed missiles that could attack with little warning and insufficient time to arm Blue Streak missiles. The UK's ICBM nuclear missile launch mode was changed in 1960, to <a href="/wiki/Submarine-launched_ballistic_missile" title="Submarine-launched ballistic missile">submarine-launched ballistic missiles</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missile_launch_facility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The German idea of an underground missile silo was adopted and developed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> for missile launch facilities for its intercontinental ballistic missiles. Most silos were based in <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">Nebraska</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">South Dakota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a> and other western states away from heavily populated cities. They had many defense systems to keep out intruders and other defense systems to prevent destruction (see <a href="/wiki/Safeguard_Program" title="Safeguard Program">Safeguard Program</a>).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Atlas_missiles">Atlas missiles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missile_launch_facility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Atlas missiles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlas_missile" title="Atlas missile">Atlas missiles</a> used four different storage and launching methods.</p>
<ul>
<li>The first version were vertical and above ground launchers, at <a href="/wiki/Vandenberg_Air_Force_Base" title="Vandenberg Air Force Base">Vandenberg Air Force Base</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Central_Coast_(California)" title="Central Coast (California)">Central Coast of California</a>.</li>
<li>The second version were stored horizontally in a shed-like structure with a retractable roof, to then be raised to the vertical and launched, at <a href="/wiki/Francis_E._Warren_Air_Force_Base" title="Francis E. Warren Air Force Base">Francis E. Warren Air Force Base</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a>.</li>
<li>The third version were stored horizontally, but better protected in a concrete building known as a "coffin", then raised to the vertical shortly before launch. These rather poorly protected designs were a consequence of the cryogenic liquid fuels used, which required the missiles to be stored unfueled and then be fueled immediately prior to launch.</li>
<li>The fourth version were stored vertically in underground silos, for the Atlas F ICBM. They were fueled in the silo, and then since they could not be launched from within the silo, were raised to the surface to launch. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titan_I" title="Titan I">Titan I</a> missile used a similar silo basing of the fourth version.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Configurations">Configurations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missile_launch_facility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Configurations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Launch facility (LF) configurations varied by U.S. missile systems.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/LGM-25C_Titan_II" title="LGM-25C Titan II">LGM-25C Titan II</a> (deactivated) ICBMs were in a one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Launch_control_center_(ICBM)" title="Launch control center (ICBM)">ICBM launch control center</a> (LCC) with one LF configuration (1 × 1). Titan missiles (both I and II) were located near their command and control operations personnel. Access to the missile was through tunnels connecting the launch control center and launch facility. An example of this can be seen at the Titan Missile Museum, located south of Tucson, Arizona.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_fuel_rocket" title="Solid fuel rocket">solid fueled</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LGM-30" title="LGM-30">LGM-30</a> series Minuteman I, II, III, and <a href="/wiki/LGM-118_Peacekeeper" title="LGM-118 Peacekeeper">Peacekeeper</a> ICBM configurations consist of one LCC that controls ten LFs (1 × 10). Five LCCs and their fifty associated LFs make up a squadron. Three squadrons make up a wing. Measures were taken such that if any one LCC was disabled, a separate LCC within the squadron would take control of its ten ICBMs.</li>
<li>The LGM-30 LFs and LCCs are separated by several miles, connected only electronically. This distance ensures that a nuclear attack could only disable a very small number of ICBMs, leaving the rest capable of being launched immediately.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dense_Pack" title="Dense Pack">Dense Pack</a> was a proposed configuration strategy for basing <a href="/wiki/LGM-118_Peacekeeper" title="LGM-118 Peacekeeper">LGM-118 Peacekeeper</a> ICBMs, developed under the Reagan administration, for the purpose of maximizing their survivability in case of a surprise nuclear first-strike on their silos conducted by a hostile foreign power. According to the Dense Pack strategy, a series of ten to twelve hardened silos would be grouped closely together in a line. This line of silos would generally run north-to-south, as the primary flight path for <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> inbound nuclear ICBMs would be expected to come from the north over the <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North Pole</a>. The proposed Dense Pack initiative met with strong criticism in the media and in the government, and the idea was never implemented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_Union">Soviet Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missile_launch_facility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Soviet Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> had missile silos in Russia and adjacent Soviet states during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Plok%C5%A1tin%C4%97_missile_base" title="Plokštinė missile base">Plokštinė missile base</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Main_Centre_for_Missile_Attack_Warning" title="Main Centre for Missile Attack Warning">Main Centre for Missile Attack Warning</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Solnechnogorsk" title="Solnechnogorsk">Solnechnogorsk</a> outside Moscow, was completed in by the Soviet Union in 1971, and remains in use by the Russian Federation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rapid_launch_underground_silos">Rapid launch underground silos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missile_launch_facility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rapid launch underground silos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the introduction of the Soviet <a href="/wiki/UR-100" title="UR-100">UR-100</a> and the U.S. <a href="/wiki/LGM-25C_Titan_II" title="LGM-25C Titan II">Titan II</a> missile series, underground silos changed in the 1960s. Both missile series introduced the use of <a href="/wiki/Hypergolic_propellant" title="Hypergolic propellant">hypergolic propellant</a>, which could be stored in the missiles, allowing for rapid launches. Both countries' liquid fueled missile systems were moved into underground silos. The introduction of solid fuel systems, in the later 1960s, made the silo moving and launching even easier.</p>
<p>The underground missile silo has remained the primary missile basing system and launch facility for land-based missiles since the 1960s. The increased accuracy of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inertial_guidance" title="Inertial guidance">inertial guidance</a> systems has rendered them somewhat more vulnerable than they were in the 1960s. The U.S. spent considerable effort and funds in the 1970s and 1980s designing a replacement, but none of the new and complex system designs were ever produced.</p>
<p>The United States built many missile silos in the Midwest, away from populated areas. Many were built in Colorado, Nebraska, South Dakota, and North Dakota. Today they are still used, although many have been decommissioned and hazardous materials removed. Today they are popular houses and sites of urban exploration.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobile_intercontinental_ballistic_missiles">Mobile intercontinental ballistic missiles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missile_launch_facility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mobile intercontinental ballistic missiles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>, the former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and current <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian Federation</a> possess mobile ICBMs. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">United States of America</a> had plans to develop mobile ICBMs but these projects were canceled at the end of the Cold War.</p>
<p>They include the:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/DF-31" title="DF-31">DF-31 (CSS-9)</a>: China, a land-based variant of the submarine-launched <a href="/wiki/JL-2" title="JL-2">JL-2</a>, successor of the <a href="/wiki/DF-4" title="DF-4">DF-4</a> mobile ICBM.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/LGM-118_Peacekeeper#Basing_options" title="LGM-118 Peacekeeper">LGM-118 Peacekeeper Mobile Protective Shelters (MPS) Plan</a>: United States, 1976-1980s proposal for 200 <a href="/wiki/LGM-118_Peacekeeper" title="LGM-118 Peacekeeper">LGM-118 Peacekeeper</a> missiles be rotated/shuttled between 4,600 soft shelters. The 1986 <a href="/wiki/Peacekeeper_Rail_Garrison" title="Peacekeeper Rail Garrison">Peacekeeper Rail Garrison</a> was adjunct plan of the MPS for 50 Peacekeeper missiles to be deployed on the nation's rail network, developed from 1986 until project was cancelled in 1991.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/MGM-134_Midgetman" title="MGM-134 Midgetman">MGM-134 Midgetman</a>: United States, the Small Intercontinental Ballistic Missile (SICBM) program was developed and tested, but the project was cancelled in 1992 before production commenced.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/RT-2PM_Topol" title="RT-2PM Topol">RT-2PM Topol</a>: Soviet Union, introduced in 1985, continues in service within the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Missile_Troops" title="Strategic Missile Troops">Strategic Missile Troops</a> of the Russian Federation.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/RT-2PM2_Topol-M" title="RT-2PM2 Topol-M">RT-2PM2 Topol-M</a>: Russian Federation, one of the most recent ICBMs to be deployed, and the first developed in Russia after the dissolution of the Soviet Union.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_day">Present day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missile_launch_facility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Present day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cuban_missiles.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1121" data-file-width="1160" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Cuban_missiles.jpg/220px-Cuban_missiles.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Cuban_missiles.jpg/330px-Cuban_missiles.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Cuban_missiles.jpg/440px-Cuban_missiles.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cuban_missiles.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Soviet MRMB base, photographed during the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Missile_Crisis" title="Cuban Missile Crisis">Cuban Missile Crisis</a>. The inability of ground based launch complexes like this one to move makes them susceptible to discovery and long term monitoring by airborne and/or space-based surveillance systems, resulting in a push from some nuclear capable nations to place a greater number of their weapons on more mobile platforms, such as <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile_submarine" title="Ballistic missile submarine">ballistic missile submarines</a> or <a href="/wiki/Transporter_erector_launcher" title="Transporter erector launcher">transporter erector launchers</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>China has silo-based weapons, but is now concentrating development on expanding its submarine and road-capable mobile weapons, especially for underground tunnel networks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> has silo-based weapons, having built a system of underground missile silos to protect missiles from detection and (above-ground) launch facilities from aerial destruction.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">Democratic People's Republic of Korea (DPRK)</a>, the North Korea <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strategic_Rocket_Forces_(North_Korea)" title="Strategic Rocket Forces (North Korea)">Strategic Rocket Forces</a> (Missile Guidance Bureau) are the strategic missile forces division of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_People%27s_Army" title="Korean People's Army">Korean People's Army</a>.</li>
<li>Russia has silo-based weapons, but has downsized their arsenal to a handful of mobile and silo-based weapons, with more now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta_class_submarine" title="Delta class submarine">Delta IV</a> submarine-launched ballistic missiles. The <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Missile_Troops" title="Strategic Missile Troops">Strategic Rocket Forces of the Russian Federation (RVSN RF)</a> (Strategic Missile Troops) controls Russia's land-based inter-continental ballistic missiles</li>
<li>The United States has many silo based warheads in service, however, they have lowered their number to around 1800 and have transferred most of their missiles to Nuclear Submarines and are focusing on more advanced conventional weapons.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt>Decommissioned missile silos</dt>
</dl>
<p>The increase of decommissioned missile silos has led governments to sell some of them to private individuals. Some buyers convert them into unique <a href="/wiki/Home" title="Home">homes</a>, ultimate <a href="/wiki/Safe_room" title="Safe room">safe rooms</a>, or for other purposes.</p>
<p>In 2000 <a href="/wiki/William_Leonard_Pickard" title="William Leonard Pickard">William Leonard Pickard</a> and a partner were convicted, in the largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LSD" title="LSD">lysergic acid diethylamide (LSD)</a> manufacturing case in history, of conspiracy to manufacture large quantities of LSD in a decommissioned <a href="/wiki/SM-65_Atlas" title="SM-65 Atlas">SM-65 Atlas</a> missile silo near <a href="/wiki/Wamego,_Kansas" title="Wamego, Kansas">Wamego, Kansas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
