<p>A <b>mission school</b> or <b>missionary school</b> is a religious school originally developed and run by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_(Christian)" title="Mission (Christian)">Christian missionaries</a>. The mission school was commonly used in the <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonial</a> era for the purposes of <a href="/wiki/Westernization" title="Westernization">Westernization</a> of local peoples. These may be <a href="/wiki/Day_school" title="Day school">day schools</a> or <a href="/wiki/Boarding_school" title="Boarding school">residential schools</a> (as in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Indian_residential_school_system" title="Canadian Indian residential school system">Canadian Indian residential school system</a>).</p>
<p>Mission schools were established in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> as early as the 16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> They eventually appeared on almost every continent, and persisted in some regions to the late 20th century.</p>
<p>These schools often adopted an evangelical and "heavily denominational" approach to religious education, with the intention of producing new teachers and religious leaders to propagate Christianity among the local population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> They also provided academic and vocational training, and usually discouraged the traditional practices of the local people. Mission schools were sometimes government-funded, for example in the US "when Congress felt less inclined to provide the large sums of money needed to establish government schools" to educate the <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">American Indian</a> population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
