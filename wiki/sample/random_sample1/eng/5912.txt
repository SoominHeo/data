<p><b>Miso</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">みそ <span lang="en" xml:lang="en">or</span> 味噌</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a traditional <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cuisine" title="Japanese cuisine">Japanese seasoning</a> produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">fermenting</a> <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybeans</a> with salt and koji (the fungus <i><a href="/wiki/Aspergillus_oryzae" title="Aspergillus oryzae">Aspergillus oryzae</a></i>) and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, or other ingredients. The result is a thick paste used for sauces and spreads, pickling vegetables or meats, and mixing with <a href="/wiki/Dashi" title="Dashi">dashi</a> soup stock to serve as <a href="/wiki/Miso_soup" title="Miso soup">miso soup</a> called <i>misoshiru</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">味噌汁</span>)</span>, a Japanese culinary staple. High in <a href="/wiki/Protein_(nutrient)" title="Protein (nutrient)">protein</a> and rich in <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamins</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dietary_mineral" title="Dietary mineral">minerals</a>, miso played an important <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutritional</a> role in <a href="/wiki/History_of_Japan#Feudal_Japan_.281185-1603.29" title="History of Japan">feudal Japan</a>. Miso is still widely used in Japan, both in traditional and modern cooking, and has been gaining worldwide interest.</p>
<p>Typically, miso is salty, but its flavor and aroma depend on various factors in the ingredients and fermentation process. Different varieties of miso have been described as salty, sweet, earthy, fruity, and savory. The traditional Chinese analogue of miso is known as <a href="/wiki/Tauco" title="Tauco">dòujiàng</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miso&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origin of the miso of Japan is not completely clear.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain">Grain</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_(food)" title="Fish (food)">fish</a> misos had been manufactured in Japan since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neolithic_era" title="Neolithic era">Neolithic era</a> (<a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Dmon_period" title="Jōmon period">Jōmon period</a> (14,000–300 BC)).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> These are called "jōmon miso" and are similar to the early fish and soy-based sauces produced throughout East Asia.</li>
<li>This miso predecessor originated in China during the third century BC or earlier. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hishio&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hishio (page does not exist)">Hishio</a> and other fermented soy-based foods likely were introduced to Japan at the same time as Buddhism in the sixth century AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This fermented food was called "<a href="/wiki/Douchi" title="Douchi">shi</a>".</li>
</ul>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Kamakura_shogunate" title="Kamakura shogunate">Kamakura era</a> (1192–1333), a common meal was made up of a bowl of rice, some dried fish, a serving of miso, and a fresh <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetable</a>. Until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muromachi_era" title="Muromachi era">Muromachi era</a> (1337 to 1573), miso was made without grinding the soybeans, somewhat like <i><a href="/wiki/Natt%C5%8D" title="Nattō">nattō</a></i>. In the Muromachi era, <a href="/wiki/Bhikkhu" title="Bhikkhu">Buddhist monks</a> discovered that soybeans could be ground into a paste, spawning new cooking methods using miso to flavor other foods. In medieval times, the word <i>temaemiso</i>, meaning home-made miso, appeared. Miso production is a relatively simple process, so home-made versions spread throughout Japan. Miso was used as military provisions during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sengoku_era" title="Sengoku era">Sengoku era</a> and making miso was an important economic activity for <i><a href="/wiki/Daimy%C5%8D" title="Daimyō">daimyō</a></i>s of that era.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a> (1603–1868), miso was also called <i>hishio</i> (醤) and <i>kuki</i> (豆支) and various types of miso that fit with each local climate and culture emerged throughout Japan.</p>
<p>These days, miso is produced industrially in large quantities and traditional home-made miso has become a rarity. In recent years, many new types of miso have appeared. For example, ones with added soup stocks or calcium, or reduced salt for health, among other varieties, are available.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flavor">Flavor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miso&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Flavor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The taste, aroma, texture, and appearance of miso all vary by region and season. Other important variables that contribute to the flavor of a particular miso include temperature, duration of fermentation, salt content, variety of <i>kōji</i>, and fermenting vessel. The most common flavor categories of miso are:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Shiromiso</i>, "white miso"</li>
<li><i>Akamiso</i>, "red miso"</li>
<li><i>Awasemiso</i>, "mixed miso"</li>
</ul>
<p>Although white and red (<i>shiromiso</i> and <i>akamiso</i>) are the most common types of misos available, different varieties may be preferred in particular regions of Japan. In the eastern <a href="/wiki/Kant%C5%8D_region" title="Kantō region">Kantō region</a> that includes <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>, the darker brownish <i>akamiso</i> is popular while in the western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kansai" title="Kansai">Kansai</a> region encompassing <a href="/wiki/Osaka" title="Osaka">Osaka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kobe" title="Kobe">Kobe</a> the lighter <i>shiromiso</i> is preferred.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ingredients">Ingredients</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miso&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ingredients">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ingredients used to produce miso may include any mix of <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybeans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buckwheat" title="Buckwheat">buckwheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a> seed, and <a href="/wiki/Cycad" title="Cycad">cycad</a>, among others. Lately, producers in other countries have also begun selling miso made from <a href="/wiki/Chickpea" title="Chickpea">chickpeas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azuki_bean" title="Azuki bean">azuki beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amaranth" title="Amaranth">amaranth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Quinoa" title="Quinoa">quinoa</a>. Fermentation time ranges from as little as five days to several years. The wide variety of Japanese miso is difficult to classify, but is commonly done by grain type, color, taste, and background.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>mugi</i> (麦): barley</li>
<li><i>tsubu</i> (粒): whole wheat/barley</li>
<li><i>genmai</i> (玄米): <a href="/wiki/Brown_rice" title="Brown rice">brown rice</a></li>
<li><i>moromi</i> (醪): chunky, healthy (<i>kōji</i> is unblended)</li>
<li><i>nanban</i> (南蛮): mixed with hot chili pepper for dipping sauce</li>
<li><i>taima</i> (大麻): hemp seed</li>
<li><i>sobamugi</i> (蕎麦): buckwheat</li>
<li><i>hadakamugi</i> (裸麦): rye</li>
<li><i>nari</i> (蘇鉄): made from <a href="/wiki/Cycad" title="Cycad">cycad</a> pulp, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> temple diet</li>
<li><i>gokoku</i> (五穀): "five-grain": soy, wheat, barley, <a href="/wiki/Proso_millet" title="Proso millet">proso millet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Foxtail_millet" title="Foxtail millet">foxtail millet</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Many regions have their own specific variation on the miso standard. For example, the soybeans used in <a href="/wiki/Sendai" title="Sendai">Sendai</a> miso are much more coarsely mashed than in normal soy miso.</p>
<p>Miso made with rice such as <i>shinshu</i> and <i>shiro</i> are called <i>kome</i> miso.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miso&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Types of miso are divided by their main ingredients.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Kome</i> miso (米味噌) or "rice miso" can be yellow, yellowish white, red, etc. Whitish miso is made from boiled soybeans, and reddish miso is made from steamed soybeans. <i>Kome</i> miso is consumed more in eastern Japan and the Hokuriku and Kinki areas.</li>
<li><i>Mugi</i> miso (麦味噌) or "barley miso" is a whitish miso which is produced in Kyushu, western Chugoku, and Shikoku areas. Another reddish <i>mugi</i> miso is produced in the northern Kanto area. <i>Mugi</i> miso has a peculiar smell.</li>
<li><i>Mame</i> miso (豆味噌) or "soybean miso" is a darker, more reddish brown than <i>kome</i> miso. This is not so sweet as some other varieties, but has some astringency and good <i>umami</i>(旨味). This miso requires a long maturing term. <i>Mame</i> miso is consumed mostly in Aichi prefecture, part of Gifu prefecture, and part of Mie prefecture. Soybean (grain-free) miso is also labeled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hatcho_miso" title="Hatcho miso">hatcho miso</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Chougou</i> (調合) or 'Awase' (合わせ) miso, or "mixed miso" comes in many types, because it is a mixture or compound of other varieties of miso. This may improve the weak points of each type of miso. For example, <i>mame</i> miso is very salty, but when combined with <i>kome</i> miso the finished product has a mild taste.</li>
<li><i>Akamiso</i> (赤味噌) or red miso is aged, sometimes for more than one year. Therefore, due to the <a href="/wiki/Maillard_reaction" title="Maillard reaction">Maillard reaction</a>, the color changes gradually from white to red or black, thus giving it the name red miso. Characteristics of the flavor are saltiness and some astringency with umami. It is often a much stronger-tasting miso. Factors in the depth of color are the formula of the soybeans and the quantity used. Generally, steamed soybeans are more deeply colored than boiled soybeans.</li>
<li><i>Shiromiso</i> (白味噌) or white miso is the most widely produced miso, made in many regions of the country. Its main ingredients are rice, barley, and a small quantity of soybeans. If a greater quantity of soybeans were added, the miso would be red or brown. Compared with red miso, white miso has a very short fermentation time. The taste is sweet, and the umami is soft or light (compared to red miso).</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fermentation">Fermentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miso&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fermentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Miso's unique properties and flavour profile can be attributed to the compounds produced through the fermentation process. Miso, depending on the variety, consists of a starter culture called <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Koji" title="Koji">Koji</a>, soybeans, and usually a grain (either rice, barley, or rye).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The miso goes through a two step process; first creating the koji, and second the koji is combined with the other components and the mixture is left to be enzymatic digested, fermented and aged.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creating_koji">Creating koji</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miso&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Creating koji">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Koji is produced by introducing the mould, <i><a href="/wiki/Aspergillus_oryzae" title="Aspergillus oryzae">Aspergillus oryzae</a></i> onto steamed brown rice. This mould culture comes from dried <i>A. oryzae</i> spores called 'tane-koji' or 'starter koji' and is isolated from plant matter (usually rice) and cultivated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup> In the past, the natural presence of <i>A. oryzae</i> spores was relied upon to create koji, but because of the difficulty of producing the culture, tane-koji is added almost exclusively in both industrial and traditional production of miso. Tane-koji is produced much in the same way as koji, but also has a small portion of wood ash added to the mixture<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> which gives important nutrients to the fungus as well as promotes sporulation.</p>
<p><i>A. oryzae</i> is an aerobic fungus and is the most active fermenting agents in Koji<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> as it produces amylolytic, and proteolytic enzymes which are essential to creating the final miso product. Amyloytic enzymes such as amylase aid in the breakdown of starch in the grains to sugar and dextrin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> while proteolytic enzymes such as protease catalyze the breakdown of proteins into smaller peptides or amino acids. These both aid in the enzymatic digestion of the mixture of rice and soybeans. Depending on the strain of <i>A. oryzae</i>, enzymatic composition varies thereby changing the characteristics of the final miso product. For example, the strain used to create the sweeter white miso would likely produce a higher content of amylolytic enzymes, while comparatively a soybean miso might have a higher content of proteolytic enzyme.</p>
<p>To create optimal conditions for enzymatic production and the growth of <i>A. oryzae</i>, the koji's environment must be carefully regulated. Temperature, humidity and oxygen content, are all important factors in not only maximizing mould growth and enzyme production, but to prevent other harmful bacteria from producing. Once the koji has reached a desirable flavour profile it is usually mixed with salt to prevent further fermentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although other strains of fungi have been used to produce Koji, <i>A. oryzae</i> does not produce aflatoxin (a highly toxic and carcinogenic mycotoxin);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> this coupled with its other properties make it most desirable to isolate.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storage_and_preparation">Storage and preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miso&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Storage and preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Miso typically comes as a paste in a sealed container requiring refrigeration after opening. Natural miso is a living food containing many beneficial microorganisms such as <i><a href="/wiki/Tetragenococcus_halophilus" title="Tetragenococcus halophilus">Tetragenococcus halophilus</a></i> which can be killed by overcooking. For this reason, the miso should be added to soups or other foods being prepared just before they are removed from the heat. Using miso without any cooking may be even better.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shurtleff_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shurtleff-11">[11]</a></sup> Outside Japan, a popular practice is to only add miso to foods that have cooled to preserve <i><a href="/wiki/Aspergillus_oryzae" title="Aspergillus oryzae">kōjikin</a></i> cultures in miso. Nonetheless, miso and soy foods play a large role in the Japanese diet, and many cooked miso dishes are popular.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miso&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Miso_soup" title="Miso soup">Miso soup</a></div>
<p>Miso is a part of many Japanese-style meals. It most commonly appears as the main ingredient of <a href="/wiki/Miso_soup" title="Miso soup">miso soup</a>, which is eaten daily by much of the Japanese population. The pairing of plain rice and miso soup is considered a fundamental unit of <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cuisine" title="Japanese cuisine">Japanese cuisine</a>. This pairing is the basis of a traditional Japanese breakfast.</p>
<p>Miso is used in many other types of soup and soup-like dishes, including some kinds of <i><a href="/wiki/Ramen" title="Ramen">ramen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Udon" title="Udon">udon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nabemono" title="Nabemono">nabe</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Imoni" title="Imoni">imoni</a></i>. Generally, such dishes have the title <i>miso</i> prefixed to their name (for example, <i>miso-udon</i>), and have a heavier, earthier flavor and aroma compared to other Japanese soups that are not miso-based.</p>
<p>Many traditional confections use a sweet, thick miso glaze, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Mochi" title="Mochi">mochi</a><a href="/wiki/Dango" title="Dango">dango</a></i>. Miso-glazed treats are strongly associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_festival" title="Japanese festival">Japanese festivals</a>, although they are available year-round at supermarkets. The consistency of miso glaze ranges from thick and <a href="/wiki/Taffy_(candy)" title="Taffy (candy)">taffy</a>-like to thin and drippy.</p>
<p>Soy miso is used to make a type of <a href="/wiki/Pickling" title="Pickling">pickle</a> called <i>misozuke</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> These pickles are typically made from <a href="/wiki/Cucumber" title="Cucumber">cucumber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daikon" title="Daikon">daikon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nappa_cabbage" title="Nappa cabbage">Nappa cabbage</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Eggplant" title="Eggplant">eggplant</a>, and are sweeter and less salty than the standard <a href="/wiki/Tsukemono" title="Tsukemono">Japanese salt pickle</a>.</p>
<p>Other foods with miso as an ingredient include:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>dengaku</i> (sweetened miso used for grilling)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Yakimochi" title="Yakimochi">yakimochi</a> (charcoal-grilled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mochi_(food)" title="Mochi (food)">mochi</a> covered in miso)</i></li>
<li>miso-braised <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetables</a> or <a href="/wiki/Edible_mushroom" title="Edible mushroom">mushrooms</a></li>
<li>marinades: fish or chicken can be marinated in miso and <i><a href="/wiki/Sake" title="Sake">sake</a></i> overnight to be grilled.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Corn_on_the_cob" title="Corn on the cob">corn on the cob</a> in Japan is often coated with <i>shiro miso</i>, wrapped in foil and grilled.</li>
<li>sauces: sauces like <i>misoyaki</i> (a variant on <a href="/wiki/Teriyaki" title="Teriyaki">teriyaki</a>)</li>
<li>dips: used as a dip to eat with vegetables (e.g. cucumbers, daikon, <a href="/wiki/Carrot" title="Carrot">carrots</a>, etc.)</li>
<li>side dish: miso is often eaten not only as a condiment, but also as a side dish. Mixed or cooked miso with spices and/or vegetables is called <i>okazu-miso</i> (おかず味噌), often eaten along with hot rice or spread over <i><a href="/wiki/Onigiri" title="Onigiri">onigiri</a>.</i></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrition_and_health">Nutrition and health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miso&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nutrition and health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Claims that miso is high in <a href="/wiki/Cobalamin" title="Cobalamin">vitamin B<sub>12</sub></a> have been contradicted in some studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some experts suggest that miso is a source of <i><a href="/wiki/Lactobacillus_acidophilus" title="Lactobacillus acidophilus">Lactobacillus acidophilus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Miso is relatively high in salt which can contribute to increased blood pressure in the small percentage of the population with sodium-sensitive <a href="/wiki/Prehypertension" title="Prehypertension">prehypertension</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a>.</p>
