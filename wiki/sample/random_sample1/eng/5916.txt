<p><i><b>Mysterious Skin</b></i> is a 2004 Dutch-American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drama_film" title="Drama film">drama film</a> directed by American filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Araki" title="Gregg Araki">Gregg Araki</a>, who also wrote the screenplay based on <a href="/wiki/Scott_Heim" title="Scott Heim">Scott Heim</a>'s 1995 novel of the same name. The film is Araki's eighth, premiering at the <a href="/wiki/61st_Venice_International_Film_Festival" title="61st Venice International Film Festival">61st Venice International Film Festival</a> in 2004, although it was not more widely distributed until 2005.</p>
<p><i>Mysterious Skin</i> tells the story of two pre-adolescent boys who are sexually abused by their baseball coach, and how it affects their lives in different ways into their young adulthood. One boy becomes a reckless, sexually adventurous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Male_prostitute" title="Male prostitute">male prostitute</a>, while the other retreats into a reclusive fantasy of <a href="/wiki/Alien_abduction" title="Alien abduction">alien abduction</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mysterious_Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the summer of 1981 in Hutchinson, Kansas, eight-year-olds Neil McCormick and Brian Lackey, teammates in Little League, both experience life-altering events. Neil, the son of an irresponsible single mother and already discovering his homosexuality, is <a href="/wiki/Child_sexual_abuse" title="Child sexual abuse">sexually abused</a> by his Little League coach, who leaves town after that summer. Brian, with a neglectful father and a mother who's often working, remembers it starting to rain during a game. The next thing he remembers is being in a crawl space in his house with a bloody nose, having no memory of the intervening five hours.</p>
<p>Neil views the coach's abuse as love and becomes mainly attracted to "<a href="/wiki/Bear_(gay_culture)" title="Bear (gay culture)">bearish</a>" middle-aged men. He begins prostituting himself at the age of 15. Three years later, he moves to New York City, where his best friend Wendy Peterson now lives, and continues prostituting there. Neil has an emotional encounter with a "customer" dying from AIDS. Though Neil suspects the man wants sex, he actually wants nothing more than to feel another person's touch. The encounter leads Neil to start to withdraw from prostitution and take a job as a cashier.</p>
<p>For years after coming to in the crawl space, Brian suffers from chronic nosebleeds, blackouts and bedwetting. He has unsettling <a href="/wiki/Recurring_dream" title="Recurring dream">recurring dreams</a> about being touched by a strange, bluish hand. These odd dreams lead Brian to suspect that he may have been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abduction_phenomenon" title="Abduction phenomenon">abducted by aliens</a>. Eventually, another boy wearing the same Little League uniform begins to appear with him in these dreams. When he's 18, Brian meets a woman named Avalyn Friesen who also believes she was abducted by aliens. They begin to form a friendship, but when she comes onto him, he panics and refuses to speak to her again.</p>
<p>While trying to untangle his confused memories, Brian sees a photo of his Little League team, recognizing a young Neil as the boy from his dreams. Taking the initiative to meet his former teammate, Brian instead meets Neil's mother and his friend Eric Pressly, and through him, he learns more about Neil. After being beaten and raped by a trick, Neil returns to Hutchinson to spend Christmas with his mother. Then, Neil and Brian meet for the first time in over a decade. After breaking into the house that was previously rented by the baseball coach, Neil tells Brian what happened that night: after the baseball game was rained out and Brian didn't have a ride home, the coach offered to drive Brian home with Neil. Instead, they all went to the coach's house, where the coach performed sex acts on the boys and made them perform sex acts on each other. A porch light caused the atmosphere to have an eerie blue color. After the coach and Neil put Brian's clothes back on, Brian collapsed face-first onto the floor, causing the bloody nose. Learning the truth at last, Brian breaks down crying and is comforted by Neil as Christmas carolers sing "<a href="/wiki/Silent_Night" title="Silent Night">Silent Night</a>".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mysterious_Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Both <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Gordon-Levitt" title="Joseph Gordon-Levitt">Joseph Gordon-Levitt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Trachtenberg" title="Michelle Trachtenberg">Michelle Trachtenberg</a>, by the time the film went into production, were looking for <a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">independent films</a> where making money was not the main goal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ign-3">[3]</a></sup> Trachtenberg was filming <i><a href="/wiki/EuroTrip" title="EuroTrip">EuroTrip</a></i> (2004) in Prague when she first received the script, and quickly decided to join production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ign-3">[3]</a></sup> Gordon-Levitt was especially praiseful of Araki for allowing him to join production, and commented in a 2005 interview: "It is a really different role for me, and I'll always be really grateful to Gregg for believing that I could do a role like this. I've played the nice kid, and the smart one or funny one and even the angry one, but Gregg was the first one to call me sexy, and I'll always be really grateful for that."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-ign-3">[3]</a></sup> Araki approached Gordon-Levitt, who was by then struggling to find work for over a year, after seeing him in <i><a href="/wiki/Manic_(film)" title="Manic (film)">Manic</a></i> (2001).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sf_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-sf-4">[4]</a></sup> Made on a low budget, filming commenced in August 2003 and lasted only three weeks, which gave the cast and crew no possibility of doing retakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-ign-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of measures were taken to avoid exposing the child actors to the sexual and abusive aspects of the story. Although their parents were given the entire shooting script to review, the boys were given separate scripts which included only the activities they would be performing, and their roles and the characters' relationships were explained to them in innocent terms. All of the sexual abuse involving children is implied rather than being directly depicted, and the scenes in which this seduction and abuse takes place were filmed with each actor performing alone and addressing the camera rather than the other actor, then edited together, so the children did not see or hear the performance by the adult actor playing the abuser. (This subjective approach to filming was consequently used in various places throughout the film.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mysterious_Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Mysterious Skin</i> received very positive reviews. The film received an 84% "fresh" rating on review aggregator <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> based on 103 reviews with an average rating of 7.3 out of 10. The sites consensus states: "Bold performances and sensitive, spot-on direction make watching this difficult tale of trauma and abuse a thought-provoking, resonant experience."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which uses a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weighted_mean" title="Weighted mean">"metascore"</a>, the film holds 73 out of 100 based on 32 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lou Lumenick from the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Post" title="New York Post">New York Post</a></i> commented, "Not for the squeamish, but it is a beautifully crafted and thoughtful film that genuinely provokes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryll_2014_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ryll_2014-9">[9]</a></sup> Ella Taylor from <a href="/wiki/LA_Weekly" title="LA Weekly">LA Weekly</a> wrote “A warped, but beautiful and strangely hopeful, coming-of-age tale.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryll_2014_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ryll_2014-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> gave <i>Mysterious Skin</i> 3.5 out of a possible 4 stars, describing it as "at once the most harrowing and, strangely, the most touching film I have seen about child abuse". Steven Rhea of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Philadelphia_Inquirer" title="The Philadelphia Inquirer">The Philadelphia Inquirer</a></i> awarded the film 3 out of 4 stars, stating that "Mysterious Skin" ultimately "manages to deal with its raw, awful subject matter in ways that are both challenging and illuminating".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Gordon-Levitt was praised by critics for his performance, and the actor has stated that people on the streets had come up to him to applaud his performance in the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-ign-3">[3]</a></sup> His portrayal of a teenage hustler inspired director <a href="/wiki/Scott_Frank" title="Scott Frank">Scott Frank</a> to cast him in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lookout_(2007_film)" title="The Lookout (2007 film)">The Lookout</a></i> (2007).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sf_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-sf-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to psychologist <a href="/wiki/Richard_Gartner" title="Richard Gartner">Richard Gartner</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> the novel <i>Mysterious Skin</i> is an uncommonly accurate portrayal of the long-term effect of <a href="/wiki/Child_sexual_abuse" title="Child sexual abuse">child sexual abuse</a> on boys.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rating_issues">Rating issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mysterious_Skin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rating issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The US <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MPAA" title="MPAA">MPAA</a> rated the film <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NC-17" title="NC-17">NC-17</a>, which the studio appealed unsuccessfully. The film was released in the US without rating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film was the subject of some controversy in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Family_Association" title="Australian Family Association">Australian Family Association</a> requested a review of its classification, seeking to have the film outlawed due to its depiction of <a href="/wiki/Pedophilia" title="Pedophilia">pedophilia</a>. They suggested that the film could be used by pedophiles for sexual gratification or to help them <a href="/wiki/Child_grooming" title="Child grooming">groom</a> children for <a href="/wiki/Child_sexual_abuse" title="Child sexual abuse">sexual abuse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The six-member Classification Review Board voted four-to-two in favour of maintaining an R18+ rating. The controversy is referenced in a review excerpt from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sydney_Morning_Herald" title="The Sydney Morning Herald">The Sydney Morning Herald</a></i> on the <a href="/wiki/DVD_region_code" title="DVD region code">Region 4</a> <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> that reads: "How anyone could have wanted it banned is beyond me";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> film critic <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Pomeranz" title="Margaret Pomeranz">Margaret Pomeranz</a> expressed that the film does more for the case against pedophilia, stating: "People who do indulge in crimes like that, if they saw this film they would understand the damage that they do."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
