<p><i><b>The Mist</b></i> (also known as <i><b>Stephen King's The Mist</b></i>) is a 2007 American <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> <a href="/wiki/Horror_film" title="Horror film">horror film</a> based on the 1980 <a href="/wiki/The_Mist" title="The Mist">novella of the same name</a> by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_King" title="Stephen King">Stephen King</a>. The film was written and directed by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Darabont" title="Frank Darabont">Frank Darabont</a>, who had previously adapted King's works <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shawshank_Redemption" title="The Shawshank Redemption">The Shawshank Redemption</a></i> (1994 film based on King's <a href="/wiki/Rita_Hayworth_and_Shawshank_Redemption" title="Rita Hayworth and Shawshank Redemption">1982 novella</a>) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Green_Mile_(film)" title="The Green Mile (film)">The Green Mile</a></i> (1999 film based on King's <a href="/wiki/The_Green_Mile_(novel)" title="The Green Mile (novel)">1996 novel</a>). Darabont had been interested in adapting <i>The Mist</i> for the big screen since the 1980s. The film features an <a href="/wiki/Ensemble_cast" title="Ensemble cast">ensemble cast</a> including <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jane" title="Thomas Jane">Thomas Jane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marcia_Gay_Harden" title="Marcia Gay Harden">Marcia Gay Harden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Witwer" title="Samuel Witwer">Samuel Witwer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toby_Jones" title="Toby Jones">Toby Jones</a>, and future <i><a href="/wiki/The_Walking_Dead_(TV_series)" title="The Walking Dead (TV series)">The Walking Dead</a></i> actors <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_DeMunn" title="Jeffrey DeMunn">Jeffrey DeMunn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Gabriel_Pareja" title="Juan Gabriel Pareja">Juan Gabriel Pareja</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laurie_Holden" title="Laurie Holden">Laurie Holden</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Melissa_McBride" title="Melissa McBride">Melissa McBride</a>.</p>
<p>Filming for <i>The Mist</i> began in <a href="/wiki/Shreveport,_Louisiana" title="Shreveport, Louisiana">Shreveport, Louisiana</a>, in February, 2007. The film was commercially released in the United States and Canada on November 21, 2007; it performed well at the box office and received generally positive reviews. Darabont has since revealed that he had "always had it in mind to shoot <i>The Mist</i> in black and white", a decision inspired by such iconic films as <i><a href="/wiki/Night_of_the_Living_Dead" title="Night of the Living Dead">Night of the Living Dead</a></i> (1968) and the "pre-color" work of <a href="/wiki/Ray_Harryhausen" title="Ray Harryhausen">Ray Harryhausen</a>. While the film's cinematic release was in color, the director has described the black and white print (released on <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray" title="Blu-ray">Blu-ray</a> in 2008) as his "preferred version."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The director revised the ending of the film to be darker than the novella's ending, a change to which King was amenable. Darabont also sought unique creature designs to differentiate them from his creatures in past films.</p>
<p>Although a <a href="/wiki/Monster_movie" title="Monster movie">monster movie</a>, the central theme explores what ordinary people will be driven to do under extraordinary circumstances. The plot revolves around members of the small town of <a href="/wiki/Bridgton,_Maine" title="Bridgton, Maine">Bridgton, Maine</a> who, after a severe <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> causes the power to go out the night before, meet in a <a href="/wiki/Supermarket" title="Supermarket">supermarket</a> to pick up supplies. While they struggle to survive an unnatural mist which envelops the town and conceals vicious, otherworldly monsters, extreme tensions rise among the survivors.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Mist_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following a violent <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a>, David Drayton leaves his wife Stephanie at home while he ventures into town with their neighbor, Brent Norton, to buy supplies for repairs. Drayton takes his eight-year-old son Billy and Norton to a grocery store, where the patrons notice unusual police and military activity. Moments after a panicked and injured man, Dan, runs in warning of danger, a thick mist envelops the store, accompanied by earthquake-like tremors and the town's tornado siren. A mentally unstable religious woman, Mrs. Carmody, decides that this is the beginning of <a href="/wiki/Armageddon" title="Armageddon">Armageddon</a>. A shaken woman, against objections from the others, leaves the store to go home to her children. While investigating the store's generator, a bag-boy named Norm volunteers to go outside, but is snatched and pulled into the mist by a tentacle connected to an unseen monster.</p>
<p>Refusing to believe Drayton, Norton heads outside with a small group to seek help, only to be attacked and killed, prompting the rest of the survivors to barricade the shop-front windows. That night, enormous insects land on the windows and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pterodactyl" title="Pterodactyl">pterodactyl</a>-like animals attack them, breaking the glass and allowing the creatures to begin fighting inside the store. One of the insects lands on Mrs. Carmody, but doesn't sting her because she keeps still during her prayer. As a result, she starts preaching more and quickly gains followers among the distraught survivors.</p>
<p>A trip to the neighboring pharmacy to gather medical supplies goes horribly wrong and two people are killed by giant spiders. Mrs. Carmody's faction now contains most of the survivors. Two soldiers commit suicide, and a third, Private Jessup, reveals that the local military base was filled with rumors about the Arrowhead Project, the government's attempt to look into <a href="/wiki/Multiverse" title="Multiverse">other dimensions</a>, and that the scientists responsible for the experiment may have inadvertently opened a doorway into a dimension containing the creatures that are now invading the town. Mrs. Carmody convinces her followers to blame Jessup and he is stabbed and ejected from the store, quickly falling prey to a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Praying_mantis" title="Praying mantis">praying mantis</a>-like creature.</p>
<p>David and eight others secretly gather supplies to flee, but Mrs. Carmody stops them. After stealing the supplies, she gathers her followers and orders them to murder Billy and Amanda Dumfries; however, store assistant manager Ollie quickly intervenes and kills Mrs. Carmody by shooting her with Amanda's gun. After Ollie threatens the former's traumatized followers, now returning to their senses, into standing down at gunpoint, David's group exits, but only David, Billy, Dan, Amanda, and Irene make it to the car, as Ollie is eaten by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Praying_mantis" title="Praying mantis">praying mantis</a>-like creature, two others are attacked by the giant spiders, and one runs back to the market and is allowed back in.</p>
<p>Driving through the mist, David finds his house destroyed and his wife dead. Devastated, he drives the group south, passing destroyed vehicles and seeing a gigantic six-legged, tentacled beast. When they run out of gas, the group decides there is no point in going on. David shoots the others (with their assent, except for his son who is not old enough to understand) rather than have them endure horrifying deaths, but is left with no bullet to use on himself. He leaves the car and waits to be killed, but the mist suddenly recedes, revealing that the U.S. Army has arrived, rescued other survivors, and restored order. Among the survivors is the woman who left the store at the phenomenon's onset, who is completely unscathed and accompanied by her two children. David realizes that they were only moments from being rescued and had likely been driving away from help the entire time. He then drops to his knees and begins screaming in sorrow.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Mist_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Mist_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Director <a href="/wiki/Frank_Darabont" title="Frank Darabont">Frank Darabont</a> first read <a href="/wiki/Stephen_King" title="Stephen King">Stephen King</a>'s 1980 <a href="/wiki/Novella" title="Novella">novella</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mist" title="The Mist">The Mist</a></i> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Forces_(book)" title="Dark Forces (book)">Dark Forces</a></i> anthology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sdcc_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-sdcc-4">[4]</a></sup> and originally expressed interest in directing a <a href="/wiki/Film_adaptation" title="Film adaptation">film adaptation</a> for his directing debut. He instead filmed <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shawshank_Redemption" title="The Shawshank Redemption">The Shawshank Redemption</a></i>, also based on another King novella.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-envelops_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-envelops-5">[5]</a></sup> In October 1994, after completing <i>The Shawshank Redemption</i>, Darabont reiterated his interest in filming <i>The Mist</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Darabont did not immediately follow through, instead directing the <a href="/wiki/The_Green_Mile_(film)" title="The Green Mile (film)">1999 film adaptation</a> of Stephen King's <a href="/wiki/The_Green_Mile_(novel)" title="The Green Mile (novel)"><i>The Green Mile</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Darabont eventually set up a <a href="/wiki/First_look_deal" title="First look deal">first look deal</a> for <i>The Mist</i> with <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a>, having been entrusted feature film rights by Stephen King.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-envelops_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-envelops-5">[5]</a></sup> By December 2004, Darabont said that he had begun writing an adapted screenplay for <i>The Mist</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and by October 2006, the project moved from Paramount to <a href="/wiki/Dimension_Films" title="Dimension Films">Dimension Films</a>, with Darabont attached to direct and actor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jane" title="Thomas Jane">Thomas Jane</a> in negotiations to join the cast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-envelops_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-envelops-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Mist_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table cellspacing="5" class="toccolours" style="float: right; margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 2em; font-size: 85%; background:#c6dbf7; color:black; width:30em; max-width: 40%;">
<tr>
<td style="text-align: left;">"The story is less about the monsters outside than about the monsters inside, the people you're stuck with, your friends and neighbors breaking under the strain."</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td style="text-align: right;">— Darabont on <i>The Mist</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Director Darabont chose to film <i>The Mist</i> after filming the "straighter dramas" <i>The Shawshank Redemption</i> and <i>The Green Mile</i> because he "wanted to make a very direct, muscular kind of film." Darabont conceived of a new ending in translating the novella for the big screen. Author King praised Darabont's new ending, describing it as one that would be unsettling for studios. King said, "The ending is such a jolt—wham! It's frightening. But people who go to see a horror movie don't necessarily want to be sent out with a <a href="/wiki/Pollyanna" title="Pollyanna">Pollyanna</a> ending."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Darabont described <i>The Mist</i> as quaint in its elements of monsters and fear of the unknown compared to the contemporary popularity of films with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torture_porn" title="Torture porn">torture porn</a>. The director saw <i>The Mist</i> as a throwback to <a href="/wiki/Paddy_Chayefsky" title="Paddy Chayefsky">Paddy Chayefsky</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>, explaining, "It's people at each other." He highlighted the element of fear in the film in how it compelled people to behave differently. Darabont said, "How primitive do people get? It's <i><a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Flies" title="Lord of the Flies">Lord of the Flies</a></i> that happens to have some cool monsters in it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sdcc_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-sdcc-4">[4]</a></sup> He also drew parallels to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Twilight_Zone_(1959_TV_series)" title="The Twilight Zone (1959 TV series)">The Twilight Zone</a></i> episode "<a href="/wiki/The_Monsters_Are_Due_on_Maple_Street" title="The Monsters Are Due on Maple Street">The Monsters Are Due on Maple Street</a>" and the 1944 film <i><a href="/wiki/Lifeboat_(film)" title="Lifeboat (film)">Lifeboat</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shock_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-shock-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the novella, the character David Drayton - who is married - has a sexual encounter with Amanda Dumfries, who is also married. Darabont did not want to attempt conveying on screen the protagonist being involved in an extramarital affair. The characters in the film, portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jane" title="Thomas Jane">Thomas Jane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laurie_Holden" title="Laurie Holden">Laurie Holden</a> respectively, instead share a more emotional relationship. Jane explained, "We kind of form a little family, sort of surrogate family where my son and I’m a father and she becomes the mother to the son. We become a little unit as we’re trying to get through this nightmare together." Holden compared the nightmare to <a href="/wiki/Effect_of_Hurricane_Katrina_on_the_Louisiana_Superdome" title="Effect of Hurricane Katrina on the Louisiana Superdome">what refugees experienced</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louisiana_Superdome" title="Louisiana Superdome">Louisiana Superdome</a> during <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-about-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the origin of the mist in the movie is never explained in great detail, Frank Darabont did write an opening scene in a draft dated 5 August 2005, in which the thunderstorm causes a malfunction at the Arrowhead Project's lab that allows the portal to another dimension to stay open too long. The scene was never filmed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Mist_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 2006, Jane finalized negotiations with the studio to join the cast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In January 2007, actors <a href="/wiki/Andre_Braugher" title="Andre Braugher">Andre Braugher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laurie_Holden" title="Laurie Holden">Laurie Holden</a> joined Jane for the cast of <i>The Mist</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-float_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-float-13">[13]</a></sup> Production began the following February at StageWorks of Louisiana, a sound stage and movie production facility in <a href="/wiki/Shreveport,_Louisiana" title="Shreveport, Louisiana">Shreveport, Louisiana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Marcia_Gay_Harden" title="Marcia Gay Harden">Marcia Gay Harden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toby_Jones" title="Toby Jones">Toby Jones</a> joined the cast later in the month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_Sadler_(actor)" title="William Sadler (actor)">William Sadler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_DeMunn" title="Jeffrey DeMunn">Jeffrey DeMunn</a>, and Brian Libby, each of whom appeared in Darabont's previous Stephen King adaptations <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shawshank_Redemption" title="The Shawshank Redemption">The Shawshank Redemption</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Green_Mile_(film)" title="The Green Mile (film)">The Green Mile</a></i>, were cast in supporting roles. Sadler had previously played Jane's role, David Drayton, in a 1986 <a href="/wiki/Audiobook" title="Audiobook">audiobook</a> version of <i>The Mist</i>. Darabont wanted to cast King in the supporting role that eventually went to Brian Libby, an offer that King turned down because he did not want to travel to film the part.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Darabont sought to pursue "a more fluid, ragged <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentary</a> kind of direction" with <i>The Mist</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-about-11">[11]</a></sup> so he contacted the camera crew from the TV series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shield" title="The Shield">The Shield</a></i>, after having directed one episode, to use their style in the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-con_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-con-17">[17]</a></sup> Darabont attempted to film <i>The Mist</i> <a href="/wiki/Digital_cinematography" title="Digital cinematography">digitally</a> but found that it "wound up looking too beautiful". The director chose to film with 400 ASA from <a href="/wiki/Fujifilm" title="Fujifilm">Fujifilm</a>, which gave footage a <a href="/wiki/Film_grain" title="Film grain">grainy effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shock_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-shock-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the opening shot, David is drawing in his room. The drawing is based on <a href="/wiki/Stephen_King" title="Stephen King">Stephen King</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dark_Tower_(series)" title="The Dark Tower (series)">Dark Tower</a></i> series, and was painted by movie poster designer <a href="/wiki/Drew_Struzan" title="Drew Struzan">Drew Struzan</a>. Darabont also included re-productions of Struzan's posters and illustrations for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shawshank_Redemption" title="The Shawshank Redemption">The Shawshank Redemption</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Green_Mile_(film)" title="The Green Mile (film)">The Green Mile</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/John_Carpenter" title="John Carpenter">John Carpenter</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Thing_(1982_film)" title="The Thing (1982 film)">The Thing</a></i>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guillermo_Del_Toro" title="Guillermo Del Toro">Guillermo Del Toro</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Pan%27s_Labyrinth" title="Pan's Labyrinth">Pan's Labyrinth</a></i>. Struzan would go on to produce a poster for <i>The Mist</i>, but this image was not used in the film's marketing campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Darabont collaborated with the production designer to create a mix of eras to avoid appearing as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Period_piece" title="Period piece">period piece</a> but also not looking so contemporary. Cell phones were used by characters in <i>The Mist</i>, but the <a href="/wiki/Military_police" title="Military police">military police</a> in the film did not dress in modern attire. While an MP also drove an old <a href="/wiki/Jeep" title="Jeep">Jeep</a> instead of a <a href="/wiki/Humvee" title="Humvee">Humvee</a>, other cars seen in the film are modern models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shock_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-shock-1">[1]</a></sup> The city police cars in the beginning of the film are a 1987 <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Caprice" title="Chevrolet Caprice">Chevrolet Caprice</a> and a 1988 <a href="/wiki/Ford_LTD_Crown_Victoria" title="Ford LTD Crown Victoria">Ford LTD Crown Victoria</a>, cars that were standard police vehicles in the late 1980s but have not been used in force since the late 1990s.</p>
<p>Over a hundred <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra_(actor)" title="Extra (actor)">extras</a> from Shreveport, Louisiana were included in <i>The Mist</i>. Unlike conventional application of extras in the background of a film, sixty of the hundred extras were interwoven with the film's ensemble cast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Additional elements giving the film a local flavor include the prominence of local Louisiana brands such as Zapp's potato chips. Exterior shots of the house at the beginning were in Shreveport. Exterior shots of the supermarket were in <a href="/wiki/Vivian,_Louisiana" title="Vivian, Louisiana">Vivian, Louisiana</a>. Also, if looked closely at, the shields on the side of the passing firetrucks early in the film identify them as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caddo_Parish" title="Caddo Parish">Caddo Parish</a> fire department. This is possibly a mistake as the film is allegedly set in <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Mist_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Darabont chose to use music to minimal effect in <i>The Mist</i> in order to capture the "heavier feel" of the darker ending he had written to replace the one from the novella. The director explained, "Sometimes movie music feels false. I've always felt that silent can be scarier than loud, a whisper more frightening than a bang, and we wanted to create a balance. We kept music to a minimum to keep that vérité, documentary feel." Darabont chose to overlay the song "The Host of Seraphim" by the band <a href="/wiki/Dead_Can_Dance" title="Dead Can Dance">Dead Can Dance</a>, a spiritual piece characterized by wailing and chanting. As a fan of Dead Can Dance, Darabont thought that the song played "as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Requiem_mass" title="Requiem mass">requiem mass</a> for the human race."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The original score was composed by Academy Award-nominated composer <a href="/wiki/Mark_Isham" title="Mark Isham">Mark Isham</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Mist_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Darabont hired artists <a href="/wiki/Jordu_Schell" title="Jordu Schell">Jordu Schell</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Wrightson" title="Bernie Wrightson">Bernie Wrightson</a> to assist in designing the creatures for the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-con_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-con-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Greg_Nicotero" title="Greg Nicotero">Greg Nicotero</a> worked on the film's creature design and make-up effects, while Everett Burrell served as the visual effects supervisor. Nicotero initially sketched out ideas for creature design when Darabont originally expressed interest in filming <i>The Mist</i> in the 1980s. When the project was greenlit, Nicotero, Burrell, and Darabont collaborated about the creature design at round-table meetings at CaféFX.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-about-11">[11]</a></sup> The studio for visual effects had been recommended to Darabont by <a href="/wiki/Guillermo_del_Toro" title="Guillermo del Toro">Guillermo del Toro</a> after Darabont asked the director who created the visual effects for <i>Pan's Labyrinth</i>.</p>
<p>Due to the creatures' being described in only a few sentences in the novella, Darabont sought to conceive of new designs. The challenge was creating designs that felt unique. Nicotero, who was versed in film history and genre history, reviewed past creature designs to avoid having similar designs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shock_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-shock-1">[1]</a></sup> When the designs were completed, Nicotero and Burrell educated the cast on the appearance of the creatures by showing puppets and the function of their eyes and mouths. The puppet demonstrations served as reference points for the cast, who had to respond to <a href="/wiki/Motion_capture" title="Motion capture">motion capture dots</a> during filming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-about-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Mist_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The Mist</i> was screened at the film festival ShowEast on October 18, 2007, at which director <a href="/wiki/Frank_Darabont" title="Frank Darabont">Frank Darabont</a> received the Kodak Award for Excellence in Filmmaking for his previous works <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shawshank_Redemption" title="The Shawshank Redemption">The Shawshank Redemption</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Green_Mile_(film)" title="The Green Mile (film)">The Green Mile</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Mist_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film has received positive reviews from critics. On the film review aggregate website <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, <i>The Mist</i> received a 73% approval rating, based on 140 reviews, with an average rating of 6.6/10, indicating generally favorable reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> On the website <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, the film has received a metascore of 58 out of 100 based on 29 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/James_Berardinelli" title="James Berardinelli">James Berardinelli</a> wrote of the film, "<i>The Mist</i> is what a horror film <i>should</i> be - dark, tense, and punctuated by just enough gore to keep the viewer's flinch reflex intact. ... Finally, after a long list of failures, someone has done justice in bringing one of King's horror stories to the screen. Though definitely not the feel-good movie of the season, this is a must-see for anyone who loves the genre and doesn't demand "<a href="/wiki/Splatter_film" title="Splatter film">torture porn</a>" from horror."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> was negative, however, writing: "If you have seen ads or trailers suggesting that horrible things pounce on people, and they make you think you want to see this movie, you will be correct. It is a competently made Horrible Things Pouncing on People Movie. If you think Frank Darabont has equaled the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shawshank_Redemption" title="The Shawshank Redemption">Shawshank</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Green_Mile_(film)" title="The Green Mile (film)">Green Mile</a></i> track record, you will be sadly mistaken."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bloody_Disgusting" title="Bloody Disgusting">Bloody Disgusting</a> ranked the film #4 on their list of the 'Top 20 Horror Films of the Decade', with the article saying "The scary stuff works extremely well, but what really drives this one home is Darabont’s focus on the divide that forms between two factions of the townspeople – the paranoid, Bible-thumping types and the more rational-minded, decidedly left-wing members of the populace. This allegorical microcosm of Bush Jr.-era America is spot on, and elevates an already-excellent film to even greater heights."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film was later nominated for three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturn_Awards" title="Saturn Awards">Saturn Awards</a>; Best Horror Film, Best Director, and a win for Best Supporting Actress for Harden.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Mist_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film was commercially released in the United States and Canada on November 21, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Over the opening weekend in the United States and Canada, <i>The Mist</i> grossed $8,931,973. As of August 9, 2009, the film grossed $25,593,755 in the United States and Canada and $27,560,960 in other territories for a worldwide total of $57,289,103.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Mist_2007_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Mist_2007-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Mist_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Mist</i> was released on DVD and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a> on March 25, 2008. The single-disc includes an audio commentary by writer/director Frank Darabont, eight deleted scenes with optional commentary, and "A Conversation With Stephen King and Frank Darabont" featurette.</p>
<p>The two-disc edition includes the exclusive black and white presentation of the film, as well as the color version, and five featurettes ("When Darkness Came: The Making of The Mist", "Taming the Beast: Shooting Scene 35", "Monsters Among Us: A Look at the Creature FX", "The Horror of It All: The Visual FX of The Mist", and "<a href="/wiki/Drew_Struzan" title="Drew Struzan">Drew Struzan</a>: Appreciation of an Artist").</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="TV_series">TV series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Mist_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: TV series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In November 2013, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Weinstein" title="Bob Weinstein">Bob Weinstein</a> revealed that he and Darabont were developing a 10-part television series based on the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In February 2016, <a href="/wiki/Spike_(TV_network)" title="Spike (TV network)">Spike</a> picked up the pilot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In April 2016, Spike ordered the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Emmy-winning director <a href="/wiki/Adam_Bernstein" title="Adam Bernstein">Adam Bernstein</a> will direct the pilot that will premiere in the summer of 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
