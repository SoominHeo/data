<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mississippian culture</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Mississippian culture</b> was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mound_builder_(people)" title="Mound builder (people)">mound-building</a> <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> civilization archeologists date from approximately 800 CE to 1600 CE, varying regionally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-range_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-range-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was composed of a series of urban settlements and satellite villages (suburbs) linked together by a loose trading network,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-WP-2">[2]</a></sup> the largest city being <a href="/wiki/Cahokia" title="Cahokia">Cahokia</a>, believed to be a major religious center. The civilization flourished from the southern shores of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> at Western New York and Western Pennsylvania in what is now the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Eastern Midwest</a>, extending south-southwest into the lower <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mississippi_Valley" title="Mississippi Valley">Mississippi Valley</a> and wrapping easterly around the southern foot of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appalachians" title="Appalachians">Appalachians</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barrier_range" title="Barrier range">barrier range</a> into what is now the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">Southeastern</a> <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-range_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-range-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Mississippian way of life began to develop in the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> Valley (for which it is named). Cultures in the tributary <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_River" title="Tennessee River">Tennessee River</a> Valley may have also begun to develop Mississippian characteristics at this point. Almost all dated Mississippian sites predate 1539–1540 (when <a href="/wiki/Hernando_de_Soto" title="Hernando de Soto">Hernando de Soto</a> explored the area),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> with notable exceptions being <a href="/wiki/Natchez_people" title="Natchez people">Natchez</a> communities that maintained Mississippian cultural practices into the 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_traits">Cultural traits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mississippian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cultural traits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>A number of cultural traits are recognized as being characteristic of the Mississippians. Although not all Mississippian peoples practiced all of the following activities, they were distinct from their ancestors in adoption of some or all of these traits.</p>
<ol>
<li>The construction of large, truncated <a href="/wiki/Earthworks_(archaeology)" title="Earthworks (archaeology)">earthwork</a> pyramid mounds, or <a href="/wiki/Platform_mound" title="Platform mound">platform mounds</a>. Such mounds were usually square, rectangular, or occasionally circular. Structures (domestic houses, temples, <a href="/wiki/Charnel_house#New_World" title="Charnel house">burial buildings</a>, or other) were usually constructed atop such mounds.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">Maize</a>-based agriculture. In most places, the development of Mississippian culture coincided with adoption of comparatively large-scale, intensive <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a> agriculture, which supported larger populations and craft specialization.</li>
<li>The adoption and use of riverine (or more rarely marine) shells as tempering agents in their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shell_tempered_pottery" title="Shell tempered pottery">shell tempered pottery</a>.</li>
<li>Widespread trade networks extending as far west as the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rockies</a>, north to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>, south to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, and east to the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.</li>
<li>The development of the <a href="/wiki/Chiefdom" title="Chiefdom">chiefdom</a> or complex chiefdom level of social complexity.</li>
<li>The development of institutionalized <a href="/wiki/Social_inequality" title="Social inequality">social inequality</a>.</li>
<li>A centralization of control of combined political and religious power in the hands of few or one.</li>
<li>The beginnings of a settlement hierarchy, in which one major center (with <a href="/wiki/Mound" title="Mound">mounds</a>) has clear influence or control over a number of lesser communities, which may or may not possess a smaller number of mounds.</li>
<li>The adoption of the paraphernalia of the <b><a href="/wiki/Southeastern_Ceremonial_Complex" title="Southeastern Ceremonial Complex">Southeastern Ceremonial Complex</a></b> (SECC), also called the Southern Cult. This is the belief system of the Mississippians as we know it. SECC items are found in Mississippian-culture sites from <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Aztalan_State_Park" title="Aztalan State Park">Aztalan State Park</a>) to the Gulf Coast, and from <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> to <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>. The SECC was frequently tied in to ritual game-playing, as with <a href="/wiki/Chunkey" title="Chunkey">chunkey</a>.</li>
</ol>
<p>The Mississippians had no <a href="/wiki/Writing_system" title="Writing system">writing system</a> or stone architecture. They worked naturally occurring metal deposits, such as hammering and <a href="/wiki/Annealing_(metallurgy)" title="Annealing (metallurgy)">annealing</a> copper for ritual objects like <a href="/wiki/Mississippian_copper_plates" title="Mississippian copper plates">Mississippian copper plates</a> and other decorations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> but did not smelt iron or practice bronze <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgy</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chronology">Chronology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mississippian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chronology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Mississippi stage is usually divided into three or more chronological periods. Each period is an arbitrary historical distinction varying regionally. At a particular site, each period may be considered to begin earlier or later, depending on the speed of adoption or development of given Mississippian traits. The "Mississippi period" should not be confused with the "Mississippian culture". The Mississippi period is the chronological stage, while Mississippian culture refers to the cultural similarities that characterize this society.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <b>Early Mississippi period</b> (c. 1000–1200 CE) had just transitioned from the <a href="/wiki/Woodland_period" title="Woodland period">Late Woodland</a> period way of life (500–1000). Different groups abandoned <a href="/wiki/Tribe" title="Tribe">tribal</a> lifeways for increasing complexity, sedentism, centralization, and agriculture. Production of surplus corn and attractions of the regional chiefdoms led to rapid population concentrations in major centers.</li>
<li>The <b>Middle Mississippi period</b> (c. 1200–1400) is the apex of the Mississippi era. The expansion of the great metropolis and ceremonial complex at <a href="/wiki/Cahokia" title="Cahokia">Cahokia</a> (in present-day Illinois), the formation of other complex <a href="/wiki/Chiefdom" title="Chiefdom">chiefdoms</a>, and the spread and development of SECC art and symbolism are characteristic changes of this period. The Mississippian traits listed above came to be widespread throughout the region.</li>
<li>The <b>Late Mississippi period</b> (c. 1400–1540) is characterized by increasing warfare, political turmoil, and population movement. The population of Cahokia dispersed early in this period (1350–1400), perhaps migrating to other rising political centers. More defensive structures are often seen at sites, and sometimes a decline in mound-building and large scale, public ceremonialism. Although some areas continued an essentially Middle Mississippian culture until the first significant contact with Europeans, the population of most areas had dispersed or were experiencing severe social stress by 1500.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Along with the contemporaneous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancestral_Pueblo_peoples" title="Ancestral Pueblo peoples">Ancestral Pueblo peoples</a>, these cultural collapses coincide with the global climate change of the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a>. Scholars theorize drought and the reduction of maize agriculture, together with possible deforestation and overhunting by the concentrated populations, forced them to move away from major sites. This period ended with European contact in the 16th century.</li>
</ul>

<p>The term <i>Middle Mississippian</i> is also used to describe the core of the classic Mississippian culture area. This area covers the central Mississippi River Valley, the lower Ohio River Valley, and most of the Mid-South area, including western and central Kentucky, western Tennessee, and northern Alabama and Mississippi. Sites in this area often contain large ceremonial platform mounds, residential complexes and are often encircled by earthen ditches and ramparts or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palisades" title="Palisades">palisades</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEAC_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-SEAC-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Middle Mississippian cultures, especially the Cahokia polity located near <a href="/wiki/East_St._Louis,_Illinois" title="East St. Louis, Illinois">East St. Louis, Illinois</a>, was very influential on neighboring societies. High status artifacts, including <a href="/wiki/Mississippian_stone_statuary#Cahokia_Style" title="Mississippian stone statuary">stone statuary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mississippian_culture_pottery#Cahokian_pottery" title="Mississippian culture pottery">elite pottery</a> associated with Cahokia, have been found far outside of the Middle Mississippian area. These items, especially the pottery, were also copied by local artists.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cahokia" title="Cahokia">Cahokia</a>: The largest and most complex Mississippian site and the largest Pre-Columbian settlement north of Mexico, Cahokia is considered to have been the most influential of the Mississippian culture centers.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Angel_Mounds" title="Angel Mounds">Angel Mounds</a>: A chiefdom in southern <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a> near <a href="/wiki/Evansville,_Indiana" title="Evansville, Indiana">Evansville</a>. It is thought by some archaeologists that the Late Mississippian <a href="/wiki/Caborn-Welborn_culture" title="Caborn-Welborn culture">Caborn-Welborn culture</a> developed from the <a href="/wiki/Angel_Phase" title="Angel Phase">Angel Phase</a> people around 1400 CE and lasted to around 1700 CE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kincaid_Mounds_State_Historic_Site" title="Kincaid Mounds State Historic Site">Kincaid Site</a>: A major Mississippian mound center in southern <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> across the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a> from <a href="/wiki/Paducah,_Kentucky" title="Paducah, Kentucky">Paducah, Kentucky</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Moundville_Archaeological_Site" title="Moundville Archaeological Site">Moundville</a>: Ranked with Cahokia as one of the two most important sites at the core of the Mississippian culture,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mississippian_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-mississippian-11">[11]</a></sup> located near <a href="/wiki/Tuscaloosa,_Alabama" title="Tuscaloosa, Alabama">Tuscaloosa, Alabama</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Parkin_Archeological_State_Park" title="Parkin Archeological State Park">The Parkin Site</a>: The <a href="/wiki/Type_site" title="Type site">type site</a> for the "Parkin phase", an expression of Late Mississippian culture, believed by many archaeologists to be the province of <a href="/wiki/Casqui" title="Casqui">Casqui</a> visited by Hernando de Soto in 1542.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Appalachian_Mississippian">South Appalachian Mississippian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mississippian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: South Appalachian Mississippian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Etowah_statues_HRoe_2007.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="550" data-file-width="700" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Etowah_statues_HRoe_2007.jpg/200px-Etowah_statues_HRoe_2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Etowah_statues_HRoe_2007.jpg/300px-Etowah_statues_HRoe_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Etowah_statues_HRoe_2007.jpg/400px-Etowah_statues_HRoe_2007.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Etowah_statues_HRoe_2007.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Mississippian_stone_statuary" title="Mississippian stone statuary">Stone effigies</a> found at the <a href="/wiki/Etowah_Indian_Mounds" title="Etowah Indian Mounds">Etowah Site</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The term <i>South Appalachian Province</i> was originally used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W._H._Holmes" title="W. H. Holmes">W. H. Holmes</a> in 1903 to describe a regional ceramic style in the southeast involving surface decorations applied with a carved wooden paddle. By the late 1960s archaeological investigations had shown the similarity of the culture that produced the pottery and the midwestern <i>Mississippian pattern</i> defined by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Midwestern_Taxonomic_System&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Midwestern Taxonomic System (page does not exist)">Midwestern Taxonomic System</a> in 1937. In 1967 <a href="/wiki/James_Bennett_Griffin" title="James Bennett Griffin">James B. Griffin</a> coined South Appalachian Mississippian to describe the evolving understanding of the peoples of the Southeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEAC1974_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-SEAC1974-13">[13]</a></sup> South Appalachian Mississippian area sites are distributed across a contiguous area including Alabama, Georgia, northern Florida, South Carolina, and central and western North Carolina and Tennessee. Chronologically this area became Mississippianized later than the Middle Mississippian area (about 1000 CE as compared to 800 CE) to its northwest and is believed that the peoples of this area adopted Mississippian traits from their northwestern neighbors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEAC_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-SEAC-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Typical settlements were located on riverine floodplains and included villages with defensive palisades enclosing platform mounds and residential areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEAC_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-SEAC-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Etowah_Indian_Mounds" title="Etowah Indian Mounds">Etowah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ocmulgee_National_Monument" title="Ocmulgee National Monument">Ocmulgee</a> (both located in Georgia) are prominent examples of the South Appalachian Mississippian settlements.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caddoan_Mississippian">Caddoan Mississippian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mississippian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Caddoan Mississippian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Caddoan_Mississippian_culture" title="Caddoan Mississippian culture">Caddoan Mississippian culture</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Caddoan_Mississippian_culture_map_HRoe_2010.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="465" data-file-width="600" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Caddoan_Mississippian_culture_map_HRoe_2010.jpg/200px-Caddoan_Mississippian_culture_map_HRoe_2010.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Caddoan_Mississippian_culture_map_HRoe_2010.jpg/300px-Caddoan_Mississippian_culture_map_HRoe_2010.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Caddoan_Mississippian_culture_map_HRoe_2010.jpg/400px-Caddoan_Mississippian_culture_map_HRoe_2010.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Caddoan_Mississippian_culture_map_HRoe_2010.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of the Caddoan Mississippian culture and some important sites</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Caddoan Mississippian area, a regional variant of the Mississippian culture, covered a large territory, including what is now eastern <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>, western <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>, northeastern <a href="/wiki/East_Texas" title="East Texas">Texas</a>, and northwestern <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>. Archaeological evidence has led to a scholarly consensus that the cultural continuity is unbroken from prehistory to the present, and that the <a href="/wiki/Caddo" title="Caddo">Caddo</a> and related <a href="/wiki/Caddoan_languages" title="Caddoan languages">Caddo language</a> speakers in prehistoric times and at first European contact are the direct ancestors of the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caddo_Nation_of_Oklahoma" title="Caddo Nation of Oklahoma">Caddo Nation of Oklahoma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TejasLinguistics_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-TejasLinguistics-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The climate in this area was drier than areas in the eastern woodlands, hindering maize production, and the lower population on the plains to the west may have meant fewer neighboring competing chiefdoms to contend with. Major sites such as <a href="/wiki/Spiro_Mounds" title="Spiro Mounds">Spiro</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_Mound_Site" title="Battle Mound Site">Battle Mound Site</a> are in the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_River" title="Arkansas River">Arkansas River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Red_River_of_the_South" title="Red River of the South">Red River</a> Valleys, the largest and most fertile of the waterways in the Caddoan region, where maize agriculture would have been the most productive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tejas_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tejas-15">[15]</a></sup> The sites generally lacked wooden <a href="/wiki/Palisade" title="Palisade">palisade</a> fortifications often found in the major Middle Mississippian towns. Living on the western edge of the Mississippian world, the Caddoans may have faced fewer military threats from their neighbors. Their societies may also have had a somewhat lower level of <a href="/wiki/Social_stratification" title="Social stratification">social stratification</a>.</p>
<p>The Caddoan people were speakers of one of the many <a href="/wiki/Caddoan_languages" title="Caddoan languages">Caddoan languages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The Caddoan languages once had a broad geographic distribution, but many are now extinct. The modern languages in the Caddoan family include Caddo and <a href="/wiki/Pawnee_people" title="Pawnee people">Pawnee</a>, now spoken mainly by elderly people.</p>
<p>Hernando de Soto led an expedition into the area in the early 1540s, he <a href="/wiki/List_of_sites_and_peoples_visited_by_the_Hernando_de_Soto_Expedition" title="List of sites and peoples visited by the Hernando de Soto Expedition">encountered several native groups</a> now thought to have been Caddoan. Composed of many tribes, the Caddo were organized into three confederacies, the <a href="/wiki/Hasinai" title="Hasinai">Hasinai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kadohadacho" title="Kadohadacho">Kadohadacho</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Natchitoches_people" title="Natchitoches people">Natchitoches</a>, which were all linked by their similar languages.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plaquemine_Mississippian">Plaquemine Mississippian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mississippian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Plaquemine Mississippian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plaquemine_culture" title="Plaquemine culture">Plaquemine culture</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plaquemine_culture_map_HRoe_2010.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="525" data-file-width="600" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Plaquemine_culture_map_HRoe_2010.jpg/200px-Plaquemine_culture_map_HRoe_2010.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Plaquemine_culture_map_HRoe_2010.jpg/300px-Plaquemine_culture_map_HRoe_2010.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Plaquemine_culture_map_HRoe_2010.jpg/400px-Plaquemine_culture_map_HRoe_2010.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plaquemine_culture_map_HRoe_2010.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map showing the geographical extent of the Plaquemine culture and some of its major sites</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Plaquemine culture was an <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_culture" title="Archaeological culture">archaeological culture</a> in the lower <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> Valley in western <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>. Good examples of this culture are the <a href="/wiki/Medora_Site" title="Medora Site">Medora Site</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Type_site" title="Type site">type site</a> for the culture and period) in <a href="/wiki/West_Baton_Rouge_Parish,_Louisiana" title="West Baton Rouge Parish, Louisiana">West Baton Rouge Parish, Louisiana</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Anna_Site" title="Anna Site">Anna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emerald_Mound_Site" title="Emerald Mound Site">Emerald Mound</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winterville_Site" title="Winterville Site">Winterville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holly_Bluff_Site" title="Holly Bluff Site">Holly Bluff</a> sites located in Mississippi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Plaquemine culture was contemporaneous with the Middle Mississippian culture in the <a href="/wiki/Cahokia" title="Cahokia">Cahokia</a> site in St. Louis, Missouri. It is considered ancestral to the <a href="/wiki/Natchez_people" title="Natchez people">Natchez</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taensa" title="Taensa">Taensa</a> Peoples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Plaquemine_Culture.2C_AD_1000_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Plaquemine_Culture.2C_AD_1000-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Emerald_Mound_Site" title="Emerald Mound Site">Emerald Mound</a>: A <a href="/wiki/Plaquemine_culture" title="Plaquemine culture">Plaquemine Mississippian</a> period archaeological site located on the <a href="/wiki/Natchez_Trace_Parkway" title="Natchez Trace Parkway">Natchez Trace Parkway</a> near <a href="/wiki/Stanton,_Mississippi" title="Stanton, Mississippi">Stanton, Mississippi</a>. The site dates from the period between 1200 and 1730. The platform mound is the second-largest Pre-Columbian earthwork in the country, after <a href="/wiki/Monks_Mound" title="Monks Mound">Monks Mound</a> at Cahokia.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Grand_Village_of_the_Natchez" title="Grand Village of the Natchez">Grand Village of the Natchez</a>: The main village of the <a href="/wiki/Natchez_people" title="Natchez people">Natchez people</a>, with three mounds. The only mound site to be used and maintained into historic times.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Known_Mississippian_settlements">Known Mississippian settlements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mississippian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Known Mississippian settlements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Mississippian_sites" title="List of Mississippian sites">List of Mississippian sites</a></div>
<p>Although the Mississippian culture was heavily disrupted before a complete understanding of the political landscape was written down, many Mississippian political bodies were documented and others have been discovered by research.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_modern_nations">Related modern nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mississippian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Related modern nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mississippian peoples were almost certainly ancestral to the majority of the American Indian nations living in this region in the historic era. The historic and modern day American Indian nations believed to have descended from the overarching Mississippian Culture include: the <a href="/wiki/Alabama_people" title="Alabama people">Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apalachee" title="Apalachee">Apalachee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caddo" title="Caddo">Caddo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chickasaw" title="Chickasaw">Chickasaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cherokee" title="Cherokee">Cherokee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Choctaw" title="Choctaw">Choctaw</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscogee_Creek_people" title="Muscogee Creek people">Muscogee Creek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guale" title="Guale">Guale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hitchiti" title="Hitchiti">Hitchiti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Houma_people" title="Houma people">Houma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kaw_people" title="Kaw people">Kansa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missouria" title="Missouria">Missouria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mabila" title="Mabila">Mobilian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natchez_people" title="Natchez people">Natchez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osage_Nation" title="Osage Nation">Osage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quapaw" title="Quapaw">Quapaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seminole" title="Seminole">Seminole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunica-Biloxi" title="Tunica-Biloxi">Tunica-Biloxi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yamasee" title="Yamasee">Yamasee</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yuchi" title="Yuchi">Yuchi</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="15 Nov 2009 (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contact_with_Europeans">Contact with Europeans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mississippian_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Contact with Europeans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DeSoto_Map_HRoe_2008.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="810" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/DeSoto_Map_HRoe_2008.jpg/200px-DeSoto_Map_HRoe_2008.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/DeSoto_Map_HRoe_2008.jpg/300px-DeSoto_Map_HRoe_2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/DeSoto_Map_HRoe_2008.jpg/400px-DeSoto_Map_HRoe_2008.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DeSoto_Map_HRoe_2008.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A map showing the de Soto route through the Southeast</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Scholars have studied the records of <a href="/wiki/Hernando_de_Soto" title="Hernando de Soto">Hernando de Soto</a>'s expedition of 1539–1543 to learn of his contacts with Mississippians, as he traveled through their villages of the Southeast. He visited many villages, in some cases staying for a month or longer. The <a href="/wiki/List_of_sites_and_peoples_visited_by_the_Hernando_de_Soto_Expedition" title="List of sites and peoples visited by the Hernando de Soto Expedition">List of sites and peoples visited by the Hernando de Soto Expedition</a> chronicles those villages. Some encounters were violent, while others were relatively peaceable. In some cases, de Soto seems to have been used as a tool or ally in long-standing native feuds. In one example, de Soto negotiated a truce between the <a href="/wiki/Pacaha" title="Pacaha">Pacaha</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Casqui" title="Casqui">Casqui</a>.</p>
<p>De Soto's later encounters left about half of the Spaniards and perhaps many hundreds of Native Americans dead. The chronicles of de Soto are among the first documents written about Mississippian peoples, and are an invaluable source of information on their cultural practices. The chronicles of the <a href="/wiki/Narv%C3%A1ez_expedition" title="Narváez expedition">Narváez expedition</a> were written before the de Soto expedition; the Narváez expedition that informed the Court of de Soto about the New World.</p>
<p>After the destruction and flight of the de Soto expedition, the Mississippian peoples continued their way of life with little direct European influence. Indirectly, however, European introductions dramatically changed native societies in the Eastern United States. Because the natives lacked <a href="/wiki/Immunity_(medical)" title="Immunity (medical)">immunity</a> to new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_diseases" title="Infectious diseases">infectious diseases</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Measles" title="Measles">measles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a>, epidemics caused so many fatalities that they undermined the social order of many chiefdoms. Some groups adopted European horses and changed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nomadism" title="Nomadism">nomadism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Political structures collapsed in many places.</p>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Joara" title="Joara">Joara</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Morganton,_North_Carolina" title="Morganton, North Carolina">Morganton, North Carolina</a>, Native Americans of the Mississippian culture interacted with <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> colonizers of the <a href="/wiki/Juan_Pardo_(explorer)" title="Juan Pardo (explorer)">Juan Pardo</a> expedition, who built a base there in 1567 called Fort San Juan. Expedition documentation and archaeological evidence of the fort and Native American culture both exist. The soldiers were at the fort about 18 months (1567–1568) before the natives killed them and destroyed the fort. (They killed soldiers stationed at five other forts as well; only one man of 120 survived.) Sixteenth-century Spanish artifacts have been recovered from the site, marking the first European colonization in the interior of what became the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the time more documentary accounts were being written, the Mississippian way of life had changed irrevocably. Some groups maintained an oral tradition link to their mound-building past, such as the late 19th-century Cherokee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Other Native American groups, having migrated many hundreds of miles and lost their elders to diseases, did not know their ancestors had built the mounds dotting the landscape. This contributed to the myth of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mound_builder_(people)" title="Mound builder (people)">Mound Builders</a> as a people distinct from Native Americans, which was rigorously debunked by <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_Thomas" title="Cyrus Thomas">Cyrus Thomas</a> in 1894.</p>
