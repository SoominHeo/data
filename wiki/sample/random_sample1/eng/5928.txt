<p>The <b>Myanmar snub-nosed monkey</b> or <b>Burmese snub-nosed monkey</b> (<i>Rhinopithecus strykeri</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Critically_endangered" title="Critically endangered">critically threatened species</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colobine" title="Colobine">colobine</a> <a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">monkey</a> discovered in 2010 in northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burma (Myanmar)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup> It was formally <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_species_of_mammal" title="New species of mammal">novel species of primate</a> in 2011 based on its <a href="/wiki/Fur" title="Fur">fur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beard" title="Beard">beard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">[2]</a></sup> A group of the species was discovered in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-long_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-long-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The species is known in local <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialects</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lisu_people" title="Lisu people">Lisu people</a> as <i>mey nwoah</i> and Law Waw people as <i>myuk na tok te</i>, both of which mean "monkey with an upturned face".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">Rain</a> allegedly causes it to sneeze due to the short upturned <a href="/wiki/Nose" title="Nose">nasal</a> flesh around its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nostrils" title="Nostrils">nostrils</a>. People from the area report that it sits with its head directed downwards, hiding its face between its knees when it rains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NationalGeographic_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NationalGeographic-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_identification_and_taxonomy">Scientific identification and taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myanmar_snub-nosed_monkey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scientific identification and taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The species came to the attention of a team of scientists allied to the "Myanmar Primate Conservation Program"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fauna-flora1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-fauna-flora1-6">[6]</a></sup> researching the status of the <a href="/wiki/Hoolock_gibbon" title="Hoolock gibbon">hoolock gibbon</a> in early 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-primatology1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-primatology1-7">[7]</a></sup> The team, led by Swiss primatologist Thomas Geissman and Ngwe Lwin of the Myanmar <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_and_Nature_Conservation_Association" title="Biodiversity and Nature Conservation Association">Biodiversity and Nature Conservation Association</a> (BANCA), were supported by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fauna_%26_Flora_International" title="Fauna &amp; Flora International">Fauna &amp; Flora International</a> (FFI)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fauna-flora1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-fauna-flora1-6">[6]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/People_Resources_and_Conservation_Foundation" title="People Resources and Conservation Foundation">People Resources and Conservation Foundation</a> (PRCF). The <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">specific name</a> <i>strykeri</i> is given in honour of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philanthropist" title="Philanthropist">philanthropist</a> <a href="/wiki/Jon_Stryker" title="Jon Stryker">Jon Stryker</a>, president and founder of the <a href="/wiki/Arcus_Foundation" title="Arcus Foundation">Arcus Foundation</a>, which also sponsored the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type specimen</a> was identified from the Maw River area of northeastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kachin_state" title="Kachin state">Kachin state</a> in northeastern Burma. The <a href="/wiki/Holotype" title="Holotype">specimen</a> most closely examined was the skull (with mandible) and skin of a gutted adult male obtained from hunters in <a href="/wiki/Pade" title="Pade">Pade</a>, subsequently deposited in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropological_Institute_and_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anthropological Institute and Museum (page does not exist)">Anthropological Institute and Museum</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Z%C3%BCrich" title="University of Zürich">University of Zürich</a>. <a href="/wiki/Paratype" title="Paratype">Additional sample</a> skulls of animals killed some three years earlier, one male and one female, were also collected along with a bag made out of the skin of a juvenile caught in January 2010, all obtained in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Htantan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Htantan (page does not exist)">Htantan</a> village.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The team encountered seven live specimens, including an infant, but these moved out of sight before they could be photographed or studied in detail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NationalGeographic_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NationalGeographic-5">[5]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Camera_trap" title="Camera trap">camera trap</a> set by a team of FFI, BANCA and PRCF researchers captured the first known images of live snub-nosed monkeys in January 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myanmar_snub-nosed_monkey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The monkey's fur is mostly black. Its <a href="/wiki/Crown_(anatomy)" title="Crown (anatomy)">crown</a> consists of a thin, high, forward-curved crest of long, black hairs. It has protruding white ear <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuft" title="Tuft">tufts</a>, a mostly naked face with pale pink skin, a “moustache” of whitish hairs above the upper lip, and a distinct white chin beard. The perineal area is white and clearly defined, and the limbs are mostly black; the inner sides of the upper arms and upper legs are blackish brown. The lips are prominent, and the nose upturned, allegedly causing the animal to sneeze in rainy weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-11">[11]</a></sup> Its tail is approximately 1.4 times the body length: the first sample, an adult male, has a head-body length of 55.5 centimetres (21.9 in), and a tail 78 cm (31 in) long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myanmar_snub-nosed_monkey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The species spend summer months in temperate mixed forests at upper altitudes of their range, and descend to lower ground in the winter to escape snow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>When first discovered, the only known specimens existed in three or four groups within a 270 square kilometres (100 sq mi) range at 1,700 to 3,200 metres (5,600–10,500 ft) above sea level in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>, in the north-eastern section of <a href="/wiki/Kachin_State" title="Kachin State">Kachin State</a>, the northernmost part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burma (Myanmar)</a>. In 2011, a population was discovered in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lushui_County" title="Lushui County">Lushui County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a>, China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-China_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-China-13">[13]</a></sup> The species is isolated from other snub-nosed <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhinopithecus" title="Rhinopithecus">Rhinopithecus</a></i> by the <a href="/wiki/Mekong" title="Mekong">Mekong</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Salween_River" title="Salween River">Salween</a> rivers; the other 4 species, <a href="/wiki/Golden_snub-nosed_monkey" title="Golden snub-nosed monkey">golden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_snub-nosed_monkey" title="Black snub-nosed monkey">black</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gray_snub-nosed_monkey" title="Gray snub-nosed monkey">gray</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tonkin_snub-nosed_monkey" title="Tonkin snub-nosed monkey">Tonkin snub-nosed monkeys</a>, are found in China and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-primatology1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-primatology1-7">[7]</a></sup> The species shares habitat with <a href="/wiki/Shortridge%27s_langur" title="Shortridge's langur">Shortridge's langur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stump-tailed_macaque" title="Stump-tailed macaque">stump-tailed macaque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Assam_macaque" title="Assam macaque">Assam macaque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhesus_macaque" title="Rhesus macaque">rhesus macaque</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northern_pig-tailed_macaque" title="Northern pig-tailed macaque">northern pig-tailed macaque</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the morning of 16 October 2011, a forest guard at <a href="/wiki/Gaoligong_Mountains" title="Gaoligong Mountains">Gaoligongshan National Nature Reserve (GNNR)</a>, China took photos of a group of snub-nosed monkeys which were later identified as <i>R. strykery</i>. This made a breaking headline in China especially on the urgent need for a conservation programme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-long_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-long-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myanmar_snub-nosed_monkey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">Deforestation</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a> operations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fauna-flora1_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-fauna-flora1-6">[6]</a></sup> isolation and <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a> by local humans for food are considered dangers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-11">[11]</a></sup> to the small extant population. The known Burmese population size is 260-330 individuals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">[2]</a></sup> and it is believed less than 100 remain in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-China_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-China-13">[13]</a></sup> It is recognized as <a href="/wiki/Critically_endangered" title="Critically endangered">critically endangered</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup> The Chinese population is found within the <a href="/wiki/Gaoligong_Mountains" title="Gaoligong Mountains">Gaoligongshan</a> National Nature Reserve and it has been proposed to create a national park to protect this species in Burma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-long_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-long-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_Evolution">Origin and Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myanmar_snub-nosed_monkey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Origin and Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Snub-nosed monkeys and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Langurs" title="Langurs">langurs</a> are the two major groups of <a href="/wiki/Colobinae" title="Colobinae">colobine monkeys</a> in Asia. The <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyletic origin</a> of these Asian monkeys are already established through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogeny" title="Molecular phylogeny">molecular phylogeny</a> using <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_DNA" title="Nuclear DNA">nuclear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNAs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> To further resolve the <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolutionary</a> status of these monkeys with emphasis on <i>R. strykery</i>, primatologists at <a href="/wiki/German_Primate_Center" title="German Primate Center">German Primate Center</a> analysed the complete <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_genome" title="Mitochondrial genome">mitochondrial genomes</a> and 12 <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_gene" title="Nuclear gene">nuclear loci</a>, including one <a href="/wiki/X_chromosome" title="X chromosome">X chromosomal</a>, six <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y chromosomal</a> and five <a href="/wiki/Autosome" title="Autosome">autosomal loci</a>, from all ten odd-nosed monkey species. Their findings in 2012 revealed that snub nosed <i>Rhinopithecus</i> group represent the most <a href="/wiki/Lineage_(evolution)" title="Lineage (evolution)">basal lineage</a>, with <i><a href="/wiki/Proboscis_monkey" title="Proboscis monkey">Nasalis</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simias" title="Simias">Simias</a></i> forming the closest <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">group</a>, which implies that these odd-nosed monkeys originated from northern Burma and a later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_(biology)" title="Radiation (biology)">expanded</a> into <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sundaland" title="Sundaland">Sundaland</a>. The snub-nosed group <a href="/wiki/Genetic_divergence" title="Genetic divergence">diverged</a> from other Asian monkeys about 6.8-6 million years ago, and from Nasalis and <a href="/wiki/Simia" title="Simia">Simia</a> <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> about 1.2 Ma. Further, various species of the snub-nosed group split from each other about 730,000-400,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition">Recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myanmar_snub-nosed_monkey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxonomist" title="Taxonomist">Taxonomic experts</a> at the <a href="/wiki/International_Institute_for_Species_Exploration" title="International Institute for Species Exploration">International Institute for Species Exploration</a> honoured the monkey species as one of the <i>Top 10 New Species 2012</i> among new species described in 2011 throughout the world. Its unique <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">appearance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethology" title="Ethology">behaviour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">vulnerability</a> make it outstanding and not only in novelty, but also in conservation issues. It has even earned a new nickname "the sneezing monkey" for its peculiar behaviour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 2012 it was also listed at no 9 of the <i>Top 10 weirdest new species</i> by the Chinese news portal, China.org.cn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
