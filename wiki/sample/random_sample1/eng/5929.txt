<p><b>Mie Prefecture</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">三重県</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Mie-ken</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Prefectures_of_Japan" title="Prefectures of Japan">prefecture</a> of Japan, which is part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kansai" title="Kansai">Kansai</a> region on the main <a href="/wiki/Honshu" title="Honshu">Honshu</a> island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The capital is the city of <a href="/wiki/Tsu,_Mie" title="Tsu, Mie">Tsu</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Naiku_01.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Naiku_01.JPG/220px-Naiku_01.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Naiku_01.JPG/330px-Naiku_01.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Naiku_01.JPG/440px-Naiku_01.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Until the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a>, the area that is now Mie Prefecture was made up of <a href="/wiki/Ise_Province" title="Ise Province">Ise Province</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shima_Province" title="Shima Province">Shima Province</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iga_Province" title="Iga Province">Iga Province</a> and part of <a href="/wiki/Kii_Province" title="Kii Province">Kii Province</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Evidence of human habitation in Mie dates back more than 10,000 years. During the <a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Dmon_period" title="Jōmon period">Jōmon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yayoi_period" title="Yayoi period">Yayoi</a> periods, agricultural communities began to form along the river and coastal areas of the region. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ise_Shrine" title="Ise Shrine">Ise Shrine</a> is said to have been established during the Yayoi period, and in the 7th century the <a href="/wiki/Saik%C5%AB" title="Saikū">Saikū Imperial Residence</a> was built in what is now <a href="/wiki/Meiwa,_Mie" title="Meiwa, Mie">Meiwa Town</a> to serve as both a residence and administrative centre for the <a href="/wiki/Sai%C5%8D" title="Saiō">Saiō</a>, an Imperial Princess who served as High Priestess of Ise Shrine.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a>, the area now known as Mie Prefecture consisted of several feudal domains, each ruled by an appointed lord. Transport networks, including the Tokaido and Ise Roads, were built. Port towns such as Ohminato, <a href="/wiki/Kuwana,_Mie" title="Kuwana, Mie">Kuwana</a> and Anōtsu, posting stations and castle towns flourished. Pilgrimages to Ise Shrine also became very popular.</p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a>, the former provinces of Ise, Shima and Iga as well as a portion of eastern <a href="/wiki/Kii_Province" title="Kii Province">Kii</a>, were organized and reorganized repeatedly. In 1871 the area from the <a href="/wiki/Kiso_Three_Rivers" title="Kiso Three Rivers">Kiso Three Rivers</a> in the north to present-day <a href="/wiki/Tsu,_Mie" title="Tsu, Mie">Tsu</a> became Anōtsu Prefecture, and the area south of that became Watarai Prefecture. In 1872, the Anōtsu prefectural seat moved from Tsu to <a href="/wiki/Yokkaichi,_Mie" title="Yokkaichi, Mie">Yokkaichi</a>, and the prefecture itself was renamed Mie. For a variety of reasons, including the strong likelihood that Mie would eventually merge with Watarai, the prefectural seat returned to Tsu the following year, and Mie Prefecture took its present-day form in 1876, when it merged with its southern neighbor.</p>
<p>The name Mie supposedly was taken from a comment about the region made by <a href="/wiki/Yamato_Takeru" title="Yamato Takeru">Yamato Takeru</a> on his way back from conquering the eastern regions.</p>
<p>In 1959 many lives were lost as parts of Mie were devastated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoon_Vera_(1959)" title="Typhoon Vera (1959)">Ise-wan Typhoon</a>, the strongest typhoon to hit Japan in recorded history. Crops were destroyed, sea walls ruined, roads and railways damaged and a substantial number of people were injured or left homeless.</p>
<p>In May 2016, the city of <a href="/wiki/Shima,_Mie" title="Shima, Mie">Shima</a> hosted the <a href="/wiki/42nd_G7_summit" title="42nd G7 summit">42nd G7 summit</a>, the third summit without the presence of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.</p>

<p>Mie Prefecture forms the eastern part of the <a href="/wiki/Kii_Peninsula" title="Kii Peninsula">Kii Peninsula</a>, and borders on <a href="/wiki/Aichi_Prefecture" title="Aichi Prefecture">Aichi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gifu_Prefecture" title="Gifu Prefecture">Gifu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shiga_Prefecture" title="Shiga Prefecture">Shiga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Prefecture" title="Kyoto Prefecture">Kyoto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nara_Prefecture" title="Nara Prefecture">Nara</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wakayama_Prefecture" title="Wakayama Prefecture">Wakayama</a>. It is considered<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (February 2013)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kansai" title="Kansai">Kansai</a> and <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dkai_region" title="Tōkai region">Tōkai</a> regions due to its geographical proximity to <a href="/wiki/Aichi_Prefecture" title="Aichi Prefecture">Aichi Prefecture</a> and its cultural influence from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kansai" title="Kansai">Kansai</a>, such as the fact that <a href="/wiki/Kansai_dialect" title="Kansai dialect">Kansai dialect</a> is spoken in Mie. Traditionally, though, the <a href="/wiki/Iga,_Mie" title="Iga, Mie">Iga</a> region of Mie is considered to have always been a part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kansai" title="Kansai">Kansai</a>.</p>
<p>Mie Prefecture measures 170 km (106 mi) from north to south, and 80 km (50 mi) from east to west, and includes five distinct geographical areas:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pref.mie.jp_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-pref.mie.jp-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>the north-west of Mie consists of the Suzuka Mountains</li>
<li>along the coast of <a href="/wiki/Ise_Bay" title="Ise Bay">Ise Bay</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Aichi_Prefecture" title="Aichi Prefecture">Aichi</a> border to <a href="/wiki/Ise,_Mie" title="Ise, Mie">Ise City</a> lies the Ise Plain, where most of the population of Mie live</li>
<li>south of the Ise Plain is the <a href="/wiki/Shima,_Mie" title="Shima, Mie">Shima Peninsula</a></li>
<li>bordering <a href="/wiki/Nara_Prefecture" title="Nara Prefecture">Nara</a> in the central-west is the <a href="/wiki/Iga,_Mie" title="Iga, Mie">Iga Basin</a></li>
<li>running from central Mie to its southern borders is the Nunobiki Mountainous Region.</li>
</ol>

<p>Mie has a coastline that stretches 1,094.9 km (680.3 mi) and, as of 2000, Mie's 5,776.44 km<sup>2</sup> (2,230.30 sq mi) landmass is 64.8% <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a>, 11.5% <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, 6% residential area, 3.8% <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">roads</a>, and 3.6% <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a>. The remaining 10.3% are not classified.</p>
<p>The Ise Plain has a relatively moderate climate, averaging 14 to 15 degrees Celsius for the year. The Iga Basin has more daily temperature variance and averages temperatures 1 to 2 degrees cooler than the Ise Plain. Southern Mie, south of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shima_Peninsula" title="Shima Peninsula">Shima Peninsula</a>, has a warmer <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a> marine climate, with <a href="/wiki/Owase,_Mie" title="Owase, Mie">Owase Region</a> having one of the heaviest rainfall figures for all of Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pref.mie.jp_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-pref.mie.jp-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mie_Prefecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mie Prefecture has traditionally been a link between east and west Japan, thanks largely to the Tokaido and Ise Pilgrimage Roads. Traditional handicrafts such as Iga Braid, Yokkaichi Banko Pottery, Suzuka Ink, Iga Pottery and <a href="/wiki/Katazome" title="Katazome">Ise Katagami</a> flourished. With 65% of the prefecture consisting of forests and with over 1,000 km (600 mi) of coastline, Mie has a long been associated with forestry and seafood industries. Mie also produces <a href="/wiki/Green_tea" title="Green tea">tea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matsusaka_beef" title="Matsusaka beef">beef</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cultured_pearl" title="Cultured pearl">cultured pearls</a> and fruit, mainly <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_orange" title="Mandarin orange">mandarin oranges</a>. Food production companies include <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Azuma_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Azuma Foods (page does not exist)">Azuma Foods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Northern Mie is home to a number of manufacturing industries, mainly transport machinery manufacturing (vehicles and ships) and heavy chemical industries such as oil refineries. As well as this, Mie Prefecture is expanding into more advanced industries including the manufacture of semiconductors and liquid crystal displays. In <a href="/wiki/Suzuka,_Mie" title="Suzuka, Mie">Suzuka</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honda_Motor_Company" title="Honda Motor Company">Honda Motor Company</a> maintains a factory established in 1960 that built the <a href="/wiki/Honda_Civic" title="Honda Civic">Honda Civic</a> as well as other vehicles.</p>
