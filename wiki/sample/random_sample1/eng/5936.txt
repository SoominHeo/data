<p>The <b>Mizo language</b>, or <i>Mizo ṭawng</i>, is spoken natively by the <a href="/wiki/Mizo_people" title="Mizo people">Mizo people</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mizoram" title="Mizoram">Mizoram</a> state of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chin_State" title="Chin State">Chin State</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burma</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Chittagong_Hill_Tracts" title="Chittagong Hill Tracts">Chittagong Hill Tracts</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>. The language is also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lushai" title="Lushai">Lushai</a>, a colonial term, as the Lushei people were the first to have external exposure. Though still common, <i>Lushai</i> (or Lusei, or Lushei) is considered incorrect by the Mizo themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lalthangliana_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lalthangliana-3">[3]</a></sup> Much poetic language is derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pawi_language" title="Pawi language">Pawi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paite_language" title="Paite language">Paite</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hmar_language" title="Hmar language">Hmar</a>, and most known ancient poems considered to be in the Mizo language are actually in Pawi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lalthangliana_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lalthangliana-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Mizo language belongs to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kukish" title="Kukish">Kukish</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" title="Sino-Tibetan languages">Sino-Tibetan language family</a>. The numerous clans of the Mizo had respective dialects, amongst which the Lushei (Lusei, by Mizo themselves) dialect was most common, and which subsequently became the Mizo language and the <i>lingua franca</i> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kuki_peoples" title="Kuki peoples">Kuki peoples</a> due to its extensive and exclusive use by the Christian missionaries.</p>

<p>In its current form, it was devised by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_missionaries" title="Christian missionaries">Christian missionaries</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> based on <a href="/wiki/Hunterian_transliteration" title="Hunterian transliteration">Hunterian system</a> of <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">transliteration</a>.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Circumflex" title="Circumflex">circumflex</a> <i>^</i> was later added to the vowels to indicate long vowels, viz., <b>â, ê, î, ô, û</b>, which were insufficient to fully express Mizo tone. Recently,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2012)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> a leading newspaper in Mizoram, <i><a href="/wiki/Vanglaini" title="Vanglaini">Vanglaini</a></i>, the magazine <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kristian_%E1%B9%AChalai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kristian Ṭhalai (page does not exist)">Kristian Ṭhalai</a></i>, and other publishers began using <b>á, à, ä, é, è, ë, í, ì, ï, ó, ò, ú, ù, ü</b> to indicate the long intonations and tones. However, this does not differentiate the different intonations that short tones can have.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_with_other_languages">Relation with other languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mizo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relation with other languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Mizo language is related to the other languages of the Sino-Tibetan family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Kukish_languages" title="Kukish languages">Kukish languages</a> (which native Mizo speakers call <i>Zohnahthlâk ṭawngho</i>/<i>Mizo ṭawngho</i>) have a substantial amount of words in common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mizo_and_Burmese">Mizo and Burmese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mizo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mizo and Burmese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following few words suggest that Mizo and the <a href="/wiki/Burmese_language" title="Burmese language">Burmese</a> are of the same family: <i>kun</i> ("to bend"), <i>kam</i> ("bank of a river"), <i>kha</i> ("bitter"), <i>sam</i> ("hair"), <i>mei</i> ("fire"), <i>that</i> ("to kill"), <i>ni</i> ("sun") <i>hnih</i> ("two") <i>li</i> ("four") <i>nga</i> ("five")</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">Vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mizo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Vowels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Monophthongs">Monophthongs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mizo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Monophthongs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Mizo language has eight tones and intonations for each of the vowels <i>a, aw, e, i and u</i>, four of which are reduced tones and the other four long tones. The vowel <i>o</i> has only three tones, all of them of the reduced type; it has almost exactly the same sound as the <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">diphthong</a> /oʊ/ found in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tone">Tone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mizo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Tone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mizo is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tonal_language" title="Tonal language">tonal language</a>, in which differences in pitch and pitch contour can change the meanings of words. Tone systems have developed independently in many of the daughter languages largely through simplifications in the set of possible syllable-final and syllable-initial consonants. Typically, a distinction between voiceless and voiced initial consonants is replaced by a distinction between high and low tone, while falling and rising tones developed from syllable-final <i>h</i> and glottal stop, which themselves often reflect earlier consonants.</p>
<p>The eight tones and intonations that the vowel a (and the vowels <i>aw</i>, <i>e</i>, <i>i</i>, <i>u</i>, and this constitutes all the tones in the Mizo language) can have can be shown by the letter sequence <b>p</b>-<b>a</b>-<b>n</b>-<b>g</b>, as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>long high tone: <b>páng</b> as in <i><b>páng</b> là</i> (which has the same intonation as <b>sáng</b> in the sentence <i>Thingküng <b>sáng</b> tak kan huanah a ding</i>).</li>
<li>long low tone: <b>pàng</b> as in <i>Tui a kawt <b>pàng</b> pâng mai</i> (which has the same intonation as <b>vàng</b> in the word <i><b>vàng</b>laini</i>).</li>
<li>peaking tone: <b>pâng</b> as in <i>Tui a kawt pàng <b>pâng</b> mai</i> (which has the same intonation as <b>thlûk</b> in <i>I hla phuah <b>thlûk</b> chu a va mawi ve</i>).</li>
<li>dipping tone: <b>päng</b> as in <i>Tuibur a hmuam <b>päng</b> mai</i> (which has the same intonation as <b>säm</b> in <i>Kan huan ka <b>säm</b> vêl mai mai</i>).</li>
<li>short rising tone: <b>pǎng</b> as in <i>nau<b>pǎng</b></i> (which has the same intonation as <b>thǎng</b> in <i>Kan huanah <b>thǎng</b> ka kam</i>).</li>
<li>short falling tone: <b>pȧng</b> as in <i>I va inkhuih <b>pȧng</b> ve?</i> (which has the same intonation as <b>pȧn</b> in <i>I lam ka rawn <b>pȧn</b>)</i></li>
<li>short mid tone: <b>pang</b> as in <i>A dik lo nghâl <b>pang</b> (which has the same tone as <b>man</b> in</i> Sazu ka <b>man</b>)</li>
<li>short low tone: <b>pạng</b> as in <i>I <b>pạng</b> a sá a nih kha</i> (which has the same tone as <b>chạl</b> in <i>I <b>chạl</b>ah thosí a fù</i>).</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mizo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Grammar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mizo_grammar" title="Mizo grammar">Mizo grammar</a></div>
<p>Mizo contains many analyzable polysyllables, which are polysyllabic units in which the individual syllables have meaning by themselves. In a true monosyllabic language, polysyllables are mostly confined to compound words, such as "lighthouse". The first syllables of compounds tend over time to be de-stressed, and may eventually be reduced to prefixed consonants. The word <i>nuntheihna</i> ("survival") is composed of <i>nung</i> ("to live"), <i>theih</i> ("possible") and <i>na</i> (a nominalizing suffix); likewise, <i>theihna</i> means "possibility". Virtually all polysyllabic morphemes in Mizo can be shown to have originated in this way. For example, the disyllabic form <i>bakhwan</i> ("butterfly"), which occurs in one dialect of the Trung (or Dulung) language of <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a>, is actually a reduced form of the compound <i>blak kwar</i>, found in a closely related dialect. It is reported over 18 of the dialects share about 850 words with the same meaning. For example, <i>ban</i> ("arm"), <i>ke</i> ("leg"), <i>thla</i> ("wing", "month"), <i>lu</i> ("head") and <i>kut</i> ("hand").</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Word_order">Word order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mizo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Word order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mizo declarative word order is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Object-subject-verb" title="Object-subject-verb">Object-subject-verb</a> (OSV). For example:</p>
<table class="wikitable">
<tr>
<td><b>Lehkhabu</b></td>
<td><b>ka</b></td>
<td><b>ziak</b></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><i>book</i></td>
<td><i>I</i></td>
<td><i>write</i></td>
</tr>
</table>
<ul>
<li><i>Lehkhabu ka ziak</i> (I write/am writing a book)</li>
</ul>
<p>However, even if one says <i>Ka ziak lehkhabu</i>, its meaning is not changed, nor does it become incorrect; the word order becomes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subject-verb-object" title="Subject-verb-object">Subject-verb-object</a>. But this form is used only in particular situations.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Verbs">Verbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mizo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Verbs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Conjugation">Conjugation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mizo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Conjugation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The verbs (called <i>thiltih</i> in Mizo)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> are not conjugated as in languages such as <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> by changing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desinence" title="Desinence">desinence</a> of words, but the tense (in a sentence) is clarified by the <i>aspect</i> and the addition of some particles, such as<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>ang</i> (for forming simple future),</li>
<li><i>tawh</i> (for forming <a href="/wiki/Simple_past" title="Simple past">simple past</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Past_perfect" title="Past perfect">past perfect</a>),</li>
<li><i>mék</i> (for forming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progressive_tense" title="Progressive tense">progressive tenses</a>, present and past),</li>
<li><i>dáwn</i> (for forming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simple_future_tense" title="Simple future tense">simple future</a>),</li>
<li><i>dáwn mék</i> (for forming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_future" title="Near future">near future</a>),</li>
</ul>
<p>etc.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Modification_of_verbs">Modification of verbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mizo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Modification of verbs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

This modified form is also used as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Past_participle" title="Past participle">past participle</a>. Some verbs which undergo modification are tabulated below:</p>

<tr>

<p>However, even if the spelling of a verb is not changed, its <i>tone</i> is sometimes changed. For example, the verbs <i>tum</i> (to aim), <i>hum</i> (to protect) etc. change tones; the tone is lowered in the <a href="/wiki/Gerund" title="Gerund">modified form</a>. There is a third class of verbs - those which neither change tone nor are inflected (modified). Examples include <i>hneh</i> (to conquer), <i>hnek</i> (to strike with one's fist).</p>
<p>Modification of words is not restricted to verbs; adjectives, adverbs etc. are also modified.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns">Nouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mizo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Nouns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mizo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There is no <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">gender</a> for nouns, and there are no <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">articles</a>. There are some specific suffixes for forming nouns from verbs and adjectives, the most common of which are <i>-na</i> and <i>-zia</i>. The suffix <i>-na</i> is used for forming nouns from both verbs and adjectives, whereas <i>-zia</i> is used specifically for nominalising adjectives. For example,</p>
<ul>
<li>tlù (v. to fall) - tlûk<i><b>na</b></i> (n. fall)</li>
<li>hmu (v. to see) - hmuh<i><b>na</b></i> (n. sight, seeing, vision)</li>
<li>süal (adj. evil) - sual<i><b>na</b></i> (n. sin)/sual<i><b>zia</b></i> (n. evilness)</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Negation">Negation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mizo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Negation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For declarative sentences, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negation_(linguistics)" title="Negation (linguistics)">negation</a> is achieved by adding the particle <i>lo</i> (not) at the end of a sentence. For example,</p>
<table class="wikitable">
<tr>
<th>Sentence</th>
<th>Negation</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Lala a lo kal<br/>
<i>Lala is coming</i>/<i>Lala came</i></td>
<td>Lala a lo kal <i>lo</i><br/>
<i>Lala did <b>not</b> come</i></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Pathumin paruk a sem thei<br/>
<i>Three divides six</i></td>
<td>Pathumin paruk a sem thei <i>lo</i><br/>
<i>Three does not divide six</i></td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Also, for words such as <i>engmah</i> (nothing), <i>tumah</i> (nobody) etc., unlike English we have to add the negation particle <i>lo</i>; for example</p>
<table>
<tr>
<td>1. Tumah<br/>
<i>nobody</i></td>
<td>ka<br/>
<i>I</i></td>
<td>hmu<br/>
<i>see</i></td>
<td>lo<br/>
<i>not</i></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>2. Engmah<br/>
<i>nothing</i></td>
<td>ka<br/>
<i>I</i></td>
<td>rawn keng<br/>
<i>bring</i></td>
<td>lo<br/>
<i>not</i></td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Thus we have to use double negation for such cases.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unique_parts_of_speech_in_Mizo_.E1.B9.ADawng">Unique parts of speech in Mizo ṭawng</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mizo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Unique parts of speech in Mizo ṭawng">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>This award is only for books originally written in Mizo and not for translations, and it has been awarded every year since 1989. The award has been given to books on history and religion, but most of the winners are novels. Each year, the academy examines about a hundred books (in 2011, 149 books were examined),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vanglaini.2C_April_24.2C_2012_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vanglaini.2C_April_24.2C_2012-24">[24]</a></sup> out of which it selects the top 20, and then first shortlistling it further to top 10, and then to top 5, then top 3, finally chooses the winner.</p>
<p>The academy also awards lifetime achievement in Mizo literature.</p>
<p>Some of the best-known Mizo writers include James Dokhuma, Ṭhuamtea Khawlhring, C. Laizawna, C. Lalnunchanga, Vanneihtluanga etc.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistics">Statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mizo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are around 700,000 speakers of the Mizo language: 674,756 speakers in India (2001 census); 1,041 speakers in Bangladesh (1981 census); 12,500 speakers in Burma (1983 census).</p>
