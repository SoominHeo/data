<p>The <b>Missouri Compromise</b> was attained through legislation passed by the 16th Congress of the United States on May 8, 1820. The measures provided for the admission of the District of Maine as a free state and the Missouri territory without restriction on slavery. In addition, it outlawed slavery north of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Parallel_36%C2%B080%E2%80%B2_north&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Parallel 36°80′ north (page does not exist)">36°60′ parallel</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Purchase" title="Louisiana Purchase">Louisiana Purchase</a> lands, thereby committing the largest remaining portion of the territory to free-soil. South of the parallel no slavery restrictions were imposed. President <a href="/wiki/James_Monroe" title="James Monroe">James Monroe</a> signed the legislation on April 6, 1820.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The compromise bills served to quell the furious sectional debates that had first erupted during the final session of the 15th Congress. On February 3, 1819, Representative <a href="/wiki/James_Tallmadge,_Jr." title="James Tallmadge, Jr.">James Tallmadge, Jr.</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Jeffersonian_democracy" title="Jeffersonian democracy">Jeffersonian Republican</a> from New York State, had submitted two amendments to Missouri's request for statehood. The first proposed to federally prohibit further slave migration into Missouri; the second would require all slave offspring, born after statehood, freed at 25 years of age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> At issue among southern legislators was the encroachment by their northern free state colleagues in what they considered a purely sectional concern: slave labor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Northern critics including Federalists and Republicans, objected to the expansion of slavery into the Louisiana Purchase territory on the Constitutional inequalities of the <a href="/wiki/Three-Fifths_Compromise" title="Three-Fifths Compromise">three-fifths rule</a>, which conferred Southern representation in the federal government, derived from a states’ slave population. Nonetheless, the more populous North held a firm numerical advantage in the House.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Jeffersonian Republicans in the North ardently maintained that a strict interpretation of the Constitution required that Congress act to limit the spread of slavery on egalitarian grounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The slave-holding states were acutely aware that maintaining a balance in the number of free-to-slave states was necessary to ensure political equilibrium in the US Senate. With the Senate evenly split at the opening of the debates, both sections possessing 11 states, the admission of Missouri would give the South a two-seat advantage in the upper house and diminish the Northern lower house majority. The South sought to enlist Missouri to maintain Southern political preeminence and ensure security of their institutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Missouri question in the 15th Congress ended in stalemate on March 4, 1819, the House sustaining its northern antislavery position, and the Senate blocking a slavery restricted statehood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Antislavery agitation grew in the North in the aftermath of the debates, leading to widespread opposition to slavery in Missouri.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> As the 16th Congress assembled in December 1819, the two houses remained thoroughly polarized over slavery in the Louisiana Purchase territories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the free-soil <a href="/wiki/District_of_Maine" title="District of Maine">District of Maine</a> offered its petition for statehood, the Senate quickly linked the Maine and Missouri bills, making Maine admission a condition for Missouri entering the Union with slavery unrestricted. Senator <a href="/wiki/Jesse_B._Thomas" title="Jesse B. Thomas">Jesse B. Thomas</a> of Illinois added a compromise proviso, excluding slavery from all remaining lands of the Louisiana Purchase north of the 36 30’ parallel. The combined measures passed the Senate, only to be voted down in the House by those Northern representatives who held out for a free Missouri. Speaker of the House of Representatives <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay" title="Henry Clay">Henry Clay</a> of Kentucky, in a desperate bid to break the deadlock, divided the Senate bills. Clay and his pro-compromise allies succeeded in pressuring half the anti-restrictionist House Southerners to submit to the passage of the Thomas proviso, while maneuvering a number of restrictionist House northerners to acquiesce in supporting Missouri as a slave state. This was the Missouri Compromise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The legislation extracted by the compromisers served to effect a "brokered truce" or "armistice" rather than a genuine compromise. The crux of the Compromise was that it circumvented the deepening disaffection among Jeffersonian Republicans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Missouri crisis would spur the formation of two powerful political organizations – the Democratic and Whig Parties – both committed to preserving the federal Union by means of sectional compromise and the suppression of the explosive proslavery and antislavery arguments that had surfaced over Missouri statehood. The repeal of the Missouri Compromise in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kansas-Nebraska_Act_of_1854" title="Kansas-Nebraska Act of 1854">Kansas-Nebraska Act of 1854</a> would hasten the growth of a mass antislavery coalition – the Republican Party –whose precepts of which were first formulated by Jeffersonian Republican restrictionists during the Missouri crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Era_of_Good_Feelings_and_Party_.22Amalgamation.22">The Era of Good Feelings and Party "Amalgamation"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missouri_Compromise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title='Edit section: The Era of Good Feelings and Party "Amalgamation"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:James_Monroe_02.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1236" data-file-width="983" height="277" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/James_Monroe_02.jpg/220px-James_Monroe_02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/James_Monroe_02.jpg/330px-James_Monroe_02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/James_Monroe_02.jpg/440px-James_Monroe_02.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:James_Monroe_02.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
President James Monroe (1758-1831):James Monroe's presidency is almost synonymous with the Era of Good Feelings. He signed the Missouri Compromise bills with a conviction that doing so was the only way to prevent the rise of an antislavery party in the North. In emphasizing the political implications, Monroe "obscured the very real weight of antislavery sentiment involved in the [slavery] restrictionist movement." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Era_of_Good_Feelings" title="Era of Good Feelings">Era of Good Feelings</a>, closely associated with the administration of President James Monroe (1817-1825), was characterized by the dissolution of national political identities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> With the <a href="/wiki/Hartford_Convention" title="Hartford Convention">discredited</a> Federalists in decline nationally, the "amalgamated" or hybridized Republicans adopted key Federalist economic programs and institutions, further erasing party identities and consolidating their victory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The economic nationalism of the Era of Good Feelings that would authorize the <a href="/wiki/Tariff_of_1816" title="Tariff of 1816">Tariff of 1816</a> and incorporate the <a href="/wiki/Second_Bank_of_the_United_States" title="Second Bank of the United States">Second Bank of the United States</a> portended an abandonment of the Jeffersonian political formula for <a href="/wiki/Strict_constructionism" title="Strict constructionism">strict construction</a> of the constitution, a limited central government and commitments to the primacy of Southern agrarian interests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The end of opposition parties also meant the end of party discipline and the means to suppress internecine factional animosities. Rather than produce political harmony, as President James Monroe had hoped, amalgamation had led to intense rivalries among Jeffersonian Republicans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was amid the "good feelings" of this period - during which Republican Party discipline was in abeyance - that the Tallmadge amendment surfaced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Louisiana_Purchase_and_Missouri_Territory">The Louisiana Purchase and Missouri Territory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missouri_Compromise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Louisiana Purchase and Missouri Territory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The immense <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Purchase" title="Louisiana Purchase">Louisiana Purchase</a> territories had been acquired through federal executive action, followed by Republican legislative authorization in 1803 during the <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a> administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the years following the War of 1812, the region, now known as <a href="/wiki/Missouri_Territory" title="Missouri Territory">Missouri Territory</a> experienced rapid settlement, led by slaveholding planters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prior to its purchase in 1803, the rulers of Spain and France had sanctioned slaveholding in the region. In 1812, the state of Louisiana, a major cotton producer and the first to be carved from the Louisiana Purchase, had entered the Union as a slave state. Predictably, Missourians were adamant that slave labor should not be molested by the federal government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Agriculturally, the land comprising the lower reaches of the Missouri River, from which that new state would formed, had no prospects as a major cotton producer. Suited for diversified farming, the only crop regarded as promising for slave labor was hemp culture. On that basis, southern planters immigrated with their chattel to Missouri, the slave population rising from 3,000 in 1810 to 10,000 in 1820. In a total population 66,000, slaves represented about 15 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1818, the population of Missouri territory was approaching the threshold that would qualify it for statehood. An enabling act was provided to Congress empowering territorial residents to select convention delegates and draft a state constitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The admission of Missouri territory as a slave state was expected to be more or less routine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_15th_Congress_Debates:_1819">The 15th Congress Debates: 1819</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missouri_Compromise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The 15th Congress Debates: 1819">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:James_Tallmadge_portrait.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1054" data-file-width="803" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/James_Tallmadge_portrait.jpg/220px-James_Tallmadge_portrait.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/James_Tallmadge_portrait.jpg/330px-James_Tallmadge_portrait.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/James_Tallmadge_portrait.jpg/440px-James_Tallmadge_portrait.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:James_Tallmadge_portrait.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/James_Tallmadge,_Jr." title="James Tallmadge, Jr.">James Tallmadge, Jr.</a> (1778 – 1853) Congressman James Tallmadge, Jr. served only in the 15th Congress, but he left his mark on the proceeding of the 16th with his antislavery amendment to Missouri statehood.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When the Missouri statehood bill was opened for debate in the House of Representative on February 13, 1819, early exchanges on the floor proceeded without serious incident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the course of these proceedings Congressman <a href="/wiki/James_Tallmadge,_Jr." title="James Tallmadge, Jr.">James Tallmadge, Jr.</a> of New York "tossed a bombshell into the Era of Good Feelings" with the following amendments:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>Provided, that the further introduction of slavery or involuntary servitude be prohibited, except for the punishment of crimes, whereof the party shall have been fully convicted; and that all children born within the said State</i> [of Missouri], <i>after the admission thereof into the Union, shall be free at the age of twenty-five years</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>A political outsider, the 41-year old Tallmadge conceived his amendment based on a personal aversion to slavery. He had played a leading role in accelerating emancipation of the remaining slaves in New York in 1817. Moreover, he had campaigned against Illinois’ Black Codes: though ostensibly free-soil, the new Illinois state constitution permitted indentured servitude and a limited form of slavery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> As a New York Republican, Tallmadge maintained an uneasy association with Governor <a href="/wiki/DeWitt_Clinton" title="DeWitt Clinton">DeWitt Clinton</a>, a former Republican who depended on support from ex-Federalists. Clinton's faction was hostile to Tallmadge for his spirited defense of General Andrew Jackson over the <a href="/wiki/Seminole_Wars" title="Seminole Wars">Florida campaign</a> against the Seminoles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tallmadge had backing from a fellow New York Republican, Congressman <a href="/wiki/John_W._Taylor_(politician)" title="John W. Taylor (politician)">John W. Taylor</a> (not to be confused with legislator <a href="/wiki/John_Taylor_of_Caroline" title="John Taylor of Caroline">John Taylor of Caroline</a> County, Virginia) . Taylor also had antislavery credentials: In February, 1819, he had proposed similar slave restrictions on Arkansas territory in the House, but failed 89-87. He would lead the pro-Tallmadge antislavery forces during the 16th Congress in 1820.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The amendment instantly exposed the polarization among Jeffersonian Republicans over the future of slavery in the nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Northern Jeffersonian Republicans formed a coalition across factional lines with remnants of the Federalists. Southern Jeffersonian united in almost unanimous opposition. The ensuing debates pitted the northern "restrictionists" (antislavery legislators who wished to bar slavery from the Louisiana territories) and southern "anti-restrictionists" ( proslavery legislators who rejected any interference by Congress inhibiting slavery expansion).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sectional "rupture" over slavery among Jeffersonian Republicans, first exposed in the Missouri crisis, had its roots in the Revolutionary generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jeffersonian_Republicanism_and_Slavery">Jeffersonian Republicanism and Slavery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missouri_Compromise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Jeffersonian Republicanism and Slavery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jefferson-peale.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="524" height="269" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Jefferson-peale.jpg/220px-Jefferson-peale.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Jefferson-peale.jpg/330px-Jefferson-peale.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Jefferson-peale.jpg/440px-Jefferson-peale.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jefferson-peale.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Thomas Jefferson (1741-1826): The Missouri crisis roused Jefferson “like a fire bell in the night” believing the congressional impasse would lead to a sectional rupture in the American republican. Shifting from his early egalitarian origins, Jefferson would promote the theory of “diffusion” – expanding the slavery so as to end slavery<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Missouri crisis marked a rupture in the Republican Ascendency – the national association of Jeffersonian Republicans that dominated national politics in the post-War of 1812 period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Founding_Fathers_of_the_United_States" title="Founding Fathers of the United States">Founders</a> had inserted both principled and pragmatic elements in the establishing documents. The Declaration of Independence of 1776 was grounded on the claim that liberty established a moral ideal that made universal equality a common right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The Revolutionary War generation had formed a government of limited powers in 1787 to embody the principles in the Declaration, but "burdened with the one legacy that defied that principles of 1776": human bondage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In a pragmatic commitment to form the Union, the federal apparatus would forego any authority to directly interfere with the institution of slavery where it existed under local control within the states. This acknowledgment of state sovereignty provided for the participation of those states most committed to slave labor. With this understanding, slaveholders had cooperated in authorizing the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Ordinance" title="Northwest Ordinance">Northwest Ordinance</a> in 1787, and to outlawing the trans-Atlantic slave trade in 1808.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Though the Founders sanctioned slavery, they did so with the implicit understanding that the slaveholding states would take steps to relinquish the institution as opportunities arose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Southerners states, after the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">War for Independence</a>, had regarded slavery as an institution in decline (with the exception of Georgia and South Carolina). This was manifest in the shift towards diversified farming in the Upper South, and in the gradual emancipation of slaves in New England, and more significantly, in the mid-Atlantic states. Beginning in the 1790s, with the introduction of the cotton gin, and by 1815, with the vast increase in demand for cotton internationally, slave based agriculture underwent an immense revival, spreading the institution westward to the Mississippi River. Slavery opponents in the South vacillated, as did their hopes for the imminent demise of human bondage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>However rancorous the disputes among Southerners themselves over the virtues of a slave-based society, they united as a section when confronted by external challenges to their institution. The free states were not to meddle in the affairs of the slaveholders. Southern leaders – of whom virtually all identified as Jeffersonian Republicans – denied that Northerners had any business encroaching on matters related to slavery. Northern attacks on the institution were condemned as incitements to riot among the slave populations, - deemed a dire threat to white southern security.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Northern Jeffersonian Republicans embraced the Jeffersionian antislavery legacy during the Missouri debates, explicitly citing the Declaration of Independence as an argument against expanding the institution. Southern Jeffersonian leaders would renounce the document's universal egalitarian applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>These Jeffersonian paradoxes stood at the center of the Jeffersonian Ascendency's clash over Missouri statehood and the "true meaning of the American Revolution".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Struggle_for_Political_Power">Struggle for Political Power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missouri_Compromise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Struggle for Political Power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".E2.80.9CFederal_Ratio.E2.80.9D_in_the_House">“Federal Ratio” in the House</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missouri_Compromise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: “Federal Ratio” in the House">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:178px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RufusKing.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="225" data-file-width="176" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b3/RufusKing.jpg" width="176"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RufusKing.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Rufus_King" title="Rufus King">Rufus King</a> (1755 – 1827): A signatory to the US Constitution and last of the Federalist icons, Senator Rufus King of New York denounced the <a href="/wiki/Three-Fifths_Compromise" title="Three-Fifths Compromise">three-fifths rule</a> as a Southern imposition upon the liberty of the Northeastern free states. King was dismayed by the charges of a Federalist conspiracy to establish a northern antislavery party – a conspiracy decoy concocted by southerners to distract slavery restrictionists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Article_One_of_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Article One of the United States Constitution">Article One, Section Two</a> of the US Constitution supplemented legislative representation in those states where residents owned slaves. Known as the <a href="/wiki/Three-Fifths_Compromise" title="Three-Fifths Compromise">three-fifths clause</a> or the “federal ratio”, three-fifths (60%) of the slave population was numerically added to the free population. This sum was used to calculate Congressional districts per state, and the number of delegates to the Electoral College. The federal ratio produced a significant number of legislative victories for the South in the years preceding the Missouri crisis, as well as augmenting its influence in party caucuses, the appointment of judges and the distribution of patronage. It is unlikely that the three-fifths clause, prior to 1820, was decisive in affecting legislation on slavery. Indeed, with the rising northern representation in the House, the South’s share of the membership had declined since the 1790s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hostility to the federal ratio had historically been the object of the now nationally ineffectual Federalists; they blamed their collective decline on the “<a href="/wiki/Virginia_dynasty" title="Virginia dynasty">Virginia Dynasty</a>”, expressed in partisan terms rather than in moral condemnation of slavery. The pro-De Witt Clinton-Federalist faction carried on the tradition, posing as antirestrictionists, for the purpose of advancing their fortunes in New York politics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Senator <a href="/wiki/Rufus_King" title="Rufus King">Rufus King</a> of New York, a Clinton associate, was the last Federalist icon still active on the national stage, a fact irksome to Southern Republicans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> A signatory to the US Constitution, he had strongly opposed the three-fifths rule in 1787. In the 1819 15th Congress debates, he revived his critique as a complaint that New England and Mid-Atlantic States suffered unduly from the federal ratio, declaring himself “degraded” (politically inferior) to the slaveholders. Federalists, North and South, preferred to mute antislavery rhetoric, but during the 1820 debates in the 16th Congress, King and other old Federalists would expand their critique to include moral considerations of slavery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>Republican James Tallmadge, Jr. and the Missouri restrictionists deplored the three-fifths clause because it had translated into political supremacy for the South. They had no agenda to remove it from the founding document, only to prevent its further application west of the Mississippi River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>As determined as Southern Republicans were to secure Missouri statehood with slavery, the three-fifths clause failed to provide the margin of victory in the 15th Congress. Blocked by Northern Republicans - largely on egalitarian grounds – with sectional support from Federalists, the bill would die in the upper house, where the federal ratio had no relevance. The “balance of power” between the sections, and the maintenance of Southern preeminence on matters related to slavery resided in the Senate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Balance_of_Power.22_in_the_Senate">"Balance of Power" in the Senate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missouri_Compromise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title='Edit section: "Balance of Power" in the Senate'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Northern voting majorities in the lower house did not translate into political dominance. The fulcrum for proslavery forces resided in the upper house of Congress. There, constitutional compromise n 1787 had provided for exactly two senators per state, regardless of its population: the South, with its small white demographic relative to the North, benefited from this arrangement. Since 1815, sectional parity in the Senate had been achieved through paired admissions, leaving the North and South, at the time of Missouri territory application for statehood, at eleven states each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>The South, voting as a bloc on measures that challenged slaveholding interests and augmented by defections from Free State Senators with Southern sympathies, was able to tally majorities. The Senate stood as the bulwark and source of the <a href="/wiki/Slave_Power" title="Slave Power">Slave Power</a> – a power that required admission of slave states to the Union to preserve its national primacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>Missouri statehood, with the Tallmadge amendment approved, would set a trajectory towards a Free State trans-Mississippi and a decline in Southern political authority. The question as to whether the Congress could lawfully restrain the growth of slavery in Missouri took on great importance among the slave states. The moral dimensions of the expansion of human bondage would be raised by Northern Republicans on constitutional grounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Constitutional_Arguments">Constitutional Arguments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missouri_Compromise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Constitutional Arguments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Tallmadge amendment was “the first serious challenge to the extension of slavery” and raised questions concerning the interpretation of the republics’ founding documents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jeffersonian Republicans justified Tallmadge’s slavery restrictions on the grounds that Congress possessed the authority to impose territorial statutes which would remain in force after statehood was established. Representative John W. Taylor pointed to Indiana and Illinois, where their Free State status conformed to the antislavery provisions in the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Ordinance" title="Northwest Ordinance">Northwest Ordinance</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:185px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Timothy_Fuller.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="249" data-file-width="183" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1b/Timothy_Fuller.jpg" width="183"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Timothy_Fuller.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Timothy Fuller(1778-1835): Massachusetts Congressman Timothy Fuller declared that it was "the right and the duty" of Congress to inhibit its expansion into the territories, invoking the Declaration of Independence and Article Four, Section Four of the US Constitution - compelling all states to adhere to a republican form of government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:John_scott.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="798" data-file-width="495" height="258" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/John_scott.jpg/160px-John_scott.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/John_scott.jpg/240px-John_scott.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/John_scott.jpg/320px-John_scott.jpg 2x" width="160"/></a>

John Scott (1785-1861): John Scott, the territorial delegate from Missouri, made the strongest claim to a slavery precedent in Missouri, declaring that the Louisiana Purchase Treaty of 1803 provided for the protection of the resident's property - including property in slaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Further, antislavery legislators invoked <a href="/wiki/Article_Four_of_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Article Four of the United States Constitution">Article Four, Section Four</a> of the Constitution, which required that states provide a republican form of government. As the <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Territory" title="Louisiana Territory">Louisiana Territory</a> was not part of the United States in 1787, they argued, introducing slavery into Missouri would thwart the egalitarian intent of the Founders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p>Proslavery Republicans countered that the Constitution had long been interpreted as having relinquished any claim to restricting slavery within the states. The free inhabitants of Missouri, either in the territorial phase or during statehood, had the right to establish slavery – or disestablish it – exclusive of central government interference. As to the Northwest Ordinance, Southerners denied that this could serve as a lawful antecedent for the territories of the Louisiana Purchase, as the ordinance had been issue originally under the <a href="/wiki/Articles_of_Confederation" title="Articles of Confederation">Articles of Confederation</a>, not under the US Constitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a legal precedent, they offered the treaty acquiring the Louisiana lands in 1803: the document included a provision (Article 3) that extended the rights of US citizens to all inhabitants of the new territory, including the protection of property in slaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> When slaveholders embraced Jeffersonian constitutional strictures on a limited central government they were reminded that Jefferson, as US President in 1803, had deviated from these precepts when he wielded federal executive power to double the size the United States (including the lands under consideration for Missouri statehood). In doing so, he set a Constitutional precedent that would serve to rationalize Tallmadge’s federally imposed slavery restrictions. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 15th Congress debates, focusing at it did on constitutional questions, largely avoided the moral dimensions raised by the topic of slavery. That the unmentionable subject had been raised publicly was deeply offensive to Southern Congressmen, and violated the long-held sectional understanding between free and slave state legislators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>Missouri statehood confronted Southern Jeffersonians with the prospect of applying the egalitarian principles espoused by the Revolutionary generation. This would require halting the spread of slavery westward, and confining the institution to where it already existed. Faced with a population of 1.5 million slaves, and the lucrative production of cotton, the South would abandon hopes for containment. Slaveholders in the 16th Congress, in an effort to come to grips with this paradox, would resort to a theory that called for extending slavery geographically so as to encourage its decline: “diffusion”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stalemate">Stalemate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missouri_Compromise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Stalemate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On February 16, 1819, the House <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Committee_of_the_Whole" title="Committee of the Whole">Committee of the Whole</a> voted to link Tallmadge’s provisions with the Missouri enabling legislation, approving the move 79-67.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> Following the committee vote, debates resumed over the merits of each of Tallmadge's provisions in the enabling act. The debates in the House the 2nd session in 1819 lasted only three days.They have been characterized as “rancorous”, “fiery”, “bitter”, “blistering”, “furious” and "bloodthirsty".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>

<p>The enabling bill was passed to the Senate, where both parts of the bill were rejected: 22 to 16 opposed to restricting new slaves in Missouri (supported by five northerners, two of whom were the proslavery legislators from the free state of Illinois); and 31 to 7 against gradual emancipation for slave children born post-statehood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> House antislavery restrictionists refused to concur with the Senate proslavery anti-restrictionists: Missouri statehood would devolve upon the 16th Congress in December 1819.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Federalist_.E2.80.9Cplots.E2.80.9D_and_.E2.80.9CConsolidation.E2.80.9D">Federalist “plots” and “Consolidation”</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missouri_Compromise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Federalist “plots” and “Consolidation”">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:De_Witt_Clinton_by_Henry_Inman.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="539" height="261" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/De_Witt_Clinton_by_Henry_Inman.jpg/220px-De_Witt_Clinton_by_Henry_Inman.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/De_Witt_Clinton_by_Henry_Inman.jpg/330px-De_Witt_Clinton_by_Henry_Inman.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/De_Witt_Clinton_by_Henry_Inman.jpg/440px-De_Witt_Clinton_by_Henry_Inman.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:De_Witt_Clinton_by_Henry_Inman.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
DeWitt Clinton (1769 – 1828): New York Governor Clinton, a stealth-Federalist, seized upon the Missouri controversy to discredit his local Republican adversaries – the <a href="/wiki/Bucktails" title="Bucktails">Bucktails</a>, as tools of the <a href="/wiki/Slave_Power" title="Slave Power">Slave Power</a>. Clinton labeled this Republican faction, led by archenemy <a href="/wiki/Martin_Van_Buren" title="Martin Van Buren">Martin Van Buren</a>, the “Slave Ticket”. Despite Governor Clinton’s posturing as an antislavery zealot, most Federalists in New York and throughout the North were wary of the Tallmadge Amendment: Jeffersonian Republicans in the North would be its principle champions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup></div>

<p>The Missouri Compromise debates stirred suspicions among proslavery interests that the underlying purpose of the Tallmadge amendments had little to do with opposition to slavery expansion. The accusation was first leveled in the House by the Republican anti-restrictionist <a href="/wiki/John_Holmes_(Maine_politician)" title="John Holmes (Maine politician)">John Holmes</a> from the District of Maine. He suggested that Senator Rufus King’s “warm” support for the Tallmadge amendment concealed a conspiracy to organize a new antislavery party in the North – a party composed of old Federalists in combination with disaffected antislavery Republicans. The fact that King, in the Senate, and Tallmadge and Tyler, in the House – all New Yorkers – were among the vanguard for slavery restriction in Missouri lent credibility to these charges. When King was re-elected to the US Senate in January 1820, during the 16th Congress debates, and with bipartisan support, suspicions deepened and would persist throughout the crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> Southern Jeffersonian Republican leadership, including President Monroe and former President Thomas Jefferson considered it as an article of faith that Federalists, given the chance, would destabilize the Union so as to re-impose monarchal rule in North America, and “consolidate” political control over the people by expanding the functions of the central government. Jefferson, at first unperturbed by the Missouri question, soon became convinced that a northern conspiracy was afoot, with Federalists and crypto-Federalists posing as Republicans, using Missouri statehood as a pretext.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to the disarray of the Republican Ascendency brought about by amalgamation, fears abounded among Southerners that a Free State party might take shape in the event that Congress fail to reach an understanding over Missouri and slavery: Such a party would threaten Southern preeminence. Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/John_Quincy_Adams" title="John Quincy Adams">John Quincy Adams</a> of Massachusetts surmised that the political configuration for just such a sectional party already existed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> That the Federalists were anxious to regain a measure of political participation in national politics is indisputable. There was no basis, however, for the charge that Federalists had directed Tallmadge in his antislavery measures, nor was there anything to indicate that a New York based King-Clinton alliance sought to erect an antislavery party on the ruins of the Republican Party. The allegations by Southern proslavery interests of a “plot” or that of “consolidation” as a threat to the Union misapprehended the forces at work in the Missouri crisis: the core of the opposition to slavery in the Louisiana Purchase were informed by Jeffersonian egalitarian principles, not a Federalist resurgence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_in_Congress">Development in Congress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missouri_Compromise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Development in Congress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Missouri_Compromise_Line.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="593" data-file-width="959" height="247" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Missouri_Compromise_Line.svg/400px-Missouri_Compromise_Line.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Missouri_Compromise_Line.svg/600px-Missouri_Compromise_Line.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Missouri_Compromise_Line.svg/800px-Missouri_Compromise_Line.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Missouri_Compromise_Line.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Extension of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missouri_Compromise_Line" title="Missouri Compromise Line">Missouri Compromise Line</a> westward was discussed by Congress during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Texas_Annexation" title="Texas Annexation">Texas Annexation</a> in 1845, during the <a href="/wiki/Compromise_of_1850" title="Compromise of 1850">Compromise of 1850</a>, and as part of the proposed <a href="/wiki/Crittenden_Compromise" title="Crittenden Compromise">Crittenden Compromise</a> in 1860, but the line never reached the Pacific.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>To balance the number of "slave states" and "free states," the northern region of what was then <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, the <a href="/wiki/District_of_Maine" title="District of Maine">District of Maine</a>, ultimately gained <a href="/wiki/Admission_to_the_Union" title="Admission to the Union">admission into the United States</a> as a free state to become <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>. This only occurred as a result of a compromise involving slavery in Missouri, and in the federal territories of the American west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> The admission of another slave state would increase the South's power at a time when northern politicians had already begun to regret the Constitution's <a href="/wiki/Three-Fifths_Compromise" title="Three-Fifths Compromise">Three-Fifths Compromise</a>. Although more than 60 percent of <a href="/wiki/White_Americans" title="White Americans">whites</a> in the United States lived in the North, by 1818 northern representatives held only a slim majority of congressional seats. The additional political representation allotted to the South as a result of the Three-Fifths Compromise gave southerners more seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a> than they would have had if the number was based on just free population. Moreover, since each state had two Senate seats, Missouri's admission as a slave state would result in more southern than northern senators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> A bill to enable the people of the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_Territory" title="Missouri Territory">Missouri Territory</a> to draft a constitution and form a government preliminary to admission into the Union came before the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Committee_of_the_Whole" title="Committee of the Whole">Committee of the Whole</a>, on February 13, 1819. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Tallmadge" title="James Tallmadge">James Tallmadge</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> offered an amendment, named the <a href="/wiki/Tallmadge_Amendment" title="Tallmadge Amendment">Tallmadge Amendment</a>, that forbade further introduction of slaves into Missouri, and mandated that all children of slave parents born in the state after its admission should be free at the age of 25. The committee adopted the measure and incorporated it into the bill as finally passed on February 17, 1819, by the house. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> refused to concur with the amendment, and the whole measure was lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the following session (1819–1820), the House passed a similar bill with an amendment, introduced on January 26, 1820, by <a href="/wiki/John_W._Taylor_(politician)" title="John W. Taylor (politician)">John W. Taylor</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, allowing Missouri into the union as a slave state. The question had been complicated by the admission in December of <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave_state" title="Slave state">slave state</a>, making the number of slave and free states equal. In addition, there was a bill in passage through the House (January 3, 1820) to admit <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_state_(United_States)" title="Free state (United States)">free state</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Senate decided to connect the two measures. It passed a bill for the admission of Maine with an amendment enabling the people of Missouri to form a state constitution. Before the bill was returned to the House, a second amendment was adopted on the motion of <a href="/wiki/Jesse_B._Thomas" title="Jesse B. Thomas">Jesse B. Thomas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, excluding slavery from the <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Territory" title="Louisiana Territory">Louisiana Territory</a> north of the <a href="/wiki/Parallel_36%C2%B030%E2%80%B2_north" title="Parallel 36°30′ north">parallel 36°30′ north</a> (the southern boundary of Missouri), except within the limits of the proposed state of Missouri.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greeley_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greeley-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
<p>The vote in the Senate was 24 for the compromise, to 20 against. The amendment and the bill passed in the Senate on February 17 and February 18, 1820.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greeley_108-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greeley-108">[108]</a></sup> The House then approved the Senate compromise amendment, on a vote of 90 to 87, with those 87 votes coming from free state representatives opposed to slavery in the new state of Missouri.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greeley_108-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greeley-108">[108]</a></sup> The House then approved the whole bill, 134 to 42 (the latter votes being from southern states).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greeley_108-3"><a href="#cite_note-Greeley-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_Missouri_Compromise">Second Missouri Compromise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missouri_Compromise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Second Missouri Compromise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The two houses were at odds not only on the issue of the legality of slavery, but also on the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_procedure" title="Parliamentary procedure">parliamentary question</a> of the inclusion of Maine and Missouri within the same bill. The committee recommended the enactment of two laws, one for the admission of Maine, the other an <a href="/wiki/Enabling_act" title="Enabling act">enabling act</a> for Missouri. They recommended against having restrictions on slavery but for including the Thomas amendment. Both houses agreed, and the measures were passed on March 5, 1820, and were signed by President <a href="/wiki/James_Monroe" title="James Monroe">James Monroe</a> on March 6.</p>
<p>The question of the final admission of Missouri came up during the session of 1820–1821. The struggle was revived over a clause in Missouri's new constitution (written in 1820) requiring the exclusion of "free negroes and mulattoes" from the state. Through the influence of <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> Senator <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay" title="Henry Clay">Henry Clay</a> "The Great Compromiser", an act of admission was finally passed, upon the condition that the exclusionary clause of the Missouri constitution should "never be construed to authorize the passage of any law" impairing the privileges and immunities of any U.S. citizen. This deliberately ambiguous provision is sometimes known as the Second Missouri Compromise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_political_discourse">Impact on political discourse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missouri_Compromise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Impact on political discourse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:US_Slave_Free_1789-1861.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="468" data-file-width="774" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/US_Slave_Free_1789-1861.gif/260px-US_Slave_Free_1789-1861.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/US_Slave_Free_1789-1861.gif/390px-US_Slave_Free_1789-1861.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/US_Slave_Free_1789-1861.gif/520px-US_Slave_Free_1789-1861.gif 2x" width="260"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:US_Slave_Free_1789-1861.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Animation showing the free/slave status of U.S. states and territories, 1789–1861, including the Missouri Compromise.</div>
</div>

<p>During the decades following 1820, Americans hailed the 1820 agreement as an essential compromise almost on the sacred level of the Constitution itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> Although the Civil War broke out in 1861, historians often say the Compromise helped postpone the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup></p>
<p>These disputes involved the competition between the southern and northern states for power in Congress and for control over future territories. There were also the same factions emerging as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic-Republican_party" title="Democratic-Republican party">Democratic-Republican party</a> began to lose its coherence.</p>
<p>In an April 22 letter to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Holmes_(U.S._politician)" title="John Holmes (U.S. politician)">John Holmes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a> wrote that the division of the country created by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missouri_Compromise_Line" title="Missouri Compromise Line">Compromise Line</a> would eventually lead to the destruction of the Union:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>...but this momentous question, like a fire bell in the night, awakened and filled me with terror. I considered it at once as the knell of the Union. it is hushed indeed for the moment. but this is a reprieve only, not a final sentence. A geographical line, coinciding with a marked principle, moral and political, once conceived and held up to the angry passions of men, will never be obliterated; and every new irritation will mark it deeper and deeper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Congress' consideration of Missouri's admission also raised the issue of sectional balance, for the country was equally divided between slave and free states with eleven each. To admit Missouri as a slave state would tip the balance in the Senate (made up of two senators per state) in favor of the slave states. For this reason, northern states wanted Maine admitted as a free state.</p>
<p>On the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">constitutional</a> side, the Compromise of 1820 was important as the example of Congressional exclusion of slavery from U.S. territory acquired since the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Ordinance" title="Northwest Ordinance">Northwest Ordinance</a>.</p>
<p>Following Maine's 1820<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> and Missouri's 1821<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Admissions_to_the_Union" title="Admissions to the Union">admissions to the Union</a>, no other states were admitted until 1836, when Arkansas was admitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Compromise was deeply disappointing to African-Americans in both the North and South. It stopped the southern progression of gradual emancipation at Missouri's southern border and legitimized slavery as a southern institution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Repeal">Repeal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Missouri_Compromise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Repeal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The provisions of the Missouri Compromise forbidding slavery in the former <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Territory" title="Louisiana Territory">Louisiana Territory</a> north of the <a href="/wiki/Parallel_36%C2%B030%E2%80%B2_north" title="Parallel 36°30′ north">parallel 36°30′ north</a> were effectively repealed by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_A._Douglas" title="Stephen A. Douglas">Stephen A. Douglas</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Kansas%E2%80%93Nebraska_Act" title="Kansas–Nebraska Act">Kansas–Nebraska Act</a> of 1854. The repeal of the Compromise caused outrage in the North and sparked the return to politics of <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> who excoriated Douglas's act in his "<a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln%27s_Peoria_speech" title="Abraham Lincoln's Peoria speech">Peoria Speech</a>" (October 16, 1854).</p>
