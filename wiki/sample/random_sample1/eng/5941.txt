<p><i><b>Close Encounters of the Third Kind</b></i> is a 1977 American <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_film" title="Science fiction film">science fiction film</a> written and directed by <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a>, and starring <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dreyfuss" title="Richard Dreyfuss">Richard Dreyfuss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Truffaut" title="François Truffaut">François Truffaut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melinda_Dillon" title="Melinda Dillon">Melinda Dillon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Teri_Garr" title="Teri Garr">Teri Garr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Balaban" title="Bob Balaban">Bob Balaban</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cary_Guffey" title="Cary Guffey">Cary Guffey</a>. It tells the story of Roy Neary, an everyday <a href="/wiki/Blue-collar_worker" title="Blue-collar worker">blue-collar worker</a> in <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, whose life changes after an encounter with an <a href="/wiki/Unidentified_flying_object" title="Unidentified flying object">unidentified flying object</a> (UFO).</p>
<p><i>Close Encounters</i> was a long-cherished project for Spielberg. In late 1973, he developed a deal with <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a> for a science fiction film. Though Spielberg received sole credit for the script, he was assisted by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Schrader" title="Paul Schrader">Paul Schrader</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Hill_(screenwriter)" title="John Hill (screenwriter)">John Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Giler" title="David Giler">David Giler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hal_Barwood" title="Hal Barwood">Hal Barwood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Robbins_(screenwriter)" title="Matthew Robbins (screenwriter)">Matthew Robbins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Belson" title="Jerry Belson">Jerry Belson</a>, all of whom contributed to the screenplay in varying degrees. The title is derived from UFO-ologist <a href="/wiki/J._Allen_Hynek" title="J. Allen Hynek">J. Allen Hynek</a>'s classification of <a href="/wiki/Close_encounter" title="Close encounter">close encounters</a> with aliens, in which the third kind denotes human observations of aliens or "animate beings." <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Trumbull" title="Douglas Trumbull">Douglas Trumbull</a> served as the <a href="/wiki/Visual_effects_supervisor" title="Visual effects supervisor">visual effects supervisor</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Rambaldi" title="Carlo Rambaldi">Carlo Rambaldi</a> designed the aliens.</p>
<p>Made on a production budget of $20 million, <i>Close Encounters</i> was released in a limited number of cities on November 16, 1977<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBride.2C_Joseph_1997_pg_204_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-McBride.2C_Joseph_1997_pg_204-4">[4]</a></sup> and November 23, 1977<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> before expanding into wide release the following month. It was a critical and financial success, eventually grossing over $337 million worldwide.</p>
<p>The film received numerous awards and nominations at the <a href="/wiki/50th_Academy_Awards" title="50th Academy Awards">50th Academy Awards</a>, <a href="/wiki/32nd_British_Academy_Film_Awards" title="32nd British Academy Film Awards">32nd British Academy Film Awards</a>, the <a href="/wiki/35th_Golden_Globe_Awards" title="35th Golden Globe Awards">35th Golden Globe Awards</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_Award" title="Saturn Award">Saturn Awards</a> and has been widely acclaimed by the <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">American Film Institute</a>. In December 2007, it was deemed "culturally, historically, or aesthetically significant" by the United States <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a> and selected for preservation in the <a href="/wiki/National_Film_Registry" title="National Film Registry">National Film Registry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc-nfl_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-loc-nfl-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <i>Special Edition</i> of the film, featuring additional scenes, was released theatrically in 1980. A third cut of the film was issued on VHS and <a href="/wiki/LaserDisc" title="LaserDisc">LaserDisc</a> in 1998 (and later <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray" title="Blu-ray">Blu-ray</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Close_Encounters_of_the_Third_Kind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Sonoran_Desert" title="Sonoran Desert">Sonoran Desert</a>, French scientist Claude Lacombe (<a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Truffaut" title="François Truffaut">François Truffaut</a>) and his American interpreter, mapmaker David Laughlin (<a href="/wiki/Bob_Balaban" title="Bob Balaban">Bob Balaban</a>), along with other government scientific researchers, discover <a href="/wiki/Flight_19" title="Flight 19">Flight 19</a>, a squadron of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grumman_TBM_Avenger" title="Grumman TBM Avenger">Grumman TBM Avengers</a> that went missing more than 30 years earlier. The planes are intact and operational, but there is no sign of the pilots. An old man who witnessed the event claimed "the sun came out at night, and sang to him." They also find a lost cargo ship in the <a href="/wiki/Gobi_Desert" title="Gobi Desert">Gobi Desert</a> named <a href="/wiki/SS_Cotopaxi" title="SS Cotopaxi">SS <i>Cotopaxi</i></a>. At an <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control" title="Air traffic control">air traffic control</a> center in Indianapolis, a controller listens as two airline flights narrowly avoid a mid-air collision with an apparent unidentified flying object (UFO), which neither pilot chooses to report, even when invited to do so. In <a href="/wiki/Muncie,_Indiana" title="Muncie, Indiana">Muncie, Indiana</a>, 3-year-old Barry Guiler (<a href="/wiki/Cary_Guffey" title="Cary Guffey">Cary Guffey</a>) is awakened in the night when his toys start operating on their own. Fascinated, he gets out of bed and discovers something or someone (off-screen) in the kitchen. He runs outside, forcing his mother, Jillian (<a href="/wiki/Melinda_Dillon" title="Melinda Dillon">Melinda Dillon</a>), to chase after him.</p>
<p>Investigating one of a series of large-scale power outages, Indiana electrical lineman Roy Neary (<a href="/wiki/Richard_Dreyfuss" title="Richard Dreyfuss">Richard Dreyfuss</a>) experiences a close encounter with a UFO, when it flies over his truck and lightly burns the side of his face with its bright lights. The UFO, joining a group of three other UFOs, is pursued by Neary and three police cars, but the spacecraft fly off into the night sky. Roy becomes fascinated by UFOs, much to the dismay of his wife, Ronnie (<a href="/wiki/Teri_Garr" title="Teri Garr">Teri Garr</a>). He also becomes increasingly obsessed with subliminal, mental images of a mountain-like shape and begins to make models of it. Jillian also becomes obsessed with sketching a unique-looking mountain. Soon after, she is terrorized in her home by a UFO which descends from the clouds. The presence of the UFO energy field makes every appliance in Jillian's house malfunction and Barry is abducted by unseen beings.</p>
<p>Lacombe and Laughlin—along with a group of United Nations experts—continue to investigate increasing UFO activity and strange, related occurrences. Witnesses in <a href="/wiki/Dharamsala" title="Dharamsala">Dharamsala</a>, India report that the UFOs make distinctive sounds: a five-tone musical phrase in a major scale. Scientists broadcast the phrase to outer space, but are mystified by the response: a seemingly meaningless series of numbers (104 44 30 40 36 10) repeated over and over until Laughlin, with his background in <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">cartography</a>, recognizes it as a set of geographical coordinates (this is an error; the given coordinates would in reality lead one near Ft. Collins, Colorado, if the 40 had been a 44, the coordinates would be accurate). The coordinates point to <a href="/wiki/Devils_Tower" title="Devils Tower">Devils Tower</a> near <a href="/wiki/Moorcroft,_Wyoming" title="Moorcroft, Wyoming">Moorcroft, Wyoming</a>. Lacombe and the U.S. military converge on Wyoming. The United States Army evacuates the area, planting false reports in the media that a train wreck has spilled a toxic nerve gas, all the while preparing a secret landing zone for the UFOs and their occupants.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, Roy's increasingly erratic behavior causes Ronnie to leave him, taking their three children with her. When a despairing Roy inadvertently sees a television news program about the train wreck near Devils Tower, he realizes the mental image of a mountain plaguing him is real. Jillian sees the same broadcast, and she and Roy, as well as others with similar visions and experiences, travel to the site in spite of the public warnings about nerve gas.</p>
<p>While most of the civilians who are drawn to the site are apprehended by the Army, Roy and Jillian persist and make it to the site just as dozens of UFOs appear in the night sky. The government specialists at the site begin to communicate with the UFOs by use of light and sound on a large electrical billboard. Following this, an enormous mother ship lands at the site, releasing animals and over a dozen long-missing adults and children, all from different past eras. Among these returned abductees include the missing pilots from Flight 19 and sailors from the <i>Cotopaxi</i>, all of whom have strangely not aged since their abductions. Barry is also returned and reunited with a relieved Jillian. The government officials decide to include Roy in a group of people whom they have selected to be potential visitors to the mothership, and hastily prepare him.</p>
<p>As the aliens finally emerge from the mothership, they select Roy to join them on their travels. As Roy enters the mothership, one of the aliens pauses for a few moments with the humans. Lacombe uses <a href="/wiki/Kod%C3%A1ly_method#Hand_signs" title="Kodály method">Curwen hand signs</a> that correspond to the five-note alien tonal phrase. The alien replies with the same gestures, smiles, and returns to its ship, which ascends into space.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Close_Encounters_of_the_Third_Kind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Close_Encounters_of_the_Third_Kind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film's origins can be traced to director <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a>'s youth, when he and his father watched a <a href="/wiki/Meteor_shower" title="Meteor shower">meteor shower</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-making-7">[7]</a></sup> As a teenager, Spielberg completed the full-length science fiction film <i><a href="/wiki/Firelight_(1964_film)" title="Firelight (1964 film)">Firelight</a></i>. Many scenes from <i>Firelight</i> would be incorporated in <i>Close Encounters</i> on a <a href="/wiki/Shot-for-shot" title="Shot-for-shot">shot-for-shot</a> basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fire-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1970 he wrote a <a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">short story</a> called <i>Experiences</i> about a lovers' lane in a Midwestern United States farming community and the "light show" a group of teenagers see in the night sky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-state_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-state-12">[12]</a></sup> In late 1973, during <a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">post-production</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sugarland_Express" title="The Sugarland Express">The Sugarland Express</a></i>, Spielberg developed a deal with <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a> for a science fiction film. <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> previously turned down the offer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-state_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-state-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Julia_Phillips" title="Julia Phillips">Julia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Phillips_(producer)" title="Michael Phillips (producer)">Michael Phillips</a> instantly signed on as producers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>He first considered doing a documentary or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_budget_film" title="Low budget film">low-budget feature film</a> about people who believed in <a href="/wiki/Unidentified_flying_object" title="Unidentified flying object">UFOs</a>. Spielberg decided "a film that depended on <a href="/wiki/State_of_the_art" title="State of the art">state of the art</a> technology couldn't be made for $2.5 million."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-state_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-state-12">[12]</a></sup> Borrowing a phrase from the ending of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Thing_from_Another_World" title="The Thing from Another World">The Thing from Another World</a></i>, he retitled the film <i>Watch the Skies</i>, rewriting the premise concerning <a href="/wiki/Project_Blue_Book" title="Project Blue Book">Project Blue Book</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(filmmaking)" title="Pitch (filmmaking)">pitching</a> the concept to <a href="/wiki/Willard_Huyck" title="Willard Huyck">Willard Huyck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Katz" title="Gloria Katz">Gloria Katz</a>. Katz remembered "It had flying saucers from outer space landing on <a href="/wiki/Robertson_Boulevard" title="Robertson Boulevard">Robertson Boulevard</a> [in <a href="/wiki/West_Hollywood,_California" title="West Hollywood, California">West Hollywood, California</a>]. I go, 'Steve, that's the worst idea I ever heard."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-state_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-state-12">[12]</a></sup> Spielberg brought <a href="/wiki/Paul_Schrader" title="Paul Schrader">Paul Schrader</a> to write the script in December 1973 with <a href="/wiki/Principal_photography" title="Principal photography">principal photography</a> to begin in late 1974. However, Spielberg started work on <i><a href="/wiki/Jaws_(film)" title="Jaws (film)">Jaws</a></i> in 1974, pushing <i>Watch the Skies</i> back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-state_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-state-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the financial and critical success of <i>Jaws</i>, Spielberg earned a vast amount of creative control from Columbia, including the right to make the film any way he wanted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-come_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-come-14">[14]</a></sup> Schrader turned in his script, which Spielberg called, "one of the most embarrassing screenplays ever professionally turned in to a major film studio or director. It was a terribly guilt-ridden story not about UFOs at all."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreyfuss_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dreyfuss-8">[8]</a></sup> Titled <i>Kingdom Come</i>, the script's <a href="/wiki/Protagonist" title="Protagonist">protagonist</a> was a 45-year-old Air Force Officer named Paul Van Owen who worked with Project Blue Book. "[His] job for the government is to ridicule and debunk flying saucers." Schrader continued. "One day he has an encounter. He goes to the government, threatening to blow the lid off to the public. Instead, he and the government spend 15 years trying to make contact."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreyfuss_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dreyfuss-8">[8]</a></sup> Spielberg and Schrader experienced creative differences, hiring <a href="/wiki/John_Hill_(screenwriter)" title="John Hill (screenwriter)">John Hill</a> to rewrite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreyfuss_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dreyfuss-8">[8]</a></sup> At one point the main character was a police officer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-making-7">[7]</a></sup> Spielberg "[found] it hard to identify with men in uniform. I wanted to have Mr. Everyday Regular Fella." Spielberg rejected the Schrader/Hill script during <a href="/wiki/Post-production" title="Post-production">post-production</a> on <i>Jaws</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreyfuss_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Dreyfuss-8">[8]</a></sup> He reflected, "they wanted to make it like a <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">James Bond</a> adventure."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mind_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-mind-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/David_Giler" title="David Giler">David Giler</a> performed a rewrite; <a href="/wiki/Hal_Barwood" title="Hal Barwood">Hal Barwood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Robbins_(screenwriter)" title="Matthew Robbins (screenwriter)">Matthew Robbins</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-making-7">[7]</a></sup> friends of Spielberg, suggested the <a href="/wiki/Plot_device" title="Plot device">plot device</a> of a kidnapped child. Spielberg then began to write the script. The song "<a href="/wiki/When_You_Wish_upon_a_Star" title="When You Wish upon a Star">When You Wish upon a Star</a>" from <i><a href="/wiki/Pinocchio_(1940_film)" title="Pinocchio (1940 film)">Pinocchio</a></i> influenced Spielberg's writing style. "I hung my story on the mood the song created, the way it affected me personally."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreyfuss_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-Dreyfuss-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Belson" title="Jerry Belson">Jerry Belson</a> and Spielberg wrote the <a href="/wiki/Shooting_script" title="Shooting script">shooting script</a> together. In the end, Spielberg was given solo writing credit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreyfuss_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-Dreyfuss-8">[8]</a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/Pre-production" title="Pre-production">pre-production</a>, the title was changed from <i>Kingdom Come</i> to <i>Close Encounters of the Third Kind</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-come_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-come-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/J._Allen_Hynek" title="J. Allen Hynek">J. Allen Hynek</a>, who worked with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> on Project Blue Book, was hired as a scientific consultant. Hynek felt "even though the film is fiction, it's based for the most part on the known facts of the UFO mystery, and it certainly catches the flavor of the phenomenon. Spielberg was under enormous pressure to make another <a href="/wiki/Blockbuster_(entertainment)" title="Blockbuster (entertainment)">blockbuster</a> after <i>Jaws</i>, but he decided to make a UFO movie. He put his career on the line."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreyfuss_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-Dreyfuss-8">[8]</a></sup> USAF and <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> declined to cooperate on the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-come_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-come-14">[14]</a></sup> In fact, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> reportedly sent a 20-page letter to Spielberg, telling him that releasing the film was dangerous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In an interview, he said: "I really found my faith when I heard that the Government was opposed to the film. If NASA took the time to write me a 20-page letter, then I knew there must be something happening." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Principal_photography" title="Principal photography">Principal photography</a> began on May 16, 1976, though an <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a> report in August 1975 had suggested filming would start in late 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Spielberg did not want to do any <a href="/wiki/Location_shooting" title="Location shooting">location shooting</a> because of his negative experience on <i>Jaws</i> and wanted to shoot <i>Close Encounters</i> entirely on <a href="/wiki/Sound_stage" title="Sound stage">sound stages</a>, but eventually dropped the idea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morton_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-morton-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Filming took place in <a href="/wiki/Burbank,_California" title="Burbank, California">Burbank, California</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Devils_Tower_National_Monument" title="Devils Tower National Monument">Devils Tower National Monument</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a>; two abandoned World War II <a href="/wiki/Airship_hangar" title="Airship hangar">airship hangars</a> at the former <a href="/wiki/Brookley_Air_Force_Base" title="Brookley Air Force Base">Brookley Air Force Base</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mobile,_Alabama" title="Mobile, Alabama">Mobile, Alabama</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Louisville_and_Nashville_Railroad" title="Louisville and Nashville Railroad">Louisville and Nashville Railroad</a> depot in <a href="/wiki/Bay_Minette,_Alabama" title="Bay Minette, Alabama">Bay Minette</a>. The home where Barry was abducted is located outside the town of <a href="/wiki/Fairhope,_Alabama" title="Fairhope, Alabama">Fairhope, Alabama</a>. Roy Neary's home is at Carlisle Drive East in Mobile. The UFOs fly through the former toll booth at the <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Thomas_Bridge" title="Vincent Thomas Bridge">Vincent Thomas Bridge</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Pedro,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="San Pedro, Los Angeles, California">San Pedro</a>, California. The Gobi Desert sequence was photographed at the <a href="/wiki/Dumont_Dunes" title="Dumont Dunes">Dumont Dunes</a>, California, and the Dharmsala-India exteriors were filmed at the small village of Hal near <a href="/wiki/Khalapur" title="Khalapur">Khalapur</a>, 35 miles (56 km) outside <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Bombay</a>, India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morton_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-morton-19">[19]</a></sup> The hangars in Alabama were six times larger than the biggest sound stage in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-come_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-come-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pro_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-pro-20">[20]</a></sup> Various technical and budgetary problems occurred during filming. Spielberg called <i>Close Encounters</i> "twice as bad and twice as expensive [as <i>Jaws</i>]".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreyfuss_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-Dreyfuss-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Matters worsened when Columbia Pictures experienced financial difficulties. Spielberg estimated the film would cost $2.7 million to make in his original 1973 pitch to Columbia, but the final budget came to $19.4 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-come_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-come-14">[14]</a></sup> Columbia studio executive John Veich remembered, "If we knew it was going to cost that much, we wouldn't have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenlight" title="Greenlight">greenlighted</a> it because we didn't have the money."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-come_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-come-14">[14]</a></sup> Spielberg hired <a href="/wiki/Joe_Alves" title="Joe Alves">Joe Alves</a>, his collaborator on <i>Jaws</i>, as <a href="/wiki/Production_designer" title="Production designer">production designer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-actor_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-actor-9">[9]</a></sup> In addition the 1976 Atlantic hurricane season brought tropical storms to Alabama. A large portion of the sound stage in Alabama was damaged because of a lightning strike.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-making-7">[7]</a></sup> Columbia raised $7 million from three sources: <a href="/wiki/Time_Inc." title="Time Inc.">Time Inc.</a>, <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a>, and German tax shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cinematographer" title="Cinematographer">Cinematographer</a> <a href="/wiki/Vilmos_Zsigmond" title="Vilmos Zsigmond">Vilmos Zsigmond</a> said that, during the time of shooting for the film, Spielberg got more ideas by watching movies every night which in turn extended the production schedule because he was continually adding new scenes to be filmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreyfuss_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-Dreyfuss-8">[8]</a></sup> Zsigmond previously turned down the chance to work on <i>Jaws</i>. In her 1991 book <i><a href="/wiki/You%27ll_Never_Eat_Lunch_in_This_Town_Again" title="You'll Never Eat Lunch in This Town Again">You'll Never Eat Lunch in This Town Again</a></i>, producer Julia Phillips wrote highly profane remarks about Spielberg, Zsigmond, and Truffaut, because she was fired during post-production due to a cocaine addiction. Phillips blamed it on Spielberg being a perfectionist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-come_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-come-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_effects">Visual effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Close_Encounters_of_the_Third_Kind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Visual effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Douglas_Trumbull" title="Douglas Trumbull">Douglas Trumbull</a> was the <a href="/wiki/Visual_effects_supervisor" title="Visual effects supervisor">visual effects supervisor</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Rambaldi" title="Carlo Rambaldi">Carlo Rambaldi</a> designed the aliens. Trumbull joked that the visual effects budget, at $3.3 million, could have been used to produce an additional film. His work helped lead to advances in <a href="/wiki/Motion_control_photography" title="Motion control photography">motion control photography</a>. The mother ship was designed by <a href="/wiki/Ralph_McQuarrie" title="Ralph McQuarrie">Ralph McQuarrie</a> and built by <a href="/wiki/Greg_Jein" title="Greg Jein">Greg Jein</a>. The look of the ship was inspired by an oil refinery Spielberg saw at night in India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreyfuss_8-10"><a href="#cite_note-Dreyfuss-8">[8]</a></sup> Instead of the metallic hardware look used in <i>Star Wars</i>, the emphasis was on a more luminescent look for the UFOs. One of the UFO models was an oxygen mask with lights attached to it, used because of its irregular shape. As a subtle in-joke, <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Muren" title="Dennis Muren">Dennis Muren</a> (who had just finished working on <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars_(film)" title="Star Wars (film)">Star Wars</a></i>) put a small <a href="/wiki/R2-D2" title="R2-D2">R2-D2</a> model onto the underside of the mothership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-making-7">[7]</a></sup> The model of the mothership is now on display in the Smithsonian Institution's Air and Space Museum Udvar-Hazy Annex at Washington Dulles Airport in Chantilly, Virginia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-making-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since <i>Close Encounters</i> was filmed <a href="/wiki/Anamorphic_format" title="Anamorphic format">anamorphically</a>, the visual effects sequences were shot in <a href="/wiki/70_mm_film" title="70 mm film">70 mm film</a> to better conform with the <a href="/wiki/35_mm_film" title="35 mm film">35 mm film</a> used for the rest of the movie. A test reel using <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">computer-generated imagery</a> was used for the UFOs, but Spielberg found it would be too expensive and ineffective since CGI was in its infancy in the mid-1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_7-10"><a href="#cite_note-making-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The small aliens in the final scenes were played by local girls in <a href="/wiki/Mobile,_Alabama" title="Mobile, Alabama">Mobile, Alabama</a>. That decision was requested by Spielberg because he felt "girls move more gracefully than boys."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_7-11"><a href="#cite_note-making-7">[7]</a></sup> Puppetry was attempted for the aliens, but the idea failed. However, Rambaldi successfully used puppetry to depict two of the aliens, the first being a <a href="/wiki/Marionette" title="Marionette">marionette</a> (for the tall alien that is the first to be seen emerging from the mothership) and an articulated puppet for the alien that communicates via hand signals with Lacombe near the end of the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_7-12"><a href="#cite_note-making-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-production">Post-production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Close_Encounters_of_the_Third_Kind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Close Encounters</i> is the first collaboration between film editor <a href="/wiki/Michael_Kahn_(film_editor)" title="Michael Kahn (film editor)">Michael Kahn</a> and Spielberg. Their working relationship has continued for the rest of Spielberg's films. Spielberg stated that no film he has ever made since has been as hard to edit as the last 25 minutes of <i>Close Encounters</i> and he and Kahn would go through thousands of feet of footage just to find the right shots for the end sequence. When Kahn and Spielberg completed the first cut of the film, Spielberg was dissatisfied, feeling "there wasn't enough wow-ness".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_7-13"><a href="#cite_note-making-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pick-up_(filmmaking)" title="Pick-up (filmmaking)">Pick-ups</a> were commissioned but cinematographer Vilmos Zsigmond could not participate due to other commitments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-come_14-7"><a href="#cite_note-come-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_A._Alonzo" title="John A. Alonzo">John A. Alonzo</a>, <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A1szl%C3%B3_Kov%C3%A1cs_(cinematographer)" title="László Kovács (cinematographer)">László Kovács</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Slocombe" title="Douglas Slocombe">Douglas Slocombe</a> worked on the pick-ups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-come_14-8"><a href="#cite_note-come-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lacombe was originally to find <a href="/wiki/Flight_19" title="Flight 19">Flight 19</a> hidden in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_Rainforest" title="Amazon Rainforest">Amazon Rainforest</a>, but the idea was changed to the <a href="/wiki/Sonoran_Desert" title="Sonoran Desert">Sonoran Desert</a>. Composer <a href="/wiki/John_Williams" title="John Williams">John Williams</a> wrote over 300 examples of the iconic five-tone <a href="/wiki/Motif_(music)" title="Motif (music)">motif</a> before Spielberg chose the one used in the film. Spielberg called Williams' work "<a href="/wiki/When_You_Wish_upon_a_Star" title="When You Wish upon a Star">When You Wish upon a Star</a> meets science fiction".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_7-14"><a href="#cite_note-making-7">[7]</a></sup> Spielberg wanted to have "When You Wish upon a Star" in the <a href="/wiki/Closing_credits" title="Closing credits">closing credits</a>, but was denied permission (though the song's signature melody can be heard briefly just before Roy Neary turns to board the mothership). He also took 7.5 minutes out from the preview.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-actor_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-actor-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Post-production was completed by June 1977,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> too late for the film to be released as a 'summer blockbuster' which might have been just as well, as <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a></i> opened that summer.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">score</a> was composed, conducted and produced by John Williams, who had previously won an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> for his work on Spielberg's <i>Jaws</i>. Much like his two-note <i>Jaws</i> theme, the "five-tone" motif for <i>Close Encounters</i> has since become ingrained in popular culture (the five tones are used by scientists to communicate with the visiting spaceship as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematics_as_a_language" title="Mathematics as a language">mathematical language</a> as well as being incorporated into the film's signature theme). The score was recorded at Warner Bros. Scoring studios in <a href="/wiki/Burbank,_California" title="Burbank, California">Burbank, California</a>.</p>
<p>Williams was nominated for two Academy Awards in 1978, one for his score to <i>Star Wars</i> and one for his score to <i>Close Encounters</i>. He won for <i>Star Wars</i>, though he later won two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammy_Awards" title="Grammy Awards">Grammy Awards</a> in 1979 for his <i>Close Encounters</i> score (one for Best Original Film Score and one for Best Instrumental Composition for "Theme from Close Encounters").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The soundtrack album was released on <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" title="Gramophone record">vinyl album</a> (with a gatefold sleeve), <a href="/wiki/8-track_tape" title="8-track tape">8-track tape</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_cassette" title="Compact cassette">audio cassette</a> by <a href="/wiki/Arista_Records" title="Arista Records">Arista Records</a> in 1977, with a total running time of 41 minutes (it was later released on <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">compact disc</a> in 1990). The soundtrack album was a commercial success, peaking at #17 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">US Billboard album chart</a> in February 1978 and was certified Gold by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIAA" title="RIAA">RIAA</a> for 500,000 copies shipped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> It also peaked at #40 in the UK album charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although not included on the original soundtrack album, a 7" single of a disco treatment of the five-note motif, titled, "Theme from Close Encounters of the Third Kind", was included with the album as a free bonus item. Despite being a giveaway, <a href="/wiki/Billboard_charts" title="Billboard charts">Billboard chart</a> rules at that time allowed the single itself to chart, and it peaked at #13 on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> in March 1978. The single was later added as a bonus track to the cassette.</p>
<p>Following the release of the "Collector's Edition" of the film in 1998, a new expanded soundtrack was released on <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">compact disc</a> by Arista. The "Collectors Edition Soundtrack" was made using 20-bit <a href="/wiki/Audio_mastering" title="Audio mastering">digital remastering</a> from the original tapes, and contained 26 tracks totalling 77 minutes of music. The CD also came with extensive liner notes including an interview with Williams. Cues were given new titles, and it also contained previously unreleased material, as well as material that was recorded but never used in the film.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Close_Encounters_of_the_Third_Kind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Film critic Charlene Engel observed <i>Close Encounters</i> "suggests that humankind has reached the point where it is ready to enter the community of the cosmos. While it is a computer which makes the final musical conversation with the alien guests possible, the characteristics bringing Neary to make his way to Devils Tower have little to do with technical expertise or computer literacy. These are virtues taught in schools that will be evolved in the 21st century."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barry_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barry-25">[25]</a></sup> The film also evokes typical <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> <a href="/wiki/Archetype" title="Archetype">archetypes</a> and motifs. The film portrays new technologies as a natural and expected outcome of human development and indication of health and growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barry_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barry-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other critics found a variety of Judeo-Christian analogies. Devils Tower parallels <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sinai" title="Mount Sinai">Mount Sinai</a>, the aliens as God and Roy Neary as <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cecil_B._DeMille" title="Cecil B. DeMille">Cecil B. DeMille</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ten_Commandments_(1956_film)" title="The Ten Commandments (1956 film)">The Ten Commandments</a></i> is seen on television at the Neary household. Some found close relations between <a href="/wiki/Elijah" title="Elijah">Elijah</a> and Roy; Elijah was taken into a "chariot of fire", akin to Roy going in the UFO. Climbing Devils Tower behind the faltering Jillian, Neary exhorts Jillian to keep moving and not to look back, similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lot_(Bible)" title="Lot (Bible)">Lot</a>'s wife who looked back at <a href="/wiki/Sodom_and_Gomorrah" title="Sodom and Gomorrah">Sodom</a> and turned into a pillar of salt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barry_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barry-25">[25]</a></sup> Spielberg explained, "I wanted to make <i>Close Encounters</i> a very accessible story about the everyday individual who has a sighting that overturns his life, and throws it into complete upheaval as he starts to become more and more obsessed with this experience."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pro_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-pro-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Roy's wife Ronnie attempts to hide the sunburn caused by Roy's exposure to the UFOs and wants him to forget his encounter with them. She is embarrassed and bewildered by what has happened to him and desperately wants her ordinary life back. The expression of his lost life is seen when he is sculpting a huge model of Devils Tower in his living room, with his family deserting him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barry_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-Barry-25">[25]</a></sup> Roy's obsession with an idea implanted by an alien intelligence, his construction of the model, and his gradual loss of contact with his wife, mimic the events in the short story "<a href="/wiki/Dulcie_and_Decorum" title="Dulcie and Decorum">Dulcie and Decorum</a>" (1955) by <a href="/wiki/Damon_Knight" title="Damon Knight">Damon Knight</a>.</p>
<p><i>Close Encounters</i> also studies the form of "youth spiritual yearning". Barry Guiler, the unfearing child who refers to the UFOs and their paraphernalia as "toys" (although that was unscripted, with the child being drawn to smile by being shown toys offstage), serves as a motif for childlike innocence and openness in the face of the unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barry_25-4"><a href="#cite_note-Barry-25">[25]</a></sup> Spielberg also compared the theme of communication as highlighting that of tolerance. "If we can talk to aliens in <i>Close Encounters of the Third Kind</i>," he said, "why not with the <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">Reds</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Sleeping is the final obstacle to overcome in the ascent of Devils Tower. Roy, Jillian Guiler and a third invitee climb the mountain pursued by government helicopters spraying sleeping gas. The third person stops to rest, is gassed, and falls into a deep sleep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barry_25-5"><a href="#cite_note-Barry-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his interview with Spielberg on <i><a href="/wiki/Inside_the_Actors_Studio" title="Inside the Actors Studio">Inside the Actors Studio</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/James_Lipton" title="James Lipton">James Lipton</a> suggested <i>Close Encounters</i> had another, more personal theme for Spielberg: "Your father was a computer engineer; your mother was a concert pianist, and when the spaceship lands, they make music together on the computer", suggesting that Roy Neary's boarding the spaceship is Spielberg's wish to be reunited with his parents. In a 2005 interview, Spielberg stated that he made <i>Close Encounters</i> when he did not have children, and if he were making it today, he would never have had Neary leave his family and go on the mother ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Close_Encounters_of_the_Third_Kind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Close_Encounters_of_the_Third_Kind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film was originally to be released in summer 1977, but was pushed back to November because of the various problems during production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-actor_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-actor-9">[9]</a></sup> Upon its release, <i>Close Encounters</i> had a huge impact on society and became a certified <a href="/wiki/Box_office" title="Box office">box office</a> success, grossing $116.39 million in North America and $171.7 million in foreign countries, totaling $288 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-box_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-box-28">[28]</a></sup> It became <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a>' most successful film at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pro_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-pro-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Rosenbaum" title="Jonathan Rosenbaum">Jonathan Rosenbaum</a> refers to the film as "the best expression of Spielberg's benign, dreamy-eyed vision."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> A.D. Murphy of <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> gave a positive review but felt "<i>Close Encounters</i> lacks the warmth and humanity of <a href="/wiki/George_Lucas" title="George Lucas">George Lucas</a>'s <i>Star Wars</i>". Murphy found most of the film slow-paced, but praised the film's climax.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pauline_Kael" title="Pauline Kael">Pauline Kael</a> called it "a kid's film in the best sense."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fire-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jean_Renoir" title="Jean Renoir">Jean Renoir</a> compared Spielberg's storytelling to <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georges_M%C3%A9li%C3%A8s" title="Georges Méliès">Georges Méliès</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amy_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Amy-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ray_Bradbury" title="Ray Bradbury">Ray Bradbury</a> declared it the greatest science fiction film ever made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Based on 46 reviews collected by <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, 96% ("Certified Fresh") of the reviewers have enjoyed the film and the site's consensus states "Close Encounters' most iconic bits (the theme, the mashed-potato sculpture, etc.) have been so thoroughly absorbed into the culture that it's easy to forget that its treatment of aliens as peaceful beings rather than warmongering monsters was somewhat groundbreaking in 1977."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reissue_and_home_video">Reissue and home video</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Close_Encounters_of_the_Third_Kind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Reissue and home video">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the <a href="/wiki/Final_cut_privilege" title="Final cut privilege">final cut privilege</a>, Spielberg was dissatisfied with the film. Columbia Pictures was experiencing financial problems, and they were depending on this film to save their company. "I wanted to have another six months to finish off this film, and release it in summer 1978. They told me they needed this film out immediately," Spielberg explained. "Anyway, <i>Close Encounters</i> was a huge financial success and I told them I wanted to make my own <a href="/wiki/Director%27s_cut" title="Director's cut">director's cut</a>. They agreed on the condition that I show the inside of the <a href="/wiki/Mother_ship" title="Mother ship">mothership</a> so they could have something to hang a [reissue marketing] campaign on. I never should have shown the inside of the mother ship."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-making_7-15"><a href="#cite_note-making-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1979, Columbia gave Spielberg $1.5 million to produce what became the "Special Edition" of the film. Spielberg added seven minutes of new footage, but also <a href="/wiki/Deleted_scene" title="Deleted scene">deleted or shortened various existing scenes</a> by a total of ten minutes, so that the Special Edition was three minutes shorter than the original 1977 release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amy_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Amy-10">[10]</a></sup> The Special Edition featured several new character development scenes, the discovery of the <a href="/wiki/SS_Cotopaxi" title="SS Cotopaxi">SS <i>Cotopaxi</i></a> in the <a href="/wiki/Gobi_Desert" title="Gobi Desert">Gobi Desert</a>, and a view of the inside of the mothership. <i>Close Encounters of the Third Kind: The Special Edition</i> was released in August 1980, making a further $15.7 million, accumulating a final $303.7 million box office gross.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amy_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Amy-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-box_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-box-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> "thought the original film was an astonishing achievement, capturing the feeling of awe and wonder we have when considering the likelihood of life beyond the Earth. ... This new version ... is, quite simply, a better film ... Why didn't Spielberg make it this good the first time?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 1980 Special Edition was the version officially available on video for years, until <a href="/wiki/The_Criterion_Collection" title="The Criterion Collection">The Criterion Collection</a> offered both versions on LaserDisc in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> This triple-disc LaserDisc set also included an interactive "Making Close Encounters" documentary featuring interviews with Spielberg and other cast and crew involved with the film, as well as stills and script excerpts. In 1993, the Special Edition was released on VHS and LaserDisc for the last time for 14 years.</p>
<p>In 1998, Spielberg recut <i>Close Encounters</i> again for what would become the "Collector's Edition," to be released on home video and LaserDisc. This version of the film is a re-edit of the original 1977 release with some elements of the 1980 Special Edition, but omits the mothership interior scenes which Spielberg felt should have remained a mystery. The laserdisc edition also includes a new 101-minute documentary, <i>The Making of Close Encounters</i>, which was produced in 1997 and features interviews with Spielberg, the main cast and notable crew members. There have also been many other alternate versions of the film for network and syndicated television, as well as a previous LaserDisc version. Some of these even combined all released material from the 1977 and 1980 versions, but none of these versions were edited by Spielberg, who regards the "Collector's Edition" as his definitive version of <i>Close Encounters</i>. The Collector's Edition was given a limited release as part of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roadshow" title="Roadshow">roadshow</a> featuring select films to celebrate Columbia Pictures' 75th anniversary in 1999. It was the first and only time this version of the film has been shown theatrically.</p>
<p><i>Close Encounters</i> was released on DVD in June 2001 as a two-disc set that contained the "Collector's Edition".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The second disc contained a wealth of extra features including the 101-minute "Making of" documentary from 1997, a featurette from 1977, trailers and deleted scenes that included, among other things, the mothership interiors from the 1980 Special Edition.</p>
<p><i>Close Encounters</i> was given a second DVD release and its first release on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray Disc</a> in November 2007 for the film's 30th anniversary. These sets contain all three official versions of the film from 1977, 1980 and 1998, and a new 2007 interview with Spielberg, who talks about the film's impact 30 years after its release. The set also includes the 1977 featurette, various trailers and the 1997 "Making of" documentary – though this is now split over three discs on the DVD version rather than as a single feature as with the 2001 DVD release (on the Blu-ray release, it remains all on one disc). In addition to these features, the 2-disc Blu-ray set also included storyboard-to-scene comparisons, an extensive photo gallery, and a "View from Above: Editor's Fact Track" highlighting the different scenes in each version of the movie. A single disc version has also been released on Blu-ray, which is basically the first disc of the 30th-anniversary edition, which contains the three versions of the film with the "View from Above" feature.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Close_Encounters_of_the_Third_Kind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Night_Skies" title="Night Skies">Night Skies</a></div>
<p>Shortly after the film's release in late 1977, Spielberg desired to do either a <a href="/wiki/Sequel" title="Sequel">sequel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Prequel" title="Prequel">prequel</a>, before deciding against it. He explained, "The army's knowledge and ensuing <a href="/wiki/Cover-up" title="Cover-up">cover-up</a> is so subterranean that it would take a creative screen story, perhaps someone else making the picture and giving it the equal time it deserves."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mind_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-mind-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film was nominated for eight Oscars at the <a href="/wiki/50th_Academy_Awards" title="50th Academy Awards">50th Academy Awards</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">Best Director</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress">Supporting Actress</a> (Melinda Dillon), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Visual_Effects" title="Academy Award for Visual Effects">Visual Effects</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Art_Direction" title="Academy Award for Best Art Direction">Art Direction</a> (<a href="/wiki/Joe_Alves" title="Joe Alves">Joe Alves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_A._Lomino" title="Daniel A. Lomino">Daniel A. Lomino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phil_Abramson" title="Phil Abramson">Phil Abramson</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Original_Music_Score" title="Academy Award for Original Music Score">Original Music Score</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Film_Editing" title="Academy Award for Film Editing">Film Editing</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Sound" title="Academy Award for Best Sound">Sound</a> (<a href="/wiki/Robert_Knudson" title="Robert Knudson">Robert Knudson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Glass_(sound_engineer)" title="Robert Glass (sound engineer)">Robert Glass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Don_MacDougall" title="Don MacDougall">Don MacDougall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gene_Cantamessa" title="Gene Cantamessa">Gene Cantamessa</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oscars1978_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oscars1978-36">[36]</a></sup> The film's only win was for <a href="/wiki/Vilmos_Zsigmond" title="Vilmos Zsigmond">Vilmos Zsigmond</a>'s cinematography, although the Academy honored the film's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_effects" title="Sound effects">sound effects</a> editing with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Special_Achievement_Award" title="Academy Special Achievement Award">Special Achievement Award</a> (<a href="/wiki/Frank_Warner_(sound_editor)" title="Frank Warner (sound editor)">Frank Warner</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/32nd_British_Academy_Film_Awards" title="32nd British Academy Film Awards">32nd British Academy Film Awards</a>, <i>Close Encounters</i> won <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Production_Design" title="BAFTA Award for Best Production Design">Best Production Design</a>, and was nominated for <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Film" title="BAFTA Award for Best Film">Best Film</a>, <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Direction" title="BAFTA Award for Best Direction">Direction</a>, <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Screenplay" title="BAFTA Award for Best Screenplay">Screenplay</a>, <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Actor_in_a_Supporting_Role" title="BAFTA Award for Best Actor in a Supporting Role">Actor in a Supporting Role</a> (François Truffaut), <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Film_Music" title="BAFTA Award for Best Film Music">Music</a>, <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Cinematography" title="BAFTA Award for Best Cinematography">Cinematography</a>, <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Editing" title="BAFTA Award for Best Editing">Editing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Sound" title="BAFTA Award for Best Sound">Sound</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Close Encounters</i> lost the <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award_for_Best_Dramatic_Presentation" title="Hugo Award for Best Dramatic Presentation">Hugo Award for Best Dramatic Presentation</a> to <i>Star Wars</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> but was successful at the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_Award" title="Saturn Award">Saturn Awards</a>. There, the film tied with <i>Star Wars</i> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturn_Award_for_Best_Direction" title="Saturn Award for Best Direction">Direction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saturn_Award_for_Best_Music" title="Saturn Award for Best Music">Music</a>, but won <a href="/wiki/Saturn_Award_for_Best_Writing" title="Saturn Award for Best Writing">Best Writing</a>. Richard Dreyfuss, Melinda Dillon and the visual effects department received nominations. <i>Close Encounters</i> was nominated for <a href="/wiki/Saturn_Award_for_Best_Science_Fiction_Film" title="Saturn Award for Best Science Fiction Film">Best Science Fiction Film</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The film received four more nominations at the <a href="/wiki/35th_Golden_Globe_Awards" title="35th Golden Globe Awards">35th Golden Globe Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>When asked in 1990 to select a single "master image" that summed up his film career, Spielberg chose the shot of Barry opening his living room door to see the blazing orange light from the UFO. "That was beautiful but awful light, just like fire coming through the doorway. [Barry's] very small, and it's a very large door, and there's a lot of promise or danger outside that door."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fire-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2007, <i>Close Encounters</i> was deemed "culturally, historically, or aesthetically significant" by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Library_of_Congress" title="United States Library of Congress">United States Library of Congress</a>, and was added to the <a href="/wiki/National_Film_Registry" title="National Film Registry">National Film Registry</a> for preservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">American Film Institute</a> polls, <i>Close Encounters</i> has been voted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years..._100_Movies" title="AFI's 100 Years... 100 Movies">64th-greatest American film</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years..._100_Thrills" title="AFI's 100 Years... 100 Thrills">31st-most heart-pounding</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years..._100_Cheers" title="AFI's 100 Years... 100 Cheers">58th-most inspiring</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> It was also nominated for the top 10 science fiction films in <a href="/wiki/AFI%27s_10_Top_10" title="AFI's 10 Top 10">AFI's 10 Top 10</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years...100_Movies_(10th_Anniversary_Edition)" title="AFI's 100 Years...100 Movies (10th Anniversary Edition)">tenth-anniversary edition of the 100 Movies list</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The score by John Williams was nominated for <a href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years_of_Film_Scores" title="AFI's 100 Years of Film Scores">AFI's 100 Years of Film Scores</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alongside <i>Star Wars</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Superman_(1978_film)" title="Superman (1978 film)">Superman</a></i>, <i>Close Encounters</i> led to the reemergence of science fiction films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In 1985 Spielberg donated $100,000 to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_Society" title="Planetary Society">Planetary Society</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megachannel_ExtraTerrestrial_Assay" title="Megachannel ExtraTerrestrial Assay">Megachannel ExtraTerrestrial Assay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreyfuss_8-11"><a href="#cite_note-Dreyfuss-8">[8]</a></sup> In the 1979 James Bond film <i><a href="/wiki/Moonraker_(film)" title="Moonraker (film)">Moonraker</a></i> the five-note sequence is heard when a scientist punches the combination into an electronic door lock. In the <i><a href="/wiki/South_Park" title="South Park">South Park</a></i> episode "<a href="/wiki/Imaginationland_Episode_III" title="Imaginationland Episode III">Imaginationland</a>", a government scientist uses the five-note sequence to try to open a portal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Over_Logging" title="Over Logging">Over Logging</a>", a government scientist uses the five-note sequence to try to get the central Internet <a href="/wiki/Router_(computing)" title="Router (computing)">router</a> working.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The "mashed potato" sculpture was parodied in the film <i><a href="/wiki/UHF_(film)" title="UHF (film)">UHF</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> the film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Bacon_(film)" title="Canadian Bacon (film)">Canadian Bacon</a></i>, an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Spaced" title="Spaced">Spaced</a></i>, an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_X-Files" title="The X-Files">The X-Files</a></i>, an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/That_%2770s_Show" title="That '70s Show">That '70s Show</a></i>, and an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">The Simpsons</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> It was satirized in the 200th issue of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mad_Magazine" title="Mad Magazine">Mad</a></i>, July 1978, by <a href="/wiki/Stan_Hart" title="Stan Hart">Stan Hart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mort_Drucker" title="Mort Drucker">Mort Drucker</a> as <i>Clod Encounters of the Absurd Kind</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1977, <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i> had Dreyfuss as a guest host and spoofed the film as Cone Encounters of the Third Kind featuring the recurring characters the Coneheads.</p>
<p>The 1980 short <i><a href="/wiki/Closet_Cases_of_the_Nerd_Kind" title="Closet Cases of the Nerd Kind">Closet Cases of the Nerd Kind</a></i> spoofs the entire film (in condensed form).</p>
<p>In 2007, Muse have used one of the communications melodies to open their most popular live track, titled "Knights of Cydonia".</p>
<p>In 2011, <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> aired a primetime special, <i><a href="/wiki/Best_in_Film:_The_Greatest_Movies_of_Our_Time" title="Best in Film: The Greatest Movies of Our Time">Best in Film: The Greatest Movies of Our Time</a></i> that counted down the best movies chosen by fans based on results of a poll conducted by ABC and People. <i>Close Encounters of the Third Kind</i> was selected as the #5 Best Sci-Fi Film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 2012 <a href="/wiki/Deadmau5" title="Deadmau5">Deadmau5</a> album <i><a href="/wiki/Album_Title_Goes_Here" title="Album Title Goes Here">Album Title Goes Here</a></i>, track no. 9, titled "Closer," features the five-note sequence as the basis for the song's opening and main melodic motif.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, the TV Show <i><a href="/wiki/Futurama" title="Futurama">Futurama</a></i> spoofed the note sequence and Devil's Tower sequence in episode "Games of Tones".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Homestar_Runner" title="Homestar Runner">Homestar Runner</a></i> cartoon "The House That Gave Sucky Tricks," the King of Town dresses up as the mashed potato sculpture of Devil's Tower.</p>
