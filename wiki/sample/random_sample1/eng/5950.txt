<p><b>Michio Kaku</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="/tʃ/ 'ch' in 'china'">tʃ</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uː</span></span>/</a></span></span>; born January 24, 1947) is an American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theoretical_physicist" title="Theoretical physicist">theoretical physicist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Futurist" title="Futurist">futurist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Popular_science" title="Popular science">popularizer of science</a>. Kaku is a professor of <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physics" title="Theoretical physics">theoretical physics</a> at the <a href="/wiki/City_College_of_New_York" title="City College of New York">City College of New York</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CUNY_Graduate_Center" title="CUNY Graduate Center">CUNY Graduate Center</a>. He has written several books about <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and related topics, has made frequent appearances on radio, television, and film, and writes online <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blogs</a> and articles. He has written three <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Best_Seller_list" title="The New York Times Best Seller list"><i>New York Times</i> best sellers</a>: <i><a href="/wiki/Physics_of_the_Impossible" title="Physics of the Impossible">Physics of the Impossible</a></i> (2008), <i><a href="/wiki/Physics_of_the_Future" title="Physics of the Future">Physics of the Future</a></i> (2011), and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Future_of_the_Mind" title="The Future of the Mind">The Future of the Mind</a></i> (2014). Kaku has hosted several TV specials for the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Channel" title="Discovery Channel">Discovery Channel</a>, the <a href="/wiki/History_(U.S._TV_channel)" title="History (U.S. TV channel)">History Channel</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_Channel" title="Science Channel">Science Channel</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michio_Kaku&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kaku was born in <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Nisei" title="Nisei">Japanese American</a> parents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> His father, born in California and educated in both <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, was fluent in Japanese and English. Both his parents were <a href="/wiki/Internment_of_Japanese_Americans" title="Internment of Japanese Americans">interned</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tule_Lake_War_Relocation_Center" title="Tule Lake War Relocation Center">Tule Lake War Relocation Center</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, where they met and where his older brother was born.</p>
<p>While attending <a href="/wiki/Ellwood_P._Cubberley_High_School" title="Ellwood P. Cubberley High School">Cubberley High School</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palo_Alto" title="Palo Alto">Palo Alto</a>, Kaku assembled a <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerator</a> in his parents' garage for a science fair project. His admitted goal was to generate "a beam of <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a> powerful enough to create <a href="/wiki/Antimatter" title="Antimatter">antimatter</a>." At the National Science Fair in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albuquerque" title="Albuquerque">Albuquerque</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>, he attracted the attention of physicist <a href="/wiki/Edward_Teller" title="Edward Teller">Edward Teller</a>, who took Kaku as a protégé, awarding him the Hertz Engineering Scholarship. Kaku graduated <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Summa_cum_laude" title="Summa cum laude">summa cum laude</a></i> from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in 1968 and was first in his physics class. He attended the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berkeley_Radiation_Laboratory" title="Berkeley Radiation Laboratory">Berkeley Radiation Laboratory</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>, and received a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a> in 1972, and that same year held a lectureship at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>.</p>
<p>Kaku was <a href="/wiki/Draft_lottery_(1969)" title="Draft lottery (1969)">drafted</a> into the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. He completed his <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Basic_Training" title="United States Army Basic Training">basic training</a> at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Benning" title="Fort Benning">Fort Benning</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgia_(US_state)" title="Georgia (US state)">Georgia</a>, and advanced infantry training at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lewis" title="Fort Lewis">Fort Lewis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, the Vietnam War ended before he was deployed as an infantryman.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_career">Academic career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michio_Kaku&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Academic career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As part of the research program in 1975 and 1977 at the department of physics at <a href="/wiki/City_College_of_New_York" title="City College of New York">The City College of The City University of New York</a>, Kaku worked on research on <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He was a Visitor and Member (1973 and 1990) at the <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Advanced_Study" title="Institute for Advanced Study">Institute for Advanced Study</a> in <a href="/wiki/Princeton,_New_Jersey" title="Princeton, New Jersey">Princeton</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a>. He currently holds the Henry Semat Chair and Professorship in theoretical physics at the City College of New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kaku had a role in breaking the SSFL (<a href="/wiki/Santa_Susana_Field_Laboratory#Nuclear_Reactors" title="Santa Susana Field Laboratory">Santa Susana Field Laboratory</a>) story in 1979. The Santa Susana facility run by RocketDyne was responsible for an experimental sodium reactor which had an accident in Simi Valley in the 50s. Kaku was a student involved in breaking the story of the leak of radiation.</p>
<p>Kaku has had more than 70 articles published in physics journals such as <i><a href="/wiki/Physical_Review" title="Physical Review">Physical Review</a></i>, covering topics such as <a href="/wiki/Superstring_theory" title="Superstring theory">superstring theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supergravity" title="Supergravity">supergravity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supersymmetry" title="Supersymmetry">supersymmetry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadronic</a> physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1974, Kaku and Prof. <a href="/wiki/Keiji_Kikkawa" title="Keiji Kikkawa">Keiji Kikkawa</a> of <a href="/wiki/Osaka_University" title="Osaka University">Osaka University</a> co-authored the first papers describing <a href="/wiki/String_field_theory" title="String field theory">string theory in a field form</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kaku is the author of several <a href="/wiki/Textbook" title="Textbook">textbooks</a> on <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">string theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theory</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_science">Popular science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michio_Kaku&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Popular science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kaku is most widely known as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Popularizer_of_science" title="Popularizer of science">popularizer of science</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bare_url_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-bare_url-8">[8]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Physics_outreach" title="Physics outreach">physics outreach</a> specialist. He has written books and appeared on many television programs as well as film. He also hosts a weekly radio program.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio">Radio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michio_Kaku&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Radio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kaku is the host of the weekly one-hour radio program <i>Exploration</i>, produced by the Pacifica Foundation's <a href="/wiki/WBAI" title="WBAI">WBAI</a> in New York. <i>Exploration</i> is syndicated to community and independent radio stations and makes previous broadcasts available on the program's website. Kaku defines the show as dealing with the general topics of <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>, <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">war</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peace" title="Peace">peace</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>.</p>
<p>In April 2006, Kaku began broadcasting <i><a href="/wiki/Science_Fantastic_with_Michio_Kaku" title="Science Fantastic with Michio Kaku">Science Fantastic</a></i> on 90 commercial radio stations in the United States. It is syndicated by <a href="/wiki/Talk_Radio_Network" title="Talk Radio Network">Talk Radio Network</a> and now<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2014)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> reaches 130 radio stations and <a href="/wiki/America%27s_Talk" title="America's Talk">America's Talk</a> on <a href="/wiki/XM_Satellite_Radio" title="XM Satellite Radio">XM</a> and remains the only nationally syndicated science radio program. Featured guests include Nobel laureates and top researchers in the fields of string theory, time travel, black holes, gene therapy, aging, space travel, artificial intelligence and SETI. When Kaku is busy filming for television, <i>Science Fantastic</i> goes on hiatus, sometimes for several months. Kaku is also a frequent guest on many programs, where he is outspoken in all areas and issues he considers of importance, such as the program <i><a href="/wiki/Coast_to_Coast_AM" title="Coast to Coast AM">Coast to Coast AM</a></i> where, on 30 November 2007, he reaffirmed his belief that the existence of extraterrestrial life is a certainty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> During the debut of <a href="/wiki/Art_Bell" title="Art Bell">Art Bell</a>'s new radio show <i>Dark Matter</i> on September 16, 2013, Bell referred to Kaku as "the next <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a>", referring to Kaku's similar ability to explain complex science so anyone can understand it.</p>
<p>Kaku has appeared on many mainstream talk shows, discussing popular fiction such as <i><a href="/wiki/Back_to_the_Future" title="Back to the Future">Back to the Future</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lost_(TV_series)" title="Lost (TV series)">Lost</a>,</i> and the theories behind the <a href="/wiki/Time_travel" title="Time travel">time travel</a> these and other fictional entertainment focus on.</p>

<p>In 1999, Kaku was one of the scientists profiled in the feature-length film <i><a href="/wiki/Me_%26_Isaac_Newton" title="Me &amp; Isaac Newton">Me &amp; Isaac Newton</a></i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Apted" title="Michael Apted">Michael Apted</a>. It played theatrically in the United States, was later broadcast on national TV, and won several film awards.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>In 2005, Kaku appeared in the short documentary film <i>Obsessed &amp; Scientific</i> about the possibility of time travel and the people who dream about it. It screened at the Montreal World Film Festival; a feature film expansion is in development talks. Kaku also appeared in the ABC documentary <i><a href="/wiki/UFOs:_Seeing_Is_Believing" title="UFOs: Seeing Is Believing">UFOs: Seeing Is Believing</a></i>, in which he suggested that while he believes it is extremely unlikely that extraterrestrials have ever actually visited <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, we must keep our minds open to the possible existence of civilizations a million years ahead of us in technology, where entirely new avenues of physics open up. He also discussed the future of interstellar exploration and alien life in the Discovery Channel special <i><a href="/wiki/Alien_Planet" title="Alien Planet">Alien Planet</a></i> as one of the multiple speakers who co-hosted the show, and Einstein's Theory of Relativity on The History Channel.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In February 2006, Kaku appeared as presenter in the BBC-TV four-part documentary <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_(television)" title="Time (television)">Time</a></i> which seeks to explore the mysterious nature of time. Part one of the series concerns personal time, and how we perceive and measure the passing of time. The second in the series deals with cheating time, exploring possibilities of extending the lifespan of organisms. The geological time covered in part three explores the ages of the Earth and the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. Part four covers the topics of cosmological time, the beginning of time and the events that occurred at the instant of the big bang.</p>
<p>On January 28, 2007, Kaku hosted the Discovery Channel series <i><a href="/wiki/2057_(TV_series)" title="2057 (TV series)">2057</a>.</i> This three-hour program discussed how medicine, the city, and energy could change over the next 50 years.</p>
<p>In 2008, Kaku hosted the three-hour BBC-TV documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Visions_of_the_Future" title="Visions of the Future">Visions of the Future</a></i>, on the future of computers, medicine, and quantum physics, and he appeared in several episodes of the History Channel's <i>Universe</i> series.</p>
<p>On December 1, 2009, he began hosting a 12-episode weekly TV series for the Science Channel at 10 pm, called <i><a href="/wiki/Sci_Fi_Science:_Physics_of_the_Impossible" title="Sci Fi Science: Physics of the Impossible">Sci Fi Science: Physics of the Impossible</a></i>, based on his best-selling book. Each 30-minute episode discusses the scientific basis behind imaginative schemes, such as time travel, parallel universes, warp drive, star ships, light sabers, force fields, teleportation, invisibility, death stars, and even superpowers and flying saucers. Each episode includes interviews with the world's top scientists working on prototypes of these technologies, interviews with science fiction fans, clips from science fiction movies, and special effects and computer graphics. Although these inventions are impossible today, the series discusses when these technologies might become feasible in the future.</p>
<p>In 2010, he began to appear in a series on the website <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gametrailers.com" title="Gametrailers.com">Gametrailers.com</a> called <i>Science of Games</i>, discussing the scientific aspects of various popular video games such as <i><a href="/wiki/Mass_Effect_2" title="Mass Effect 2">Mass Effect 2</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_The_Force_Unleashed" title="Star Wars: The Force Unleashed">Star Wars: The Force Unleashed</a></i>.</p>
<p>Kaku is popular in mainstream media because of his knowledge and his accessible approach to presenting complex subjects in science. While his technical writings are confined to theoretical physics, his public speaking and media appearances cover a broad range of topics, from the <a href="/wiki/Kardashev_scale" title="Kardashev scale">Kardashev scale</a> to more esoteric subjects such as <a href="/wiki/Wormhole" title="Wormhole">wormholes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Time_travel" title="Time travel">time travel</a>. In January 2007, Kaku visited <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>. While there, he talked at length to select members of that country's decision makers. In an interview with local media, Dr Kaku elaborated on his vision of mankind's future. Kaku considers <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> and <a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terrorism</a> as serious threats in man's evolution from a Type 0 civilization to Type 1 on the Kardashev scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>He is featured in <a href="/wiki/Symphony_of_Science" title="Symphony of Science">Symphony of Science</a>'s songs, "<a href="/wiki/Symphony_of_Science#The_Quantum_World" title="Symphony of Science">The Quantum World</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Symphony_of_Science#Our_Place_in_the_Cosmos" title="Symphony of Science">Our Place in the Cosmos</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Symphony_of_Science#The_Secret_of_the_Stars" title="Symphony of Science">The Secret of the Stars</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/Symphony_of_Science#Monsters_of_the_Cosmos" title="Symphony of Science">Monsters of the Cosmos</a>"</p>
<p>On October 11, 2010, Michio Kaku appeared in the BBC program "What Happened Before the Big Bang" (along with <a href="/wiki/Laura_Mersini-Houghton" title="Laura Mersini-Houghton">Laura Mersini-Houghton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrei_Linde" title="Andrei Linde">Andrei Linde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Penrose" title="Roger Penrose">Roger Penrose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lee_Smolin" title="Lee Smolin">Lee Smolin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neil_Turok" title="Neil Turok">Neil Turok</a>, and other notable cosmologists and physicists), where he propounded his theory of the universe created out of nothing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over 22–25 January 2011, Kaku was invited to the fifth annual Global Competitiveness Forum (GCF), held in <a href="/wiki/Riyadh" title="Riyadh">Riyadh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, next to renowned specialists including the British journalist <a href="/wiki/Nick_Pope_(journalist)" title="Nick Pope (journalist)">Nick Pope</a>, the Canadian ufologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanton_Friedman" title="Stanton Friedman">Stanton Friedman</a>, and the French astrophysicist <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Vall%C3%A9e" title="Jacques Vallée">Jacques Vallée</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kaku appears on the DVD and Blu-ray extras of the 2012 version of <a href="/wiki/Total_Recall_(2012_film)" title="Total Recall (2012 film)">Total Recall</a>, discussing the technological aspects of the future explored in the film.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policy_advocacy_and_activism">Policy advocacy and activism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michio_Kaku&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Policy advocacy and activism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kaku has publicly stated his concerns over matters including people denying the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropogenic" title="Anthropogenic">anthropogenic</a> cause of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_armament" title="Nuclear armament">nuclear armament</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> and what he believes to be the general misuse of science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He was critical of the <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">Cassini–Huygens</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">space probe</a> because of the 72 pounds (33 kg) of <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a> contained in the craft for use by its <a href="/wiki/Radioisotope_thermoelectric_generator" title="Radioisotope thermoelectric generator">radioisotope thermoelectric generator</a>. Conscious of the possibility of casualties if the probe's fuel were dispersed into the environment during a malfunction and crash as the probe was making a <a href="/wiki/Gravity_assist" title="Gravity assist">'sling-shot' maneuver</a> around <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, Kaku publicly criticized NASA's risk assessment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>His remark from an interview in support of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SETI" title="SETI">SETI</a>, "We could be in the middle of an intergalactic conversation...and we wouldn't even <i>know</i>", is used in the third <a href="/wiki/Symphony_of_Science" title="Symphony of Science">Symphony of Science</a> installment "<a href="/wiki/Symphony_of_Science#Our_Place_in_the_Cosmos" title="Symphony of Science">Our Place in the Cosmos</a>". Michio Kaku is also a member of the <a href="/wiki/CuriosityStream" title="CuriosityStream">CuriosityStream</a> Advisory Board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
