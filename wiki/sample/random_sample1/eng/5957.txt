<p><b>Michael Psellos</b> or <b>Psellus</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: Μιχαήλ Ψελλός, <i>Mikhaēl Psellos</i>) was a <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Greeks" title="Byzantine Greeks">Byzantine Greek</a> monk, writer, philosopher, politician and historian. He was born in 1017 or 1018, and is believed to have died in 1078, although it has also been maintained that he remained alive until 1096.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography_and_political_career">Biography and political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Psellos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography and political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main source of information about Psellos' life comes from his own works, which contain extensive autobiographical passages. Michael Psellos was probably born in <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>. His family hailed from <a href="/wiki/Nicomedia" title="Nicomedia">Nicomedia</a> and, according to his own testimony, counted members of the consular and patrician elite among its ancestors. His baptismal name was <b>Constantine</b>; Michael was the monastic name he chose when he entered a monastery later in life. <b>Psellos</b> ('the stammerer') probably was a personal by-name referring to a speech defect.</p>
<p>Michael Psellos was educated in Constantinople. At around the age of ten, he was sent to work outside the capital as a secretary of a provincial judge, in order to help his family raise the dowry for his sister. When his sister died, he gave up that position and returned to Constantinople to resume his studies. While studying under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Mauropus" title="John Mauropus">John Mauropus</a>, he met the later Patriarchs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_III_of_Constantinople" title="Constantine III of Constantinople">Constantine Leichoudes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_John_VIII_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch John VIII of Constantinople">John Xiphilinos</a>, and the later emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_X" title="Constantine X">Constantine X Doukas</a>. For some time, he worked in the provinces again, now serving as a judge himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Some time before 1042 he returned again to Constantinople, where he got a junior position at court as a secretary (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ὑπογραμματεύς</span>) in the imperial chancellery. From there he began a rapid court career. He became an influential political advisor to emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_IX" title="Constantine IX">Constantine IX Monomachos</a> (reigned 1042–1055). During the same time, he became the leading professor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Constantinople" title="University of Constantinople">University of Constantinople</a>, bearing the honorary title of "Chief of the Philosophers" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ὕπατος τῶν φιλοσόφων</span>).</p>
<p>Towards the end of Monomachos' reign, Psellos found himself under political pressure for some reason and finally decided to leave the court, entering the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Olympus_(monastery)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Olympus (monastery) (page does not exist)">Olympus monastery</a> on <a href="/wiki/Uluda%C4%9F" title="Uludağ">Mount Olympus</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bithynia" title="Bithynia">Bithynia</a> in 1054. After Monomachos' death, however, he was soon recalled to court by his successor, Empress <a href="/wiki/Theodora_(11th_century)" title="Theodora (11th century)">Theodora</a> (reigned 1055–1056). Throughout the following years, he remained active in politics, serving as a high-ranking political advisor to several successive emperors. He played a decisive political role in the transition of power from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michael_VI" title="Michael VI">Michael VI</a> to <a href="/wiki/Isaac_I_Komnenos" title="Isaac I Komnenos">Isaac I Komnenos</a> in 1057; then from Isaac Komnenos to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_X" title="Constantine X">Constantine X</a> Doukas (1059); and then again from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanos_IV" title="Romanos IV">Romanos IV</a> Diogenes to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michael_VII" title="Michael VII">Michael VII Doukas</a> (1071). As Psellos had served as Michael's personal teacher during the reign of Michael's father Constantine, and as he had played an important role in helping Michael gain power against his adversary and stepfather Romanos, Psellos probably entertained hopes of an even more influential position as a teacher and advisor under him. However, Michael seems to have been less inclined towards protecting Psellos and after the mid-1070s there is no more information about any role played by Psellos at court. As his own autobiographic accounts cease at this point, there is little reliable information about his later years. Some scholars believe that Psellos had to retreat into a monastery again at some time during the 1070s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-refjoannou_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-refjoannou-2">[2]</a></sup> Following a remark by Psellos' fellow historian <a href="/wiki/Joannes_Zonaras" title="Joannes Zonaras">Joannes Zonaras</a>, it is believed by most scholars that Psellos died soon after the fall of Michael VII in 1078,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-refhunger_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-refhunger-3">[3]</a></sup> although some scholars have also proposed later dates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-refpolemis_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-refpolemis-4">[4]</a></sup> What is known is that Theophylaktos of Bulgaria wrote a letter to Psellos's brother comforting him on the death of his brother saying that, "Your brother has not died, but has departed to <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> released of both a painful life and disease <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-refTheophylaktos_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-refTheophylaktos-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_works">Historical works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Psellos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rulers_of_Byzantine_Empire_in_XI_century..pdf"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1753" data-file-width="1240" height="311" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Rulers_of_Byzantine_Empire_in_XI_century..pdf/page1-220px-Rulers_of_Byzantine_Empire_in_XI_century..pdf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Rulers_of_Byzantine_Empire_in_XI_century..pdf/page1-330px-Rulers_of_Byzantine_Empire_in_XI_century..pdf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Rulers_of_Byzantine_Empire_in_XI_century..pdf/page1-440px-Rulers_of_Byzantine_Empire_in_XI_century..pdf.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rulers_of_Byzantine_Empire_in_XI_century..pdf" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rulers of the Byzantine Empire in the 11th century. Based on <i>Chronographia</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Probably Psellos' best known and most accessible work is the <i>Chronographia</i>. It is a history of the Byzantine emperors during the century leading up to Psellos' own time. It covers the reigns of fourteen emperors and empresses, beginning with the almost 50-year-long reign of <a href="/wiki/Basil_II" title="Basil II">Basil II, the "Bulgar-Slayer"</a> (976–1025), and ending some time during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Michael_VII_Doukas" title="Michael VII Doukas">Michael VII Doukas</a> (1071–1078). It is structured mainly as a series of biographies. Unlike most other historiographical works of the period, it places much more emphasis on the description of characters than on details of political and military events. It also includes very extensive autobiographical elements about Psellos' political and intellectual development, and it gives far greater weight to those periods when Psellos held an active position in politics (especially the reign of Constantine IX), giving the whole work almost the character of political memoirs. It is believed to have been written in two parts. The first covers the emperors up to Isaac I Komnenos. The second, which has a much more strongly apologetic tone, is in large parts an <a href="/wiki/Encomium" title="Encomium">encomium</a> on Psellus' current protectors, the emperors of the Doukas dynasty.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_works">Other works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Psellos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Psellos left many other writings:</p>
<ol>
<li>"Historia syntomos", a shorter, didactic historical text in the form of a world chronicle.</li>
<li>A large number of scientific, philosophical and religious treatises. One well-known example of these is <i>De Operatione Daemonum</i>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psellus%27_classification_of_demons" title="Psellus' classification of demons">classification of demons</a>. He wrote or compiled an important work on philosophy, the <i>De omnifaria doctrina.</i> Other works deal with topics such as <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence" title="Jurisprudence">jurisprudence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laography" title="Laography">laography</a>.</li>
<li>Various didactic poems on topics such as <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammar</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhetorics" title="Rhetorics">rhetorics</a>.</li>
<li>Three <i>Epitaphioi</i> or funeral orations over the patriarchs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michael_Cerularius" title="Michael Cerularius">Michael Keroularios</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_III_of_Constantinople" title="Constantine III of Constantinople">Constantine III Leichoudes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_John_VIII_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch John VIII of Constantinople">John Xiphilinos</a>.</li>
<li>A funeral oration for his mother, including a large amount of autobiographic information.</li>
<li>Several <a href="/wiki/Panegyric" title="Panegyric">panegyrics</a>, persuasive speeches (including works against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bogomils" title="Bogomils">Bogomils</a> and <a href="/wiki/Euchites" title="Euchites">Euchites</a>) and speeches addressed to his patron emperors at court.</li>
<li>Several hundred personal letters.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">Rhetorical</a> exercises and essays on set themes.</li>
<li>Occasional, satirical, and epigrammatic verse.</li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personality">Personality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Psellos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Psellos was universally educated and had a reputation for being one of the most learned men of his time. He prided himself on having single-handedly reintroduced to Byzantine scholarship a serious study of ancient philosophy, especially of <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>. His predilection for Plato and other pagan (often <a href="/wiki/Neoplatonism" title="Neoplatonism">Neoplatonic</a>) philosophers led to doubts about the orthodoxy of his faith among some of his contemporaries, and at one point he was forced to make a public profession of faith in his defense. He also prided himself on being a master of rhetoric, combining the wisdom of the philosopher and the persuasiveness of the rhetorician. This made him the model of a political leader and advisor. Among modern commentators, Psellos' penchant for long autobiographical digressions in his works has earned him accusations of vanity and ambition. At the same time, his political career and the contents of his <i>Chronographia</i> have led commentators to characterize him as obsequious and opportunistic, because of his ostensibly uncritical stance towards some of the emperors and because of the many shifts in his political loyalty over the course of his life. However, other commentators argue that there is a powerful ironic undercurrent running through his work, especially the <i>Chronographia</i>, transmitting highly critical and subversive messages about the emperors portrayed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-refpietsch_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-refpietsch-6">[6]</a></sup> or even about Byzantine <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> beliefs and morality at large.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-refkaldellis_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-refkaldellis-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>As mentioned above, serious questions were raised during Psellos' lifetime concerning his religious beliefs. For example, according to Byzantinist Anthony Kaldellis, "In 1054 he [Psellos] was accused by his erstwhile friend, the future Patriarch John Xiphilinos, of forsaking Christ to follow Plato." [Kaldellis, 1999, p. 4] Even stronger doubts arose concerning Psellos' student, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Italos" title="John Italos">John Italos</a>, who succeeded Psellos as Chief of the Philosophers (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hypatos_ton_Philosophon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hypatos ton Philosophon (page does not exist)">Hypatos ton Philosophon</a>). Italos was publicly accused of teaching such "Hellenizing" ideas as <a href="/wiki/Metempsychosis" title="Metempsychosis">metempsychosis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Eternity_of_the_world" title="Eternity of the world">eternity of the world</a>. Italos faced such accusations twice, and both times he confessed and recanted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-refclucas_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-refclucas-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudo-Psellos">Pseudo-Psellos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Psellos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pseudo-Psellos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It was once thought that there was another Byzantine writer of the same name, <b>Michael Psellos the Elder</b> (now also called <b>Pseudo-Psellos</b>), who lived on the island of <a href="/wiki/Andros" title="Andros">Andros</a> in the 9th century, and who was a pupil of <a href="/wiki/Photios_I_of_Constantinople" title="Photios I of Constantinople">Photius</a> and teacher of emperor <a href="/wiki/Leo_VI_the_Wise" title="Leo VI the Wise">Leo VI the Wise</a>. Michael Psellos himself was also called "the younger" by some authors. This belief was based on an entry in a medieval chronicle, the <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Σύνοψις Κεδρηνοῦ-Σκυλίτση</span>, which mentions the name in that context. It is now believed that the inclusion of the name Psellos in this chronicle was the mistake of an ignorant copyist at a later time, and that no "Michael Psellos the elder" ever existed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reflemerle_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-reflemerle-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <b>Pseudo-Psellos</b> is also used in modern scholarship to describe the authorship of several later works that are believed to have been falsely ascribed to Psellos in Byzantine times.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References_in_literature">References in literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Psellos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: References in literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the gloss of <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">Samuel Taylor Coleridge</a>'s poem <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rime_of_the_Ancient_Mariner" title="The Rime of the Ancient Mariner">The Rime of the Ancient Mariner</a></i>, there is a reference to "the Platonic Constantinopolitan, Michael Psellus" as an authority on "the invisible inhabitants of this planet, neither departed souls nor angels".</p>
<p>The British poet <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Middleton_(poet)" title="Christopher Middleton (poet)">Christopher Middleton</a> includes a poem about Psellus in his 1986 collection, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Two_Horse_Wagon_Going_By&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Two Horse Wagon Going By (page does not exist)">Two Horse Wagon Going By</a></i>, 'Mezzomephistophelean Scholion'.</p>
<p>Psellos appears also in <a href="/wiki/Tim_Severin" title="Tim Severin">Tim Severin</a>'s novel <i>Viking: King's Man</i>, the final piece of the <i>Viking trilogy</i>.</p>
