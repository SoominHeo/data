<p><b>Michelangelo Antonioni</b>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_orders_of_merit" title="Italian orders of merit">Cavaliere di Gran Croce OMRI</a> (29 September 1912 – 30 July 2007), was an Italian <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">film director</a>, <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">screenwriter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Film_editing" title="Film editing">editor</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short_story_writer" title="Short story writer">short story writer</a>. Best known for his "trilogy on modernity and its discontents"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes-holden_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-holden-1">[1]</a></sup> — <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Avventura" title="L'Avventura">L'Avventura</a></i> (1960), <i><a href="/wiki/La_Notte" title="La Notte">La Notte</a></i> (1961), and <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Eclisse" title="L'Eclisse">L'Eclisse</a></i> (1962) — as well as <i><a href="/wiki/Blowup" title="Blowup">Blowup</a></i> (1966), Antonioni "redefined the concept of narrative cinema" and challenged traditional approaches to storytelling, realism, drama, and the world at large.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmovie-ma_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmovie-ma-2">[2]</a></sup> He produced "enigmatic and intricate mood pieces" and rejected action in favor of contemplation, focusing on image and design over character and story. His films defined a "cinema of possibilities".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmovie-ma_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-allmovie-ma-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Antonioni received numerous awards and nominations throughout his career, including the Cannes Film Festival <a href="/wiki/Jury_Prize_(Cannes_Film_Festival)" title="Jury Prize (Cannes Film Festival)">Jury Prize</a> (1960, 1962), <a href="/wiki/Palme_d%27Or" title="Palme d'Or">Palme d'Or</a> (1966), and 35th Anniversary Prize (1982); the Venice Film Festival Silver Lion (1955), <a href="/wiki/Golden_Lion" title="Golden Lion">Golden Lion</a> (1964), <a href="/wiki/International_Federation_of_Film_Critics" title="International Federation of Film Critics">FIPRESCI Prize</a> (1964, 1995), and Pietro Bianchi Award (1998); the Italian National Syndicate of Film Journalists <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silver_Ribbon" title="Silver Ribbon">Silver Ribbon</a> eight times; and an honorary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> in 1995. He is one of three directors to have won the <a href="/wiki/Palme_d%27Or" title="Palme d'Or">Palme d'Or</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Lion" title="Golden Lion">Golden Lion</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bear" title="Golden Bear">Golden Bear</a>, and the only director to have won these three and the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Leopard" title="Golden Leopard">Golden Leopard</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michelangelo_Antonioni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Antonioni was born into a prosperous family of landowners in <a href="/wiki/Ferrara" title="Ferrara">Ferrara</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emilia_Romagna" title="Emilia Romagna">Emilia Romagna</a>, in northern Italy. He was the son of Elisabetta (née Roncagli) and Ismaele Antonioni.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-film-reference-ma_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-film-reference-ma-3">[3]</a></sup> The director explained to Italian film critic Aldo Tassone:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>My childhood was a happy one. My mother ... was a warm and intelligent woman who had been a laborer in her youth. My father also was a good man. Born into a working-class family, he succeeded in obtaining a comfortable position through evening courses and hard work. My parents gave me free rein to do what I wanted: with my brother, we spent most of our time playing outside with friends. Curiously enough, our friends were invariably proletarian, and poor. The poor still existed at that time, you recognized them by their clothes. But even in the way they wore their clothes, there was a fantasy, a frankness that made me prefer them to boys of bourgeois families. I always had sympathy for young women of working-class families, even later when I attended university: they were more authentic and spontaneous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETassone20026_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETassone20026-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Michelangelo Antonioni</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>While still a child, Antonioni was fond of drawing and music. A precocious violinist, he gave his first concert at the age of nine. Although he abandoned the violin with the discovery of cinema in his teens, drawing would remain a lifelong passion. "I have never drawn, even as a child, either puppets or silhouettes but rather facades of houses and gates. One of my favorite games consisted of organizing towns. Ignorant in architecture, I constructed buildings and streets crammed with little figures. I invented stories for them. These childhood <i>happenings</i> - I was eleven years old - were like little films."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETassone20027_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETassone20027-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Upon graduation from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bologna" title="University of Bologna">University of Bologna</a> with a degree in <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, he started writing for the local Ferrara newspaper <i>Il Corriere Padano</i> in 1935 as a film journalist.</p>
<p>In 1940, Antonioni moved to Rome, where he worked for <i>Cinema</i>, the official <a href="/wiki/Italian_Fascism" title="Italian Fascism">Fascist</a> film magazine edited by <a href="/wiki/Vittorio_Mussolini" title="Vittorio Mussolini">Vittorio Mussolini</a>. However, Antonioni was fired a few months afterward. Later that year he enrolled at the Centro Sperimentale di Cinematografia to study film technique, but left it after three months. He was drafted into the army afterwards. During the war Antonioni survived being condemned to death for his membership in the resistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-film-quarterly-bachman_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-film-quarterly-bachman-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michelangelo_Antonioni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_film_work">Early film work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michelangelo_Antonioni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early film work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1942, Antonioni co-wrote <i><a href="/wiki/A_Pilot_Returns" title="A Pilot Returns">A Pilot Returns</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Rossellini" title="Roberto Rossellini">Roberto Rossellini</a> and worked as assistant director on Enrico Fulchignoni's <i>I due Foscari</i>. In 1943, he travelled to France to assist <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Carn%C3%A9" title="Marcel Carné">Marcel Carné</a> on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Les_visiteurs_du_soir" title="Les visiteurs du soir">Les visiteurs du soir</a></i> and then began a series of <a href="/wiki/Short_film" title="Short film">short films</a> with <i>Gente del Po</i> (1943), a story of poor fishermen of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Po_valley" title="Po valley">Po valley</a>. After the Liberation, the film stock was stored in the East-Italian Fascist "<a href="/wiki/Italian_Social_Republic" title="Italian Social Republic">Republic of Salò</a>" and could not be recovered and edited until 1947 (the complete footage was never retrieved). These films were <a href="/wiki/Italian_neorealism" title="Italian neorealism">neorealist</a> in style, being semi-<a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentary</a> studies of the lives of ordinary people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECook2004535_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECook2004535-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, Antonioni's first full-length feature film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cronaca_di_un_amore" title="Cronaca di un amore">Cronaca di un amore</a></i> (1950) broke away from neorealism by depicting the middle classes. He continued to do so in a series of other films: <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_vinti" title="I vinti">I vinti</a></i> ("The Vanquished", 1952), a trio of stories, each set in a different country (France, Italy and England), about juvenile delinquency; <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_signora_senza_camelie" title="La signora senza camelie">La signora senza camelie</a></i> (<i>The Lady Without Camellias</i>, 1953) about a young film star and her fall from grace; and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_amiche" title="Le amiche">Le amiche</a></i> (<i>The Girlfriends</i>, 1955) about middle class women in Turin. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Il_grido" title="Il grido">Il grido</a></i> (<i>The Outcry</i>, 1957) was a return to working class stories, depicting a factory worker and his daughter. Each of these stories is about <a href="/wiki/Social_alienation" title="Social alienation">social alienation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECook2004535_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECook2004535-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_recognition">International recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michelangelo_Antonioni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: International recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <i>Le Amiche</i> (1955), Antonioni experimented with a radical new style: instead of a conventional narrative, he presented a series of apparently disconnected events, and he used <a href="/wiki/Long_take" title="Long take">long takes</a> as part of his film making style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECook2004535_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECook2004535-7">[7]</a></sup> Antonioni returned to their use in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%27avventura" title="L'avventura">L'avventura</a></i> (1960), which became his first international success. At the <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a> it received a mixture of cheers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian-houston_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-houston-8">[8]</a></sup> and boos,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian-bradshaw_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-bradshaw-9">[9]</a></sup> but the film was popular in art house cinemas around the world. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_notte" title="La notte">La notte</a></i> (1961), starring <a href="/wiki/Jeanne_Moreau" title="Jeanne Moreau">Jeanne Moreau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marcello_Mastroianni" title="Marcello Mastroianni">Marcello Mastroianni</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Eclisse" title="L'Eclisse">L'Eclisse</a></i> (1962), starring <a href="/wiki/Alain_Delon" title="Alain Delon">Alain Delon</a>, followed <i>L'avventura</i>. These three films are commonly referred to as a trilogy because they are stylistically similar and all concerned with the alienation of man in the modern world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGazetas2008246_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGazetas2008246-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWakeman198865_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWakeman198865-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECameron_.26_Wood1971105_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECameron_.26_Wood1971105-12">[12]</a></sup> <i>La notte</i> won the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bear" title="Golden Bear">Golden Bear</a> award at the <a href="/wiki/11th_Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="11th Berlin International Film Festival">11th Berlin International Film Festival</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berlinale-1961_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-berlinale-1961-13">[13]</a></sup> His first color film, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Il_deserto_rosso" title="Il deserto rosso">Il deserto rosso</a></i> (<i>The Red Desert</i>, 1964), deals with similar themes, and is sometimes considered the fourth film of the "trilogy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes-holden_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-holden-1">[1]</a></sup> All of these films star <a href="/wiki/Monica_Vitti" title="Monica Vitti">Monica Vitti</a>, his lover during that period.</p>
<p>Antonioni then signed a deal with producer <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Ponti" title="Carlo Ponti">Carlo Ponti</a> that would allow artistic freedom on three films in English to be released by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MGM" title="MGM">MGM</a>. The first, <i><a href="/wiki/Blowup" title="Blowup">Blowup</a></i> (1966),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kulleraugen-tast_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-kulleraugen-tast-14">[14]</a></sup> set in <a href="/wiki/Swinging_London" title="Swinging London">Swinging London</a>, was a major international success. The script was loosely based on the short story <i>The Devil's Drool</i> (otherwise known as <i>Blow Up</i>) by Argentinian writer <a href="/wiki/Julio_Cort%C3%A1zar" title="Julio Cortázar">Julio Cortázar</a>. Although it dealt with the challenging theme of the impossibility of objective standards and the ever-doubtable truth of memory, it was a successful and popular hit with audiences, no doubt helped by its sex scenes, which were explicit for the time. It starred <a href="/wiki/David_Hemmings" title="David Hemmings">David Hemmings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vanessa_Redgrave" title="Vanessa Redgrave">Vanessa Redgrave</a>. The second film was <i><a href="/wiki/Zabriskie_Point_(film)" title="Zabriskie Point (film)">Zabriskie Point</a></i> (1970), his first set in America and with a <a href="/wiki/Counterculture_of_the_1960s" title="Counterculture of the 1960s">counterculture</a> theme. The soundtrack carried popular artists such as <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a> (who wrote new music specifically for the film), the <a href="/wiki/Grateful_Dead" title="Grateful Dead">Grateful Dead</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolling_Stones" title="Rolling Stones">Rolling Stones</a>. However, its release was a critical and commercial disaster. The third, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Passenger_(1975_film)" title="The Passenger (1975 film)">The Passenger</a></i> (1975), starring <a href="/wiki/Jack_Nicholson" title="Jack Nicholson">Jack Nicholson</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maria_Schneider_(actor)" title="Maria Schneider (actor)">Maria Schneider</a>, received critical praise, but also did poorly at the box office. It was out of circulation for many years, but was re-released for a limited theatrical run in October 2005 and has subsequently been released on <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a>.</p>
<p>In 1972, in between <i>Zabriskie Point</i> and <i>The Passenger</i>, Antonioni was invited by the <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao</a> government of the <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">People's Republic of China</a> to visit the country. He made the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Chung_Kuo,_Cina" title="Chung Kuo, Cina">Chung Kuo, Cina</a></i>, but it was severely denounced by the Chinese authorities as "anti-Chinese" and "anti-communist".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEcho_and_Leefeld19778.E2.80.9312_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEcho_and_Leefeld19778.E2.80.9312-15">[15]</a></sup> The documentary had its first showing in China on 25 November 2004 in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> with a film festival hosted by the <a href="/wiki/Beijing_Film_Academy" title="Beijing Film Academy">Beijing Film Academy</a> to honor the works of Michelangelo Antonioni.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_career">Later career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michelangelo_Antonioni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1980, Antonioni made <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Il_mistero_di_Oberwald" title="Il mistero di Oberwald">Il mistero di Oberwald</a></i> (<i>The Mystery of Oberwald</i>), an experiment in the electronic treatment of color, recorded in video then transferred to film, featuring <a href="/wiki/Monica_Vitti" title="Monica Vitti">Monica Vitti</a> once more. It is based on <a href="/wiki/Jean_Cocteau" title="Jean Cocteau">Jean Cocteau</a>'s play <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Aigle_%C3%A0_deux_t%C3%AAtes" title="L'Aigle à deux têtes">L'Aigle à deux têtes</a></i> (<i>The Eagle With Two Heads</i>). <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Identificazione_di_una_donna" title="Identificazione di una donna">Identificazione di una donna</a></i> (<i>Identification of a Woman</i>, 1982), filmed in Italy, deals one more time with the recursive subjects of his Italian trilogy. In 1985, Antonioni suffered a stroke, which left him partly paralyzed and unable to speak. However, he continued to make films, including <i><a href="/wiki/Beyond_the_Clouds_(1995_film)" title="Beyond the Clouds (1995 film)">Beyond the Clouds</a></i> (1995), for which <a href="/wiki/Wim_Wenders" title="Wim Wenders">Wim Wenders</a> filmed some scenes. As Wenders has explained, Antonioni rejected almost all the material filmed by Wenders during the editing, except for a few short interludes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWenders200079_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWenders200079-16">[16]</a></sup> They shared the FIPRESCI Prize at the <a href="/wiki/Venice_Film_Festival" title="Venice Film Festival">Venice Film Festival</a> with <i><a href="/wiki/Cyclo_(film)" title="Cyclo (film)">Cyclo</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1994 he was given the Honorary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> "in recognition of his place as one of the cinema's master visual stylists." It was presented to him by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Nicholson" title="Jack Nicholson">Jack Nicholson</a>. Months later, the statuette was stolen by burglars and had to be replaced. Previously, he had been nominated for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Awards</a> for Best Director and Best Screenplay for <i><a href="/wiki/Blowup" title="Blowup">Blowup</a></i>. Antonioni's final film, made when he was in his 90s, was a segment of the <a href="/wiki/Anthology_film" title="Anthology film">anthology film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Eros_(film)" title="Eros (film)">Eros</a></i> (2004), entitled "Il filo pericoloso delle cose" ("The Dangerous Thread of Things"). The short film's episodes are framed by dreamy paintings and the song "Michelangelo Antonioni", composed and sung by <a href="/wiki/Caetano_Veloso" title="Caetano Veloso">Caetano Veloso</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bright-lights-johnston_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-bright-lights-johnston-17">[17]</a></sup> However, it was not well-received internationally; in America, for example, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> claimed that it was neither erotic nor about eroticism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chicago-st-ebert_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-chicago-st-ebert-18">[18]</a></sup> The U.S. DVD release of the film includes another 2004 short film by Antonioni, <i>Lo sguardo di Michelangelo</i> (<i>The Gaze of Michelangelo</i>).</p>
<p>Antonioni died aged 94 on 30 July 2007 in Rome, the same day that another renowned film director, <a href="/wiki/Ingmar_Bergman" title="Ingmar Bergman">Ingmar Bergman</a>, also died. Antonioni lay in state at City Hall in Rome where a large screen showed black-and-white footage of him among his film sets and behind-the-scenes. He was buried in his home town of Ferrara on 2 August 2007.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michelangelo_Antonioni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Film historian Virginia Wright Wexman describes Antonioni's perspective on the world as that of a "<a href="/wiki/Irreligion" title="Irreligion">postreligious</a> <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Existentialist" title="Existentialist">existentialist</a> intellectual."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWexman2006312_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWexman2006312-19">[19]</a></sup> In a speech at Cannes about <i>L'Avventura</i>, Antonioni said that in the modern age of reason and science, mankind still lives by "a rigid and stereotyped morality which all of us recognize as such and yet sustain out of cowardice and sheer laziness". He said his films explore the paradox that "we have examined those moral attitudes very carefully, we have dissected them and analyzed them to the point of exhaustion. We have been capable of all this, but we have not been capable of finding new ones." Nine years later he expressed a similar attitude in an interview, saying that he loathed the word 'morality': "When man becomes reconciled to nature, when space becomes his true background, these words and concepts will have lost their meaning, and we will no longer have to use them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-euro-screenwriters-antonioni_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-euro-screenwriters-antonioni-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-euro-screenwriters-samuels_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-euro-screenwriters-samuels-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the recurring themes in Antonioni's films is characters who suffer from <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ennui" title="wiktionary:ennui">ennui</a> and whose lives are empty and purposeless aside from the gratification of pleasure or the pursuit of material wealth. Film historian <a href="/wiki/David_Bordwell" title="David Bordwell">David Bordwell</a> writes that in his films, "Vacations, parties and artistic pursuits are vain efforts to conceal the characters' lack of purpose and emotion. Sexuality is reduced to casual seduction, enterprise to the pursuit of wealth at any cost."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBordwell_and_Thompson2002427.E2.80.93428_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBordwell_and_Thompson2002427.E2.80.93428-22">[22]</a></sup> Antonioni's films tend to have spare plots and dialogue, and much of the screen time is spent lingering on certain settings, such as the seven-minute <a href="/wiki/Long_take" title="Long take">continuous take</a> at the end of <i>The Passenger</i> and the beautiful long-take near the beginning that "mixes time", or the scene in <i>L'Eclisse</i> in which Monica Vitti stares curiously at electrical posts accompanied by ambient sounds of wires clanking. Virginia Wright Wexman summarizes his style in the following terms: "The camera is placed at a medium distance more often than close in, frequently moving slowly; the shots are permitted to extend uninterrupted by cutting. Thus each image is more complex, containing more information than it would in a style in which a smaller area is framed ... In Antonioni's work we must regard his images at length; he forces our full attention by continuing the shot long after others would cut away."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWexman2006312_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWexman2006312-19">[19]</a></sup> Antonioni is also noted for exploiting colour as a significant expressive element of his cinematic style, especially in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Il_deserto_rosso" title="Il deserto rosso">Il deserto rosso</a></i>, his first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colour_film" title="Colour film">colour film</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrant200647_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrant200647-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bordwell explains that Antonioni's films were extremely influential on subsequent <a href="/wiki/Art_film" title="Art film">art films</a>: "More than any other director, he encouraged filmmakers to explore elliptical and open-ended narrative".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBordwell_and_Thompson2002427.E2.80.93428_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBordwell_and_Thompson2002427.E2.80.93428-22">[22]</a></sup> Film director <a href="/wiki/Akira_Kurosawa" title="Akira Kurosawa">Akira Kurosawa</a> considered Antonioni one of the most interesting filmmakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick" title="Stanley Kubrick">Stanley Kubrick</a> listed <i>La Notte</i> as one of his ten favorite films in a 1963 Poll.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECiment200334_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECiment200334-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mikl%C3%B3s_Jancs%C3%B3" title="Miklós Jancsó">Miklós Jancsó</a> considers Antonioni as his master.</p>
<p>Antonioni's spare style and purposeless characters, however, have not received universal acclaim. <a href="/wiki/Ingmar_Bergman" title="Ingmar Bergman">Ingmar Bergman</a> stated in 2002 that he considered some of Antonioni's films, including <i><a href="/wiki/Blowup" title="Blowup">Blowup</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_notte" title="La notte">La notte</a></i>, masterpieces for their detached and dreamlike quality, but found the other films boring and noted that he had never understood why Antonioni was held in such esteem. <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">Orson Welles</a> regretted the Italian director's use of the <a href="/wiki/Long_take" title="Long take">long take</a>: "I don't like to dwell on things. It's one of the reasons I'm so bored with Antonioni - the belief that, because a shot is good, it's going to get better if you keep looking at it. He gives you a full shot of somebody walking down a road. And you think, 'Well, he's not going to carry that woman all the way up that road.' But he <i>does</i>. And then she leaves and you go on looking at the road after she's gone."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBogdanovich1992103.E2.80.93104_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBogdanovich1992103.E2.80.93104-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>American actor <a href="/wiki/Peter_Weller" title="Peter Weller">Peter Weller</a>, whom Antonioni directed in <i><a href="/wiki/Beyond_the_Clouds_(1995_film)" title="Beyond the Clouds (1995 film)">Beyond the Clouds</a></i>, explained in a 1996 interview: "There is no director living except maybe <a href="/wiki/Akira_Kurosawa" title="Akira Kurosawa">Kurosawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ingmar_Bergman" title="Ingmar Bergman">Bergman</a>, or Antonioni that I would fall down and do anything for. I met Antonioni three years ago in <a href="/wiki/Taormina" title="Taormina">Taormina</a> at a <a href="/wiki/Taormina_Film_Fest" title="Taormina Film Fest">film festival</a>. I introduced myself and told him that I adored his movies, his contributions to film, because he was the first guy who really started making films about the reality of the vacuity between people, the difficulty in traversing this space between lovers in modern day ... and he never gives you an answer, Antonioni—that's the beautiful thing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cigar-aficionado-durrani_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-cigar-aficionado-durrani-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
