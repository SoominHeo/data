<p>The <b>Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-25</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Микоян и Гуревич МиГ-25</span>) (<a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO reporting name</a>: <b>Foxbat</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Supersonic_aircraft" title="Supersonic aircraft">supersonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Interceptor_aircraft" title="Interceptor aircraft">interceptor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_aircraft" title="Reconnaissance aircraft">reconnaissance aircraft</a> that was among the <a href="/wiki/Flight_airspeed_record" title="Flight airspeed record">fastest military aircraft</a> to enter service. It was designed by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan" title="Mikoyan">Mikoyan-Gurevich</a> bureau primarily using stainless steel; one of the few combat aircraft to do so. It was the last plane personally designed by <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gurevich_(aircraft_designer)" title="Mikhail Gurevich (aircraft designer)">Mikhail Gurevich</a> before his retirement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flieger_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flieger-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first prototype flew in 1964, and the aircraft entered service in 1970. It has an operational top speed of <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach</a> 2.83 (Mach 3.2 is possible but at risk of significant damage to the engines), and features a powerful <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> and four <a href="/wiki/Air-to-air_missile" title="Air-to-air missile">air-to-air missiles</a>. When first seen in reconnaissance photography, the large wing suggested an enormous and highly maneuverable fighter, at a time when U.S. <a href="/wiki/Energy%E2%80%93maneuverability_theory" title="Energy–maneuverability theory">design theories were also evolving towards higher maneuverability</a> due to combat performance in the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. The appearance of the MiG-25 sparked serious concern in the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">West</a> and prompted dramatic increases in performance for the <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F-15_Eagle" title="McDonnell Douglas F-15 Eagle">McDonnell Douglas F-15 Eagle</a> then under development in the late 1960s. The capabilities of the MiG-25 were better understood in 1976 when Soviet pilot <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Belenko" title="Viktor Belenko">Viktor Belenko</a> defected in a MiG-25 to the United States via <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. It turned out that the aircraft's weight necessitated its large wings.</p>
<p>Production of the MiG-25 series ended in 1984 after completion of 1,190 aircraft. A symbol of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, the MiG-25 flew with Soviet allies and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Former_Soviet_republics" title="Former Soviet republics">former Soviet republics</a>, remaining in limited service in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_of_Russia" title="Military of Russia">Russia</a> and several other nations. It is one of the highest-flying military aircraft,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and the second fastest after the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_SR-71_Blackbird" title="Lockheed SR-71 Blackbird">SR-71</a> reconnaissance aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global_Aircraft_--_Top_50_Fastest_Aircraft_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Global_Aircraft_--_Top_50_Fastest_Aircraft-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_development">Design and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the Cold War, <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Air_Defence_Forces" title="Soviet Air Defence Forces">Soviet Air Defence Forces</a>, PVO (not to be confused with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_Air_Force" title="Soviet Air Force">Soviet Air Force</a>, VVS) was given the task of strategic air defence of the USSR. In the decades after World War II, this meant not only dealing with accidental border violations, but more importantly defending the vast airspace of the USSR against US <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_aircraft" title="Reconnaissance aircraft">reconnaissance aircraft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bomber" title="Strategic bomber">strategic bombers</a> carrying free-fall nuclear bombs. The performance of these types of aircraft was steadily improved. Overflights by the very high altitude American <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_U-2" title="Lockheed U-2">Lockheed U-2</a> in the late 1950s revealed a need for higher altitude interceptor aircraft than currently available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The subsonic <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-47_Stratojet" title="Boeing B-47 Stratojet">Boeing B-47 Stratojet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-52_Stratofortress" title="Boeing B-52 Stratofortress">Boeing B-52 Stratofortress</a> strategic bombers were followed by the Mach 2 <a href="/wiki/Convair_B-58_Hustler" title="Convair B-58 Hustler">Convair B-58 Hustler</a>, with the Mach 3 <a href="/wiki/North_American_XB-70_Valkyrie" title="North American XB-70 Valkyrie">North American B-70 Valkyrie</a> on the drawing board at that time. A major upgrade in the PVO defence system was required,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and at the start of 1958 a requirement was issued for manned interceptors capable of reaching 3,000 km/h (1,864 mph) and heights of up to 27 km (88,583 ft). Mikoyan and Sukhoi responded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Mikoyan-Gurevich <a href="/wiki/OKB" title="OKB">OKB</a> had been working on a series of interceptors during the second half of the 1950s: the I-1, I-3U, I-7U, I-75, Ye-150, Ye-150A, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_Ye-152" title="Mikoyan-Gurevich Ye-152">Ye-152</a>, Ye-152A, Ye-152P, and Ye-152M. The Ye-150 was noteworthy because it was built specifically to test the <a href="/wiki/Tumansky_R-15" title="Tumansky R-15">Tumansky R-15</a> engine, two of which would later be used for the MiG-25. This led to Ye-152, alternatively known as Ye-166, which set several world records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The Ye-152M (converted from one of the two Ye-152 aircraft) was intended to be the definite heavy interceptor design. But before it was finished, the PVO had selected the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-128" title="Tupolev Tu-128">Tupolev Tu-128</a>. As the work on the MiG-25 was well under way, the single-engine Ye-152M was abandoned.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Designing_a_new_interceptor">Designing a new interceptor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Designing a new interceptor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Work on the new Soviet interceptor that became the MiG-25 started in mid-1959,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup> a year before Soviet intelligence learned of the American Mach 3 <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_A-12" title="Lockheed A-12">A-12</a> reconnaissance aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Great_Book_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Great_Book-10">[10]</a></sup> It is not clear if the design was influenced by the American <a href="/wiki/North_American_A-5_Vigilante" title="North American A-5 Vigilante">A-5 Vigilante</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The design bureau studied several possible layouts for the new aircraft. One had the engines located side-by-side, as on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MiG-19" title="MiG-19">MiG-19</a>. The second had a stepped arrangement with one engine amidships, with exhaust under the fuselage, and another in the aft fuselage. The third project had an engine arrangement similar to that of the <a href="/wiki/English_Electric_Lightning" title="English Electric Lightning">English Electric Lightning</a>, with two engines stacked vertically. Option two and three were both rejected because the size of the engines meant any of them would result in a very tall aircraft which would complicate maintenance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The idea of placing the engines in underwing nacelles was also rejected because of the dangers of any thrust asymmetry during flight. Having decided on engine configuration, there was thought of giving the machine <a href="/wiki/Variable-sweep_wing" title="Variable-sweep wing">variable-sweep wings</a> and a second crew member, a navigator. Variable geometry would improve maneuverability at subsonic speed, but at the cost of decreased fuel tank capacity. Because the reconnaissance aircraft would operate at high speed and high altitude the idea was soon dropped. Another interesting but impractical idea was to improve the field performance using two RD36-35 lift-jets. Vertical takeoff and landing would allow for use of damaged runways during wartime and was studied on both sides of the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Curtain" title="Iron Curtain">Iron Curtain</a>. The perennial problem with engines dedicated to vertical lift is they become mere deadweight in horizontal flight, and also occupy space in the airframe needed for fuel. The MiG interceptor would need all the fuel it could get, so the idea was abandoned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p108_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p108-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ye-155.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="170" data-file-width="746" height="50" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Ye-155.png/220px-Ye-155.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Ye-155.png/330px-Ye-155.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Ye-155.png/440px-Ye-155.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ye-155.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ye-155R3 Reconnaissance prototype with a 5,280 litre drop tank under the belly, 1964</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first prototype was a reconnaissance variant, designated "Ye-155-R1", that made its first flight on 6 March 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bel_p398_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bel_p398-12">[12]</a></sup> It had some characteristics that were unique to that prototype, and some of these were visually very evident: the wings had fixed wingtip tanks (600 litre capacity) to which small winglets were attached for stability purposes, but when it was found that fuel sloshing around in the tanks caused vibrations they were eliminated. The aircraft also had attachments for movable foreplanes, <a href="/wiki/Canard_(aeronautics)" title="Canard (aeronautics)">canards</a>, to help with pitch control at high speed (provisions for canards had previously been installed, but not used, on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_Ye-152" title="Mikoyan-Gurevich Ye-152">Ye-152P</a>.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p108_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p108-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bel_p272-4_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bel_p272-4-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first flight of the interceptor prototype, "Ye-155-P1", took place on 9 September 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bel_p391_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bel_p391-14">[14]</a></sup> Development of the MiG-25, which represented a major step forward in Soviet <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineering" title="Aerospace engineering">engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgy</a>, took several more years to complete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bel_p391_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bel_p391-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bel_387-8_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bel_387-8-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 9 July 1967, the new aircraft was first shown to the public at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_air_shows" title="Soviet air shows">Domodedovo air show</a>, with four prototypes (three fighters and a reconnaissance aircraft) making a flypast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p109_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p109-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Record_breaker">Record breaker</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Record breaker">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Mikoyan-Gurevich design bureau soon realized that the performance of the new aircraft gave it great potential to set new flight records. In addition to their normal duties, the prototypes Ye-155-P1, Ye-155-R1, Ye-155-R3 were made lighter by removing some unneeded equipment, and were used for these attempts. Under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federation_Aeronautique_Internationale" title="Federation Aeronautique Internationale">Federation Aeronautique Internationale</a> (FAI) classification the Ye-155 type belonged to class C1 (III), which specifies jet-powered land planes with unlimited maximum take-off weight. Records set included:</p>
<ul>
<li>The first claim was for world speed records with no payload and payloads of 1,000 and 2,000 kilograms. MiG OKB Chief Test Pilot Alexander V. Fedotov reached an average speed of 2,319.12 km/h over a 1,000 km circuit on 16 March 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bel_p391_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bel_p391-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>For pure speed, with no payload, test pilot Mikhail M. Komarov averaged 2,981.5 km/h over a 500 km closed circuit on 5 October 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bel_p391_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bel_p391-14">[14]</a></sup> On the same day, Fedotov reached an altitude of 29,977 metres (98,350 ft) with a 1,000 kilogram payload.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bel_p392_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bel_p392-17">[17]</a></sup> The MiG eventually became the first aircraft to go higher than 35,000 metres (115,000 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bel_p392_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bel_p392-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>Time to height records were recorded on 4 June 1973 when Boris A. Orlov climbed to 20,000 m in 2 min 49.8 sec. The same day, Pyotr M. Ostapenko reached 25,000 m in 3 min 12.6 sec and 30,000 m in 4 min 3.86 sec.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bel_p392_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bel_p392-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>On 25 July 1973, Fedotov reached 35,230m with 1,000 kg payload, and 36,240 m with no load (an absolute world record).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bel_p392_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bel_p392-17">[17]</a></sup> In the thin air, the engines flamed out and the aircraft coasted in a ballistic trajectory by inertia alone. At the apex the speed had dropped to 75 km/h.</li>
<li>On 31 August 1977, "Ye-266M" again flown by Fedotov, set the recognized absolute altitude record for a jet aircraft under its own power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bel_p406-7_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bel_p406-7-18">[18]</a></sup> He reached 37,650 metres (123,520 ft) at Podmoskovnoye, USSR in <a href="/wiki/Zoom_climb" title="Zoom climb">zoom climb</a> (the absolute altitude record is different from the record for sustained altitude in horizontal flight). The aircraft was actually a MiG-25RB re-engined with the powerful R15BF2-300. It had earlier been part of the program to improve the aircraft's top speed that resulted in the MiG-25M prototype.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>In all 29 records were claimed, of which seven were all-time world records for time to height, altitudes of 20,000 m and higher, and speed. Several records still stand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_description">Technical description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Technical description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Because of the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">thermal stresses</a> incurred in flight above Mach 2, the Mikoyan-Gurevich OKB had difficulties choosing what materials to use for the aircraft. They had to use E-2 heat-resistant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plexiglas" title="Plexiglas">Plexiglas</a> for the canopy, and high-strength stainless steel for the wings and fuselage. Using titanium rather than steel would have been ideal, but it was expensive and difficult to work with. The problem of cracks in welded titanium structures with thin walls could not be solved, so the heavier <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> steel was used instead. It cost far less than titanium and allowed for welding, along with heat-resistant seals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup> The MiG-25 was constructed from 80% nickel-steel alloy, 11% <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, and 9% <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eden_p308_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eden_p308-19">[19]</a></sup> The steel components were formed by a combination of <a href="/wiki/Spot_welding" title="Spot welding">spot welding</a>, automatic <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Machine_welding&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Machine welding (page does not exist)">machine welding</a>, and hand <a href="/wiki/Arc_welding" title="Arc welding">arc welding</a> methods.</p>
<p>Initially, the interceptor version was equipped with the <span id="Smerch"></span>TL-25 <i>Smerch-A</i> (also referred to as <i>Product 720</i>) <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>, a development of the system carried by the earlier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tu-128" title="Tu-128">Tu-128</a>. While powerful, and thus long-ranged and resistant to jamming, the system – due to the age of its design and its intended purpose (tracking and targeting high- and fast-flying US bombers and reconnaissance aircraft), lacked <a href="/wiki/Look-down/shoot-down" title="Look-down/shoot-down">look-down/shoot-down</a> capability, which limited its effectiveness against low-flying targets (this is one of the reasons why it was replaced with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-31" title="Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-31">Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-31</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Zaslon" title="Zaslon">Zaslon</a> radar has that capability). By the time the MiG-25 entered service in 1969, this was a serious shortcoming, as strategic bombing doctrine was shifting towards low-level penetration of enemy territory. After Belenko's defection to Japan exposed this flaw to the West, a government decree issued on November 4, 1976 called for urgent development of a more advanced radar. This resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Pulse-Doppler_radar" title="Pulse-Doppler radar">pulse-Doppler radar</a> <i>Sapphire-25</i> system fitted to the MiG-25PD variant.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automatic_control_system" title="Automatic control system">Automatic control system</a> SAU 155П1 (interceptor) or SAU 155Р1 (scout): <a href="/wiki/Navigation_system" title="Navigation system">navigation system</a> «Peleng-D (ETC, DM)»; <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_radar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Heat radar (page does not exist)">heat radar</a> 26Ш-1; <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_guidance_system&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Radio guidance system (page does not exist)">radio guidance system</a> «Lazur» (for interceptor); <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direction_finder" title="Direction finder">direction finder</a> ARC-10; <a href="/wiki/Altimeter" title="Altimeter">altimeter</a> great heights RV-18 (RV-19); <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Warning_Station_on_the_irradiation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Warning Station on the irradiation (page does not exist)">SPO</a> Sirena-3M» or LO 06 «Birch»; <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Control_system_air_intake&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Control system air intake (page does not exist)">control system air intake</a> СРВМу-2A; system range radionavigation RSBN-6C «coral»; marker radio MCI-56П; intercom SPU-7; the Respondent CO-63B; <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=System_of_air_signals&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="System of air signals (page does not exist)">system of air signals</a> SVS-MO-5; <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Voice_informer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Voice informer (page does not exist)">voice informer</a> RI-65; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flight_data_recorder" title="Flight data recorder">voice recorder</a> MS-61 and others On late production was installed SEC «rainbow».</p>
<p>All interceptors installed «Polet-1I,» consisting of the electronic systems of navigation and landing, курсовертикали, air signals and systems of the automatic flight control. The spies were established: photographic equipment And 70 or A-72 station <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_intelligence" title="Electronic intelligence">Electronic intelligence</a> Cube-3M», «Sabre» <a href="/wiki/Side_looking_airborne_radar" title="Side looking airborne radar">Side looking airborne radar</a>, the station of <a href="/wiki/Signals_intelligence" title="Signals intelligence">signals intelligence</a> «Virazh» or «Pitch».</p>
<p>Armament interceptor includes four supersonic (M&gt;5) UR great range <a href="/wiki/R-40_(missile)" title="R-40 (missile)">R-40T (R-40TD)</a> <a href="/wiki/Air-to-air_missile" title="Air-to-air missile">air-to-air missiles</a> with thermal homing head and R-40P (R-40RD) with semi-active radar guidance system (maximum launch range, on altitudinal goal on a collision course – 35–60 km). Under the fuselage can suspended fuel tank. The aircraft could carry unguided gravity bombs in a rudimentary strike capability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> As the bombs would weigh no more and incur no more drag than its regular load of R-40 missiles, its performance was not impaired, leading to some impressive bombing feats; when released at an altitude of 20,000m (66,000 ft) and a speed above Mach 2, a 500 kg bomb would have a glide range of several tens of kilometres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The MiG-25 was theoretically capable of a maximum speed of Mach 3+ and a ceiling of 90,000 ft (27,000 m). Its high speed was problematic: although sufficient thrust was available to reach Mach 3.2, a limit of Mach 2.83 had to be imposed as the engines tended to overspeed and overheat at higher speeds, possibly damaging them beyond repair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Into_production">Into production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Into production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Full-scale production of the <i>MiG-25R</i> ('Foxbat-B') began in 1969 at the Gorkii aircraft factory (Plant No.21). The <i>MiG-25P</i> ("Foxbat-A") followed in 1971, and 460 of this variant was built until production ended in 1982. The improved PD variant that replaced it was built from 1978 till 1984 with 104 aircraft completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup> But from then on the Gorkii factory switched over production to the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MiG-31" title="MiG-31">MiG-31</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_intelligence_and_the_MiG-25">Western intelligence and the MiG-25</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Western intelligence and the MiG-25">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mig-25.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="1200" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Mig-25.jpg/220px-Mig-25.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Mig-25.jpg/330px-Mig-25.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Mig-25.jpg/440px-Mig-25.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mig-25.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
MiG-25RBSh with markings of 2nd Sqn/47th GvORAP (Guards independent recce Regiment)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Inaccurate intelligence analysis caused the West initially to believe the MiG-25 was an agile air-combat fighter rather than an interceptor. In response, the United States started a new program which resulted in the <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F-15_Eagle" title="McDonnell Douglas F-15 Eagle">McDonnell Douglas F-15 Eagle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> obtained a better understanding of the MiG-25's capabilities on 6 September 1976, when a <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Air_Defence_Forces" title="Soviet Air Defence Forces">Soviet Air Defence Forces</a> pilot, Lt. <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Belenko" title="Viktor Belenko">Viktor Belenko</a>, <a href="/wiki/Defection" title="Defection">defected</a>, landing his MiG-25P at <a href="/wiki/Hakodate_Airport" title="Hakodate Airport">Hakodate Airport</a> in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barron_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barron-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The pilot overshot the runway on landing and damaged the front landing gear. Despite Soviet protests, the Japanese invited U.S. Air Force personnel to investigate the aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barron_p131_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barron_p131-27">[27]</a></sup> On 25 September, it was moved by a C-5A transport to a base in central Japan, where it was carefully dismantled and analyzed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barron_photo_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barron_photo-28">[28]</a></sup> After 67 days, the aircraft was returned by ship to the Soviets, in pieces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barron_p180_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barron_p180-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The aircraft was reassembled and is now on display at the Sokol plant in Nizhny Novgorod.</p>
<p>The analysis, based on technical manuals and ground tests of engines and avionics, revealed unusual technical information:</p>
<ul>
<li>Belenko's particular aircraft was brand new, representing the latest Soviet technology.</li>
<li>The aircraft was assembled quickly, and was essentially built around its massive <a href="/wiki/Tumansky_R-15" title="Tumansky R-15">Tumansky R-15</a>(B) turbojets.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">Welding</a> was done by hand. Rivets with non-flush heads were used in areas that would not cause adverse <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">aerodynamic drag</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barron_pp._169.E2.80.93171_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barron_pp._169.E2.80.93171-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>The aircraft was built of a nickel-steel alloy and not <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> as was assumed (although some titanium was used in heat-critical areas). The steel construction contributed to the craft's high 29,000 kg (64,000 lb) unarmed weight.</li>
<li>Maximum acceleration (<a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force"><i>g</i>-load</a>) rating was just 2.2 <i>g</i> (21.6 m/s²) with full fuel tanks, with an absolute limit of 4.5 <i>g</i> (44.1 m/s²). One MiG-25 withstood an inadvertent 11.5 <i>g</i> (112.8 m/s²) pull during low-altitude dogfight training, but the resulting deformation damaged the airframe beyond repair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>Combat radius was 299 kilometres (186 mi), and maximum range on internal fuel (at subsonic speeds) was only 1,197 kilometres (744 mi) at low altitude (&lt; 1000 meters).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Airspeed_indicator" title="Airspeed indicator">airspeed indicator</a> was redlined at Mach 2.8, with typical intercept speeds near Mach 2.5 in order to extend the service life of the engines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barron_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barron-25">[25]</a></sup> A MiG-25 was tracked flying over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinai" title="Sinai">Sinai</a> at Mach 3.2 in the early 1970s, but the flight led to the engines being damaged beyond repair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barron_pp._169.E2.80.93171_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barron_pp._169.E2.80.93171-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>The majority of the on-board <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">avionics</a> were based on <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum-tube</a> technology, not <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_electronics" title="Solid-state electronics">solid-state electronics</a>. Although they represented aging technology, vacuum tubes were more tolerant of temperature extremes, thereby removing the need for environmental controls in the avionics bays. With the use of vacuum tubes, the MiG-25P's original <i>Smerch-A</i> (<i>Tornado</i>, NATO reporting name "Foxfire") <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> had enormous power – about 600 <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">kilowatts</a>. As with most Soviet aircraft, the MiG-25 was designed to be as robust as possible. The use of vacuum tubes also made the aircraft's systems resistant to an <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_pulse" title="Electromagnetic pulse">electromagnetic pulse</a>, for example after a nuclear blast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_versions">Later versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Later versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the result of Belenko's defection and the compromise of the MiG-25P's radar and missile systems, beginning in 1976, the Soviets started to develop an advanced version, the <i>MiG-25PD</i> ("Foxbat-E").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Plans for a new aircraft to develop the Foxbat's potential to go faster than the in-service limit of Mach 2.8 were designed as a flying prototype. Unofficially designated MiG-25M, it had new powerful engines R15BF2-300, improved radar, and missiles. This work never resulted in a machine for series production, as the coming <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan_MiG-31" title="Mikoyan MiG-31">MiG-31</a> showed more promise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_Union">Soviet Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Soviet Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MiG-25PU_rvb_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="481" data-file-width="588" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/MiG-25PU_rvb_2.jpg/220px-MiG-25PU_rvb_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/MiG-25PU_rvb_2.jpg/330px-MiG-25PU_rvb_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/MiG-25PU_rvb_2.jpg/440px-MiG-25PU_rvb_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MiG-25PU_rvb_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
MiG-25PU</div>
</div>

<p>The unarmed 'B' version had greater impact than the interceptor when the USSR sent two MiG-25R, and two MiG-25RB to Egypt in March 1971 and stayed until July 1972. They were operated by the Soviet 63rd Independent Air Detachment (Det 63) set up specially for this mission. Det 63 flew over Israeli held territory in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinai" title="Sinai">Sinai</a> on reconnaissance missions roughly 20 times. The flights were in pairs at maximum speed and high altitude (between 17,000–23,000 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-10"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 6 November 1971, a Soviet MiG-25 operating out of Egypt flying at Mach 2.5 was met by Israeli F-4Es and fired upon unsuccessfully.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinai_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sinai-34">[34]</a></sup> A MiG-25 was tracked flying over Sinai at Mach 3.2 during this period. The MiG-25 engines went into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overspeed" title="Overspeed">overspeed</a>, which led to them being scrapped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Great_Book_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Great_Book-10">[10]</a></sup> Unit Det 63 was sent back home in 1972, though reconnaissance Foxbats were sent back to Egypt in 19–20 October 1973 during the <a href="/wiki/Yom_Kippur_War" title="Yom Kippur War">Yom Kippur War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinai_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sinai-34">[34]</a></sup> Unit Det 154 remained there until late 1974.</p>
<p>During the 1970s, the Soviet air force conducted reconnaissance overflights across Iran using its MiG-25RBSh aircraft in response to <a href="/wiki/Project_Dark_Gene" title="Project Dark Gene">joint US-Iran recon operations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Air_Force" title="Swedish Air Force">Swedish Air Force</a> observed via radar a Soviet Air Defence MiG-25 at 63,000 ft trailed 2.9 km behind the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_SR-71_Blackbird" title="Lockheed SR-71 Blackbird">Lockheed SR-71 Blackbird</a> at 72,000 ft over the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> in the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbth2012_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-rbth2012-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syria">Syria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Syria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 13 February 1981, the <a href="/wiki/Israeli_Air_Force" title="Israeli Air Force">Israeli Air Force</a> sent two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RF-4" title="RF-4">RF-4</a>Es over <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a> as decoys for Syrian MiG-25 interceptors. As the MiGs scrambled, the RF-4Es turned back delivering chaff and using ECM pods. Two IDF/AF <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-15" title="F-15">F-15</a>As were waiting for the MiGs and shot one of them down with AIM-7F missiles. The other MiG was able to escape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In a similar engagement, on 29 July 1981, a Syrian MiG-25 was again downed by an Israeli F-15A,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_1997.2C_p._53_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_1997.2C_p._53-39">[39]</a></sup> after which a second MiG-25 launched its R-40 missiles at the F-15 and its wingman, but they missed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> However, other sources state the missiles hit and downed one of the F-15s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_1997.2C_p._53_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_1997.2C_p._53-39">[39]</a></sup> On 31 August 1982, a third Syrian MiG-25 was damaged by an Israeli <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hawk_missile" title="Hawk missile">Hawk SAM</a> and then destroyed by an F-15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iraq">Iraq</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Iraq">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Air-to-air_right_underside_rear_view_of_a_Soviet_MiG-25_Foxbat_aircraft.jpg"><img alt="Air-to-air right underside rear view of a Soviet MiG-25 Foxbat aircraft.jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2252" data-file-width="3000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Air-to-air_right_underside_rear_view_of_a_Soviet_MiG-25_Foxbat_aircraft.jpg/220px-Air-to-air_right_underside_rear_view_of_a_Soviet_MiG-25_Foxbat_aircraft.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Air-to-air_right_underside_rear_view_of_a_Soviet_MiG-25_Foxbat_aircraft.jpg/330px-Air-to-air_right_underside_rear_view_of_a_Soviet_MiG-25_Foxbat_aircraft.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Air-to-air_right_underside_rear_view_of_a_Soviet_MiG-25_Foxbat_aircraft.jpg/440px-Air-to-air_right_underside_rear_view_of_a_Soviet_MiG-25_Foxbat_aircraft.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iran.E2.80.93Iraq_War">Iran–Iraq War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Iran–Iraq War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The MiG-25 was in service with the <a href="/wiki/Iraqi_Air_Force" title="Iraqi Air Force">Iraqi Air Force</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93Iraq_War" title="Iran–Iraq War">Iran–Iraq War</a>. On 19 March 1982 an Iranian <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F-4_Phantom_II" title="McDonnell Douglas F-4 Phantom II">F-4E</a> was badly damaged by a missile fired by an Iraqi MiG-25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Iraqi MiG-25s made another kill against Iran in February 1983, when an Iraqi MiG-25PD shot down an Iranian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-130" title="C-130">C-130</a>. In April 1984, an Iraqi MiG-25PD shot down an Iranian <a href="/wiki/Northrop_F-5" title="Northrop F-5">F-5E</a>. On 21 March 1984, an Iraqi MiG-25PD shot down an Iranian F-4E and on 5 June 1985 an Iraqi MiG-25PD shot down a second Iranian F-4E. On 23 February 1986, an Iraqi MiG-25PD shot down an Iranian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EC-130E" title="EC-130E">EC-130E</a> and on 10 June an <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F-4_Phantom_II" title="McDonnell Douglas F-4 Phantom II">RF-4E</a>, later in October 1986, an Iraqi MiG-25PDS shot down a second RF-4E.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most successful Iraqi MiG-25 pilot of the war was Colonel Mohammed Rayyan, who was credited with 10 kills. Eight of these were while flying the MiG-25P from 1981 to 1986. In 1986, after attaining the rank of Colonel, Rayyan was shot down and killed by Iranian F-14s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> For the majority of the air combat Iraqi pilots used <a href="/wiki/R-40_(missile)" title="R-40 (missile)">R-40</a> missiles.</p>
<p>On 3 May 1981, an Iraqi MiG-25PD shot down an Algerian Gulfstream III. On 2 October 1986, an Iraqi MiG-25PD shot down a Syrian MiG-21RF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to research by journalist Tom Cooper, at least 10 MiG-25s (9 reconnaissance and 1 fighter) may have been shot down by Iranian F-14s (one of them shared with an F-5<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>) during the Iran-Iraq war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Combat_2004.2C_pp._85-88_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Combat_2004.2C_pp._85-88-47">[47]</a></sup> Only three MiG-25 losses (to ground fire and air combat) were confirmed by Iraq.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Persian_Gulf_War">Persian Gulf War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Persian Gulf War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Defense.gov_News_Photo_990528-O-9999M-011.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="935" data-file-width="1398" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Defense.gov_News_Photo_990528-O-9999M-011.jpg/220px-Defense.gov_News_Photo_990528-O-9999M-011.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Defense.gov_News_Photo_990528-O-9999M-011.jpg/330px-Defense.gov_News_Photo_990528-O-9999M-011.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Defense.gov_News_Photo_990528-O-9999M-011.jpg/440px-Defense.gov_News_Photo_990528-O-9999M-011.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Defense.gov_News_Photo_990528-O-9999M-011.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Post Operation Desert Storm assessment photograph of an Iraqi aircraft bunker with the remains of a MIG-25 Foxbat after being attacked with a 2,000 pound laser-guided bomb.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Persian Gulf War</a>, a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F/A-18_Hornet" title="McDonnell Douglas F/A-18 Hornet">F/A-18</a>, piloted by Lt Cdr <a href="/wiki/Scott_Speicher" title="Scott Speicher">Scott Speicher</a>, was shot down on the first night of the war by a missile fired by a MiG-25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIA_summ_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-CIA_summ-50">[50]</a></sup> The kill was reportedly made with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bisnovat_R-40" title="Bisnovat R-40">Bisnovat R-40</a>TD missile fired from a MiG-25PDS flown by Lt. Zuhair Dawood of the 84th squadron of the IQAF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two IQAF MiG-25s were shot down by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> F-15s on January 19. The MiGs attempted to hide from the F-15s by using chaff and electronic jammers in order to engage the F-15s undetected. However the F-15 pilots were able to reacquire the two Iraqi MiG-25s and shot both down with <a href="/wiki/AIM-7_Sparrow" title="AIM-7 Sparrow">AIM-7 Sparrow</a> missiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> In another incident, an Iraqi MiG-25PD, after eluding eight USAF F-15s at long range, fired three missiles at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_EF-111A_Raven" title="General Dynamics EF-111A Raven">General Dynamics EF-111A Raven</a> electronic warfare aircraft, forcing them to abort their mission and leave attacking aircraft without electronic jamming support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[N 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Samurra_Air_Battle" title="Samurra Air Battle">different incident</a>, two MiG-25s approached a pair of F-15s, fired missiles at long range which were evaded by the F-15s, and then outran the American fighters. Two more F-15s joined the pursuit, and a total of 10 air-to-air missiles were fired at the MiG-25s, though none reached them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup> According to the same sources, at least one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-111" title="F-111">F-111</a> was also forced to abort its mission by a MiG-25 on the first 24 hours of hostilities, during an air raid over <a href="/wiki/Tikrit" title="Tikrit">Tikrit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the war, on 27 December 1992, a U.S. <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_F-16_Fighting_Falcon" title="General Dynamics F-16 Fighting Falcon">F-16D</a> downed a MiG-25 that violated the <a href="/wiki/Iraqi_no-fly_zones" title="Iraqi no-fly zones">no-fly zone</a> in southern Iraq with an <a href="/wiki/AIM-120_AMRAAM" title="AIM-120 AMRAAM">AIM-120 AMRAAM</a> missile. It was the first USAF F-16 air-to-air victory and the first AMRAAM kill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 23 December 2002, an Iraqi MiG-25 shot down a U.S. Air Force unmanned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MQ-1_Predator" title="MQ-1 Predator">MQ-1 Predator</a> drone, which was performing armed reconnaissance over Iraq. This was the first time in history that an aircraft and an unmanned drone had engaged in combat. Predators had been armed with <a href="/wiki/AIM-92_Stinger" title="AIM-92 Stinger">AIM-92 Stinger</a> air-to-air missiles, and were being used to "bait" Iraqi fighter aircraft, then run. In this incident, the Predator did not run, but instead fired one of the Stingers, which missed, while the MiG's missile did not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>No Iraqi aircraft were deployed in the U.S. invasion of Iraq in 2003, with most Iraqi aircraft being hidden or destroyed on the ground. In August 2003, several dozen Iraqi aircraft were discovered buried in the sand.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ex_IAF_Mig-25.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="979" data-file-width="1306" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Ex_IAF_Mig-25.jpg/220px-Ex_IAF_Mig-25.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Ex_IAF_Mig-25.jpg/330px-Ex_IAF_Mig-25.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Ex_IAF_Mig-25.jpg/440px-Ex_IAF_Mig-25.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ex_IAF_Mig-25.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
MiG-25R of No. 102 Squadron IAF on display at the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Air_Force_Museum,_Palam" title="Indian Air Force Museum, Palam">Indian Air Force Museum, Palam</a></div>
</div>

<p>The MiG-25 was kept a guarded secret in India, designated <i><a href="/wiki/Garuda" title="Garuda">Garuda</a></i> after the large bird-like creature from Hindu and Buddhist scriptures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-India_retires_MiGs_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-India_retires_MiGs-60">[59]</a></sup> It was used extensively in the <a href="/wiki/Kargil_War" title="Kargil War">Kargil War</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2001%E2%80%932002_India%E2%80%93Pakistan_standoff" title="2001–2002 India–Pakistan standoff">Operation Parakram</a>, conducting aerial reconnaissance sorties over Pakistan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[N 2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rahul_K._Bhonsle_2006.2C_p._256_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rahul_K._Bhonsle_2006.2C_p._256-62">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 1997, an <a href="/wiki/Indian_Air_Force" title="Indian Air Force">Indian Air Force</a> Mikoyan MiG-25RB reconnaissance aircraft created a furor when the pilot flew faster than Mach 2 over Pakistani territory following a reconnaissance mission into Pakistan airspace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TT20131210_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-TT20131210-63">[61]</a></sup> The MiG-25 broke the sound barrier while flying at an altitude of around 65,000 feet (20,000 m), otherwise the mission would have remained covert, at least to the general public. The Pakistani Government contended that the breaking of the sound barrier was a deliberate attempt to make the point that the Pakistan Air Force (PAF) had no aircraft in its inventory that could come close to the MiG-25's cruising altitude (up to 74,000 feet (23,000 m)).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TT20131210_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-TT20131210-63">[61]</a></sup> India denied the incident but Pakistan's Foreign Minister, Gohar Ayub Khan, believed that the Foxbat photographed strategic installations near the capital, <a href="/wiki/Islamabad" title="Islamabad">Islamabad</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TT20131210_63-2"><a href="#cite_note-TT20131210-63">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lack of spare parts and India's acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">unmanned aerial vehicles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">satellite imagery</a> eventually led to its retirement in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[N 3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-India_retires_MiGs_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-India_retires_MiGs-60">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rahul_K._Bhonsle_2006.2C_p._256_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rahul_K._Bhonsle_2006.2C_p._256-62">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Libya">Libya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Libya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Libya was a major user of the MiG-25 as it imported 96 MiG-25PD interceptor, MiG-25PU trainer and MiG-25RBK reconnaissance aircraft in the late 1970s and early 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-defenceweb.co.za_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-defenceweb.co.za-66">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 1980s, Libya confronted the United States over some claims over the extension of its territorial waters. These incidents prompted a number of encounters between the opposing forces as it happened during the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Sidra_incident_(1981)" title="Gulf of Sidra incident (1981)">Gulf of Sidra incident (1981)</a> with the Libyan MiG-25s taking part in them.</p>
<p>During the following years, the Libyan MiG-25 fleet was grounded, lacking maintenance. As MiG-25 air-frames were grounded for several years, NATO attacks spared them during the <a href="/wiki/Libyan_Civil_War_(2011)" title="Libyan Civil War (2011)">Libyan Civil War (2011)</a>.</p>
<p>In 2014 and 2015, the Libyan forces under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_General_National_Congress" title="New General National Congress">New General National Congress</a> controlled a number of former LARAF air-frames, that were retired and stored before the Libyan Civil War in 2011, among them a number of MiG-25s. Technicians started working on some of the air-frames to push them back to service in the fight against the opposing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_Deputies" title="Council of Deputies">internationally recognized Libyan government</a> forces.</p>
<p>On 6 May 2015, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_General_National_Congress" title="New General National Congress">New General National Congress</a> MiG-25PU crashed near <a href="/wiki/Zintan" title="Zintan">Zintan</a> while attacking the civilian airport controlled by the opposing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_Deputies" title="Council of Deputies">internationally recognized Libyan government</a>, the pilot ejected and was captured by opposing forces which also claimed they downed the jet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[64]</a></sup> The jet may have been on one of its first flights after re-entering service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-defenceweb.co.za_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-defenceweb.co.za-66">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prototypes">Prototypes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Prototypes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>Ye-155R</dt>
<dd>Reconnaissance prototypes. Two prototypes (<i>Ye-155R-1</i> and <i>Ye-155R-2</i>) followed by four pre-production aircraft fitted with reconnaissance equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p108-9_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p108-9-68">[65]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Ye-155P</dt>
<dd>Interceptor fighter prototypes. Two prototypes (<i>Ye-155P-1</i> and <i>Ye-155P02</i>) followed by nine pre-production aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p109-0_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p109-0-69">[66]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Ye-266</dt>
<dd>Designation applied to prototypes and pre-production aircraft (<i>Ye-155R-1</i>, <i>Ye-155R-3</i> and <i>Ye-155P-1</i>) used for record breaking purposes in official documentation supplied to the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_A%C3%A9ronautique_Internationale" title="Fédération Aéronautique Internationale">Fédération Aéronautique Internationale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p110_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p110-70">[67]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interceptors">Interceptors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Interceptors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>MiG-25P</dt>
<dd>Single-seat all-weather interceptor fighter aircraft, powered by two <a href="/wiki/Tumansky_R-15" title="Tumansky R-15">Tumansky R-15</a>B-300 turbojet engines, fitted with RP-25 <i>Smerch</i>-A1 radar and armed with four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bisnovat_R-40" title="Bisnovat R-40">R-40</a> air-to-air missiles. NATO designation <i>Foxbat-A</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p110-1_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p110-1-71">[68]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>MiG-25PD</dt>
<dd>Improved single-seat all-weather interceptor fighter aircraft, which entered service from 1979. Fitted with R-15BD-300 engines and new N-005 <i>Saphir</i>-25 (RP-25M) <a href="/wiki/Pulse-Doppler_radar" title="Pulse-Doppler radar">Pulse-Doppler radar</a> with look-down/shoot down capability, based on the radar of the <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-23" title="Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-23">MiG-23ML</a>. Could be fitted with four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molniya_R-60" title="Molniya R-60">R-60</a> air-to-air missiles replacing outermost two R-40 missiles. Late examples fitted with an undernose IR search and track system. NATO designation <i>Foxbat-E</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p112_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p112-72">[69]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>MiG-25PDS</dt>
<dd>Upgrade of surviving MiG-25Ps to MiG-25PD standard from 1979. NATO designation <i>Foxbat-E</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p113_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p113-73">[70]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>MiG-25PDSL</dt>
<dd>Single MiG-25PD modified by addition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_countermeasures" title="Electronic countermeasures">electronic countermeasures</a> (ECM) equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p113_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p113-73">[70]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>MiG-25PDZ</dt>
<dd>Single MiG-25PD modified with retractable in-flight refuelling probe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p113_73-2"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p113-73">[70]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>MiG-25M</dt>
<dd>Two testbeds (one converted from a MiG-25RB and one from a MiG-25PD) for more powerful (98.04 kN (22,045 lbf) dry, 129.71 kN (29,166 lbf) with afterburner) engines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p114_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p114-74">[71]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Ye-266M</dt>
<dd>Designation applied to MiG-25M when used for record breaking in 1975 and 1977, including setting an absolute altitude record for a <a href="/wiki/Flight_altitude_record#Jet_aircraft" title="Flight altitude record">jet aircraft</a> of 37,650 m (123,524 ft) on 31 August 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p114_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p114-74">[71]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Izdelye 99</dt>
<dd>Two aircraft used as testbeds for <a href="/wiki/Soloviev_D-30" title="Soloviev D-30">Soloviev D-30</a>F <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a> as later used in MiG-31.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p112_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p112-72">[69]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>

<dl>
<dt>MiG-25R</dt>
<dd>Single-seat high-altitude daylight reconnaissance aircraft, fitted with cameras, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ELINT" title="ELINT">ELINT</a> equipment. NATO codename <i>Foxbat-B</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p115_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p115-75">[72]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>MiG-25RB</dt>
<dd>Single-seat reconnaissance-bomber derivative of MiG-25R, fitted with improved reconnaissance systems and a Peleng automatic bombing system. The aircraft can carry a bombload of eight 500 kg (1,102 lb) bombs. Entered service in 1970. NATO codename <i>Foxbat-B</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p115_75-1"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p115-75">[72]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>MiG-25RBV</dt>
<dd>Modernised single-seat reconnaissance-bomber with revised Elint equipment (SRS-9 <i>Vraz</i>). NATO codename <i>Foxbat-B</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ_34_p116_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ_34_p116-76">[73]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>MiG-25RBT</dt>
<dd>Further improved reconnaissance-bomber, with <i>Tangaz</i> Elint equipment. NATO codename <i>Foxbat-B</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p117_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p117-77">[74]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>MiG-25RBN</dt>
<dd>Dedicated night reconnaissance aircraft, carrying 10 photoflash bombs under the fuselage. Only single prototype built. NATO codename <i>Foxbat-B</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p117_77-1"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p117-77">[74]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>MiG-25RR</dt>
<dd>Conversion of eight reconnaissance aircraft for high-altitude radiation sampling role. Used to monitor Chinese nuclear tests between 1970 and 1980. NATO codename <i>Foxbat-B</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p117_77-2"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p117-77">[74]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>MiG-25RBK</dt>
<dd>Single-seat dedicated Elint aircraft, with <i>Kub-3K</i> Elint system. Bombing capability retained but cameras not fitted. NATO codename <i>Foxbat-D</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p117_77-3"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p117-77">[74]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>MiG-25RBF</dt>
<dd>Conversion of MiG-25RBK with new <i>Shar-25</i> Elint equipment. NATO codename <i>Foxbat-D</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p118_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p118-78">[75]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>MiG-25RBS</dt>
<dd>Single-seat radar-reconnaissance aircraft, with <i>Sabla-E</i> side looking airborne radar (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SLAR" title="SLAR">SLAR</a>). Cameras not fitted but bombing capability retained. NATO codename <i>Foxbat-D</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p118_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p118-78">[75]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>MiG-25RBSh</dt>
<dd>MiG-25RBS fitted with more capable <i>Shompol</i> SLAR. NATO codename <i>Foxbat-D</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p118_78-2"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p118-78">[75]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>MiG-25BM "Foxbat-F"</dt>
<dd>Single-seat <a href="/wiki/Suppression_of_Enemy_Air_Defenses" title="Suppression of Enemy Air Defenses">defence-suppression</a> aircraft, armed with <a href="/wiki/Kh-58" title="Kh-58">Kh-58</a> or <a href="/wiki/Kh-31" title="Kh-31">Kh-31</a> air-to-surface missiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gunston_and_Spick_pp._132.E2.80.93133_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gunston_and_Spick_pp._132.E2.80.93133-79">[76]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>

<dl>
<dt>MiG-25PU</dt>
<dd>Two-seat conversion trainer for MiG-25P interceptors. Fitted with a new nose section with two separate cockpits. It has no radar and no combat capability. NATO codename <i>Foxbat-C</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p114_74-2"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p114-74">[71]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>MiG-25RU</dt>
<dd>Two-seat conversion trainer for reconnaissance versions. Fitted with MiG-25R navigation system. NATO codename <i>Foxbat-C</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p118_78-3"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p118-78">[75]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Ye-133</dt>
<dd>Designation given to single MiG-25PU used by <a href="/wiki/Svetlana_Savitskaya" title="Svetlana Savitskaya">Svetlana Savitskaya</a> to establish a number of women's speed and height records, starting with speed over a 15–25 km course of 2,683.45 km/h (1.667.47 mph) on 22 June 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WAPJ34_p114_74-3"><a href="#cite_note-WAPJ34_p114-74">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bel_p404_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bel_p404-80">[77]</a></sup></dd>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surviving_aircraft">Surviving aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Surviving aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>MiG-25PD Red 49 (c/n N84008895) is on display at the Central Armed Forces Museum, Moscow, Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[84]</a></sup></li>
<li>MiG-25RB (s/n 25105) is in the restoration facility at the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_the_United_States_Air_Force" title="National Museum of the United States Air Force">National Museum of the United States Air Force</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dayton,_Ohio" title="Dayton, Ohio">Dayton, Ohio</a>, US. This aircraft was found in 2003 during the opening months of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Iraqi_Freedom" title="Operation Iraqi Freedom">Operation Iraqi Freedom</a> by American forces, buried in the sand near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al_Taqaddum_Airbase" title="Al Taqaddum Airbase">Al Taqaddum Airbase</a>, about 250 km west of <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a>. The aircraft had been buried to prevent its destruction on the ground by coalition aircraft. When uncovered, the MiG-25RB was incomplete, as the wings could not be located. This aircraft was one of two MiG-25s transported by a <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_C-5_Galaxy" title="Lockheed C-5 Galaxy">Lockheed C-5A Galaxy</a> from Iraq to <a href="/wiki/Wright-Patterson_Air_Force_Base" title="Wright-Patterson Air Force Base">Wright-Patterson Air Force Base</a> for examination. It was donated to the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_the_United_States_Air_Force" title="National Museum of the United States Air Force">National Museum of the United States Air Force</a> in December 2006. The museum's restoration staff is currently attempting to locate a set of wings to complete the aircraft for display.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatMusAF_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-NatMusAF-88">[85]</a></sup></li>
<li>MiG-25R (s/n KP355) is on display at the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Air_Force_Museum,_Palam" title="Indian Air Force Museum, Palam">Indian Air Force Museum</a> at Palam, New Delhi and two MiG-25U trainers (s/n DS361 and DS362) are preserved at <a href="/wiki/Kalaikunda_Air_Force_Station" title="Kalaikunda Air Force Station">Kalaikunda Air Force Station</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midnapore_District" title="Midnapore District">Midnapore District</a> of <a href="/wiki/West_Bengal" title="West Bengal">West Bengal</a>, India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[86]</a></sup></li>
<li>MiG-25RBSh was bought by <a href="/wiki/Estonian_Aviation_Museum" title="Estonian Aviation Museum">Estonian Aviation Museum</a> in 2016 from a private owner in Finland, who in turn had purchased it from Russia after the dissolution of Soviet Union. The aircraft is displayed as part of the museum's exposition in <a href="/wiki/Tartu_County" title="Tartu County">Tartumaa</a>, Estonia since June 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[87]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specifications_.28MiG-25P.29">Specifications (MiG-25P)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Specifications (MiG-25P)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MiG-25.svg"><img alt="MiG-25.svg" data-file-height="1077" data-file-width="1469" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/MiG-25.svg/300px-MiG-25.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/MiG-25.svg/450px-MiG-25.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/MiG-25.svg/600px-MiG-25.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a></div>
<p style="font-size: 90%; margin:0; padding: .2em"><i>Data from</i> <i>The Great Book of Fighters</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[88]</a></sup> <i>International Directory of Military Aircraft</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frawley_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frawley-92">[89]</a></sup> <i>Combat Aircraft since 1945</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>General characteristics</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Crew:</b> One</li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 19.75 m (64 ft 10 in)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">Wingspan</a>:</b> 14.01 m (45 ft 11.5 in)</li>
<li><b>Height:</b> 6.10 m (20 ft 0.25 in)</li>
<li><b>Wing area:</b> 61.40 m² (660.93 ft²)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Manufacturer%27s_empty_weight" title="Manufacturer's empty weight">Empty weight</a>:</b> 20,000 kg (44,080 lb)</li>
<li><b>Loaded weight:</b> 36,720 kg (80,952 lb)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">Powerplant</a>:</b> 2 × <a href="/wiki/Tumansky_R-15" title="Tumansky R-15">Tumansky R-15</a>B-300 afterburning <a href="/wiki/Turbojet" title="Turbojet">turbojets</a>
<ul>
<li><b>Dry thrust:</b> 73.5 kN (16,524 lbf) each</li>
<li><b>Thrust with <a href="/wiki/Afterburner" title="Afterburner">afterburner</a>:</b> 100.1 kN (22,494 lbf) each</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Performance</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/V_speeds#Regulatory_V-speeds" title="V speeds">Maximum speed</a>:</b><br/>
<ul>
<li><b>High altitude:</b> <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach</a> 3.2<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Great_Book_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Great_Book-10">[10]</a></sup> (3,470 km/h, 2,170 mph); Mach 2.83 (3,200 km/h, 1,920 mph) continuous engine limit<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Great_Book_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Great_Book-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Low altitude:</b> 1,200 km/h (648 knots, 746 mph) at high altitude<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frawley_92-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frawley-92">[89]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Range_(aeronautics)" title="Range (aeronautics)">Range</a>:</b> 1,730 km (935 nmi, 1,075 mi) with internal fuel</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Range_(aeronautics)" title="Range (aeronautics)">Ferry range</a>:</b> 2,575 km (1,390 nmi)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ceiling_(aeronautics)" title="Ceiling (aeronautics)">Service ceiling</a>:</b> 20,700 m (67,915 ft) with four missiles; over 24,400 m (80,000 ft) for RB models</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Rate_of_climb" title="Rate of climb">Rate of climb</a>:</b> 208 m/s (40,950 ft/min)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wing_loading" title="Wing loading">Wing loading</a>:</b> 598 kg/m² (122.5 lb/ft²)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Thrust-to-weight_ratio" title="Thrust-to-weight ratio">Thrust/weight</a>:</b> 0.41<br/></li>
<li><b>Time to altitude:</b> 8.9 min to 20,000 m (65,615 ft)</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Armament</b></span><br/></p>
<ul>
<li>2x radar-guided <a href="/wiki/R-40_(missile)" title="R-40 (missile)">R-40R (AA-6 "Acrid")</a> <a href="/wiki/Air-to-air_missile" title="Air-to-air missile">air-to-air missiles</a>, and</li>
<li>2x infrared-guided R-40T missiles</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Avionics</b></span><br/></p>
<ul>
<li>RP-25 Smerch radar</li>
<li>A RV-UM or a RV-4 <a href="/wiki/Radar_altimeter" title="Radar altimeter">radar altimeter</a></li>
