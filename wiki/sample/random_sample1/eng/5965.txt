<p><b>Massimo 'Miki' Biasion</b> (born January 7, 1958) is an <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> rally driver, two-time <a href="/wiki/World_Rally_Championship" title="World Rally Championship">World Rally</a> champion.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miki_Biasion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lancia_Delta_S4_005.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="1280" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Lancia_Delta_S4_005.JPG/220px-Lancia_Delta_S4_005.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Lancia_Delta_S4_005.JPG/330px-Lancia_Delta_S4_005.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Lancia_Delta_S4_005.JPG/440px-Lancia_Delta_S4_005.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lancia_Delta_S4_005.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Biasion's <a href="/wiki/Lancia_Delta_S4" title="Lancia Delta S4">Lancia Delta S4</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Born at <a href="/wiki/Bassano_del_Grappa" title="Bassano del Grappa">Bassano del Grappa</a>, Biasion came to prominence in the early 1980s, winning both the Italian and European Rally Championships in 1983, driving a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lancia_037" title="Lancia 037">Lancia 037</a>. He was later drafted in to play a key role for the works <a href="/wiki/Lancia" title="Lancia">Lancia</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Rally_Championship" title="World Rally Championship">World Rally Championship</a> team in the mid-1980s as the squad sought to regroup after previous star driver <a href="/wiki/Henri_Toivonen" title="Henri Toivonen">Henri Toivonen</a>'s fatal crash, and would go on to dominate early <a href="/wiki/Group_A" title="Group A">Group A</a> rallying, taking the world championship in the years 1988 and 1989. At his peak, Biasion was notching victories in almost three quarters of all events he entered. He also was only the second driver to successfully defend his title (after <a href="/wiki/Juha_Kankkunen" title="Juha Kankkunen">Juha Kankkunen</a>) and the third one to win two titles (after Kankkunen and <a href="/wiki/Walter_R%C3%B6hrl" title="Walter Röhrl">Walter Röhrl</a>).</p>
<p>After his championship years, Biasion was never able to achieve that level of success again. He failed to win an event for Lancia in 1991, and switched to Ford for 1992 on a contract that made him the highest-paid driver of his day, and gave him some managerial input. The optimism did not last. Biasion reputedly described the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Sierra_RS_Cosworth" title="Ford Sierra RS Cosworth">Ford Sierra RS Cosworth 4x4</a> as "a pile of shit" after his first event for the team (the 1992 <a href="/wiki/Monte_Carlo_Rally" title="Monte Carlo Rally">Monte Carlo Rally</a>), although on the <a href="/wiki/Rally_de_Portugal" title="Rally de Portugal">Portuguese Rally</a> he did give the car its best WRC result by finishing second. The following year, driving the new <a href="/wiki/Ford_Escort_RS_Cosworth" title="Ford Escort RS Cosworth">Ford Escort RS Cosworth</a>, he won the <a href="/wiki/Acropolis_Rally" title="Acropolis Rally">Acropolis Rally</a> and led the driver's championship for part of the season.</p>
<p>Throughout Biasion's tenure at Ford he was overshadowed by the younger <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Delecour" title="François Delecour">François Delecour</a>, who generally proved to be faster, especially on tarmac. Moreover, Delecour's road accident, which forced him to sit out much of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1994_World_Rally_Championship_season" title="1994 World Rally Championship season">1994 season</a>, exposed the weaknesses in the team. Insufficient resources meant that development of the Escort slipped behind its rivals, and Biasion's relationship with the team worsened as the season progressed. He was subsequently dropped for the following year. After contesting a couple of rallies for private teams, he retired quietly at the end of the 1995 season (his 2 last WRC events were part of the 2LWC prior to the late <a href="/wiki/Colin_McRae" title="Colin McRae">Colin McRae</a>'s honor as '95 World Rally Champion, but this truly last WRC event was the RAC (now Wales Rally GB) in '94 when it was won by McRae himself and Didier Auriol's honor as '94 World Rally Champion).</p>
<p>Throughout his entire career he was co-driven by <a href="/wiki/Tiziano_Siviero" title="Tiziano Siviero">Tiziano Siviero</a>, the exception being the Portuguese Rally of 1988, where Siviero was replaced by Carlo Cassina due to health problems.</p>
<p>Biasion won the World Truck Championship in 1998 and 1999 driving an <a href="/wiki/Iveco" title="Iveco">Iveco</a>.</p>
<p>His presence in the starting grid of the <a href="/wiki/2007_Dakar_Rally" title="2007 Dakar Rally">Lisbon-Dakar 2007</a> has been announced. He will be driving a factory works <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiat" title="Fiat">Fiat</a> Panda Cross.</p>
