<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mithras Liturgy</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The "<b>Mithras Liturgy</b>" is a <a href="/wiki/Magical_texts" title="Magical texts">magical text</a> from the Great Magical Papyrus of Paris, part of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Magical_Papyri" title="Greek Magical Papyri">Greek Magical Papyri</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> numbered <i>PGM</i> IV.475-834.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The modern name by which the text is known originated in 1903 with <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_Dieterich" title="Albrecht Dieterich">Albrecht Dieterich</a>, its first translator,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> based on the invocation of Helios Mithras (Ἥλιοϲ Μίθραϲ) as the god who will provide the initiate with a revelation of immortality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The text is generally considered a product of the <a href="/wiki/Syncretism" title="Syncretism">religious syncretism</a> characteristic of the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_religion" title="Hellenistic religion">Hellenistic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Imperial</a> era, as were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mithraic_mysteries" title="Mithraic mysteries">Mithraic mysteries</a> themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Some scholars have argued that it has no direct connection to particular Mithraic ritual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Others consider it an authentic reflection of Mithraic liturgy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> or view it as Mithraic material reworked for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magic_in_the_Greco-Roman_world" title="Magic in the Greco-Roman world">syncretic tradition of magic and esotericism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Codex" title="Codex">codex</a> containing the text was acquired by the <a href="/wiki/Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France" title="Bibliothèque nationale de France">Bibliothèque Nationale</a> in 1857.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It is thought to date to the early 4th century AD, though Dieterich proposed a date of composition as early as 100–150 AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Its likely provenance in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Egypt" title="Roman Egypt">Egypt</a>, where evidence of Mithraic cult is rare, presents a major obstacle to regarding it an authentic liturgy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithras_Liturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marvin_Meyer" title="Marvin Meyer">Marvin Meyer</a> divides the Mithras Liturgy into two sections:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Lines 475–750 are a liturgy for the mystic ascent of the soul through seven stages, and 751–834 provide instructions on how to enact the liturgy.</p>
<p>The text begins by invoking <a href="/wiki/Providentia" title="Providentia">Providence</a> <i>(Pronoia)</i> and <a href="/wiki/Cupid_and_Psyche" title="Cupid and Psyche">Psyche</a> ("Soul") or in other readings <a href="/wiki/Tyche" title="Tyche">Tyche</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The speaker of the invocation announces that he is writing down the mysteries to offer instruction and not for gain, and that he seeks a revelation of the universe and immortality guided by an <i>archangelos</i> (ἀρχάγγελος, "high messenger") of Helios Mithras (lines 475–485).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ascent">Ascent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithras_Liturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ascent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ascent through seven grades is viewed by Meyer as representing Mithraic initiation, but it also bears a more general resemblance to the ascent of the initiate in <a href="/wiki/Theurgy" title="Theurgy">theurgy</a>, with parallels in fragments from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaldaean_Oracles" title="Chaldaean Oracles">Chaldaean Oracles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1._Four_Elements">1. Four Elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithras_Liturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1. Four Elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The speaker invokes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Four_elements" title="Four elements">four classical primordial elements</a>, punctuated by <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voces_magicae" title="Voces magicae">voces magicae</a></i>, magical sounds, in the following sequence:</p>
<ul>
<li><small>PPP SSS PHR[E]</small>, a popping and hissing sound characteristic of incantations</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Pneuma" title="Pneuma">pneuma</a></i> (wind, breath, spirit)</li>
<li><small>MMM</small></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fire_(classical_element)" title="Fire (classical element)">fire</a></li>
<li><small>ĒY ĒIA EĒ</small></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Water_(classical_element)" title="Water (classical element)">water</a></li>
<li><small>ŌŌŌ AAA EEE</small></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Earth_(classical_element)" title="Earth (classical element)">earth</a></li>
<li><small>YĒ YŌĒ</small></li>
</ul>
<p>These elements he refers to as "first origin of my origin" from which his "complete body" is made. He identifies himself by name, and by the name of his mother. The soul's encounter with the four elements is rehearsed as both generation and regeneration (lines 485–537).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mithraism_525_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mithraism_525-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2._Lower_powers_of_air">2. Lower powers of air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithras_Liturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2. Lower powers of air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>At this level (lines 537–585), the revelation-seeker is supposed to breathe deeply and feel himself lifted up, as if in midair, hearing and seeing nothing of mortal beings on earth. He is promised to see instead the divine order of the "visible gods" rising and setting. Ritual silence is prescribed, followed by another sequence of hissing, popping, and thirteen magic words: "Then you will see the gods looking graciously upon you and no longer rushing at you, but rather going about in their own order of affairs." After a shocking crash of thunder, another admonition of silence, and a magic incantation, the disk of the sun is to open and issue five-pointed stars. The eyes are to be closed for the following prayer.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="3._Aion_and_powers">3. Aion and powers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithras_Liturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 3. Aion and powers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In this prayer (lines 585–628), the speaker again names himself and his mother, followed by an extensive list of translatable epithets such as "Light-maker" and "Fire-driver" interspersed with magic names. These are "planetary guardians of the gates of heaven".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mithraism_525_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mithraism_525-16">[16]</a></sup> Among the invocations are <a href="/wiki/Aion_(deity)" title="Aion (deity)">Aion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tetragrammaton" title="Tetragrammaton">Iao</a>. An extensive series of vowels are pronounced "with fire and spirit." After thunder and a feeling of physical agitation, another series of magic words elicits a vision of Helios.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="4._Helios">4. Helios</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithras_Liturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 4. Helios">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Helios is described as "a youthful god, beautiful in appearance, with fiery hair, and in a white tunic and a scarlet cloak, and wearing a fiery crown." He is to be given the "fire greeting" (lines 628–657), and asked for protection while kissing <a href="/wiki/Amulet" title="Amulet">phylacteries</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="5._Seven_Tychai">5. Seven <i>Tychai</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithras_Liturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 5. Seven Tychai">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The celestial doors are thrown open to reveal seven virgins dressed in linen and with faces of <a href="/wiki/Asp_(reptile)" title="Asp (reptile)">asps</a>, an element identified as Egyptian. They carry golden wands, and are to be hailed individually (lines 657–672).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="6._Seven_Pole-Lords">6. Seven Pole-Lords</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithras_Liturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 6. Seven Pole-Lords">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Next to come forth are the seven Pole-Lords, wearing linen loincloths and with faces of bulls. They have seven gold diadems, and are also to be hailed individually by name. These have powers of thunder, lightning, and earthquakes, as well as the capacity to grant physical health, good eyesight and hearing, and calmness (lines 673–692). The two groups of seven, female and male, are both depicted in an Egyptian manner and represent the "region of the fixed stars."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mithraism_525_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mithraism_525-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="7._Highest_god">7. Highest god</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithras_Liturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 7. Highest god">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the midst of lightning and tremors of the earth, the highest god appears, youthful and bright in appearance, wearing a white tunic, a golden crown, and trousers. He holds the shoulder of a bull in what seems to be an astronomical reference (lines 696–724). His eyes project lightning bolts, and stars issue from his body. The instructions are to "make a long bellowing sound, straining your belly, that you may excite the five senses; bellow long until out of breath, and again kiss the phylacteries."</p>
<p>The encounter with the highest god is intended to result in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divine_revelation" title="Divine revelation">divine revelation</a> and <i>apathanatismos</i>, a technical term for the temporary achieving of a state of immortality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enactment_and_use">Enactment and use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithras_Liturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Enactment and use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>LInes 751–834 are instructions on how to enact the liturgy. The practitioner is warned not to misuse the <i>mysterion</i> (lines 724–834), and is given instructions on the preparation of magical accoutrements: a <a href="/wiki/Scarab_(artifact)" title="Scarab (artifact)">sun scarab</a> ointment (751–778), the herb <i>kentritis</i> (778–792), and the protective phylacteries for the ritual (813–819). The section also offers some additional information and incantations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mithraism_525_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Mithraism_525-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Magic_context">Magic context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithras_Liturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Magic context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Book IV of the Greek Magical Papyri in which the "Mithras Liturgy" occurs, lines 1-25 are a spell calling on Egyptian and Jewish powers in order to obtain information. Lines 1127-64 are a spell for exorcising a demon, using Coptic words of Christian origin,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> with instructions for preparing an amulet. Lines 1716-1870 are headed "Sword of Dardanos" and is a love spell.</p>
<p>The Mithras Liturgy shares several elements found widely in magic as practiced in the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman_world" title="Greco-Roman world">Greco-Roman world</a>, which drew on or claimed the authority of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_religion" title="Ancient Egyptian religion">Egyptian religion and magic</a>. These include the preparation of <a href="/wiki/Amulet" title="Amulet">amulets</a> and ointments, the timing of rituals based on astronomical phenomena or <a href="/wiki/Horoscope" title="Horoscope">horoscopes</a>, and the manipulation of breath and speech. Vocalizations include popping and hissing sounds for <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeia</a>, variations on the sequence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_vowels" title="Ancient Greek vowels">Greek vowels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glossolalia" title="Glossolalia">glossolalia</a>, and words that are untranslatable but seem to derive from or are intended to sound like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_language" title="Ancient Egyptian language">Egyptian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Hebrew" title="Ancient Hebrew">Hebrew</a> and other languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mithraism_525_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-Mithraism_525-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Mithras Liturgy is the only text in Betz's collection of the <i>Greek Magical Papyri</i> that involves a request for immortality.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It is an example of the difficulties in distinguishing between the categories of "magic" and "religion" in the ancient world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Questions_of_Mithraic_content">Questions of Mithraic content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mithras_Liturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Questions of Mithraic content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name "the Mithras liturgy" was given to it by Dieterich, who dedicated the edition to <a href="/wiki/Franz_Cumont" title="Franz Cumont">Franz Cumont</a>. But Cumont could not see the text as being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mithraic" title="Mithraic">Mithraic</a> in origin. Gee believes that its origins should be sought in this context, while Hans Dieter Betz thinks rather of a wandering philosophical origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Classicist Johan C. Thom notes that opinions regarding the context of the text differ, for example, Mithraism or another mystery cult, ancient magic, the Egyptian cult regarding the dead, or <a href="/wiki/Theurgy" title="Theurgy">theurgy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Mithraic scholars such as Cumont, <a href="/wiki/Ulrich_von_Wilamowitz-Moellendorff" title="Ulrich von Wilamowitz-Moellendorff">Ulrich von Wilamowitz-Moellendorff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_P._Nilsson" title="Martin P. Nilsson">Martin P. Nilsson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Burkert" title="Walter Burkert">Walter Burkert</a> do not identify the text as a Mithraic liturgy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Cumont argued that the text lacked Mithraic <a href="/wiki/Eschatology" title="Eschatology">eschatology</a>, the Mithraic doctrine of the passage of the soul through the seven planetary spheres, and Mithras as a guide in the ascension.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Betz believes that the Mithras Liturgy is a product at the meeting-point of Greek, Egyptian, and Mithraic traditions, finally identifying the central 'ascent' section as a product of early <a href="/wiki/Hermeticism" title="Hermeticism">Hermeticism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Richard Gordon, however, doubts that Hermeticism influenced the text.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marvin_Meyer" title="Marvin Meyer">Marvin Meyer</a> is certain that the text has connections to <a href="/wiki/Mithraism" title="Mithraism">Mithraism</a> and believes that it "contributes a great deal to the study of magic, miracle, and ritual in religions in antiquity and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_antiquity" title="Late antiquity">late antiquity</a>, including Christianity, and the stories of miracles attributed to Jesus and others may profitably be studied with texts like the Mithras Liturgy at hand."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
