<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Meatloaf</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Meatloaf</b> is a dish of <a href="/wiki/Ground_meat" title="Ground meat">ground meat</a> mixed with other ingredients, formed into a <a href="/wiki/Loaf" title="Loaf">loaf</a> shape, then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baked" title="Baked">baked</a> or <a href="/wiki/Smoking_(cooking)" title="Smoking (cooking)">smoked</a>. The loaf shape is formed by either <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">cooking</a> it in a <a href="/wiki/Bread_pan" title="Bread pan">loaf pan</a>, or forming it by hand on a flat baking pan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zeldes_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-zeldes-1">[1]</a></sup> Meatloaf is usually made from <a href="/wiki/Beef" title="Beef">ground beef</a>, although <a href="/wiki/Lamb_and_mutton" title="Lamb and mutton">lamb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pork" title="Pork">pork</a>, <a href="/wiki/Veal" title="Veal">veal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venison" title="Venison">venison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seafood" title="Seafood">seafood</a> are also used.</p>
<p>The meatloaf has European origins; meatloaf of <a href="/wiki/Mincing" title="Mincing">minced</a> meat was mentioned in the famous Roman cookery collection <i><a href="/wiki/Apicius" title="Apicius">Apicius</a></i> as early as the 5th century. Meatloaf is a traditional <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgian</a> dish, and it is a cousin to the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> <a href="/wiki/Meatball" title="Meatball">meatball</a>. American meatloaf<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meatloaf_history_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meatloaf_history-2">[2]</a></sup> has its origins in <a href="/wiki/Scrapple" title="Scrapple">scrapple</a>, a mixture of ground pork and <a href="/wiki/Cornmeal" title="Cornmeal">cornmeal</a> served by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German-Americans" title="German-Americans">German-Americans</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> since <a href="/wiki/Colonial_history_of_the_United_States" title="Colonial history of the United States">Colonial times</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meatloaf_history_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Meatloaf_history-2">[2]</a></sup> However, meatloaf in the contemporary American sense did not appear in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cookbooks" title="Cookbooks">cookbooks</a> until the late 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zeldes_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-zeldes-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<dl>
<dt>Argentina</dt>
<dd>In Argentina it is usually called <i>Pan de carne</i>. It is typically filled with ham, cheese, carrots or some other vegetable.</dd>
<dt>Austria</dt>
<dd>The Austrian meatloaf version is called <i>Faschierter Braten</i>. Most of the time it is not filled (e.g., in Germany), but it is wrapped in ham before baking it. Often it is served with mashed potatoes (when warm) or with <a href="/wiki/Cumberland_sauce" title="Cumberland sauce">sauce cumberland</a> (when cold).</dd>
<dt>Belgium</dt>
<dd>The Belgian version of meatloaf is called <i>vleesbrood</i> (meatbread) in Dutch and <i>pain de viande</i> in French. It is usually served warm and can then be served with various sauces, but can also be eaten cold with a loaf of bread.</dd>
<dt>Bulgaria</dt>
<dd><i>Rulo Stefani</i> (<a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>: <span lang="bg">Руло Стефани</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The Bulgarian <i>rulo Stefani</i> meatloaf is similar to the Hungarian <i>Stefánia</i> meatloaf, with hard-boiled eggs in the middle.</dd>
<dt>Chile</dt>
<dd>Chilean meatloaf, known as <i>Asado Aleman</i> (German roasted meat) is a staple of southern Chile cuisine, especially in areas known for having been influenced by the arrival of German settlers during the 18th and 19th century. The most common recipe nowadays consists of ground beef, carrots, sausages, boiled eggs and breadcrumbs, cooked on the oven and normally served with a side-dish of mashed potatoes or rice.</dd>
<dt>Cuba</dt>
<dd>The Cuban meatloaf is called <i>pulpeta</i>. It is made with ground beef and ground ham, and stuffed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_boiled_eggs" title="Hard boiled eggs">hard boiled eggs</a>, and it is cooked on the stovetop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The dish was brought to public attention, mistakenly referred to as a sausage, in the second episode of the third season of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cosby_Show" title="The Cosby Show">The Cosby Show</a></i> entitled "Food for Thought".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Czech Republic</dt>
<dd>In the Czech Republic, meatloaf is referred to as <i>sekaná</i> ('chopped'). It is optional to put hard boiled eggs, gherkins, or wienerwurst inside.</dd>
<dt>Denmark</dt>
<dd>Danish meatloaf is called <i>forloren hare</i> 'mock hare' or <i>farsbrød</i> 'ground-meat bread' and is usually made from a mixture of ground pork and beef with strips of bacon or cubed bacon on top. It is served with boiled or mashed potatoes and brown sauce sweetened with red currant jelly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Finland</dt>
<dd>Finnish meatloaf is called <i>lihamureke</i>. It is completely based on the basic meatball recipe. The only spices used are salt and pepper. It is not customary to stuff <i>lihamureke</i> with anything. The usual side dish is mashed potatoes, and <i>lihamureke</i> is usually served with brown sauce.</dd>
<dt>Germany</dt>
<dd>In <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, meatloaf is referred to as <i>Hackbraten</i>, <i>Faschierter Braten</i> or <i>Falscher Hase</i> 'mock hare'. In some regions it often has boiled eggs inside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Greater Middle East</dt>
<dd><i>Kafta</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Kofta" title="Kofta">kofta</a></i> is a similar dish which the mixture can be made into <a href="/wiki/Hamburger" title="Hamburger">hamburgers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kebab" title="Kebab">kebabs</a>. It usually has <a href="/wiki/Parsley" title="Parsley">parsley</a> in it.</dd>
<dt>Greece</dt>
<dd>In <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, meatloaf is referred to as <i>rolo</i> (Ρολό) and it is usually filled with hard boiled eggs, although several other variations exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Hungary</dt>
<dd>Stefania meatloaf (<a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>: <span lang="hu"><i>Stefánia szelet</i></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> or Stefania slices are a type of <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_cuisine" title="Hungarian cuisine">Hungarian</a> long meatloaf baked in a loaf pan, with 3 hard boiled eggs in the middle, making decorative white and yellow rings in the middle of the slices.</dd>
<dt>Italy</dt>
<dd>In Italy, meatloaf is called <i>polpettone</i> and can be filled with eggs or ham and cheese.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd>
<dt>Jewish cuisine</dt>
<dd>In <a href="/wiki/Jewish_cuisine" title="Jewish cuisine">Jewish cuisine</a>, meatloaf is called <i>Klops</i> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he">קלופס</span>‎‎) and can be served cold or hot. It is sometimes filled with whole boiled eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The source of the word might be German, <i>Klops</i>, meaning meatball.</dd>
<dt>Palestine/Lebanon</dt>
<dd>"Kafta" or "Kofta" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar">كفتة</span>‎‎) is a Middle Eastern version of meatloaf. It is usually beef or lamb mixed with onions, parsley, and various spices flattened into a square loaf and covered in either tomato sauce or tahini sauce. It is baked with the sauces and enjoyed with rice and potatoes (usually baked with the kafta). Kafta or Kofta is also turned into kebabs and grilled during the summer months.</dd>
<dt>Macedonia</dt>
<dd><i>Rolat</i> is a similar dish to the chiefly Arab, though also Persian and South-Asian, kofta. Ground beef is rolled and cooked until brown. It can be cooked with vegetables and various sauces.</dd>
<dt>Mexico</dt>
<dd>It is known as <i>albondigón</i> and has a small size.</dd>
<dt>Mongolia</dt>
<dd>хучмал (khoochmal) is served with mashed potatoes cooked over the ground meat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Philippines</dt>
<dd>There is a meatloaf dish called <i>embotido</i> (not to be confused with the Spanish <a href="/wiki/Embutido" title="Embutido">embutido</a>) made of well-seasoned ground pork, raisins, minced carrots, sausages, and whole boiled eggs. The meat is molded into a roll with the sausages and hard boiled eggs set in the middle. Another variation of the dish involves wrapping the meatloaf with pork crow or <a href="/wiki/Mesentery" title="Mesentery">mesentery</a>. It is then wrapped in aluminum foil (historically, banana leaves) and steamed for an hour. The cooked <i>embotido</i> may be stored in freezers. It is usually served fried and sliced for breakfast. In some parts of the Philippines, the embotido is erroneously called "<i>morcon</i>". However, the Filipino <a href="/wiki/Morc%C3%B3n" title="Morcón">morcón</a> itself is another dish though prepared in a similar way.</dd>
<dt>Poland</dt>
<dd>Called <i>pieczeń rzymska</i> ("Roman roast") or <i>klops</i> is made of ground pork and/or beef, onions and garlic, with obligatory hard boiled egg inside.</dd>
<dt>Puerto Rico</dt>
<dd>In Puerto Rican cuisine, meatloaf is known as <i>albondigón</i>. Puerto Rican style meatloaf is made with ground pork, beef, turkey, <a href="/wiki/Adobo" title="Adobo">adobo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire_sauce" title="Worcestershire sauce">worcestershire sauce</a>, milk, ketchup, potatoes, red beans, breadcrumb, parsley, with a hard-boiled egg in the middle.</dd>
<dt>Romania</dt>
<dd>In Romanian cuisine, there is a meatloaf dish called <i>drob</i>, similar to other minced meat dishes in the region like the Bulgarian <i>Rulo Stefani</i> or the Hungarian <i>Stefánia</i> meatloaf, the major difference being that it is always made with lamb organs (or a mixture of lamb organs and pork or veal) and the hard boiled eggs in the centre of the <i>drob</i> are optional.</dd>
<dt>South Africa</dt>
<dd>One of South Africa's most popular dishes is a form of meatloaf called <a href="/wiki/Bobotie" title="Bobotie">bobotie</a>. Food in South Africa varies by region, and bobotie is considered a Cape Town or Western Cape dish, but is so popular throughout the country that it is sometimes considered one of South Africa's signature dishes. It is a slightly sweet meatloaf flavored with curry spices, dried apricots and almonds, and is topped with whipped egg and milk topping which when baked on top of the meat filling creates an egg "topping". It arrived in South Africa several hundred years ago from the East Indies and became one of the signature dishes of Cape Town's Cape Malay community.</dd>
<dt>Sweden</dt>
<dd>Swedish meatloaf is called <i>köttfärslimpa</i> and is usually made from a mixture of ground pork and beef. It is served with boiled or mashed potatoes, brown sauce and lingonberry jam.</dd>
<dt>Turkey</dt>
</dl>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_cuisine" title="Turkish cuisine">Turkish cuisine</a> there is a meatloaf with vegetables dish called <i>dalyan köfte</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yegul2010_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yegul2010-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>United Kingdom</dt>
<dd>In the UK there are regional meatloaf dishes known as <a href="/wiki/Haslet" title="Haslet">Haslet</a>.</dd>
<dt>United States</dt>
<dd>In 2007, meatloaf was voted the seventh-favorite dish in the United States according to <i><a href="/wiki/Good_Housekeeping" title="Good Housekeeping">Good Housekeeping</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd>
<dd>During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, cooking meatloaf was a way to stretch the food budget for families, using an inexpensive type of meat and other ingredients as leftovers; along with spices,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meatloaf_history_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Meatloaf_history-2">[2]</a></sup> it was popular to add <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereal</a> grains, bread or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saltine" title="Saltine">saltines</a> to the meatloaf to stretch the meat. The tradition lives on with the merits of producing a lower-fat dish with superior binding and consistency.</dd>
<dd>The meatloaf is typically eaten with some kind of <a href="/wiki/Sauce" title="Sauce">sauce</a> or <a href="/wiki/Relish" title="Relish">relish</a>. Many of these recipes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tasty_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tasty-13">[13]</a></sup> call for pasta sauce or tomato sauce to be poured over the loaf to form a crust during baking. The tomato-based sauce may be replaced with simple brown gravy or onion gravy, but the meatloaf is prepared in a similar manner. <a href="/wiki/Barbecue_sauce" title="Barbecue sauce">Barbecue sauce</a>, tomato <a href="/wiki/Ketchup" title="Ketchup">ketchup</a>, or a mixture of both tomato ketchup and <a href="/wiki/Mustard_(condiment)" title="Mustard (condiment)">mustard</a> may also be used. American meatloaf may be <a href="/wiki/Garnish_(food)" title="Garnish (food)">garnished</a> with plain ketchup or a "meatloaf sauce" consisting of ketchup and <a href="/wiki/Brown_sugar" title="Brown sugar">brown sugar</a> mixed.</dd>
<dd>Another variety of meatloaf is prepared by frosting it with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mashed_potatoes" title="Mashed potatoes">mashed potatoes</a>, drizzling it with a small amount of <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">butter</a>, and browning in the oven.</dd>
<dd>The meatloaf is normally served warm as part of the main course, but can also be found sliced as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_cut" title="Cold cut">cold cut</a>. Meatloaf can also be considered a typical <a href="/wiki/Comfort_food" title="Comfort food">comfort food</a> and is served in many diners and restaurants today.</dd>
<dt>Vietnam</dt>
<dd>The Vietnamese meatloaf version is called giò. It is boiled rather than baked or smoked. There are many versions of giò that differ by the ingredients used.</dd>
</dl>
